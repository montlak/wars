3. Consequential Damages and Costs--Whistleblower Case

Consequential damages were made available under the 1994 amendments to the Whistleblower Protection Act (discussed in Chapter 13 under "Whistleblowing Reprisal"), providing relief to employees and applicants who can demonstrate that job actions were improperly tainted by reprisal against whistleblowing activities. The 2012 Whistleblower Protection Enhancement Act added compensatory damages to the mix of remedies in a whistleblower reprisal case. Consequential damages (or compensatory damages under the WPEA) are available when the Board orders corrective action in a whistleblower appeal under 5 USC 1221 and when the Board orders corrective action in a Special Counsel complaint under 5 USC 1214. Seibel v. Dept. of Treasury, 87 MSPR 260, 266 n.3 (2000). The 1994 legislation did not define consequential damages. [Refer later in this chapter to "Compensatory Damages --Whistleblower Case."]

5 USC 1214(a)(3), as amended in 2012, provides:

Except in a case in which an employee, former employee, or applicant for employment has the right to appeal directly to the Merit Systems Protection Board under any law, rule, or regulation, any such employee, former employee, or applicant shall seek corrective action from the Special Counsel before seeking corrective action from the Board. An employee, former employee, or applicant for employment may seek corrective action from the Board under section 1221, if such employee, former employee, or applicant seeks corrective action for a prohibited personnel practice described in section 2302(b)(8) or section 2302(b)(9) (A)(i), (B), (C), or (D) from the Special Counsel....

5 USC 1214(g), as amended in 2012, adds:

If the Board orders corrective action under this section, such corrective action may include--

(1) that the individual be placed, as nearly as possible, in the position the individual would have been in had the prohibited personnel practice not occurred; and

(2) reimbursement for attorney's fees, back pay and related benefits, medical costs incurred, travel expenses, any other reasonable and foreseeable consequential damages, and compensatory damages (including interest, reasonable expert witness fees, and costs).

5 USC 1221, as amended in 2012, provides, in part, as to IRA appeals:

(g)(1)(A) If the Board orders corrective action under this section, such corrective action may include--

(i) that the individual be placed, as nearly as possible, in the position the individual would have been in had the prohibited personnel practice not occurred; and

(ii) back pay and related benefits, medical costs incurred, travel expenses, any other reasonable and foreseeable consequential damages, and compensatory damages (including interest, reasonable expert witness fees, and costs).

5 USC 1221(i) provides (as amended by the WPEA, but not materially different than the pre-2012 version):

Subsections (a) through (h) shall apply in any proceeding brought under section 7513(d) if, or to the extent that, a prohibited personnel practice as defined in section 2302 (b)(8) or section 2302 (b)(9)(A)(i), (B), (C), or (D) is alleged.

Although consequential damages were available for IRA appeals (first presented through OSC) and for OSC corrective actions, but in cases raising whistleblowing reprisal on direct appeal to the Board from an otherwise appealable action, notwithstanding Bohac v. USDA, 239 F.3d 1334, 1338-43 (Fed. Cir. 2001), post, (an adverse action appeal that discussed consequential damages), the Federal Circuit determined, not without ambiguity, in Prichard v. DOD, 484 Fed. Appx. 489, 490-91 (Fed. Cir. 2012 NP), as to a Chapter 43 performance appeal not involving a whistleblower reprisal claim but asserting a claim for consequential damages nonetheless:

The Board's authority to award consequential and compensatory damages is limited. See Lane v. Pena, 518 U.S. 187, 192 (1996) ("To sustain a claim that the Government is liable for awards of monetary damages, the waiver of sovereign immunity must extend unambiguously to such monetary claims."). While the Board has the power to award consequential damages when ordering corrective action pursuant to a Special Counsel complaint proceeding under 5 U.S.C. § 1214(g)(2) and 5 C.F.R. § 1201.202(b)(2), this power does not avail Prichard for two reasons. First, Prichard seeks damages to remedy his non-pecuniary losses. But the consequential damages provision of § 1214(g)(2) does not authorize damages for non-pecuniary losses. See Bohac v. Dep't of Agriculture, 239 F.3d 1334, 1342 (Fed. Cir. 2001) (explaining, in reaching the same conclusion regarding the corresponding provision of 5 U.S.C. § 1221, that "section 1214...was designed to allow only for reimbursement of out-of-pocket costs" and not non-pecuniary damages). Second, nothing waives sovereign immunity to grant the Board authority to award § 1214 damages in a direct appeal as opposed to a Special Counsel investigation. See Giove v. Office of Personnel Management, 106 M.S.P.R. 53, 57 (2007) ("The Board's authority to award damages is limited to a few categories of cases, i.e., cases in which the Board orders corrective action in an individual right of action appeal, those in which it orders corrective action in response to a petition filed by the Special Counsel under 5 U.S.C. § 1214, and those in which it finds intentional discrimination of a kind covered under the Civil Rights act of 1991."); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.201(c) ("An award of consequential damages is authorized in only two situations: Where the Board orders corrective action in a whistleblower appeal...and where the Board orders corrective action in a Special Counsel complaint."). The Board was, thus, correct in concluding that Prichard's claim for consideration of his alleged harmful procedural error and prohibited personnel practices evidence was moot because the Board, in granting reinstatement, back pay, and benefits, had granted all the relief he may obtain.

The court's discussion in Prichard may have been somewhat attenuated, since the quoted section was not really necessary for the result reached by the court and may not have been the true focus of the opinion writer. Prichard was a Chapter 43 case, not covered by 5 USC 1221(i), concerning adverse actions appealed under 5 USC 7513(d). Statutory analysis suggests that consequential damages (and now compensatory damages) should be available when a whistleblower claim is successfully pursued as an affirmative defense in an adverse action. The limitations expressed in Prichard on the scope of consequential damages may have been overcome by the 2012 Whistleblower Protection Enhancement Act.

The Board did not cite Prichard, but noted the availability of consequential damages only in OSC corrective actions or IRA cases in Voeller v. Dept. of Army, AT-0752-12-0447-C-1 (NP 9/30/2013):

With respect to consequential damages, the Board may make such an award only in two circumstances: (1) as authorized by 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii), where the Board orders corrective action in a whistleblower appeal to which 5 U.S.C. § 1221 applies; or, (2) as authorized by 5 U.S.C. § 1214(g)(2), where the Board orders corrective action in a Special Counsel complaint under 5 U.S.C. § 1214. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.202(b)(1)-(2). Although the appellant indicated that she would also like to file a whistleblower claim with her suspension appeal, and she requested an award of consequential damages in connection with it, the administrative judge did not adjudicate the appellant's affirmative defenses. Thus, the essential predicate that might allow for such an award has not occurred, and, in the absence of any express statutory authorization to award such damages, the Board lacks authority to do so. See Wilson v. U.S. Postal Service, 38 M.S.P.R. 156, 159 (1988).

Voeller may not provide complete analysis of the statutory underpinnings for consequential damages, for the circumstances of the case were such that the AJ had not adjudicated the appellant's affirmative defense of whistleblowing. The Board's discussion was not essential to the result achieved. See Massimino v. VA, 58 MSPR 318, 323-24 (1993) ("The Whistleblower Protection Act of 1989 (WPA) at 5 U.S.C. § 1222 states that '[e]xcept as provided in section 1221(i), nothing in...chapter [12] or chapter 23 shall be construed to limit any right or remedy available under a provision of statute that is outside of [either chapter] .' Thus, section 1222 strongly suggests that any rights under 5 U.S.C. chapter 75, such as the right to raise affirmative defenses in matters appealable under those chapters, are not restricted."). Bohac v. USDA, 239 F.3d 1334 (Fed. Cir. 2001), discussed post, is not definitive because, although it involved an adverse action, the appellate court appeared to analyze the case as an IRA appeal seeking damages for pain and suffering, i.e., compensatory damages. The court's essential ruling was that compensatory damages were not encompassed in the then statutorily-authorized consequential damages.

Hickey v. DHS, 766 Fed. Appx. 970, 978-79 (Fed. Cir. 2019 NP), affirming the denial of consequential damages in an IRA appeal, explained under the facts of that appeal the lack of proof of out-of-pocket expenses or monetary losses:

IV. Consequential Damages

A. Legal Standard

If the [MSPB] orders corrective action...such corrective action may include," inter alia, "reasonable and foreseeable consequential damages." 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)(A), (g)(1)(A)(ii). We have explained that "the term 'consequential damages' [under § 1221(g) of the WPA[7]] is limited to reimbursement of out-of-pocket costs and does not include non-pecuniary damages." Bohac, 239 F.3d at 1343. This is because "Congress intended a narrow construction of 'consequential damages.'" Id. at 1342.

[7] In Bohac, we discussed the WPEA's predecessor, the WPA. See 239 F.3d at 1334. We explained that while the "WPA does not in fact provide for recovery of consequential 'damages,'" but rather "consequential changes," "the reference to 'changes' is obviously a mistake." Id. at 1338 (emphasis added). "It is well-established that an appellate court may interpret statutes to correct such obvious mistakes" and we did so to allow recovery of consequential damages at that time. Id.; see U.S. Nat'l Bank of Or. v. Indep. Ins. Agents of Am., 508 U.S. 439, 462 (1993) (disregarding a scrivener's error on punctuation because it was against the "overwhelming evidence from the structure, language, and subject matter of the...Act").

B. Substantial Evidence Supports the MSPB's Determination that Mr. Hickey Is Not Entitled to Consequential Damages

The MSPB determined that Mr. Hickey "did not prove that he incurred actual monetary losses or out of pocket costs" and is, therefore, "not entitled to consequential damages." Mr. Hickey asserts that he is entitled to consequential damages because it "is undisputed that for nineteen months [DHS] forced Mr. Hickey to remain in travel status for more than twelve hours per day." We agree with the MSPB.

Substantial evidence supports the MSPB's decision that Mr. Hickey is not entitled to consequential damages. To recover consequential damages, Mr. Hickey was required to provide evidence of his out-of-pocket expenses or monetary losses that resulted from his sixty-three-mile commute. See Bohac, 239 F.3d at 1343 (explaining that consequential damages are limited to "reimbursement of out-of-pocket costs" and limiting consequential damages under the WPA to out-of-pocket expenses"). Because Mr. Hickey did not present any evidence, such as gas or credit card receipts, to demonstrate his actual monetary losses or out-of-pocket expenses, Mr. Hickey has failed to demonstrate error in the MSPB's decision. See id. at 1339. We, therefore, conclude that the MSPB's consequential damages determination is supported by substantial evidence.

See MacLean v. DHS, 754 Fed. Appx. 950, 953 (Fed. Cir. 2018 NP) ("Consequential damage awards under 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii) are designed to allow for reimbursement of only actual monetary losses or out-of-pocket costs. See Bohac v. There are not many Board final decisions finding whistleblower reprisal; fewer analyze specific components of consequential damages.

With one exception, we find no error in the AJ's well-reasoned findings regarding the appellant's entitlement to some, but not all, of the damages he seeks based on his successful IRA appeal. Accordingly, but for that exception, we do not disturb the AJ's findings. The exception is the AJ's order to restore to the appellant 76 hours of annual leave he spent pursuing his appeal. Until now, the Board has not considered whether "consequential damages," as provided for in 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii) of the Whistleblower Protection Act (WPA), includes reimbursing a pro se appellant for annual leave expended in pursuing an IRA appeal. Upon review, and as set forth below, we find that the AJ's order to grant such relief was error.

To reach this conclusion, we begin by examining an earlier version of the WPA than that currently in effect. Under the WPA of 1989, a prevailing employee, in addition to the corrective action, could recover only "reasonable attorney's fees and any other reasonable costs incurred." 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)-(2) (1988). In Wiatr v. Department of the Air Force, 50 M.S.P.R. 441, 446-53 (1991), the Board refused to grant, to the successful appellant in an IRA appeal, an award of out-of-pocket litigation-related expenses as a component of attorney fees, on the basis that, under the statute, only such costs as were incurred by an attorney on the appellant's behalf were recoverable. Id. at 452. The Board subsequently overturned that specific holding in Bonggat v. Department of the Navy, 59 M.S.P.R. 175, 178-79 (1993), finding that direct, out-of-pocket expenses incurred by the appellant were instead part of the "costs" incidental to attorney representation, even though they were incurred by the appellant.

Bonggat did not, however, disturb another of the holdings in Wiatr, that the successful appellant was not entitled to restoration of annual leave he used to pursue his IRA appeal. The Board held that:

With respect to the appellant's request for restoration of his annual leave, we find that it does not concern the issue of attorney fees and is more appropriately a matter for a petition for enforcement.... To the extent, however, that the appellant is contending that he is entitled to reimbursement for annual leave he used in pursuing his appeal, as we have held here, he is not entitled to such reimbursement under 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1). See Drake v. Department of Transportation, 37 M.S.P.R. 676, 681 & n.2 (1988) (there is no legal authority that entitles a pro se appellant to compensatory time in lieu of attorney fees for annual leave used in successfully pursuing his pro se actions or to paid time not chargeable to annual or sick leave....).

The WPA was amended in 1994, Act of October 29, 1994, Pub.L. No. 103-424, § 8(b)(2), 1994 U.S.C.C.A.N. (108 Stat.) 4361, 4365. The 1994 Amendments provided additional rights and remedies for protected whistleblowers, specifically, "that the individual be placed, as nearly as possible, in the position the individual would have been in had the prohibited personnel practice not occurred; and back pay and related benefits, medical costs incurred, travel expenses, and any other reasonable and foreseeable consequential damages." 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)(A) (emphasis added).

In finding that, under this statutory provision, the appellant was entitled to reimbursement for annual leave he used in pursuit of his appeal, subject only to a reasonable standard, the AJ relied on Forrest v. Department of Agriculture, 74 M.S.P.R. 213, 224-25 (1997). There, the Board found that an appeal was not moot, despite the agency's rescission of the action, because the appellant's claim for reimbursement of annual leave he used to meet with his attorney constituted a valid claim for compensatory damages under the Civil Rights Act of 1991 (CRA). Acknowledging that compensatory damages and consequential damages are "not interchangeable," the AJ in the instant case nonetheless found them similar enough to entitle the appellant to reimbursement for the reasonable amount of annual leave he used in pursuit of his appeal.

We disagree. The CRA specifically authorized the Board to exercise broadened remedial power, consistent with the established "make whole" objective of that statute. Forrest, 74 M.S.P.R. at 224; Currier v. U.S. Postal Service, 72 M.S.P.R. 191, 196-97 (1996). The authority to award damages in this case, however, is the WPA. The Board, and our reviewing court, have concluded that Congress intended a narrow construction of "consequential damages" in the 1994 amendments to the WPA. Kinney v. Department of Agriculture, 82 M.S.P.R. 338 ¶¶ 4-10, review dismissed, 215 F.3d 1342 (Fed. Cir. 1999) (Table); Bohac v. Department of Agriculture, 239 F.3d 1334, 1342 (Fed. Cir. 2001). Moreover, while there is language in the WPA's legislative history, specifically, the Senate Report, S. Rep. No. 103-358, at 11 (1994), reprinted in 1994 U.S.C.C.A.N. 3549, 3559, to the effect that the WPA "allows the Board to order corrective action that will make, as nearly as possible, the individual 'whole,"' the court in Bohac examined that statement, finding that it was "merely a general statement about the purpose of the statute" that did not contradict the government's view that it should be interpreted narrowly. Bohac, 239 F.3d at 1343.

We conclude, therefore, that it was error for the AJ to expansively interpret "consequential damages" to include the use of annual leave by the appellant to prosecute his IRA appeal pro se. Not every loss or expense is recoverable. See, e.g., Bohac, 239 F.3d at 1339-43 (the term "consequential damages" in section 1221(g) is limited to reimbursement of out-of-pocket costs and does not include non-pecuniary damages). Where an appellant acts pro se, it is commonly understood that he is expending time in lieu of attorney fees, and there is nothing in the WPA to suggest that Congress intended for that common understanding to be overturned. Representation costs are not items of damages. See, e.g., Taxpayers for the Animas-La Plata Referendum v. Animas-La Plata Water Conservancy District, 739 F.2d 1472, 1480 (10th Cir. 1984) (expenses such as legal fees and costs are generally not considered "damages" because they are not the legitimate consequences of the tort or breach of contract sued upon); Kania v. United States, 227 Ct. Cl. 458, 650 F.2d 264, 269 (1981) (counsel fees and other litigation expenses are not the kind of consequential damages awarded in contract breach cases). Leave used by an appellant to pursue an appeal before the Board has long been regarded as voluntary and not reimbursable, being merely a claim for an award of money in lieu of attorney fees. See, e.g., Sanders v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 45 M.S.P.R. 693, 696 (1990); see also Drake, 37 M.S.P.R. at 681, n.2; Roman v. Department of the Army, 72 M.S.P.R. 409, 420 (1996) (under section 1221(g), "the appellant may not recover attorney fees for the work he himself performed in litigating this appeal"), aff'd, 129 F.3d 134 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (Table), cert. denied, 523 U.S. 1052, 118 S. Ct. 1373, 140 L. Ed.2d 521 (1998). An appellant's voluntary choice to represent himself, and to take accrued leave to do so, is simply not an out-of-pocket type of expense within the meaning of "consequential damages" as defined by our reviewing court, or under our own precedent. Under the reasoning of Bohac and Wiatr, the Board lacks the statutory authority to expansively read "consequential damages" under section 1221(g) to permit such an award.

See Sooter v. Dept. of Commerce, CH-1221-12-0588-B-1 (NP 4/1/2022) (consequential damages included lost pay (plus interest) to the time that appellant's NTE appointment would have ended).

Carson v. Dept. of Energy, 92 MSPR 440, 446-48 ¶¶ 12-15 (2002), aff'd, Carson v. Dept. of Energy, 64 Fed. Appx. 234 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP), post, an IRA case, determined both that consequential damages do not include leave without pay taken by an appellant to work on his case and that the appellant did not demonstrate that sick leave he used was tied to reprisal:

After the ID was issued, we addressed this issue for the first time and held that consequential damages do not include reimbursing an appellant for annual leave expended in pursuing an IRA appeal. Reams, 91 M.S.P.R. 447 ¶¶ 8, 14. Thus, the appellant is not entitled to restoration of any annual leave. See id.

The question of whether LWOP spent pursuing an IRA appeal is recoverable under 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii), is an issue of first impression with the Board. However, the same rationale we used in Reams for annual leave applies to LWOP. In finding that consequential damages does not include reimbursing an appellant for annual leave expended in pursuing an IRA appeal, we noted Congress's intention that consequential damages be interpreted narrowly and recent case law limiting consequential damages to reimbursement of out-of-pocket costs and excluding non-pecuniary damages. Reams, 91 M.S.P.R. 447 ¶¶ 13-14; see Bohac, 239 F.3d at 1339-43. We relied on case law finding that representation costs are not items of damages, Reams, 91 M.S.P.R. 447 ¶ 14; e.g., Taxpayers for the Animas-La Plata Referendum v. Animas-La Plata Water Conservancy District, 739 F.2d 1472, 1480 (10th Cir. 1984), and Board precedent finding that leave used by an appellant to pursue an appeal before the Board is considered voluntary and not reimbursable, Reams, 91 M.S.P.R. 447 ¶ 14; e.g., Sanders v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 45 M.S.P.R. 693, 696 (1990). These reasons for finding that annual leave taken to pursue an IRA appeal is not compensable apply equally to LWOP taken to pursue an IRA appeal. Therefore, we find that consequential damages do not include LWOP expended in pursuing an IRA appeal. The AJ thus erred in ordering the agency to reimburse the appellant for LWOP taken from January 1, 1997, to February 3, 2000.

The AJ also erred in finding that the appellant was entitled to consequential damages for the sick leave he allegedly used as a result of the agency's whistleblowing reprisal. "[M]edical costs incurred" are one of the statutory examples of consequential damages. 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii); Pastor v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 87 M.S.P.R. 609 ¶ 19 (2001). Such costs include "medical expenses which are a result of the retaliation and which can be proven with reasonable certainty." Pastor, 87 M.S.P.R. 609 ¶ 19. Below, the appellant requested restoration of 400 hours of sick leave he used since January 1997, and submitted leave and earnings statements, which document the number of hours and the dates sick leave was taken. He claimed that he "has been frequently ill as a result of the stress and effort." He further claimed that this sick leave, as well as the annual leave and LWOP he used during that same period, are a "pittance compared to the cost of time, stress, fatigue, resultant illness [he] ha[s] experienced as a consequence of the agency's cruel, calculated, and repeated, efforts to punish [him] and [his] family during this time."

However, other than these bare assertions of frequent illness, stress, and fatigue and his leave and earning statements, and despite the AJ's and the agency's notification that he has the burden of proving his claimed damages with specificity and must submit evidence establishing that his claimed damages were reasonable, foreseeable, and causally related to the agency's whistleblowing reprisal, the appellant has not submitted any evidence showing that he incurred leave as a result of the agency's whistleblowing reprisal. He has not alleged nor provided any evidence that he was ever diagnosed with a medical condition during this sick leave period, nor that he ever sought medical treatment for the alleged "stress" or "illness." Nor has he demonstrated a causal connection between the agency's whistleblowing reprisal against him in 1995 and 1997, and the hundreds of hours of sick leave he took from January 1997, through February 3, 2000. Under these circumstances, even assuming that restoration of sick leave is payable as "medical costs incurred" under 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii), the appellant has not shown that he is entitled to such relief.[9] See Pastor, 87 M.S.P.R. 609 ¶ 19.

[9] In light of this finding, we need not and do not address, here, whether sick leave would ever be compensable as "medical costs incurred," i.e., as consequential damages, under 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii).

The Federal Circuit affirmed, Carson v. Dept. of Energy, 64 Fed. Appx. 234, 240-41 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP):

We agree with the government that the Board did not err in reversing the AJ's awards of restored annual and sick leave and compensation for leave without pay. In Bohac v. Department of Agriculture, 239 F.3d 1334 (Fed. Cir. 2001), we examined the meaning of the term "consequential damages" in 5 U.S.C. § 1221 (g)(1)(A)(ii). Rejecting the argument that we should adopt that term's common law meaning, we held that "consequential damages," as used in § 1221 (g), are limited to reimbursement of out-of-pocket costs and do not include non-pecuniary damages. Id. at 1339-43. Carson's request for consequential damages for the leave that he took, however, are not out-of-pocket costs. Restored leave or monetary compensation for the leave that Carson voluntarily took from his job to pursue his appeal before the Board would effectively be compensation for Carson's own time, rather than reimbursement of an out-of-pocket expense. In addition, to the extent that he took leave due to stress and fatigue, Carson's request is akin to a request for non-pecuniary damages. That Carson has characterized his request for damages in terms of hours of leave to be restored or compensated does not change the essential nature of the damages he seeks. We thus conclude that leave voluntarily taken from work to pursue an appeal is not a reimbursable out-of-pocket expense and that Carson is therefore not entitled to consequential damages for annual leave, sick leave, and leave without pay.

Corrective action in an IRA appeal may include "back pay and related benefits, medical costs incurred, travel expenses, and any other reasonable and foreseeable consequential [damages]." 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii); see Carson v. Department of Energy, 92 M.S.P.R. 440 ¶ 8 n.5 (2002) (although the statute refers to "consequential changes," it is well settled that consequential "damages" was intended), aff'd, 64 Fed. Appx. 234 (Fed. Cir. 2003). The portion of the statute at issue in this case is generally "reasonable and foreseeable consequential [damages]." The appellant has the burden of proving her claimed damages. See Carson, 92 M.S.P.R. 440 ¶ 15. Thus, she must prove both that she incurred "consequential damages" and that the damages were "reasonable and foreseeable," i.e., causally related to the agency's reprisal against her. Id.

Our reviewing court has concluded that consequential damages under 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g) are limited to out-of-pocket costs and do not include non-pecuniary damages. Bohac v. Department of Agriculture, 239 F.3d 1334, 1343 (Fed. Cir. 2001). In reaching that conclusion, the court found that recovery of damages under the statute may not be allowed unless the government has waived sovereign immunity. It noted that such a waiver must be unequivocally expressed in statutory text and strictly construed in favor of the sovereign. Id. at 1339. Applying the interpretive rule of ejusdem generis, the court found that Congress intended a narrow construction of "consequential damages" in the 1994 amendment to the WPA. Id. at 1342. It further found that the general phrase "any other reasonable and foreseeable consequential [damages]" should be read to cover only items similar in nature to the specific items listed in the statute, i.e., back pay and related benefits, medical costs incurred, and travel expenses. It noted that those items are all actual monetary losses or out-of-pocket expenses. Id.

The appellant asserts that the AJ erred in denying her consequential damages to compensate her for the interest lost because of the delay in granting her VSIP and for the interest on her lump-sum payment for annual leave. Citing Pastor v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 87 M.S.P.R. 609 (2001), she emphasizes the WPA's remedial nature. She argues that, absent the agency's reprisal, she would have retired and received the VSIP and lump-sum payment by no later than May 31, 2002. Because the agency filed a PFR, however, it did not process her retirement until October 2003. She contends that the interest that she seeks is a reasonable and foreseeable consequence of the agency's decision. She claims that she is due $1,838.30 for interest accrued from June 1, 2002, through October 30, 2003, on her VSIP, and $1,710.16 for interest accrued from June 1, 2002, through October 30, 2003, on her lump-sum payment for annual leave.

The appellant has not shown that the AJ erred in declining to grant her consequential damages for that interest. First, she has failed to establish that consequential damages includes such interest. She has referred only to the general language of Pastor, 87 M.S.P.R. 609, to support her claim. Admittedly, Pastor emphasized the remedial and "make whole" aspect of 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)(A) by citing to the statute's legislative history. Id. ¶¶ 11, 13-15. Pastor, however, preceded Bohac. In Bohac, the court found that the legislative history cited in Pastor was "merely a general statement about the purpose of the statute." Bohac, 239 F.3d at 1343. Indeed, the Board subsequently recognized that Congress intended a narrow construction of "consequential damages" in the 1994 amendments to the WPA. See, e.g., Reams, 91 M.S.P.R. 447, ¶ 13. In Reams, the Board found that the AJ erred in "expansively interpret[ing] 'consequential damages.'" Id. ¶ 14.

Moreover, the appellant's claim for interest as consequential damages implicates the "no interest rule." In Krape v. Department of Defense, 97 M.S.P.R. 430 ¶ 9 (2004), the Board quoted Library of Congress v. Shaw, 478 U.S. 310, 311 (1986), in noting that "interest cannot be recovered in a suit against the Government in the absence of an express waiver of sovereign immunity from an award of interest." The Board further cited the explanation of Shaw in Chiu v. United States, 948 F.2d 711, 719 (Fed. Cir. 1991). There, the court found that Shaw set forth two principles. First, it rejected any distinction between types of awards for purposes of the no-interest rule so long as the award compensated for the time value of money. Second, it found that no award in the nature of interest against the United States is permitted unless expressly and unambiguously authorized by statute. Krape, 97 M.S.P.R. 430 ¶ 9.

Here, 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii) does not contain an express and unambiguous authorization for awarding interest as consequential damages. As previously noted, consequential damages cover only items similar to the specific items listed in the statute, i.e., back pay and related benefits, medical costs incurred, and travel expenses. Bohac, 239 F.3d at 1342. Those items do not include interest or anything that could be interpreted as compensation for the time value of money. Thus, we conclude that consequential damages do not include the interest that the appellant seeks.

Second, even if consequential damages could be interpreted to include that interest, the appellant has not shown that the interest was a reasonable and foreseeable consequence of the agency's 1998 reprisal against her. An agency may not offer early retirement under VERA to an employee who has been removed for misconduct. See 5 U.S.C. § 8336(d)(2); 5 C.F.R. § 831.114. Even though the appellant had received an ID reversing her removal for misconduct, that ID did not become final until the Board's July 2003 Order. Thus, the agency's decision not to offer her VERA and VSIP in May 2002 was in accordance with the statute and regulation and not in reprisal for her protected whistleblowing. Because the appellant has failed to show agency reprisal in declining to process her VERA and VSIP application in May 2002, she has consequently failed to show that she is entitled to consequential damages for the interest she allegedly lost because of the delay in processing and the related interest on her lump-sum payment for annual leave. Cf. Williams v. Department of the Army, 97 M.S.P.R. 246 ¶¶ 11-14 (2004) (upholding the agency's decision not to pay interest on William's back-pay award absent an administrative decision finding that he was affected by an unjustified or unwarranted personnel action).

The appellant next asserts that the AJ erred in not awarding her consequential damages for the increased tax liability she incurred when she received both a lump-sum back pay award and a lump-sum CSRS annuity benefits award in 2003. She reiterates the emphasis in Pastor, 87 M.S.P.R. 609, on the WPA's remedial nature. The appellant contends that the AJ erred in finding that she submitted insufficient evidence to substantiate her claim, and that she is entitled to $24,388.01. In that regard, she argues that prior Board decisions denying payment for tax consequences did not involve consequential damages under the WPA.

The appellant has failed to show that the AJ erred in declining to grant consequential damages for her increased tax liability. As discussed above, the general statement in Pastor does not provide a basis for a specific consequential damages award. Further, it is unnecessary to address the sufficiency of the appellant's evidence because she has not shown that her increased tax liability should be considered consequential damages. Specifically, she has provided no support for finding that the statute expressly waives the federal government's sovereign immunity for such an award or that it is similar to the specific items the statute identifies as recoverable consequential damages. In that regard, she has not explained why the Board should treat her argument concerning increased tax liability resulting from receipt of her awards any differently from the way it treats such arguments in cases not involving the WPA where the appellants have been awarded lump-sum back pay because of unwarranted personnel actions. The Board has found that it lacks authority to order any remedy for the tax consequences of a back-pay award. See, e.g., Holtgrewe v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 65 M.S.P.R. 137, 140 (1994). Moreover, the appellant again has not shown that her increased tax burden in 2003 was a reasonable and foreseeable consequence of her 1998 improper removal.

She asserts that she requested $2,248.24 for interest accrued from June 1, 2002, through October 30, 2003. She cites Cooper v. Here, the appellant's claim to interest is based on the same general argument considered and rejected above, that is, that the agency acted in reprisal against her by not processing her retirement application until October 2003. The appellant contends that the AJ also did not address her claim for "denial of pay for sick leave that [she] lost by reason of the agency's not restoring same during the interim relief period, which caused [her] to accrue LWOP during interim relief illnesses, and, that subsequently was forfeited upon OPM's processing of [her] retirement application as same was not in 'full months.'" Again, the appellant has not shown that she is entitled to consequential damages for the alleged denial of 55 hours of sick leave. Therefore, any adjudicatory error in failing to address her claim provides no basis for reversing the ID. See Panter, 22 M.S.P.R. at 282. In any event, the present case involves the appellant's possible entitlement to consequential damages, not to interim relief. Granted, the Board has allowed for the possibility that sick leave may be considered compensable as "medical costs incurred" under 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii). See Carson, 92 M.S.P.R. 440 ¶¶ 14-15. Rather, the appellant did not return to work after May 31, 2002, because she believed that she was entitled to early retirement. As found above, the appellant has failed to show that the agency's decision not to grant early retirement at that time was reprisal for her whistleblowing.

The agency has filed a cross-PFR contesting the AJ's award of the $12,291.07 for interest the appellant paid until September 10, 2003, on loans for money that she had borrowed between 1998 and 2002 ($4,839.02); interest she lost on funds she had to spend from inheritances she received in 2000 ($6,749.43); interest she lost on an IRA she opened in 2001 that she had to withdraw early ($452.62); and the IRA early withdrawal penalty ($250). The agency asserts that the award is not substantiated by the record. For the reasons discussed above, we find that the appellant has not shown that the interest and penalty should be considered consequential damages. Further, she has not shown that these items were reasonable and foreseeable consequences of the agency's reprisal. Thus, despite any agency failure to contest those amounts below, the Board may not award them. See, e.g., Carson, 92 M.S.P.R. 440 ¶ 11 (addressing, on the Board's own motion, the issue of whether consequential damages include the appellant's use of annual leave, sick leave, and LWOP because "payments of money from the public fisc 'are limited to those authorized by statute,' and we cannot grant the appellant a money remedy that Congress has not authorized.") Accordingly, we find that the AJ erred in awarding the $12,291.07 as consequential damages.

Pastor v. VA, 87 MSPR 609, 614-17 ¶¶ 12-19 (2001), following remand, Pastor v. VA, 94 MSPR 353 (2003), post, an IRA case, determined that consequential damages awards may include future medical expenses:

The question of whether future medical costs are recoverable under section 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii) is an issue of first impression with the Board. The administrative judge's interpretation of the phrase "medical costs incurred" is not an unreasonable one. However, as explained below, we find that the interpretation which most comports with congressional intent is one which allows an appellant to claim future medical costs resulting from the retaliatory personnel action.

The WPA is remedial legislation, intended to expand and improve protections afforded federal employees who are whistleblowers. See Massimino v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 58 MSPR 318, 324 (1993); Mulherin v. Department of the Air Force, 45 MSPR 289, 291 (1990). In general, remedial statutes are to be liberally construed. See Sutherland Stat. Const. § 60.01 (5th ed. 1992). Further, the whole of a statute should be considered in ascertaining its meaning, Sterling Federal Systems, Inc. v. Goldin, 16 F.3d 1177, 1185 (Fed. Cir. 1994), and the provisions of a unified statutory scheme should be read in harmony, leaving no provision inoperative or superfluous or redundant or contradictory, Holley v. United States, 124 F.3d 1462, 1468 (Fed. Cir. 1997).

Section 1221(g)(1)(A)(i) provides that a person who has been the subject of retaliation for whistleblowing activity "be placed, as nearly as possible, in the position the individual would have been in had the prohibited personnel practice not occurred." The legislative history cited by the administrative judge reflects Congress' intent that an appellant who prevails in an IRA appeal be made whole, as nearly as possible, and recover the losses suffered as a result of the retaliatory conduct. Where a statute's stated purpose is to make an individual whole for losses suffered on account of a prohibited practice and to place her, as nearly as possible, in the position she would have been in but for the illegal action, the tribunal should fashion "the most complete relief possible" within the terms of the statute. Albemarle Paper Co. v. Moody, 422 U.S. 405, 421, 95 S. Ct. 2362, 2373 (1975).

To make an appellant whole, section 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii) authorizes the Board to award medical costs "incurred" as part of consequential damages. If, as the administrative judge found, that term is construed to include only medical expenses that have been incurred as of the date of the closing of the record in the consequential damages phase of a Board proceeding, an appellant's ability to claim her losses will be arbitrarily cut short depending on how quickly her case is adjudicated. Such a result would not comport with the make-whole provision of section 1221(g)(1)(A)(i), which contemplates that the agency will be required to pay for medical treatment to the extent necessary to alleviate, as much as possible, the results of its retaliatory acts. As explained below, such treatment includes future medical expenses.

Congress is presumed to know the law. Cannon v. University of Chicago, 441 U.S. 677, 696-98, 99 S. Ct. 1946, 1957-1958 (1979). Where, as here, Congress uses a legal term, a tribunal should interpret that term in its "familiar legal sense," Henry v. United States, 251 U.S. 393, 395, 40 S. Ct. 185, 186 (1920), unless Congress has provided a "contrary direction," Morissette v. United States, 342 U.S. 246, 263, 72 S. Ct. 240, 250 (1952); see also Florida National Guard v. Federal Labor Relations Authority, 699 F.2d 1082, 1087 (11th Cir.), cert. denied, 464 U.S. 1007 (1983) ("Congress is deemed to know the...judicial gloss given to certain language and thus adopts the existing interpretation unless it affirmatively acts to change the meaning."). For example, in determining the meaning of "punitive damages" in the Federal Tort Claims Act, the Supreme Court looked to the nature of that term as it is commonly applied in the law and adopted that usage, having found no reason to assume that Congress intended any other meaning. Molzof v. United States, 502 U.S. 301, 306-07, 112 S. Ct. 711, 715-16 (1992).

Courts have found that consequential damages include various forms of future losses or expenses. See, e.g., Rice v. Community Health Association, 203 F.3d 283, 288-89 (4th Cir. 2000) (loss of future identifiable professional opportunities); United States v. Merritt Meridian Construction Corporation, 95 F.3d 153, 161 (2d Cir. 1996) (loss of future business opportunities); Toltec Fabrics, Inc. v. August Inc., 29 F.3d 778, 780-81 (2d Cir. 1994) (loss of goodwill and customers in the future); Hendricks & Associates, Inc. v. Daewoo Corporation, 923 F.2d 209, 217 (1st Cir. 1991) (loss of prospective profits); Swearingen v. Long, 889 F. Supp 587, 590 (N.D.N.Y. 1995) (future expenses for medical monitoring). However, such future costs cannot be based on conjecture, surmise, or speculation, but must be proven with reasonable certainty. See Rice, 203 F.3d at 289; Merritt Meridian Construction Corporation, 95 F.3d at 161; Hendricks & Associates, 923 F.2d at 217; State Office Systems, Inc. v. Olivetti Corporation of America, 762 F.2d 843, 846 (10th Cir. 1985). We therefore find that the term "consequential damages" encompasses future provable losses or expenses. Moreover, we discern no hint of a contrary intention either in the 1994 Act or its legislative history. Indeed, as pointed out above, the statute and its legislative history suggest that Congress intended to make prevailing appellants in IRA appeals as whole as possible.

A statute is ambiguous if it is susceptible of differing, reasonable interpretations. De Jesus v. Office of Personnel Management, 63 MSPR 586, 592 (1994), aff'd, 62 F.3d 1431 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table). The term "medical costs incurred" in 5 USC § 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii) is ambiguous in that it could reasonably be interpreted, as the administrative judge did, to mean only medical costs that have already been incurred, or it could be read to also include future provable medical expenses. Where there is a silence or an ambiguity in a statute, the agency responsible for applying it--in this case the Board--must determine the proper interpretation. See Santella v. Special Counsel, 86 MSPR 48 ¶ 14 (2000).

In construing the term "medical costs incurred" we have considered that the WPA is remedial legislation which should be liberally interpreted. We have recognized that the 1994 Act, read as a whole, authorizes the Board to award relief that places the appellant, as nearly as possible, in the position she would have been in had not the agency retaliated against her. We have taken into account that the appellant might not be made whole if she were not reimbursed for provable future medical costs resulting from the agency's prohibited acts. We also have noted that the term "consequential damages," of which "medical costs incurred" is one statutory example, has been judicially interpreted to include various forms of future losses or expenses. Finally, neither the legislative history nor the statute indicates any intent by Congress to limit medical costs only to those incurred before the record closes in the Board proceeding. Indeed, as discussed above, if that were the case, an appellant's award of medical costs would depend on the arbitrariness of that closing date, irrespective of whether she has been placed, as near as possible, in the position in which she would have been had the prohibited personnel practice not occurred. Taking all of these factors into account, we find that the term "medical costs incurred" can include future medical expenses which are a result of the retaliation and which can be proven with reasonable certainty.

"Consequential damages" under 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii) must be narrowly construed.

As the prevailing party in a Board appeal in which the administrative judge ordered corrective action based upon the finding of whistleblower reprisal, the appellant is entitled to an award of "back pay and related benefits, medical costs incurred, travel expenses, and any other reasonable and foreseeable consequential [damages]." 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii).[3] The Federal Circuit has concluded that consequential damages under 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g) are limited to out-of-pocket costs and do not include nonpecuniary damages. Bohac, 239 F.3d at 1343. In reaching this conclusion, the court found that the recovery of damages under the statute may not be allowed unless the government has waived sovereign immunity. Id. at 1339; Johnston v. Department of the Treasury, 100 M.S.P.R. 78, ¶ 14 (2005). It further noted that such a waiver must be expressed unequivocally in statutory text and strictly construed in favor of the sovereign. Bohac, 239 F.3d at 1339; Johnston, 100 M.S.P.R. 78, ¶ 14. Applying the interpretive rule of ejusdem generis,[4] the court also found that Congress intended a narrow construction of "consequential damages" in the 1994 amendments to 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g).[5] Bohac, 239 F.3d at 1342. It further found that the general phrase "any other reasonable and foreseeable consequential [damages]" should be read to cover only items similar in nature to the specific items listed in the statute, i.e., back pay and related benefits, medical costs incurred, and travel expenses. Id. It noted that those items are all actual monetary losses or out-of-pocket expenses. Id. Applying Bohac, the Board has explained that, to receive a consequential damages award, an appellant must prove that she incurred consequential damages and that her claimed damages were reasonable, foreseeable, and causally related to the agency's prohibited personnel practice. See Johnston, 100 M.S.P.R. 78, ¶ 13.

As we explained in King, the appellant is not entitled to a compensatory damages award because her appeal was pending at the time of the WPEA's enactment. See King, 119 M.S.P.R. 663, ¶ 18.

The compromised loan amount is not recoverable as a consequential damage under 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii).

Upon reviewing the agency's challenge to the administrative judge's award of $64,949.00, we begin with the proposition articulated in Bohac that "Congress intended a narrow construction of 'consequential damages.'" Bohac, 239 F.3d at 1342. Applying the rule of ejusdem generis, the court held that an award of consequential damages must be "of the same kind" as those specifically articulated in the statute: lost back pay and benefits, medical costs, or travel expenses. Id. Subsequent to Bohac, the Board has declined to restore either annual leave used, or leave without pay (LWOP) incurred, to pursue a Board appeal as part of a consequential damages award. See Carson, 92 M.S.P.R. 440, ¶ 13 (declining to award LWOP); Reams v. Department of the Treasury, 91 M.S.P.R. 447, ¶¶ 13-14 (2002) (declining to restore annual leave). It also has found that awards of interest and compensation for an increased tax liability are beyond the scope of recoverable consequential damages. See Johnston, 100 M.S.P.R. 78, ¶¶ 18, 21.

In keeping with the Board's historical approach toward awards of consequential damages, we cannot conclude that the compromised loan amount resulting from the short sale of the appellant's home is recoverable. First, the compromised loan amount is not similar in nature to the other types of consequential damages specified in the statute, i.e., lost back pay, medical costs, or travel expenses. See id., ¶ 14 (any other reasonable and foreseeable consequential damages should be read to cover only items similar in nature to those listed in the statute). The compromised loan amount, rather, is more like those categories of damages which the Board has declined to award under section 1221(g). See, e.g., id., ¶¶ 18, 21 (declining to award a lump-sum interest payment on restored leave and additional compensation for increased tax liability); Reams, 91 M.S.P.R. 447, ¶ 14 (reversing an award of annual leave used to prosecute a Board appeal). Applying the doctrine of ejusdem generis, we find that section 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii)'s "any other reasonable and foreseeable consequential [damages]" language does not provide for the recovery of the compromised loan amount following the short sale of the appellant's house.

Additionally, we find that the compromised loan amount is not a reasonable and foreseeable consequential damage which is causally related to the agency's reprisal. See Johnston, 100 M.S.P.R. 78, ¶ 13. The appellant testified that, among other things, upon her reduction in grade and pay, she could not cover her living expenses and felt compelled to seek employment elsewhere. She applied, and was selected, for a higher-paying position with the agency in Los Angeles, California, and thereafter elected to sell her home in Texas at a loss through a short sale. However, we find that, in between the agency's reprisal and the appellant's decision to sell her house and move to California, there were several intervening events which break the causal link needed to establish an entitlement to a consequential damages award. See Johnston, 100 M.S.P.R. 78, ¶ 13. For example, the appellant could have applied for and received a position closer to where she resided in Texas (either with the agency or another employer), thus eliminating the need for her to move or sell her house. Alternatively, she could have accepted the position in California, but kept her house,[6] or she might not have had to sell her house at a loss had she negotiated a different price or had the housing market been stronger. Cf. Restatement (Second) of Torts § 433(b) (1965) (relevant considerations when considering whether an actor's conduct is a substantial factor in bringing about harm to another include "whether the actor's conduct has created a force or series of forces which are in continuous and active operation up until the time of the harm").[7] As illustrated above, we cannot conclude that the agency's prohibited personnel practice created a "series of forces which [were] in continuous and active operation" through the appellant's decision to sell her home at a loss and enter into a compromise with the VA. See Restatement (Second) of Torts § 440 (a superseding cause is that of a third person or other force which by its intervention prevents the actor from being liable for harm to another).

We find it within the range of possibility that the appellant could have rented her house in Texas instead of selling it at a loss.

We further agree with the agency's argument on review that there is no evidence in the record that the appellant has experienced an actual, compensable monetary loss or out-of-pocket expense.[8] See Bohac, 239 F.3d at 1342 (consequential damages are limited to actual monetary losses or out-of-pocket expenses). As explained above, upon the sale of the appellant's house, she entered into a compromise with the VA for the outstanding amount of her loan, which does not require that she repay the VA unless and until she wishes to be eligible for another VA loan. The appellant, therefore, can present no evidence that she has incurred an actual monetary loss, all she has lost is the eligibility to apply for a VA loan, and we agree with the agency that the loss generated upon the sale of the appellant's house has been sustained by the VA and not by her. The appellant's ineligibility to secure another VA loan, moreover, is best categorized as a nonpecuniary consequence which falls outside of the scope of recovery under section 1221(g). See Bohac, 239 F.3d at 1342. Similarly, we find that the reference to the loan compromise on the appellant's credit history is also nonpecuniary in nature and cannot justify an award of consequential damages.[9]

[8] The fact that the appellant has not personally sustained an actual monetary loss or out-of-pocket expense only adds support to our decision, and had the appellant personally sustained an actual monetary loss on the sale of her house, we would reach the same conclusion that the loss is not within the scope of recoverable consequential damages under 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii).

[9] We note, for example, that, even if we were to award the appellant this sum, there is no guarantee that she would repay the VA this amount, in which case the reference to the compromised loan would remain on her credit history. Additionally, even if the appellant were to repay the VA this amount, it is not clear that her credit history would be expunged.

Finally, we note that the remedial purpose of the consequential damages provision of section 1221(g) does not warrant a different result. See 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)(A)(i) ("corrective action may include...that the individual be placed, as nearly as possible, in the position the individual would have been in had the prohibited personnel practice not occurred"); Bohac, 239 F.3d at 1343 (discussing the legislative history of the 1994 amendments to section 1221(g)). In Bohac, the Federal Circuit found that the statute's legislative history could not support a reading that Congress intended to allow prevailing parties to recover nonpecuniary losses in consequential damages awards. See Bohac, 239 F.3d at 1343. Although the Board had previously cited the statute's "make-whole" language and its legislative history to support an expansive reading of the scope of recovery under section 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii), see Pastor v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 87 M.S.P.R. 609, ¶¶ 14-15 (2001) (finding that an award of future medical costs may be awarded under section 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii)), subsequent to Bohac, the Board has eschewed reliance on the broad make-whole language of the statute, instead recognizing that Congress intended a narrow construction of the consequential damages provision, see Johnston, 100 M.S.P.R. 78, ¶ 16. In light of the foregoing, we conclude that the appellant is not entitled to a consequential damages award covering the compromised loan amount. Accordingly, the administrative judge's award of $64,949.00 in consequential damages is REVERSED.

The administrative judge's remaining consequential damages award is AFFIRMED.

The agency has not challenged the administrative judge's consequential damages award of $3,871.20 in moving expenses, $2,418.02 in travel costs, and 280 restored sick leave hours. Upon our review of the record, we find no reason to differ with the administrative judge's monetary award for travel and moving costs, which we accordingly AFFIRM. Additionally, although the Board has previously withheld deciding whether sick leave used as a result of the agency's whistleblower reprisal would be compensable under 5 U.S.C. § 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii), see Carson, 92 M.S.P.R. 440, ¶ 15 n.9, here, where there is unrefuted evidence in the record demonstrating the appellant's increased use of sick leave following the agency's prohibited personnel practice, along with supporting medical documentation, see HCD; DF, Tabs 46-47 (the appellant's medical documentation and leave history), we find that sick leave used as a result of the agency's whistleblower reprisal is a recoverable consequential damage as a medical cost incurred under section 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii). The administrative judge's award of 280 restored sick leave hours is AFFIRMED.

Dept. of Army, 72 MSPR 409, 412-15 (1996), involving an IRA appeal by a pro se whistleblower who demonstrated that the agency's failure to promote him constituted whistleblowing reprisal, determined that the 1994 amendments were not retroactive. While discussing retroactivity, the Board considered the scope of the consequential damages provision:

Section 3(a) of the Whistleblower Protection Act of 1989 (WPA) states that if an appellant is the prevailing party before the Board based on a finding of a prohibited personnel practice, "the agency involved shall be liable to [him] for reasonable attorney's fees and any other reasonable costs incurred." Pub. L. No. 101-12, 3(a), 103 Stat. 16, 30 (1989). This provision of the WPA was codified at 5 USC 1221(g)(1). In 1994, Congress amended section 1221(g) by providing, inter alia, that if the Board orders corrective action in an IRA appeal, "such corrective action may include back pay and related benefits, medical costs incurred, travel expenses, and any other reasonable and foreseeable consequential changes." See Act of October 29, 1994 (1994 Act), Pub. L. No. 103-424, 8(b)(2), 108 Stat. 4361, 4365. The Board has found that although this amendment, which is codified at 5 USC 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii), "refers to consequential 'changes,' it is apparent that consequential 'damages' were intended." Hoever v. Department of the Navy, 70 MSPR 386, 388 n.2 (1996). Thus, the appellant properly based his claim for consequential damages on 5 USC 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii).

With respect to the 1994 Act, Congress provided that "[t]he provisions of this Act and the amendments made by this Act shall be effective on and after the date of the enactment of this Act," October 29, 1994. Pub. L. No. 103-424, 14, 103 Stat. at 4368. Corrective action in this appeal was ordered with respect to the agency's November 18, 1990 decision not to promote the appellant to a GM-14 position. Because the personnel action took place nearly four years before the enactment of the 1994 Act, the appellant would not be entitled to consequential damages if 5 USC 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii) does not apply retroactively, assuming that he properly filed his request for such damages with the administrative judge....

Neither party argues on petition for review that the administrative judge erred in giving retroactive effect to section 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii). However, we reopen the appeal on our own motion to discuss this issue because payments of money from the public fisc "are limited to those authorized by statute," OPM v. Richmond, 496 U.S. 414, 416, 110 S. Ct. 2465, 2467, 110 L. Ed.2d 387 (1990), and we cannot grant the appellant a money remedy that Congress has not authorized, see Benjamin v. OPM, 49 MSPR 324, 329 (1991). For the reasons set forth below, we find that 5 USC 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii) does not apply retroactively to the appellant's claim for consequential damages.

In Landgraf v. USI Film Products, 511 U.S. 244, 114 S. Ct. 1483, 128 L. Ed.2d 229 (1994), the Supreme Court of the United States stated that, when a case implicates a Federal statute enacted after the events in suit, a tribunal must first determine whether Congress has expressly prescribed the statute's temporal reach. See 511 U.S. at 280, 114 S. Ct. at 1505. If the tribunal finds that Congress has not done so, it must determine whether the statute should have retroactive effect based on a determination of whether retroactivity would "impair rights a party possessed when he acted, increase a party's liability for past conduct, or impose new duties with respect to transactions already completed." Id. The Court noted that only prospectivity is presumed absent clear congressional intent to the contrary. See id. at 273, 279, 114 S. Ct. at 1501, 1505.

The Court applied the "traditional" presumption against giving a statute retroactive effect to find that certain provisions of the Civil Rights Act of 1991, Pub. L. No. 102-166, 105 Stat. 1071 (1991 Act), did not apply to conduct that occurred before the enactment of that statute. See 511 U.S. at 278, 286, 114 S. Ct. at 1504, 1508. Among the provisions that did not apply retroactively was one authorizing the recovery of compensatory damages for intentional discrimination violative of Title VII. See id. at 282, 114 S. Ct. at 1506. The Court held that the compensatory damages provision did not apply retroactively because it attached "an important new legal burden" to conduct that took place before the enactment of the 1991 Act, and it conferred a "new right to monetary relief" under Title VII. See id. at 283, 114 S. Ct. at 1506. Acknowledging that retroactive application of the compensatory damages provision might vindicate the purpose of the statute more fully, the Court nonetheless found that this consideration was insufficient to rebut the presumption against retroactivity, which "is founded upon sound considerations of general policy and practice, and accords with long held and widely shared expectations about the usual operation of legislation." Id. at 286, 114 S. Ct. at 1507-08.

With respect to the retroactivity of the 1994 Act, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit has stated that there is nothing in the statute or the legislative history indicating that it should be applied retroactively. See Caddell v. Department of Justice, 96 F.3d 1367, 1371 (1996). Hence, the Court held that the traditional presumption against giving a statute retroactive effect should be used when determining whether a particular provision of the 1994 Act applies in an appeal, such as this one, involving conduct that occurred before October 29, 1994. See id. at 1371.

Applying the principles set forth in Landgraf, the Court found that the 1994 Act's classification of a decision to order psychiatric testing or examination as a "personnel action" does not apply retroactively because that provision "clearly imposes new duties on government officials wishing to utilize fitness-for-duty examinations, and arguably...could increase a government official's liability for past conduct." Id. at 1371. Moreover, the Court concluded that the amendment did not fall within any of the exceptions to the presumption against retroactivity set forth in Landgraf. See id. at 1371....

In this appeal, we find that the consequential damages provision of the 1994 Act, 5 USC 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii), attached a new legal burden to conduct that took place before its enactment and conferred a new right to monetary relief under the WPA. Further, the amendment does not fall within any of the exceptions to the presumption against retroactivity....

Consequential damages are unavailable to remedy problems relating to the implementation of interim relief. See O'Regan v. VA, 74 MSPR 134, 142 (1997) (IRA case challenging failure to secure reassignment and promotion: Board lacks jurisdiction to consider claims of reprisal resulting from agency's treatment of the appellant in interim relief).

Before the 2012 Whistleblower Protection Enhancement Act amendments, compensatory damages were not statutorily authorized in whistleblower reprisal cases. A few decisions of the Board and the Federal Circuit addressed claims for damages for emotional distress under the statutes in effect prior to 2012. Kinney v. USDA, 82 MSPR 338, 339-42 ¶¶ 1-10 (1999), an IRA case, determined that damages for mental or emotional suffering were not authorized by 5 USC 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii) (before the 2012 WPEA):

This case is before the Board on the administrative judge's certification of an interlocutory appeal of his ruling that the Board lacks authority to award nonpecuniary damages for mental distress under the consequential damages provisions of the Whistleblower Protection Act. As detailed below, we find that the administrative judge's ruling is correct.

BACKGROUND

The appellant prevailed in her appeal which contended that she had been coerced into seeking a geographic reassignment because of acts taken in reprisal for whistleblowing activities. Kinney v. Department of Agriculture, MSPB Docket No. SE-1221-96-0569-W-1 (Initial Decision, May 6, 1998). The initial decision became final and the appellant moved for consequential damages, based in significant part on emotional distress. After requesting briefs on the issue, the administrative judge held that the Board lacks authority to award nonpecuniary damages for mental distress under the consequential damages provision of the Whistleblower Protection Act, 5 USC § 1221(g)(1)(A).

ANALYSIS

Section 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii) of Title 5 of the United States Code authorizes the Board, in cases where it orders corrective action for actions taken in reprisal for whistleblowing, to award "back pay and related benefits, medical costs incurred, travel expenses, and any other reasonable and foreseeable consequential changes." Since the Board's final decision on the merits granted the appellant corrective action, reversing her reassignment after finding that she was coerced into requesting it by acts taken in reprisal for whistleblowing in violation of 5 USC § 2302 (b)(8), there is no question that this provision for consequential damages applies to her. The question, however, is whether the statute authorizes the Board to award monetary damages for nonpecuniary harm or whether any award is limited to out-of-pocket losses. As explained below, we find that the statute does not consent to governmental liability for damages based on emotional distress, i.e., for pain and suffering.

Section 1221(g)(1)(A) authorizes the Board's corrective action orders in covered appeals to include prospective relief in subsection (i) and retrospective, monetary relief in subsection (ii). The appellant's claim for damages relies on the concluding phrase of the latter section, "and any other reasonable and foreseeable consequential [damages]." This general language follows a list of specific authorized forms of relief, "back pay and related benefits, medical costs incurred, [and] travel expenses." The rule of ejusdem generis (of the same kind), an established principle of statutory construction, requires that a general statutory term be understood in light of the specific terms that surround it. Hughey v. United States, 495 U.S. 411, 419 (1990). The rule mandates that a catchall phrase such as that relied on by the appellant be read as applying only to categories similar to those specifically enumerated. Federal Maritime Commission v. Seatrain Lines, Inc., 411 U.S. 726, 734 (1973).

The justification for ejusdem generis is that, if the general language were literally interpreted to have the widest possible application, the specific examples would be superfluous. The rule avoids the redundancy of the specific examples by giving them the function of defining the limits of the general language. It recognizes that the purpose of describing a class by examples and a general catchall phrase is to indicate how extensively a provision is intended to be applied. 2A Sutherland Statutory Construction, §§ 47.17-47.18 (N. Singer, 5th ed. 1992). See United States v. Thomas, 930 F.2d 526, 529 (7th Cir. 1991). Here, where the statute authorizes the Board to order the agency to reimburse the appellant for specific out of pocket losses and expenses resulting from the unjustified action, the rule dictates that the phrase, "and other reasonable and foreseeable consequential damages," be read only to authorize reimbursement for other resulting monetary losses. The language cannot be read to extend the award of consequential damages to include nonpecuniary injuries such as emotional or mental distress.

We recognize that the restrictive interpretation of statutory language called for by the principle of ejusdem generis does not apply where there is a clear manifestation of a contrary intent. Sutherland, supra, § 47.22. No such intent can be found elsewhere in the statute or in the sparse legislative history of section 1221 (g)(1)(A)(ii) and its parallel section, 1214(g)(1), however.

A single sentence in the Senate Report accompanying the 1994 Act addresses the two provisions expanding the authority to award attorney fees and adding the authority to award back pay, medical costs, travel expenses, and other consequential damages: "Section 8 allows the Board to order corrective action that will make, as nearly as possible, the individual whole." S. Rep. No. 103-358, at 11 (1994), reprinted in 1994 USCCAN 3549, 3559. Although this language could be read in an expansive manner, the other reference to these provisions in the legislative history strongly leads in the opposite direction. The House bill, H.R. 2970, as introduced in the House on August 6, 1993, as voted out of the Subcommittee on Civil Service on October 20, 1993, as voted out of the Committee on Post Office and Civil Service on August 10, 1994, and as described in the House report, H. Rep. No. 103-769, 103d Cong., 2d Sess. (1994), contained provisions related to the recovery of "fees for legal representation" but made no reference to damages. Prior to the House vote on the amended Senate version adding these sections, the bill's sponsor stated with reference to them that "[t]he expanded provisions for consequential damages and attorney fees are intended to provide a realistic expectation that employees who prevail will recover their costs." 140 Cong. Rec. H11421 (daily ed., Oct. 7, 1994) (statement of Rep. McCloskey). Nonpecuniary damages, such as pain and suffering or emotional distress, are not ordinarily viewed as "costs." Read together, these two brief references in the legislative history suggest that the purpose of the statute is to make a prevailing appellant financially whole. Most assuredly, they provide no clear manifestation of a legislative intent to provide compensation for nonpecuniary losses which would require the ejusdem generis rule to be disregarded.

Further, the absence of any reference in the legislative history to an intent to authorize damages for pain and suffering is in marked contrast to the history of the Civil Rights Act of 1991, which added 42 USC § 1981a providing for awards of compensatory damages to employees prevailing on claims of discrimination. The history of that provision expressly states that damages for emotional or mental distress were authorized, and the issue of whether excessive damage awards might result from enactment was debated in the committee reports. See H.R. Rep. No. 102-40(I), at 14-15, 64-65, 70-74, 108 (1991), reprinted in 1991 USCCAN at 552-53, 602-03, 608-10, 646; H.R. Rep. No. 102-40(11), at 24-29, 42-43, 70-71 (1991), reprinted in 1991 USCCAN at 717-22, 736-37, 756-57. Moreover, section 1981a(b)(3) places a ceiling on the amount of damages that can be awarded. The omission of such a provision from the 1994 Act and the lack of expressions of concern in the history about the potential cost impact of the consequential damages provisions are consistent with the narrow reading required by the ejusdem generis rule.

Finally, we note that the consequential damages provisions are waivers of sovereign immunity. The scope of provisions which consent to governmental liability must be interpreted narrowly in favor of the sovereign, Lane v. Pena, 518 U.S. 187 (1996), and should not be enlarged beyond what the statutory language requires. United States v. Nordic Village, Inc., 503 U.S. 30, 34 (1992).

For these reasons, we conclude, as the administrative judge did, that damages for mental or emotional suffering are not authorized by section 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii).

The exclusion of damages for emotional distress for whistleblowing reprisal was confirmed by the Federal Circuit in Bohac v. USDA, 239 F.3d 1334, 1338-43 (Fed. Cir. 2001), involving an adverse action appeal from a removal decided before the 2012 statutory changes:

At the outset we are confronted by the inconvenient fact that the WPA does not in fact provide for the recovery of consequential "damages," although both parties urge us to treat it as though it did. Rather, as enacted, and as it appears in the Statutes at Large and in the U.S. Code, the provision, 5 USC § 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii), allows recovery for "consequential changes." The reference to "changes" is obviously a mistake.[3] It is well-established that an appellate court may interpret statutes to correct such obvious mistakes. See United States Nat'l Bank of Or. v. lndep. Ins. Agents of Am., 508 U.S. 439, 462 (1993) (disregarding a scrivener's error on punctuation because it was against the "overwhelming evidence from the structure, language, and subject matter of the...Act"); United States v. Colon-Ortiz, 866 F.2d 6, 10 (1st Cir.) (finding that extra language was "an inadvertent drafting error, and should be stricken from the statute," and that "in legislative (as in judicial) affairs, allowance must be made for human error and inadvertence"), cert. denied, 490 U.S. 1051 (1989); 2A Norman J. Singer, Statutes and Statutory Construction § 46.06, at 192-93 (6th ed. 2000). This is such a situation where it is appropriate for us to interpret the statute by disregarding an obvious mistake in the text of the statute.

[3] The Board has consistently agreed, holding that although the "statutory provision refers to consequential 'changes'...it is apparent that consequential 'damages' were intended." Hoever v. Dep't of the Navy, 70 MSPR 386, 388 n.2 (1996); see also Kinney v. Dep't of Agric., 82 MSPR 338, 339-40, appeal dismissed, 215 F.3d 1342 (Fed. Cir. 1999) (unpublished disposition); Hasler v. Dep't of the Air Force, 79 MSPR 415, 422 n.2 (1998) (Vice Chair Slavet, dissenting).

The question, however, becomes: what was the mistake? The parties urge that "changes" in section 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii) was intended to be "damages." However, it is also possible that "changes" was intended to be "charges." On balance, we think that the parties' reading is correct. Section 1214(g) of title 5, the counterpart provision of section 1221(g) for actions brought by the OSC rather than as an individual right of action, provides for recovery of "consequential damages." These two provisions were enacted at the same time in the 1994 Amendment, and contain nearly identical language. Pub. L. 103-424, §§ 8(a) and (b)(2), 108 Stat. 4361, 4364-65. The inference is strong that Congress intended the same scope of recovery under section 1221(g) as under section 1214(g).

Lastly, the legislative history apparently does not contain any references to "changes" but instead refers to "damages." 140 Cong. Rec. H11421 (daily ed. Oct. 7, 1994) (statement of Rep. McCloskey) ("[t]he expanded provisions for consequential damages and attorney fees are intended to provide a realistic expectation that employees who prevail will recover their costs...." (emphasis added)). We conclude that "changes" should be construed to be "damages" in section 1221 (g)(1)(A)(ii).

As might be expected, the parties urge opposite positions with respect to the interpretation of the phrase "consequential damages." Petitioner suggests that Congress intended to incorporate the contract law concept of consequential damages, meaning foreseeable damages, as most famously announced in Hadley v. Baxendale, 9 Ex. 341, 156 Eng. Rep. 145 (1854), and that non-pecuniary damages are recoverable. The government, on the other hand, urges that the term refers to pecuniary damages by which it means reimbursement of out-of-pocket costs. While the issue is not free from doubt, we conclude that the government is correct.

Because this is a suit against the government, we cannot allow recovery unless there has been a waiver of sovereign immunity. The Supreme Court ruled in Lane v. Pena, 518 U.S. 187, 192 (1996), that a "waiver of the Federal Government's sovereign immunity must be unequivocally expressed in statutory text...and will not be implied." See also United States v. Nordic Village, Inc., 503 U.S. 30, 33-34 (1992). Moreover, a waiver of sovereign immunity must be strictly construed in favor of the sovereign. Lane, 518 U.S. at 192; Nordic Village, 503 U.S. at 34; Library of Congress v. Shaw, 478 U.S. 310, 318 (1986). While the Supreme Court recently appears to have allowed greater latitude for interpreting the scope of sovereign immunity waivers in the light of "statutory purposes, history, and the absence of convincing reason for denying" a waiver, West v. Gibson, 527 U.S. 212, 222 (1999), we are satisfied that even under the more liberal standard of West v. Gibson, the petitioner's interpretation must fail.

First, the term "consequential damages" does not have a well-established common law meaning that includes non-pecuniary damages. In interpreting a Congressional statute using common law terminology, there is a presumption that Congress intended the common law meaning. As the Supreme Court noted in Morissette v. United States, 342 U.S. 246, 263 (1952), "where Congress borrows terms of art in which are accumulated the legal tradition and meaning of centuries of practice" it is presumed to know and adopt the common law concepts. It is therefore a "settled principle of statutory construction that, absent contrary indications, Congress intends to adopt the common law definition of statutory terms." United States v. Shabani, 513 U.S. 10, 13 (1994); see also United States v. Wells, 519 U.S. 482, 491 (1997) (articulating the presumption "that Congress incorporates the common-law meaning of the terms it uses if those terms have accumulated settled meaning under the common law and the statute does not otherwise dictate" (quoting Nationwide Mut. Ins. Co. v. Darden, 503 U.S. 318, 322 (1992)).

Many of the authorities discussing the term "consequential damages" cited by the parties here do not reflect common law usage at all. Instead they reflect casual use by courts to describe particular types of damages or use of the term in a variety of other statutory contexts. The usage in those contexts varies considerably, and the authorities are essentially unhelpful.[4]

[4] See, e.g., United States v. Burke, 504 U.S. 229, 239 (1992) (stating that "[n]othing in this remedial scheme [of Title VII prior to the Civil Rights Act of 1991] purports to recompense a...plaintiff for any of the other traditional harms associated with personal injury, such as pain and suffering, emotional distress, harm to reputation, or other consequential damages (e.g., a ruined credit rating)" (emphasis added)); Schweiker v. Chilicky, 487 U.S. 412, 425, 436-37 (1988) (Brennan, J., dissenting) (discussing how the "continuing disability review" program statute provides for "economic consequential damages" which does not include damages for "emotional or dignitary harms"); Lutz Farms v. Asgrow Seed Co., 948 F.2d 638, 646 (10th Cir. 1991) (upholding district court finding that defendant's attempted exclusion of "consequential damages" by a limitation on the bottom of an invoice was void because it was unconscionable); Ga. Code Ann. § 11-2a-520(2) (defining the types of "consequential damages" that may be awarded for a lessor's default); Ga. Code Ann. § 11-4-402(b) (defining a bank's liability to its customer for damages caused by wrongful dishonor and limiting bank's liability to "actual damages proved...and damages for arrest or prosecution of the customer or other consequential damages"); Ga. Code Ann. § 51-12(b) (defining "consequential damages" as "those which are the necessary and connected effect of a tortious act, even though they are to some extent dependent upon other circumstances").

More pertinently, petitioner urges us to apply the common law contract law concept of "consequential damages," including the foreseeability test of Hadley v. Baxendale. Even if we were to assume, as petitioner urges, that Congress intended to incorporate the meaning of the phrase "consequential damages" from contract law, this does not suggest the result that petitioner seeks. The concept of "consequential damages" in contract law relates to the concept of foreseeability at the time the contract is executed, not, as the petitioner would have it, foreseeability at the time of the breach. See Prudential Ins. Co. of Am. v. United States, 801 F.2d 1295, 1300 (Fed. Cir. 1986); Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 351(1), cmt. a (1979); see also 11 Samuel Williston, Williston on Contracts §§ 1347, 1348, at 251-53, 259 (3d ed. 1968). In general, this has led the courts to limit recovery to pecuniary damages, which are most likely to be foreseeable at the time of contracting, as opposed to non-pecuniary damages, which are more dependent on the circumstances of the individual plaintiff at the time of the breach. See, e.g., Pierce v. Int'l Ins. Co. of Ill., 671 A.2d 1361, 1367 (Del. 1996); Stanback v. Stanback, 254 S.E.2d 611, 616-17 (N.C. 1979).

The cases have focused particularly on damages for emotional distress. Under the traditional contract law approach, "[i]t is well established that, as a general rule, no damages will be awarded for the mental distress or emotional trauma that may be caused by a breach of contract." John D. Calamari & Joseph M. Perillo, The Law of Contracts § 14.5(b), at 549 (4th ed. 1998); see also Williston, Williston on Contracts §§ 1338, 1341, at 200, 214; Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 353. To be sure there are exceptions, such as contracts of carriers and innkeepers with passengers and guests, contracts for the carriage or proper disposition of dead bodies, and contracts for the delivery of messages concerning death. Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 353 cmt. a; 5 Arthur L. Corbin, Corbin on Contracts § 1076, at 434 (1964). In these cases, however, breach of the contract is particularly likely to cause serious emotional disturbance. Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 353, cmt. a. Moreover, with the limited exception of contracts for the delivery of messages concerning death, damages for mental suffering are usually refused absent wanton and willful misconduct. Corbin, Corbin on Contracts § 1076, at 434. Ordinary negligence does not suffice. Id. at 434-35.

This case clearly does not fit into any of these categories of cases for which awards of mental suffering were awarded for breach of contract at common law. Indeed, a whistleblowing claim seems most closely akin to a claim for wrongful discharge and, historically, at common law only traditional contract damages were recoverable for such claims. See 2 Mark A. Rothstein et al., Employment Law § 8.21, at 315-16 (1999).

Petitioner cites a number of wrongful discharge cases beginning in the 1980s, such as Wiskonotoni v. Michigan National Bank-West, 716 F.2d 378 (6th Cir. 1983) and Smith v. Atlas Off-Shore Boat Service, Inc., 653 F.2d 1057 (5th Cir. 1981), which have allowed emotional distress damages. Such wrongful discharge claims are sometimes based in contract and sometimes in tort. See generally Rothstein et al., Employment Law § 8.21, at 315-16; Mark A. Player, Employment Discrimination Law § 1.06a, at 11-12 (1988). Some courts, such as those in Wiskontoni and Smith, have upheld awards of emotional distress damages for wrongful discharge where public policy concerns are implicated. Others have refused to award emotional distress damages for wrongful discharge, thereby limiting damages to ordinary contract damages. See, e.g., Francis v. Lee Enters, Inc., 971 P.2d 707, 708 (Haw. 1999) (rejecting cause of action for breach of employment contract based in torts, and limiting emotional distress damages to situations where parties specifically provided for them in contract); Hummer v. Evans, 923 P.2d 981, 987 (Idaho 1996) (breach of covenant of good faith and fair dealing in at-will employment context raises claim under contract law, not tort law). Thus, while the common law limits on wrongful discharge damages may have changed in at least some states, it is clear that historically there was no uniform common law rule in this area allowing recovery of non-pecuniary damages, much less an established common law meaning of the term "consequential damages" that would include non-pecuniary damages in the context of contract law. We have also been unable to determine that the term "consequential damages" has any well-defined common law meaning in the tort context, and neither party urges that it does.

Second, if Congress had intended to allow broad recovery for non-pecuniary damages, it seems likely that it would have used the well-understood term "compensatory damages." "'Compensatory damages' are the damages awarded to a person as compensation, indemnity or restitution for harm sustained by him." Restatement (Second) of Torts § 903 (1979). Compensatory damages are divided into two categories: pecuniary and non-pecuniary. Id. at §§ 905 and 906. Non-pecuniary compensatory damages include compensation for bodily harm and emotional distress, and are awarded without proof of pecuniary loss. Id. at § 905. It is well-understood that the term "compensatory damages" includes non-pecuniary damages such as pain and suffering. For example, when Congress passed the Civil Rights Act of 1991, adding 42 USC § 1981a, it expressly provided for awards of "compensatory and punitive damages" to employees prevailing on claims of discrimination. 42 USC § 1981a(a)(1). Congress provided a precise definition of "compensatory and punitive damages" in the Civil Rights Act, listing "future pecuniary losses, emotional pain, suffering, inconvenience, mental anguish, loss of enjoyment of life, and other nonpecuniary losses" in the language of the statute itself, and then set limits on the amount that an award for these damages could total. 42 USC § 1981a(b)(3). Unlike the Civil Rights Act of 1991, section 1221 of the WPA neither expressly grants "compensatory damages" nor lists examples of the types of damages that would include non-pecuniary damages. This comparison illustrates that Congress knows how to provide damages for non-pecuniary losses, including pain and suffering, when it so desires. It did not do so in the 1994 amendments to the WPA.

Third, petitioner points to a language difference between sections 1214(g) and 1221(g). Section 1214 provides for "reimbursement for attorney's fees, back pay and related benefits, medical costs incurred, travel expenses, and any other reasonable and foreseeable consequential damages," while section 1221 simply provides "back pay and related benefits, medical costs incurred, travel expenses, and any other reasonable and foreseeable consequential [damages]." Compare 5 USC § 1214(g)(2) with 5 USC § 1221 (g)(1)(A)(ii) (emphasis added). The difference in language is most likely explained by the fact that section 1214 includes attorney's fees in the list of damages that "may" be awarded, while section 1221 treats attorney's fees separately, mandating that they "shall" be awarded. Compare 5 USC § 1214(g)(2) with 5 USC § 1221(g). Indeed, the use of the term "reimbursement" in section 1214, and the intended parallelism between sections 1214 and 1221 is further confirmation that section 1221, like section 1214, was designed to allow only for reimbursement of out-of-pocket costs.[5]

[5] Petitioner urges that the term "consequential damages" must include nonpecuniary damages because not all prohibited personnel actions cause pecuniary damages. In particular, petitioner argues that the 1994 amendments expanded the definition of these "personnel actions" which may constitute a prohibited personnel action to also include certain actions that allegedly do not cause pecuniary damages. For example, petitioner notes that the 1994 amendments, Pub. L. No. 103-424, § 5(a), 108 Stat. 4361, 4363, added "a decision to order psychiatric testing or examination" to the prohibited personnel actions listed at 5 USC § 2302(a)(2)(A). We do not agree with petitioner's assertion that these personnel actions could not result in out-of-pocket expenses being incurred. For example, an order to undergo psychiatric testing could certainly result in the employee incurring medical bills and other expenses. In any event, even if not all prohibited personnel actions in fact result in pecuniary damages, the main remedy for a prohibited personnel action is a "corrective action" which restores the employee to his or her previous position, or a position with equivalent pay and status. Congress did not assume that all prohibited personnel actions necessarily cause consequential damages.

Fourth, application of the interpretive rule of ejusdem generis to 5 USC § 1221(g)(1)(A)(ii) leads us to the conclusion that Congress intended a narrow construction of "consequential damages" in the 1994 amendment to the WPA. "Under the rule of ejusdem generis, which means 'of the same kind,' where an enumeration of specific things is followed by a general word or phrase, the general word or phrase is held to refer to things of the same kind as those specified." Sports Graphics, Inc. v. United States, 24 F.3d 1390, 1392 (Fed. Cir. 1994); see generally 2A Norman J. Singer, Statutes and Statutory Construction § 47.17, at 273-74 (6th ed. 2000) ("Where general words follow specific words in a statutory enumeration, the general words are construed to embrace only objects similar in nature to those objects enumerated by the preceding specific words."). It is appropriate to apply ejusdem generis in interpreting the language of 5 USC § 1221 (g)(1)(A)(ii). The section includes a list of three specific authorized forms of relief: (1) back pay and related benefits; (2) medical costs incurred; and (3) travel expenses. The section then includes a concluding phrase: "and any other reasonable and foreseeable consequential [damages]." Under ejusdem generis, the general phrase "any other reasonable and foreseeable consequential [damages]" should be read to cover only items similar in nature to the specific items. In this case, the three specifically listed items are all actual monetary losses or out-of-pocket expenses. None of these items includes non-pecuniary damages such as those sought by petitioner.

Finally, the legislative history of the 1994 amendments to the WPA makes no reference to compensatory damages or the types of damages for which recovery is now sought. There is a noticeable lack of discussion concerning the type of damages that an aggrieved party may receive. For example, two references in the Senate Report to damages available to a prevailing whistleblower hardly endorse the petitioners theory. The Senate Report states that "a prevailing whistleblower is entitled to attorneys' fee and any other reasonable costs incurred directly or indirectly by the whistleblower in connection with the litigation." S. Rep. No. 103-358, at 8 (1994), reprinted in 1994 USCCAN 3549, 3557 (emphasis added). This statement does not suggest an intention to expand the damages available to also include non-pecuniary damages. The petitioner further relies on a statement in the Senate Report that the Act "allows the Board to order corrective action that will make, as nearly as possible, the individual 'whole.'" S. Rep. No. 103-358, at 11 (1994), reprinted in 1994 USCCAN 3549, 3559. This is merely a general statement about the purpose of the statute and does not contradict the government's interpretation of the statute. Contrary to petitioner's assertions, making an individual "whole" does not mean that the individual is entitled to non-pecuniary losses. Rather, this statement could just as easily mean that an individual will be wholly reimbursed for his out-of-pocket or pecuniary losses. The House Committee Report for the proposed 1994 amendments discusses making a whistleblower "whole" in the context of reimbursement of expenses. That report, H.R. Rep. No. 103-769, at 18 (1994), for example, criticizes a body of case law that "does not permit employees to be made whole through payment of consequential expenses incurred as a result of an improper personnel action." (Emphasis added).

CONCLUSION

For all of these reasons we conclude that the term "consequential damages" in section 1221(g) is limited to reimbursement of out-of-pocket costs and does not include non-pecuniary damages.
