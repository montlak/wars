c. Information or Documents in the Evidence File Not Referenced in the Proposal

A proposal letter of six or eight pages may be supported by an evidence file of hundreds of pages. Is due process violated if the deciding official relies on information in the evidence file that is not specifically referenced in the proposal as support for the penalty determination? Pauli v. DHS, DC-0752-13-6815-I-1 (NP 3/10/2016), concluded no error resulted from reliance on information in the evidence file that was not highlighted in the proposal:

When a deciding official receives new and material information relating to either the merits of the underlying charge or to the penalty determination by means of ex parte communications, then a due process violation has occurred and the appellant is entitled to a new constitutionally correct procedure. Ward v. U.S. Postal Service, 634 F.3d 1274, 1280 (Fed. Cir. 2011); Stone v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 179 F.3d 1368, 1376-77 (Fed. Cir. 1999). The deciding official testified that the appellant's performance progress reviews and discussion reflected "a trend" of poor safety and lack of candid communication. Specifically, the progress reviews, dated August 19, 2010, and February 2, 2011, stated that the appellant "must improve" in the safety area and that he must keep his supervisor informed of any safety infractions. The discussion documentation indicated that, on December 2, 2011, the appellant failed to make a scheduled pickup and delivery and did not report this omission to his supervisor. On review, the appellant argues that, although he received these documents as supporting material along with the notice of proposed removal, the agency never informed him of the purpose for which they would be used, yet the deciding official considered these matters in crediting the contractor employees' version of event over the appellant's, and in determining that the appellant lacked rehabilitative potential. The administrative judge analyzed this issue as a potential due process violation, but found that the agency did not violate the appellant's due process rights because these documents contained merely cumulative information.

We modify the initial decision to find that an analysis under Stone is unnecessary because these documents did not constitute an ex parte communication or otherwise new information upon which the deciding official relied in reaching his decision. Rather, they were included in the packet of information that the appellant received with his notice of proposed removal. See Alvarado v. Department of the Air Force, 97 M.S.P.R. 389, ¶ 15 (2004) (explaining that a notice of proposed adverse action need not be a self-contained document; the notice requirement is satisfied when the proposal and any attachments to it, taken together, provide the employee with specific notice of the charges against him so that he can make an informed and meaningful reply).

We disagree with the appellant's assertion that it was reasonable to assume that the progress reviews reflected prior satisfactory performance, a potential mitigating factor. It is evident that the agency included only excerpts of the reviews containing negative comments. Further, regarding the appellant's past work record, the proposing official stated that the appellant had performed at the "meets" level but that he "is not dependable and cannot be trusted."[6] We therefore find that the agency provided sufficient notice that it viewed the progress reviews as reflecting negatively on him.[7]

[6] We distinguish this situation from one in which the information contained in the documents is not referenced in the proposal notice and the documents are not attached. An agency violates the appellant's right to due process if it relies, without notice, on unidentified instances of misconduct as an aggravating factor in its penalty analysis. Silberman v. Department of Labor, 116 M.S.P.R. 501, ¶¶ 2, 9-10, 12, 14 (2011).

[7] If the Board finds that the deciding official's consideration of an ex parte communication did not constitute a due process violation under Stone, it then should determine whether the ex parte communication constituted harmful procedural error. Ward, 634 F.3d at 1282. The administrative judge in this appeal did not proceed to this second step of the analysis. The appellant likewise has not identified any procedural error. We find that the agency provided the appellant with the evidence underlying its action as required under 5 C.F.R. § 752.404(b)(1). Therefore, we find no procedural error. See Parker v. Defense Logistics Agency, 1 M.S.P.R. 505, 513 (1980) (observing that to prove that the agency committed harmful procedural error under 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)(2)(A), the appellant must show both that the agency committed procedural error and that the error was harmful).

Harding v. U.S. Naval Academy, 567 Fed. Appx. 920, 924-25 (Fed. Cir. 2014 NP), demonstrated a similar approach involving an evidence file:

Ms. Harding's second due process claim is that she was not given notice that the USNA's deciding official would rely on the police report of her January 12, 2012, arrest in making his decision to remove her. The notice of proposed removal, however, stated that Ms. Harding and her representative could "review the material relied upon to support proposing your removal," including "the official case file." Ms. Harding does not contend that the police report was not in the case file or that she was denied the opportunity to review the police report. Under these circumstances, providing access to the materials the agency relied upon to support the removal action was sufficient to satisfy any possible due process concerns. See Darnell v. Dep't of Transp., 807 F.2d 943, 945 (Fed. Cir. 1986); Novotny v. Dep't of Transp., 735 F.2d 521, 523 (Fed. Cir. 1984). Moreover, as Ms. Harding has never disputed any of the underlying facts relating to the charges against her, it is unclear how earlier access to the police report would have been of any assistance to her

When the evidence file supporting a proposal contained material relating to a criminal prosecution, relied on by the deciding official but not explicitly referred to in the proposal, no due process violation resulted from consideration of the material; the information was referenced in the appellant's reply to the adverse action proposal. But the deciding official's reliance on a criminal conviction to preclude consideration of a lesser penalty was a due process violation when the conviction was not referenced in the proposal, Henderson v. DHS, PH-0752-11-0520-I-1 (3/29/2013):

The Board has held that there is no requirement that a proposal notice be in all cases a self-contained document. Alvarado v. Department of the Air Force, 97 M.S.P.R. 389, ¶ 15 (2004). Thus, the Board has held that the notice requirement is satisfied when the proposal and any attachments to it, taken together, provide the employee with specific notice of the charges against him so that he can make an informed and meaningful reply. Id. Here, we find that the appellant, by virtue of the agency's attachment of portions of the criminal trial transcript to its proposal notice, had sufficient notice of the agency's reliance upon her prosecution to make an informed and meaningful reply.

In fact, in her reply, the appellant clearly responded to the issue of her criminal prosecution. The decision letter addresses the relationship between the criminal action and the media coverage, the latter of which was mentioned in the proposal. In other words, in proposing to remove the appellant, the agency relied on the notoriety arising from the media coverage of her misconduct and her criminal prosecution; the appellant disputed it and, in so doing, reintroduced the prosecution into the discussion in an effort to downplay the media coverage; and the decision letter ties the criminal prosecution back to the notoriety issue. The agency thus provided the appellant with notice that it was relying in part on her criminal prosecution given its attachment of the criminal trial transcript to the proposal notice, and the appellant had an opportunity to respond to it and did so. Accordingly, there was no due process violation on this basis. Cf. Coppola v. U.S. Postal Service, 47 M.S.P.R. 307, 312 (1991) (although the notice of proposed removal was insufficient on its face to put the appellant on notice of the date of an AWOL specification, an Investigative Memorandum, which was relied upon by the proposing official and made available to the appellant, identified the date in question; because the appellant responded to the Investigative Memorandum in his response to the proposal notice, he had sufficient notice of the agency's charge); Bias v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 20 M.S.P.R. 216, 219 (1984) (when an appellant comes forward and refutes a charge, the Board cannot find that she was not on notice of the charge).

However, at the hearing, when questioned as to whether he considered alternate penalties, such as demotion or reassignment, the deciding official testified that penalties other than removal were "not an option" due to the appellant's status as a "convicted felon." Thus, the deciding official's belief that her criminal conviction required such an outcome was clearly material to his decision to remove her. Because nothing in the proposal or decision letters indicates that the agency believed that the conviction alone compelled removal, the appellant was left with no meaningful opportunity to respond to that basis for removing her. See Lopes, 116 M.S.P.R. 470, ¶ 5. As such, the deciding official's consideration of the appellant's conviction as grounds for removal without first notifying her of the significance that he attached to her criminal status effected a due process violation. See Ward, 634 F.3d at 1280.

Based on the foregoing, we find that the deciding official's consideration of aggravating factors without the appellant's knowledge was "so likely to cause prejudice that no employee can fairly be required to be subjected to a deprivation of property under such circumstances." See Stone, 179 F.3d at 1377. Consequently, because the agency violated the appellant's due process guarantee to notice, the agency's error cannot be excused as harmless, and the appellant's removal must be cancelled. See Ward, 634 F.3d at 1280. The agency may not remove the appellant unless and until he is afforded a "new constitutionally correct removal procedure." See Stone, 179 F.2d at 1377; see also Ward, 634 F.3d at 1279.
