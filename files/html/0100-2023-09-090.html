m. Prior or Subsequent Notice of Filing Deadline

The rule requiring extension of the filing period in the absence of notice applies even when an adverse action is postponed and the employee was advised of his appeal rights in an earlier decision notice. Mirach v. USPS, 19 MSPR 453 (1984). Notwithstanding Mirach, an appellant may be charged with knowing the time for appeal from some earlier communication in a component of his case, despite the absence of full notice of regulatory time limits to the appellant from the agency when the agency issued a decision that was appealed. In Dyer v. TVA, 972 F.2d 1353 (Fed. Cir. 1992), the Board was directed to consider how formal notice of Board regulatory deadlines would serve to mitigate a late filing when the employee was subjected to a RIF, filed an EEO complaint, and received a final decision on that complaint stating, without further elaboration, that the appellant could file a Board appeal within 20 calendar days. The case was sent back to the Board for closer analysis of the timeliness question with the observation:

Dyer contends...that because the TVA did not furnish him with a copy of the MSPB regulations or appeal form at the time of the agency's October 31, 1990 decision, his failure to comply precisely with required procedures should be excused. Specifically, 5 CFR 1201.21 states that "[w]hen an agency issues a decision notice to an employee on a matter that is appealable to the Board, the agency must provide the employee" not only with notice of the time limits for appealing to the board, but also with a copy or access to a copy of the board's regulations, and a copy of the MSPB appeal form. While these documents were not included with the TVA's final decision, the TVA argues that the regulations and appeal form furnished Dyer at the time of the RIF decision in January 1989, some 22 months earlier, satisfied the regulatory requirement.

Because the AJ made no findings as to whether Dyer's December 7, 1990 letter and later filed appeal form were sufficient to make Dyer's appeal timely, or to warrant a waiver of the time limitation for filing an appeal, we vacate the board's decision and remand for this purpose. On remand, the board should also consider whether 5 C.F.R. § 1201.21 required the TVA to furnish Dyer with the regulations and appeal form after the October 31, 1990 decision in the administrative EEO proceeding.

See Miyashiro v. USPS, 79 MSPR 628, 631 (1998) (restoration appeal was untimely after the Board attributed to the appellant notice of Board deadlines through appellant's involvement in a prior restoration appeal prosecuted by a union representative); Dunklebarger v. Dept. of Army, 67 MSPR 607, 610 (1995) (rejecting imputed knowledge of appeal rights in a restoration case through a prior removal notice), supra.

Notice of a Board appeal right from an adverse action may have sufficed to trigger the appeal time from a constructive suspension, Johnson v. USPS, 105 MSPR 654, 656-58 ¶¶ 6-11, 2007 MSPB 135 (2007) (after remand, 108 MSPR 502, 2008 MSPB 83 (2008)):

A Board appeal must be filed no later than 30 days after the effective date, if any, of the action being appealed, or 30 days after the date of receipt of the agency's decision, whichever is later. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.22(b). Pursuant to 5 C.F.R. § 1201.21, when issuing a decision on a matter appealable to the Board, an agency is required to provide an employee with notice of the Board's timeliness requirements, the address of the appropriate Board office for filing an appeal, and access to a copy of the Board's regulations. Rivera v. Department of Transportation, 94 M.S.P.R. 216, ¶ 13 (2003). As a general matter, an agency's failure to notify an employee of his or her Board appeal rights under circumstances requiring it to do so will justify a waiver of the filing deadline. Sellers v. U.S. Postal Service, 98 M.S.P.R. 44, ¶ 12 (2004). In such a case, an appellant is not required to show that he exercised due diligence in attempting to discover his appeal rights; the question is whether he was diligent in filing an appeal after he learned he could do so. Id.

Notice from a source other than the agency of a general appeal right to the Board does not excuse the agency's failure to inform an employee of his appeal rights, where the notice does not inform the employee of the time limit for filing an appeal to the Board and lacks other information on where and how to file such an appeal. Nicoletti v. Department of Justice, 55 M.S.P.R. 557, 559-560 (1992); see also Shafford v. United States Postal Service, 103 M.S.P.R. 657, ¶ 13 n.2 (2006). Thus, the mere fact that the appellant learned from his union in September 2005 that he may have been entitled to appeal his removal to the Board would not, ordinarily, excuse the agency's failure to inform him of such rights.

The Board has dismissed an appeal as untimely filed, however, where the record established that the appellant knew of where, when, and how to file an appeal, but did not show he was diligent in doing so. In Lohf v. United States Postal Service, 72 M.S.P.R. 597, 598 (1996), the agency constructively suspended the appellant from September 22, 1993, to September 27, 1994, but did not provide him with notice of Board appeal rights. The agency subsequently removed the appellant, effective July 14, 1995. Id. at 599. The removal notice informed the appellant that he had the right to appeal his removal in writing to the Board's field office within 30 calendar days of the effective date of the decision and provided the address of the Board's field office; a copy of the Board's regulations and an appeal form were attached. Id. at 599-600. On July 18, 1995, the appellant, in an equal employment opportunity submission, cited to published Board decisions for the principle that constructive suspensions were appealable to the Board. Id. at 598-99. The Board therefore found that, as of July 18, 1995, the appellant knew from the results of his own research that constructive suspensions could be appealed to the Board, and knew from the agency's removal notice and the Board's regulations where, when, and how to file a Board appeal. Id. at 600. The Board accordingly dismissed as untimely the appellant's constructive suspension appeal, filed December 18, 1995, because he did not show he was diligent in filing the appeal after learning that he could do so. Id.

Here, the appellant asserted in his notarized statement that, in September 2005, he "learned [of] the possibility that I was not given due rights to a MSPB hearing." He also submitted a statement in which he wrote, "[T]his past Fall (2005) I went to Washington, D.C., and while there I went to the National Postal Mail Handlers Union where I spoke to a representative about what had transpired with the Postal Service and was told what I did not know--'That my rights were violated.'" The appellant did not indicate that he received any information on where, when, and how to file a Board appeal when he talked to his union's representative. There is evidence in the file, however, that indicates the appellant may have had such information at that time. Specifically, the agency's file contains a Notice of Proposed Removal (Veteran), dated November 24, 1998, which gave the appellant his Board appeal rights, the Board's address, and the deadline for filing an appeal. The notice also indicates that the Board's regulations and an appeal form were attached.

Although the table of contents of the agency's file describes the November 24, 1998 notice as "Letter of Decision: Notice of Proposed Removal (Veteran) issued to Appellant," the agency does not argue in any of its pleadings that it actually sent this notice to the appellant. Moreover, the notice itself is not signed, there is no evidence that it was ever mailed to the appellant, and there is no document signed by the appellant acknowledging receipt of the notice. In this regard, we note that the agency acknowledges that the appellant had not been at work since July 1997. Thus, it is not clear from the evidence in the record whether the appellant did, in fact, receive this notice and therefore knew where, when, and how to file a Board appeal in September 2005.

An AJ may not dismiss an appeal as untimely filed without sufficient evidence that establishes the untimeliness, and he should ask both parties to submit evidence on the issue. See Hamilton v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 75 F.3d 639, 646 (Fed. Cir. 1996). Here, the AJ did not alert the parties to the significance of the November 24, 1998 notice, and ask them to submit evidence and argument on the issue of whether this notice was sent to and received by the appellant. The appeal therefore must be remanded so that the AJ may so alert the parties, and order them to submit evidence and argument on this issue before deciding anew whether the appellant had sufficient information to file a timely appeal when he learned of his appeal rights in September 2005. If he did not have sufficient information, the AJ shall determine whether the appellant showed good cause for the delay in filing his appeal.
