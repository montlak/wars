b. Identity of Parties; Privity

The Board has offered no definitive guidance on what degree of relation must exist between different parties so that a decision as to one binds some or all of the others. There are references to "privies" in the decisions analyzing res judicata," but there are no definitions in Board law of the term. Explanation was provided in Wright v. Dept. of Transp., 89 MSPR 571, 576 ¶ 17 (2001):

The Board in Peartree v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 M.S.P.R. 332 (1995), cited Federated Department Stores, Inc. v. Moitie, 452 U.S. 394, 101 S.C. 2424, 69 L. Ed.2d 103 (1981), for the proposition that under the doctrine of res judicata, "a valid, final judgment on the merits of an action bars a second action involving the same parties or their privies based on the same cause of action." 66 M.S.P.R. at 337. The question in Federated was whether a judgment issued by a federal district court against seven plaintiffs should bar the same action after it was refiled in state court by two plaintiffs who did not appeal the district court's decision. 452 U.S. at 396-98 and 400-41, 101 S.C. at 2427-29. The Supreme Court held that res judicata barred relitigation of the district court's adverse unappealed decision. Id. at 400-02, 101 S.C. at 2429-30. In so holding, the Court stated the long-recognized principle that "[p]ublic policy dictates that there be an end to litigation; that those who have contested an issue shall be bound by the result of the contest, and that matters once tried shall be considered forever settled as between the parties." Id. at 401, 101 S.C. at 2429 (emphasis added; citation omitted).

Padilla v. OPM, 83 MSPR 12, 13-14 ¶ 2 (1999), applied preclusion to a wife's claim for retirement benefits that was earlier litigated as to the husband, upon whose employment the retirement claim depended:

The doctrine of res judicata precludes a second action involving the same parties or their privies and based on the litigation of claims that were, or could have been, asserted in a prior proceeding. See Nevada v. United States, 463 U.S. 110, 130, 103 S.C. 2906, 77 L. Ed.2d 509 (1983); Spears v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 766 F.2d 520, 523 (Fed. Cir. 1985); Peartree v. United States Postal Service, 66 MSPR 332, 337 (1995). All of the petitioners have been parties or privies[2] to appeals from OPM's denial of civil service retirement benefits filed pursuant to 5 USC § 8347(d). At issue in those appeals was whether petitioners were properly excluded from civil service retirement coverage under 5 CFR § 831.201(a)(13). Neither the Board nor the Federal Circuit disagreed with OPM's determination that exclusion was proper. Additionally, many of the petitioners have previously filed requests for review of 5 CFR § 831.201(a)(13) pursuant to 5 USC § 1204(f). The Board denied those requests and the Federal Circuit dismissed the petitions for judicial review. To the extent that petitioners' claims of invalid implementation of 5 CFR § 831.201(a)(13) in this proceeding are distinguishable from petitioners' claims made in the prior retirement appeals and regulation review proceedings, the instant claims could have been raised in the earlier proceedings. See Francisco v. Office of Personnel Management, 80 MSPR 684, 687 (1999). Thus, petitioners' requests for review of the invalid implementation of 5 CFR § 831.201(a)(13) are precluded by res judicata. Accordingly, the requests for regulation review are DENIED....

The estoppel may occur through indirect status as a party. A judgment in a class action binds the class if the interests of the members of the class have been adequately represented or if they actually participated in the litigation. An absent member will not be bound if he proves the procedure did not adequately insure the protection of his interests. Although class members may be precluded from litigating an issue decided in a class action, a class member may litigate an issue that was not common to that previously decided in the class action. See Maximo v. OPM, 30 MSPR 510, 512 (1986) (air traffic controller suitability case) (citing Laskey v. UAW, 638 F.2d 954 (6th Cir. 1981)). Special Counsel v. Kehoe, 33 MSPR 56, 64-65 (1987), recognized, in passing, the existence of estoppel through "virtual representation," precluding a litigant from litigating an issue based on a prior lawsuit involving interests closely aligned to the party in the present litigation and adequately represented in the earlier litigation. The doctrine was inapplicable to Kehoe's appeal because the interests of a state government in federal civil rights litigation, involving issues with some similarity to the political activity issues presented in Kehoe's case, were not sufficiently similar to the interests of OSC in the Hatch Act proceeding before the Board to permit the conclusion that OSC's interests were adequately protected in the state government litigation in federal court.
