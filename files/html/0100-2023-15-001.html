CHAPTER 8

SUBSTANTIVE OFFENSES

"Let the punishment match the offense." (Cicero, De Legibus, III, 3). Analysis of the most common types of substantive offenses helps to determine elements of proof and the importance imputed by the Board to each category of misconduct. The categories of misconduct listed in this chapter are illustrative; agencies are not required by the Board to pigeonhole offenses, although agencies may do so to comply with tables of penalties that describe specific offenses. The Office of Personnel Management declined to compile a list of removal causes promoting the "efficiency of the service," because the phrase has been in law for many years and its meaning has been determined by many appellate bodies. The failure of a removal proposal or notice to specify a cause, published in some fashion, promoting the efficiency of the service is not a basis for setting the action aside. Penaloza v. DHHS, 4 MSPR 322, 327, 4 MSPB 389 (1980); see Schlegel v. United States, 416 F.2d 1372, 1377 (Ct. Cl. 1969) ("No regulatory scheme can anticipate every particular instance where employee misconduct might occur. Consequently, regulations are frequently couched in general terms...."). Not all misconduct leading to disciplinary action is specifically prohibited by statute or regulation. Hatch v. Dept. of Air Force, 40 MSPR 260, 266 (1989); see Brown v. Dept. of Navy, 229 F.3d 1356, 1363 (Fed. Cir. 2000) ("The fact that there may be no direct precedent for the action taken in a particular case does not, however, prohibit the agency from taking adverse action if it is consistent with general principles of federal employment law."); Pascal v. United States, 543 F.2d 1284, 1287-88 (Ct. Cl. 1976) ("But it is not the principle of the government world that everything is permissible which is not specifically prohibited by regulation or statute.").

This chapter involves adverse actions within the Board's appellate authority. Defined at 5 USC 7512, those adverse actions are disciplinary actions against statutorily-defined employees who are removed, suspended for more than 14 days, subjected to reductions in pay or grade, or furloughed for less than 30 days. Not included are disciplinary cases brought by the Office of Special Counsel, performance-based actions (removals or reductions in grade) under Chapter 43 (discussed in Chapter 9), a national security removal under 5 USC 7532, or a disciplinary complaint against an administrative law judge (discussed in Chapter 5). Constructive adverse actions of all stripes are discussed in Chapter 6. Given the considerable volume of law on the subject, the number of adverse actions decided by the Board is relatively small. The FY 2020 Board Annual Report states that in that year 2,129 adverse actions were decided by AJs, but of that number 1,240 were dismissed, and of those not dismissed, 427 settled, leaving but 462 adverse actions, or about eight or ten per MSPB AJ, decided on the merits.

There is no requirement that an employee violate a specific written policy before he can be disciplined under Chapter 75. The sole criterion under Chapter 75 is that the adverse action be "for such cause as will promote the efficiency of the service." 5 USC 7513(a); Fontes v. Dept. of Transp., 51 MSPR 655, 663 (1991). Penaloza v. DHHS, 4 MSPR 322, 327, 4 MSPB 389 (1980), explained that conduct need not be described by a particular label to be actionable:

Appellant also argued that no action may be taken pursuant to Section 7513 until OPM prescribes, by regulation, what are adequate causes for removal. OPM declined to compile a list of causes for removal which promote the efficiency of the service because "the term has been in law for many years and its meaning determined by many appellate bodies." 44 Fed. Reg. 47030 (1979).

This comment is supported by courts which have considered the term as used in the context of prior statutes concerned with removal of federal government employees. As the D.C. Circuit Court stated in Norton v. Macy, 417 F.2d 1161, 1165 (1969):

Congress did not attempt a definition of "cause," providing only that it must be one that would promote the efficiency of the service. Some latitude was thus left to those responsible for administering the Act. Faithfulness to its purpose to reward those who served in the armed forces by granting a protective preference must be maintained. But the preference granted does not protect a veteran in a position when removal therefrom, viewed in the light of competing policies and all the circumstances, can reasonably be said to lead to increased efficiency in...governmental operations. (Footnotes omitted)

Because the law does not require a particular characterization of conduct, it is sufficient for an agency to describe the misconduct, in the necessary degree of detail, and to prove it, allowing the Board to determine what category of cases or misconduct most nearly describes the offense. Offenses as broadly stated as "conduct unbecoming a federal employee" are reviewed and sustained by the Board. See, e.g., Miles v. Dept. of Army, 55 MSPR 633, 637 (1992) (using the dictionary definition of "unbecoming" to interpret a charge); Gatling v. NTSB, 17 MSPR 43, 46 (1983) ("conduct unbecoming a Federal Officer" coupled with other charges). [Refer to Chapter 11, "Cumulative Charges; Merger of Charges."]

As to the characterization of misconduct, Goldstein v. Dept. of Treasury, 62 MSPR 622, 627 (1994), declared:

The Board has found regarding this issue, however, that an agency is not required to describe in detail all potentially proscribed employee conduct and resulting discipline. See Brown v. Federal Aviation Administration, 15 MSPR 224, 233 (1983), rev'd in part on other grounds, 735 F.2d 543 (Fed. Cir. 1984). In doing so, it cited the following observation by the Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit:

[I]t is not feasible or necessary for the Government to spell out in detail all that conduct which will result in retaliation. The most conscientious of codes that define prohibited conduct of employees include "catchall" clauses prohibiting employee "misconduct," "immorality," or "conduct unbecoming."

See id., citing Meehan v. Macy, 392 F.2d 822, 835, modified, 425 F.2d 469 (1968), aff'd en banc, 425 F.2d 472 (D.C. Cir. 1969), as cited in Brousseau v. United States, 640 F.2d 1235, 1247, 226 Ct. Cl. 199 (1981). As the Board noted, this observation has been quoted with approval by the Supreme Court. See id., citing Arnett v. Kennedy, 416 U.S. 134, 161-62, 94 S. Ct. 1633, 1647, 40 L. Ed.2d 15 (1974). The framing of the conduct unbecoming charge under a provision proscribing general misconduct prejudicial to the government thus did not render the charge unconstitutionally vague. See Brown, 15 MSPR at 233....

We note also that a charge must be analyzed in terms of the underlying specifications and circumstances, not technically construed with respect to the charge's title. See Creggett v. Department of the Army, 41 MSPR 584, 587 (1989)....

Most appeals involve specifically defined charges, making it convenient to classify Board's decisions. I. STATUS

Was it Woody Allen who said that eighty percent of success is showing up? Many misconduct cases involve employees whose attendance is irregular. Included in status-related offenses are individuals who place themselves at odds with their employer through conduct that is inconsistent with their positions as employees: those involved in unauthorized outside employment, and those involved in activities in conflict or apparent conflict of interest with their job responsibilities. Also included in this discussion are appeals involving employees in whom particular trust is imposed, law enforcement officers being an example, who commit offenses contrary to that trust.
