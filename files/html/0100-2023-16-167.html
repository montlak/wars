g. Details and Reassignments

It was a ULP for an overseas activity to refuse to recommend the extension of an overseas tour for an employee whose union activities--a multitude of ULP complaints, grievances, and negotiability appeals--embarrassed the local command. US Forces Korea and NFFE Local 1363, 11 FLRA 434, 436 (1983). A union official was the victim of unlawful interference who was denied the opportunity to work on a special project or detail after management told him that his position as a union official or negotiator would somehow be compromised if he took the assignment. Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv. and NTEU, 19 FLRA 956, 969 (1985) (ALJ Decision). Details motivated by concerns about the filing of grievances by two employees were set aside as unlawful in Customs Serv. and NTEU, 24 FLRA 773 (1986).

A reassignment motivated by animus toward the union will result in an order directing the agency to offer the employee a return to the original job. See Customs Serv., Region IV, Miami and NTEU, 36 FLRA 489, 499 (1990). A transfer to another work crew resulting from a supervisor's irritation over the announced intention of an employee to grieve his working conditions was a violation of § 7116(a)(2). There was no requirement that the violation be conditioned on a loss of pay or other tangible benefits. Portsmouth Naval Shipyard and Lacroix, 17 FLRA 773, 782 (1985) (ALJ Decision). Statements were coercive that the "front office" did not approve of an employee's or steward's union activities and that if an employee continued to file grievances she could lose her job or receive an unfavorable reassignment. NRC and NTEU, 28 FLRA 820, 830-31 (1987) (ALJ Decision). Management commits a ULP by determining or suggesting that an employee's protected activity renders him or her unsuitable for transfer to a new or different position. US Air Force Lowry AFB and AFGE Local 1974, 16 FLRA 952, 956 (1984) (ALJ Decision); see Dept. of Army, Aviation Ctr. and AFGE Local 1815, 46 FLRA 535, 543 (1992) (ALJ Decision). A detail based on an employee's grievance activity was a ULP. Dept. of Air Force, 60th AMB, Travis AFB and AFGE Local 1764, 59 FLRA 632, 638 (2004) ("in the absence of evidence showing that the detail was going to take place irrespective of the lead clerk's grievances, the Respondent's affirmative defense fails").

Dept. of Air Force, Scott AFB and NAGE Local R7-23, 34 FLRA 956, 963-65 (1990), resulted in a ULP finding when a selecting official told a union official that he had not chosen him for a position involving rotating shifts because he was afraid that he would invoke a contract provision granting union officials the option of working day shifts:

...In both Veterans Administration Medical Center, Leavenworth, Kansas, 31 FLRA 1161, 1170-71 (1988) [discussed, post], and Department of the Air Force, Scott Air Force Base, 20 FLRA 761, 764 (1985) [discussed, post]...the supervisors alerted the employees to a conflict between the agencies' ability to manage efficiently and the employees' right to engage in union activity, and discussed with the employees how that conflict could be accommodated.

In VAMC, the Authority...found that a supervisor's statement that the employee was rated low on his mid-term evaluation because of his use of official time for union activities did not violate section 7116(a)(1).... In that case, the supervisor had told the employee that he was deficient in certain duties because he took too much official time when those duties should have been performed. In a subsequent conversation, the supervisor explained that the employee could not go on official time until after certain work was completed and, when that work was completed, permitted the employee to spend the rest of the day on union business. The Authority found that, under the circumstances, a reasonable employee would have understood that the supervisor was trying to reach an accommodation between the parties' respective rights; that is, he "was trying to encourage [the employee] to schedule his [u]nion activities so that he could also perform the [work]."...

Similarly, in Scott AFB, the Authority held that the agency had not violated...the Statute when its supervisors stated to an employee, who was also a union president, that the employee was "spending too much time on union work and too little on the [agency's] work and that disciplinary action might be taken against him if he did not put more work into the...project." The Authority noted the supervisors' prior efforts to reach an accommodation with the employee, the responses of the employee to management's efforts, the time-sensitive nature of the work to be done, and the lack of evidence of union animus. It was further noted that there was no evidence of a denial of any future requests for official time and that the employee had not been disciplined. In those circumstances, the Authority found that the supervisors were lawfully seeking an accommodation between the employee's and the agency's conflicting rights....

We find that Affolter's first statement to Cissell did not constitute an attempt at accommodation. At the time Affolter made his remark, he had already chosen someone else for the job. Therefore, there was no existing conflict for him to accommodate....

...Under these circumstances, we conclude that it is reasonable for a unit employee to interpret the statement to mean that Affolter would not consider hiring a Union official for shift work for fear that the contractual provision would be invoked, and that, therefore, an employee could never successfully compete for a job with rotating shifts so long as he was a Union official. As a result, the statement interfered with Cissell's section 7102 right to act as a representative of the Union and, therefore, violated section 7116(a)(1) of the Statute....
