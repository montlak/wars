A. PERFORMANCE AS BASIS FOR ADVERSE ACTION

We consider here the use of Chapter 75 misconduct procedures to address performance issues under the "service efficiency" standard. The more complex processes of Chapter 43, with the lower standard of proof and absence of Board mitigation authority, are addressed in Chapter 9.

Removal for unsatisfactory performance promotes service efficiency under Chapter 75. Hershfield v. Dept. of Navy, 5 MSPR 444, 446, 5 MSPB 450 (1981); see Korman v. United States, 462 F.2d 1382, 1385 (Ct. Cl. 1972) ("Reasonable adverse action against a high official who exercises or displays bad judgment (in a significant aspect of his work),...promotes the efficiency of the service."); Kirby v. VA, 14 MSPR 185, 187 (1982) (concerning the removal of a clerk typist: a supervisor testified that because of numerous errors, the appellant's work sometimes had to be rechecked, even when done correctly, because the work product generated no sense of reliability or dependability; coworkers complained of the deficiencies; the employee was counseled and reassigned without improvement); Hatcher v. Dept. of Air Force, 11 MSPR 183, 185 (1982) (removal of an officers' mess manager based on the failure to manage the mess in accordance with specific instructions; "agency provided clear notice to appellant of performance deficiencies and provided him with goals and recommendations for improvement in inspection reports").

In a Chapter 75 case, the Board intimated that a charge of unsatisfactory performance may also include misconduct, e.g., insubordination and failure to cooperate. Special Counsel v. Dept. of Army, 16 MSPR 178, 185 n.10 (1983); see Hill v. Kennedy, 310 F. Supp. 455 (E.D. Mich. 1970) (pre-Reform Act case sustaining removal of an IRS employee for, among other charges, excessive talking and visiting with other employees); Perez v. R.R. Retirement Bd., 65 MSPR 287, 289 (1994) ("The agency bears the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, its charge that the appellant neglected his duty in failing to carry out assigned work."); Watts v. VA, 24 MSPR 421, 424 (1984) (charges of willful idleness, time-wasting and inattentiveness to duty); Vilt v. DOJ, 10 MSPR 171, 175-76 (1982) (suspension for inattentiveness to duty). Whether the conduct is labeled as "unwillingness" or "inability" to perform work, if the appellant has notice of the charged inadequacies and those inadequacies are established by the evidence, adverse action will be sustained, subject to mitigating factors. See Thompson v. Smithsonian Inst., 11 MSPR 447, 450 (1982). An agency regulation calling for adverse action if an employee failed to improve his or her performance was advisory; the regulation provided for adverse action not only if the employee failed to improve, but also if the employee failed to meet the requirements of the position. Patterson v. VA, 12 MSPR 336, 337 (1982).

If a manager is charged with substandard performance, the agency may be expected to show how the program run by the appellant could have been improved through the appellant's efforts, Trueheart v. Dept. of Army, 15 MSPR 191, 193 (1983):

In regard to appellant's management of DARCOM, the presiding official found that the agency presented virtually no evidence on how appellant could have improved his performance and that testimony presented by witnesses on appellant's behalf showed that DARCOM could not have satisfied the expectations of the agency. Also, the presiding official found that testimony by appellant, as well as other witnesses, established that appellant, in compliance with his supervisors' suggestion, frequently contacted DARCOM officials in order to resolve the existing problems but that his efforts were to no avail. Additionally, the presiding official found that appellant's supervisor might not have fully comprehended the considerations involved in balancing and reconciling the DARCOM force and that the supervisor failed to understand and to respond to appellant's requests for assistance.

The question of whether an agency may proceed against an employee under both Chapters 43 and 75 was raised, but not definitively answered, in Combs v. DHS, DC-0432-18-0552-I-2, n.7 (NP 2/16/2023):

It is questionable whether the administrative judge should have adjudicated the performance-based charge under both chapter 43 and chapter 75 standards. It is well established that an agency may take a performance-based action under either chapter 43 or chapter 75, Lovshin v. Department of the Navy, 767 F.2d 826, 843 (Fed. Cir. 1985), and may convert the action from chapter 43 to chapter 75 if a hearing has not yet occurred, Ortiz v. U.S. Marine Corps, 37 M.S.P.R. 359, 363 (1988). It is also permissible for an agency to bring a hybrid action, charging an employee with unacceptable performance under chapter 43 procedures while bringing an additional or alternative charge, such as conduct unbecoming, under chapter 75 procedures. Lovshin, 767 F.2d at 843. However, we are unaware of any case in which the Board has considered allegations of unacceptable performance under both standards. Moreover, the Board has suggested in dicta that chapter 43 and chapter 75 procedures are, by statute, mutually exclusive with respect to the same charge. See Aguzie v. Office of Personnel Management, 112 M.S.P.R. 276, ¶ 4 n.2 (2009) (citing 5 U.S.C. § 7512(D)), reconsidered on other grounds, 116 M.S.P.R. 64 (2011)....
