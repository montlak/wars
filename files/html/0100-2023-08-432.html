4. Examples of Regulation Implementation Review

The Board considered a complaint that an agency adopted a bell curve to force distribution of performance ratings or merit pay determinations, a practice that would both violate OPM regulations on appraisal systems and would likely constitute a prohibited personnel practice. See McDiarmid v. Fish and Wildlife Serv., 19 MSPR 347, 23 MSPB 420 (1984).

An agency could validly decide to consider individuals on a list of applicants eligible for promotion, rather than those personnel eligible for noncompetitive reassignment, Ward v. OPM, 79 MSPR 530, 533-35 (1998) (aff'd in part and dismissed in part, Ward v. OPM (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 99-3044)):

[P]etitioner's arguments against the validity of the FCC's implementation of the regulation are weak. The regulation at issue, 5 CFR § 335.103, sets out the requirements for an agency's merit promotion program, as well as other types of placement. The regulations differentiate between those actions subject to competitive procedures and those not requiring such procedures. 5 CFR § 335.103(c). Competitive procedures are required for personnel actions that result in a transfer to a position at a higher grade than a position previously held, on a permanent basis, in the competitive service. 5 CFR § 335.103(c)(1)(v). The selectee, a GS-11 applicant for the GS-12 position, was therefore subject to these procedures. In contrast, a reinstatement to a position having promotion potential no greater than the potential of a position an employee currently holds, or previously held, on a permanent basis, in the competitive service, may be excepted from competitive procedures by the agency. Thus, the regulations provide for differentiation between categories of applicants for a position. Consistent with that differentiation, the agency drew up an applicant list consisting of individuals deemed eligible and best qualified for promotion consideration. Because the petitioner's last and highest paid grade as a government employee was as a GS-12, the agency properly did not include him on this list of applicants eligible for promotion. Rather, the agency viewed him as eligible for the noncompetitive personnel action of reinstatement. Thus, the agency did not violate 5 CFR § 335.103 when it failed to put the petitioner's name on the competitive promotion list.

Further, the regulations indicate that the FCC has discretion as to which sources it will use to fill its positions and that it may select or not select from the applicants who responded to the vacancy announcement. 5 CFR § 335.103(b)(4). The FCC asserted, and the petitioner has not disputed, that the agency decided to fill the position with those applicants referred on the competitive merit promotion list and extended interviews only to those candidates. The FCC did not extend interviews to any applicants, like petitioner, who were designated nonstatus and noncompetitive. Given the discretion granted the agency by 5 CFR § 335.103, petitioner has failed to show a violation of the regulation in limiting its consideration to applicants on the merit promotion list.

Absent a violation of 5 CFR § 335.103, petitioner cannot establish that the prohibited personnel practice described in 5 USC § 2302(b)(6) occurred. Section 2302(b)(6) prohibits giving a preference not authorized by law, rule or regulation, to any employee or applicant for employment, for the purpose of improving or injuring the prospects of any particular person for employment. The preference that petitioner complains of, that the selectee was considered under a procedure that excluded him, was a preference authorized by 5 CFR § 335.103, which permits the agency to select applicants from different sources. Petitioner was properly excluded from consideration in the merit promotion pool of applicants for the GS-12 writer-editor position because his appointment to the position, if it occurred, would not be a promotion but a reinstatement, petitioner's last grade as a government employee being a GS-12. Accordingly, petitioner has failed to show that implementation of section 335.103 constituted an unlawful preference as described in 5 USC § 2302(b)(6).

Similarly, petitioner has failed to show that implementation of section 335.103 constituted the prohibited personnel practice described in 5 USC § 2302(b)(11) [now 2302(b)(12)] . To establish a claim that the agency's failure to consider him in the pool of merit promotion eligible applicants violated 5 USC § 2302(b)(11) [now 2302(b)(12)], petitioner must show: (1) that the FCC violated 5 CFR § 335.103; and (2) that the violated provision is one which "implements" or "directly concerns" the merit system principles. For the reasons already stated, the FCC's failure to place petitioner's name on the merit promotion applicant list and consider him in this applicant pool did not violate section 335.103. Accordingly, petitioner cannot establish the first element of a section 2302(b)(11) [now 2302(b)(12)] violation. Given the lack of support for petitioner's claims of prohibited personnel practices and the availability of an equivalent remedy for the FCC's allegedly improper activities, the Board declines to exercise its discretionary authority to review the FCC's implementation of 5 CFR § 335.103.

Under Saya v. Dept. of Air Force, 68 MSPR 493, 495-97 (1995), a regulation implementation challenge involving nonpromotion must be based on an agency's misuse of a test or practice implementing OPM selection procedures. An agency's assessment of relative qualifications of candidates for a given position will not likely meet the requirements necessary for Board review:

Petitioner asserts that her nonselection for the Aircraft Mechanic position was improper and discriminatory. According to petitioner, the procedure used by the agency in filling this position invalidly implemented 5 CFR 300.103, the OPM regulation which establishes the basic requirements for the employment practices of individual agencies as well as for those of the Federal Government generally. Petitioner contends that the Air Force's selection criteria violated sections 300.103(b) and (c) because there was no rational relationship between them and performance in the job, they did not meet the standards established by the Uniform Guidelines on Employment Selection Procedures, and they discriminated against her on the basis of her sex.

Under 5 CFR 1203.11(b)(ii), the individual requesting review of the implementation of an OPM regulation is required to submit a statement of why implementation of the regulation requires commission of a prohibited personnel practice. Sex discrimination is, of course, a prohibited personnel practice under 5 USC 2302(b)(1)(A). In support of her discrimination claim, petitioner alleges various unfair or improper actions by supervisors before and after her nonselection with at best only an indirect bearing on her invalid implementation claim. Principally, petitioner contends that the Air Force selected a male individual who was not qualified for the position and was not eligible to be considered under the Announcement because he was not an employee of Westover Air Force Base. Apparently, petitioner contends that the area of consideration for a selection is an employment practice subject to the requirements of OPM's regulation, and she attributes her nonselection to the agency's consideration of a nonemployee of the Base.

Under section 300.101, employment practices "affect the recruitment, ranking and selection," and include "the development and use of examinations, qualification standards, tests, and other measurement instruments." Practices other than such merit-based tests also fall within the definition if they affect selection. See Dowd v. United States, 713 F.2d 720, 722-24 (Fed. Cir. 1983) (time-in-grade restrictions on promotions); Lackhouse v. MSPB, 773 F.2d 313, 315 (Fed. Cir. 1985), cert. denied, 475 U.S. 1049, 106 S. Ct. 1271, 89 L. Ed.2d 580 (1986) (rule-of-three permitting dropping candidate from further consideration after three considerations). However, an individual agency action or decision which is not a rule or practice of some kind does not qualify as an "employment practice." Cf. Maule v. OPM, 40 MSPR 388, 393 (1989) (a procedural decision affecting an application is not an employment practice), aff'd, 892 F.2d 1050 (Fed. Cir. 1989) (Table).

Petitioner contends that the Air Force Merit Promotion Plan is an employment practice implementing section 300.103 and that OPM's regulation therefore requires the Plan's provisions to be rationally related to performance in the positions to be filled and to avoid prohibited discrimination. Petitioner objects to the selection of a male individual not employed by Westover Air Force Base for the Aircraft Mechanic position, evidently contending that her nonselection was the result of the wider area of consideration allowed by the agency's Merit Promotion Plan. Assuming without deciding that a policy concerning the area of consideration for a position is an employment practice implementing 5 CFR 300.103, petitioner has advanced no specific reason for finding that this feature of the Air Force's Merit Promotion Plan violates the requirements of section 300.103 or for concluding that it required a selection which discriminated against petitioner because of her sex.

If the challenge to an agency's implementation of an OPM regulation lies under 5 USC 2302(b)(6), the challenge must be to a situation involving advancement of or injury to the employment prospects of a particular individual rather than of a group, as explained in Avery v. OPM, 94 MSPR 212, 215 ¶¶ 5-6 (2003), involving a disagreement over the application of veterans preference:

The essence of the petitioner's claim is that a prohibited personnel practice in violation of 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(6) is committed where a purportedly unauthorized preference improves the prospects of veterans as a class at the expense of nonveterans as a class. The Board has not viewed the language of section 2302(b)(6) so broadly; rather, the Board interprets section 2302(b)(6) to cover situations where the prospects of a specific person are injured or improved by an unauthorized preference. Weir v. Office of Personnel Management, 62 M.S.P.R. at 94; see Special Counsel v. Byrd, 59 M.S.P.R. 561, 570 (1993), affd sub nom. Byrd v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 39 F.3d 1196 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table). This is consistent with the language of the statute which, as indicated above, addresses the improper granting of any preference for "the purpose of improving or injuring the prospects of any particular person for employment." 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(6) (emphasis supplied); see Weir, 62 M.S.P.R. at 94.

The petitioner does not allege that GSA's policy results in the unauthorized awarding of a preference for the purpose of helping or harming the employment prospects of any specific individual. Extra points were granted to all preference eligible candidates and the petitioner acknowledges that the granting of such a preference has been GSA policy for years. Thus, even if GSA's practice of granting a preference to veterans seeking promotion was contrary to statute, it does not constitute a prohibited personnel practice. See Weir, 62 M.S.P.R. at 94. Accordingly, the Board has no authority to invalidate the practice. Id.
