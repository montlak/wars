16. Authority to Reopen and Reconsider

5 CFR 1201.118 (2022), provides:

Board reopening of final decisions.

Regardless of any other provision of this part, the Board may at any time reopen any appeal in which it has issued a final order or in which an initial decision has become the Board's final decision by operation of law. The Board will exercise its discretion to reopen an appeal only in unusual or extraordinary circumstances and generally within a short period of time after the decision becomes final.

In commentary accompanying the revised regulation on reopening, the Board commented, 77 Fed. Reg. 62349, 62359 (10/12/2012):

...The Board believes that reopening or reconsidering a final decision must be confined to rare and limited circumstances and that nothing in the proposed regulation conflicts with the grant of authority given to the MSPB under 5 U.S.C. 7701(e)(1).

Procedurally distinct from the PFR process, but serving a related purpose, is the Board's authority to reopen and reconsider initial decisions. The PFR procedure depends on one or the other party timely filing a review request and, at least in theory, the grounds for a PFR are quite narrow. The reopening procedure, defined at 5 CFR 1201.118 (2022), reserves to the Board the authority to "reopen an appeal and reconsider a decision of an AJ on its own motion at any time, regardless of any other provisions of [5 CFR Part 1201]." The authority is derived from 5 USC 7701(e)(1)(B), explaining that decisions on appeals become final if there is neither a timely PFR nor a decision by the Board to reopen and reconsider a case. If OPM is not a party or intervenor in an appeal, OPM is required to petition the Board for reconsideration of a decision that it seeks to have judicially reviewed. 5 USC 7703(d).

The statutory authority to reopen is reiterated by the Board's regulation, 5 CFR 1201.118 (2022), granting the Board authority to reopen and reconsider an AJ's decision at any time. The Board, like other administrative agencies, has inherent authority to reopen and reconsider its decisions on its own initiative provided that proper notice is given to the parties and the right is exercised within a reasonable time. Rysavy v. DHUD, 28 MSPR 263, 264 (1985); see Dawson v. MSPB, 712 F.2d 264, 266-67 (7th Cir. 1983) ("Upon our examination of the enabling statute and the Board's interpretative regulation, we are unable to find any language that places a time limit on the Board's authority to reopen a case on its own motion," citing Bookman v. United States, 453 F.2d 1263 (Ct. Cl. 1972)); Sutcliff v. OPM, 86 MSPR 101, 104-05 ¶ 7 (2000) (reopening to avoid a manifest injustice arising out of an administrative error involving the appellant's military service deposit and annuity entitlement); Fields v. OPM, 79 MSPR 659, 661 ¶ 6 (1998) (reopening to prevent a manifest injustice to the appellant's substantive rights involving entitlement to survivor annuity benefits); Special Counsel v. Bradford, 69 MSPR 247, 248-49 (1995) (noting that the authority to reopen on the Board's own motion extends to its interlocutory decisions); Lary v. OPM, 68 MSPR 206, 210 (1995) ("We are empowered to reopen an appeal, even after a final decision has been issued, to correct our own errors or to modify our previous decision."); cf. Weaver v. Dept. of Navy, 2 MSPR 129, 132, 2 MSPB 297 (1980) (AJ's authority is delegated and the Board retains residual authority to review the initial decision).

The Board may reopen a case on its own volition. It may do so based on "unusual equitable or legal circumstances." Choco v. OPM, 69 MSPR 638, 641 (1996); see Shannon v. DHS, 100 MSPR 629, 637 ¶ 18 (2005) (Board reopened on its own to ensure consideration of a complex jurisdictional issue arising from an Individual Right of Action appeal). Although neither the regulation nor the statute specifically allows a party to seek reconsideration, most reconsideration requests come through motions filed by parties that are then considered by the Board and may result in a published decision whether or not to reopen the case. See Tarrant v. Dept. of Transp., 87 MSPR 308, 309 ¶ 1 (2000) (reopening after considering an appellant's request to reopen a final decision); Basco v. Dept. of Army, 67 MSPR 490, 491-92 (1995) (explaining that the Board considers motions to reopen). The Board may reopen an appeal to accept a settlement, or an amendment to the settlement, into the record. E.g., Dalaki v. Dept. of Army, AT-1221-20-0232-W-1 (NP 9/26/2022). Although the Board's regulation speaks in terms of reopening a decision of an AJ, the regulation has been construed to authorize reopening of final Board decisions as well. See Vesser v. OPM, 67 MSPR 239, 244 (1995); cf. Jay v. Dept. of Navy, 90 MSPR 635, 640 ¶ 9 (2001) (noting that Vesser was reopened to permit the Board to consider the effect of a court decision that reversed the Board's prior final decision but failed to address an appropriate remedy or to remand a case to the Board). Lincoln v. USPS, 113 MSPR 486, 491 ¶ 9, 2010 MSPB 64 (2010), restated some bases for reopening a final decision:

...The Board has authority to reopen, on its own motion, appeals in which it has rendered a final decision. 5 U.S.C. § 7701(e)(1)(B); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.118. However, absent unusual circumstances, such as misinformation or new and material evidence, the Board will not reinstate an appeal once it has been withdrawn. Nahoney v. U.S. Postal Service, 112 M.S.P.R. 93, ¶ 15 (2009). As discussed above, the appellant has neither introduced new evidence nor shown that her withdrawal was the result of misinformation. Nor do we discern any other extraordinary circumstances that might warrant reopening the appeal, such as an intervening court, Board, or OWCP decision, the discovery of fraud, or a potentially momentous change in the law. See Crumpler v. Department of Defense, 113 M.S.P.R. 94, ¶¶ 5-6 (2009); Anthony v. Office of Personnel Management, 70 M.S.P.R. 214, 219 (1996).

The authority to reopen and the limitations on that authority were also summarized in Anthony v. OPM, 70 MSPR 214, 218-19 (1996):

The Board has authority to reopen, on its own motion, appeals in which it has rendered a final decision. See 5 USC § 7701(e)(1)(B); 5 CFR § 1201.117; Golden v. U.S. Postal Service, 60 MSPR 268, 272 (1994); Stevenson v. Department of Defense, 55 MSPR 625, 629 (1992). Such authority is consistent with the general rule that administrative agencies have power to reconsider their own decisions as long as proper notice is given to the parties and the right is exercised within a reasonable period, measured in weeks, rather than years. See Anderson, 46 MSPR at 348; Marshall v. Government Printing Office, 43 MSPR 346, 350 (1990). In deciding whether to reopen, the Board will balance the desirability of finality against the public interest in reaching the right result. See Golden, 60 MSPR at 273. Reopening may be appropriate where there is clear and material legal error resulting in a conflict between the holding in a decision and controlling precedent or statute. See Tipsword v. Department of the Army, 66 MSPR 53, 55 (1994); Stevenson, 55 MSPR at 629. Similarly, reopening may be appropriate, in the interests of justice, where the evidence is of such weight as to warrant a different outcome. See Parkinson v. US Postal Service, 58 MSPR 393, 397 (1993), aff'd, 31 F.3d 1177 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table).

However, the Board's exercise of its authority to reopen an appeal has uniformly been held to be discretionary, and required only in unusual or extraordinary circumstances. See Lauer v. Department of Transportation, 65 MSPR 224, 226 (1994), citing Moriarty v. Rhode Island Air National Guard, 56 MSPR 144, 148-50 (1992); Blackmer v. Department of the Navy, 47 MSPR 624, 632 (1991). Moreover, the Board will exercise its discretion to reopen only if good cause is shown, such as an intervening event (court, Board or Office of Workers' Compensation Programs decision), or the discovery of misrepresentation or fraud after the issuance of the initial decision. See, e.g., Payne v. U.S. Postal Service, 69 MSPR 503, 506-07 (1996) (reversal of the appellant's conviction, after the Board's decision sustaining his removal based on the conviction, was sufficient to warrant reopening); Welber v. U.S. Postal Service, 62 MSPR 98, 103 (1994) (Office of Workers' Compensation Programs decision issued after Board's final decision was found to justify reopening); Baugh v. OPM, 49 MSPR 58, 62-63 (1991) (reopening found warranted where applicant for survivor annuity demonstrated that her former husband misrepresented that he had remarried). The Board will not reopen an appeal where the party requesting reopening fails to exercise due diligence under the particular circumstances of the case. See, e.g., Payne, 69 MSPR at 507; Guzy v. Department of the Interior, 64 MSPR 470, 472 (1994); Pearce v. Department of the Air Force, 52 MSPR 251, 252-53 (1992); Armstrong v. Office of Personnel Management, 36 MSPR 37, 41 (1987).

Timmers v. OPM, 101 MSPR 305, 310 ¶ 13, 2006 MSPB 47 (2006), reiterated the principles governing reopening appeals:

...The Board generally will not reopen an appeal to cure an untimely filing. See, e.g., Nelson v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 68 M.S.P.R. 1, 3 (1995), aff'd, 83 F.3d 1375 (Fed. Cir. 1996). However, where there are unusual equitable or legal circumstances, the Board may exercise its discretion to reopen an appeal. Guenther v. U.S. Postal Service, 68 M.S.P.R. 667, 670 (1995). Under appropriate circumstances, the Board may reopen an appeal to correct a clear and material legal error. Id. at 670-71; see Beck v. General Services Administration, 86 M.S.P.R. 489, ¶ 10 (2000) (reopening "may be appropriate when there is clear and material legal error, and a conflict between the holding of the decision and a controlling precedent, either because of oversight or a change in the controlling law between the date of the original decision and the reopening request"). Another way of stating this principle is that the Board may reopen an appeal to prevent "manifest injustice." Sutcliff v. Office of Personnel Management, 86 M.S.P.R. 101, ¶ 7 (2000); see Wright v. U.S. Postal Service, 183 F.3d 1328, 1332 (Fed. Cir. 1999) ("The board has broad discretion in deciding whether reconsideration of a decision is necessary to preserve consistency or achieve the 'right result.'") (citing Azarkhish v. Office of Personnel Management, 915 F.2d 675, 679 (Fed. Cir. 1990)).

Lange v. Dept. of Interior, 95 MSPR 227, 230 ¶ 5 (2003), added by way of a brief summary of reopening authority:

...In deciding whether to reopen a closed appeal, the Board will balance the desirability of finality against the public interest in reaching the correct result. Jay, 90 M.S.P.R. 635 ¶ 7; Moss, 82 M.S.P.R. 309 ¶¶ 8. The Board, however, will exercise its discretion to reopen only in unusual or extraordinary circumstances, such as an intervening event (court, Board or Office of Workers' Compensation Programs decision), or the discovery of misrepresentation or fraud after the issuance of the initial decision. Anthony, 70 M.S.P.R. at 219.

Authority to reopen an appeal is reserved to the Board. "Administrative judges lack the authority to reopen or reinstate appeals in which there has been a final Board decision; that authority is reserved to the Board." Pradier v. Lincoln v. If a case is remanded to an AJ, the Board may reopen its decision on a legal issue that it finally decided in the decision remanding the case to the AJ for further development. See Whittacre v. OPM, 120 MSPR 114, 2013 MSPB 71 (2013).

Searcy v. MSPB, 740 Fed. Appx. 988, 991-92 (Fed. Cir. 2018 NP), summarized reopening considerations:

...The Board has the authority to reopen or reconsider a final decision on its own motion "to correct its own errors or to modify its judgment, decree, or order." Golden v. U.S. Postal Serv., 60 M.S.P.R. 268, 272 (1994); see 5 U.S.C. § 7701(e)(1)(B). The Board's authority to reopen an appeal "must be exercised within a 'reasonable' amount of time, which the Board has held to be measured in weeks rather than years." Golden, 60 M.S.P.R. at 272. The Board has said reopening "may be appropriate where there is clear and material legal error resulting in a conflict between the holding in a decision and controlling precedent or statute." Deciding whether to reopen an appeal requires "'balancing the desirability of finality and the public interest in reaching what ultimately appears to be the right result.'" Jones v. OPM, 84 MSPR 504, 506 ¶ 6 (1999) ("Among other reasons, the Board may reopen a request to reach the 'right result.'"); Costin v. DHHS, 72 MSPR 525, 534 (1996) (reciting balance, summarizing cases); Lary v. OPM, 68 MSPR 206, 210 (1995) ("We are empowered to reopen an appeal, even after a final decision has been issued, to correct our own errors or to modify our previous decision.").

In Special Counsel v. Sullivan, 7 MSPR 357, 7 MSPB 239 (1981), the Board was asked by OSC to reconsider its dismissal of the charges brought against the respondent. The Board recited its understanding of the law. The federal courts uniformly hold that administrative tribunals have inherent or implicit authority to reconsider for correction of clerical or inadvertent ministerial errors. Beyond that authority, however, factors considered include the nature of the agency mission, the specific agency action in issue, the need for finality, detrimental reliance on the decision, and the availability of judicial review. If jurisdiction to reconsider is present, its exercise is discretionary and required only in extraordinary circumstances. The only standard uniformly accepted for reconsideration or rehearing is clear and material legal error. Even that criterion is generally confined to conflicts between the holding of the decision and a controlling precedent or statute--conflicts that develop because of oversight by the court or because of a change in the controlling law between the date of the original decision and the rehearing request. Courts are rarely persuaded to reconsider or rehear on the basis of new facts, even if the proffered facts are material and there is a showing of prior unavailability, despite due diligence. To warrant reconsideration the new evidence must be likely to substantially change the posture of the case; it is not sufficient that it updates the record or that it provides some new insight into the matters in issue. In light of these consistently stringent limitations on reconsideration and rehearing, petitions cannot be granted for reargument of matters, either factual or legal, deliberated and decided upon by the tribunal. On this matter, "rules, cases, and justices speak with one voice." Sullivan, 7 MSPR at 360 (citing Louisell and Degnan, Rehearing in American Appellate Courts, 44 Calif. L. Rev. 627, 635 (1956)). Getting down to specifics, the Board determined that OSC was essentially rearguing the weight that should be given to evidence in the case against Mr. Sullivan. Noting that 5 USC 1207(c) [now 5 USC 1215(a)(4)] precluded any administrative appeal in cases decided by the Board on OSC complaints, and determining that the OSC petition for reconsideration neither made any showing of clear legal error nor purported to present new evidence, the Board denied the petition.

Requests by parties for reconsideration of final Board decisions are not readily granted, as Mudrich v. USDA, 93 MSPR 313, 314-15 ¶ 2 (2003), implied:

While the Director of the Office of Personnel Management may petition for reconsideration of a Board final order within 35 days after the date of service, see 5 C.F.R. § 1201.119, there is no similar rule permitting other parties or nonparties to do so. Under the law of the case doctrine, a tribunal will not reconsider issues that have already been decided in an appeal. Camastro v. Department of Justice, 86 M.S.P.R. 267, 271 (2000); Griffin v. Office of Personnel Management, 75 M.S.P.R. 263, 269-70 (1997). There are, however, three exceptions to the doctrine: 1) the availability of new and substantially different evidence; 2) a contrary decision by controlling authority that is applicable to the question at issue; or 3) a showing that the prior decision in the same appeal was clearly erroneous and would work a manifest injustice. Id.

The authority to reopen is discretionary with the Board and "is required only in 'extraordinary circumstances,' such as clear and material error or the emergence of new facts likely to change substantially the posture of the case." Special Counsel v. Sullivan, 7 MSPR 357, 360, 7 MSPB 239 (1981); see New v. VA, 99 MSPR 404, 411-12 ¶ 15 (2005) ("The Board will exercise its discretion to reopen only in unusual or extraordinary circumstances, however, such as an intervening event that directly bears on the result or the discovery of misrepresentation or fraud after the issuance of the initial decision."); Murphy v. Dept. of Treasury, 91 MSPR 239, 245 ¶ 13 (2002) (Board will reopen on its own motion to address a clear legal error in the initial decision); Pastor v., VA, 87 MSPR 609, 613 ¶ 7 (2001) (despite the failure of the appellant to object to the prehearing ruling of the AJ precluding an IRA consequential damages award for future medical expenses, the Board reopens to correct "a clear and material legal error where that error appears to have prejudiced an appellant's substantive rights"); Long v. USPS, 46 MSPR 93, 94 (1990) ("Although the Board's regulations make no provision for a party...to request reconsideration of a final decision, where a party files an untimely petition for review, he may request that good cause be found for the delay....where jurisdiction to reconsider is present, its exercise is purely discretionary and is required only in 'extraordinary circumstances,' such as clear and material legal error or the emergence of new facts likely to change substantially the posture of the case."); cf. Robey v. USPS, 105 MSPR 539, 546 ¶ 21, 2007 MSPB 120 (2007) ("The appellant has not alleged that his original appeal was erroneously dismissed or that he withdrew his appeal because he was misled or misinformed, and he has not submitted any new and material evidence. Under these circumstances, we find no basis for reopening or reinstating the appellant's original appeal. See Duncan, 96 M.S.P.R. 448, ¶ 8.").

Compared to the number of appeals annually decided through PFR, very few appeals are reopened under 5 CFR 1201.118. The Board may reach out and revisit an AJ's decision; more often, the Board receives a petition to reopen from a party after the initial decision has become final. The petition to reopen may constitute a belated PFR directly challenging an initial decision, or a party may seek to reopen not because of a defect in the AJ's analysis, but because of a new development in the law or because of evidence recently discovered that suggests the initial decision was the product of perjured testimony or fraudulent documentation. The Board may reopen if, during its review of a PFR, it finds a defect in the AJ's analysis not raised by a party but that Board decides to address. Since the Board's authority to reopen is broad, there are many reasons advanced for reopening, but few cases are actually reopened. Many of the reasons advanced in petitions for reopening and reconsideration are the same reasons relied on in petitions for review. The subsequent treatment in this chapter of the bases for PFRs also includes commentary on cases adjudicated by the Board following its receipt of petitions for reopening.

Requests for reopening or reconsideration are filed frequently in some tribunals, yet they are rarely successful in reversing a prior result. As to appellants and agencies, it can generally be said that a request for reconsideration is unlikely to succeed. This is for good reason, as was explained in Tobias, Administrative Reconsideration, Some Recent Developments in New York, 28 NYU L. Rev. 1262, quoted with approval in Civil Aeronautics Board v. Delta Air Lines, Inc., 367 U.S. 316, 321 (1961):

Reexamination and reconsideration are among the normal processes of intelligent living. Admittedly no warranty of correctness or fitness attaches to a decision or an action simply because it is a thing of the past. Everyday experience teaches to the contrary: while the choice first made may well remain the course ultimately followed, often enough it is found on further consideration to require revision. On the other hand, constant reexamination and endless vacillation may become ludicrous, self-defeating and even oppressive. Whether for better or worse so far as the merits of the chosen course are concerned, a point may be reached at which the die needs to be cast with some "finality." An opposition may thus develop between the right result and the final one.

The decisions considering reconsideration requests by parties were summarized in Long v. USPS, 46 MSPR 93, 94 (1990), denying reconsideration. The parties to a case may request reopening, but requests for reconsideration made by a party to an appeal are rarely granted. See Gende v. DOJ, 25 MSPR 234, 234 (1984). The Board is not required to conduct a full review on the merits in each case in order to determine whether to reopen a case that has become final. Lacking a showing of exceptional circumstances, the Board's decision not to reopen a case cannot be deemed an abuse of discretion. Romero v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1995 NP No. 95-3278); see Landahl v. Dept. of Commerce (Fed. Cir. 2001 NP No. 01-3089) ("board did not abuse its discretion in refusing to sua sponte reopen its decision and consider the newly submitted evidence"). But see Timmers v. MSPB, 126 Fed. Appx. 482, 484-86 (Fed. Cir. 2005 NP) (requiring the Board to consider reopening to correct a manifest error in an annuity determination).

Occasionally the Board exercises its right to reopen on the merits of a charge, as in St. Clair v. VA, 59 MSPR 634, 635 (1993), when the Board denied an agency PFR from an AJ's decision to mitigate a penalty to a reprimand. In St. Clair, the appellant did not file a PFR, but the Board reopened the case to dismiss entirely the charge and the penalty. The Board's review authority extends to counsel fee awards. Kling v. DOJ, 2 MSPR 464, 468, 2 MSPB 620 (1980). The Board reopened to modify one decision to square with another that was issued earlier. See Grover v. Dept. of Treasury, 59 MSPR 58, 60 (1993) (harmonizing one case, involving interim relief, with another case, involving a compliance issue). In Battle v. USPS, 61 MSPR 509 (1994), the Board reopened on its own motion to correct an error by its AJ in the analysis of the appellant's veterans preference status. As a result of the Board's intervention, the appellant was able to pursue a Board appeal that was previously dismissed by the AJ. Battle reconfirmed that the Board has authority to reopen and reconsider a case on its own motion. The Board explained that the "process is proper for the present appeal, in which the Board's jurisdiction is implicated." Battle, 61 MSPR at 511. See LaMotta v. Dept. of Navy, 80 MSPR 225, 228 ¶ 7 (1998) ("The Board may reopen an appeal on its own motion to correct its own error, modify its judgment, decree, or order, or when reopening would be in the interests of justice."); Rivard v. Dept. of Interior, 30 MSPR 311, 312 n.* (1986) (reopening to determine jurisdiction when the parties were given proper notice and the appellant's PFR was pending before the Board).

One purpose of the reopening procedure, it was suggested, is to allow the Board a means to assure consistency between and among initial decisions. See Azarkhish v. OPM, 915 F.2d 675 (Fed. Cir. 1990); Ketchem v. Dept. of Transp., 11 MSPR 9, 10 (1982) (initial decision decided key issues involved in the appeal of an air traffic controller fired for striking; the appeal was reopened so that interested persons could comment on the issues, decided in the appeal, that would affect pending appeals of 10,900 other ATC appellants). The Board may also reopen "when the evidence is of such weight as to warrant a different outcome," and "[a] finding of fraud on the part of a party to an appeal might justify reopening." Parkinson v. USPS, 58 MSPR 393, 397 (1993); see Costin v. DHHS, 72 MSPR 525, 534 (1996) ("[A]n important consideration in deciding whether to reopen an appeal is whether the Board has become aware of new evidence that would warrant a different outcome from that reached in the previous Board decision.").

The Board announced that "[a] showing of circumstances casting doubt on the existence of a valid settlement...may form a basis for reopening a closed appeal without respect to the time limits of 5 CFR § 1201.114." Panther v. USDA, 52 MSPR 419, 422 (1992). But see Thompson v. NASA, 68 MSPR 135 (1995) (treating as a request to reopen and reconsider an appellant's submission of a PFR challenging compliance with the settlement; declining to reopen the settlement agreement that the appellant claimed was coerced when the appellant waited months after the Board accepted the agreement to raise a coercion allegation; remanding for treatment as an enforcement petition); cf. Saunders v. Dept. of Interior, 56 MSPR 671, 673-74 (1993) (party desiring to set aside an invalid settlement should file a PFR within the regulatory time limit, starting with discovery of the grounds for invalidating the agreement).

Cited in Panther was Barker v. Dept. of Navy, 34 MSPR 248, 252 (1987). Although not concerned with the same issue as Panther, Barker spoke of "special circumstances or strong equities in his favor" concerning the use of the reopening procedure in the place of an untimely PFR. Whatever its decisional rationale, both the statute and the regulations allow the Board to reopen and reconsider an AJ's decision at any time; this authority has been approved by reviewing courts. Knuckles v. Bolger, 654 F.2d 25 (8th Cir. 1981). Although the Board may use the reopening procedure to circumvent the failure of a party to file a PFR, it may decline to do so if it would place itself in a position adverse to the appellant, as, for example, in a disability retirement case. See Armstrong v. OPM, 36 MSPR 37, 41 (1987). The Board is less inclined to use the reopening procedure when the delay by a party seeking reopening has been unreasonable and when "considerations of finality dictated that [the] case not be reopened." See Turner v. VA, 59 MSPR 37, 39 n.2 (1993).

The Board used the reopening procedure to excuse the failure of an agency to describe adequately its interim relief efforts (initially resulting in the dismissal of its PFR). Stevenson v. DOD, 55 MSPR 625, 629, 633 (1992). In Stevenson, the Board majority opined that its use of the reopening procedure involved "balancing the desirability of finality and the public interest in reaching what ultimately appears to be the right result." Chairman Levinson dissented and noted in Stevenson that the agency did not carry its burden of explaining its compliance position and that the Board "is neither required to figure out what the agency has done when it does not appear to comply with interim relief, nor is it required to overlook that which the agency does not make clear on its petition for review."

The Board's refusal to reopen an appeal will not often be disturbed by the Federal Circuit. The court said that "it is not beyond question whether petitioner even has standing to contest the Board's declining to sua sponte reopen the appeal," but assuming that standing exists, a petitioner has a heavy burden to demonstrate that the Board abused its discretion by not reopening an appeal. Azarkhish v. OPM, 915 F.2d 675, 679 (Fed. Cir. 1990). But see McCarthy v. MSPB, 809 F.3d 1365, 1369-70 (Fed. Cir. 2016) (judicial review may be sought from the Board's denial of an initial request to reopen an appeal based on an intervening change in law). The policy goal behind 5 CFR 1201.117 [currently 5 CFR 1201.118 (2022)] "presumably is aimed at assuring consistency between and among initial decisions." Id. The procedure is not meant to obtain a right result for an individual whose appeal has not otherwise been timely prosecuted. See Nelson v. FDIC, 83 F.3d 1375 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (assumes, without deciding, that the court may review the Board's decision not to reopen an appeal on its own motion under 5 CFR 1201.118 for an abuse of discretion; a dissent by Judge Newman suggested that if the Board discusses the merits of the case as it denies a motion to reopen, the decision not to reopen should be reviewable as would be a Board final decision).
