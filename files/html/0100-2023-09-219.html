G. REPRESENTATIVES

Most appellants appear before the Board without counsel. All agencies are represented; most agency representatives are attorneys. According to the report Cases Decided by the U.S. Merit Systems Protection Board for Fiscal Year 1997, of the 7,223 cases adjudicated at the Board's regional and field offices in FY 97, private attorneys were used in 2,533 cases, and union counsel in another 40 cases. Appellants appeared without counsel at the rate of about 64%. Appellants represented themselves in 3,171 cases; union representatives appeared in 587 appeals; and "others" (presumably family, friends, coworkers or paid nonattorney representatives) provided representation in 891 cases. A sampling of 2016 initial decisions by AJs showed a pro se rate of about 50% and 30% attorney representation. Pro se cases took an average of 94 days to process, while cases involving appellants represented by counsel took an average processing time of 136 days. Although the results in individual cases vary based on the strength of the case and the quality of advocacy from whatever source it springs, in FY 97 reversal rates of initial appeals with private counsel reached 22%, while the reversal rate for pro se appellants amounted to 7.7%. Considering only cases decided at the PFR level for a single month in 2014, an informal review showed that of 209 cases decided for the month, 143 were handled pro se, or about 68%; 66 were handled through representatives, or about 32%. Of the cases handled by representatives, 38 cases appeared to have been presented through attorneys (the others being union representatives, relatives, friends or just plain folk), meaning that of the 209 decided cases, counsel for appellants were involved in about 18% of the appeals--a rather low number considering the economic importance to appellants of adverse action and retirement appeals. In the Federal Circuit, pro se representation is probably about 70-80% for civil service cases. For those individuals who lose appeals before the Board and blame the results on their representatives, the Board offers no comfort. The incompetency of one's representation is not a basis for reversal of an initial decision. See Sparks v. Dept. of Interior, 62 MSPR 369, 371 (1994) ("presence of inadequate counsel does not constitute a ground for reversal"); Snowden v. Dept. of State, 12 MSPR 487, 488 (1982) ("an appellant is deemed responsible for the action or inaction of his representative on appeal").

Whether measured at the regional or headquarters (PFR) level, pro se representation at the Board is the norm. Review of Federal Circuit decisions over the years shows a higher level of pro se representation in court than occurs before the Board. Why is this so? Likely it is a matter of finances. Representation is costly. Through whatever fee system a private sector lawyer uses, the lawyer needs to make a living, and few of them are starving. Representation is most costly at the hearing level, involving discovery, motion practice, prehearing submissions, and a hearing. Resources of many (often unemployed) appellants are exhausted at the initial stage (and they may well have been diluted in the reply process prior to the Board appeal), leaving little left to pay lawyers through successive waves of appellate litigation. Unlike some commercial and personal injury litigation, where the possibility of large verdicts or settlements makes contingency arrangements attractive, the amount of money involved in a backpay award is not likely to be sufficiently large to attract lawyers who are willing to undertake the risk of a negative result, and no recovery, balanced against a contingency recovery of portion of a limited backpay award. Some lawyers handle cases pro se, and they are worthy but few; others select cases where the a favorable outcome is likely and a statutory fee award is equally likely. Some (relatively) fortunate appellants have insurance to cover the cost of representation; others are in bargaining units with union counsel made available.

To provide some balance to an adjudicatory process involving so many pro se appellants, an AJ provides basic information about the hearing process and controlling decisions to the unrepresented appellant. Some of that information is provided through the acknowledgment order, by references to Board regulations, through status or settlement conferences, and through the prehearing conference and written summary of that conference. With or without formal or informal guidance from the AJ, the pro se appellant is at a tactical disadvantage in Board litigation involving litigation against a well-lawyered federal government. Nevertheless, appellants are not required to use representatives, who need not be attorneys, and some pro se appellants prevail (often through careful and impartial analysis of issues by AJs and the lawyers at Board headquarters who review PFRs).
