1. Pro Se Appellant

"I could be well content to be mine own attorney in this case." Shakespeare, King Henry VI (Part I, V, ii).

And many appellants are--their own attorney. About 65% of Board appeals are litigated by appellants without the benefits or burdens of counsel. [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Representatives," for the statistics.]

Board regulations, written by lawyers, are sufficiently ambiguous that considerable case law construes the regulations. Unrepresented, or pro se, appellants, although not always less informed about regulatory requirements than lawyers, are given some special consideration by the Board. The Judges' Handbook, Chapter 2, section 7 advises:

7. PRO SE APPELLANTS. The MSPB's policy is to make special efforts to accommodate pro se appellants. These efforts may include the following: the AJ may schedule a status conference early in the process to explain what will be required of the pro se appellant and to advise that the pro se appellant may contact the RO or FO with questions regarding procedural matters. Generally, the AJ should not reject filings by pro se appellants for failing to comply with technical requirements, unless the violations are repeated after a clear warning. The AJ ordinarily should not impose sanctions for failing to comply with an order unless the record establishes that the pro se appellant received instructions that a reasonable person, unfamiliar with Board procedures, would have understood. The AJ may allow greater latitude to the pro se appellant in questioning witnesses and in giving testimony. The AJ may allow some leading questions, and may need to instruct the pro se appellant regarding the correct method of questioning. The Board has stated, in this regard, that AJs "should provide more guidance to pro se appellants and interpret their arguments in the most favorable light." Miles v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 84 M.S.P.R. 418, 421 (1999).

"The Board demonstrates heightened sensitivity to pro se appellants where the administrative judge failed to set forth jurisdictional elements and burdens, and where the agency either failed to file a motion to dismiss or filed a motion or other submission that did not put the appellant on notice of his burden." Mesbah v. DOJ, 87 MSPR 491, 495 ¶ 8 (2001). Nevertheless, there must be notice of a claim. Although appellants who do not have the benefit of legal counsel need not plead issues with the precision required of an attorney in a judicial proceeding, "an appellant must provide sufficient notice of his or her claims so that the agency can prepare a response to the appeal." Washington v. Dept. of Navy, 75 MSPR 150, 153 (1997) (appellant's reference to 5 USC 1214(a)(3) and 1221, together with repeated references to retaliation, was sufficient to state a possible whistleblowing claim); see Ford v. OPM, DC-0831-16-0647-I-1 (NP 10/11/2022) (survivor annuity appeal: "Because this pro se appellant was not fully apprised of her burden on the dispositive issue and because the instant appeal concerns an application for civil service retirement benefits, see Irvine v. Office of Personnel Management, 83 M.S.P.R. 643, ¶ 9 n.1 (1999) (finding that the interest in reaching the correct result warranted some deviation from the strict adversarial model of adjudication in an OPM benefits case), we remand the appeal to the Washington Regional Office to give the appellant an additional opportunity to prove her claim in light of this Order.").

Miles v. VA, 84 MSPR 418, 421 ¶ 10 (1999), makes clear the AJ's affirmative duty to assist the pro se appellant in understanding substantive and procedural law:

The Board has held that administrative judges should provide more guidance to pro se appellants and interpret their arguments in the most favorable light. See Patterson v. U.S. Postal Service, 71 MSPR 332, 335 (1996) (Board interpreted appellant's petition for enforcement in the way most favorable to appellant), aff'd, 106 F.3d 425, 1997 WL 7287 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (Table); Harless v. Office of Personnel Management, 71 MSPR 110, 113 (1996) (Board and administrative judges have obligation to pro se appellants to inform them of what is needed to establish their eases); MSPB Judges' Handbook, Chapter 2--Reviewing the Appeal (the administrative judge ordinarily should not impose sanctions for failing to comply with an order unless the record establishes that the pro se appellant received instructions that a reasonable person, unfamiliar with Board procedures, would have understood)....

See Jeanquart v. VA, CH-0752-14-0565-I-1 (NP 8/17/2022) ("Although administrative judges are obligated to provide more guidance to pro se appellants and interpret their arguments in the most favorable light, see Miles v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 84 M.S.P.R. 418, ¶ 10 (1999), the affirmative responsibility to present relevant evidence is fundamentally that of the parties, Richardson v. Department of Justice, 11 M.S.P.R. 186, 195 (1982). Here, the administrative judge advised the appellant regarding the relevant issues and burdens of proof, and the appellant had the opportunity to present evidence at the hearing."); cf. Ottah v. Bracewell LLP, ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2022 NP) ("For a pro se litigant, the pleadings must be 'construe[ d]...broadly, and interpret[ed]..."to raise the strongest arguments that they suggest."' Cruz v. Gomez, 202 F.3d 593, 597 (2d Cir. 2000) (quoting Graham v. Henderson, 89 F.3d 75, 79 (2d Cir. 1996)). But a pro se litigant's factual allegations must still 'be enough to raise a right to relief above the speculative level.' Twombly, 550 U.S. at 555.").

AJs provide information to appellants through their acknowledgment orders, during status and prehearing conferences, and in their prehearing orders. They may also do so during hearings. [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Judge Must Ensure Jurisdictional Issues Are Identified; Burgess Notice" and "Jurisdictional Issues."]

The Board explained some boundaries governing assistance by an AJ to a pro se, Durham v. CFTC, DC-1221-08-0791-W-2 (NP 12/17/2010):

While the appellant is correct that administrative judges are obligated to provide more guidance to pro se appellants and interpret their arguments in the most favorable light, see Miles v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 84 M.S.P.R. 418, ¶ 10 (1999), the affirmative responsibility to present relevant evidence is fundamentally that of the parties, see 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c); Richardson v. Department of Justice, 11 M.S.P.R. 186, 195 (1982); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(a) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b)] . Here, the appellant had the opportunity to present extensive testimonial and written evidence at the hearing, and we discern no abuse of discretion by the administrative judge by not acting as the appellant's advocate.

The appellant also asserts that she was prejudiced by the administrative judge's failure to instruct her about the need for sworn testimony. We find, however, that the appellant was made aware that, given the hundreds of unorganized pages she submitted, for evidence and argument to be considered by the administrative judge the appellant was required to provide sworn testimony or introduce at hearing any exhibit on which she was relying. Therefore, we discern no error by the administrative judge with respect to this issue.

See Webster v. VA, DE-1221-16-0091-W-1 (NP 9/8/2016) ("Although administrative judges are obligated to provide more guidance to pro se appellants and interpret their arguments in the most favorable light, see Miles v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 84 M.S.P.R. 418, ¶ 10 (1999), the affirmative responsibility to present relevant evidence is fundamentally that of the parties, Richardson v. Department of Justice, 11 M.S.P.R. 186, 195 (1982). Here, the appellant was advised regarding the relevant issues and burdens of proof and had the opportunity to present evidence at the hearing.").

Assistance to the pro se appellant does not require a particular form of help during the hearing. The Board rejected the contention that the AJ should have proceeded by question and answer, rather than by narrative testimony, for a pro se, Powell v. Dept. of Army, SF-0752-10-0363-I-1 (NP 9/14/2010):

The appellant argues on petition for review that she wanted the administrative judge to ask her questions at the hearing so that she could respond, but the administrative judge failed to do so. However, the administrative judge did not abuse her discretion in not eliciting the appellant's testimony in a question and answer format. The administrative judge is vested with the discretion to regulate the course of the hearing as appropriate, see 5 C.F.R. § 1201.41(b)(6), and she permitted the appellant to testify on her own behalf for more than two hours, Hearing Compact Disc (HCD) (testimony of the appellant). Moreover, even if the administrative judge should have elicited the appellant's testimony in a question and answer format, the appellant has not explained what additional evidence that procedure would have uncovered, or why it would otherwise warrant a different outcome from that of the initial decision. See Karapinka v. Department of Energy, 6 M.S.P.R. 124, 127 (1981) (the administrative judge's procedural error is of no legal consequence unless it is shown to have adversely affected a party's substantive rights).

Although AJs provide guidance on governing law and Board procedure to pro se appellants through their acknowledgment orders, orders to show cause, and prehearing conferences and summaries (as well as comments during hearings), the pro se is responsible for the conduct and outcome of his or her case. Gatling v. DHS, SF-0752-08-0601-C-2 (NP 4/12/2011) ("it is well established that an appellant who chooses to proceed pro se must accept the consequences of that decision. See Yanopoulos v. Department of the Navy, 796 F.2d 468, 470 (Fed. Cir. 1986); Brum v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 109 M.S.P.R. 129, ¶ 5 (2008)"); see Graham v. Dept. of Army, DC-1221-15-0661-W-3 (NP 5/4/2023) ("The consideration the Board affords pro se litigants as they pursue their appeals does not extend to a less strict interpretation of the law."); Arufe v. Dept. of Interior, SF-0752-16-0217-I-1 (NP 10/6/2022) ("Having been informed of his right to obtain representation, and choosing to proceed pro se, the appellant is responsible for the consequences of that decision, see, e.g., Brum v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 109 M.S.P.R. 129, ¶ 5 (2008). Moreover, such confusion was not due to any misleading or incorrect information provided by the Board. The administrative judge's order specifically notified the appellant of potential affirmative defenses and their applicable burdens of proof, and the appellant could have sought additional clarification of the order to the extent necessary."); cf. Morton v. SSA, AT-0752-14-0806-I-1 (NP 5/27/2015) ("Although the administrative judge's duties include ensuring development of the record on significant issues, see 5 C.F.R. § 1201.41(b)(5)(ii), the appellant offers no authority to support the assertion that the administrative judge bears the responsibility for independently investigating agency functions."). The Federal Circuit commented on the responsibility of a pro se appellant to follow Board rules, Patacca v. Dept. of Navy, 766 Fed. Appx. 986, 993-95 (Fed. Cir. 2019 NP):

The AJ's decision to grant or deny an extension of time is reviewed for an abuse of discretion. See Koch v. SEC, 48 Fed. Appx. 778, 783 (Fed. Cir. 2002) (citing Johnson v. Dep't of the Treasury, 721 F.2d 361, 364-65 (Fed. Cir. 1983)). Such discretion must include consideration of the efforts of aggrieved persons to obtain the assistance of counsel. In turn, the Board's regulatory requirement for consultation with the opponent is not unreasonable. The government stresses that despite her pro se status, Ms. Patacca remains subject to the Board's procedural regulations. See, e.g., Triestman v. Fed. Bureau of Prisons, 470 F.3d 471, 477 (2d Cir. 2006) (explaining that despite being entitled to a "special solicitude," a party's "pro se status does not exempt a party from compliance with relevant rules of procedural and substantive law" (internal quotation marks omitted)).

We give special attention to pro se litigants efforts to obtain representation. However, Ms. Patacca does not discuss her attempts to obtain counsel or provide any explanation of her non-compliance with the Board's regulations. On the record presented, we conclude that the AJ acted within his discretion in adhering to the hearing schedule.

Although it is imperative to administrative and judicial fairness that pro se participants have adequate opportunity to present their side of the case, here the AJ administered reasonable rules, which had been identified to Ms. Patacca. It has not been shown that the AJ acted unreasonably or unfairly. See Davis v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 918 F.2d 944, 946 (Fed. Cir. 1990) ("A determination whether the testimony of witnesses should be presented is within the sound discretion of the AJ.").
