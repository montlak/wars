broida mspb Chapter 16: Settlement, Remedies, and Enforcement Chapter 16: Settlement, Remedies, and Enforcement, Subchapter 03: Enforcement, Section A: Enforcement of MSPB Decision, Subsection 06: Reinstatement to the Same Position; Status Quo Ante Remedy, Part l: Other Working Conditions
l. Other Working Conditions

Although most complaints concerning compliance involve restoration of pay or duties, collateral working conditions may be subject to litigation. In Payne v. USPS, 77 MSPR 97, 101 (1997), the appellant asserted that he should have been restored to a private office he occupied before the agency's unsuccessful adverse action. The Board declined the remedy:

[T]he appellant requests that she be given a private office, since, prior to her demotion, she had a private office. The appellant argues that the status quo ante remedy includes providing her with a private office. We disagree. When the Board corrects a wrongful personnel action, it is required to ensure that the employee is returned, as nearly as possible to the status quo ante. Duffrin v. Department of Transportation, 70 MSPR 557 (1996). This principle applies not only where an action has been ordered canceled, but also where, as here, the penalty has been mitigated. Gomez v. Department of Agriculture, 68 MSPR 202, 205 (1995). However, return to the status quo ante requires that the employee be placed back in his or her former position or in a position substantially equivalent in scope and status to the former position, specifically, an employee who prevails in an appeal to the Board is entitled to be returned to a position of the same grade, pay, status, and tenure. Duffrin v. Department of Transportation, 70 MSPR 557. Because the appellant did not introduce evidence below showing that she was harmed by not having a private office, she has not established that the lack of a private office falls within the status quo ante remedy. See Smith v. U.S. Postal Service, 62 MSPR 417 (1994) (in the absence of a loss of pay differential or other evidence of harm, an agency has discretion to change a returning appellant's work schedule).

See Nielson v. Dept. of Navy, 37 MSPR 324, 328 n.2 (1988) (directing restoration of the appellant to the same status and conditions he previously enjoyed "[a]ppellant alleges, without dispute, that he has been relocated to a significantly smaller office, denied the private secretary and support staff he was once assigned, denied attendance at various weekly meetings, and otherwise denied the trappings of his former position and responsibilities").

At issue in the enforcement litigation in Gorny v. Dept. of Interior, 115 MSPR 520, 524-25 ¶¶ 9-14, 2011 MSPB 14 (2011), was whether the appellant, a public affairs specialist, on restoration to work, should have been furnished an office with a window, as she occupied before her removal, and, with or without regard to the window, whether the agency had placed the appellant into an office in an inappropriate location. A room with a view was not a required element of restoration of the status quo, but the agency erred by restoring appellant to an office location that interfered with appellant's ability to perform her work:

The appellant alleges that the agency has failed to fulfill its duty to restore her as nearly as possible to the status quo ante in regard to her physical office location. The appellant challenges two aspects of her office location: (a) it is inferior to the office she occupied before her removal, as her prior office had a window, and (b) it is separated from the Office of Communications, on a different floor, which "communicates to other BLM employees that she is still not an accepted member of the office."

The Board takes guidance from its analysis in Payne v. U.S. Postal Service, 77 M.S.P.R. 97 (1997), and Tubesing, 112 M.S.P.R. 393. In Payne the Board found the appellant was not entitled to be returned to her private office as part of a status quo ante remedy, when she failed to introduce evidence that she was harmed by not having a private office. 77 M.S.P.R. at 101. In Tubesing the Board re-affirmed that principle, holding that when an agency reinstates an appellant to duty, it has the discretion to change her work schedule, in the absence of a pay differential or other evidence of harm. 112 M.S.P.R. 393, ¶ 19.

In the case at hand, the appellant has not shown how she has been harmed by not having a window office. Thus, she has not established that a window office falls within the status quo ante remedy, and we decline to order such a remedy. Id.

The appellant has, however, demonstrated harm by being physically separated on a different floor from the Office of Communications. One of the bases for the administrative judge's finding that the agency failed to place the appellant in a position equivalent to her former position was that the agency had placed her in "a separate area from all of the other Public Affairs Specialists." The administrative judge found that "her exclusion from the Office of Communications has resulted in her isolation from all of the other Public Affairs Specialists who actually perform public affairs duties." The agency's submission in response to the administrative judge's Recommendation does not dispute this finding, but it also does not provide any evidence that it has physically relocated her. We agree with the administrative judge's Recommendation on this issue, and because the agency has not submitted evidence of its compliance here, we find the agency to be in noncompliance.

The appellant reports, and does not dispute, a claim by the agency that it is conducting a "space analysis" to accommodate more individuals in the Office of Communications. However, pending completion of the agency's space analysis and implementation of any office-wide changes, the appellant asks to be returned to her prior office or a comparable office. The appellant suggests that the agency could accomplish returning her to her prior office or a comparable office, by moving any current occupant to an office that is used by interns, and by moving any such interns to her current office, which is physically separated from the Office of Communications. The agency has not filed any evidence contradicting the appellant's factual assertions on this issue.

The Board notes that the displacement of other individuals is sometimes necessary to afford an appellant, as nearly as possible, status quo ante relief. Cf. Taylor v. Department of the Treasury, 43 M.S.P.R. 221, 227 (1990) (rejecting agency argument that it would be unduly disruptive to return an appellant to her former position because it would displace the current incumbent). For the agency to come into compliance on this issue, the agency will need to submit evidence that it has restored the appellant to office space that is in the same physical area as her co-workers and that is commensurate with the office space of her co-workers of similar status and responsibilities.

If a removal for failure to maintain a weapons certification is reversed for lack of proper familiarization training, on reinstatement, assuming the same certification requirement, the appellant is entitled to the training. Cloude v. Dept. of Navy, 83 MSPR 184, 186 ¶ 7 (1999) (under Kerr, placing the appellant in the status quo means the restoration of the situation, as nearly as possible, to what it would have been had there been no wrongful personnel action). If a criminal investigator would normally carry a particular type of badge and credentials, and be authorized to carry a weapon, restoration of that individual should entail the same authorities. See Black v. DOJ, 85 MSPR 650, 653-54 ¶¶ 6-8 & n.2 (2000) (Board has jurisdiction under 5 USC 1204(a)(2) to order the appellant's reinstatement with all of the privileges of the position, including Special Agent credentials and a Special Agent badge; the "fact that the appellant can carry out the duties of her current assignment...without a firearm does not indicate that the privilege is unimportant as it affects her ability to return to the type of street duty assignments she had prior to her removal"); cf. Vaher v. VA, NY-0752-12-0174-C-1 (NP 2/25/2015) (settlement agreement calling for restoration of the status quo, followed by inability of agency to locate and return the appellant's original credentials, but substituted credentials were provided: "Placing the appellant in status quo ante means restoring him, as nearly as possible, to the situation he would have been in had the wrongful personnel action not occurred. Tubesing v. Department of Health & Human Services, 112 M.S.P.R. 393, ¶ 5 (2009). While the Board does not require perfect consistency with all aspects of an appellant's pre- and post-removal positions, a return to status quo ante requires that the agency place the appellant in a position with all the essential privileges of his previous position. Black v. Department of Justice, 85 M.S.P.R. 650, ¶ 6 (2000).").

A Blackberry was not a component of the status quo on restoration, Tubesing v. DHHS, 112 MSPR 393, 401 ¶ 20, 2009 MSPB 192 (2009):

The administrative judge also found that the agency's failure to provide the appellant with a government "Blackberry" did not constitute noncompliance. In his objections, the appellant argues that assignment of a "Blackberry" is necessary for restoration to the status quo ante, is required for performing the public health advisor position, and is customary for field assignees in the branch of the agency containing the public health advisors. The appellant's argument is misplaced, however, because as noted above, he has been assigned to a different position in a different branch of the agency. In any event, in the absence of a demonstrated harm, we are aware of nothing requiring an agency to provide a "Blackberry" to an employee as part of status quo ante relief. Cf. Payne v. U.S. Postal Service, 77 M.S.P.R. 97, 101 (1997) (finding the agency in compliance where an employee was not provided a private office upon reinstatement even though she had such an office prior to the wrongful removal action).

In Tubesing, supra, the Board declined to intervene in a post-restoration dispute over a schedule adjustment, but the Board remanded the case for consideration of whether the appellant's telework arrangement was commensurate with other employees in the position he occupied. As to the schedule adjustment, Tubesing, supra, concluded, 112 MSPR 393, 401 ¶ 19:

In her compliance recommendation, the administrative judge found that the appellant was not necessarily entitled to a maxiflex schedule. In his submission to the Board, the appellant objects to the administrative judge's finding and argues that restoration of a maxiflex schedule is necessary for status quo ante relief and for the appellant to perform the duties of the public health advisor position. The appellant's argument is inapposite because the Board has held that when an agency reinstates an appellant to duty, it has the discretion to change his work schedule in the absence of a loss of pay differential or other evidence of harm. See Conaway v. U.S. Postal Service, 93 M.S.P.R. 6, ¶ 15 (2002); Smith v. U.S. Postal Service, 62 M.S.P.R. 417, 423 (1994). The appellant has not shown how he has been harmed in his current position by changing his work schedule. In sum, we conclude that the agency is in compliance in this regard.
