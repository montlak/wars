14. PFR of Fee Decision

A PFR of a fee decision must be submitted, if at all, within 35 days of the date of the AJ's determination (subject to mailing delays allowed for in the regulation governing PFR filings). 5 CFR 1201.203(g) (2022); 5 CFR 1201.114(e) (2022). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Supplemental or Corrected Motion"; refer to Chapter 5 for extensive discussion of petitions for review and reopening.]

The normal rules apply to PFRs in counsel fee cases. The AJ's decision, favoring one side or another, carries no weight other than that reflected by accurate factual and legal analysis. See Phillips v. Dept. of Transp., 30 MSPR 356, 359 (1986) ("[t]he fact that the agency prevailed before the presiding official and the regional director is not, in our view, a sufficient basis for finding that an award of attorney fees is not warranted."). If there is an error made by the AJ in his adjudication of the fee petition, the error should be raised on PFR; the error cannot be raised, without a cross-PFR, in the response to an opponent's PFR on the fee issue. Sandland v. GSA, 28 MSPR 260, 260 n.* (1985). New argument and new evidence cannot ordinarily be considered for the first time in the PFR. The rule applies to appellant and agency alike. See Taylor v. DOJ, 38 MSPR 398, 401 (1988). The Board occasionally dispenses its own brand of justice: "in deciding whether fees are warranted in the interest of justice, the Board may look beyond the parties' pleadings and the judge's analysis." Vann v. Dept. of Navy, 38 MSPR 411, 415 (1988) (considering factors in the "interest of justice" formulation). Applying Vann, SSA v. Price, 94 MSPR 337, 346 ¶ 22 (2003), aff'd, Price v. SSA, 398 F.3d 1322 (Fed. Cir. 2005), determined that "[w]here there is no finding of a prohibited personnel practice in an underlying appeal, the Board will not grant attorney fees on that basis."

The AJ may deny fees on the basis of a perceived lack of legal entitlement. Reasonableness of the rates and hours claimed is not reached. Should the Board find that fees are warranted as a matter of law, it favors a remand to the AJ for adjudication of the reasonableness of the fees and expenses. Evans v. OPM, 67 MSPR 357, 360 (1995) ("Generally, the administrative judge who decided the appeal is in the best position to evaluate documentation submitted by counsel in determining the reasonableness of the attorney fees requested and the quality of legal representation provided by counsel."); Feller v. OPM, 39 MSPR 437, 441 (1989) ("The appellant asks the Board to remand this case to enable the administrative judge to rule on the reasonableness of her requested fees and expenses. We agree that remand is appropriate in this case. As the Board stated in Sprenger v. Department of the Interior, 34 M.S.P.R. 664, 669 (1987), the administrative judge who decided the case on the merits is in the best position to evaluate the documentation submitted by counsel to determine whether the amount requested is reasonable and to assess the quality of the representation afforded by counsel. Because the administrative judge did not consider whether the requested fees were reasonable the Board lacks the benefit of his evaluation as to either the rates charged or the hours expended."). If the record is adequate, however, the Board may decide the contest over fees without remand. See Thomas v. USPS, 86 MSPR 635, 644 ¶ 18 (2000); Thompson v. Dept. of Navy, 83 MSPR 509, 513 ¶ 13 (1999) ("The itemized charges and hours claimed by the appellant were not challenged by the agency and they appear reasonable on their face."). If there is no hearing on the fee dispute, under Howard v. OPM, 79 MSPR 172, 174-75 ¶ 7 (1998), the determinations on fee motions may be made without deference to the AJ.
