e. Defenses

One defense is denial of an alleged threat or assault. Resolution of assault charges may depend on conflicting evidence. To survive appellate review, the evidence must have substance. The Board must be swayed by the weight of the evidence rather than the nature of the charges. McCann v. Dept. of Treasury, 852 F.2d 1294 (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP), reversed a removal based on alleged sexual assault for lack of substantial evidence. The court discounted four-year-old identification testimony and faulted the Board for failing to consider other evidence detracting from the identifications. The length of time between the attacks and the initial identification by the victims, combined with the inaccuracies in the descriptions given by the victims, and the age of the victims at the time of the attacks, made the identification unreliable.

The Board expressed its views on a defense of mental illness to an assault charge in McMillan v. USPS, 48 MSPR 590, 590-92 (1991), although the Board did not address the question of disability accommodation (perhaps because the issue was not raised):

[W]e conclude that the medical evidence does not support a finding that the appellant's misconduct in assaulting the supervisors was caused by his mental illness....

In his letter, Dr. Blackwell stated that the appellant had a flare-up of his mental illness, described as "paranoid schizophrenia, in partial remission," when he disobeyed the direct order and assaulted supervisors on the job. In addition, the record contains an opinion from Dr. Karl E. Tomlinson, who stated that the appellant was suffering from "an acute psychotic episode" when he disobeyed the order and assaulted the supervisors.... We find, however, that these reports are too conclusory to credit. They assert that the appellant was deranged when he assaulted his supervisors but they offer no clinical findings or explanation to support that conclusion....

In any event, the statutory provisions of 5 USC 7513 permit removal "for such cause as will promote the efficiency of the service." They do not require that charged misconduct result from wrongful intent. See Murray v. Department of the Army, 40 MSPR 250, 255-56 (1989). The agency did not charge that the appellant "intentionally" assaulted his supervisors. Hence, in order to sustain the charge, it was not necessary to establish that the assault resulted from the appellant's wrongful intent....

An unusual decision considered whether an appellant established a defense of mental disability against a charge that involved threats against a supervisor. Her conduct seemed aberrant; she dressed and acted "strangely" on the day of the incident; her conduct and past history of hospitalization should have raised the possibility with agency officials that the misconduct was due to a mental condition. These factors, among others, led the Board to reduce the penalty from removal to a 90-day suspension. The Board may have been suggesting that mental impairment may be a mitigating factor even if impairment does not rise to the level of a disability under EEO law. Cochran v. INS, 16 MSPR 343, 352-53 (1983); see Colon v. Dept. of Navy, 58 MSPR 190, 198 (1993), post; Wellman v. Dept. of Navy, 49 MSPR 149, 153 (1991) (suspension is generally appropriate for physical altercations involving no serious injury or use of weapons, when the employee has a history of satisfactory performance, when there is no prior disciplinary record, where there is an element of provocation, and where there are other mitigating factors such as unusual job tensions; mitigation was directed, in part, because the evidence suggested that an altercation may have been related to the appellant's medical condition of depression and anxiety).

It is proper in an assault case for the AJ to determine whether the appellant was acting within the scope of his duties. See Tatum v. DOJ, 21 MSPR 583, 584 (1984) (charge of physical abuse of aliens). Allman v. Dept. of Air Force, DA-0752-09-0562-I-1 (NP 7/2/2010), determined that assaultive behavior would be evaluated on the basis of charges of violation of agency regulations rather than on external law argued by the appellant to avoid the charge:

The agency removed the appellant, a Criminal Investigator with the Air Force Office of Special Investigation (AFOSI), for serious misconduct and behavior unbecoming a Federal employee on the basis that he "physically and emotionally abused approximately seven (7) suspected Taliban detainees during interrogations," while he was deployed to Kandahar Air Base, Afghanistan. In proposing the removal, the agency cited to the appellant's signed statement of acknowledgment and understanding regarding Guidance Memorandum 10-01, AFOSI Professional Responsibilities in Detainee Interviews and Interrogations.

In his petition for review, the appellant argues that the administrative judge incorrectly found that he was not privileged to engage in hostilities pursuant to the Law of Armed Conflict. He asserts that his immunity as a lawful combatant bars the agency from disciplining him as a result of his lawful acts of war and that his "behavior, as a matter of law, must be assessed based upon the [Law of Armed Conflict], and not the agency's internal rules, which apply in peacetime domestic operations." However, the appellant offers no support for his assertion that the agency's rules only apply in peacetime domestic operations and his claim conflicts with the plain text of those rules. Guidance Memorandum 10-01, by its own terms, required the appellant's compliance with protocols like the Law of Armed Conflict and the Geneva Convention that are inapplicable in domestic peacetime situations. Further, the administrative judge quoted the deciding official's testimony that the agency's standards for conducting interrogations during wartime did not differ from the standards applicable at other times. Thus, the appellant's assertion that the agency's internal rules did not apply to the charged conduct is without merit.

In addition, the appellant claims that his actions were in self-defense or in the defense of others, asserting that the detainees overtly threatened him and another employee with death, informing him "that they had 'spotters' on the convoy route back to Kandahar Air Base, [and] that the convoy would be attacked...." He asserts that he only used such force as was "necessary and proper" to elicit the spotters' location and redirect the convoy, and that his conduct was authorized under the Authorization for Use of Military Force passed by Congress just after September 11, 2001. [S] ee Authorization for Use of Military Force, Pub. L. 107-40, Sept. 18, 2001, 115 Stat. 224, also found at 50 U.S.C. § 1541 note.

In another case in which the Board affirmed a charge of conduct unbecoming, the Board found that the law under which that appellant asserted her claim of self-defense was immaterial to the charge. Mahan v. Department of the Treasury, 89 M.S.P.R. 140, ¶ 6 (2001). In Mahan, the appellant claimed self-defense in response to the agency's charge that she engaged in conduct unbecoming an agency employee when she fired her service revolver in the general direction of her husband. Id. The assigned administrative judge rejected the appellant's claim of self-defense and the appellant argued on review that the administrative judge erred by failing to apply the criminal law of Tennessee to her claim of self-defense. Id. The Board found the appellant's argument without merit because the agency did not charge that appellant with committing a crime or violating Tennessee criminal law, but had instead charged her with conduct unbecoming an agency employee. Id.

Similarly, in the instant matter, the agency charged the appellant with serious misconduct and behavior unbecoming a federal employee. Because the agency did not base its charge on other sources of law, they are immaterial to the appeal. See Mahan, 89 M.S.P.R. 140, ¶ 6. Thus, the appropriate measure by which to determine if the specified conduct was unbecoming a federal employee is the agency's internal rules regarding its employees' conduct during the activity at issue, the interrogation of detainees, i.e., Guidance Memorandum 10-01, AFOSI Professional Responsibilities in Detainee Interviews and Interrogations.

For a decision requiring a showing of intent as to assault, refer to Smith v. Dept. of Air Force, 48 MSPR 594, 599 (1991) ("Willfulness, while not specifically charged in the notice of proposed removal, is clearly implied in the detailed specifications thereto.").
