2. Pleading Requirements; Scope of Review

A regulation or implementation review request is filed with the full Board (through the Clerk); the case is not filed initially with a Board regional or field office. If the case is first filed with a regional or field office, the assigned AJ transmits the regulation or implementation challenge to the Board's clerk for docketing as a regulation review petition under 5 USC 1204(f). See Smith v. OPM, 79 MSPR 199, 201 (1998) (noting "The administrative judge forwarded Petitioner's pleadings to the Clerk of the Board for docketing as a regulation review").

Review proceedings challenging OPM regulations can only be triggered by review requests that specifically identify the OPM rule or regulation at issue and describe the prohibited personnel practice that results from application or implementation of the rule or regulation. See Prewitt v. MSPB, 133 F.3d 885, 887 (Fed. Cir. 1998) (the appellant failed to specify the OPM regulations he was challenging or how those regulations required agency employees to commit prohibited personnel practices); Thoms v. OPM, 103 MSPR 652, 655 ¶ 9, 2006 MSPB 314 (2006) ("even if petitioner's allegations are given the broadest reading to state an allegation of invalid implementation of part 310 of 5 C.F.R., they do not explain why OPM's failure to promulgate a regulation directing federal hiring officials to gather information about job applicants' relationship to current public officials requires an employee, or has required an employee, to commit a prohibited personnel practice. Because petitioner has failed to specifically allege how the regulations in question would require or have required employees to commit a prohibited personnel practice, he has failed to state a claim under 5 U.S.C. § 1204(f). In light of our conclusion, we find it unnecessary to address OPM's arguments regarding petitioner's failure to meet the Board's prudential criteria for review"); Caracciolo v. OPM, 102 MSPR 140, 142 ¶ 4, 2006 MSPB 137 (2006) ("Thus, 5 U.S.C. § 1204(f), and 5 C.F.R. Part 1203 do not vest the Board with a general authority to review OPM regulations based merely on a bare assertion."); Brooks v. OPM, 94 MSPR 297, 299 ¶ 6 (2003) ("a petitioner alleging that a regulation or its implementation results in the commission of...a prohibited personnel practice must identify the merit system principle at issue and explain the way in which the regulation at issue implements or directly concerns that principle"); Tatro v. OPM, 93 MSPR 106, 107 ¶ 4 (2002) ("The petitioner has not identified the OPM guidance he challenges by citation or by any other means that would permit the Board to locate the guidance."); Bush v. OPM, 91 MSPR 415, 418 ¶ 7 (2002) (the petitioner must clearly identify the OPM rule or regulation under challenge; the petitioner must identify the prohibited personnel practice at issue); DiJorio v. OPM, 54 MSPR 498, 499-500 (1992) (considering the pleading requirements of 5 CFR 1203.11(b) (1992): "Mr. DiJorio's request does not specify an OPM regulation under challenge, and therefore fails to comply with subsection (ii). Similarly, his request fails to identify a prohibited personnel practice in issue as required by subsection (iv), nor does it contain a statement 'describing in detail' why any OPM regulation would require the commitment of a prohibited personnel practice as required by subsection (iii)."); Smith v. OPM, 79 MSPR 199, 202 (1998) (a review petition was dismissed for lack of a statement describing in detail why the implementation of regulations would require commission of a prohibited personnel practice); Alver v. OPM, 68 MSPR 174, 175 (1995) (without a statement identifying a prohibited personnel practice through implementation of an OPM regulation, a review request is beyond the Board's jurisdiction).

Mootness doctrines apply. If the agency acts quickly to correct an error in its implementation of a regulation, the Board may decline to entertain a petition for implementation review. See Fields v. Dept. of Treasury, 49 MSPR 345, 348 (1991) (no Board action upon finding an error by the agency in granting the appellant preference when it constructed a selection register for competitive appointment; the agency did not fill the position and advised the Board that it would not again use an improperly constructed register). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Moot, Withdrawn, and Waived Appeals," for mootness of appeals.]

The specificity required for obtaining review of OPM regulations and agency implementation of OPM rules was described in NTEU v. MSPB, 743 F.2d 895 (D.C. Cir. 1984). The Board provided more detail concerning pleading requirements in a regulation review proceeding in Hernandez v. OPM, 68 MSPR 196, 198 (1995):

Under 5 USC 1204(f), the Board has the authority to review OPM-issued regulations and their implementation. However, under 5 USC 1204(f)(2), the Board can declare a regulation invalid only if it determines that the regulation, on its face, or as implemented, would require an employee to commit a prohibited personnel practice under 5 USC 2302(b). In light of the statute's mandatory language, the Board's implementing regulations, at 5 CFR 1203.11(b)(1)(iii)-(iv), make clear that the individual requesting review must specifically describe how the regulation would require an employee to commit a prohibited personnel practice in enforcing the regulation, and must specifically identify the prohibited personnel practice in 5 USC 2302(b) that would be violated by the regulation or its implementation. See Hill v. Dept. of the Army, 59 MSPR 303, 304 (1993).

Ms. Hernandez's request does not specify, much less identify, a prohibited personnel practice that would be committed, nor does it contain a statement "describing in detail" why the OPM regulations would require the commission of a prohibited personnel practice, as required by 5 CFR 1203.11(b)(1)(iii). Under these circumstances, Ms. Hernandez has failed to allege a claim under 5 USC 1204(f)(2) or 5 CFR 1203.11(b), and her request is therefore beyond the Board's jurisdiction. See Di Jorio v. OPM, 54 MSPR 498, 499 (1992). Accordingly, the petitioner's request for regulation review is DISMISSED.

Jones v. OPM, 107 MSPR 115, 117-18 ¶¶ 5-6, 2007 MSPB 255 (2007), restated the pleading requirements in a regulation review case:

The Board has original jurisdiction to review rules and regulations issued by OPM. See 5 U.S.C. § 1204(f)(1); Clark, 95 F.3d at 1141-42. In exercising that jurisdiction, the Board has the "sole discretion" to grant a petition for regulation review "filed with the Board by any interested person." See 5 U.S.C. § 1204(f)(1)(B).

Moreover, under 5 U.S.C. § 1204(f)(2), the Board shall declare a regulation invalid only if it determines that such regulation, on its face, or as implemented, would require an employee to commit a prohibited personnel practice, as defined in 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b). See Prewitt v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 133 F.3d 885, 887 (Fed. Cir. 1998). In light of the statute's mandatory language, the Board's regulations, at 5 C.F.R. § 1203.11(b)(iii)-(iv), make clear that the individual requesting review must specifically describe how the regulation would require an employee to commit a prohibited personnel practice in enforcing the regulation, and must specifically identify the prohibited personnel practice in 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b) that would be violated by the regulation or its implementation. See Burnett v. Office of Personnel Management, 93 M.S.P.R. 225, ¶ 5 (2003). Thus, 5 U.S.C. § 1204(f), and 5 C.F.R. Part 1203 do not vest the Board with a general authority to review OPM regulations based merely on a bare assertion. See generally Hernandez v. Office of Personnel Management, 68 M.S.P.R. 196, 198 (1995).

Hed v. OPM, 70 MSPR 361, 363 (1996), involving a challenge to an OPM regulation, 5 CFR 351.501(d)(2) (1996), again summarized the pleading requirements:

Mr. Hed's request does not specify a prohibited personnel practice in issue, nor does it contain a statement "describing in detail" why the OPM regulation would require the commitment of a prohibited personnel practice, as required by 5 CFR 1203.11(b)(1)(iii). Despite the fact that OPM's response pointed out that these are the requirements of Board regulation review, the petitioner's subsequent submission failed to mention the specific prohibited personnel practice under section 2302(b) that the regulation, 5 CFR 351.501(d)(2), would violate and merely reiterated that OPM's regulation was erroneous and inconsistent with the statute. Under these circumstances, Mr. Hed has failed to allege a claim under 5 USC 1204(f)(2) or 5 CFR 1203.11(b), and his request is therefore beyond the Board's jurisdiction.[*] See DiJorio v. OPM, 54 MSPR 498, 499-500 (1992).

[*]  Because of this jurisdictional determination, the Board cannot review the merits of the petitioner's arguments.

The petition for regulation implementation review must identify how a regulation on its face, or as implemented, requires an employee to commit a prohibited personnel practice. Alver v. OPM, 75 MSPR 465, 468 (1997). The appellant must make plain his complaint, Hill v. Dept. of Army, 59 MSPR 303, 304 & n.* (1993):

Under its original jurisdiction, the Board has, within its sole discretion, the power "to review rules and regulations issued by OPM (Office of Personnel Management), including whether they are implemented by an agency in such a way as would require an employee to commit a prohibited personnel practice as defined by 5 USC 2302(b)." See Welber v. OPM, 52 MSPR 23, 25 (1991) (citing 5 USC 1204(a)(4) and (f) and 5 CFR 1203.1, et seq.)[*] ; see also 5 CFR 1203.12(a).

The appellant's request fails to comply with the regulatory requirements of 5 CFR 1203.11. Under this section, a party requesting Board review of an agency's implementation of an OPM regulation must specify in its request the OPM regulation under challenge, identify the prohibited personnel practice in issue, and describe in detail why the implementation of the regulation requires an employee to commit a prohibited personnel practice. See Di Jorio v. OPM, 54 MSPR 498, 499-500 (1992). Since the appellant has not identified any prohibited personnel practice in issue nor described why the agency's implementation of 5 CFR 335.103 requires an employee to commit a prohibited personnel practice, we find that dismissal is appropriate....

Of jurisdictional importance in an assertion of a prohibited personnel practice, not just a violation of merit system principle. See Fisher v. OPM, CB-1205-12-0021-U-1 (NP 1/30/2013) ("However, alleged violation of the merit system principles alone does not give rise to Board jurisdiction; rather, the petitioner must allege that the challenged regulation requires commission of a prohibited personnel practice, as defined in 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b). See DiJorio, 54 M.S.P.R. at 500; 5 C.F.R. § 1203.11(b). The merit system principles are hortatory and do not themselves impose legal requirements. See Brooks v. Office of Personnel Management, 59 M.S.P.R. 207, 212 (1993), superseded on other grounds as recognized in Johnson v. Office of Personnel Management, 93 M.S.P.R. 265, 269 n.5 (2003); 5 U.S.C. § 2301(b) ('Federal personnel management should be implemented consistent with the following merit system principles....') (emphasis added). Therefore, failure to adhere to the merit system principles is not by definition commission of a prohibited personnel practice under section 2302(b). See Brooks, 59 M.S.P.R. at 212. The petitioner's claim that the challenged regulation violates various merit system principles thus does not establish Board jurisdiction over his review request, unless he can identify a specific prohibited personnel practice committed by the agency.").
