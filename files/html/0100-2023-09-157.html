a. Retirement Appeals

Brown v. OPM, 86 MSPR 417, 420 ¶ 8 (2000), showed a modicum of compassion for the appellant in a disability retirement appeal, proceeding pro se, who was required to refile an appeal within the earlier of 30 days of the issuance of an OWCP decision or by August 19. The appellant refiled on August 24, a day after she received the OWCP decision. The AJ dismissed her appeal as untimely by five days. The Board directed reinstatement of the appeal:

In a case such as this where a pro se appellant's refiled appeal is untimely by only a few days, there is some indication of confusion by the appellant, and the decision dismissing the original appeal without prejudice set a relatively arbitrary refiling deadline and included an alternative refiling deadline, the Board will find good cause for the untimeliness. Our dismissal without prejudice practice, which appears to have served the interests of the appellant, OPM and the Board in this disability retirement case, should not become a trap that would deny the unwary pro se appellant of the opportunity to have her case decided on the merits. We, therefore, find the appellant has established good cause for the untimely filing of her refiled appeal. Further, OPM has not indicated nor shown that it will be prejudiced by waiver of the time limit in this appeal. See Alonzo, 4 MSPB 262, 4 MSPR at 184.

Kjeldsen v. OPM, CH-831M-07-0395-I-4 (NP 3/28/2012), displaying some leniency toward belated DWOP refilings in cases involving retirement matters, differentiated appeals involving annuity entitlement and those involving a collateral component of an annuity already granted, e.g., an overpayment dispute:

The appellant also argues that the Board has made an exception for retirement cases, which are substantially different from adverse action cases as they are less adversarial, citing Edney v. Office of Personnel Management, 79 M.S.P.R. 60, ¶¶ 5-6 (1998). While it is true that the Board has made a distinction between retirement cases and adverse actions in determining whether to find good cause for an untimely filing, the reasons for doing so are not present here. In Edney, the Board noted that, in a retirement appeal, the primary concern is whether the appellant is entitled under law to a benefit. Edney, 79 M.S.P.R. 60, ¶ 6. The Board then concluded that any doubt as to whether the Board should reopen a retirement appeal for an adjudication of its merits should be resolved in favor of the appellant to determine whether the appellant is, in fact, entitled to the benefit sought. Id. Here, however, OPM has not denied the appellant any benefit to which he is entitled because OPM has already granted the appellant a Civil Service Retirement System (CSRS) annuity. Thus Edney is inapposite.

Jackson v. OPM, 89 MSPR 302, 304-05 ¶¶ 6-10 (2001), a disability retirement appeal, extended Brown to cases handled by attorneys and refiled without substantial delay after a DWOP refiling deadline:

Here, the appellant timely filed her initial petition for appeal. Her delay in refiling her appeal resulted from her attorney incorrectly identifying the refiling date as April 11 instead of April 1. When the attorney discovered her mistake, she promptly refiled the appeal. Because April 1 was a Saturday, the appellant's refiled petition for appeal was not due until April 3, and thus it was only eight days late. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.23.

In Brown v. Office of Personnel Management, 86 M.S.P.R. 417 (2000), the Board found good cause for the filing delay under similar circumstances. There, the appellant filed a timely petition for appeal of OPM's reconsideration decision denying her disability retirement application, the administrative judge granted her counsel's request for a dismissal without prejudice, and the administrative judge set a refiling date. Acting pro se, the appellant refiled her appeal five days late, and the administrative judge dismissed it as untimely. Id. ¶¶ 2-4. The Board, though, found good cause for the filing delay. Among the, factors supporting our decision, we cited the relatively de minimis delay and the relatively arbitrary refiling deadline. Most important for the present case, we stated: "Our dismissal without prejudice practice, which appears to have served the interests of the appellant, OPM and the Board in this disability retirement case, should not become a trap that would deny the unwary pro se Appellant...the opportunity to have her case decided on the merits." Id. ¶ 8. We then noted that OPM had not indicated or shown that it would be prejudiced by waiving the filing deadline, id. ¶ 8, and waived the time limit, id. ¶ 9.

Similarly, in Moore v. Department of Defense, 84 M.S.P.R. 341 (1999), the administrative judge dismissed the appellant's initial appeal without prejudice to refiling, setting a specific time limit in which the refiling had to occur. The appellant refiled her appeal six days after the deadline and the administrative judge dismissed the refiled appeal as untimely. In waiving the time limit, we agreed with the appellant's assertion, among others, that the brevity of her delay warranted excusing the filing delay. Id. at 342. Moreover, in Hemphill, 77 M.S.P.R. at 511-12 & n.2, the Board noted that it has considered, as further grounds for waiving the filing deadline for a petition for appeal of an OPM reconsideration decision, the appellant's clear intention to seek review of the reconsideration decision within the Board's filing time limit and the lack of evidence indicating that OPM would be prejudiced by granting the waiver.

Although the appellant in this case was not proceeding pro se when her appeal was refiled, the delay in refiling, as in Brown and Moore, was de minimis. Admittedly, in Bailey v. U.S. Postal Service, 85 M.S.P.R. 105 (2000), the Board declined to waive the deadline for a refiled appeal that was four days late. There, however, the appellant had previously timely refiled his appeal twice, and all three appeals had been dismissed without prejudice to refiling. Id. ¶ 2. Moreover, some of the same situations that the appellant cited to excuse his untimely refiling were also present when he previously timely refiled his appeals. Because of this, we found that he had not shown good cause for the delay in refiling the appeal. Id. ¶ 6. Here, in contrast, the appellant's appeal was dismissed without prejudice only once.

In that regard, we find the most significant factor favoring waiver in this case is our finding in Brown that our dismissal without prejudice practice should not become a trap to deny an appellant the opportunity to have her case decided on the merits. 86 M.S.P.R. 417 ¶ 8. That finding is consistent with our previous statement in Hemphill that an appellant should not be denied an opportunity to have his case heard on the merits, where his intention to file a Board appeal has been clear throughout the proceedings and the appeal was initially timely filed. 77 M.S.P.R. at 512. Moreover, as in Brown, the deadline for refiling here was apparently relatively arbitrary, OPM did not object to the dismissal without prejudice, and OPM has not asserted that it would be prejudiced by waiving the filing time limit. 86 M.S.P.R. 417 ¶ 8. We therefore exercise our discretion, in the interest of justice, to waive the deadline for the appellant's refiled appeal.
