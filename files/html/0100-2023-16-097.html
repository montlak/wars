2. Comparison of Employees' Performance

Defensive use of evidence of comparative performance is difficult. An employee's performance is judged according to the established performance standards and not according to a predecessor's performance in the position. Under Nesson v. Dept. of Treasury, 4 MSPR 255, 256, 4 MSPB 329 (1980), it is improper for the AJ to overturn a performance action because one or two other employees doing work similar to the appellant's had, for short periods, performed at or below his level and received no adverse treatment. The Board, considering the defense of "like penalties for like offenses," determined that "[I]n the absence of evidence that the agency has retained employees with production rates consistently as low as the appellant's, the record will not support a finding that this principle was violated by the agency." Nesson was remanded for reconsideration, and on reconsideration, Nesson v. Dept. of Treasury, 11 MSPR 346 (1982), the appeal was successfully prosecuted by the agency under Chapter 75. See Burnett v. DOJ, SF-0432-11-0400-I-1 (NP 2/29/2012) ("The appellant also claims that other GS-12 Forensic Chemists exceeded the 10% error rate for multiple months but that they were not advised that their performance was unacceptable based on the fact that their technical error rates for the year were less than 10%. However, the appellant's technical error rate consistently remained above 10% even after counseling at his FY 2008 midyear progress review, his formal FY 2008 full year review, and his FY 2009 midyear progress review, whereas other Forensic Chemists who periodically had technical error rates above 10% responded to their supervisor's counseling by decreasing their rates to below 10%."); Jackson v. DHUD, 11 MSPR 329, 331 (1982) (witnesses who were granted WIGIs provided testimony of little value regarding the quality of the appellant's work compared to their work because they were "not responsible for supervising appellant on a full-time basis"); cf. Punch v. NASA, DA-0432-13-4323-I-2 n.12 (NP 2/9/2016) (as to comparators: "The appellant did not raise an allegation of disparate penalty below, and, in any event, the Board has no authority to mitigate a penalty taken under 5 U.S.C. chapter 43. See, e.g., Davis v. Department of Health & Human Services, 58 M.S.P.R. 538, 541 (1993), aff'd, 26 F.3d 139 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table).").

The ongoing significance of Nesson, implying that there may be circumstances warranting employee-to-employee comparisons of work, may be diminished by another decision that without a claim of discrimination, the Board will not review, and discovery will not be permitted as to, an allegation that other employees with similar performance standards were treated better than the appellant. The key to a disparity analysis is an allegation of discrimination. Rutz v. Dept. of Labor, 28 MSPR 677, 680 (1985) (citing Buggie v. DHHS, 27 MSPR 109, 111-12 & n.4 (1985)). A prohibited personnel practice allegation would accomplish the same result. See Thompson v. Farm Credit Admin., 51 MSPR 569, 584 (1991) (Chapter 43 action was taken in reprisal for protected activity; the Board noted, among other factors: "the appellant's supervisor made critical for the appellant an element that was not critical for others working under the same performance standards.").

Clarke v. DHHS, 48 MSPR 566, 571 (1991), suggested that a standard requiring performance for a clinical nurse at the level of one's peers ("medication error [rate] comparable to peers") required the agency to demonstrate comparative performance among those working under the same standard:

...We find in this appeal that the agency must meet its burden to show that (a) the appellant failed to demonstrate or sustain acceptable performance during the PIP, (b) the performance standard at issue is valid, and (c) the performance standard was properly applied to the appellant because she was not held to a higher level of performance during the PIP than other Clinical Nurses working under the same standard.

As to allegations of discrimination and reprisal, the appellant, a manager, succeeded in introducing evidence of production rates at comparable offices. The Board did not fully explain the basis for admitting the evidence, stating: "Moreover, a comparison of the appellant's office with other offices was appropriate, given Phillips's testimonial acknowledgment that there was no requirement, under the performance standard at issue, that the appellant be absolutely error-free on loan classifications, but that the 'magnitude' of any errors were subjectively evaluated to determine whether an employee met the 'fully successful' performance standard." Carter v. SBA, 61 MSPR 656, 663 (1994).

Comparative performance may be presented as affirmative evidence by the agency. The Board examines evidence of employees working under the same standard when that evidence is offered by the agency to defend the reasonableness of the standard. See Thomas v. DOD, 95 MSPR 123, 129 ¶ 17 (2003) ("Substantial evidence also indicates that the standard in question, as applied by the agency, is readily achievable by nearly all employees."), aff'd, Thomas v. DOD, 117 Fed. Appx. 722 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP); Cruz v. Dept. of Army, 122 Fed. Appx. 452, 456 (Fed. Cir. 2005 NP) ("Mr. Cruz asserts that his situation falls within the court's example because the standard of committing no more than four errors in one year 'could not be achieved by anyone.' The evidence, however, does not support Mr. Cruz's assertion. For example, one of Mr. Cruz's former co-workers testified that she never made more than four errors in any rating period. The Lead Clerk also testified that none of the PAS clerks other than Mr. Cruz had committed more than four errors in an entire rating year. Accordingly, the record reflects that it was not unreasonable to expect employees to commit no more than four errors in a rating period."); Blain v. VA, 36 MSPR 322, 326-27 (1988) (examining the circumstances of other employees but finding a lack of similarity). Performance goals were considered fair based on evidence that almost all of the examiners in the appellant's group met or exceeded their goals for patent examining. Kramer v. Dept. of Commerce (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 87-3589); see Greer v. Dept. of Army, 79 MSPR 477, 485 (1998) (the agency presented evidence that the research plans prepared by the appellant were deficient in comparison to the research plans of other scientists). But see Rocheleau v. SEC, 29 MSPR 193, 195 (1985) ("As the presiding official noted, no one in the Boston Regional Office at any grade had ever conducted 30 examinations in one year. Moreover, no other examiners nationwide had numerical quotas in their performance standards.").

The Board may determine that the agency does not prove the fairness of a standard through showing that other employees met the standard if the other employees are not shown to perform the same work, have the same training, or occupy the same grade levels. Russi v. Dept. of Army, 40 MSPR 585, 590-91 (1989). Walker v. Dept. of Treasury, 28 MSPR 227, 230 (1985), decided that the agency's undocumented assertion that others are currently meeting the standard at issue is insufficient to overcome a proper challenge to the propriety of the standard.
