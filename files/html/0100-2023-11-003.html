1. Informal Adjudication

Formal rules of evidence do not control an MSPB hearing. Debose v. USDA, 700 F.2d 1262, 1268 (9th Cir. 1983). Technical rules for exclusion of evidence, e.g., the best evidence rule, parol evidence rule, and formal authentication techniques, do not apply to proceedings before administrative agencies. Anderson v. USPS, 28 MSPR 58, 59 n.2 (1985) (citing Sanders v. Dept. of Air Force, 23 MSPR 88, 90 (1984) (a challenge to videotapes)); see Howard v. OPM, 31 MSPR 617, 620 (1986) (best evidence rule is inapplicable); Middleton v. DOJ, 23 MSPR 223, 227 (1984) (hearsay is admissible; the best evidence rule is not applicable). Objections based on technical rules of evidence are not appropriate. Hoffman v. Dept. of Air Force (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-572). But see Jones v. USDA, 19 MSPR 133, 134 n.2 (1984) (implying the possible application of the best evidence rule, but determining that the record contained "ample corroborating evidence to support the agency's charge").

According to the furlough decision styled Tinker Hearing A-D v. Dept. of Air Force, DA-0752-14-0289-I-1 (NP 6/5/2015):

It is well settled that technical rules for the exclusion of evidence, such as appropriate authentication, do not apply to proceedings before federal administrative judges. Sanders v. Department of the Air Force, 23 M.S.P.R. 88, 90 (1984). Here, the appellants have not claimed that the documents submitted by the agency are inauthentic. Their arguments are based on the Federal Rules of Evidence. The Board relies on the Federal Rules of Evidence as guidance, but an administrative judge is not required to strictly adhere to them in determining what evidence is to be admitted at a hearing. See Arterberry v. Department of the Air Force, 25 M.S.P.R. 582, 583 (1985). The appellants' arguments rely entirely on the type of evidence on which the agency relied to meet its burden of proof. Administrative judges are not prohibited from admitting any category of evidence and have considerable discretion in the manner in which they conduct hearings. Hatley v. Veterans Administration, 9 M.S.P.R. 585, 590 (1982)....

The Federal Rules of Evidence are not even mentioned in most Board hearings. AJs tend to listen to anything that seems relevant to them, as defined by the charges and the affirmative defenses, and as limited by their time and patience and curiosity, rather than risk adjudicative error and possible remand following exclusion of evidence. AJs are not prohibited from admitting any category of evidence. Arterberry v. Dept. of Air Force, 25 MSPR 582, 583 (1985). Normally, evidence of actions taken by the agency or the appellant after an appealed decision is not relevant to the merits of the action appealed. See Means v. Dept. of Labor, 60 MSPR 108, 114 (1993) (reviewing the arbitration award); Schindler v. Dept. of Army, 42 MSPR 432, 433 (1989) (AJ erred by considering facts that arose after the charged falsifications, but the result of the case was supported by other evidence). But even that evidentiary limitation must be tempered with the knowledge that some cases may produce circumstances involving postdecisional actions by a party that do relate to the merits of the charges or the propriety of a penalty. [Refer to Chapter 5, "Review of New Evidence" and refer to Chapters 7 and 8 for the elements of, defenses to, and mitigating factors applicable to various charges, as well as analysis of evidence of comparable penalties.]

In the Hearings Procedures Guide (still used by the Board for training and orientation although it has been superseded by the Judges' Handbook), the Board discussed formal rules of evidence at 25-26:

H. EVIDENCE

Formal rules of evidence do not apply to Board proceedings and the AJ is not prohibited from admitting any category of evidence. Debose v. Dept. of Agric., 700 F.2d 1262, 1268 (9th Cir. 1983), aff'g 5 MSPR 162 (1981); Brousseau v. United States, 640 F.2d 1235, 1241-42 (Ct. Cl. 1981); Arterberry v. Dept. of Air Force, 25 MSPR 582, 583 (1985) (unsworn statements admissible); Sanders v. Dept. of Air Force, 23 MSPR 88, 90 (1984); Hayden v. USPS, 15 MSPR 296, 302 (1983) (offered proof sufficient under Board's regulations). See also 5 CFR 1201.62(a).

However, the AJ may look to the Federal Rules of Evidence for guidance. Lynch v. Dept. of Justice, 32 MSPR 33, 42 (1986); Edmond v. TVA, 23 MSPR 489, 492 (1982).
