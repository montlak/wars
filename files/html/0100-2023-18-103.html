e. Past Discipline as Rebuttal to Mitigation

Although past discipline is supposed to be referenced in a proposal letter if it is relied on to enhance discipline for a currently-charged offense, past discipline may be omitted from a proposal letter yet considered by a deciding official or the AJ as to mitigation of a disciplinary penalty, although that analysis is likely undercut by Ward, post, Spearman v. USPS, 44 MSPR 135, 140-41 (1990):

The appellant alleged that the administrative judge committed harmful procedural error because, in finding that removal was an appropriate penalty, he relied on prior disciplinary records that were not included in the proposal notice and the agency had agreed not to cite them "in any further proceedings."...

Although these records were not included in the proposal notice, the record shows that the appellant had raised the issue of his past performance and conduct in challenging agency witnesses who testified regarding the appropriateness of the penalty. The agency was free to include these exhibits in the record to rebut the appellant's claims of satisfactory conduct and performance, and that he was not on notice that the agency would not tolerate the kind of misconduct that occurred....

Moreover, even if we were to find that the administrative judge erroneously considered these records, the appellant has, nevertheless, failed to establish that he has been harmed thereby, since we do not rely here upon these records in determining the maximum reasonable penalty for the appellant's conduct. See Karapinka, 6 MSPR at 127.

Under Bess v. Dept. of Navy, 46 MSPR 583, 591 (1991), again, pre-Ward, it is permissible for an agency to produce evidence of past discipline, not otherwise mentioned in the proposal notice, as a rebuttal to appellant's contention that the agency treated him differently than another employee arguably similarly situated:

Generally, the Board's review of prior disciplinary actions is limited to determining whether they were clearly erroneous where: (1) The employee was informed of the action in writing; (2) it was made a matter of record; and (3) he was given an opportunity to dispute it to a different authority. Bolling v. Department of the Air Force, 8 MSPB 658, 9 MSPR 335, 339-40 (1981). However, where, as here, the appellant raises the issue of disparate treatment, the agency is required to present evidence to refute such a challenge. See Herndon v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 15 MSPR 660, 661 (1983). Thus, if the appellant contends that a penalty exceeds that imposed upon other, similarly-situated employees, the agency may present rebuttal evidence of prior discipline imposed on the appellant, despite its failure to have provided such information in the advance or final notices. See id. at 661-62; Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 MSPR 280, 304 n.65 (1981). Therefore, in the present case, the administrative judge did not err by allowing the agency to rebut the appellant's claim of disparate treatment by presenting testimony regarding disciplinary actions taken against the appellant that were not referenced in the notice of proposed removal.
