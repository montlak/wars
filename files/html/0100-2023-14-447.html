broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 15: Pay, Premiums, and Allowances, Section F: Pay and Employment for Employees of Particular Agencies, Subsection 05: FDIC

5. FDIC

NTEU Chap. 207 and FDIC, 14 FLRA 598 (1984) (remanded, NTEU v. FLRA, 813 F.2d 472 (1987), on remand, 28 FLRA 625 (1987), remand decision vacated as moot, 40 FLRA 966 (1991), post), considered whether a compelling need prevented negotiation of a salary structure for unit employees in Washington different from that of employees, not in the unit, in the regional offices. FLRA concluded that a compelling need existed, barring negotiation of differentiated rates of pay under a statute that permitted discretion as to salaries. The compelling need arose from a resolution of the agency's governing board that established the national structure, 14 FLRA at 611-12:

...The Agency has demonstrated that the need for uniformity in its pay system is an integral aspect of the Agency's stated objective of pay equity. The Agency's need for uniformity is thus not one of mere administrative convenience.... In this regard, the Agency has shown that in the pay setting area, under the circumstances presented, lack of uniformity would result in pay inequity which in turn would result in disruption inimical to the accomplishment of the Agency's mission and execution of its functions in a manner consistent with an effective and efficient government....

[T]here is no indication...that the Agency could achieve its objective of pay equity by any means except implementing the union's proposal for all Agency employees. While it appears that the Agency has discretion to implement the union's proposal for all Agency employees, such action would require revising the salary structure for the more than 80% of the Agency's employees who are not in the bargaining unit. Thus...where less than 20% of the Agency's employees are in the bargaining unit, the Authority finds that it would be consistent with an effective and efficient Government for the Agency's regulations to bar negotiation of the Union's proposal.

Our colleague, who today dissents from this finding, among other things, sees the issue as one best left to the collective bargaining process, as it would be if the matter had arisen in the private sector context. However, it should be clear by now, over 5 years having passed since the enactment of the Statute, that the legal framework which governs our decision today was "designed to meet the special needs and requirements of government." [5 USC 7101(b).]

Member Haughton dissented, arguing that there was nothing essential about a uniform pay system viewed by agency management as a convenience. His view was that the Authority majority was confusing compelling need with its view of the merits of the proposal. The merits, he said, could be resolved either by the parties through negotiations or by the FSIP in the event of impasse. There was no need to preclude bargaining altogether.

On the FDIC pay negotiation issue, FLRA was required by a remand from the D.C. Circuit, NTEU v. FLRA, 813 F.2d 472 (1987) (Table; unpublished opinion), to reconsider NTEU Chap. 207 and FDIC, 14 FLRA 598 (1984), involving the negotiability of a proposal that the FDIC salary structure be maintained with certain adjustments for cost of living and comparability with General Schedule employees. FLRA's first decision was that the proposal was barred by an agency resolution supported by a compelling need, i.e., the need for uniformity of pay administration. The purpose of the remand was for FLRA to consider the effect of some intervening changes by the agency in salary determination and cost-of-living adjustments. In a split decision on remand, FLRA adopted the court's suggestion that the proposal was negotiable. FLRA declined to consider newly raised arguments concerning compelling need and instead ruled that the subject matter of the proposals was not covered by and not inconsistent with law or regulation. NTEU Chap. 207 and FDIC, 28 FLRA 625, 631-32 (1987) (Proposal 1; Chairman Calhoun dissented and argued that negotiability of wages should depend on affirmative evidence of congressional intent). The remand decision was ordered vacated as moot in NTEU Chap. 207 and FDIC, 40 FLRA 966, 967 (1991), as a result of a law effective on November 5, 1990, the Federal Employees Pay Comparability Act of 1990, Pub. L. 101-509, having the effect of abolishing processes earlier involved in the federal pay-setting process for FDIC. Previously, with no factually supported compelling need claim, FDIC was required to negotiate over a proposal that would vary the waiting periods for step increases, its employees not being subject to OPM restrictions governing other General Schedule employees. AFGE Local 3804 and FDIC, Madison Region, 21 FLRA 870, 883-84 (1986) (Proposal 8). Another proposal, suggesting the future negotiability of modifications in benefit systems, including dental care, was negotiable. NTEU Chap. 207 and FDIC, 28 FLRA 738, 738-41 (1987), reconsideration denied, 29 FLRA 1465 (1987); see FDIC and NTEU, 41 FLRA 272, 277-79 (1991) (ULP litigation arising out of FDIC's unilateral changes in rates and open season for its health insurance plan).
