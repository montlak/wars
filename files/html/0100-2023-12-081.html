broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section B: Interim Relief, Subsection 03: Obligation to Return Employee to Work and Pay; Undue Disruption, Part a: Undue Disruption Determination
a. Undue Disruption Determination

Under 5 CFR 1201.116 (a) (2022), if the appellant prevails and the AJ orders interim relief, the agency's PFR (or cross-PFR) must be "accompanied by a certification that the agency has complied with the interim relief order either by providing the required interim relief or by satisfying the requirements of 5 USC 7701(b)(2)(A)(ii) and (B)." The statute calls for the agency to grant the relief ordered by the AJ unless the agency determines that it would be unduly disruptive to the work environment to return or have present the employee or applicant to his or her place of employment. If the agency makes a determination that prevents the return or presence of the employee, the employee receives pay and benefits pending the outcome of a PFR. The statute requires an agency determination. The form of that notification is not spelled out in the current Board regulation: the agency must certify that the statutory requirements have been met, including if applicable, the agency's determination that return or the presence of the employee or applicant at the workplace is unduly disruptive to the work environment. The appellant is allowed the opportunity to challenge the agency's certification. OPM regulations at 5 Part 772 (2012), define some back pay consequences of interim relief orders, but those regulations do not define agency interim relief notice requirements.

Until the regulatory revisions of 1999, the interim relief regulation required more than a certification by the agency. There was a required "undue disruption" notification that was to be given to the AJ and to the appellant; evidence was also required for the Board that, when the PFR was filed, the agency had arranged for the appellant to be paid. The regulatory relaxation was, according to the Board's rulemaking notice at 64 FR 27899 (May 24, 1999), to avoid procedural dismissals based on violations of regulatory interim relief requirements when those violations caused no substantive harm to the appellant. Before 1999, the law was that the agency must make an explicit and written decision, and provide the necessary written notice that the employee's return to work would be unduly disruptive. "The only interim relief action that did not require an undue disruption determination is the return of the appellant to his former position and duties." Sherron v. R.R. Retirement Bd., 70 MSPR 498, 501 (1996). Withee v. VA, 74 MSPR 309, 312-13 (1997), summarized the law governing undue disruption determinations prior to the 1999 amendments.

Until the Federal Circuit reversed it, and before the 1999 regulatory amendments, the Board's position was that the agency should, but was not required to, make an explicit declaration that the return of the appellant to regular duties would be unduly disruptive; that finding was implied in the assignment of the appellant to different job duties. Wayne v. Dept. of Navy, 55 MSPR 322, 328 (1992). The point was reaffirmed in DeLaughter v. USPS, 55 MSPR 380 (1992), rev'd, DeLaughter v. USPS, 3 F.3d 1522, 1524-25 (Fed. Cir. 1993), post, determining that the failure of the agency to notify the appellant or the AJ that the appellant's return to work would be disruptive was not fatal to the agency's PFR. But the Federal Circuit opted for a stricter approach in DeLaughter v. USPS, 3 F.3d 1522, 1524-25 (Fed. Cir. 1993):

In this case it is abundantly clear that the Postal Service failed to follow the appellate review procedure mandated by Congress for petitions for review of an AJ's initial decision returning an employee to work. Congress has decided that if a deciding official returns an employee to his place of work the agency must effectuate that remedy. The only way in which the Postal Service could avoid putting Mr. DeLaughter back to work was to determine that his presence would be "unduly disruptive." In such circumstances, the Postal Service may deny the employee the benefits of being on the job, although it must continue to ensure that the employee receives his monetary remedy by placing him on administrative leave.

The Postal Service did not fulfill the unmistakably clear statutory obligation. By excusing the Postal Service's failure to make an unduly disruptive finding as harmless error, the Board failed to enforce this statutory obligation. In so doing, the Board inappropriately equated compensation with employment. There are many benefits, both tangible and intangible, which may accrue to an employee who is actually present and working, instead of being away from the work place while on administrative leave. For example, the employee may gain valuable work experience; he may be eligible for performance awards; he may be assigned overtime duty; or he may benefit psychologically from earning his pay rather than merely receiving it. Thus, such failure to comply with the statute can hardly be harmless error. We are frankly astonished that the Postal Service maintained both in its brief as well as at oral argument that it was frivolous for Mr. DeLaughter to contest the Postal Service's willful disregard of the measures Congress instituted to enforce its stated preference for returning an employee to work.

We particularly point out that the Postal Service's outrage over Mr. DeLaughter's failure to inform us that he was placed on administrative leave and subsequently returned to work is misplaced. When an employee is the prevailing party before an AJ of the Board, section 7701(b)(2) requires the agency to do one of two things: return the employee to the work place or place him on administrative leave after making an unduly disruptive determination. In either case, the employee will be compensated monetarily. Unless notified that the Postal Service had failed to comply with this statutory requirement, this court would have correctly assumed that Mr. DeLaughter was indeed in some sort of pay status. As discussed above, however, Mr. DeLaughter's pay status is irrelevant to the issue raised by this appeal.

Because the Postal Service failed to comply with the directives contained in section 7701(b)(2)(A), the Board had no choice but to dismiss the Postal Service's petition for review. We therefore remand this case to the Board with directions to dismiss the Postal Service's petition for review pursuant to 5 USC 7701(b)(2)(A) and 5 CFR 1201.115(b)(4).

See Simpkins v. USPS, 68 MSPR 620, 622 (1995) ("In DeLaughter, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit held that, in placing the appellant on paid administrative leave without making an express undue disruption determination, the agency had failed to meet its interim relief obligations under 5 USC § 7701(b)(2)(A).").

Following DeLaughter, the Board then concluded that the determination of undue disruption must be explicitly made or the agency PFR would be dismissed if the employee has not been returned to his prior job, duties, and location. See White v. Dept. of Air Force, 71 MSPR 607, 611 (1996) (King v. Jerome, 42 F.3d 1371 (Fed. Cir. 1994), implicitly "sanctioned the Board's reading of the interim relief statute to permit agencies to detail, assign, and even transfer employees to different commuting areas as interim relief, if such agencies have made a determination that it would be unduly disruptive to effect the relief ordered by the administrative judge, and appropriate pay, compensation and benefits are provided"); Burke v. Smithsonian Inst., 69 MSPR 407, 410 (1996) ("Under 5 USC § 7701(b)(2)(A)(ii), where an agency opts to comply with an administrative judge's interim relief order, not by returning the appellant to her former position and duty station, but by placing her on administrative leave or returning her to duty in some alternative position or duty station, the agency must make a determination that the appellant's return or presence in the workplace would be unduly disruptive."); Boutin v. USPS, 65 MSPR 487, 491 (1994) (agency may detail an employee if it determines that returning the employee to his former position would be unduly disruptive, but the detail does not constitute proper interim relief without an undue disruption determination, even if the agency has justification, and the availability of light duty); Shaishaa v. Dept. of Army, 60 MSPR 359, 362 (1994) ("Here, the evidence submitted by the agency with its petition for review shows that it assigned the appellant to an office other than the one where he worked before his removal, but it does not show that the agency determined that a return to his former office would be unduly disruptive."); White v. USPS, 60 MSPR 314, 317 (1993) (agency failed to show that it made a determination that the appellant's return to work would be unduly disruptive to its operations); Heath v. Dept. of Navy, 60 MSPR 183, 185-86 (1993) (noting the necessity for an actual, not an implied, undue disruption determination; the agency could not assign the appellant to another duty station, pending disposition of the PFR, without determining that returning her to her former duty station would be unduly disruptive); cf. Nadolski v. MSPB, 105 F.3d 642 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (interim relief may include restoration to a different job or duties; the agency is not limited to alternatives of restoring the employee to his original position or placing the employee on administrative leave). The undue disruption determination was tied to other elements of interim relief, discussed below. "If the agency makes an undue disruption determination, the agency must show that the appellant has received a paycheck for the interim period or that it has taken appropriate administrative action by the filing deadline date for its petition for review that will result in the issuance of such a paycheck." Purzycki v. GSA, 81 MSPR 188, 193 ¶ 7 (1999).

Under earlier law, bad faith could be raised as a challenge to an undue disruption determination. Brown v. Dept. of Navy, 65 MSPR 245, 250 (1994), decided that the agency may assign an employee to another position during the interim relief period, including a position that is lower in grade than the position the employee formerly occupied, if the employee is provided the full pay and benefits he would receive in the former position, the agency determines that the appellant's return to his former position during the interim relief period would be unduly disruptive, and the agency's determination is not based on bad faith. The "appellant's complaint concerning the grade level of the position to which he was assigned and his belief that the assignment is 'degrading and demeaning' for that reason does not constitute a prima facie showing that the assignment was made in bad faith." Id. See Storm v. Dept. of Army, 64 MSPR 14, 25-27 (1994) (comparing the descriptions of assigned duties provided by the appellant and the agency to determine whether the interim relief was provided in bad faith). The Federal Circuit overruled this basis for a challenge in King v. Jerome, 42 F.3d 1371 (Fed. Cir. 1994), supra. Now the Board will not weigh the relative merits and demerits of an appellant's placement into nonduty status following an agency determination of undue disruption. See Scott v. DOJ, 69 MSPR 211, 220-21 (1995) (declining to entertain the appellant's motion, pending MSPB consideration of an OPM request for reconsideration, for reinstatement based on an argument by the appellant that "the injuries he sustains each day as a result of his placement in a non-duty pay status significantly outweigh any disruption that his presence may cause"); cf. Slayton v. USDA, SF-0752-17-0370-I-1 (NP 2/23/2023) ("Further, it is well settled that the Board lacks the authority to review the merits of the agency's undue disruption determination. King v. Jerome, 42 F.3d 1371, 1374-75 (Fed. Cir. 1994); Parbs v. U.S. Postal Service, 107 M.S.P.R. 559, ¶ 6 (2007), aff'd, 301 F. App'x 923 (Fed. Cir. 2008); Byers v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 89 M.S.P.R. 655, ¶ 5 (2001). Thus, the Board may not consider the appellant's claim that the basis of the agency's undue disruption determination is 'demonstrably false.'").
