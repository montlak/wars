D. SPECIAL STATUTORY PAY RATES

Pursuant to 5 USC 5303, OPM may set and then periodically adjust pay rates for defined groups of employees depending on disparities between federal pay rates and pay for comparably situated employees in the private sector. Negotiable in NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, IRS, 37 FLRA 147, 151-54 (1990), were proposals that the employer request specific special salary rate adjustments from OPM. The proposals did not interfere with the power of the agency to establish its own budget:

We...reject the Agency's assertion that the Union's proposals do not concern conditions of employment because SSRs are specifically provided for by Federal statute. Although 5 U.S.C. § 5303 provides for the establishment of SSRs and their adjustment, the statute does not specifically provide either the rate to be paid under the SSRs, or the level of any revisions to the rate of SSRs which the President may periodically establish. The prerogative to establish specific SSRs or to revise them is left to the President, who has delegated this responsibility to OPM. Therefore, we conclude that the Agency has not shown that SSRs are specifically provided for by Federal statute.

...

[W]e find that the Agency has not supported its argument that implementation of the Union's proposals would directly interfere with management's right to determine its budget because the proposals "would by necessity cause a significant increase in the IRS's budget, an increase not offset by any demonstrable or even speculative benefit."...

The sole claim made by the Agency in this regard is that "the total cost of Special Salary Rates requests sent to Treasury for OPM's approval on August 31, 1988, will cost the [Agency] in their first year of implementation $23,000,000."... This figure covers SSRs already requested by the Agency, however. The Agency offers no evidence as to the cost of the Union's proposals or the impact the proposals would have on its overall budget.

...

Finally, we reject the Agency's argument that, should the Union's proposals be found negotiable, "the possibility exists that a third party...could order the [Agency] to request Special Salary Rates despite merit arguments to the contrary."... In National Treasury Employees Union, Chapter 83 and Department of the Treasury, Internal Revenue Service, 35 FLRA 398, 414-15 (1990), we concluded that "the possibility that the [FSIP] may, in fulfilling its mandated role to resolve negotiation impasses, impose a proposal on parties provides no basis for finding that proposal nonnegotiable" because "such action by the FSIP is wholly consistent with the collective bargaining process provided by Congress under the Statute."

AFGE Nat'l Council of SSA Field Assessment Locals and DHHS, SSA, Reg'l Office of Program and Integrity Review, Atlanta, 49 FLRA 582, 585 (1994), pronounced negotiable a provision that an agency consider and make internal recommendations concerning special pay rates. Proposal 2 was that:

Management will make a good-faith, diligent, and concerted effort to provide special pay for the DRTS. It is understood that other SSA components have the special pay for substantially equal work and the DRTs in ROPIR should not be disadvantaged. Management will keep the union fully informed in their efforts in this area.

Under Nat'l Council, 49 FLRA at 585-87:

Proposal 2 obligates "management" to take specified efforts to obtain special pay for DRTS. The Union explains that the reference to "management" in Proposal 2...refers to local ROPIR management and that, as authority to provide special pay rests with higher-level management within the Social Security Administration, the proposal merely obligates ROPIR management to seek such approval from higher-level management. According to the Union, higher-level management retains the discretion under the proposal to approve or not approve special pay for DRTS.

The Union's interpretation of Proposal 2 is consistent with its plain wording, and we adopt the interpretation for the purposes of this decision. Accordingly, Proposal 2 only requires local ROPIR management to provide a recommendation to higher-level management concerning the provision of special pay for certain employees.

As previously noted, under regulations promulgated by OPM, 5 C.F.R. § 530.301-307, the minimum rates of pay for certain employees in certain geographic areas may be increased in order to overcome impediments to the recruitment or retention of personnel. In particular, these regulations provide procedures by which agencies may request OPM approval of special pay requests. Accordingly, unlike Proposal 1, which required intra-Agency recommendations concerning matters subject to the Agency's discretion pursuant to the exercise of its management rights, Proposal 2 concerns a matter where management rights are not involved and, instead, discretion to approve the recommended action rests outside the Agency.

An agency is obligated to bargain to the extent of its discretion on otherwise negotiable matters. See American Federation of State, County, and Municipal Employees, AFL-CIO, Local 2477, et al. and Library of Congress, Washington, D.C., 7 FLRA 578, 585-86 (1982), enforced sub nom. Library of Congress v. FLRA, 699 F.2d 1280 (D.C. Cir. 1983). There is nothing in the record in this case which indicates that the Agency lacks discretion under 5 C.F.R. § 530.301-307 to request OPM to approve special pay for certain employees. Thus, a proposal obligating the Agency to make such a request to OPM, would be negotiable....

In this case, however, the Agency is not actually required to seek such approval from OPM. That is, based on the Union's interpretation of Proposal 2, which we have adopted for the purposes of this decision, the proposal only requires local ROPIR management to provide a recommendation to higher-level management concerning the provision of special pay for specified employees. In this connection, nothing in 5 C.F.R. § 530.301-307 precludes local management from making recommendations to higher-level management within an agency concerning a decision to request OPM to approve special pay for specified employees. Thus, insofar as the Agency contends that Proposal 2 is inconsistent with a Government-wide regulation, we disagree....

We also reject the Agency's claim that the proposal concerns classification matters which are excluded from the definition of conditions of employment by section 7103(a)(14)(B) of the Statute. Nothing in the wording of the proposal, or in the record in this case, requires the Agency to modify or change a DRT's classification....

Special salary rates were also examined in NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, IRS, Chicago, 38 FLRA 1605 (1991). The first proposal was that employees assigned to an agency district supporting an OPM-approved special salary rate, but who were at posts of duty not in that district, would receive annual incentive awards equal to the OPM-approved special salary rate. The proposal concerned incentive awards, not special salary rates. The proposal was contrary to the then-applicable government-wide regulation, at 5 CFR 451.104(d), providing that "[a]n award under this part shall not be used...as a substitute for pay." The second and third proposals required that employees detailed or reassigned for more than 10 days to an area approved for a special salary rate receive that rate, and that employees permanently assigned to an area calling for a special rate continue to receive that rate when they were detailed or reassigned to posts of duty where those rates were not implemented. The proposal requiring that employees brought into a special statutory rate area through reassignment was consistent with OPM regulations at 5 CFR 530.306(d) and (e); to the extent the proposal required payment of that rate to detailees, however, it was inconsistent with the OPM regulation. Proposals allowing for payment for temporary promotions were distinguishable under law developed by the Comptroller General. The last proposal, that the special rate follow an employee out of a special rate area, was nonnegotiable as to employees reassigned from special rate areas; it was, however, negotiable for employees detailed out of a special pay rate area. The last proposal could have been, but was not, properly structured for situations allowing pay retention for employees detailed between positions at the direction of management.

NAGE Local R1-109 and VAMC Newington, 53 FLRA 403, 414-16 (1997), opined to be nonnegotiable Proposal 6, calling on the employer to agree to pay locality pay without regard to concurrent payment of a special salary rate. An OPM regulation, 5 CFR 531.606 (2022), provides that employees shall receive the greater of either their rate of basic pay, including a special salary rate, or an applicable locality rate of pay.
