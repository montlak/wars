d. Duty to Supply Information; Accounting

The Board does not specifically instruct an agency on the amounts it is to pay to satisfy a back pay obligation, Cox v. USPS, 87 MSPR 437, 440 ¶ 5 (2000):

The agency has mistaken the Board's role in this matter. The Board does not calculate the amount of back pay due the appellant in the first instance. Nor does the Board consider competing proposals for the amount of back pay from the agency and the appellant and instruct the agency which proposal to implement. Rather, it is the agency's responsibility to calculate and pay the appellant the appropriate amount of back pay and provide the Board sufficient evidence to demonstrate that its calculations were proper....

It is the agency's responsibility to explain how the appellant's monetary remedy was calculated. Antunes v. USPS, 61 MSPR 408, 410 (1994), made clear the agency's responsibility to provide a cogent explanation of back pay calculations:

We conclude that the agency has not adequately explained how it calculated the interest on the back pay award or the penalties under the Prompt Payment Act (31 USC § 3902(e)). Thus the agency has not complied with the Board's regulation at 5 CFR § 1201.183(a)(1)(i) (the agency must submit "[e]vidence of compliance, including a narrative explanation of the calculation of back pay and other benefits...."). See Blanchard v. Department of Justice, 40 MSPR 513, 515 (1989) ("A submission of satisfactory evidence of compliance by the agency must include an explanation of how it arrived at each of these figures, as well as a correct tallying of the total back pay award.").

The responsibility of an agency to provide an explanation of benefits to the appellant extends to disability retirement cases and benefits administered by OPM. See Lua v. OPM, 100 MSPR 431 (2005) (dispute concerning calculations for cost of life insurance premiums deducted from an annuity); Ogden v. OPM, 92 MSPR 522, 525 ¶ 7 (2002) ("[g]iven the appellant's allegations regarding noncompliance, OPM was required to show that its calculation of those benefits was proper"). Corum v. USPS, DC-0353-06-0728-X-1 (NP 3/31/2022), expanded on the explanation due from the agency:

When the Board finds a personnel action unwarranted or not sustainable, it orders that the appellant be placed, as nearly as possible, in the situation she would have been in had the wrongful personnel action not occurred. House v. Department of the Army, 98 M.S.P.R. 530, ¶ 9 (2005). The agency bears the burden to prove its compliance with a Board order. Id., ¶ 6. An agency's assertions of compliance must include a clear explanation of its compliance actions supported by documentary evidence. Vaughan v. Department of Agriculture, 116 M.S.P.R. 319, ¶ 5 (2011). The appellant may rebut the agency's evidence of compliance by making "specific, nonconclusory, and supported assertions of continued noncompliance." Brown v. Office of Personnel Management, 113 M.S.P.R. 325, ¶ 5 (2010). A careful review of the agency's latest submission shows that the agency has now reached full compliance. Viewed together with the agency's narrative statements, the agency's submitted documentation demonstrates that the agency properly calculated the appellant's overtime back pay using the correct comparators and paid the appellant an amount at least equal to what it owed her, along with interest. While the agency ultimately did not use the correct method to determine the appellant's overtime back pay, its error resulted in her earning more overtime back pay than she otherwise would have received under the proper methodology, and thus leads to the conclusion that the agency was in compliance with the requirement to pay her the correct amount of overtime back pay owed. Therefore, we find that the agency is now in full compliance with the Board's July 10, 2012 Opinion and Order.

Under Favreau v. Dept. of Army, SF-0752-11-0273-C-2 (NP 12/15/2016):

...For the agency to be found in compliance regarding the provision of back pay, interest on back pay, and benefits, the agency must provide a detailed and clear explanation of the calculations that it made in determining the amount due the appellant. The agency must clearly set forth the following: the gross amount due the appellant and how that amount was determined; the amount and reason for all deductions, reductions, and offsets from the gross amount due the appellant; the source and amount of all checks or electronic payments already received by the appellant and provide evidence that such checks or electronic payments were received; and the amount of interest due the appellant and how that amount was calculated. Bruton v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 111 M.S.P.R. 489, ¶ 17, vacated on other grounds, 112 M.S.P.R. 313 (2009). The agency also must clearly set forth its calculations relating to the appellant's sick and annual leave balances; Thrift Savings Plan account, including both those of the appellant and of the agency; and any other benefits of employment the appellant would have received but for the agency's unwarranted personnel action. Id. Finally, the agency must take the appropriate steps to restore the appellant's health insurance benefits and to provide evidence that it has done so. Id. In addition to the calculations, the agency must provide a clear and detailed narrative explanation of its calculations so that the Board may understand the calculations and verify that they are correct. Id. The agency also must provide an explanation of all codes and abbreviations used in its documentation. Id.

A description of a deficient accounting is provided in Guinn v. Dept. of Labor, 93 MSPR 316, 319 ¶ 10 (2003):

Moreover, the appellant's base salary, and thus the deductions, for pay period 26 differs from the other pay periods without explanation.... Further, the abbreviations for the columns are not defined and some, such as "GLI," "ALLOT," and "MC" are not readily discernable.... In addition, the agency paid the appellant in two installments: one check for $20,044.30; and one check for $1,604.83. It has not explained what pay periods the specific checks relate to. Thus, we are unable to determine whether the appellant was properly paid six months' back wages under the settlement agreement. See, e.g., Simons v. Department of Health & Human Services, 89 M.S.P.R. 685 ¶ 5 (2001) (satisfactory evidence of compliance with a back pay order must include an explanation of how the agency arrived at its figures and an accurate accounting of any deductions). Therefore, we must remand the petition for enforcement for the AJ to determine whether the agency accurately calculated the appellant's back wages. The AJ should also ensure that the agency explains the deductions taken from the back wages' amount. See Cuevas, 90 M.S.P.R. 391 ¶ 5.

See Graves v. Dept. of Navy, SF-3330-10-0788-X-1 (NP 12/27/2012) ("The agency bears the burden to prove its compliance with the Board's order. The agency's assertions of compliance must include a clear explanation of its compliance actions supported by documentary evidence. Vaughan v. Department of Agriculture, 116 M.S.P.R. 319, ¶ 5 (2011)."); Bruton v. VA, 111 MSPR 489, 496 ¶ 17, 2009 MSPB 110 (2009) ("The agency must also clearly set forth its calculations relating to the appellant's sick and annual leave balances, his Thrift Savings Plan account, including both the appellant's and the agency's contributions, and any other benefits of employment the appellant would have received but for the agency's unwarranted personnel action. Finally, the agency must take the appropriate steps to restore the appellant's health insurance benefits and provide evidence that it has done so. In addition to the calculations, the agency must provide a clear and detailed narrative explanation of its calculations so that the Board may understand the calculations and verify that they are correct. The agency must provide an explanation of all codes and abbreviations used in its documentation.") (following remand, Bruton v. VA, 112 MSPR 313, 2009 MSPB 149 (2009)).

Sustained efforts may be required by the Board to obtain proper accountings for pay and benefits owed. E.g., Cobia v. VA, PH-0752-16-0283-X-1 (NP 1/6/2023) (requiring narrative statements and supporting documentation from VA regarding pay, leave, TSP, insurance premiums, notice concerning elections as to benefits restoration; appellant's problems with unexplained pay stubs referencing leave hours restored, lack of restoration of TSP benefits, termination of insurance benefits and lack of retroactive restoration; requirement that VA document its efforts to obtain related information from DFAS; requirement of biweekly status reports from VA [18 in all, stretching over about three years]). If the appellant does not specifically challenge the agency's handling of restored benefits, the Board will not direct the agency to perform an audit to enforce the Board's final remedial order. See Rawlings v. USPS, 110 MSPR 404, 407 ¶ 6, 2009 MSPB 7 (2009) ("[i]n the absence of a specific objection to the agency's explanation and calculations, we find that the appellant's response is insufficient to rebut the agency's specific evidence of compliance."); Bullock v. Dept. of Air Force, 93 MSPR 217, 220 ¶ 8 (2002) ("The appellant seeks an audit of his leave and earnings records. However, the appellant has not specifically alleged that the agency is in noncompliance on this matter. As noted above, he has conceded that he received regular back pay and that he has been credited with the leave he earned during the back pay period, and he is not entitled to restoration of leave that he actually used. Therefore, the appellant has not shown that the Board should order an audit of the his leave and earnings records in order to enforce the final order reversing his removal. Edwards v. Department of Justice, 90 M.S.P.R. 537, 542 ¶ 9 (2002)."). But see Collins v. USPS, 64 MSPR 684 (1994) (appellant submitted several times his explicit contentions relating to a back pay claim; that claim was not waived when the appellant did not timely respond to one order of the AJ calling for information on the same claim). There is no right to an accounting in the absence of a Board final order requiring agency action. See Caracciolo v. Dept. of Treasury, 105 MSPR 663, 669 ¶ 13, 2007 MSPB 139 (2007) ("the appellant has not identified any law, rule, or regulation that affords the Board jurisdiction over a claim of entitlement to an accounting of payments made outside the context of a petition to enforce a final decision of the Board, and we are aware of none").

The agency should supply not only a check for back pay owed to the appellant, but also an accounting for that back pay. Collins v. USPS, 66 MSPR 531, 534 (1995); see Camastro v. DOJ, 86 MSPR 267, 273 ¶ 14 (2000) ("While it is generally not sufficient to show error by simply stating that the agency is in noncompliance where an agency has provided calculations of back pay, the agency's explanation must be sufficient to allow the Board to determine whether compliance has been effected."); Toth v. USPS, 85 MSPR 404, 408 ¶ 9 (2000) (appellant was entitled to a narrative explanation of a back pay calculation under 5 CFR 1201.183(a)(1)); Payne v. USPS, 77 MSPR 97, 100 (1997) (agency's explanation of deductions from back pay was inadequate); Welber v. USPS, 76 MSPR 601, 604 (1997) (agency was required to supply a narrative explanation of the calculation of back pay and other benefits, along with supporting documents; if the records were unavailable, the agency must use the most relevant available records to determine the appellant's entitlements); Malloy v. OPM, 73 MSPR 179, 182 (1997) (remanding appeal involving a challenge to retirement calculations for clarification by OPM of a computer printout containing unexplained entries and codes); Franklin v. DOJ, 70 MSPR 554, 556 (1996) ("[w]e note that the administrative judge did not supplement the record with worksheets explaining the calculations for the second of the two agency back-pay payments.... [a]n agency is not in compliance with the Board's regulations where it does not adequately explain how it calculated the interest on its back-pay award."); Keenan v. USPS, 69 MSPR 497, 502 (1996) ("Without explanation, payroll records that include only abbreviations and numbers aid us little in determining whether an agency has complied with a Board order. See 5 CFR 1201.183(a)(1)(i) (1994) (requiring an agency to submit a narrative explanation of the calculation of back pay and other benefits)."); Ocbina v. OPM, 67 MSPR 388, 390-91 (1995) (agency failed to provide a proper accounting of a retirement annuity computation following the initiation of enforcement proceedings: "[t]he agency did not provide any evidence as to how the amount to be paid was calculated, when the appellant will be paid, or how the amount that is already past due will be disbursed. In sum, the agency has merely stated an intention to comply, but has not produced any evidence of actual compliance."); Green v. USPS, 67 MSPR 62, 66 (1995) (ordering hearing to determine if the agency fully complied with Board orders awarding the appellant back pay, interest, benefits, and restoration of annual leave; the agency was directed to submit a "complete annotated account to the appellant explaining how it has fully complied"); Blanchard v. DOJ, 40 MSPR 513, 515 (1989) ("A submission of satisfactory evidence of compliance by the agency must include an explanation of how it arrived at each of these figures, as well as a correct tallying of the total back pay award."); Dancer v. USPS, 36 MSPR 235, 238-39 (1988) ("[t]he agency has not served the appellant or the Board with any agency form showing the appellant's sick leave balance as of the date that he was removed[.]"); cf. Pokrivnak v. USPS, 93 MSPR 134, 137 ¶ 11 (2002) ("[t]here is no understandable and convincing explanation in the record, from either party, regarding the payments due for the various items set forth in the settlement agreement[.]"); Ward v. VA, 74 MSPR 171, 173 (1997) (ordering agency to assist the appellant in preparing forms or providing other assistance to aid the appellant in squaring back pay issues with the IRS or Social Security Administration).

Bruton v. VA, 111 MSPR 489, 496-97 ¶ 17, 2009 MSPB 110 (2009) (following remand, Bruton v. VA, 112 MSPR 313, 2009 MSPB 149 (2009)), offered guidance on how an agency is to explain its back pay calculations:

To be in compliance regarding the provision of back pay, interest on back pay, and benefits, the agency must provide a detailed and clear explanation of the calculations it has made in determining the amount due the appellant. Among other things, the agency must: 1) clearly set forth the gross amount due the appellant and show how that amount was determined; 2) clearly set forth the amount and reason for all deductions, reductions, and offsets from the gross amount due the appellant;[1] 3) clearly set forth the source and amount of all checks or electronic payments already received by the appellant and provide evidence that such checks or electronic payments were received;[2] and 4) clearly set forth the amount of interest due the appellant and how that amount was calculated. The agency must also clearly set forth its calculations relating to the appellant's sick and annual leave balances, his Thrift Savings Plan account, including both the appellant's and the agency's contributions, and any other benefits of employment the appellant would have received but for the agency's unwarranted personnel action. Finally, the agency must take the appropriate steps to restore the appellant's health insurance benefits and provide evidence that it has done so. In addition to the calculations, the agency must provide a clear and detailed narrative explanation of its calculations so that the Board may understand the calculations and verify that they are correct. The agency must provide an explanation of all codes and abbreviations used in its documentation.

[1] There is no basis for the appellant's assertion that the agency should pay all deductions from his back pay. Under the Back Pay Act, an agency is required to deduct from a back pay award the appropriate amounts for retirement, Medicare, federal and state taxes, and any payment the appellant received for accrued annual leave. Tanaka v. Department of the Navy, 788 F.2d 1552, 1553-54 (Fed. Cir. 1986); Hargett v. Department of the Navy, 82 M.S.P.R. 180, ¶ 6 (1999). Similarly, in determining the amount of back pay due the appellant, the agency may consider the compensation he has received from the Office of Workers' Compensation Programs (OWCP). White v. U.S. Postal Service, 110 M.S.P.R. 461, ¶ 10 (2009) (stating that an appellant is not entitled to back pay for the period he was receiving OWCP benefits); Special Counsel ex rel. Steen v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 81 M.S.P.R. 601, ¶ 9 (1999) (stating that the employee was only entitled to the back pay he would have received beyond the OWCP payments he already received).

[2] To the extent that the appellant has returned to the agency checks previously provided to him, the agency should either reissue those checks or incorporate the amount of those checks in any new payment to the appellant. To the extent that the appellant may have returned checks issued by other agencies, such as OWCP or OPM, to those agencies, the appellant must contact those agencies to determine if the checks can be reissued.

Once an issue, e.g., back pay entitlement, is raised during enforcement proceedings, the appellant must provide to the region all the information and argument pertinent to that issue. The region should ensure that the appellant has the opportunity to respond to agency submissions, changing appellant's address, if necessary, during the course of proceedings. Murray v. Dept. of Treasury, 38 MSPR 422, 425 (1988) (appellant was denied the right to file information or conduct discovery on his case when AJ sent an acknowledgment order on the enforcement case to the wrong address).

If an agency offers calculations concerning back pay to show that it has complied with a petition, the appellant must supply more than assertions that the calculations are incorrect. Specifics must be provided to the Board demonstrating the agency's error. See Shobert v. Dept. of Air Force, 90 MSPR 262, 266 ¶ 8 (2001) ("Where an agency offers calculations concerning back pay to show that it has complied, the appellant must supply more than assertions that the calculations are incorrect."); Martinez v. USPS, 89 MSPR 130, 132 ¶ 3 (2001) ("Specifics must be provided to the Board demonstrating the agency's error.") (partially overruled as to TSP calculations by Bills v. Dept. of Air Force, 122 MSPR 367, 2015 MSPB 25 (2015)); Camastro v. DOJ, 80 MSPR 326, 327 (1998) (once the agency offered an accounting of the benefits paid or due to the appellant, it was insufficient for the appellant simply to contend that payment figure was "too low"); Singletary v. USPS, 79 MSPR 76, 79 (1998) ("Where an agency has provided calculations of back pay, an appellant must generally offer more than an unsupported assertion that the calculations are incorrect. Lavelle v. Dept. of Navy, 44 MSPR 531, 532 (1990) ("The Agency makes a general statement that it has completed all of the items in the agreement. However, its evidence of compliance does not include any specific documentation of its compliance with the Board order. Nor does the agency's evidence of compliance include sufficient explanation regarding the steps it has taken to comply with the Board order."). However, the agency must provide sufficient information to allow the appellant to raise a specific objection to its evidence. Cross v. Department of Transportation, 27 MSPR 588, 589-90 (1985)."), followed, Kruger v. VA, 94 MSPR 106, 108 ¶ 4 (2003) ("If an agency offers calculations concerning back pay to show that it has complied with a petition [for enforcement], the appellant must supply more than assertions that the calculations are incorrect"; "[s]pecifics must be provided to the Board demonstrating the agency's error."); Alexander v. USPS, 86 MSPR 434, 437 ¶ 8 (2000) ("While the appellant suggests that the agency may have failed to compensate him for approximately $100.00 of back pay, he does not point to any particular error of the agency."); Shaishaa v. Dept. of Air Force, 65 MSPR 273, 275 (1994) ("The appellant does not explain why he believes that the interest was calculated quarterly or what the correct award should be, but rather makes conclusory statements which are unsupported by evidence."); cf. Murphy-Martin v. VA, DC-1221-16-0259-X-1 (NP 3/9/2023) ("Although the agency's submissions do not provide a clear explanation of its compliance actions, the appellant, who is represented by counsel, has not responded to them, despite being informed of her opportunity to do so and the consequences of not responding. Accordingly, the Board assumes she is satisfied with the agency's compliance. See Baumgartner v. Department of Housing and Urban Development, 111 M.S.P.R. 86, ¶ 9 (2009).").

Inadequate information from the Defense Finance and Accounting Service does not excuse failure properly to account for back pay, Perlick v. VA, NY-1221-19-0052-X-1 (NP 9/27/2020):

...The agency also did not produce any documentary evidence to the Board to support any calculations it might have made. Instead, the agency produced SF-50s, which show that personnel actions were taken in accordance with the Board's underlying decision. While the SF-50s reflect the appellant's gross annual salary, they do not show "the gross amount due the appellant" for the relevant time periods, "the amount and reasons for all deductions, reductions, and offsets from the gross amount," "the amount of interest due the appellant," or the appellant's benefits. Bruton, 111 M.S.P.R. 489, ¶ 17. Given that the agency has not provided "a clear explanation of its compliance efforts supported by understandable documentary evidence," Bruton, 111 M.S.P.R. 489, ¶ 13, we determine that it has not met its burden of proof. This burden rests with the agency, and the agency cannot evade its responsibility by relying on unexplained calculations from its payor, DFAS. See Raymond v. Department of the Navy, 116 M.S.P.R. 223, ¶¶ 14-17 (2011) (rejecting "unsworn, vague" DFAS memorandum and spreadsheet as evidence of compliance with the agency's agreement to provide back pay to the appellant, in part because they lacked narrative explanation of calculations); see also Tichenor v. Department of the Army, 84 M.S.P.R. 386, ¶ 8 (1999) (rejecting the agency's argument that DFAS, not the agency, improperly withheld payment from the appellant because DFAS is the agency's agent and no indication existed that DFAS would have refused to pay the appellant if the agency ordered it to do so); Bradstreet v. Department of the Navy, 83 M.S.P.R. 288, ¶ 12 (1999) (holding that "any failure on the part of DFAS ‘to follow through' or its ‘extraordinary' processing requirements" did not "excuse the agency's substantial delay" in complying with interim relief order). The agency must timely work with DFAS to supply the required information in a form that the Board can understand.

The appellant cannot be expected to provide specific objections to an agency compliance response when the agency has not provided a specific accounting of back pay calculations, Cross v. Dept. of Transp., 27 MSPR 588, 589-90 (1985):

Appellant filed a petition for enforcement of a final Board decision which reversed his removal for participation in the nationwide air traffic controllers' strike of 1981. The Director of the Chicago Regional Office found that the agency had failed to pay appellant the back pay due him for overtime he would have worked but for his wrongful removal. The Director certified the case to the Board for processing pursuant to 5 CFR § 1201.184 on March 1, 1985. The Board subsequently received from the agency copies of letters to appellant notifying him of the amount of his overtime back pay award, and the approximate date when he would receive it. In a supplemental submission the agency informed the Board that appellant's award had been recalculated and would be increased by 10 to 20 percent. The agency said that appellant would be notified of the amount of the supplemental award by April 8, 1985.

On March 14, 1985, in light of the agency's evidence of compliance, the Board gave appellant 30 days to show cause why his petition for enforcement should not be dismissed as moot.

Appellant responded by stating that, while he agrees with the methodology employed in calculating his overtime, he objects to the amount of the award, and believes it to be inaccurate by at least 50 percent. Appellant further stated that he had asked the personnel office at the facility where he works for the data used to make the calculations, but was advised that the office did not have the data.

The agency responded on April 8, 1985, by arguing that appellant's objections are speculative and thus not specific enough to meet the requirements of the Board's regulation, 5 CFR § 1201.181(a). The agency also stated that it is providing appellant with a computer generated document showing him the average overtime calculations at his facility and the specific calculations for appellant.

We find that appellant's allegations are specific enough, under the circumstances to meet the Board's criteria. Appellant was unable to be more specific without the underlying data, which the agency did not originally give him. However, the agency has stated its intention to provide him with that data, and appellant will be able to state specific objection after receiving it.

Of the appellant's obligation to supply information to the agency to secure back pay, Russo v. USPS, 107 MSPR 296, 300-01 ¶ 10, 2007 MSPB 277 (2007), determined the agency should not delay payment of undisputed amounts of back pay because the appellant fails to complete, or delays the completion of a form, that is not necessary to the back pay calculation and relates to some other aspect of the remedy, overtime or holiday pay calculations.

If the AJ sets a date for the close of a record on enforcement disputes, and that date allows for an agency submission that the appellant may wish to challenge, the appellant should either object to the AJ's ruling and request an opportunity for the reply or, assuming no objection and no modification of the date, the appellant can submit information to the AJ after the close of record date and before the AJ's compliance decision, or to the Board on PFR, Wilkes v. VA, DA-0432-11-0466-C-1 (NP 9/22/2015):

...The appellant neither objected to this notice when it was issued nor attempted to file a response to the agency's submission during the month between the close of the record and the issuance of the compliance initial decision. The appellant's failure to timely object to the administrative judge's ruling precludes him from doing so on petition for review. See McCarthy v. International Boundary & Water Commission, 116 M.S.P.R. 594, ¶ 25 (2011) (finding that the appellant's failure to timely object to rulings during the hearing precludes his doing so on petition for review), aff'd, 497 F. App'x 4 (Fed. Cir. 2012), cert. denied, 134 S. Ct. 386 (2013). Moreover, the appellant was given multiple opportunities to rebut the agency's compliance evidence below, and he now has had the opportunity to raise any outstanding compliance issues through his petition for review. The administrative judge did not abuse his discretion by determining that the record would close after the agency had one final opportunity to respond to the compliance issues that remained after considerable efforts by both parties to resolve those issues.
