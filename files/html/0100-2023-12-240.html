L. STAY REQUESTS

5 USC 705 provides:

When an agency finds that justice so requires, it may postpone the effective date of action taken by it, pending judicial review. On such conditions as may be required and to the extent necessary to prevent irreparable injury, the reviewing court, including the court to which a case may be taken on appeal from or on application for certiorari or other writ to a reviewing court, may issue all necessary and appropriate process to postpone the effective date of an agency action or to preserve status or rights pending conclusion of the review proceedings.

Section 705 does not explicitly apply to the Board, but it is applied by the Board, e.g., Special Counsel v. Hathaway, 52 MSPR 375, 377 (1992), and in its discretion, the Board may stay the enforcement of a final decision pending judicial review. The stay considerations include: (1) the likelihood of success on appeal, (2) irreparable harm, (3) substantial harm to other interested parties, and (4) the public interest. Special Counsel v. Camillieri, 35 MSPR 170, 173 (1987) (Hatch Act). The Board does not give equal weight to these factors. If the latter three factors strongly favor the issuance of a stay, the stay will be issued if a serious legal question exists on the merits. Special Counsel v. Bianchi, 59 MSPR 282, 285 (1993) (Hatch Act); see Cofield v. GPO, 26 MSPR 261, 264 (1985) ("In each case, the Board weighs the likelihood of success against the other three criteria. In those cases where the last three criteria strongly favor the granting of a stay, a stay will be granted if a serious legal question exists on the merits. If support for a stay by the last three criteria is slight, however, a stay will be granted only if the likelihood of success is strong."). That the Board reverses an initial decision does not suggest the likelihood of success of a case brought in court to review the Board final decision. See Harris v. DOJ, 27 MSPR 397, 400 (1985) (appellant's possible permanent replacement by another employee is not irreparable harm; if the Board's order is reversed, the appellant will be restored to his former position). [Refer to Chapter 13, "Stays of Personnel Actions," and "Stay Pending Judicial Review" concerning stays requested in prohibited personnel practice appeals.]

One of the more comprehensive decisions on stays of enforcement of Board final decisions stems from litigation over construction of OPM ALJ registers, Azdell v. OPM, 88 MSPR 319, 324-27 (2001), rev'd on other grounds sub nom Meeker v. MSPB, 319 F.3d 1368 (Fed. Cir. 2003):

The Board may exercise its discretion to stay the enforcement of a final decision pending judicial, review or reconsideration. There are four factors that guide our exercise of that discretion: (1) whether the stay applicants have made a strong showing that they are likely to prevail on the merits; (2) whether the stay applicants will be irreparably harmed absent a stay; (3). whether the issuance of the stay will substantially harm the other parties interested in the proceeding; and, (4) where the public interest lies. See, e.g., Hilton v. Braunskill, 481 U.S. 770, 776, 107 S. Ct. 2113, 95 L. Ed.2d 724 (1987); Standard Havens Products, Inc. v. Gencor Industries, Inc., 897 F.2d 511, 512 (Fed. Cir. 1990); Parton v. Federal Communications Commission, 9 MSPB 258, 9 MSPR 638, 640 (1982). Since the traditional stay factors contemplate individualized judgments in each case, the formula cannot be reduced to a set of rigid rules. Hilton, 481 U.S. at 777, 107 S. Ct. 2113. Each factor need not be given equal weight. Standard Havens, 897 F.2d at 512 (citations omitted). Also, likelihood of success is not a rigid concept. Id. (citations omitted). When harm to the stay applicant is great enough, courts have stated that they will not require "a strong showing" that the applicant is "likely to succeed on the merits." Id. at 513 (citations omitted).

For the reasons set forth below, we conclude that a 60 day stay is warranted, based primarily on the factors of irreparable harm and the public interest.

Irreparable Harm

With respect to this factor, the intervenors maintain that the Board's decision requires OPM to develop its lists and certificates of eligible ALJ appointees only from an approved list of class members. They assert that there are likely to be a relatively small number of ALJ appointments during the next several years, and, given that the intervenors are "forbidden" from being placed on a register, they will never be eligible for ALJ appointments. In addition, the intervenors have provided evidence to the Federal Circuit which they allege shows that OPM has begun providing priority consideration to the class members. The appellants' stay request asks that the Board stay its decision until OPM has fully disclosed its process for implementing priority consideration.

We note that at the April 5, 2001 status conference, SSA indicated that it plans to hire 120 ALJs as early as the week of April 16th. At this point, there is no way to verify the validity of the lists of eligibles provided to SSA. Absent a detailed explanation of how OPM reconstructed the certificates of eligibles and identified these individuals, and an opportunity to review the documents used in the process, the Board cannot ascertain whether OPM has correctly determined who is entitled to priority consideration, pursuant to the Board's October 2000 decision. If SSA fills the 120 ALJ positions by acting on the certificates issued to it by OPM, it will be almost impossible to reverse that action. United States v. Testan, 424 U.S. 392, 402, 96 S. Ct. 948, 47 L. Ed.2d 114 (1976). On this ground, we find that the intervenors have made a strong showing of irreparable harm if a stay is not granted.

Substantial Harm to the Other Parties

This factor requires that we consider whether the issuance of a stay will substantially harm the other parties interested in the proceeding. Thus, we have considered the representations made by the SSA, as a neutral participant at the status conference. The SSA representatives informed the Board that the agency had a pressing need to fill 120 ALJ job vacancies because SSA's ALJ corps had significantly decreased in the past two years and because of an increased work load. They indicated that the agency planned to make offers by April 16, 2001, to candidates on certificates of eligibles provided to SSA by OPM. They further indicated that would begin training for new ALJs on May 21, 2001. On this basis SSA contends that a stay will cause substantial harm to its ability to provide public services. However, we conclude that any harm based on the hiring delay that would result if a stay were granted is significantly outweighed by the harm that would result from allowing ALJs to be hired in the absence of any evidence that OPM has properly reconstructed the ALJ register, as required by the Board's decision on the merits. Critical to our consideration of this factor is the fact that, if a candidate is hired from an improperly reconstructed certificate of eligibles, remedying this mistake would be extremely difficult, if not impossible. Testan, 424 US. at 402. 96 S. Ct. 948.

The Public Interest

In addressing this factor, the intervenors emphasize that the veterans' preference is a policy mandated by Congress. They argue that the Board, by ordering priority consideration for nonveterans, has effectively disqualified veterans from ALJ appointments and state that "[t]he American public deserves to have a group of administrative law judge appointees whose ranks include, rather than exclude, qualified veterans." In their view, this case will decide the "status and legitimacy" of the veterans' preference rights. The appellants, who also have requested a stay, responded that the, remedy ordered by the Board would not eliminate veterans from the ALJ examination process and that, in fact, qualified veterans would still be able to compete for ALJ Appointments. Balancing these competing factors, we, conclude that the public interest is best served by insuring fairness to all ALJ applicants, whether or not preference eligible. In addition, we find that the public interest favors an opportunity for the Board to reconsider its decision on the merits, as OPM has requested, prior to the implementation of the ordered relief.

Likelihood of Prevailing on the Merits

Finally, we consider whether the intervenors have made a strong showing that they are likely to prevail on the merits. They argue that the Board's decision is erroneous because, inter alia, the Board 1) does not have jurisdiction because the matter appealed does not constitute an "employment practice" pursuant to 5 CFR § 300.104(a); 2) did not grant the appropriate deference to OPM's policymaking; 3) erroneously construed the application of veterans' preference points; 4) does not have authority to order priority consideration of class members; and 5) failed to provide due process to veterans' preference ALJ applicants. The appellants provide counterarguments on each of these points. While it is not clear whether the intervenors will prevail on the merits, we find that the stay requests filed by both the intervenors and the appellants demonstrate that the issues involved in this case are both serious and complex.

Because of this fact, and the substantial showing that the intervenors have made on the other three stay criteria, we conclude that a stay is warranted. Briggs v. National Council on Disability, 68 MSPR 296, 299 (1995) (Where the stay, applicant convincingly argues the last three criteria are met, a stay will be issued if a serious question exists on the merits).

Length and conditions of the stay

In reviewing these factors and in attempting to achieve an equitable result, we find that the relief ordered in Azdell should be stayed for 60 days from the date of this order. The Board's decision on the merits, holding that the 1996 scoring formula is invalid, remains in effect and is not stayed by this order.

This 60-day period is necessary in order to give the appellants' representatives the opportunity to examine and consider OPM's compliance efforts, to give the Board the opportunity to review OPM's information and evidence, and to insure that the certificates of eligibles are properly reconstructed. This time will allow OPM to explain its compliance efforts and to provide supporting documentation (including but not limited to a detailed narrative explanation of its efforts and illustrative examples of how it recalculated candidates' scores), the appellants' representatives to evaluate and to respond to OPM's explanation and evidence, and the Board to consider and assess the parties' submissions on this matter. In sum, the Board believes that an additional stay will insure that agencies do not select ALJ candidates from certificates which may be improperly constructed.
