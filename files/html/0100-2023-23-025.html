9. Modification, Amendment of Agreement

The parties may modify a settlement agreement after its execution and the Board will retain jurisdiction to determine if the parties are in compliance with the modifications. See Kemp v. USPS, 44 MSPR 10, 14 (1990), followed in Frizzell v. Dept. of Air Force, 53 MSPR 413, 416 (1992) ("However, the Board has no authority to require the parties to modify the agreement. See Cranfield v. TVA, 44 MSPR 384, 387 (1990)."). Hutchison v. USPS, 39 MSPR 327, 330-31 (1988), outlined the basic requirements concerning changes in the obligations imposed by settlement agreements:

A contract may be modified at any time after it is executed...and an agreement to modify existing obligations after formation of the contract may be inferred from the conduct of the parties.... The burden of proving that an oral modification has occurred, however, rests on the party asserting that the original agreement has been altered.

Hutchinson also noted that there "is no requirement that modifications to a settlement agreement in a case before the Board be in writing." Id. at 331 n.5. See Kemp v. USPS, 44 MSPR 10, 14 (1990) ("the parties may modify a settlement agreement after its execution and...the Board will retain jurisdiction to determine if the parties are in compliance with the modifications").

Roache v. USPS, DC-0752-09-0130-X-1 (NP 5/25/2011), noted of an addendum to a settlement:

We note that the March 8, 2011 addendum to the settlement agreement did not explicitly request that the addendum be entered into the record for enforcement purposes or that the Board retain jurisdiction for enforcement. However, the addendum specified that it was "not intended to, and shall not have the effect of, modifying or altering any other term or provision of" the September 1, 2010 settlement agreement. The September 1, 2010 settlement agreement, as memorialized in the administrative judge's Initial Decision, was entered into the record for enforcement purposes so that the Board would have jurisdiction to enforce it. Failure likewise to retain jurisdiction of the addendum would alter a provision of the settlement agreement, in violation of the addendum. Accordingly, we construe the addendum as requesting that the Board enter its terms into the record for enforcement purposes. We also find that the parties have entered into an addendum to the settlement agreement, that they understand its terms, and that they want the Board to enforce those terms. See Mahoney v. U.S. Postal Service, 37 M.S.P.R. 146, 149 (1988).

Before accepting a settlement agreement (or the terms thereof) into the record for enforcement purposes, the Board must determine whether the agreement is lawful on its face, whether the parties freely entered into it, and whether its subject matter is within the Board's jurisdiction. See Stewart v. U.S. Postal Service, 73 M.S.P.R. 104, 107 (1997). We find here that the addendum is lawful on its face, that the parties freely entered into it, and that the subject matter of this case--the appellant's appeal of his removal for physical inability to perform the requirements of his position--is within the Board's jurisdiction. See 5 U.S.C. §§ 7511(a)(1)(B), 7512, 7513(d), and 7701(a). Accordingly, we accept the addendum to the settlement agreement into the record for enforcement purposes.

Modifications must be mutual, Dunakoskie v. Nat'l. Capital Planning Comm'n., DC-0432-10-0179-C-1 (NP 8/9/2011):

...The burden of proving that a modification of the agreement has occurred rests on the party asserting that the original agreement has been altered. Rothwell v. U.S. Postal Service, 64 M.S.P.R. 473, 477 (1994), see Hutchison v. U.S. Postal Service, 39 M.S.P.R. 327, 331 (1988). The conduct of the parties in this case implies that no modification occurred, but that the agency was seeking to be helpful to the appellant and provided access that it was not obliged to provide. Therefore, the emails with respect to the agency's willingness to let the appellant use the agency's email or telephone systems are not contractual amendments subject to the Board's jurisdiction.

Modification of a settlement requires the parties' mutual assent. Carson v. Dept. of Energy, 77 MSPR 453, 458 (1998) (citing 1 Arthur L. Corbin, Corbin on Contracts § 9 (1963)), following remand, Carson v. Dept. of Energy, 79 MSPR 502, 504 (1998); Durham v. TVA, 42 MSPR 480, 484 (1989) (citing Caffrey Farms, Inc. v. Williams Pipe Line Co., 739 F.2d 1366, 1368 (8th Cir. 1984), for the proposition that "one party to a contract cannot alter its terms without assent of the other; the minds of the parties must meet as to the proposed modification"). If a filed agreement is amended or superseded, the parties must file and obtain the Board's acceptance of the subsequent document, if it is to be enforced. Cf. Drummond v. USPS, 47 MSPR 289, 291 (1991) ("The...agreement, however, is not incorporated into the previous agreement, and thus is still not part of the official appeal record in this case. It therefore remains unenforceable by the Board."). Determining whether the appellant seeks modification of an agreement or whether he seeks to enforce an agreement that he claims should have included what the Board is treating as a modification can be complex. In Keeley v. USPS, 53 MSPR 650, 652 (1992), apparently there was a settlement agreement allowing the appellant to resign from his position. During enforcement proceedings, the appellant alleged that in a telephone conversation with the AJ, the appellant said he would resign only if he received a set amount of back pay. The AJ subsequently denied an enforcement petition seeking the back pay, determining that the agreement did not provide for the relief. The AJ, however, failed to address the appellant's contention that he imposed conditions on the settlement during his telephone call to the AJ. Keeley concluded that the "allegation that the requested relief was incorporated into the agreement through his [the appellant's] telephone call with the administrative judge is unsupported by the record." The Board also noted that the appellant had a representative and did not seek to incorporate additional terms into the agreement. Unfortunately, the Board's decision does not explain why the appellant's explanation of his conversation with the AJ did not constitute record evidence. Although the analysis in Keeley is less than complete, it makes the point that attempted oral modifications of settlements should be documented and properly recorded with the Board. See Cook v. Dept. of Army, DE-0752-16-0285-I-1 (NP 9/16/2022) (when a settlement agreement was entered into the record, but a correction to that agreement was not, on PFR the Board considered the appellant's allegation that the correction was fraudulently induced).
