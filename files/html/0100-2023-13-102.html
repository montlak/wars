d. EEO Settlement

In addition to the sources of light duty obligations, i.e., agency policies, regulations, contracts, or accommodative requirements, an EEO settlement was the basis for the agency's obligation to provide light duty in McLain v. USPS, 82 MSPR 526, 529-30 ¶¶ 8-11 (1999). McLain was constructively suspended when he was assigned light duty work beyond that allowed by his medical restrictions. The agency committed to providing the employee with light duty work through an EEO settlement. OWCP determined that the appellant could not perform the work that was assigned. Because the agency assigned work beyond the employee's capabilities, and in apparent violation of its settlement agreement, the employee was constructively suspended:

Here, the appellant has worked with certain medical restrictions since 1988 when he became a Mail Processor, and has, in fact, never performed the full range of that position's duties. At first, the restrictions were the result of a settlement agreement reached between the parties in connection with an appeal the appellant had filed with the Board. In 1993, the restrictions were increased based on the results of a fitness-for-duty examination that was conducted pursuant to a settlement agreement reached between the parties in connection with an equal employment opportunity complaint the appellant had filed. Those restrictions were in place at the time in question. Thus, the agency was bound to comply with them. The issue before the administrative judge was whether the agency was meeting its requirement of offering the appellant work within those restrictions on or about July 26, 1996, at the time he absented himself from work, and the administrative judge found, on the record before her, that it was.

The appellant, however, has submitted evidence with his petition for review which challenges that finding. The evidence is a December 10, 1997, decision issued by OWCP on his request for reconsideration of an earlier decision.... The decision concludes that the appellant was "not provided suitable light-limited duty within his work tolerance limitations at the time he stopped work on July 25, 1996," because both of his described duties included repetitive bending, lifting, stooping, reaching, and twisting for up to eight hours per day, work that exceeded his limitations. While OWCP's decisions generally do not bind the Board, see Cox v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 817 F.2d 100, 101 (Fed. Cir. 1987), that is the case only when the Board or the agency acts within its own statutory sphere of authority. See Minor v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 819 F.2d 280, 283 (Fed. Cir. 1987). Decisions on the suitability of an offered position are within the exclusive domain of OWCP, and it is that agency, not the employing agency and not the Board, which possesses the requisite expertise to evaluate whether a position is suitable in light of that employee's particular medical condition. See 20 CFR § 10.124(c); New v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 142 F.3d 1259, 1264 (Fed. Cir. 1998); Garza v. Office of Personnel Management, MSPB Docket No. DE-844E-97-0538-I-1, slip op. at 6 (Sept. 14, 1998).

Based on OWCP's determination that the agency was required to, but did not, offer the appellant work within his medical restrictions beginning on or about July 25, 1996, and continuing until the present, we find that the agency subjected him to a constructive suspension for more than fourteen days. We further find that, since the agency provided him with none of the procedural protections of 5 USC § 7513(b), its action violated his constitutional right to minimum due process and cannot be sustained. See Tyler v. U.S. Postal Service, 62 MSPR 509, 514 (1994). Furthermore, because the appellant's suspension is an appealable action, he must be afforded an opportunity to develop and prove his allegations of disability discrimination and reprisal for protected activity. See Polite v. Department of the Navy, 49 MSPR 653, 657 (1991).

Despite the absence of a final decision on the appellant's discrimination and reprisal allegations, under the circumstances of this case, we find it appropriate that the agency now cancel its suspension action and restore the appellant to his position. See 5 USC § 1204(a)(1) and (2). The continued processing of, and ultimate decision on, the discrimination and reprisal issues can have no effect on the appellant's entitlement to return to duty under our holding in this Opinion and Order. Tyler, 62 MSPR at 513.
