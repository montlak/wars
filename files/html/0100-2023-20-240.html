3. Extension of Stay

Special Counsel v. Dept. of Interior, 68 MSPR 266, 268-69 (1995), explained the nature of the Board's authority to extend a stay granted to OSC:

Under 5 USC § 1214(b)(1)(B), a statute enacted as part of the Whistleblower Protection Act of 1989, the Board may grant extensions of stays at its discretion while viewing the record in the light most favorable to the Special Counsel. Special Counsel v. Federal Emergency Management Agency, 44 MSPR 544, 545-46 (1990). The use of the term "may" in the statute demonstrates that the Board must exercise its discretion in determining whether the stay should be extended. Under this standard, the Board will grant the Special Counsel's request for an extension if the Special Counsel's prohibited personnel practice claim is not clearly unreasonable. Id. This standard is consistent with the legislative history, which shows that Congress intended to make it easier for the Special Counsel to obtain a stay under the Whistleblower Protection Act of 1989. Id.

Special Counsel ex rel. Tines v. VA, 98 MSPR 510, 511 ¶¶ 3, 5 (2005), noted that, "In evaluating a request for an extension of a stay, the Board will view the record in a light most favorable to OSC and will grant an extension provided OSC's prohibited personnel practice claim is not clearly unreasonable," adding:

The agency has filed comments with evidentiary support in opposition to OSC's request for an extension of the stay. The agency asserts that the Board should not grant the extension because OSC's factual assertions fail to show that it has a reasonable basis for believing that the agency has engaged in a prohibited personnel practice. A stay proceeding is not intended to be a substitute for a complete hearing on the merits of a prohibited personnel practice claim. Acting Special Counsel ex rel. Finkel v. Department of Labor, 93 M.S.P.R. 409 ¶ 11 (2003). OSC's stay request need merely fall within the range of rationality to be granted. Office of Special Counsel, ex rel. Hopkins v. Department of Transportation, 90 M.S.P.R. 154 ¶ 6 (2001). The Board reviews the facts as alleged by OSC in the light most favorable to a finding that there are reasonable grounds to believe that a prohibited personnel practice was (or will be) committed. Special Counsel ex rel. Rigdon v. Department of the Army, 98 M.S.P.R. 110 ¶ 2 n.1 (2004).

See Special Counsel ex rel. Cefalu v. DOJ, CB-1208-13-0006-U-2 (NP 12/6/2012) ("A stay proceeding is not intended to be a substitute for a complete hearing on the merits of a prohibited personnel practice claim. See, e.g., Special Counsel v. Department of Transportation, 74 M.S.P.R. at 157. OSC's stay request need merely fall within the range of rationality to be granted. Special Counsel ex rel. Tines v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 98 M.S.P.R. 510, ¶ 5 (2005). Given the points and authorities set forth by OSC, we cannot say that its prohibited personnel practice theory is irrational or clearly unreasonable.").

During a stay, the employee for whom the stay is granted is expected to conform to ordinary workplace standards, Special Counsel ex rel. Klein v. VA, CB-1208-16-0023-U-7 (NP 7/13/2017):

Although OSC's request for an extension is being granted, nothing in this order, or any prior order in this matter, should be interpreted as authorizing Dr. Klein to refuse to comply with a lawful instruction to return to work. Dr. Klein's return to the status quo ante does not invest him with any special rights to dictate or unilaterally change the terms of his employment.

See Special Counsel ex rel. Missal v. Dept. of Interior, CB-1208-17-0025-U-2 n.5 (NP 9/15/2017) ("it is noted that the agency contends that Mr. Missal has continued to engage in misconduct that was the subject of his removal since the imposition of the stay. The Board's stay orders have temporarily returned Mr. Missal to the status quo ante, but they do not insulate him from appropriate charges based on misconduct while so employed. See Special Counsel ex rel. Perfetto v. Department of the Navy, 85 M.S.P.R. 454, ¶ 24 (2000).").

Special Counsel ex rel. Klein v. VA, 2017 MSPB 6, ¶ 7 (2016), recognized that when the Board is without a quorum, no stay extension could be granted under the legislation then existing, since an extension required a vote of the full Board (with at least quorum):

At present, there is only one Board member and the Board lacks a quorum. Thus, the Board lacks the authority to act upon OSC's request for an extension of the stay. However, Congress has recently passed legislation amending 5 U.S.C. § 1214 to allow an individual Board member to extend a stay granted under section 1214(b)(1)(A) during periods when the Board lacks a quorum. See S. 1083 115th Cong. (as passed by the House of Representatives on May 25, 2017, and by the Senate on June 14, 2017). The Vice Chairman has publicly stated support for this legislation and has so informed the White House Office of Policy Development and the Office of Management and Budget of this support. There does not appear to be any opposition to the legislation's enactment. Following the enactment of this legislation, OSC again can petition the Board for an extension of the previously granted stay. Given the unusual circumstances present in this case and the fact that the Board has been without a quorum since January 7, 2017, should OSC request an extension of the stay following enactment of the pending legislation, OSC also may include a request that the stay be granted retroactive to May 13, 2017.

The bill referenced in Klein was enacted on June 27, 2017, Pub. L. 115-42:

To amend section 1214 of title 5, United States Code, to provide for stays during a period that the Merit Systems Protection Board lacks a quorum.

Section 1214(b)(1)(B) of title 5, United States Code, is amended--

(1) by inserting "(i)" before "The Board may"; and

(2) by adding at the end the following: "(ii) If the Board lacks the number of members appointed under section 1201 required to constitute a quorum, any remaining member of the Board who was appointed, by and with the advice and consent of the Senate, may, upon request by the Special Counsel, extend the period of any stay granted under subparagraph (A).'"

The stay was then extended. Special Counsel ex rel. Klein v. VA, CB-1208-16-0023-U-7 (NP 6/29/2017).

Board regulations at 5 CFR 1201.136(b) (2022) contemplate that an extension be requested at least fifteen days before the expiration of a stay and that an agency response be "received by the Board no later than 8 days before the expiration date of the stay." The Board suggests facsimile filing of the response to permit adequate time for its consideration. See Special Counsel v. SSA, 77 MSPR 22, 25 (1997); Special Counsel v. SSA, 77 MSPR 18, 20-21 (1997) (encouraging the parties to use expedited means of service, rather than the mails, for stay extension pleadings). The regulations are not closely followed in practice. The Board will grant a belated request for a stay extension, on motion for the extension of the filing deadline, or without the motion for the filing deadline extension when the respondent agency does not oppose the extension. See Special Counsel ex rel. Bricker v. DHS, 98 MSPR 185, 187 ¶¶ 7-8 (2005); cf. Special Counsel ex rel. Missal v. Dept. of Interior, CB-1208-17-0025-U-3 (10/26/2017) ("Moreover, pursuant to 5 U.S.C. § 1214(b)(2)(C), the agency has 'a reasonable period of time' to correct a prohibited personnel practice before OSC may petition the Board for corrective action, and OSC's letter to the Secretary of the Interior asked for a response within 30 days of that letter. Because the OSC investigator's unrebutted declaration asserts that OSC sent its report to the Secretary of the Interior on October 12, 2017, the agency's response is not due until approximately 2 weeks after the current stay is set to expire. Id. at 6. In light of the factors discussed above, a 45-day extension of the stay is appropriate; thus, OSC's request is granted in part.").

An extension is normally granted, even if granted for a period longer or shorter than that requested by OSC. The length of a requested extension of a stay requires a separate Board determination. See Special Counsel v. IRS, 67 MSPR 175, 176-77 (1995). The proper length of a stay extension is based on the totality of the circumstances. Special Counsel v. Fish & Wildlife Serv., 64 MSPR 410, 412 (1994). But see Special Counsel v. Customs Serv., 31 MSPR 568, 570 (1986) (agency argument concerning the strength of an OSC case was premature relative to a request to extend a stay; the investigation was, as yet, incomplete). A stipulation between OSC and a respondent amounting to a consented extension will be considered by, but does not bind, the Board. See, e.g., Special Counsel v. Dept. of Navy, 85 MSPR 92, 96 ¶ 11 (2000); Special Counsel v. SBA, 73 MSPR 261, 263 (1997).

Too many extensions produce criticism from the Board, Special Counsel v. SSA, 78 MSPR 489, 490-91 ¶¶ 6-7 (1998):

Despite our approval of OSC's request, we note that five of its eight requests for an extension of the stay in this matter have been premised on progress toward a negotiated settlement. We further note that the two extension requests preceding the present one have been for 21 days each, and that the present request asks for only an additional 14 days. It does not serve the efficiency of the adjudicative process for the parties and the Board to be spending excessive amounts of time and effort determining the duration of the stay. We strongly urge OSC to be as realistic as possible in the future in its assessment of the probability and timing of reaching negotiated settlements, and of the length of time that is reasonably required for an extension of a stay.

We also reiterate the observation we made in granting OSC's fourth extension request--that stays should not extend for prolonged periods of time, and that OSC should proceed with a corrective action case in a timely manner after it has secured a stay. See Special Counsel ex rel Shaw v. Social Security Administration, 77 MSPR 301, 304 (1998); Special Counsel v. U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service, 64 MSPR 413, 415 (1994). The stay in this matter has been in effect for almost eight months. Although the Board of course encourages settlement agreements in matters that come before it, see Special Counsel v. Department of the Treasury, 72 MSPR 62, 64 (1996), it is time for this matter to be either settled or proceed to the filing of a petition for corrective action.

The Board occasionally tweaks OSC by slightly reducing an extension with the result that OSC is reminded of the need to produce a bona fide explanation for an extension request, Special Counsel v. IRS, 66 MSPR 26, 28 (1994):

The length of the requested extension, however, is not supported by the request. While OSC asserts that a 60-day extension is necessary, in the event negotiations fail, to complete its investigation, write an investigative report, conduct a legal analysis, analyze the evidence and determine if further action is warranted, OSC's request for its first extension was based, as set out above, on similar reasons. Absent a more precise explanation of what remains to be done to complete OSC's investigation, we agree with the agency that the period of time requested for the extension is too long. Furthermore, the agency has asserted that OSC need not discontinue its investigation during negotiations. In view of the intent of Congress that stays not be extended for long periods of time and that the Special Counsel present any corrective action in a timely manner, we believe that an extension of 45-days is appropriate in this case....

But see Special Counsel ex rel. Schwarz v. Dept. of Navy, CB-1208-17-0022-U-4 (NP 10/12/2017) ("Although the agency opines that a 30-day extension is appropriate to allow OSC to complete its investigation, OSC is in a better position than the agency to determine how much time is needed to complete the review process. See Special Counsel v. General Services Administration, 45 M.S.P.R. 601, 605 (1990). Further, a 60-day extension would allow OSC time to initiate any future action it deems appropriate, such as drafting a prohibited personnel practice report, seeking resolution of the matter with the agency, and/or filing a petition for corrective action with the Board. See 5 U.S.C. § 1214."); cf. Special Counsel ex rel. Spalding v. Dept. of Treasury, CB-1208-22-0016-U-3 (NP 11/9/2022) ("Relevant to this determination is the fact that much of the 60-day stay extension period will take place during the fall and winter holidays, when it may be difficult to schedule witness interviews and OSC and agency personnel may be on scheduled leave, which may impact OSC's ability to complete its investigation. See Special Counsel v. Department of Transportation, 59 M.S.P.R. 559, 560 (1993) (finding that a 90-day extension of a stay was appropriate because of, inter alia, the difficulty inherent in scheduling investigatory interviews during the holiday season). For these reasons, and viewing the record in the light most favorable to OSC, we find that OSC has shown reasonable cause to justify extending the stay an additional 60 days.").

A stay extension of a removal was denied in Special Counsel ex rel. Cefalu v. DOJ, CB-1208-13-0006-U-6 (NP 6/17/2013) (Vice Chair Wagner dissenting, noting the lack of an agency objection, and suggesting just one more stay), involving an initial stay and several extensions extending for about eight months:

As explained above, this stay has been in effect since October 23, 2012, and has been extended continuously since that time pursuant to four separate extension requests from OSC, each of which the Board granted. Under the terms of the Board's most recent stay extension order, this stay will expire after June 19, 2013--nearly 8 months after it began. We think that that is long enough for OSC to decide whether it is going to pursue corrective action in this case, and we find that any further extension would be inappropriate. See 5 U.S.C. § 1214(b)(1)(B). Stays under 5 U.S.C. § 1214(b)(1) were not meant to be extended for prolonged periods of time, and the Board has an obligation to press OSC to present any corrective action case in a timely manner. Special Counsel v. Small Business Administration, 72 M.S.P.R. 306, 310 (1996) (citing Special Counsel v. Federal Emergency Management Agency, 44 M.S.P.R. 544, 546-47 (1990)). We understand that OSC is still contemplating the Department of Justice's 9-page memorandum of May 21, 2013. However, OSC has not described what issues the memorandum raised that require 2 additional weeks to consider. See Special Counsel v. U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service, 64 M.S.P.R. 413, 415-16 (1994) (denying the Special Counsel's request for a stay extension because, among other things, she failed to provide the details of the newly-discovered information underlying the request). Indeed, OSC seems already to have taken the position that this case hinges on a "narrow legal issue" and that the additional issues raised in the May 21 memorandum are "peripheral" at best. For these reasons, we will not stay this personnel action any further unless and until OSC decides to pursue corrective action. At that point, we will revisit the matter if OSC so requests.

A stay extension was denied when no reasonable basis was advanced in support of the request by OSC. Special Counsel v. Dept. of Navy, 84 MSPR 659, 662 ¶ 11 (2000).

The absence of an objection from an agency is implied consent to the extension. See Special Counsel v. Dept. of Navy, 84 MSPR 590, 592 ¶ 9 (1999); Special Counsel v. Dept. of Navy, 84 MSPR 291, 294 ¶ 7 (1999) ("The absence of any objection from the agency is implicit consent to the extension."); Special Counsel v. Dept. of Navy, 83 MSPR 468, 470 ¶ 6 (1999); Special Counsel v. DOJ, 80 MSPR 502, 504 ¶ 6 (1999) (noting absence of objection by the agency to a stay extension); cf. Special Counsel v. DHS, 97 MSPR 615, 616-17 ¶¶ 4-5 (2004) (70-day extension warranted on the basis of OSC's difficulty in interviewing a witness and the statement by OSC that the agency did not oppose the extension). A stay extension may be appropriate when the agency does not cooperate with OSC requests for information and OSC has to resort to the use of a subpoena. See Special Counsel ex rel. Waddell v. DOJ, 104 MSPR 141, 144 ¶ 5, 2006 MSPB 331 (2006).

Special Counsel v. FEMA, 44 MSPR 544, 545-47 (1990), provided guidance concerning criteria governing extensions of stays sought under the WPA, involving a request for a 90-day extension:

Under 5 USC 1214(b)(1)(B), a statute newly enacted as part of the Whistleblower Protection Act of 1989, the Board "may" extend a stay issued by a single Member "for any period which the Board considers appropriate." This differs from its predecessor, 5 USC 1208(c) (1988), in that the Board is no longer required to concur in the Special Counsel's determination that there are reasonable grounds to believe that a prohibited personnel practice has occurred."[*] Although the Board is no longer required to concur in the Special Counsel's determination before granting an extension, the statute does not make the extension automatic. The use of the term "may" in the statute demonstrates that the Board must exercise its discretion in determining whether the stay should be extended.

[*] because the Board is no longer required to concur in the Special Counsel's reasonable grounds determination, the case law developed under that provision is no longer directly applicable to requests for extensions of stays....

In exercising its discretion, the Board will view the record in the light most favorable to the Special Counsel and will grant the request if the Special Counsel's prohibited personnel claim is not clearly unreasonable....

...

[T]he period of time requested for the extension is too long. It is the intent of Congress that stays not be extended "for prolonged periods of time."... Moreover, Congress has encouraged the Board to press the Special Counsel to present any corrective action case in a timely manner. In view of this obligation, and given the Special Counsel's own description of what remains to be investigated, we believe that an extension of 45 days is appropriate....

See Special Counsel ex rel Elmers v. VA, CB-1208-15-0007-U-3 (NP 2/27/2015) (90-day initial stay extension); Special Counsel ex rel Patrick v. Dept. of Energy, CB-1208-10-0019-U-2 (NP 10/15/2010) (120-day extension granted after prior stays in a complex case requiring many OSC interviews: "Absent extraordinary circumstances, we fully anticipate that this will provide ample time for OSC to complete its investigation and that further extensions of the stay should not be necessary unless a complaint is filed by OSC."); Special Counsel ex rel Patrick v. Dept. of Energy, CB-1208-10-0019-U-3 (NP 2/11/2011) (the 2010 decision was optimistic: "We were hopeful when granting OSC's request for a 120-day extension on October 15, 2010, that OSC would be able to complete its investigation in that time period. In our consideration of OSC's request for an additional 90-day extension of the stay, we take special note of the fact that DOE does not oppose the request or the length of time requested. Accordingly, the Board finds, given the facts described and OSC's assertions regarding the scheduled interviews, the potential implications of those scheduled interviews, and the follow-up analysis required, that 90 days is an appropriate period of time to extend the stay, especially in light of DOE's acquiescence to the request."); Special Counsel v. Dept. of Navy, 84 MSPR 291, 294 ¶ 8 (1999) (extending a stay, despite any apparent advance in the investigation, to permit OSC and the agency representative to meet to discuss OSC's recommendations); Special Counsel v. DOJ, 81 MSPR 493, 495 ¶ 7 (1999) (an extension of 45 days to settle or file a corrective action request); Special Counsel v. DOD, 76 MSPR 625, 629 (1997) (limiting the extension to 45 days to press OSC to file a corrective action request); Special Counsel v. Dept. of Treasury, 71 MSPR 419, 421-22 (1996) (pressing OSC to move along); Special Counsel v. Dept. of Interior, 68 MSPR 266, 269 (1995) ("Congress has encouraged the Board to press the Special Counsel to present any corrective action case in a timely manner.").

A request for the extension of the stay would not be denied when the agency consented and OSC provided information showing that the matter was proceeding to resolution (one way or the other), Special Counsel ex rel. David Butterfield v. DHS, CB-1208-11-0018-U-3 (NP 11/16/2011):

OSC asserts that on November 1, 2011, it sent a corrective action letter to the Secretary of the Department of Homeland Security reporting that the Special Counsel had reasonable grounds to believe that Mr. Butterfield's removal, as well as other personnel actions taken against him, constituted prohibited personnel practices under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8)-(9), and recommending that the Secretary take corrective action. OSC submits a copy of its report to the Secretary and requests a 60-day extension of the stay to provide the agency with time to consider OSC's request and afford OSC time to petition the Board for corrective action if the agency does not take appropriate action. OSC has indicated that under 5 U.S.C. § 1214(b)(2)(C), the Secretary is entitled to a reasonable period of time to correct the prohibited personnel practice, and that OSC has asked the Secretary to respond to the report within 30 days. The agency does not oppose OSC's request for an extension of the stay but asserts that by not opposing the stay it does not admit or concede any findings or conclusions set forth in OSC's report.

Under the circumstances set forth above, we find that a further stay is appropriate. See Special Counsel ex rel. Feilke v. Department of Defense Dependents Schools, 76 M.S.P.R. 625, 627-29 (1997); Special Counsel v. Department of the Navy, 67 M.S.P.R. 614, 616 (1995). However, the length of the extension requires a separate determination. See Waddell, 104 M.S.P.R. 141, ¶ 7. It is the intent of Congress that stays not be extended "for prolonged periods of time." Special Counsel v. Department of the Treasury, 71 M.S.P.R. 419, 421 (1996). Thus, the Board has an obligation to press OSC to present corrective action cases in a timely manner. Waddell, 104 M.S.P.R. 141, ¶ 7. Given OSC's submission of a report to the Secretary and the agency's lack of opposition to this stay request, a 60-day extension of the stay is warranted, and we GRANT OSC's request. See Special Counsel v. Internal Revenue Service, 67 M.S.P.R. 175, 176 (1995). Nevertheless, because a response to OSC's report should be forthcoming within 30 days of November 1, 2011, we find that OSC and the agency are being given ample time with this 60-day extension to either resolve this matter or have OSC file a petition for corrective action. In this regard, we note that the agency is only entitled to a "reasonable period of time" to correct a prohibited personnel practice before OSC may petition the Board for corrective action. See 5 U.S.C. § 1214(b)(2)(C). Given our obligation to press OSC to act in a timely manner, the Board is unlikely to grant additional extensions of the stay if the case remains in its current procedural posture. See Special Counsel ex rel. Perfetto v. Department of the Navy, 84 M.S.P.R. 659, ¶¶ 1, 10-12 (2000) (denying a request for a fourth stay extension where, among other things, OSC had over 1 month to obtain an agency response).

A request for a lengthy extension will require a Board determination separate from the stay order initially issued. See Special Counsel v. IRS, 65 MSPR 143, 145 (1994). If the agency represents to the Board that it intends to take no action against an employee pending completion of an OSC investigation, the Board may decline to extend a stay, although the Board may also make clear that if the agency's actions vary from its stated intention, OSC may renew the stay application. See Special Counsel v. FEMA, 45 MSPR 512, 514 (1990). But see Special Counsel v. Dept. of Navy, 83 MSPR 468, 470 ¶ 6 (1999) ("No change in the record is a factor in favor of extending the stay.").

Matters were again explained in Special Counsel v. VA, 45 MSPR 271, 272-73 (1990). Under 5 USC 1214(b)(1)(B), the Board may grant extensions of stays at its discretion while viewing the record in the light most favorable to OSC. Under this standard, the Board grants OSC's request for an extension if OSC's prohibited personnel practice claim is not clearly unreasonable. See Special Counsel v. SBA, 72 MSPR 306, 309 (1996) (granting a stay: "Although the agency now contradicts the petitioner's allegations...it does not show that the initial prohibited personnel practice claim is clearly unreasonable."); Special Counsel v. Dept. of Treasury, 70 MSPR 281, 283 (1996) (extensions are discretionary with the MSPB; the fifth extension was granted). In making this determination, because the agency has the right under statute to have its comments on an extension request considered by the Board, Special Counsel v. DEA, 46 MSPR 525, 526 (1991), the Board may also consider, among other factors, the failure of the respondent agency to provide comment concerning the requested extension of a stay. Special Counsel v. FEMA, 45 MSPR 313, 315 (1990).

In making its decision to grant or deny an extension of a stay sought by OSC, the Board may consider evidence from the respondent agency concerning the conduct of OSC's investigation. Evidence of a desultory investigation would weigh against a long extension. But OSC is in the best position to determine the duration of its investigation. Special Counsel v. GSA, 45 MSPR 601, 605 (1990); see Special Counsel v. Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., 68 MSPR 603, 606 (1995) (in exercising its discretion to decide whether to extend a stay granted by one Board member, the Board views the record in the light most favorable to OSC and grants the request if OSC's prohibited personnel practice claim is not clearly unreasonable); Special Counsel v. Fish & Wildlife Serv., 64 MSPR 407, 409 (1994) ("OSC is in a better position than the agency to determine how much time is needed to complete the review process."); Special Counsel v. DOJ, 62 MSPR 195, 197 (1994) (noting that the facts presented by OSC in its extension request were viewed in the light most favorable to OSC: "Although the agency contradicts OSC's allegations, it does not show that OSC's prohibited personnel claim is clearly unreasonable."); Special Counsel v. Dept. of Air Force, 61 MSPR 229, 231 (1994) (extending a stay to permit the continuation of an OSC investigation); cf. Special Counsel v. DOJ, 78 MSPR 675, 677 ¶ 5 (1998) (OSC represented in a stay application that it was discussing with the agency OIG the relation between the investigations of both organizations concerning the employee's prohibited personnel practice claims). Broadly stated agency claims of hardship seem not to sway the Board. See Special Counsel v. Dept. of Interior, 68 MSPR 266, 269 (1995); Special Counsel v. Dept. of Navy, 66 MSPR 173, 175-76 (1995) ("The agency's claim of hardship cannot be allowed to override protection of the employee pending consideration of OSC's prohibited personnel practice claim.").

The Board may consider the agency's failure to contradict OSC's explanation of why she thinks reprisal has occurred. See Special Counsel v. VA, 61 MSPR 5, 8 (1994). Of interest to the Board will be any delay by the respondent agency in producing files or witnesses. See Special Counsel v. Dept. of Air Force, 55 MSPR 482, 484 (1992). If the agency contends that a stay should not be extended because the agency did not commit a prohibited personnel practice, the agency should submit evidence rather than contentions to support its position. See Special Counsel v. Dept. of Interior, 68 MSPR 266, 269 (1995).

The Board does not seem to favor making credibility determinations when it is asked to extend a stay. See Special Counsel v. Dept. of Army, 4 MSPR 469, 472-73 (1980). But see Special Counsel v. DHUD, 11 MSPR 382 (1982) (as it reviewed a request to extend a stay, the Board engaged in some factfinding based on facts that were either undisputed or insufficiently established). If an extension of the stay is denied, and the agency proceeds with action against the employee, then the Board's decision on the stay, being limited to the evidence presented by OSC, does not determine any appeal by the employee once the agency's adverse action is undertaken. See Special Counsel v. Dept. of Energy, 14 MSPR 333, 337-38 (1983).

A stay extension may be granted to facilitate completion of settlement discussions. See Special Counsel v. Dept. of Treasury, 72 MSPR 62, 64 (1996) (granting an extension of 20 days "defers to the Congressional admonition that the Board must press the Special Counsel to take corrective action in a timely manner."); Special Counsel v. Dept. of Treasury, 67 MSPR 216, 218 (1995) (allowing the parties a stay extension of 30 days to negotiate a settlement); Special Counsel v. Dept. of Navy, 47 MSPR 199, 199-200 (1991) (extending stay for 30 days to permit continuation of settlement negotiations); Special Counsel v. Dept. of Navy, 46 MSPR 504, 505 (1990) (extending stay for 45 days to permit continuation of settlement discussions). The stay may be granted to permit OSC to continue its investigation. See Special Counsel v. SSA, 77 MSPR 301, 304 (1988) (brief stay after several prior stays; OSC should be able to complete the investigative review and analysis more quickly). An extension was granted when OSC represented that its investigation was complicated and that it had to interview numerous witnesses who were geographically dispersed. See Special Counsel v. SBA, 74 MSPR 328, 330 (1997) (stay was necessitated by the need for witnesses to travel to Washington); Special Counsel v. Dept. of Air Force, 56 MSPR 365, 367 (1992) (extending stay to permit witness interviews); Special Counsel v. Dept. of Commerce, 26 MSPR 118, 122 (1985) (OSC had interviewed 40 witnesses by the time of an extension request and represented that it had nearly completed its investigation).

An extension was appropriate when the scope of the investigation expanded from an inquiry into a reassignment to include a proposed removal and related actions. See Special Counsel v. Dept. of Transp., 59 MSPR 559, 560 (1993). The stay may also be extended to permit OSC time to submit its investigative results to the employing agency and to permit the agency reasonable time to correct the action before OSC petitions the Board for relief. See Special Counsel v. Dept. of Navy, 67 MSPR 614, 616 (1995) (extending stay to permit the agency to review OSC's letter recommending corrective action); Special Counsel v. Dept. of Navy, 67 MSPR 464, 466 (1995) (extending stay for submission by OSC of its investigative findings to the agency to permit it to initiate corrective action); Special Counsel v. NASA, 66 MSPR 29, 31 (1994) (extending stay for, among other reasons, the time needed for OSC to consider the agency's response to the OSC investigation report); Special Counsel v. Fish & Wildlife Serv., 64 MSPR 410, 412 (1994) (allowing extension of stay to permit OSC to finish its investigation and to permit the agency a reasonable period of time to respond to the OSC request for corrective action before OSC filed a petition with the Board); Special Counsel v. VA, 60 MSPR 202, 203 (1994) (extending stay for a period sufficient for OSC to submit its corrective action letter to the agency, evaluate the agency's response and, if necessary, for OSC to determine whether to petition the Board for corrective action); Special Counsel v. VA, 60 MSPR 40, 41 (1993) (indefinite extension of stay of reassignment following filing of corrective action petition by OSC); Special Counsel v. VA, 59 MSPR 431, 433 (1993) (extending a stay in a complex case, over agency objections, to permit the completion and internal review of the OSC investigator's report).

If a stay is requested to permit the agency to take corrective action and the agency quickly declines to correct its action, the Board may grant a shorter extension to permit OSC an opportunity to file its corrective action request with the Board. See Special Counsel v. VA, 59 MSPR 216, 218 (1993). Special Counsel v. Dept. of Treasury, 66 MSPR 176, 179 (1995), granting a 90-day extension, held: "Despite the fact that OSC has completed its investigation, it is appropriate to extend the stay while OSC attempts to secure corrective action from the agency, and, if necessary to present its case to the Board.... Further, protection of the employee pending consideration of allegations of prohibited personnel practices override considerations of agency efficiency and managerial discretion."

Should OSC request an extension of a stay to consider filing a request for corrective action, and should the Board consider the time requested too long, the Board will substitute a shorter extension. See Special Counsel v. SBA, 72 MSPR 306, 310 (1996) (allowing a 70 rather than a 90-day extension); Special Counsel v. Customs Serv., 68 MSPR 603, 606 (1995) (after the investigation had been underway for nine months, the Board granted a stay extension of about 45 days rather than the 60 days requested by OSC); Special Counsel v. VA, 61 MSPR 424, 425 (1994) (noting the Board's obligation "to press the Special Counsel to seek corrective action in a timely manner"); Special Counsel v. VA, 61 MSPR 155, 156 (1994) (denying a 60-day extension: "The evidentiary record in this matter is unchanged. In view of our obligation to press the Special Counsel to seek corrective action in a timely manner, we believe that an extension of 30 days is appropriate[.]"). Protestations of overwork by OSC may not be sufficient to justify repeated and extended stays. See Special Counsel v. IRS, 66 MSPR 369, 371 (1995) (reducing from 60 to 45 days an extension of a preexisting stay: "The Board agrees with the agency that the workload of the OSC attorney assigned to this case cannot serve to justify such a lengthy extension. As the affidavit of OSC's attorney suggests, much of the requested extension would be spent working on other cases.").

The Board declined an indefinite extension of a stay, allowing 60 days for OSC to determine if it needed to pursue corrective action, absent settlement, in Special Counsel v. DHUD, 111 MSPR 48, 52-53 ¶¶ 16-17 (2009):

The Board will grant OSC's request for an extension if OSC's prohibited personnel practice claim is not clearly unreasonable. Special Counsel ex rel. Waddell v. Department of Justice, 105 M.S.P.R. 208, ¶ 3 (2007); Special Counsel v. Internal Revenue Service, 65 M.S.P.R. 143, 144 (1994). OSC's original request for a stay was based on its allegation that DHUD failed to appoint Meyers to the Boise Field Office Director position because of his status as a Schedule C political appointee, a nonmerit factor, and therefore committed a prohibited personnel practice under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(12). Viewing these facts in the light most favorable to the Acting Special Counsel, we find that OSC's prohibited personnel practice claim is not clearly unreasonable. See Beam v. Office of Personnel Management, 66 M.S.P.R. 469, 475-77 (1995) (recognizing that Schedule C status is a nonmerit factor). Under the specific circumstances of this case and in light of the fact that the evidentiary record supporting OSC's initial stay request has not changed significantly since the stay went into effect by operation of law, a further stay is appropriate.

A separate determination must be made on the length of the requested stay. See Special Counsel ex rel. Waddell, 105 M.S.P.R. 208, ¶ 5. The Board may extend the period of a stay for any period which it considers appropriate. 5 U.S.C. § 1214(b)(1)(B); Special Counsel ex rel. Waddell, 105 M.S.P.R. 208, ¶ 3. Here, we find that an indefinite extension of the stay is not appropriate. As stated above, OSC has not yet filed a petition for corrective action. It is the intent of Congress that stays not be extended for prolonged periods of time. Special Counsel v. Department of the Treasury, 71 M.S.P.R. 419, 421 (1996) (quoting Special Counsel v. Federal Emergency Management Agency, 44 M.S.P.R. 544, 546 (1990)). Moreover, the Board is obligated to press OSC to present corrective action cases in a timely manner. See id. at 422; see also Special Counsel ex rel. Feilke v. Department of Defense Dependent Schools, 76 M.S.P.R. 625, 629 (1997) (indefinite extension of stay of an employee's termination was not appropriate, in part because OSC had not yet filed petition for corrective action); cf. Special Counsel v. Department of the Interior, 68 M.S.P.R. 537, 538 (1995) (an indefinite extension of a stay pending resolution of the petition for corrective action that OSC filed is appropriate). In view of this obligation, and considering further the fact that the agency's response to OSC's request for voluntary corrective action is due before April 4, 2009, we believe that an extension of 60 days provides adequate time for OSC to review the agency's response and file a corrective action petition with the Board if OSC deems such action necessary. See Special Counsel v. Department of the Treasury, 70 M.S.P.R. 6, 8 (1996).

See Special Counsel ex rel. Spalding v. Dept. of Treasury, CB-1208-22-0016-U-5 (NP 1/23/2023) ("However, we encourage the agency to avoid any further delay of OSC's investigation and we caution OSC that time is of the essence. See, e.g., Special Counsel ex rel. Waddell v. Department of Justice, 104 M.S.P.R. 505, ¶ 8 (2007) (discussing an already lengthy stay and granting one more extension but warning that the Board would not be inclined to grant another); compare Special Counsel v. U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service, Department of the Interior, 64 M.S.P.R. 413, 415-16 (1994) (denying OSC's fourth request for an extension, noting that OSC had the complaint for 10 months and extensions are not given on demand), with Special Counsel ex rel. Perfetto v. Department of the Navy, 85 M.S.P.R. 454, ¶ 15 (2000) (granting an indefinite stay extension after OSC filed a petition for corrective action)."); Special Counsel ex rel. Tarantino v. In light of the factors discussed above, we find that a 72-day extension of the stay is warranted, and we therefore grant OSC's request in part."); Special Counsel v. Dept. of Navy, 85 MSPR 92, 97 ¶ 13 (2000) (directing the employing agency to advise the Board of its position of indefinite stay requested by OSC); Special Counsel v. Dept. of Transp., 71 MSPR 87, 90-91 (1996) (allowing a 60-day extension rather than an indefinite extension after the agency filed a petition for corrective action); Special Counsel v. Dept. of Treasury, 70 MSPR 6, 8 (1996) (allowing a 60-day extension of a stay for OSC to file a petition for corrective action; "[t]hereafter, the Board will entertain a request for an indefinite stay pending the resolution of the petition"); Special Counsel v. Dept. of Treasury, 67 MSPR 458, 460 (1995) ("It is appropriate to grant an indefinite extension of a stay pending the outcome of a corrective action petition.").

The Board granted a "last stay" when it lost faith in OSC's ability to complete an investigation expeditiously. Special Counsel v. Dept. of Treasury, 69 MSPR 458, 461 (1996) (following a year-long investigation, with various extensions of stay granted: "[t]he Board finds that no further extensions will be granted unless the Special Counsel finds, under 5 USC § 1214(b)(2)(A), that there are reasonable grounds to believe that a prohibited personnel practice has occurred, exists, or is to be taken which requires corrective action, and reports this finding to the Board, the agency, and to the Office of Personnel Management, or the Special Counsel determines that disciplinary action should be taken against any employee for having committed a prohibited personnel practice under 5 USC § 1215(a)(1)(A)."); see Waddell v. DOJ, 104 MSPR 505, 508 ¶ 8, 2007 MSPB 18 (2007) (several prior extensions; OPM had more than seven months to decide what action to take: 60 days more and no more: "this will be the final extension of the stay that the Board will grant in this matter").

The Board denied a stay extension when OSC took too long to decide how to handle a case, or when OSC's proof was plainly inadequate, Special Counsel v. Fish & Wildlife Serv., 64 MSPR 413, 415-16 (1994):

We find that the stay extension request in this case is clearly unreasonable. The Special Counsel's request is based on bare allegations. Although she asserts that the investigation has revealed a potential 2302(b)(1) violation which may change the course of action taken by the Special Counsel, there are no details of the newly-discovered evidence nor any reasons given why it could not have been found much earlier in this investigation. The Special Counsel has had this complaint for over ten months and this is the fourth request for an extension of the initial stay. As the Board noted in FEMA, "[i]t is the intent of Congress that stays not be extended 'for prolonged periods of time.'... Moreover, Congress has encouraged the Board to press the Special Counsel to present any corrective action case in a timely manner." 44 MSPR at 546-47, citing H.R. Rep. No. 274 at 23, 100th Cong., 1st Sess. (1987). Although we must view the record in the light most favorable to the Special Counsel, extensions are not given on demand as they must be supported by sufficient facts to justify the request. Because there is no showing here as to the validity of the newly-discovered evidence nor why it was not discovered much earlier as well as the fact that substantial delays have been incurred, we can find no basis for further extensions in this case.

Conversely, if the Board decides the OSC stay request is for too brief a period, the Board may grant the stay for a longer period than OSC requested. See Special Counsel v. Dept. of Navy, 84 MSPR 590, 593 ¶¶ 11-12 (1999) (to facilitate communications between OSC and a distant agency station, the Board granted 30 days rather than the 20 days requested); Special Counsel v. DOJ, 81 MSPR 439, 442 ¶ 6 (1999) (granting a stay longer than the requested 10 days so that OSC could timely notify the respondent if a further extension was necessary); Special Counsel v. DOJ, 78 MSPR 675, 678 ¶ 6 (1998) (granting a 30-day stay rather than the 14 days requested by OSC).
