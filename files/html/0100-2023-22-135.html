d. Clearly Separate Claim

Roman v. Dept. of Army, 72 MSPR 409, 419-20 (1996), provides an example of clearly separate claims. The appellant sought reimbursement for counsel fees incurred in an unsuccessful claim for consequential damages under the Whistleblower Protection Act. Because the fees incurred for the unsuccessful claim were distinct from the other components of the case, the fees were denied:

An appellant may be deemed a "prevailing party" for purposes of an attorney fees award if all or a significant portion of the relief sought in his petition was obtained as a result of the filing of the petition. See Stein v. USPS, 63 MSPR 537, 540, aff'd, 41 F.3d 1521 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table). The appellant stated that he retained an attorney, Michael W. Lilley, to assist him with his claim for consequential damages. Moreover, the appellant does not allege error in the administrative judge's finding that Mr. Lilley provided assistance to him solely with respect to his claim for consequential damages, and "did not help him with the costs portion of his motion."

The appellant's claim for consequential damages is distinct from his claim for costs because, as explained above, the claims are based on different statutory provisions. Given that Mr. Lilley assisted the appellant only with his claim for consequential damages, and in view of the fact that the appellant has not prevailed on any part of that claim, we find that the appellant is not entitled to the attorney fees he seeks, assuming that he would otherwise be entitled to such fees and that such fees were reasonable. See Taylor v. Department of Justice, 69 MSPR 299, 304 (1996) ("If a party 'fail[s] to prevail on a claim that is distinct in all respects from his successful claims, the hours spent on the unsuccessful claim should be excluded in considering the amount of a reasonable fee.'" (citations omitted))....

Fees for federal district court litigation that did not significantly result to the result in a successful MSPB whistleblower reprisal case were denied in Carson v. Dept. of Energy, 126 Fed. Appx. 486 (Fed. Cir. 2005 NP). One decision split fees between two compliance claims: the Board granted fees for the successful pursuit of one claim (the legal necessity of an award of back pay up to reinstatement), and denied fees for another claim (an unsuccessful attempt to secure overtime pay as part of the compliance efforts). There was no common core of facts underlying the allegations. The facts were sufficiently distinct to have enabled the appellants to file separate claims for relief. Spezzaferro v. FAA, 32 MSPR 643, 648-50 (1987); see Guerrero v. VA, 2008 MSPB 100 (2008) (when an AJ awards fees for compliance efforts involving several compliance issues, the AJ must explain how fees are justified based on the disposition of each of those issues); Wright v. Dept. of Transp., 53 MSPR 427, 432 (1992) (the appellant pursued one unsuccessful compliance issue; the Board deducted one hour from the claimed 9.5 hours spent on the pleading). The Federal Circuit awarded fees as a result of compliance proceedings based on the compliance claims that were successfully pursued and denied them as to the claims that were not successfully pursued. See Greco v. Dept. of Army, 852 F.2d 558 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (discussed earlier as to awards of fees in compliance proceedings). But claims made for time spent in an addendum proceeding following a merits determination were not recoverable when the time was for matters that should properly be raised in later compliance proceedings. See Rumsey v. DOJ, 123 MSPR 502, 515-16 ¶ 25, 2016 MSPB 28 (2016).
