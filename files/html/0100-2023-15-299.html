a. Comparative Performance

An employee's poor performance and disparate treatment defenses should be evaluated with regard to a given group of employees under the same supervisor, doing similar work, not in relation to employees doing comparable work under the guidance of another supervisor. Kuhlmann v. DHHS, 10 MSPR 356, 358 (1982). Frequency of errors is significant in a Chapter 75 performance case, but assessment of the significance of the number of work errors may be tempered by the rate of errors doing the same work. See Jackson v. Dept. of Air Force, 10 MSPR 118, 122 (1982) (considering work of individuals in the same branch as the appellant); Richardson v. DHHS, 13 MSPR 30, 32 (1982) (removal of a claims examiner: "record fails to indicate that any other employee had difficulty meeting the 80 percent quality standard"). For similarity in work to be found, it seems that the work must be nearly identical. Fairall v. VA, 33 MSPR 33, 50 (1987), aff'd, Fairall v. VA, 844 F.2d 775 (Fed. Cir. 1987), distinguished errors of commission from errors of omission on the part of employees responsible for maintaining lab records. Evidence that other staff members were fully able to meet performance requirements imposed on the appellant dispels the defense that those requirements were unreasonable. See Bridges v. DHHS, 6 MSPR 223, 225, 6 MSPB 194 (1981); cf. Baum v. Dept. of Treasury, 14 MSPR 392, 395 (1983) (claim that the agency deliberately assigned too much work to the appellant was rejected as an allegation of a prohibited personnel practice under 5 USC 2302(b)(6); despite an initial showing of a disparity in the level of assignments among employees "there was ample testimonial evidence establishing that appellant performed relatively little work, completing only approximately 10 percent of the filing he was assigned.").

Solomon v. VA, AT-0752-10-0370-I-1 (NP 5/27/2011), noted of a defensive argument concerning other employees' performance in workgroup:

The appellant further argues that the Douglas factors were not properly considered in his case. Specifically, he reiterates that he has never been disciplined while working at the Dorn Medical Center and he vaguely argues that other supervisors were disciplined less severely for worse infractions. The administrative judge determined, however, and we agree, that the deciding official appropriately considered the relevant Douglas factors in her decision. In particular, the deciding official did consider the appellant's past work record as one of the mitigating factors but ultimately concluded that the mitigating factors were not sufficient to mitigate the demotion penalty. In addition, although the administrative judge did not address the consistency of the penalty with those imposed upon his supervisors in his initial decision, the deciding official did testify regarding the appellant's allegations, concluding that all three men received the appropriate penalty based upon their individual performance deficiencies.
