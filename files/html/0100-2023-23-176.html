f. Reasons Related to Original Action

When an appellant's removal is canceled, neither the agency nor the appellant's supervisors may rely on the removal in any future interactions or evaluations. Berger v. DOJ, DE-0432-11-0293-X-1 (NP 6/12/2014). The agency should avoid barring reinstatement for subjective reasons that involve the charges underlying the original unsuccessfully prosecuted adverse action. When the Board ordered a demotion to a nonsupervisory position with minimum grade reduction, the agency could not demote the appellant to a lower position on the basis that the appellant was untrustworthy. The Board's mitigation order precluded the agency from relitigating the appropriate penalty by injecting its speculative notions of the appellant's fitness for the position. Implementation of the Board's remedial order is not the time for the agency's consideration of Douglas mitigation factors. Michael v. USPS, 41 MSPR 4, 5-6 (1989); see Vincent v. DOJ, 32 MSPR 263, 267-68 (1987) (agency could not decline to return appellant, a correctional officer, to his prior duties for reasons that were reviewed and not sustained in the underlying adverse action).

Briggs v. Nat'l Council on Disability, 68 MSPR 296, 301-02 (1995), prevented the agency from refusing reinstatement of the appellant for essentially the same reasons underlying its unsuccessful removal efforts:

The Board's final order in this appeal directed the agency to cancel the appellant's removal and retroactively restore her effective November 12, 1992. Briggs v. National Council on Disability, 65 MSPR 509 (1994) (Table). Upon reversal of a removal, the agency is required to place the employee "as nearly as possible in the status quo ante." Kerr v. National Endowment for the Arts, 726 F.2d 730, 733 (Fed. Cir. 1984). While the law generally requires that the employee be reinstated to the position from which he or she was removed, an exception exists where the agency establishes that it has "a strong overriding interest," or "compelling" reasons for placing the employee in a different position. Mann v. Veterans Administration, 29 MSPR 271, 274 (1985).

In its opposition to the Recommendation, the agency contends, as it did before the administrative judge, that restoring the appellant to her former position of Executive Director would cause the agency irreparable harm because: (1) the agency has a statutorily imposed ceiling of eight full-time staff members; (2) the Executive Director position is a confidential, policy-advocating position and the appellant cannot function in this position because the agency has lost confidence in her; and (3) the appellant cannot be shifted to another position because none is equivalent to the Executive Director position. We agree with the administrative judge's finding in her Enforcement Recommendation that the agency has not met this test. Here, one of the agency's proffered reasons for refusing to reinstate the appellant--that her former position is a confidential, policy-making position--is directly contrary to the Board's final decision in this case, and therefore cannot support the agency's continued noncompliance with the Board's restoration order. It is well established that an agency cannot refuse to comply with a Board reinstatement order based on reasons that were rejected by the Board in the decision reversing the action. See Sarver v. Department of the Treasury, 26 MSPR 685, 688 (1985). Further, the agency's unsupported allegations that it will suffer irreparable harm if the appellant is reinstated do not meet the standard of a strong overriding interest that justifies its failure to comply with the Board's final decision. See Williams v. Department of Health and Human Services, 32 MSPR 259, 262 (1987) (agency's assertion that appellant's former position has been filled and that reinstating her to that position would be unduly disruptive found insufficient to justify noncompliance with Board reinstatement order).
