15. Other Problems

Frustration or doubt as to one's case did not justify delay, London v. MSPB, 494 Fed. Appx. 54, 56 (Fed. Cir. 2012 NP):

...Mr. London's contentions that he was unable to afford to litigate the case and doubted that he would prevail do not constitute good cause for delay. See, e.g., Ford-Clifton v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, 661 F.3d 655, 659 (Fed. Cir. 2011) (holding that "the Board properly held that neither alleged financial hardship nor inability to engage counsel establish good cause for [a] delay" of more than 11 months); Zamot v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 332 F.3d 1374, 1378 (Fed. Cir. 2003) ("an election not to pursue further remedies because of a belief that further review would be fruitless does not constitute a showing of good cause for a filing delay"); Tamayo v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 88 M.S.P.R. 685, 687-88 (2001) (stating that "inability to secure a representative," "financial difficulty," or "the appellant's assertions about lacking the faith and courage to proceed" do not establish good cause for untimely filing).

The record supports the administrative judge's conclusion that, taken together, the Walls factors indicate that Mr. London did not establish good cause for his 25-year filing delay. Although the record is unclear as to whether he was notified of his right to appeal upon his termination, by his own admission his only excuse for the delay was financial hardship and doubt regarding his chance of success, neither of which establishes good cause.

The disruptive effect of a snowstorm on communications at the time an appeal was due furnished good cause for waiving the filing deadline. Woolard v. Dept. of Army, 11 MSPR 504, 506 (1982). Contamination of the mail system with anthrax during the fall of 2002 excused a delayed appeal. Terrance v. OPM, 94 MSPR 77, 80-81 ¶ 8 (2003). A combination of personal calamities sufficed to excuse a belated appeal. Cook v. OPM, 31 MSPR 683, 685 n.3 (1986) (including: deaths of mother and stepfather; care of ill mother; care of ill daughter; appellant's own depression and related treatment). But deaths among family members did not excuse a late appeal. Mansaray v. VA, PH-0752-15-0174-I-1 (NP 7/15/2015) ("The appellant's general statement that she was under stress from the deaths in her family does not establish good cause for her untimely filed appeal. See Robinson v. Office of Personnel Management, 56 M.S.P.R. 325, 327 (finding that a general allegation that an appellant is under stress does not establish good cause for an untimely filing), aff'd, 5 F.3d 1505 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table); see also Virgil v. U.S. Postal Service, 75 M.S.P.R. 109, 113 (1997)."). Appellant's inability to recall when an adverse action decision was received did not excuse late filing when the decision letter stated the applicable time limits and appellant did not allege that he did not receive the notice until after expiration of the filing period. Hampton v. Dept. of Navy, 38 MSPR 28, 30 (1988). Delay caused by entrusting possession of an OPM reconsideration decision to a bookkeeper was excused in Pitre v. OPM, 39 MSPR 693, 695-96 (1989). Writer's block did not avoid a late appeal. Huff v. MSPB, 60 Fed. Appx. 288, 289 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP).

Delay was not excused when the appellant was seeking employment and attempting to prevent the foreclosure of her home. Harris v. DOD, 101 MSPR 123, 125 ¶ 7, 2006 MSPB 23 (2006). Stress and embarrassment did not allow a late appeal. Modest-Coleman v. MSPB, 332 Fed. Appx. 615, 617 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP). Financial difficulty does not provide good cause for untimely filing. Robey v. USPS, 105 MSPR 539, 544-45 ¶ 16, 2007 MSPB 120 (2007). Dacus v. OPM, 106 MSPR 567, 570-71 ¶¶ 8-9, 2007 MSPB 216 (2007), rejected the appellant's explanation that correspondence from OPM "was lost or misplaced during this time gap due to my grandchildren misplacing (losing) the correspondence in one of their rooms," but the appeal was adjudicated because the notice from OPM provided insufficient information as to the appellant's obligations and options. Although not concerning the timeliness of an appeal, the Board noted as to one issue concerning timely submission of evidence that a computer glitch causing Internet or email access loss was not excused when "businesses and libraries provide Internet access to the public for little or no charge, and the appellant failed to assert that she attempted to retrieve her email from any such location." Bynum v. USPS, 112 MSPR 403, 411 ¶¶ 21, 2009 MSPB 194 (2009) (aff'd on other grounds, Bynum v. USPS, 382 Fed. Appx. 934 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP)). Wickware v. Dept. of Army, DA-0752-10-0220-I-1 (NP 9/30/2010) (aff'd, Wickware v. OPM, 417 Fed. Appx. 972 (Fed. Cir. 2011 NP)), considered unconvincing a variety of reasons offered for an untimely appeal: failure of the agency to provide documents, when the appellate deadline was clearly stated; the need of the appellant to care for his ailing father; difficulties in a divorce and custody battle; medical problems without a showing of how those difficulties prevented the filing of a timely appeal; lack of knowledge of how to request an extension of time to file an appeal.
