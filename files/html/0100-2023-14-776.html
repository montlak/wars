broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 23: Management and Supervisory Practices, Section D: Duties to be Performed by Supervisors, Managers

D. DUTIES TO BE PERFORMED BY SUPERVISORS, MANAGERS

A proposal cannot ordinarily specify tasks to be performed by particular managers or supervisors. See, e.g., AFGE Local 1858 and Army Missile Command, Redstone Arsenal Commissary, 27 FLRA 69 (1987). Proposals that effectively remove supervisory status and responsibilities from a position that has been designated as supervisory and convert the position to a nonsupervisory job directly determine conditions of employment of supervisory personnel and are not within the duty to bargain. IFPTE Local 49 and Army Corps of Eng'rs, S. Pac. Div., 52 FLRA 830, 836 (1996). Even a proposal that would otherwise be negotiable may become nonnegotiable if the proposal specifies the level of supervision that is necessary to perform certain functions sought to be negotiated, e.g., scheduling of employees. See Nat'l Union of Hosp. and Health Care Employees, Dist. 1199 and VAMC, Dayton, 28 FLRA 435, 464 (1987) (Proposal 15). A proposal was nonnegotiable that required specific supervisors to resolve leave scheduling conflicts. Illinois Nurses Ass'n and VAMC, N. Chicago, 27 FLRA 714, 721-22 (1987) (Proposal 3); see AFGE Local 1923 and DHHS, HCFA, Baltimore, 44 FLRA 1405, 1426-29 (1992) (Proposal 5 nonnegotiable: requiring personnel director to provide written certification of qualifications of individuals involved in agency EEO program).

An arbitration award was set aside that found a violation of a negotiated agreement by assignment of a supervisor to work normally performed by a unit employee on overtime. The award violated the right to assign work. 172d Infantry Brigade, Ft. Richardson and AFGE Local 1712, 19 FLRA 542, 543 (1985). A proposal was inconsistent with management's right to assign work that required supervisors to assist in the work of the office in the event of a backlog. NFFE Local 108 and USDA, Ark. State Office of FHA, 14 FLRA 19, 21-22 (1984) (Proposal 2). Another proposal, for assignment or partition of office space by supervisors, was set aside as inconsistent with the right to assign work. AFGE Local 12 and Dept. of Labor, 27 FLRA 363, 365-66 (1987). Nonnegotiable was a proposal seeking to regulate then number of management participants on a total quality management team. The conditions of employment of supervisors are outside the duty to bargain. DOD, DCAA, Cent. Region and AFGE Local 3529, 47 FLRA 512, 522-24 (1993) (Provision 4). [Refer in this chapter to "Work Preservation."]

The Authority has held that an agency does not have a duty to bargain with a union over proposals that directly implicate supervisors' conditions of employment. Fraternal Order of Police, Lodge #1F, 57 FLRA 373, 380 (2001) (Lodge #1F) (citing NATCA, Rochester Local, 56 FLRA 288, 291 (2000) (NATCA)). But proposals that principally relate to the conditions of employment of unit employees are not removed from the mandatory scope of bargaining simply because they indirectly affect supervisors. See IFPTE, Local 49, 52 FLRA 830, 835-36 (1996).

The Authority has found that a proposal that mandated a minimum number of supervisors who must be on duty during off-hour shifts directly implicated the conditions of employment of supervisors and was outside the duty to bargain. Lodge #1F, 57 FLRA at 380. Proposal 2 explicitly limits the amount of leave a supervisor may take during peak travel times between May and September. Similarly, Proposals 3 and 4 expressly require the Agency to schedule supervisors' shifts evenly throughout the day and to schedule their RDOs evenly throughout the week. Id. These proposals directly affect supervisors' conditions of employment because they explicitly direct when supervisors must be scheduled and how much leave they may take. Thus, we find that Proposals 2-4 are outside the duty to bargain. See NATCA, 56 FLRA at 290 (finding a proposal to be outside the duty to bargain because it required the agency to schedule supervisors "as efficiently as possible").

We find that Proposals 2-4 are outside the duty to bargain because they directly implicate supervisors' conditions of employment, but that Proposal 1 does not directly implicate supervisors' conditions of employment.

Many proposals come before FLRA that appear negotiable--except that the proposals call for a "supervisor" to perform a specific task. FLRA will occasionally caution the parties concerning the language and give an opinion on the negotiability of a technically deficient proposal. That occurred in New York State Nurses Ass'n and VA Bronx Med. Ctr., 30 FLRA 706, 707-08 (1987):

Another preliminary matter concerns the technical defects in many of the Union's proposals. We have held that management's right to assign work includes the right to determine the individuals who will perform particular tasks. This right includes assignment to bargaining unit and nonbargaining unit employees. Many of the Union's proposals require the assignment of work to specific employees or portions of the Agency's organization and are nonnegotiable on that basis. For example, although on its face the fifth sentence in Proposal 1 appears very reasonable, we are required to find it to be nonnegotiable, consistent with a long line of Authority precedent, because it requires unit employees to schedule official time "with their immediate supervisor." This sentence would be negotiable if the Union substituted "management" or "the Agency" for the reference to the immediate supervisor.... In most of the proposals, these defects would not permit management to exercise its congressionally accorded discretion to assign the tasks involved to whomever it elects. These defects are easily cured. In this decision, we have noted those proposals which are nonnegotiable solely on the basis that they assign work to particular individuals or organizational segments. We urge the parties to work together to resolve these technical defects and pursue agreement on the substantive issues involved in the proposals.

A proposal is not improper if it calls on the agency to perform a task but allows the agency to determine who will perform the task, e.g., review wage data. FLRA stated of a contrary argument, Illinois Nurses Ass'n and VAMC, N. Chicago, 27 FLRA 714, 727-28 (1987) (Proposal 4):

[T]aken literally, the Agency's argument would mean that any proposal which required management to take some action, for example, merely giving notice to the Union of some proposed change in a condition of employment, would interfere with management's right to assign work. Construed in this way, section 7106(a)(2)(B) would completely nullify the duty to bargain because no obligation of any kind could be placed on management by negotiation.

See SEIU Local 150 and VAMC, Milwaukee, 35 FLRA 521, 527-29 (1990) (Provision 3 required supervisor to post schedule, but parties had agreed that notice need not be posted by particular individual).

POPA and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 47 FLRA 954, 958-59 (1993), explained the degree that a proposal may effectively assign work in its discussion of Provision 2, calling on reviewers to identify errors to employees:

...If these additional actions--identifying in writing the portions of the work reviewed and listing the errors found--are to be accomplished at all, it appears only reasonable and logical that they would be accomplished by the official designated by management to review the work and identify the errors.... [N]oting again that the Agency is free under the provision to designate whomever it chooses to act as reviewers, we find no basis on which to conclude that the second sentence of Provision 2 substantively limits the Agency's right to assign work by designating particular officials to whom the disputed work must be assigned.... [M]ost procedures are not self effectuating. That is, most procedures...are satisfied only through some action by some agency personnel. In recognition of this, the Authority holds that otherwise negotiable procedures entailing some assignment of work to employees or management officials do not necessarily directly interfere with an agency's right to assign work....

A proposal may call on a particular official, e.g., the personnel director, to perform a task if the proposal is couched in terms that the work can be performed by someone else of the agency's choosing, e.g., the "Director of Personnel or his designee." There is nothing improper about a proposal that identifies the official to whom a request will be made by an employee, as long as the employer is free to determine who will act on the request, e.g., employees will present requests for sick leave to their supervisors. New York State Nurses Ass'n and VA Bronx Med. Ctr., 30 FLRA 706, 716 (1987); see NTEU Chap. 243 and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 49 FLRA 176, 186 (1994) (Provisions 1 and 2 were negotiable, requiring agreement that employees could elevate through the ranks of management their objections to complaints about employees' attire: "The effect of the provisions on management's right to assign work is moderated by the fact that the provisions permit some flexibility in the choice of the specific agency officials who will perform the reviews. In this regard, the provisions require that the first review will be performed by 'the appropriate office Director, Group Director, or equivalent management official' and, according to the Union, those officials are permitted to designate others to act for them. Also the Personnel officer is permitted to authorize a designee to act for him or her in making the final judgment and, if necessary, counsel the employee.").

Nonnegotiable in NFFE Local 2079 and USDA, Forest Serv., Umpqua Nat'l Forest, 49 FLRA 396, 403-04 (1994), was Proposal 8, that the "principal teacher" develop a team approach to solving problems. The distinction between negotiable and nonnegotiable assignments involving supervisors was made in NFFE Local 405 and Army Aviation Sys. Command, 33 FLRA 604, 611-12 (1988) (Provisions 2 and 3), approving as negotiable a proposal that permitted an employee to request a written rationale from the labor relations office, but disapproving as nonnegotiable a proposal that the labor relations officer respond in writing to that request.

A negotiable proposal required that, in the event of nonselection for repromotion, the aggrieved employee be provided written, persuasive reasons for nonselection, and that the employee be given seven days to rebut those reasons to the selecting official (who would then respond in writing). AFSCME Local 2027 and ACTION, 27 FLRA 191, 194-96 (1987) (Proposal 1). Negotiable was a proposal concerning the right of employees to secure higher-level review of decisions not to promote them, apparently within career ladder promotions. The proposal specified the level and manner of review. CREA and Library of Congress, Congressional Research Serv., 25 FLRA 306, 328-29 (1987) (Proposal 15). A proposal can be negotiated that designates the selecting official as the first-line supervisor unless otherwise designated by management. Bremerton Metal Trades Council and Naval Supply Ctr. Puget Sound, 32 FLRA 643, 651-52 (1988) (Provision 4).

In AFGE Local 446 and Dept. of Interior, Nat'l Park Serv., Blue Ridge Parkway, 43 FLRA 836, 844-45 (1991), Proposal 2B, deemed negotiable, required the supervisor, as part of the process leading to "reasonable suspicion" drug testing, to document his concerns about an employee's drug use and to have those concerns or suspicions approved in writing by the next higher level of supervision:

...In American Federation of Government Employees Local 3732 and U.S. Department of Transportation, United States Merchant Marine Academy, Kings Point, New York, 39 FLRA 187 (1991) (Merchant Marine Academy), we found that proposals that are procedural in nature but entail some assignment of work to agency personnel do not necessarily directly interfere with the exercise of management's right to assign work. We reach the same conclusion here. Proposal 2B simply requires a supervisor to take the procedural step of documenting the basis for that supervisor's suspicions and for a higher level of management to approve the supervisor's suspicions. We note that the obligation to substantiate the basis for reasonable suspicion testing is consistent with the requirement contained in FPM Letter 792-19 that "[w]here testing is conducted based on reasonable suspicion, each agency should promptly detail in writing the circumstances which formed the basis of its determination that reasonable suspicion exists to warrant the testing." 54 Fed. Reg. 14026. Under these circumstances, and as no other basis exists for finding that the proposal is inconsistent with management's right to assign work, we find that the proposal does not directly interfere with that right.

If the union advances a proposal as a negotiable arrangement that requires assignment of tasks to specific officials, the union should explain in its position statement what specific benefits to employees result by requiring the agency to assign the specified functions only to the identified management officials, unless the benefits are apparent from the proposal. See AFGE Local 1345 and Dept. of Army, Ft. Carson, 48 FLRA 168, 176-78 (1993) (Proposal 9: safety inspection proposal required specific actions by supervisor and safety officer).

Should management elect to bargain at the local level over responsibilities assigned to supervisors, FLRA will enforce the negotiated provision, as in AFGE Local 1815 and Army Aviation Ctr., Ft. Rucker, 53 FLRA 606, 623-24 (1997), involving a proposal (Provision 13) that sought agreement that "[p]rospective interviewers will be provided training by the CPD, which also will assure compliance with Privacy Act provisions":

[O]nce an agency and a union agree to a proposal concerning the working conditions of supervisors, the proposal is enforceable provided that it is otherwise consistent with the Statute. See AFGE, Local 3302, 52 FLRA at 681-82. Consequently, contrary to the Agency's assertion, Provision 13 is not unlawful merely because it seeks to regulate the conditions of employment of supervisors. As the Agency does not challenge Provision 13 on any other grounds under the Statute, we conclude that it is not contrary to law as alleged. See id. at 682-83. Accordingly, we direct the Agency head to rescind its disapproval of Provision 13.
