e. Misrepresentation Concerning Pay Rates

It may occur that an individual receives a promise of a certain salary level or grade and step when he is recruited for a position, only to find upon entry into duty that the appointment is not what was promised. Although the case does not, strictly speaking, involve Board jurisdiction, Riplinger v. United States, 695 F.2d 1163, 1164-65 (9th Cir. 1983), makes clear that federal salaries are ordinarily set only by appointment, not by contract:

Appellant Riplinger, a contract analyst for the Department of the Navy, was recruited from the private sector with the express representation that he would be employed initially at grade GS-11, step 10. Through a series of administrative errors, none attributable to Riplinger, necessary government approval for step increases was not obtained, and the maximum pay authorized by statute and regulations was GS-11, step 1. In grade GS-11, the pay for step 1 is considerably less than for step 10.

Riplinger learned of his appointment at grade GS-11, step 1, after he had entered upon duty, and he began prompt action for administrative relief. This suit followed, with a claim for money damages under a breach of contract theory based on Tucker Act jurisdiction under 28 USC 1346(a)(2), or alternatively, for back pay under the Back Pay Act, 5 USC 5596. All parties admit the correctness of Riplinger's factual recitals, and all should admit the equity of his claim, but it is well-settled that the law provides no relief.

The Back Pay Act is of no avail to appellant. The statute permits recovery where an employee suffers injury from wrongfully reduced or improperly calculated pay, measured by the emoluments of his duly appointed position. United States v. Testan, 424 U.S. 392, 407, 96 S. Ct. 948, 957, 47 L. Ed.2d 114 (1976); Donovan v. United States, 580 F.2d 1203, 1208 (3d Cir. 1978). The question here is the rank and appointment to which appellant was entitled at the outset; and as we conclude that he was not entitled to appointment at step 10 of grade GS-11, the Back Pay Act simply is not applicable.

Whatever may be the case where the status of an employee is not specified by the Congress, see Hopkins v. United States, 427 U.S. 123, 96 S. Ct. 2508, 49 L. Ed.2d 361 (1976), where the position held is an appointment provided for by law, a federal employee is entitled only to the salary of the appointed position. In this respect, it is said that federal employees serve by appointment, not by contract. Though a distinction between appointment and contract may sound dissonant in a regime accustomed to the principle that the employment relationship has its ultimate basis in contract, the distinction nevertheless prevails in government service. The terms of the appointment displace previous understandings, understandings that in other contexts might have created a contractual right. In so holding, it appears the courts follow the rationale that the government should not be bound by representations at variance with promulgated statutes and regulations fixing the pay of its employees, a rule sometimes expressed simply as a part of the doctrine of sovereign immunity. See United States v. Testan, 424 U.S. 392 at 400, 96 S. Ct. at 954.

We agree with the Court of Claims in Shaw v. United States, 640 F.2d 1254, 1260 (1981), which declared:

[P]laintiff may not base his theory of recovery on contract law since he was a federal employee. Federal officials who by word or act generate expectations in persons they employ do not ipso facto create a contract liability running from the Federal Government to the employee, as they might if the employer were not the government.

Federal employees are not entitled to rely upon the promises of government officials that they will be appointed to a particular grade or step, id.; and see Ganse v. United States, 376 F.2d 900 (Ct. Cl. 1967). The appointment of federal employees is a discretionary act, see Sampson v. Murray, 415 U.S. 61, 70 n.17, 94 S. Ct. 937, 943 n.17, 39 L. Ed.2d 166 (1974); United States v. Testan, 424 U.S. at 406, 96 S. Ct. at 957, and the emoluments they are entitled to receive is determined by statute, not by prior representations respecting pay or grade.

A reduction in pay did not occur when the appellant was appointed to a position that the appellant believed was properly set at a higher rate of pay than what the appellant received. The appellant's remedy is through the classification appeal process. See Hunt v. MSPB, 250 Fed. Appx. 329 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP) (ALJ maintained that when he was appointed to a chief ALJ position, his pay rate should have advanced to the higher level held by the previous incumbent of the job). [Refer to Chapter 6, "Cancelation of Appointment, Promotion."]

A reassignment involving a reduction in basic pay, if involuntary, may be appealable as a reduction in pay. The appellant makes a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction by asserting that the agency misrepresented the effect of a reassignment, otherwise voluntary, upon the rate of the appellant's basic pay. See Wright v. Dept. of Transp., 99 MSPR 112, 115-16 ¶¶ 9-11 (2005); cf. Montgomery v. Dept. of Interior, DC-3443-15-1083-I-1 (NP 3/16/2016) ("Accepting a position based on agency-supplied misinformation regarding pay may constitute a nonfrivolous allegation of an involuntary reduction in pay that would be within the Board's jurisdiction. See Wright v. Department of Transportation, 99 M.S.P.R. 112, ¶ 10 (2005). However, the appellant admits that he voluntarily accepted the position knowing he would be paid at a lower rate to avoid a break in service. The appellant has not alleged that he accepted the position based on agency-supplied misinformation, coercion, or duress."). A reduction in pay did not occur when the appellant was geographically reassigned at his request to a position paid at a rate that conformed to the Federal Workplace Flexibility Act of 2004, even though the appellant had been given some assurance that his rate of pay after the reassignment would not diminish. The action did not constitute a reduction in pay. The pay rate set for the appellant was required by law and a higher pay rate would have been contrary to law. Cook v. Dept. of Air Force, 251 Fed. Appx. 675 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP) (affirming the MSPB finding, however, that the appellant did not reasonably rely upon information concerning retention of his prior pay rate). [Refer to Chapter 6, "Involuntary Downgrading."]

In Paszek v. DOD, 50 MSPR 534, 538-39 (1991), [discussed in Chapter 6, "Involuntary Downgrading"] the Board recognized that a voluntary downgrade may become an involuntary, appealable adverse action if it is undertaken based on pay representations that the agency changes after demotion to correct an "administrative error." See McGarigle v. USPS, 36 MSPR 610, 614-15 (1988) (hearing was required when issues arose as to the authority for a pay adjustment and as to the possibility that the appellant was misled concerning the pay rate of new position). Lavergne v. MSPB, 298 Fed. Appx. 963 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP), involved an employee who voluntarily took a position with another agency at a lower rate of basic pay but at a higher rate of aggregate pay (basic pay, locality pay, and premium pay). After he arrived at the new agency, with the lower basic pay, he asserted to the Board that he suffered an appealable reduction in pay because the gaining agency did not properly consider his prior service. The Board concluded that there was no reduction in pay, although it recognized that there was a dispute over pay. The gaining agency did not reduce employee's pay. The Federal Circuit affirmed the result reached by the Board, noting that the appellant did not assert that he was unaware that the rate of basic pay for the new position was lower than it was for the prior position.
