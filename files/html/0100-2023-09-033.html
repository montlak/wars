c. Significance of Actual Mailing Date; Mailstream Rule: Actual Filing Date

As a result of law developed on the timeliness of petitions for review, the Board relaxed its rule concerning the effect of a postmark on a mailed appeal. The locus of focus may become when the appeal entered the mailstream, Lawrence v. Dept. of Treasury, 53 MSPR 7, 8-9 (1992):

[T]he postmark date is merely presumptive of the date of filing, and...a credible, unrebutted affidavit or sworn statement submitted by a petitioner that shows that the pleading was placed in the Postal Service mail stream before the filing deadline is enough to overcome the presumption that the postmark date is the date of filing. See Raphel v. Dept. of Army, 50 MSPR 614, 616-17 (1991), and Peloquin v. USPS, 51 MSPR 435, 437-38 (1991). In this case, the affidavit submitted by an employee in the office of the appellant's counsel is specific and credible concerning the steps taken to mail the petition for appeal, and shows that it was placed in the Postal Service mail stream on the last day for filing. Moreover, the agency has not rebutted the affidavit. Therefore, the affidavit is sufficient to overcome the postmark date presumption, and we find that the appellant's petition for appeal was timely filed.

See Drake v. OPM, 95 MSPR 367, 369-70 ¶¶ 5, 6 (2004) (appellant's statement on the timeliness of an appeal need not be in sworn or affidavit form, unless specifically required by the AJ; since the AJ did not require that the appellant's statement on timeliness to be in sworn or affidavit form, he should have "afforded significant evidentiary weight to the appellant's unsworn but undisputed statement" concerning mailing of the appeal; Post Office express mail receipts corroborated the appellant's assertions concerning mailing); Burnett v. Dept. of Navy, 71 MSPR 34, 37 (1996) ("A party nevertheless may establish by credible, unrebutted evidence that, despite the postmark date, the pleading was actually placed in the Postal Service mail stream before the filing deadline and thus was timely filed." Certificates of service and certified mail receipts corroborated the appellant's account concerning the mailing of the appeals.); Swinarski v. Dept. of Army, 54 MSPR 513, 515-16 (1992) (presumption of the date of filing created by a postmark was overcome by sworn statements describing the circumstances of mailing the appeal).

The Board initially declined to extend its understanding of modern business practices to accepting an appeal as filed on the day it is placed into the mail drop box of an establishment tasked with commercial mailhandling, even if the metered postage on the envelope containing the appeal was dated on the last day of the filing period. Rousselle v. Dept. of Army, 61 MSPR 595 (1994). But the Board reopened its earlier decision, and in Rousselle v. Dept. of Army, 69 MSPR 531, 535-36 (1996), recognizing decisions from the Federal Circuit urging greater flexibility in application of appeal deadlines, excused a day's delay in the postmarking of an appeal:

The facts here are undisputed, and support a waiver of the time limit. As noted above, on the last day for filing an appeal, the appellant deposited his petition with proper postage in the mail box at Kinko's. Companies such as this routinely provide business-related services such as photocopying, computer rental, postage sale, and mail receipt. The envelope containing the appellant's appeal had affixed to it a postage meter tape bearing the date of the last day of the appeal period. The envelope also contained the date of the U.S. Postal Service's cancellation imprint, however, and that imprint was dated the day after the close of the appeal period.

This is not a situation in which an appellant sits idly by and does nothing until after the appeal period is closed. This is a situation in which it is clear that the appellant was acting diligently to pursue his appeal within the time limits. In pursuing his appeal he attempted to timely file by depositing the appeal in a mail box in a commercial facility in the business of receiving and otherwise handling mail for its patrons. Just as in Walls, 29 F.3d at 1581, the delay was minimal. Moreover, here the appellant clearly believed that he had timely filed his appeal.

While the appellant's explanation for the delay could have been more complete, it is sufficient to show diligence that the appellant's representative averred that the appeal was placed in the Kinko's mail box on the last day of the filing period with a meter stamp showing the date it was placed in the mailbox. Keeping in mind the statement in Alonzo that "any doubt about whether good cause has been shown should be resolved in favor of an appellant," the lack of greater specificity in the appellant's explanation for the delay should not defeat a good cause finding. See Alonzo, 4 MSPR at 186. Here, the appellant clearly believed that he had timely filed his "appeal."

...

The length of the delay here is only one day. The mailing of the appeal on the last day for filing at a commercial facility that routinely accepts mail for its patrons demonstrates the appellant's diligence. His one-day tardiness is hereby excused, and his case will be heard on the merits.
