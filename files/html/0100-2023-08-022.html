5. Quality Review

Through case assignments from chief administrative judges (CAJs or regional directors), AJs receive authority to hear and decide appeals. By that delegation the CAJs do not abdicate authority to monitor and evaluate AJs' performance or control the quality of their work. In Notice R82-5 (Nov. 22, 1982), the Board Chairman stated that regional directors (CAJs) must utilize quality review procedures to review initial decisions. And quality control is a component of chief judges' performance standards. They may review initial decisions themselves or with the assistance of senior judges. The chairman noted that judges have always informally called on one another for advice and assistance, to discuss issues in their cases, and to share ideas and information on processing problems and hearing experiences, a practice he determined should continue. Even as to cases handled for the Board by administrative law judges, whose decisional independence is guaranteed by the Administrative Procedure Act, the Board may provide assistance to the ALJ, in an effort to improve his decision, through "needed support staff or making editorial suggestions" that do not interfere with the ALJ's actual factual findings, credibility determinations, and conclusions of law. Special Counsel v. Eubanks, 76 MSPR 405, 418-19 & n.3 (1997).

The propriety of peer and supervisory review of initial decisions was challenged because of concerns that the assessment of the AJ who heard the appeal might be compromised through review of a draft initial decision by AJs who had not heard the evidence. The Board rejected the challenge, determining that peer review by supervisors and a team leader does not compromise the AJ's ability to arrive independently at factual conclusions based upon record evidence. The review process was not ex parte because it did not involve communications between the AJ and a party to the proceeding. Guy v. Farm Credit Admin., 25 MSPR 500, 504 (1984). The quality review program was determined to be statutorily acceptable in Fuller v. United States (D.D.C. 1985 NP No. 84-1699); cf. Doe v. Hampton, 566 F.2d 265 (D.C. Cir. 1977) (considering what constitutes the level of impermissible contacts by a civil service adjudicator with its own advisors or experts).

Initial decisions are to identify and dispose of issues, summarize evidence, explain legal authority, describe how and why issues of credibility are resolved, and adhere to clear legal precedent. A decision should be clear and avoid obvious flaws in logic, grammar, or spelling, or misstatements of the record. Disagreements over changes suggested following internal review are to be resolved by the CAJ. If the AJ assigned to adjudicate the appeal is unpersuaded by the comments and recommendations of the CAJ, under the Notice, "issuance of his or her decision should be authorized if it otherwise satisfies the required quality standard." If the AJ will not make required changes, the CAJ may withdraw that individual's authority to decide the case. The appeal must then be decided either by the CAJ or by some other AJ with regard for proper evaluation of credibility evidence, meaning that the case may have to be reheard. Unless his authority is withdrawn, the AJ has the responsibility to sign an initial decision and subsidiary rulings without a superior's countersignature.
