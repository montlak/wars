b. Possibility of Relief

A grievance is not mooted by the employee's removal from the agency if there remains some possibility of relief, even in another forum, pertaining to the matter leading to the grievance, SBA and AFGE Local 2951, 55 FLRA 179, 183 (1999):

A dispute becomes moot when the parties no longer have a legally cognizable interest in the outcome. Department of Justice v. FLRA, 991 F.2d 285, 289 (5th Cir. 1993) (citing Powell v. McCormack, 395 U.S. 486, 496 (1969)) (although employee no longer worked for agency, case was not moot because FLRA still had an interest in controversy and Statute still provided available remedies). The Supreme Court has held that the burden of demonstrating mootness "is a heavy one." See United States v. W.T. Grant Co., 345 U.S. 629, 633 (1953). The party urging mootness meets its burden of demonstrating that neither party has a legally cognizable interest in the final determination of the underlying questions of fact and law, upon satisfaction of two conditions: (1) that "'there is no reasonable expectation'...that the alleged violation will recur," and (2) "interim relief or events have completely [or] irrevocably eradicated the effects of the alleged violation." County of Los Angeles v. Davis, 440 U.S. 625, 631 (1979) (internal quotations and citations omitted).

In this case, the Agency has not established that the Union no longer has a legally cognizable interest in the grievant's placement. In National Labor Relations Board and National Labor Relations Board Professional Association, 50 FLRA 88 (1995), the Authority recognized that a portion of an arbitrator's remedy, requiring an agency to take an action regarding an employee who had resigned, can be rendered moot by the resignation. Here, both parties have stated that the grievant was removed from employment. The Union stated in its pleadings, however, that the grievant "had requested a reasonable accommodation pursuant to federal statute, but the Agency declined said request[,] which is still another violation of a federal statute which will eventually be determined." The Union's assertion that issues related to the removal "will eventually be determined"...is not disputed. Thus, although the award in this case does not require reinstatement, the portion of the award which requires a change of position from one that the grievant encumbered when she was removed could have viability in the event the grievant were reinstated as a result of another proceeding. In these circumstances, the Agency has not shown that the award is rendered moot because of the grievant's removal from employment....

Dept. of Air Force Joint Base Elemendorf-Richardson and AFGE Local 1101, 69 FLRA 541, 543-44 (2016) (Member Pizzella dissenting), declined to conclude that a grievance was moot when it reached arbitration, even though an admonition by then was removed from the employee's file:

...An arbitrator's determination regarding the timeliness or mootness[35] of a grievance concerns the procedural arbitrability of that grievance. And, generally, the Authority will not find an arbitrator's procedural-arbitrability determination deficient on grounds that directly challenge the determination itself.[36] However, a procedural-arbitrability determination may be found deficient on grounds that do not directly challenge the determination itself, which include claims that an arbitrator was biased or exceeded his or her authority.[37] In addition, the Authority has stated that a procedural-arbitrability determination may be found deficient on the ground that it is contrary to law.[38] But, for a procedural-arbitrability determination to be found deficient as contrary to law, the appealing party must establish that the ruling conflicts with statutory procedural requirements that apply to the parties' negotiated grievance procedure.[39]

The Agency contends that the Arbitrator's award was "predicated upon" the issues raised in the Union's filing, which "were moot and/or untimely...and[,] therefore[,]...contrary to law, rule or regulation under § 7122(a)(1) [of the Statute]." Insofar as the Agency is contending that the Arbitrator's timeliness and mootness determinations are contrary to § 7122(a)(1), the Agency has not explained how the procedural requirements in that statutory section apply to the parties' negotiated grievance procedure. Therefore, that claim fails.

The Agency also further challenges the Arbitrator's mootness determination, claiming that the grievance was moot because the Agency had removed the etiquette letter from the grievant's file prior to the arbitration hearing. However, the Agency's claim that the matter was moot directly challenges the Arbitrator's determination that the matter was "not moot." Thus, consistent with the principles set forth above, the Agency's contention provides no basis for finding the award deficient.[50]
