d. Abuse of Family Leave

The appellant who abuses or misuses family leave is subject to adverse action. Jones v. USPS, 103 MSPR 561, 564-67 ¶¶ 7, 11-13, 2006 MSPB 290 (2006), aff'd, Jones v. USPS, 248 Fed. Appx. 160 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP) post, presents an example of what MSPB determined to be misuse of FMLA leave:

Under specification 2, the agency stated as follows: On July 20, 2005, the appellant requested FMLA sick leave dependent care. On July 29, 2005, during an investigative interview, the appellant stated that he had provided "all day" care for his wife on July 20, 2005. After being shown a surveillance video of him and his wife leaving their home at 12:42 P.M., he changed his answer to state that his wife's attack had lasted only a few hours. Under specification 3, the agency stated as follows: On July 27, 2005, the appellant requested FMLA sick leave dependent care. After being shown a surveillance video of his wife leaving their residence at 12:08 P.M., he stated that probably she was "done with her attack." The agency charged that the appellant did not notify management that he did not need FMLA or "all day" FMLA. It stated that he was observed shopping, attempting to dine at a steak house, and engaging in other activities while inappropriately taking sick leave dependent care.

...

We agree with the agency that the AJ erred in not sustaining specifications 2 and 3. In relevant part, the statutory FMLA provision states: "[s]ubject to section 2613 of this title, an eligible employee shall be entitled to [FMLA leave] [i]n order to care for the spouse...of the employee, if such spouse...has a serious health condition." 29 U.S.C. § 2612(a)(1)(C). Section 2613 states that an employer may require that a request for leave under section 2612(a)(1)(C) be supported by certification issued by the health care provider of the spouse. 29 U.S.C. § 2613(a). The certification shall be sufficient if it states, inter alia, that the employee is "needed to care for the...spouse." 29 U.S.C. § 2613(b)(4)(A). The applicable regulation describes "needed to care for" as encompassing both physical and psychological care and including "situations where, for example, because of a serious health condition, the family member is unable to care for his or her own basic medical, hygienic, or nutritional needs or safety, or is unable to transport himself or herself to the doctor, etc." 29 C.F.R. § 825.116(a).

The appellant's activities on July 20, 2005, in accompanying his wife to visit friends and family, eat at a restaurant, and shop at a mall do not fall within the definition of "needed to care for." In his investigative interview, the appellant stated that his wife's attack was "done" by noon. Moreover, the appellant's wife testified only that she was "a little tired," but suffered no more attacks, after noon on July 20, 2005. Similarly, the AJ's finding that the appellant's "being available in the event of another attack," is an acceptable use of FMLA leave is incorrect. The regulation states that the family member "is unable to care for her basic needs," not that she might be. Indeed, the appellant's wife testified that, although her doctor told her that relapses could occur after an attack, "there's no guarantee when, whether they will, how long the relapses can last, so just to be careful." She added that "he's never given me any restrictions or anything like that."

Likewise, the appellant's staying at home during the afternoon of July 27, 2005, without his wife does not fall within the definition of "needed to care for." Indeed, the appellant testified that he was not caring for his wife while she was out of the house. Therefore, he was not entitled to FMLA-protected leave for that period of time. That the appellant may have had to either request other leave or go to work for a short period of time is irrelevant to whether the agency proved the specification. Moreover, regardless of whether the agency explicitly notified the appellant that he was required to change his leave status, the appellant testified that he knew not to use FMLA sick leave dependent care when he was not caring for his wife. In addition, he admitted that his wife's doctor recognized that leave might be necessary for only 1/2 day.

Affirming, Jones v. USPS, 248 Fed. Appx. 160, 161 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP), noted that the FMLA regulations, 29 CFR 825.309(a), permit an employer to require an employee on FMLA leave to report periodically on the employee' status and intent to return to work, but the court implied that the regulation does not impose a duty on the employer to ensure that the employee is properly using FMLA leave.
