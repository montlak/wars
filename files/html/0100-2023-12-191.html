broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section G: Timeliness of PFR, Subsection 09: Justification for Late Filing, Part t: Pending Review by Another Adjudicator
t. Pending Review by Another Adjudicator

Indefinite delay of the Board proceeding, without a demonstration of good cause, is not consonant with the congressional mandate for expeditious processing of appeals with due regard to the rights of the parties. Moore v. Panama Canal Comm'n, 7 MSPR 349, 351, 7 MSPB 233 (1981) (denying request for an indefinite extension of time to file the PFR, noting that the appellant requested additional time to pursue her PFR while OSC reviewed her case). The appellant's decision to pursue her appeal rights through other forums, rather than filing a PFR with the Board, does not establish good cause for the untimely filing of her petition. Shimmin v. DOJ, 63 MSPR 435, 438 (1994); see King v. OPM, 114 MSPR 181, 186 ¶ 10, 2010 MSPB 112 (2010) (pursing a remedy in another forum does not support a showing of good cause for a delayed PFR); Shannon v. VA, 110 MSPR 365, 369-70 ¶ 8, 2009 MSPB 2 (2009) (appellant's decision to file her claims in other fora does not establish good cause for an untimely filed petition for review); Anderson v. Dept. of Army, 93 MSPR 463, 465 ¶ 6 (2003) ("[T]he fact that the appellant may have been pursuing other appeals or waiting for decisions by other agencies does not constitute good cause for waiving the regulatory time limit for filing his petition for review."); Sessions v. Dept. of Army, 74 MSPR 343, 347 (1997) ("His claim that he was pursuing his case against the agency, apparently in other forums, does not establish good cause."); Robinson v. VA, 33 MSPR 483, 486 (1987) ("[T]he fact that the appellant may have pursued a collateral challenge to the initial decisions does not constitute good cause for waiver of the time limit."). Salazaar v. Dept. of Energy, 107 MSPR 407, 408-09 ¶¶ 5, 8, 2007 MSPB 290 (2007), provides an example of a PFR denied as untimely when the appellant, before filing the PFR seeking to set aside a settlement agreement, sought redress through the EEOC, the inspector general of the agency, and Congress.

The Board summarized its decisions, Lange v. Dept. of Interior, 95 MSPR 227, 230 n.* (2003):

[*] In cases where an appellant has pursued rights in other forums after the Board has issued an initial decision but did not specifically seek review of the initial decision in another forum, the Board has treated the appellant's submission before the Board as a petition for review and held that the appellant's decision to pursue rights in other forums rather than filing a timely petition for review with the Board does not establish good cause for the untimely filing of the petition. See Anderson v. Department of the Army, 93 M.S.P.R. 463 ¶¶ 5-6 (2003); Zamot v. U.S. Postal Service, 91 M.S.P.R. 475 ¶¶ 4-6 (2002), aff'd, 332 F.3d 1374 (Fed. Cir. 2003); Shimmin v. Department of Justice, 63 M.S.P.R. 435, 438, aff'd, 43 F.3d 1486 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table).

Many decisions demonstrate the Board's reluctance to excuse a late PFR based on efforts to remedy the underlying or a collateral employment problem through other means, procedures, or fora. Examples of these cases follow.

Court challenges: Sahagun v. OPM, 69 MSPR 53, 54 (1995) ("To the extent that the submissions suggest that the appellant contacted the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, we note that an appellant's decision to pursue his appeal rights through other forums does not establish good cause for an untimely filed appeal to the Board."); Criddell v. USPS, 60 MSPR 30, 33 (1993) ("The appellant also chronicles her efforts to pursue her appeal in other forums, i.e., the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit and the Supreme Court of the United States as well as efforts to seek assistance from Congressional representatives. The appellant's decision to pursue her appeal rights through other forums, rather than filing a petition for review with the Board, does not establish good cause for the untimely filing of her petition"); Turner v. USPS, 25 MSPR 588, 589 (1985) (PFR filing time was not extended because the appellant sought redress in federal court); Couvillion v. Dept. of Army, 20 MSPR 163, 164 (1984) ("Moreover, the fact that the appellant may have pursued a collateral challenge to the initial decision before the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit, and the fact that the Court has stayed its proceedings pending further Board review, are immaterial in the absence of a remand by the Court."). As to a petition to reopen filed after a court reviewed a Board decision, Lange, supra, added, id. at ¶ 5:

The Board has the authority to reopen and reconsider appeals in which it has rendered a final decision, even after a court has affirmed the Board's final decision, but this authority is limited to matters that were not before the court at the time it issued its decision in the case. Jay, 90 M.S.P.R. 635 ¶ 7; Moss v. Department of the Air Force, 82 M.S.P.R. 309 ¶¶ 7-8, aff'd, 230 F.3d 1372 (Fed. Cir. 1999); Anthony v. Office of Personnel Management, 70 M.S.P.R. 214, 218 (1996); see also Standard Oil Co. of California v. United States, 429 U.S. 17, 18 (1976) (an "appellate mandate relates to the record and issues then before the court, and does not purport to deal with possible later events").

EEO activity: Bonk v. DHS, 109 MSPR 210, 212 ¶ 7, 2008 MSPB 134 (2008) (appeal to EEOC when the appellant should have filed an MSPB PFR did not excuse a later, untimely PFR), aff'd, Bonk v. MSPB, 301 Fed. Appx. 965 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP); Zamot v. USPS, 91 MSPR 475, 479 ¶ 6 (2002) (a decision to await an EEOC decision before filing a PFR does not constitute good cause for a late filing), aff'd, Zamot v. MSPB, 332 F.3d 1374 (Fed. Cir. 2003); Williams v. EPA, 86 MSPR 620, 622 n.* (2000) (an untimely PFR was not excused by efforts, following the PFR, to obtain review by the EEOC); Njaka v. USPS, 85 MSPR 264, 267-68 ¶ 11 (2000) (belated PFR from a mixed case initial decision was not excused because the appellant decided to pursue a separate EEO complaint through agency processes); Miller v. USPS, 63 MSPR 646, 648 (1994) ("[T]he appellant's assertion that he was waiting for a final decision from the EEOC before filing his petition for review from his removal is without merit because the equal employment opportunity complaint is apparently based on an earlier disciplinary action."); Murray v. Dept. of Army, 48 MSPR 338, 341 (1991) ("Instead of filing a timely petition for review with the Board, the appellant elected to pursue an administrative review of the issue of disability discrimination by filing a petition for review with the EEOC."); Martin v. DLA, 39 MSPR 324, 326 (1988) (an eight-year delay in filing a PFR was not excused by the appellant's interest in obtaining a decision from the EEOC; that decision contained no information necessary for timely filing); James v. IRS, 34 MSPR 364, 367 (1987) (declining to reopen for the purpose of honoring the appellant's request to refer an EEO claim to the agency for further review after the appellant lost the initial decision and unsuccessfully pursued an EEO case through agency channels); Holt v. USPS, 33 MSPR 480, 481 n.3 (1987) (delay was not justified because the agency EEO office asked the appellant to continue with its processing of his case); Edwards v. EEOC, 5 MSPR 331, 332 (1981) (delay of nearly a year in filing a PFR was not excused by reason the appellant's interest in awaiting completion of an EEOC investigation).

Grievances and arbitration: Waller v. USPS, 78 MSPR 696, 698-99 (1998) (lateness of a PFR was not excused because the appellant was awaiting the result of arbitration); Key v. USPS, 77 MSPR 513, 515 (1998) ("pursuit of a grievance in another forum does not constitute good cause for the waiver of Board time limits"); Kimble v. USPS, 74 MSPR 539, 542-43 (1997) (delay in a PFR was not excused because of problems with the grievance process); Miller v. USPS, 63 MSPR 55, 57 (1994) (attempts to obtain reinstatement through arbitration and through a congressional representative were not good cause for late PFR); Bachelor v. Dept. of Army, 56 MSPR 108, 110 (1992) (were the appellant confused concerning the choice between grievance arbitration and a Board appeal he could have contacted the Board and requested instruction); Figueroa v. DHHS, 45 MSPR 286, 287-88 (1990) (belated PFR was not excused because the appellant awaited an arbitration award challenging a performance plan; the arbitration award was immaterial to the validity of the settlement agreement challenged through PFR); McLaughlin v. USPS, 35 MSPR 255, 257 (1987) (rule also applies if the appellant is considering or pursuing a grievance under a negotiated agreement).

Compensation claims: Perry v. Dept. of Navy, 56 MSPR 159, 161 (1992) ("[E]ven assuming that the administrative judge advised the appellant that he had a right to file an OWCP claim, the fact that the appellant chose to pursue a right he may have had in another forum does not establish good cause for the untimely filing of his petition for review."); Blackburn v. USPS, 54 MSPR 612, 614 (1992) (pending unemployment compensation proceedings suffice to justify the delay of a PFR); Copley v. Dept. of Energy, 58 MSPR 437, 439 (1993) (rejecting late PFR: even if unemployment compensation decisions were new evidence, it was not shown how they were material and the appellant waited months to submit them); Bennett v. Dept. of Army, 56 MSPR 485, 487 (1993) (same as Copley).

Settlement efforts: Deville v. GPO, 93 MSPR 187, 191-92 ¶ 10 (2002) (delayed PFR was not excused because of appellant's belated knowledge of settlement; if appellant's counsel did not provide a copy of the agreement to him, the appellant was responsible for the acts of his attorney); Keatting v. Dept. of Navy, 77 MSPR 642, 645 (1998) (PFR delay was not excused: "[n]or did the appellant exercise due diligence or ordinary prudence in simply waiting for the agency to resolve the matter"); West v. EEOC, 69 MSPR 310, 312 n.1 (1996) (delay was not excused by the discussion of a settlement); Carney v. VA, 51 MSPR 313, 316 (1991) (neither an appellant's pursuit of a collateral, mixed case challenge to the initial decision in the courts nor his attempts to reach settlement with the agency constitute good cause for waiving the time limit); Maples v. DLA, 31 MSPR 667, 671 (1986) ("Moreover, the fact that appellant may have pursued a collateral challenge to the initial decision in the courts or sought to reach a settlement with the agency does not constitute good cause for waiving the time limit.").

Other challenges: Barker v. Dept. of Air Force, 98 MSPR 10, 13 ¶ 9 (2004) (declining to permit late PFR because the appellant was involved in proceedings before a hearing examiner of the Michigan ANG pertaining to his removal; the appellant could have requested an extension to file the PFR); Hayes v. USPS, 66 MSPR 448, 451 (1995) (PFR delay was not excused while awaiting a reconsideration decision from OPM on a retirement matter somewhat related to the appeal of an involuntary retirement pursued through a PFR); Block v. DLA, 40 MSPR 198, 200 (1989) (period for filing a PFR is not extended indefinitely to await the outcome of a request by appellant to OSC for investigation of her case); Carpenter v. DHHS, 40 MSPR 55, 56 (1989) (if a PFR is due from a Board decision, the PFR should not be delayed pending the outcome of a compliance petition); Gallagher v. DHHS, 23 MSPR 93, 94 (1984) (the PFR period was not extended because of a desire to clear an employee's record through the Privacy Act or other means); cf. Treacy v. OPM, 113 MSPR 46, 49-50 ¶¶ 9-10, 2010 MSPB 3 (2010) (PFR two months late was dismissed after the appellant filed an annuity appeal, OPM rescinded its reconsideration decision and the appeal was dismissed for lack of jurisdiction; the Board did not accept the appellant's argument that he was awaiting the outcome of the OPM adjudication, but noted that the appellant could file a new appeal when OPM reached a final decision on the annuity claim).
