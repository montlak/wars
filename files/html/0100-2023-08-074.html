a. Appointment From Register

Until the Federal Circuit's decision in McCormick v. Dept. of Air Force, 307 F.3d 1339, 1341-43 (Fed. Cir. 2002), reh'g en banc den., 329 F.3d 1354 (Fed. Cir. 2003), the rule was that, despite preexisting federal service, individuals hired from registers into competitive service positions were probationers, subject to whatever credit they received toward completion of probation for "tacking" of prior service. McCormick changed the equation. Under McCormick, the employee could be appointed to a position requiring a probationary period, but the employee who was discharged from that position could have appeal rights based on service prior to the probationary appointment. [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Counting Service Time in a Prior Position or Appointment; McCormick," concerning the Federal Circuit's decision.]

Stoute v. Dept. of Navy, 98 MSPR 409, 410-15 ¶¶ 2, 5-14 (2005), determined that McCormick provided appeal rights to a probationer hired under a register (also referred to as a certificate of eligibles) whose past service qualified for appeal rights under 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii):

On July 31, 2000, the appellant was appointed to the competitive service position of Machine Tool Operator with the agency. The appointment SF-50 indicates that his appointment was subject to the completion of a 1-year initial probationary period. The appellant completed his probationary period and, in March 2002, he applied for the competitive service position of Machinist with the agency. On September 9, 2002, the appellant was appointed to that position from a certificate of eligibles. The appointment SF-50 stated that the appellant would be required to complete another one-year initial probationary period. On July 29, 2003, the agency terminated him from the position during his probationary period for poor performance and tardiness.

...

An individual is entitled to appeal to the Board under 5 U.S.C. § 7513(d) if he is an "employee" as that term is defined at 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a), which provides, in pertinent part:

(1) "employee" means--

(A) an individual in the competitive service--

(i) who is not serving a probationary or trial period under an initial appointment; or

(ii) who has completed 1 year of current continuous service under other than a temporary appointment limited to 1 year or less...."

5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1).

In McCormick v. Department of the Air Force, 307 F.3d 1339 (Fed. Cir. 2002), the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit held that an individual who is excluded from "employee" status under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(i) is nevertheless an "employee" if the individual meets the definition provided at 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii). 307 F.3d at 1342-43. In other words, a competitive service employee serving a probationary period is nevertheless entitled to appeal to the Board if he "has completed 1 year of current continuous service under other than a temporary appointment limited to 1 year or less." Id. Office of Personnel Management (OPM) regulations define "current continuous employment" as "a period of employment or service immediately preceding an adverse action in the same or similar positions[1] without a break in Federal civilian employment of a workday." 5 C.F.R. § 752.402(b).

[1] The "same or similar positions" requirement does not apply to competitive service employees. See Mathis v. U.S. Postal Service, 865 F.2d 232, 234 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (recognizing that 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A) does not include the "same or similar positions" requirement for individuals in the competitive service); Ellefson v. Department of the Army, 98 M.S.P.R. 191, ¶¶ 10-14 (2005) (finding the same). Thus, in McCormick, the Federal Circuit did not analyze, or instruct the Board to analyze on remand, whether Ms. McCormick's positions of "Social Insurance Specialist" and "Contract Negotiator" were similar. See McCormick, 307 F.3d at 1343 (stating that "the record shows" that Ms. McCormick completed more than 1 year of current continuous service).

Although the agency argues that McCormick was wrongly decided, the Board is bound by precedential decisions of the Federal Circuit, the Board's reviewing court. See Fairall v. Veterans Administration, 33 M.S.P.R. 33, 39, aff'd, 844 F.2d 775 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (Table). Moreover, the rule of McCormick is retroactive and must be applied to all pending cases, whether or not those cases involve predecision events. See Reynoldsville Casket Co. v. Hyde, 514 U.S. 749, 752 (1995) (when a court decides a case and applies a new legal rule of that case to the parties before it, then it and other courts must treat that same new legal rule as "retroactive," applying it, for example, to all pending cases, whether or not those cases involve predecision events); see also Porter v. Department of Defense, 98 M.S.P.R. 467-69 ¶¶ 11-14, 2005 WL 901811 (2005) (discussing the retroactivity of McCormick and current Supreme Court case law on the topic).

The record supports the AJ's determination that the appellant completed 1 year of current continuous service prior to his termination. He was employed with the agency from July 31, 2000 until his termination, which was effective July 29, 2003. The appellant alleges, and the agency does not dispute, that he had no break in service during that time period. Thus, the AJ correctly concluded that the appellant completed 1 year of current continuous service immediately preceding his termination without a break in federal employment of a workday, and that he therefore qualifies as an "employee" under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii).

The agency contends that McCormick does not apply to initial appointments from a list of eligibles. The agency's argument is based on 5 C.F.R. § 315.801(a)(1), which provides that the first year of service of an employee who is appointed from a competitive list of eligibles is a probationary period. However, the fact that the appellant was appointed from a register is not relevant to the issue of whether he completed 1 year of current continuous service.

The court in McCormick held that 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(i) and (ii) provide two alternative definitions of "employee." The court held that an individual who is excluded from "employee" status under subsection (i), because he is serving a probationary period, is nevertheless an "employee" if he meets the definition provided at subsection (ii). McCormick, 307 F.3d at 1342-43. The regulation at 5 C.F.R. § 315.801(a)(1) is relevant in determining whether the appellant is an "employee" under subsection (i), i.e., if he is a probationer pursuant to section 315.801(a)(1), then he is not an "employee" as defined under subsection (i). His probationary status, however, does not preclude him from falling within the definition of "employee" at subsection (ii). In short, the manner of appointment is not relevant to the question of whether an individual in the competitive service has completed 1 year of current continuous service under other than a temporary appointment limited to 1 year or less.

The agency next relies on the Board's recent decision in Ramos v. Department of Justice, 94 M.S.P.R. 623 (2003), for the proposition that an individual who meets the definition of an "employee" under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A) may waive his chapter 75 appeal rights. Mr. Ramos, like the appellant, had more than 1 year of current continuous service with the same agency. Ramos, 94 M.S.P.R. at 627 ¶ 8. He began his service in March 1998 as a Border Patrol Agent. Id. at 626 ¶ 4. On March 11, 2001, Mr. Ramos was appointed from a register to the competitive service position of Deportation Officer. The SF-50 memorializing his appointment stated that it was subject to the completion of a 1-year initial probationary period. Id. Mr. Ramos was terminated effective June 29, 2001. Id. at 625 ¶ 2. Thus, Mr. Ramos was an "employee" under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii).

Nevertheless, the Board concluded that Mr. Ramos waived his appeal rights based on a Probationary Period Agreement. The agreement stated:

I, James Ramos, understand that upon entrance on duty to the position of Deportation Officer, GS-5, I will be required to begin a new probationary period in accordance with Immigration & Naturalization policy and Office of Personnel Management regulations as put forth in the code of Federal Regulations part 315--Career and Career-Conditional Employment.

My rights and entitlements for adverse and disciplinary actions will be processed in accordance with the provisions of the code of Federal Regulations part 315.

Id. at 629 ¶ 11. The Board found that Mr. Ramos entered into the agreement voluntarily and that he understood its terms.

Under the Ramos analysis, the issue is whether the employee knowingly and voluntarily agreed to waive his appeal rights at the time of appointment. Here, the appellant, unlike Mr. Ramos, did not sign a written agreement waiving his appeal rights or limiting his appeal rights to those in Part 315 of the Code of Federal Regulations. The agency contends that the appellant received sufficient notice that his appointment was conditioned on a 1-year probationary period from a conversation with the agency's human resources specialist and a notation on the appointment SF-50. The agency's argument fails to distinguish McCormick. Ms. McCormick also received an SF-50 stating that her appointment was subject to a 1-year probationary period, see McCormick, 307 F.3d at 1340, but that document did not alter her statutory status as an "employee" under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii). It is well established that "the SF-50 is not a legally operative document controlling on its face an employee's status and rights." Grigsby v. Department of Commerce, 729 F.2d 772, 776 (Fed. Cir. 1984). Moreover, even assuming, for sake of argument, that the appellant agreed to serve a probationary period, it does not follow that he agreed to forfeit his Board appeal rights under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii). In short, the record in the instant case does not reveal any evidence that the appellant knowingly and voluntarily waived his appeal rights.

Under law predating McCormick and Stoute, and in cases unaffected by those decisions, the rule concerning appointments from registers produced and continues to produce results that were harsh, probably inconsistent with the purpose of probation: a probationary period in the federal service, not in every job that is held during federal service. Under pre-McCormick law (and to those cases that are not now controlled by McCormick), it was immaterial that the employee could have been hired through reinstatement procedures that would have circumvented the requirement of probation. Neither did it matter that the appointment from the register involved no change in career fields after service of more than a year in one job; the probationary period must still be served. An employee selected for a career-conditional appointment from a register was required to serve a probationary period notwithstanding prior service or completion of prior probationary periods. 5 CFR 315.801(a)(1) (2022). See Ford v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1986 NP No. 86-1472) (requirement of the late FPM that an eligible given career-conditional or career appointment by selection from a certificate of eligibles must serve a (one-year) probationary period applies not only to the first appointment of that kind, but to any subsequent career or career-conditional appointment by selection from a certificate of eligibles, regardless of whether the appointee previously completed a probationary period); Lawrence v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 85-1351) (even if the individual already served the probationary period, if he secured another position by appointment from the certificate of eligibles, he must again serve the probationary period); Ramos v. DOJ, 94 MSPR 623, 627 ¶ 6 (2003) ("An employee who is selected from a register for a career-conditional appointment in the competitive service is required to serve a new 1-year probationary period, even if he has already completed a probationary period in another position.") (rev'd and remanded based on preexisting service, Ramos v. DOJ, 240 Fed. Appx. 409 (Fed. Cir. 2005 NP)); Pierce v. DHHS, 13 MSPR 85, 86 (1982) (appellant, despite 20 years of federal service, was a probationer when the last appointment was from a register and termination occurred within a year of that appointment); Dowden v. USDA, 11 MSPR 374, 375 (1982) (applying rule, noting that the agency's offer letter stated the appellant would be required to serve probation of a year); Rochniak v. Dept. of Navy, 10 MSPR 603, 605-06 (1982) ("As to the requirement that an individual serve a probationary period extends to all individuals appointed from a register, it is immaterial whether such appointment is in the individual's initial appointment to the Federal service or a subsequent appointment."); Chandler v. ICC, 3 MSPR 55, 56, 3 MSPB 173 (1980) (employee appointed from a certificate of eligibles is a probationer regardless of prior employment in the excepted service with a different agency); cf. Aizin v. DOD, 52 MSPR 146, 149 (1991) (an employee must serve a probationary period if he is appointed to a job, serves a portion of probation, and is then appointed by the same agency to a higher grade from another register; the next period of probation is subject to possible credit for earlier service in the same line of work); cf. Arispe v. Dept. of Air Force, 43 MSPR 96, 98-99 (1990) (recognizing exceptions established by the defunct Federal Personnel Manual ("FPM")). [Refer later in this chapter to "Transfer."]
