e. Pro Se Appeal

The Federal Circuit will accept an appeal within its jurisdiction filed pro se, that is, by an individual acting on his own behalf and without a lawyer. Many appellants plead their own cases before the Federal Circuit. Unless there are exceptional circumstances, the Federal Circuit is unwilling or unable to appoint attorneys to represent pro se litigants in civil litigation, see Olsen v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 95-3589) (citing Volk v. Hobson, 866 F.2d 1398, 1404 (Fed. Cir. 1989) (noting the authority of a court to appoint counsel under 28 USC 1915(c), the in forma pauperis statute)), and Lariscey v. United States, 861 F.2d 1267, 1270 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (denying request to appoint counsel), although the court may suggest the desirability of an amicus brief from a bar association, and the Federal Circuit Bar Association has, on occasion, successfully convinced a member of the bar to represent an appellant or to prepare an amicus brief in a civil service case before the Federal Circuit. Although the right of a litigant to represent himself is unchallenged, when he does so, the amount of judicial time expended on a case may be increased because it may become more difficult to discern the possible legal basis of a pro se litigant's claim. Ferris v. United States (Fed. Cir. 1989 NP No. 89-1031).

The Federal Circuit publishes a "Assistance for Unrepresented Parties," available on the circuit's website, https://cafc.uscourts.gov/home/information-for/unrepresented-parties/. The Federal Circuit has an informal procedure for efficiently deciding pro se appeals. The appellant is sent a notice giving her the choice of following the ordinary briefing procedure or filing four copies of an informal brief ("typed or otherwise legible"), with deadlines for the submission. An example of a past letter from the Clerk to an appellant informing her of the informal procedure requests that the informal brief respond to some of the following questions:

1. Has the petitioner ever had another case in this court? __ In a United States District Court? __ In the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission? __ If so, identify the case.

2. Did the MSPB or the arbitrator incorrectly decide or fail to take into account any facts? __ If so, what facts?

3. Did the MSPB or the arbitrator apply the wrong law? __ If so, what law should be applied?

4. Did the MSPB or the arbitrator fail to consider important grounds for relief? __ If so, what grounds?

5. Are there other reasons why the MSPB's or the arbitrator's decision was wrong? __ If so, what reasons?

6. What action (relief) do you want the court to take in this case?

7. Do you want to argue before the court in person? __ If yes, what are the reasons why argument will aid the court?

Although briefs of pro se litigants are not held to the stringent standards of briefs filed by attorneys, Redmond v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 99-3137) (citing Hughes v. Rowe, 449 U.S. 5, 9, 101 S. Ct. 173, 176 (1980), and Hilario v. Sec'y, VA, 937 F.2d 586, 589 (Fed. Cir. 1991)), even an informal brief to the Federal Circuit should provide the requested information. An appeal was dismissed upon the single assertion by the appellant that "I would like Back Pay." Shephard v. Dept. of Commerce (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 87-3278). But the issues raised by a pro se appellant need not be artfully phrased. Roche v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1998 NP No. 97-3454), determined that a petitioner properly raised the issue in court of whether his employment status resulted from discrimination against Vietnam veterans when his informal brief to the court asked: "Why are Vietnam Veterans treated like chopped liver?" In another case, Ruffin v. Dept. of Treasury, 83 Fed. Appx. 324, 326 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP), the court admonished the Board for failing properly to address a straightforward argument by a pro se appellant, and the court remanded the case for reconsideration by the Board after the court noted of the appellant: "his brief stated his argument, in the kind of utmost clarity often delivered in pro se writings, bluntly: 'the vacancy was not just limited to permanent employees.'" But see Agarwal v. Morbark, LLC, ___Fed. Appx.___, n.1 (Fed. Cir. 2022 NP) ("Although we generally read pro se filings liberally, even assuming (for argument's sake) that doing so would change our assessment of Mr. Agarwal's argument, we decline to do so here because Mr. Agarwal is an attorney admitted to practice before this court. See, e.g., Tracy v. Freshwater, 623 F.3d 90, 102 (2d Cir. 2010) (collecting cases supporting the proposition that, at least in civil actions, "a lawyer representing himself ordinarily receives no such solicitude at all").").

The government may respond to an informal brief with an informal brief of its own or the government may submit a formal brief after receiving the appellant's informal brief. The pro se appellant may, by the deadline established by the Federal Circuit's rules, file a statement in response to the government's brief. The appellant may request oral argument, and the clerk will notify him of whether the argument is granted or whether the case will be decided without argument. For all cases filed in the Federal Circuit, the court may decline to hear oral argument if under Federal Rules of Appellate Procedure 34(a) the dispositive issues have been authoritatively decided, the facts and legal argument are adequately presented in the briefs and record, and the decisional process would not be aided by oral argument. Cashe v. Dept. of Transp. (Fed. Cir. 1986 NP No. 85-1446).

For those appellants who cannot afford to pay filing fees, the court accepts motions for leave to proceed in forma pauperis, Form 6 on the court's website, .https://cafc.uscourts.gov/wp-content/uploads/RulesProceduresAndForms/Forms/form6.pdf. Refer to Federal Circuit Rule 24. USERRA cases are filed without docketing fees. Refer to the "Practice Note" accompanying Federal Circuit Rule 24, and to Circuit Form 6.

Filings with the Federal Circuit for appeals prosecuted by counsel are generally made and retrieved through the PACER e-filing system. There are some charges for retrieval of some documents under that system. The Federal Circuit will not, without a showing of inability to pay the charges, waive those fees, as it noted in Searcy v. USDA (Fed. Cir. 2013 NP No. 2013-3175):

Andrew Searcy, Jr. ("Searcy") applies for an exemption from the user fees associated with electronic access to court records.

The Public Access to Court Electronic Records ("PACER") system allows users to view and print case filings, judicial opinions, and other docket information from the federal trial, bankruptcy, and appellate courts. A small fee is ordinarily required to search and print such documents, but the Electronic Public Access Fee Schedule ("Schedule") carves out a narrow exception to that rule, allowing courts discretion to exempt certain persons or classes of persons from payment.

The Schedule makes clear, however, that "[c]ourts should not... exempt individuals and groups that have the ability to pay the statutorily established access fee." Accordingly, a movant seeking a fee waiver must demonstrate that the exemption "is necessary in order to avoid unreasonable burdens and to promote public access to information." Based on the papers submitted, the court is unable to say that Searcy has met this standard as he has not demonstrated the inability to pay the fee. Thus, this court must deny his request.

Pro se appellants are treated with consideration by the Circuit, but Sankey v. MSPB, 740 Fed. Appx. 718, 720 (Fed. Cir. 2018 NP), cautions:

... Although we liberally construe a pro se party's pleadings, see, e.g., Durr v. Nicholson, 400 F.3d 1375, 1380 (Fed. Cir. 2005), we "may not... take a liberal view of that jurisdictional requirement and set a different rule for pro se litigants only," Kelley v. Sec'y, U.S. Dep't of Labor, 812 F.2d 1378, 1380 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (italics omitted).
