9. Defense of Fee Motion

Fees may be sought for the time it takes an appellant's counsel to defend his fee petition. See Stein v. USPS, 63 MSPR 537, 543 (1994) (allowing time spent in research to anticipate and prepare for agency arguments in response to a fee petition); Bodrick v. District of Columbia Gov't, 20 MSPR 532, 533 (1984); McKenzie v. District of Columbia Gov't, 20 MSPR 528, 531 (1984) (fees awarded for the preparation of the opposition to an agency PFR to a fee petition); Koch v. Dept. of Commerce, 19 MSPR 219, 222 (1984) (fees awarded for time spent opposing agency PFR of an addendum decision awarding fees); Davis v. USPS, 4 MSPR 274, 276, 4 MSPB 346 (1980) (noting but not adjudicating a request for fees for responding to the agency's PFR). Entitlement ran to an hour of time spent preparing an appellant's reply to an agency response to the fee petition. The Board observed that a reply to a response is not a submission contemplated by Board regulations and that AJs should carefully scrutinize gratuitous submissions included in fee requests. The Board felt that counsel was entitled to be paid for that part of the work on a reply, generally not authorized by Board regulations, that served to clarify the fee petition submitted to the AJ. Bartel v. FAA, 30 MSPR 451, 455 (1986). Concerning responses to the agency reply, refer to Wilson v. DHHS, 834 F.2d 1011 (Fed. Cir. 1987), discussing due process requirements in fee adjudications. If the attorney submits a brief fee petition and then responds to new arguments in the agency's opposition, counsel will be able to recover the fees incurred in preparation of the response. The appellant should not be penalized for her counsel's attempt to reduce the billable time spent on the fee application and related litigation. See Wright v. Dept. of Transp., 53 MSPR 427, 431 (1992) (counsel anticipated a possible additional submission when, in the original fee petition, he stated that his submission was brief to avoid increasing the agency's cost and that he anticipated filing a response to any reply filed by the agency).

Even though an appellant prevails in a fee motion, he may file a cross-PFR if the agency challenges the fee decision through a PFR. The Board seems inclined, should it not overturn the fee decision, to compensate counsel for the time spent responding to the agency PFR as well as for the time initiating his own PFR, according to Bauer v. Dept. of Army, 79 MSPR 656, 657-58 (1998) (appellant's counsel requested additional fees for responding to agency PFR as part of the response to the Board):

The appellant seeks $1,575 (9 hours at $175 per hour) for time spent reviewing the agency's petition for review, preparing a response in opposition to the petition, and preparing a cross petition for review. The Board has held that counsel for a prevailing appellant may be compensated for services performed in connection with an agency's petition for review of an addendum initial decision that awarded counsel fees. Howard v. U.S. Postal Service, 76 MSPR 680, 681-82 (1997); Meadows v. Department of the Navy, 68 MSPR 463, 464 (1995). In addition, the Board has held that where there are related claims, a party who has won substantial relief should not have his attorney's fees reduced simply because he did not prevail on all his contentions. Taylor v. Department of Justice, 69 MSPR 299, 304 (1996). The Board has also held, however, that time expended on an unsuccessful claim will be disallowed if that claim is "distinct in all respects from his successful claims." Id.

Here, the appellant has identified the time he spent on the response as 9 hours. The agency has not responded in opposition to the appellant's request for fees incurred in responding to its petition for review, and we have no cause to doubt the reasonableness of the numbers expended by counsel in this effort. Meadows, 68 MSPR at 465. Further, even though we are denying the appellant's cross petition for review, we have not reduced the time claimed because the arguments in his response to the agency's petition for review and his cross petition for review are procedurally and substantively inseparable. Taylor, 69 MSPR at 305. Thus, the appellant is entitled to the additional time devoted to preparing a response.

See Thomas v. USPS, 86 MSPR 635, 646 ¶ 24 (2000) ("The response to the agency's opposition required significant time to specifically respond to arguments and cases cited by the agency, and the response to the administrative judge's order required significant time to develop a brief, citing Board cases to persuade the administrative judge that fees were warranted in the interest of justice for the two supplements to the petition for enforcement."); Morgan v. Dept. of Air Force, 13 MSPR 246, 250 (1982) ("prevailing appellant may be compensated for services provided in reference to an agency's petition for review of an addendum decision which awarded attorney fees to counsel"); cf. Gubino v. Dept. of Transp., 85 MSPR 518, 529 ¶ 26 (2000) (sustaining an award of 25 hours of time for responding to the agency's objections to a claim of fees made in an enforcement petition; the time was reasonable in light of the size of the claim, approximately $42,000, and the fact that the appellant was awarded 91% of the fees he sought).
