2. Preservation of Claims Supporting Entitlement; Significance of Final Merits Decision

Powell v. Dept. of Treasury, 19 MSPR 174, 183-84 (1984), determined that if an appellant does not raise a claim supporting fee recovery during the course of an appeal, the AJ cannot consider that claim in a later fee application. This raises interesting problems. An appellant may win a case because the agency fails in its proof. That alone might not warrant counsel fees. But to prove that the agency took the action in bad faith or that the action was discriminatory involves proof of an entirely different case, regarding either invidious motivation or malice. It does not behoove the Board to encourage litigation of tangential issues before the main issue is decided. The Board's approach stands, however, and appellants will pursue every reasonable defensive stratagem if they wish to recover fees later. The facts and defenses leading to a fee award must be developed before the AJ. If a counsel fee decision is to be based on the commission of a prohibited personnel practice or a discriminatory act, there must be a finding in the initial decision that the prohibited personnel practice or discriminatory practice occurred; without that finding, the Board will not make the finding in the course of adjudication of a fee petition. Madrid v. DOJ, 5 MSPR 404, 405 n.2, 5 MSPB 417 (1981); see SSA v. Price, 94 MSPR 337, 346 ¶ 22 (2003) ("Where there is no finding of a prohibited personnel practice in an underlying appeal, the Board will not grant attorney fees on that basis."), aff'd, Price v. SSA, 398 F.3d 1322 (Fed. Cir. 2005). If an appellant prevails but does not win on all issues, e.g., a prohibited personnel practice allegation, the appellant may not petition for fees and then raise on a PFR from the addendum decision whether he should have prevailed on the basis of the issue not decided, or decided unfavorably, in the initial decision. The appellant must protect his right to a decision on that issue by filing a PFR from the initial decision, not from the addendum decision. Gordon v. DOJ, 23 MSPR 497, 500 (1984); Powell v. Dept. of Treasury, supra.

Under the Federal Circuit's decision in Yorkshire v. MSPB, 746 F.2d 1454 (Fed. Cir. 1984), subsequently discussed, the first initial decision that ripens into a final decision establishes the bases supporting a fee petition. See Brunning v. GSA, 63 MSPR 490, 493 (1994) ("The administrative judge's findings from the initial decision, however, control in determining whether an award of attorney fees is warranted in the interest of justice."), followed in Gensburg v. VA, 80 MSPR 187, 191 ¶ 5 (1998), following remand, Gensburg v. VA, 85 MSPR 198, 207 ¶ 14 (2000). If a prohibited personnel practice is not found in the initial decision, it cannot be established as a basis for fees in an addendum decision. Yow v. Dept. of Treasury, 28 MSPR 411, 413 (1985). The Board emphasizes that a petition for counsel fees and the response may address fee entitlement only upon findings in the AJ's initial decision on the merits. Other issues may not be litigated along with the fee issue. Salvail v. Dept. of Labor, 5 MSPR 521, 522 n.1, 5 MSPB 513 (1981). But see Cason v. NASA, 46 MSPR 401, 403-05 (1990) (if an EEO issue arose in enforcement proceedings, it could serve as a basis for fee entitlement, even though the issue might have been addressed through the PFR process). If an argument on the merits of the underlying appeal was not advanced to the AJ, the argument cannot be raised and litigated during the fee litigation. See Francis v. OPM, 36 MSPR 43, 45 (1987) (as to an agency argument in a counsel fee dispute that the AJ refused to consider the agency's final submission, the agency did not file a PFR from that decision, but complied with it; as a result, "all issues contained in that appeal are moot and the entire matter is removed from controversy").
