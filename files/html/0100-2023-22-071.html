4. Reduction in Force

Sowa v. VA, 96 MSPR 408, 415 ¶ 14 (2004), determined that fees were warranted due to an agency's gross procedural error when it failed to follow required RIF regulations. The agency's alleged good faith belief that it was not required to invoke RIF regulations when it conducted a staff adjustment did not establish a basis for denying fees. Not every error reversing a RIF warrants fees. Fees were denied after reversal resulted from an error in the determination of assignment rights. The agency (inappropriately) relied on OPM classification standards to determine the employee's qualifications for a job sought during the RIF. Johnson v. Dept. of Interior, 24 MSPR 209, 212-13 (1984) (not reaching the "gross error" standard, but denying fees in an analysis of other factors). Johnson was not mentioned in Carter v. SBA, 28 MSPR 296, 299 (1985), involving an agency's improper use of reclassification downgradings rather than RIF procedures to correct loss of duties:

[W]e find that the agency's failure to provide appellants with their statutory rights under RIF procedures constituted gross procedural error. The protections and rights an employee receives when RIF procedures are applied are varied and important. Many of these rights are substantive in nature. See for example, Coleman v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 23 MSPR 670, 673 (1984) (the right to a properly constituted competitive level is a substantive RIF requirement). Thus, the agency's actions denying these rights in the instant case prejudiced appellants and amounted to gross error. Compare Morrow v. Department of the Army, 11 MSPB 205, 12 MSPR 592 (1982) (denial of adverse action procedures in an involuntary downgrading constituted gross error entitling appellant to an award of fees).

Thomas v. USPS, 77 MSPR 502, 507 (1998), also described circumstances involving presumptive fee entitlement following a successful RIF appeal:

...Specifically, the AJ found that the agency, under 5 CFR part 351, did not give the appellant the required specific RIF notice, did not establish the required competitive areas, competitive levels, and retention lists, did not consider or honor his bump and retreat rights, and did not accord him priority placement required for preference-eligible employees. Further, the agency conceded that it did not afford the appellant either RIF procedures or notice of appeal rights to the Board. In view of the foregoing, we find that the nature of the agency's procedural error was not only harmful, but gross, prejudicing the appellant's substantive RIF rights and warranting an award of attorney fees in the interest of justice with regard to the 1993 RIF demotion appeal. See, e.g., Carter v. Small Business Administration, 28 MSPR 296, 299 (1985) (the agency's failure to provide the employees their statutory rights under RIF procedures prejudiced the employees and constituted gross procedural error entitling them to an award of attorney fees); see also Olson v. U.S. Postal Service, 62 MSPR 261, 264-65 ("gross procedural error" is more than simple harmful error, which by itself could warrant reversal of the agency's action), review dismissed, 39 F.3d 1195 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table); King v. U.S. Postal Service, 20 MSPR 467, 469-70 (1984) (the agency's failure to afford the employee any procedural protections in effecting his constructive suspension of more than fourteen days severely prejudiced his ability to defend and constituted gross procedural error).

An agency's failure to provide proper assignment rights led to a counsel fee award in McKenna v. Dept. of Navy, 108 MSPR 404, 410-11 ¶ 10-11, 2008 MSPB 69 (2008), a decision that considered as part of the "gross procedural error" equation the agency's lack of an excuse for the error:

..."Gross procedural error" constitutes an error which prolonged the proceeding or severely prejudiced the employee, and must amount to more than harmful error. Olson v. U.S. Postal Service, 62 M.S.P.R. 261, 265, dismissed, 39 F.3d 1195 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table); Dunn v. Department of the Army, 4 M.S.P.R. 407, 409 (1980); see Allen, 2 M.S.P.R. at 428 (noting that the mere fact that an appellant is the prevailing party in an action does not establish that attorney fees are warranted in the interest of justice). Although the Board has not set forth a bright-line distinction between gross procedural error and mere harmful error, case law provides a fair amount of guidance on the subject, particularly when an agency denies an employee proper RIF procedures. Cf. Price, 94 M.S.P.R. 337, ¶ 9 (to determine whether payment of fees is warranted in the interest of justice, the Board will consider all of the relevant circumstances set forth in the record of the underlying appeal). To determine whether a gross procedural error has occurred, a balance is struck between the agency's excuse for committing the error and the prejudice and burden that the error caused the employee; if prejudice and burden to the employee predominates, gross procedural error exists and the employee is entitled to a fee award. Swanson v. Defense Logistics Agency, 35 M.S.P.R. 115, 118 (1987). Furthermore, the protections and rights that an employee receives when RIF procedures are applied, many of which are substantive in nature, are varied and important; an agency's failure to provide an appellant with his statutory rights under RIF procedures may constitute gross procedural error. Carter v. Small Business Administration, 28 M.S.P.R. 296, 299 (1985); see Thomas v. U.S. Postal Service, 77 M.S.P.R. 502, 506-07 (1998) (finding that the agency committed gross procedural error when it failed to provide the appellant with appropriate procedural rights as required by RIF statutes and regulations). Indeed, as noted by the Board in the Opinion and Order relating to the appellant's previous attorney fee motion, a finding as to whether the agency's error prejudiced the 8 appellant's substantive entitlement under the RIF was determinative of his attorney fee motion. McKenna, 104 M.S.P.R. 22, ¶ 12.

Here, the administrative judge found that "the appellant submitted his resume and other information to a member of the agency's RIF Team,...and she, inadvertently or otherwise, did not forward that information to the other members of the RIF team." Because the RIF Team was not in possession of the complete set of materials, the process was "patently unfair" to the appellant. Moreover, the Board subsequently found that the agency's error prejudiced the appellant's substantive entitlement under the RIF and ordered the agency to retroactively place the appellant into one of the positions he cited in his Board appeal. McKenna, 105 M.S.P.R. 373. The agency has proffered no excuse for its error. Thus, in light of the lack of any agency excuse for prejudicing the appellant's substantive entitlement under the RIF, and the prejudice and burden that the error heretofore has caused the appellant, gross procedural error exists and the award of attorney fees and expenses is warranted in the interest of justice. See Swanson, 35 M.S.P.R. at 118.
