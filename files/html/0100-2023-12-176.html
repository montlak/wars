broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section G: Timeliness of PFR, Subsection 09: Justification for Late Filing, Part f: Physical Or Mental Condition of Appellant Or Counsel
f. Physical or Mental Condition of Appellant or Counsel

There are four points bearing upon applications to excuse PFRs filed belatedly because of the appellant's medical condition. First, there is the matter of whether the appellant was ill; second, the Board expects that there be a connection made between the effects of the illness and the delay in filing; third, the appellant may be expected to produce documentation of the illness before the Board will seriously consider the waiver request; fourth, the appellant must file her PFR quickly after cessation of the disabling condition. Some decisions involving late PFRs may suggest that if an individual is incapacitated for a portion of the filing period, she should use the remainder of the filing period to perfect the PFR. But there are decisions in Chapter 2, "Sickness or Hospitalization," involving late appeals that imply that if a person is disabled for a portion of the filing period for the appeal, the appellant should have the benefit of the full filing period once the disability ceases.

The delay in filing a PFR by an individual suffering a medical condition will receive consideration from the Board based on that condition and other factors, Ortiz v. DOJ, 103 MSPR 621, 627-30 ¶¶ 16-25, 2006 MSPB 305 (2006):

The appellant acknowledges that his PFR was filed "long after the Board's finality date." As noted above, the PFR was filed slightly more than 3 years after the finality date. The appellant argues, however, that the effects of his depression and other medical conditions constitute good cause for the delay in filing his PFR.

The Board will excuse the untimely filing of a PFR only upon a showing of good cause for the delay. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.114(f). To establish good cause for an untimely filing, a party must show that he exercised due diligence or ordinary prudence under the particular circumstances of the case. Alonzo v. Department of the Air Force, 4 M.S.P.R. 180, 184 (1980). To determine whether an appellant has shown good cause, the Board will consider the length of the delay, the reasonableness of his excuse and his showing of due diligence, whether he is proceeding pro se, and whether he has presented evidence of the existence of circumstances beyond his control that affected his ability to comply with the time limits or of unavoidable casualty or misfortune which similarly shows a causal relationship to his inability to timely file his petition. Moorman v. Department of the Army, 68 M.S.P.R. 60, 62-63 (1995), aff'd, 79 F.3d 1167 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table).

the Board must consider the length of the delay in every good cause determination. Walls v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 29 F.3d 1578, 1582 (Fed. Cir. 1994). Here, the length of the delay in filing the PFR--more than 3 years--is very significant. See Summers v. U.S. Postal Service, 87 M.S.P.R. 403, ¶¶ 6, 12 (2000) (describing delays of 15 days and 1 month in filing petitions for review as "significant"), aff'd, 25 Fed. Appx. 827 (Fed. Cir. 2001).

The appellant relies on his illnesses to justify his delay in filing. To establish that an untimely filing was the result of an illness, the party must: (1) Identify the time period during which he suffered from the illness; (2) submit medical evidence showing that he suffered from the alleged illness during that time period; and (3) explain how the illness prevented him from timely filing his appeal or a request for an extension of time. Lacy v. Department of the Navy, 78 M.S.P.R. 434, 437 (1998).

...

After considering all of the relevant factors under Moorman, 68 M.S.P.R. at 62-63, we conclude that the appellant has not shown good cause for the delay of more than 3 years in filing his PFR. He clearly suffered from depression and other conditions during the relevant time period, and it appears from the appellant's statement and Dr. Cantor's letter that those conditions had at least some impact on his ability to meet the filing deadline. Certainly, the appellant's medical conditions would justify some amount of delay in filing his PFR. See Earl v. Department of the Army, 79 M.S.P.R. 194, ¶¶ 7, 9-10 (1998) (finding good cause for a delay of slightly more than 3 months where the appellant's depression and other conditions caused a "significant decline in her overall level of cognitive functioning"); Le Master v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 79 M.S.P.R. 680, ¶¶ 9-10 (1998) (finding good cause for a delay of 8 days where the appellant's depression "made him unable to complete routine life tasks"). However, given the extremely lengthy delay in this case, we find that the evidence submitted by the appellant is insufficient to justify the entire period of the delay. Dr. Cantor's letter indicates that the appellant had difficulty remaining employed, but does not state that his condition was so severe that he could not file a PFR or seek help to do so. E.g., Earl, 79 M.S.P.R. 194, ¶ 9 (the appellant's doctor indicated that she was not competent to file forms or claims during the period of the delay).

We also note that the appellant, an attorney, was able to represent himself quite competently before the administrative judge. He responded promptly and appropriately to the administrative judge's order and provided both legal and factual support for his claims. We further note that the appellant has represented himself in an equally competent manner on PFR. He has made reasoned legal arguments and presented supporting evidence for his positions with respect to both the merits and the timeliness of his PFR. There is no evidence showing that the appellant's condition in November and December 2002, during which he should have timely filed his PFR, was significantly worse than earlier in 2002, when he was able to represent himself before the administrative judge. Nor is there evidence that his condition in December 2005, when he eventually filed his PFR, was significantly better than at any time during the preceding 3 years. In the absence of such evidence, we are unable to conclude that the appellant's medical conditions justify the lengthy delay in filing his PFR.

The appellant's medical conditions, while serious, simply do not justify his failure to file his PFR or to request an extension, either on his own or with the assistance of others, for more than 3 years after the filing deadline. Accordingly, we find that the appellant has not shown good cause for the untimely filing of his PFR, and we therefore dismiss it.

The type of information that should be supplied to the Board to excuse an untimely PFR was indirectly described in Lacy v. Dept. of Navy, 78 MSPR 434, 437-38 (1998), imposing on AJs the requirement of notifying an appellant what information must be provided to the AJ when the appellant asserts that injury or illness affected his ability to file a timely appeal:

The Board will find good cause for waiver of its filing time limits where a party demonstrates that he suffered from an illness that affected his ability to file on time. See generally Hill v. Department of the Air Force, 54 MSPR 254, 256 (1992), aff'd, 991 F.2d 808 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table). To establish that an untimely filing was the result of an illness, the party must: (1) Identify the time period during which he suffered from the illness; (2) submit medical evidence showing that he suffered from the alleged illness during that time period; and (3) explain how the illness prevented him from timely filing his appeal or a request for an extension of time. See generally Andrews v. Department of the Army, 76 MSPR 147, 151 (1997).

...

When, as here, an appellant states that the reason for a filing delay is physical or mental illness, he must receive explicit information regarding the legal standard for establishing good cause on that basis, and he must be afforded a fair opportunity to submit evidence and argument to show that he met that standard.

The requirement that information to the appellant be provided by the Board as to how a waiver can be requested applies to belated PFRs, if the Board is on notice that a medical condition is urged to excuse the filing delay. See Smith v. MSPB, 168 F.3d 1305 (Fed. Cir. 1999); Simpson v. USPS, 83 MSPR 253, 257-58 ¶¶ 8-11 (1999) (under Lacy, the appellant need not establish that he was "incapacitated" during the period for filing the PFR; "rather, the ultimate test is whether his ability to timely file with the Board was 'affected' or 'impaired' by illness"; the issue is not whether the appellant could file the PFR at any point, but whether he could timely file the PFR). The Lacy notice described in Lacy v. Dept. of Navy, 78 MSPR 434, 437-38 (1998), is not optional. It cannot be omitted when the Board is on fair notice that the appellant asserts that illness may have resulted in a late PFR, irrespective of the Board's belief that the appellant would not have been able to comply with or meet the Lacy notice requirements. See Kelley v. MSPB, 117 Fed. Appx. 103, 105-06 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP).

The Board added in Hall v. OPM, 85 MSPR 371, 374-75 ¶¶ 8-10 (2000), concerning waiver of the deadline for a PFR filed by a person who was apparently suffering from mental incompetence while she attempted to secure (and litigated the denial of) a retirement annuity, that it is "inappropriate to require strict adherence to Board procedures by mentally ill appellants":

We further find that good cause exists for waiving the petition for review filing deadline. In that regard, we find that the evidence submitted is sufficient to meet the standard set forth in Lacy v. Department of the Navy, 78 MSPR 434 (1998). For example, in an August 3, 1993 letter to OPM, Dr. Thomas C. Scott stated that the appellant had been a patient of his since October 10, 1992. He further stated that she has a "disabling emotional problem that has prohibited her from understanding her disability and the process involved," and "very direct evidence of a personality disorder and a manic-depressive disorder." He concluded "with all reasonable medical certainty that [the appellant] is permanently and totally disabled." Where a party is diagnosed with a medical condition that is by its nature permanent or progressive in severity, it will be assumed to continue to exist after the date of diagnosis absent evidence to the contrary. Saroli v. Office of Personnel Management, 83 MSPR 249, 252 ¶ 9 (1999); see also Terry v. U.S. Postal Service, 83 MSPR 128, 130 ¶ 5 (1999).

More significantly, in French v. Office of Personnel Management, 810 F.2d 1118 (Fed. Cir. 1987), reh'g denied, 823 F.2d 489 (Fed. Cir. 1987), the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, having found that the appellant was presently mentally incompetent, not only instructed the Board to request pro bono representation in disability retirement appeals for appellants who assert that they are incompetent, but also held that it is patently unreasonable and fundamentally unfair to require or allow a presently incompetent person to show his own incompetence for many years in the past. In doing so, it cited the obvious desire of Congress that employees who become disabled during honorable service as a result of mental incompetence not lose their entitlements because of the effects of their illness. It noted that an appellant need not have been a "raving lunatic" continuously from the period in issue to trigger the statutory waiver provision. Rather, the statute may be satisfied "by one having some minimal capacity to manage his own affairs." Id. at 1119-20; see Saroli, 83 MSPR 249, 252-53 ¶¶ 8-10.

Thus, it is clear that both Congress and the court believe it inappropriate to require strict adherence to Board procedures by mentally ill appellants. Accordingly, we find that it is in the interests of adjudicatory efficiency to find that the appellant is presently incompetent and to waive our time limits for filing a petition for review. See Terry, 83 MSPR 128, 129-30 ¶¶ 4-5.

As to a mental disorder claimed to have caused a filing delay, there may be a duty of the Board to exercise special care in examining the record for supporting evidence. Alternatively, the Board may be expected to take special pains to ensure the record is adequately developed to make an informed finding on the claimed mental impairment and its relationship to the delayed filing. The Federal Circuit stated as to a belated petition for enforcement, Vargo v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 95-3410):

...The attorney for Vargo contends, as he did before the board, that the mental condition of his client prevented gaining access to Vargo's medical information to file with the board. He proffers to this court some sketchy evidence that could indicate mental disability. The history of this case, including the board's earlier waiver of an untimely filing and the findings of the United States Postal Service (USPS), seems to support the claim of mental disability. The MSPB in Vargo's original appeal recognized that he suffered from mental and emotional problems and found that good cause existed for the untimely filing of that appeal. It is also undisputed that the USPS documentation at the time Vargo was suspended from his position indicated that he had "mental and/or emotional problems" that classify him as "a hazard to [himself] or [his] fellow employees." There are, thus, clear indications that Vargo has in the past suffered from some mental disabilities.

Based on the record in this particular petition for enforcement, the board found that Vargo was able to pursue back pay and benefits from the USPS following his settlement agreement and that no evidence established mental disability sufficient to show good cause for the untimely filing. While that may be true on the limited record in this case, we are convinced that the board should consider fully the record in the prior board cases involving Vargo and such evidence as may be in the USPS files that relate to Vargo's disability. The board may also consider the sketchy evidence presented to this court together with any evidence of recent treatment if Vargo's attorney can obtain authorization for its release promptly. Based on such additional information, the board can determine more accurately whether Vargo has a continuing mental disability that would give rise to good cause for waiving the untimeliness of his petition for enforcement. See Pyles v. MSPB, 45 F.3d 411, 413 (Fed. Cir. 1995) ("Where...a party is diagnosed with a medical condition that is by its nature 'permanent or progressive' in severity, it will be assumed to continue to exist after the date of diagnosis absent rebuttal evidence of record to the contrary.").

The Board can discount medical evidence that is not developed in reasonable proximity to the period of illness that accounts for the delayed PFR. See Turman-Kent v. OPM, AT-0831-04-0365-I-1 (NP 2/11/2011) ("[T]his medical evidence is insufficient to support the appellant's claim for three major reasons: (1) Dr. Cavanaugh had not seen the appellant after the appellant and her husband moved to Atlanta until the appellant returned in July 2006; (2) there is no indication that Dr. Cavanaugh reviewed medical evidence concerning the status of the appellant's medical condition during the time period the appellant was in Atlanta; and (3) Dr. Cavanaugh's August 31, 2010 letter post-dates the appellant's petition for review, and there is no contemporaneous medical evidence to show the status of the appellant's medical condition between June 21, 2004, and August 10, 2010, which is the entire period of delay. See Jerusalem v. Department of the Air Force, 107 M.S.P.R. 660, ¶ 5 (2008), aff'd, 280 F. App'x 973 (Fed. Cir. 2008) (proffered medical evidence must address the entire period of the delay to show that an untimely filing was the result of an illness); Lacy v. Department of the Navy, 78 M.S.P.R. 434, 437 (1998)."), aff'd, Turman-Kent v. MSPB, 657 F.3d 1280 (Fed. Cir. 2011), post.

Turman-Kent was affirmed by the Federal Circuit (Circuit Judge Reyna dissenting), Turman-Kent v. MSPB, 657 F.3d 1280, 1282-84 (Fed. Cir. 2011):

After considering Ms. Turman-Kent's motion, the Board denied her petition for review as untimely filed. The Board found the statement of Dr. Cavanaugh insufficient to support Ms. Turman-Kent's claim because Dr. Cavanaugh had not examined Ms. Turman-Kent for several years during the six-year period of delay in filing her petition for review. The Board noted that Dr. Cavanaugh did not allege that she reviewed Ms. Turman-Kent's medical records for that time period, and it observed that Dr. Cavanaugh's statement post-dated the filing of Ms. Turman-Kent's petition for review. Finding no credible medical evidence regarding Ms. Turman-Kent's condition between June 2004 and July 2006, the Board determined that Ms. Turman-Kent "ha[d] not submitted sufficient evidence to support that her medical condition impaired her ability to timely file her petition for review, or to request an extension of time."

Ms. Turman-Kent bears a "heavy burden" to overturn the Board's determination that good cause has not been shown for her untimely filing. Zamot v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 332 F.3d 1374, 1377 (Fed. Cir. 2003); see Mendoza v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 966 F.2d 650, 653 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (en banc) ("whether the regulatory time limit for an appeal should be waived based upon a showing of good cause is a matter committed to the Board's discretion"). Board regulations require tardy petitioners to file a "specific and detailed description of the circumstances causing the late filing, accompanied by supporting documentation or other evidence." 5 C.F.R. § 1201.114(f). The Board has held that when petitioners allege delay for medical reasons, they must affirmatively identify medical evidence that addresses the entire period of delay. Jerusalem v. Dep't of the Air Force, 107 M.S.P.R. 660, 663, aff'd, 280 Fed. Appx. 973 (Fed. Cir. 2008).

Ms. Turman-Kent did not provide the Board with medical evidence that accounted for the entire six-year period of delay at issue in this case. In particular, there was no medical evidence regarding her condition between June 2004 and July 2006. The Board found it probative that Dr. Cavanaugh had not examined Ms. Turman-Kent during the year prior to her husband's death or for a three-year period after his death. Ms. Turman-Kent states that she saw two other physicians during that period when she lived in Georgia, yet no evidence from either of those physicians was presented, nor did Dr. Cavanaugh purport to rely on any such evidence in her evaluation of Ms. Turman-Kent's condition during that period. The dissent refers to a letter written by Ms. Turman-Kent's neurologist in 1988. That letter, which predates the period in question by 16 years, simply addresses the circumstances giving rise to Ms. Turman-Kent's illness and the fact that she would not be able to continue in her previous profession as an accountant.

We have recognized that the length of delay is an important factor for the Board to consider in determining whether a petitioner has shown good cause for an untimely filing. Walls v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 29 F.3d 1578, 1582 (Fed. Cir. 1994). The length of delay in this case--six years--is exceptional. Even in cases of ongoing illness, the Board has found the absence of medical evidence covering the entirety of a multi-year period to be fatal to a finding of good cause. See, e.g., Wilson v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 83 M.S.P.R. 223, 227 (1999) (noting that documentation of mental illness from 1988 to 1996 did not excuse absence of medical evidence from period between 1996 and 1999); Phillips v. Dep't of the Army, 77 M.S.P.R. 305, 309 (1998) (evidence of "recurrent major depressive disorder" diagnosed in 1995 did not establish petitioner's condition between 1995 and 1997). The Board did not abuse its discretion in demanding a well-documented explanation of the cause for Ms. Turman-Kent's delay in filing her appeal.

Before this court, Ms. Turman-Kent has submitted several pieces of medical evidence that were not presented to the Board. That evidence includes several annual disability certifications issued to her by the Social Security Administration, numerous pharmacy reports of medicines prescribed to her between 2003 and 2011, and an additional letter from Dr. Cavanaugh dated March 2011 that elaborates on Ms. Turman-Kent's condition. Because those items were not presented to the Board, they are not part of the record on appeal and are not properly before us. See Mueller v. U.S. Postal Serv., 76 F.3d 1198, 1201-02 (Fed. Cir. 1996) ("Because we are limited to reviewing decisions of the Board based on the record before the deciding official, we decline to base our judgment on evidence that was not part of the record before the administrative judge.").

The dissent relies on our decision in Pyles v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 45 F.3d 411 (Fed. Cir. 1995). The petitioner in Pyles presented the Board with a medical finding that she had "increasingly severe dementia," a "progressive organic brain disease[ ]" defined by "the loss of intellectual faculties." This court concluded that unrebutted medical evidence of dementia was sufficient to establish good cause for untimely filing. Id. at 415. The dissent argues that because brain cell death caused by an intracerebral hemorrhage is permanent, Pyles requires the Board to adopt a presumption that Ms. Turman-Kent's cognitive function could not have improved at any point during her six-year delay in filing a petition for review. However, our holding in Pyles was predicated not only on the permanence of dementia but also on its very nature as "[a] structurally caused permanent or progressive decline in several dimensions of intellectual function that interferes substantially with the individual's normal social or economic activity." Id. The medical literature cited by the dissent does not suggest that cognitive function never improves in persons who have suffered an intracerebral hemorrhage.

Ms. Turman-Kent's motion before the Board states that her illness had temporarily "flare[d] out of control," but that at some later time her condition had "shifted" and "improved" and she "became better able to remember business details, at times." That statement conflicts with Dr. Cavanaugh's assessment of Ms. Turman-Kent's condition as "stable" throughout the six-year period of delay. Moreover, Ms. Turman-Kent timely filed pleadings before OPM in 2003 and before the Board in 2004. Those filings were made during the multiyear period in which she was not under the care of Dr. Cavanaugh, and they undermine her claim of an ongoing, irreversible medical condition suffered a quarter-century ago that has permanently prevented her from timely filing paperwork in support of her claim. See Ortiz v. Dep't of Justice, 103 M.S.P.R. 621, 630 (2006) (finding no good cause for delay due to ongoing illness where evidence did not explain change in circumstances between period in which appellant complied with Board deadlines and period in which he failed to comply); Choco v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 69 M.S.P.R. 638, 641 (1996) (rejecting appellant's contention that he was precluded from timely filing a petition for review for more than five years, "especially in view of the fact that he was able to file a petition for review with the Board's regional office" four months before his petition for review was due for filing); Hawkins v. Dep't of the Navy, 67 M.S.P.R. 559, 562 (1995) (no good cause for eight-month delay in filing petition for review when appellant "was capable of filing a petition for enforcement [of a settlement agreement]" early in that period); Sing v. Dep't of the Navy, 51 M.S.P.R. 251, 254 (1991) (finding appellant's allegations of incapacity to file paperwork in a timely fashion undermined by timely filing of other papers during the period in question).[1]

[1] The procedures set forth in this court's decision in French v. Office of Personnel Management, 810 F.2d 1118 (Fed. Cir. 1987), for claimants seeking disability retirement benefits are available only upon a showing of incompetence. See Rapp v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 483 F.3d 1339, 1341 (Fed. Cir. 2007); Frank v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 111 M.S.P.R. 206, 209 (2009) (same for claimant seeking survivor annuity). In this case, the Board found that Ms. Turman-Kent has not shown that she was incompetent or otherwise incapable of filing a petition for review during the entire period of delay. Therefore, the decision in French does not provide an independent ground for reversing the Board's ruling in this case.

Ms. Turman-Kent also alleges that she did not receive "proper forms" in a timely manner and was therefore unable to file a timely petition for review. The document that she references is the January 2004 OPM reconsideration decision letter that denied her claim for survivor benefits. Following her receipt of that letter, Ms. Turman-Kent filed a timely appeal with the Board contesting OPM's denial of her claim for benefits. The OPM reconsideration letter is irrelevant to the timeliness of her subsequent petition for review by the full Board of the administrative judge's initial decision, which is the issue before us. Ms. Turman-Kent has never alleged that she did not receive the administrative judge's initial decision informing her of the deadline for submitting a petition for review to the full Board.

Finally, Ms. Turman-Kent states that she was hospitalized for hallucinations and seizures during the period following her return to Illinois. That allegation does not constitute evidence of her condition between June 2004 and July 2006 because she did not return to Illinois until after that time period. Her statement therefore cannot undermine the Board's ruling. Accordingly, we affirm the dismissal of Ms. Turman-Kent's petition for review as untimely.

If the appellant claims he was too ill to file the PFR, he should demonstrate that a representative already involved, or someone else, could not have filed it on his behalf or requested an extension of time for filing. Duatin v. OPM, 47 MSPR 565, 566 (1991) ("Even assuming that he was confined to his bed most of the time, there is no showing that someone else could not have filed on his behalf or requested an extension of time for filing."), followed, Martinez v. Dept. of Army, 60 MSPR 118, 120 (1993) ("Moreover, there is no showing by the appellant that one of her two representatives could not have filed on her behalf or requested an extension of time."); see White v. DHUD, DC-0752-14-0358-I-1 (NP 9/14/2015) ("The Board may find good cause for a delay in filing due to an employee's hospitalization, ill health, or incapacitation extending beyond the time limit as prescribed in the Board's regulations. Suchecki v. U.S. Postal Service, 47 M.S.P.R. 27, 29 (1991). Under circumstances similar to the ones in this appeal, however, the Board has found that appellants failed to establish good cause for a filing delay. See, e.g., id. (finding that, although the appellant showed that he was unable to file due to his hospitalization until a certain date, he failed to establish good cause for the 20-day delay in filing after his release from the hospital, and he also failed to show why his attorney was unable to file a petition on his behalf, or request an extension of time in which to file)...."); Suchecki v. USPS, 47 MSPR 27, 29 (1991) (appellant was ill, but was represented by counsel who, arguably, could have filed the PFR). A medical condition of the appellant that precludes him from filing a PFR will serve to extend the time allowed for the surviving spouse to file the petition. Cardinali v. Dept. of Army, 43 MSPR 414, 415 (1990) (still dismissing the PFR because of an extensive filing delay following contact with the attorney).

The Federal Circuit followed the Board's evaluation of medical reasons offered to justify delayed PFRs--looking for a clear connection between the medical condition and resultant inability to file the PFR on time, Ford-Clifton v. VA, 661 F.3d 655, 659 (Fed. Cir. 2011):

To demonstrate on appeal that the Board abused its discretion in not waiving the filing deadline for a petition for review, Petitioner bears a "heavy burden." Zamot v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 332 F.3d 1374, 1377 (Fed. Cir. 2003). The Board is afforded considerable discretion in such analyses. Id.; Mendoza v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 966 F.2d 650, 653 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (en banc) ("[W]hether the regulatory time limit for an appeal should be waived based upon a showing of good cause is a matter committed to the Board's discretion and this court will not substitute its own judgment for that of the Board.").

Board regulations require petitioners to file "[a] specific and detailed description of the circumstances causing the late filing, accompanied by supporting documentation or other evidence." 5 C.F.R. § 1201.114(f)(2). The Board has held that when petitioners allege delay for medical reasons, they must affirmatively identify medical evidence that addresses the entire period of delay and explain how the illness prevented a timely filing. Jerusalem v. Dep't of the Air Force, 107 M.S.P.R. 660, 663, aff'd, 280 Fed. Appx. 973 (Fed. Cir. 2008); see also Lacy v. Dep't of the Navy, 78 M.S.P.R. 434, 437 (1998).

The Board's determination that Petitioner's medical evidence failed to show good cause based on illness was reasonable and supported by substantial evidence. Petitioner contends that the delay was the result of depression and deep vein thrombosis, but did not provide evidence of a medical condition that prevented a timely filing between April 30, 2009 and April 17, 2010--the entire period of delay. The record evidence describing Petitioner's depression is limited to episodes occurring in mid-2008, many months prior to the relevant period. The only medical evidence actually dated from within the period of delay was for deep vein thrombosis; but, as the Board properly found, there is no accompanying explanation of how this condition prevented a timely filing.

Nor did the Board err in rejecting the other non-medical excuses raised. Relying upon established precedent, the Board properly held that neither alleged financial hardship nor inability to engage counsel establish good cause for delay. See Uson v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 105 M.S.P.R. 402, 403, aff'd, 250 Fed. Appx. 326 (Fed. Cir. 2007); Melville v. Dep't of the Air Force, 99 M.S.P.R. 233, 234 (2005). We therefore affirm the Board's determination that good cause was not shown to excuse Petitioner's filing delay of more than eleven months.

See Lachiewicz v. MSPB, 566 Fed. Appx. 927, 929 (Fed. Cir. 2014 NP) ("With respect to delays allegedly resulting from medical impairment, the Board has held that petitioners must 'affirmatively identify medical evidence that addresses the entire period of delay and explain how the illness prevented a timely filing.' Ford-Clifton v. Dep't of Veteran Affairs, 661 F.3d 655, 659 (Fed. Cir. 2011) (affirming Board where there was 'no accompanying explanation of how th[e] condition [deep vein thrombosis] prevented a timely filing' (citing Jerusalem v. The determinants of a "good cause" showing based on mental or physical incapacity were summarized, Phillips v. Dept. of Army, 77 MSPR 305, 309 (1998):

To establish good cause for an untimely petition for review on the basis of mental or physical incapacity, an appellant must identify the time period during which he was incapacitated, support the petition with corroborating medical evidence, and explain how his medical condition prevented him from timely filing a petition for review or a request for an extension of time. See Alexander v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 76 MSPR 285, 287-289 (1997). The medical evidence must cover the entire period of the delay. Id., 76 MSPR at 288-289.

See Turner-Thompson v. OPM, 112 MSPR 418, 421 ¶ 7, 2009 MSPB 193 (2009) ("The appellant's submissions do not reference any medications that she is taking. Additionally, the appellant's evidence fails to account for her filing delay by explaining how her conditions prevented her from filing her petition for review or a request for an extension of time."); Copeland v. USPS, 91 MSPR 469, 474 ¶ 10 (2002) ("Although his medical documents show that he was diagnosed with depression, they do not state that his condition affected him at the time of the filing deadline or during the period of the delay."); Luther v. DOD, 89 MSPR 172, 176 ¶ 11 (2001) (not excusing a late PFR: "no evidence of psychotherapy or any other form of continuing treatment, and none of hospitalizations"; the appellant was able to timely file an appeal and act as his own representative, suggesting that, despite depression, he was not incapacitated); Cornelius v. NCUA, 87 MSPR 497, 501-02 ¶¶ 7-9 (2001) (restating the proof required to excuse an extended delay in filing a PFR by an appellant claiming mental incapacity). An appellant's allegation that his appeal was delayed because he was in a state of mental shock was insufficient to warrant acceptance of a late PFR. Pate v. USPS, 31 MSPR 163, 164 (1986).

One concern of the Board if the appellant is aware that she will be incapacitated toward the end of the filing period is whether the appellant took prudent steps to permit the filing of an appeal during the period the appellant was not incapacitated, Olson v. USPS, 66 MSPR 383, 387 (1995):

The Board has found that an appellant's generalized claim of a medical problem, without a specific explanation of how the problem prevented her from meeting the filing deadline, does not constitute good cause to waive the Board's deadline. See Dooley v. Dept. of the Air Force, 57 MSPR 684, 690 (1993), aff'd, 22 F.3d 1105 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table). Here, the Attending Physician's Statement indicates that the appellant was "scheduled" to undergo surgery on August 15, 1994, two days before the expiration of the filing period. Although she states that she underwent major surgery, she does not indicate the period of any hospitalization or incapacity during that time.

We find that this case is distinguishable from Anderson v. Dept. of Justice, 999 F.2d 532, 534 (Fed. Cir. 1993), where the Federal Circuit addressed an appellant's claim that his one-day delay in filing a Board appeal resulted from his hospitalization in an alcohol and drug treatment program until seven days before the filing deadline, and his inability to contact his attorney until four days after his release from the hospital. In contrast, the appellant in this case did not undergo surgery until two days before the expiration of the filing period. Thus, she had time to make arrangements for the timely filing of her petition or to request the Clerk of the Board for an extension of time. See 5 CFR § 1201.114(e). Moreover, since she has not stated how long she was incapacitated, she has not shown that her filing the petition fourteen days late was due to circumstances beyond her control. See Anderson, 999 F.2d at 584.

If the appellant is incapacitated for but a portion of the period for filing a PFR, he should request an extension of time to file the PFR before the appeal period expires. See Purnell v. Dept. of Transp., 43 MSPR 406, 409 (1990). The Board may be disinclined to accept illness of an appellant as an excuse if the appellant had a representative who could have requested an extension of time for filing a PFR. See Magdaluyo v. OPM, 43 MSPR 132, 136 (1990). Whatever the circumstances, the Board considers how the petitioner who asserts illness used the time that was available to her. See Charles v. USPS, 86 MSPR 389, 393 ¶ 9 (2000) (medical reports did not explain how the appellant's conditions adversely affected her ability to file a PFR or request an extension during a period of three months); Wilson v. OPM, 83 MSPR 223, 226 ¶ 8 (1999) (appellant failed to support a claim of mental inability to pursue a PFR through documentation covering the period from the issuance of the initial decision in 1996 through the filing of the PFR in 1999); Nesby v. OPM, 81 MSPR 118, 121-22 ¶ 7 (1999) (declining to excuse a late PFR from an appellant who was confined in the hospital for mental incapacity, but who failed to explain the failure to file, or to demonstrate continuing mental incompetency, for several months following his release from hospitalization); cf. Cobarrubia v. OPM, 39 MSPR 697, 699 (1989) ("Although the appellant's submissions show that he suffered from chronic osteoarthritis and benign prostate hypertrophy during the relevant time period, they do not show that he was so incapacitated during the entire three years that he could not have filed--or could not have asked someone else to file on his behalf--a request to reopen and reconsider."). But see Duvall-Little v. Dept. of Navy, 89 MSPR 416, 419 ¶ 7 (2001) (substantial documentation with a late PFR showed a serious and incapacitating mental illness; although the documentation did not cover the relevant period for filing, the time limit for a PFR was waived when it appeared that the appellant's mental state impaired her judgment relative to time in a manner beyond her control); Jackson v. Dept. of Treasury, 13 MSPR 304, 305 (1982) (incoherent documentation from appellant; the Board extends the period for a PFR because it "appears that appellant's mental state perhaps has impaired his judgment relative to time in a manner beyond his control").

A generalized claim of a medical problem without a specific explanation of how the problem prevented the appellant from meeting the filing deadline does not constitute good cause. Black v. USDA, 104 MSPR 353, 357 ¶ 10, 2006 MSPB 340 (2006). In an enumerated format, to establish that an untimely filing was the result of illness or mental or physical incapacity, "the party must: (1) Identify the time period during which he suffered from the illness; (2) submit corroborating evidence showing that he suffered from the alleged illness during that time period; and (3) explain how the illness prevented him from timely filing his appeal or a request for an extension of time." The medical evidence should address the entire period of the delay. The evidence should establish that the appellant was prevented from filing a timely PFR as a consequence of his condition. Brenner v. USPS, 80 MSPR 394, 396 ¶ 7 (1998) (physician's statement insufficient that "it is possible" that appellant's cognition and ability to make sound decisions could be affected by his condition), aff'd, Brenner v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 99-3124); see Raleigh v. MSPB, 328 Fed. Appx. 639, 640 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP) (affirming the Board's determination that the appellant did not show how her medical condition caused her filing delay).

"Although 'medical evidence' is preferable as supporting or corroborating evidence in establishing good cause for an untimely filing on grounds of physical or mental illness, the Board will accept nonmedical supporting evidence if the appellant explains why medical evidence is not available." Osborne v. SBA, 81 MSPR 357, 360 ¶ 6 (1999); see Wilson v. OPM, 83 MSPR 223, 226 ¶ 6 (1999) (same as Osborne). When it evaluates mental incapacity, the Board may consider whether the appellant has filed pleadings pro se or whether the pleadings were prepared by or with the assistance of counsel or a guardian. See Hass v. OPM, 84 MSPR 110, 112-13 ¶¶ 5-7 (1999) (assessing whether mental incapacity justified delay in filing a disability retirement application). The Board will attempt to analyze whatever information is provided with the PFR to determine whether the appellant was sufficiently disabled that he could not request an extension for the PFR or file his PFR on the deadline. See Gaines v. USPS, 96 MSPR 504, 509-10 ¶¶ 9-11 (2004) (examining somewhat conflicting evidence from the appellant's treating physician to determine if the appellant was well enough to have filed a timely PFR).

A late PFR is excused by medical evidence that during the filing period the appellant was under medical care for a condition that impaired his ability to file legal papers. See Potter v. VA, 116 MSPR 256, 259 n.2, 2011 MSPB 20 (2011) (indications of psychiatric disorder permitted a five week delay in the PFR); Monley v. USPS, 74 MSPR 27, 30 (1997) (a belated PFR was excused by evidence showing the appellant had a psychiatric problem that precluded him from properly attending to his legal affairs); Robinson v. GSA, 28 MSPR 328, 329 n.* (1985) (four-month delay was justified by a physician's letter diagnosing post-traumatic stress disorder affecting the appellant's ability to timely file the PFR); Draper v. Dept. of Army, 21 MSPR 594, 595 (1984) (psychiatric problems); cf. Hampton v. VA, 25 MSPR 246 (1984) (late appeal). Confinement in a medical clinic justifies a delay in the PFR. Bondoc v. OPM, 31 MSPR 14, 15 n.1 (1986). So does an illness that caused the appellant to be bedridden, confined to home during his illness, and, as a result of the illness, unable to walk. Bagalayos v. OPM, 52 MSPR 617, 621 (1992).

The Board is concerned about generalized claims that PFRs were delayed by stress-related illnesses. Although mental impairment may justify delay in filing the PFR, Connelly v. USPS, 35 MSPR 614 (1987), an unsubstantiated allegation of mental impairment will not justify an extension of the filing date. Morderosian v. GSA, 29 MSPR 17, 18 (1985) (alleged mental impairment); see Nohr v. USPS, 112 MSPR 220, 222-23 ¶ 9, 2009 MSPB 174 (2009) (PTSD: "Although Ms. Gossens' and Mr. Jones' letters generally state the appellant's conditions and symptoms, they do not reference any medications that the appellant is taking and indicate that the appellant is able to work. Additionally, the appellant's evidence fails to account for her filing delay by explaining how her conditions prevented her from filing her petition for review or a request for an extension of time."); Nunn v. DOJ, 79 MSPR 368, 371 ¶ 6 (1998) (without an explanation of how personal circumstances prevented a timely PFR, the appellant's duress, resulting from the AJ's dismissal of his appeal, did not justify a six-month delay); Poole v. GPO, 63 MSPR 603, 606 (1994) ("The appellant, however, has not provided any medical corroboration of her alleged mental illness."); Brady v. Dept. of Labor, 57 MSPR 341, 344 (1993) ("vague, uncorroborated allegations of mental impairment that are unsupported by medical documentation...do not constitute good cause" for delayed PFR); Bond v. Dept. of Army, 51 MSPR 322, 325 (1991) (allegation that the appellant entered into a settlement agreement involuntarily as the "result of fraud and mutual mistake, based on [his] mental stress and physical condition" was too vague to warrant a waiver of the time limit); Livingston v. Dept. of Air Force, 46 MSPR 10, 12 n.* (1990) (alleged mental and physical impairment: the Board rejected as a medical explanation an affidavit from the appellant that also bore the unexplained signature of her physician).

There must be a credible explanation linking the disability with the filing delay. Gulbenkian v. Dept. of Navy, 55 MSPR 237, 240 (1992) (a petition to reopen was considered unreasonably delayed (ten years) when submitted by an appellant who provided evidence of mental incapacity but who apparently had lucid intervals that would have permitted earlier filing of petition); see Harbison v. OPM, 79 MSPR 328, 331-32 ¶¶ 6-7 (1998) (information was supplied as to Lupus, chronic fatigue syndrome, and other problems, but there was no linkage between the medical conditions and the inability to file a PFR during the relevant period of time); Phillips v. Dept. of Army, 77 MSPR 305, 309-10 (1998) (depression did not excuse a PFR delay of approximately 20 months; the appellant was able to secure earlier assistance to file an EEO complaint and a court case); Davenport v. USPS, 68 MSPR 539, 543 (1995) (medical evidence did not demonstrate that the appellant's mental condition prevented him from filing a PFR after he received documentation, constituting new evidence, of his mental disorder), aff'd, Davenport v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 96-3008) (decision to give a 100% schizophrenia rating was based on the appellant's almost complete unemployability due to the destructive impact of schizophrenia on his ability to work with others); Pineda v. OPM, 64 MSPR 578, 580 (1994) (no demonstration of how weakness and blindness in one eye caused a filing delay of more than four years); Owens v. DOJ, 64 MSPR 397, 399 (1994) ("the appellant's medical evidence does not explain how his mental condition prevented him from either filing his petition, requesting an extension, or asking someone else to file for him...[m]oreover, both the appellant and his physician indicate that his mental difficulties lasted until February 1994, and therefore they do not explain why his petition was not filed until the following June"); Amaral v. Dept. of Army, 63 MSPR 480, 484 (1994) (evidence of depression and stress, but no demonstration of how these conditions incapacitated the appellant or prevented her from filing a timely PFR); Weeks v. Dept. of Navy, 58 MSPR 16, 18 (1993) (insufficient for the appellant to state that the PFR was delayed because he was under the care of a doctor and "I have been on constant medication and have not been [emotionally] stable enough to file this appeal."); Brooks v. USPS, 56 MSPR 291, 293 (1993) (as to a claimed incompetency during the PFR filing period: the appellant "fails to furnish sufficient details explaining his condition and submits no medical evidence demonstrating that he was so incapacitated that he was precluded from filing in a timely manner"); Stewart v. USPS, 56 MSPR 14, 16 (1992) (as to an alleged psychological disorder and stress: noting that the appellant "appears not to have tried to get someone else to file a timely petition for him, nor to have sought--on his own or through another--an extension of the filing deadline"); Abrams v. OPM, 56 MSPR 6, 8 (1992) (the appellant's assertions of illness were not supported by medical evidence, not did they demonstrate circumstances that prevented the appellant from timely refiling her PFR).

A typical ruling is that "[t]he appellant's claims of agitation and depression are not supported by medical evidence and thus do not establish good cause for the delay." Ellison v. USPS, 45 MSPR 244, 246 (1990); see Hatcher v. DOJ, 76 MSPR 97, 100-01 (1997) (evidence of emotional problems did not cover the whole period of a filing delay, a recovery date was not established with precision, and the appellant's claims of disability were undercut by the fact that he worked for several months prior to filing a PFR); Gilley v. Dept. of Army, 65 MSPR 277, 280 (1994) (as to assertions of stress, drug and alcohol abuse, and PTSD: the submissions did not establish that the appellant was incapacitated during the relevant time period and was unable to timely file his petition himself, request an extension of time, or ask someone else to file the petition for him); Smith v. AID, 56 MSPR 19, 21 (1992) (assertions, unsupported by medical evidence, that she was depressed, and unable to secure union representation did not justify a delay); Dorris v. USPS, 55 MSPR 678, 681 (1992) ("[C]onclusory and uncorroborated statement that mental and physical stress caused him to make irrational judgments and decisions does not, without more, establish good cause for the untimely filing...."; alleging that the agency representative convinced the appellant that withdrawing appeal and resigning were right thing to do addressed the merits of PFR, not its timeliness); Hill v. Dept. of Air Force, 54 MSPR 254, 256 (1992) (a claim of depression was insufficient to excuse a lengthy delay: "[t]o establish good cause for the untimely filing, the appellant must establish how her illness prevented her from meeting the Board's filing requirements, and she must submit corroborating medical evidence."); Williams v. USPS, 51 MSPR 419, 421 (1991) (a statement that the appellant was "angry and upset" was insufficient; he did "not claim illness or other circumstances sufficient to result in his inability to promptly file his petition.... Pate v. USPS, 31 MSPR 163, 163 (1986) (employee's mere statement that he 'was in mental shock' was insufficient to establish good cause)."). Another decision determined that "[a] contention that stress made it impossible for an appellant to timely file a PFR, unaccompanied by medical documentation that the person was actually impaired, has not been found to be good cause for waiving time requirements for filing." Rufus v. USPS, 50 MSPR 62, 65 (1991); see Bryant v. Dept. of Navy, 59 MSPR 70, 72 n.2 (1993) ("[A]general unsupported allegation that an appellant is under stress does not establish good cause for an untimely filing."). "[C]laims of stress, unaccompanied by medical documentation showing that the appellant was actually impaired, do not show good cause for the untimely filing of a petition for review." McCarthy v. Dept. of Air Force, 56 MSPR 475, 477 (1993). But see Robinson v. GSA, 28 MSPR 328, 329 n.1 (1985) (PFR late by four months was excused based on a physician's diagnosis of PTSD); Watson v. OPM, 79 MSPR 308, 313 ¶ 9 (1998) (PTSD, the medical condition that formed the basis for the appellant's FERS retirement application, excused a PFR filing delay of one day).

Hawkins v. Dept. of Navy, 67 MSPR 559, 561-63 (1995), declined to excuse PFR delay that the appellant alleged resulted from mental disability:

In his response to a notice from the Clerk of the Board, the appellant submitted a declaration made under penalty of perjury. The appellant stated that, during the time period for filing a petition for review, he "was suffering from such severe mental depression, anxiousness, hypertension, and disorientation that [he] did not understand the nature and consequences of [his] actions or inactions." He stated that, because he could not afford legal counsel, there was no one looking out for his best interests during this time because he was unable to do so himself.

The appellant also submitted an April 6, 1994 letter from his psychiatrist that indicated that his symptoms "as of this date indicate that he is at the present time clinically depressed," and that his symptoms include insomnia, instability, feelings of helplessness and hopelessness, extreme mood swings, confusion, and some memory loss. The psychiatrist felt "quite strongly" that reinstating the appellant to a position with a different supervisor or in a different mail room "would alleviate this situational depression right away."

To establish good cause for an untimely submission on the basis of mental incapacity, an appellant must submit corroborating medical evidence, and must explain how his medical condition prevented him from timely filing his submission. See Gilley v. Dept. of the Army, 65 MSPR 277, 280 (1994). The medical evidence must address the entire period of the delay. See Sing v. Dept. of the Navy, 51 MSPR 251, 253-54 (1991). Here, although the appellant's medical evidence shows that he was diagnosed as being clinically depressed as of April 6, 1994, it does not show that he was incapacitated during the entire filing period, and it does not indicate how his condition prevented him from filing a petition for review. The record also does not show when the appellant recovered from his alleged incapacity; thus, we cannot determine if he acted diligently upon his recovery. See Hill v. Dept. of the Air Force, 54 MSPR 254, 256 note (1992), aff'd, 991 F.2d 808 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table). In this regard, we note that the appellant was capable of filing a petition for enforcement of the settlement agreement in September 1994. The appellant's inability to retain and/or afford an attorney does not establish good cause for the delay. See, e.g., Sing, 51 MSPR at 254.

In Pyles v. MSPB, 45 F.3d 411, 415 (Fed. Cir. 1995), the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit found that an appellant who submitted a letter from her doctor that suggested that she suffers from a degree of dementia, showed good cause for her delay in filing a Board appeal. The court found that, when a party is diagnosed with a medical condition, like dementia, that is, by its nature, permanent or progressive in severity, it will be assumed to continue to exist after the date of diagnosis absent rebuttal evidence of record to the contrary. Id.

We find that Pyles is distinguishable from this case. The medical evidence submitted in Pyles indicated that Pyles was incapable of making any legal or medical decisions due to her dementia. See id. at 413. Here, the appellant's medical evidence does not indicate that he was, at any time, incapable of making a legal decision such as filing a petition for review. The appellant also has not shown that his condition was, by its nature, permanent or progressive in severity....

See Lundy v. Dept. of Navy, 95 MSPR 407, 410 ¶ 11 (2004) (Board declined to excuse a four-year delay in the filing of a PFR based on the appellant's hypertension and discouragement about the likely outcome of his case).

Hudson v. OPM, 68 MSPR 96, 98-99 (1995), dismissed as untimely a PFR filed on April 26 that should have been filed by March 8. The Board considered unpersuasive appellant's claims of stress:

We find here that the appellant has not shown specifically how her medical condition prevented her from timely filing her petition for review. In her declaration, the appellant states:

I became ill in January of this year 1995. My doctor put me on a medical leave of absence from January through [sic].

I was so stressed out that I was on heavy medication to help me to relax. I was unable to constructively answer the petition for review after the judge made his decision at the appropriate time due to my illness.

The appellant's statement does not explain how her "stressed out" condition and the medication she was taking impaired her ability to timely file her petition for review. See Martinez, 60 MSPR at 120-21; Caballero, 59 MSPR at 301-02.

As to the appellant's supporting medical documentation, Dr. Acosta stated in his January 10, 1995 medical statement that the appellant was "suffering from severe stress, aggravating her physical health," and advised that she be permitted "a leave of absence for about 6 weeks."

In his January 18, 1995 medical statement, Dr. Acosta stated that the appellant was under his care, that she was suffering from chronic anxiety that was controlled with medication, and that she had recently been experiencing many physical symptoms that she claimed were stress related. Dr. Acosta stated that he agreed that the appellant's "stress and anxiety level [was] very high, and she need[ed] to learn to cope." He noted that the appellant believed that her job was "one of her major stressors" and recommended that she be granted a 6-week absence from work.

In his March 6, 1995 medical statement, Dr. Acosta, in addition to reiterating the appellant's conditions, stated that she was "emotionally in no shape to return to work" and recommended a continued leave of absence for an additional 3-weeks.

Finally, in his April 3, 1995 medical statement, Dr. Acosta stated that the appellant still remained under his care and was unable to return to work at that time. He recommended an additional 2 weeks of leave.

While Dr. Acosta's medical statements indicate that the appellant was experiencing job-related stress and anxiety problems, those statements neither establish that the appellant's problems prevented her from timely filing her appeal nor explain how they prevented her from doing so. See Blaine, 59 MSPR at 452; see also Booker, 53 MSPR at 470.

See Zuberi v. Dept. of Navy, 67 MSPR 555, 558 (1995) (considerable delay was not excused by psychological impairment when medical information did not explain the degree of incapacitation over years of delay in a request for reopening); Brown v. Dept. of Air Force, 64 MSPR 93, 95-96 (1994) (same result involving paranoid schizophrenia, distinguishing a delay excused in Anderson v. DOJ, 999 F.2d 532 (Fed. Cir. 1993), for the individual who was hospitalized and, on release, encountered some delays in contacting his attorney); Hatcher v. DHHS, 27 MSPR 557, 558 (1985) (once the appellant was released from the hospital for stress-related headaches, he should have acted more quickly than waiting several months to file a PFR). But see Dunn v. In Reed v. GSA, 38 MSPR 346, 348 (1988), after the appellant lost his case before the Board with an attorney and, after he unsuccessfully pursued an appeal to the Federal Circuit pro se, he engaged another attorney, filed a PFR, and sought to have the Board reopen his case to receive additional evidence substantiating his claim that he was suffering from a mental disability. The appellant provided the affidavit of a physician who said that the appellant's "judgment and thinking have been more impaired than had been suspected." In his affidavit to the Board, the appellant stated that he was under a psychiatrist's care and was not mentally competent to "assist" in his appeal. The Board declined to waive the time limit that barred the PFR:

[T]he medical report from the appellant's doctor merely states in a conclusory manner that the appellant's judgment and thinking must have been more impaired than had been suspected given the handling of his appeal. It is also significant that the report does not state that the appellant was mentally incompetent. Moreover, his designation of an attorney to represent him in his appeal, and his subsequent pro se appeal of the initial decision to the Federal Circuit, are actions that belie an assertion of mental incompetence....

Whether the filing delay results from mental disability or another type of illness or injury, there should be a demonstration from the evidence submitted of how a medical condition prevented timely filing of the PFR. Marquez v. OPM, 54 MSPR 89, 91 (1992), illustrated the Board's approach to some cases involving appellants who seek to justify long delays in filing based on illness:

The appellant's claim of poor health alone is not sufficient to establish a medical condition that rendered him immobile and unable to function on his own behalf every day of the nearly 2 1/2 year delay period. The appellant has not claimed that he could not leave his bed or that he was incapacitated to the point that he could not have asked someone to assist him in appealing his case. The appellant's doctor has corroborated the appellant's arthritis and high blood pressure but has not provided any analysis of the severity of these conditions. Thus, the appellant has not shown that his poor health precluded him from taking any affirmative action to prosecute his appeal during the entire 28 month period.

Whittington v. DHHS, 51 MSPR 141, 143 (1991), declined to waive the time limit exceeded by a few days by an appellant who claimed that he had, during most or all of the filing period, been medically incapacitated and was an in-patient being treated for alcohol and drug abuse. The Board observed that "[a]lthough the appellant's affidavit is unrefuted, it is insufficient to establish that he was so incapacitated that he could not timely file. The appellant did not furnish any details or supporting documentary evidence to buttress his bare assertion of incapacity," Board Vice Chairman Amador dissented in Whittington, arguing that the Clerk's show cause order to the appellant did not inform him of the type of information that was required to support his claim of incapacitation. Davis v. USPS, 48 MSPR 114, 116 (1991), collected reasons for denying late PFRs unsupported by medical evidence with the explanation that:

We find that the appellant has not established that his ailments were of sufficient severity or duration to prevent him from timely refiling his petition.... The appellant's statement that he was ill for the "past month or so," is unsworn, and does not address the entire period of the delay.... Moreover, the appellant's comments regarding his various ailments and their effect on his ability to open the Board's correspondence are not supported by medical evidence and thus do not establish good cause for the delay....

[T]he appellant has not established that his condition was of sufficient severity or duration to prevent him from timely filing his petition.... Although it accepts and relies on appellants' statements of their medical condition that are submitted in disability retirement cases, see, e.g., Easterwood v. OPM, 48 MSPR 125, 132 (1991), the Board expects that, as to the procedural issue of the timeliness of a PFR, assertions concerning medical impairments preventing timely filings should be accompanied by corroborative evidence and that the information from the appellant should be submitted in the form of an affidavit or a statement signed under the penalty of perjury. Gentry v. OPM, 40 MSPR 46, 48 (1989) (additionally, failure to notify the Board of an address change is not excusable neglect); see Miller v. VA, 55 MSPR 227, 229 (1992) ("In her statements on the issue of timeliness, the appellant appears to allege that she was hospitalized on March 27, 1992, and from April 21-30, 1992, and was thus unable to file a petition for review. However, the appellant submitted no evidence in support of this allegation and has failed twice in response to show-cause orders on timeliness to provide an affidavit or statement signed under penalty of perjury that explained her delay in filing. Waiver of the timeliness requirement here thus is not appropriate."); Morton v. Dept. of Navy, 53 MSPR 165, 167 (1992) ("We find that the appellant's assertions that she occasionally suffers from "depressive states" because of her unemployment, which is unsupported by medical evidence, and that she worked on her petition during her spare time, even if true, do not demonstrate that degree of diligence and prudence required for a good cause showing."); Chaney v. OPM, 43 MSPR 276, 279-80 (1990) (allegation of impairment was unsupported by medical documentation; evidence from the respondent indicated that during the period for filing a PFR the appellant filed and confirmed the filing of an application with OPM for a refund of retirement deductions).

General claims that the delay was caused by the time spent by appellant recovering from illness will not excuse a late PFR. Stibbe v. GPO, 57 MSPR 5, 6-7 (1993) (a physical therapy program and studies did not excuse delayed a PFR); see DeMar v. Dept. of Navy, 71 MSPR 292, 294 (1996) (sick leave status was insufficient to justify a delayed PFR when "[t]he appellant has neither identified her medical condition nor shown how it prevented her from timely filing her petition for review."); Luna v. Dept. of Air Force, 67 MSPR 69, 72 (1995) ("[a] generalized claim of a medical problem, without a specific explanation of how the problem prevented [the appellant] from meeting the filing deadline, does not constitute good cause for the untimeliness" of a PFR); Peras v. OPM, 61 MSPR 401, 402 (1994) ("general allegations of illness and old age do not provide a basis for waiver of the filing deadlines"); Bulatao v. OPM, 45 MSPR 337, 339 (1990) ("Although the appellant requests a waiver of untimeliness in his petition, he indicates no ground for the delay in filing, other than that he was recovering from an illness. In the absence of further specification or supporting evidence, this is not sufficient to constitute good cause for waiver of the Board's time limits.").

Medical evidence showing good cause for a late PFR must be submitted by the appellant, it must be specific, and it must be timely. The Board did not abuse its discretion by refusing to excuse a late PFR from an employee who stated she was ill in the months following issuance of the initial decision and invited the Board to contact her physician for further information. It was not the Board's duty to seek evidence from her physician. Ragland v. DOD, 32 MSPR 177, 178 (1987), aff'd, Ragland v. DOD (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 87-3306). A months old statement from a physician indicating that the appellant was suffering from anxiety and depression and was unable to withstand ordinary stresses of everyday living was not enough to show that the appellant was unaware of her responsibilities in prosecuting her appeal or in finding a representative. Lucas v. Dept. of Army, 29 MSPR 455, 456 n.* (1985). The medical explanation must show that the condition disabled the appellant from filing the PFR during the period that the condition existed. There must be a demonstration of a causal relationship, not evidence of a medical problem at a time unrelated to the time that the PFR was to have been filed. See Worley v. VA, 38 MSPR 6, 8 (1988) (evidence of epilepsy and psychological problems did not alone justify a nine month delay in filing a PFR). If an appellant is hospitalized and relies upon his confinement to justify a late PFR, he must provide some indication of the dates of the hospitalization to justify the filing delay. Jackson v. Dept. of Army, 15 MSPR 365, 367 (1983); see Miller v. USPS, 63 MSPR 646, 648 (1994) ("[T]he appellant's bare assertion that his physician confined him to 'bed rest' without supporting proof as to the nature of the disability and how this condition precluded him from timely filing his petition does not establish good cause.").

An assertion that the appellant was ill during the two days that his PFR was delayed and that he could not obtain medical documentation until after the appeal period expired was insufficient when no documentation or affidavit supported the allegation of illness. Jacobo v. OPM, 32 MSPR 219, 220 (1987). An allegation that it was impossible to obtain medical documentation in a timely fashion because of the appellant's inability to pay for the production of that information was insufficient to warrant waiver of the delay of many months in producing the evidence. The Board reasoned that "appellant had ample time either to resolve her financial difficulties or to make alternative arrangements to produce other medical evidence...." Dozier v. Dept. of Educ., 35 MSPR 258, 260 n.2 (1987).

Delos Reyes v. OPM, 55 MSPR 54, 55-56 (1992), provided typical discussion of a PFR arguably delayed by medical and other problems:

To establish good cause for the untimely filing of an appeal, a party must show that he exercised due diligence or ordinary prudence under the particular circumstances of the case....

On May 19, 1992, the appellant's brother sent a letter to the Board, stating that the appellant was sick and could not speak or write. No medical documentation was submitted in support of this statement. The letter was not sworn to and did not specifically mention or address the issue of timeliness. These unverified statements fail to comply with the requirements of 5 CFR § 1201.114(f) and do not constitute good cause for the untimely filing....

Moreover, even if we consider the letter, to the extent that the statement of the appellant's health in it can be construed as an explanation for the untimely petition, it does not establish good cause for the untimely filing. A mere assertion that illness prevented an appellant from timely filing a petition for review is insufficient to establish good cause for delay.... Because the appellant has not shown how his illness prevented him from meeting the Board's filing requirements, and did not submit corroborating medical evidence, he has not established good cause for his delay.... Accordingly, the appellant's petition for review must be dismissed as untimely filed.

See Flocco v. Dept. of Navy, 44 MSPR 505, 508 (1990) (insufficient showing of disabling anxiety, stress, depression, and other medical problems when the evidence consisted of letters from the appellant, his family, and his doctor that failed to identify the time period of incapacitation and did not explain how the condition prevented him from timely filing a PFR or requesting an extension).

The ability to work may support a determination that a medical condition did not prevent the appellant from filing a PFR. Wirzberger v. Dept. of Treasury, 101 MSPR 448, 451 ¶ 9, 2006 MSPB 74 (2006); cf. Bilbrew v. USPS, 111 MSPR 34, 39 ¶ 11, 2009 MSPB 41 (2009) (appellant's return to work following a suspension supported a finding that his medical condition did not prevent him from filing a timely appeal). Active participation by the appellant in other legal proceedings may suggest that the appellant's medical condition did not prevent her from timely filing a PFR. See Stribling v. Dept. of Educ., 107 MSPR 166, 2007 MSPB 261 (2007) (during the period leading to the PFR deadline the appellant filed an application for disability retirement and responded to a communication from OPM and the appellant made written submissions in the course of a state administrative proceeding). Wade v. USPS, DA-0353-10-0418-I-1 (NP 8/18/2011), explained how participation in litigation-related activities during a period of incapacitation may undercut a request to waive a PFR deadline:

...The Board has held that an appellant's ability to participate in other litigation during a period of purported disability fatally undermines the appellant's claim that his disability prevented him from filing a petition for review or a request for an extension. See Stribling v. Department of Education, 107 M.S.P.R. 166, ¶ 14 (2007); see also Miller v. Department of Homeland Security, 110 M.S.P.R. 258, ¶ 19 (2008) (the appellant's claim that his delay was due to his medical condition was fatally undermined by his repeated attempts to contact his attorney and the bar association); see generally Pradier v. U.S. Postal Service, 113 M.S.P.R. 495, ¶ 12 (2010) (the appellant failed to establish good cause for his delay where he pursued informal attempts to resolve the claim in the interim). We also note that the appellant's delay of over 4 years was material. See generally Stribling, 107 M.S.P.R. 166, ¶ 14 (even for a pro se appellant, a delay of a year is significant).

Jordan v. USPS, 83 MSPR 500, 506-07 ¶ 15 (1999) (aff'd, Jordan v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 00-3046)), found inconsistent with a claim of mental disability appellant's several pro se litigation efforts:

Moreover, any alleged mental or physical incapacity is belied by the circumstantial evidence of capacity in this case. For example: The appellant applied for and was awarded OPM disability retirement benefits on or about July 1996; he appealed an unfavorable state unemployment compensation decision on July 11, 1996, and January 27, 1997; he appealed OWCP's decision to discontinue his workers' compensation benefits on July 30, 1996, and January 2, 1998; he conducted settlement negotiations with the agency's representative concerning his removal on July 31, 1996; he filed and pursued a grievance of his removal from September 1996-98; and on November 8, 1996, he requested reconsideration of EEOC's decision dismissing his discrimination complaint as untimely filed. All of these pro se acts constitute significant circumstantial evidence that the appellant was not mentally or physically incapacitated from filing a petition for review or requesting an extension of time from June 1996 through January 27, 1999. Accordingly, because the appellant has not shown that his mental or physical condition prevented him from filing a petition for review or requesting an extension of time during the entire 31-month period of the delay, he has failed to show good cause to excuse the untimeliness of his petition for review. Illness of counsel may qualify as a basis for acceptance of an otherwise untimely PFR. Williams v. VA, 32 MSPR 91, 91 (1987); see Hennelly v. Dept. of Transp., 26 MSPR 595, 596 n.* (1985) (excusing delay of a day caused by illness of counsel). But delay in filing a PFR was not excused by an attorney's disability causing a delay of one year: although the appellant is represented in Board proceedings, he remains responsible for the prosecution of his appeal. Vice v. OPM, 20 MSPR 121, 122-23 (1984). Good cause for delay is not shown if the representative is ill, but the illness does not cover the period allowed for filing the PFR. See De Galope v. OPM, 41 MSPR 13, 15-16 (1989). In Mashack v. USPS, 96 MSPR 174, 177 ¶ 11 (2004), despite repeated hospitalizations and serious health problems of counsel, the Board dismissed a PFR that was not filed rapidly enough after counsel's recovery.
