a. Real Estate Activity--DHUD

Wild v. DHUD, 692 F.2d 1129, 1133 (7th Cir. 1982), aff'g Wild v. DHUD, 7 MSPR 595 (1981), considered the discharge of an employee who inadequately maintained privately held rental housing units. The court distinguished off-duty misconduct having no relation to a job or agency mission from "the problem of the public employee whose private conduct mocks the mission of the agency that employs him." In an opinion provoking a strong dissent arguing for the need for proof rather than presumption of nexus, the court declared:

[W]here an employee's off-duty behavior is blatantly inconsistent with the mission of the employer and is known or likely to become known, most any employer, public or private, however broadminded, would want to fire the employee and would be reasonable in wanting to do so; and we find no evidence that Congress intended to deny this right to federal agencies....

It may be replied that all Wild is asking is that the agency be forced to prove a reduction of its efficiency due to off-duty misconduct, rather than being allowed to infer from the relation between the misconduct and the agency's mission. But proof of that relation is the substantial evidence that the statute requires; to require more proof would be unnecessary and unrealistic.... [w]e will not force HUD to continue employing a "slumlord" in a responsible position until it can prove, by the cumbersome methods of litigation, what ought to be obvious--that the credibility and effectiveness of the department are undermined by such discordance between public duty and private conduct.

We have said it is obvious; but that does not mean it would be easy to prove. HUD is a vast organization. To prove by the methods of litigation that anything which a nonsupervisory employee did would impair the efficiency of the organization would be a daunting task, and one that would rarely be worth undertaking to remove a single employee.... We do not think it was the purpose of the Civil Service Reform Act to make it impossible as a practical matter to get rid of a civil servant whose off-duty conduct is in direct conflict with the mission of the agency that employs him....

Removal was affirmed of a GS-11 Single Family Housing Specialist who was involved in a personal transaction with a close personal associate to purchase a HUD property in violation of agency regulations governing transactions and disclosures of financial interests by agency employees. Circumstantial evidence, involving review of all the steps of the transactions, and the cumulative effect of the elements of the transaction, persuaded the Board that the appellant's intent was to circumvent the restrictions imposed by agency rules. Suarez v. DHUD, 96 MSPR 213, 220-21 ¶¶ 20-23 (2004), found the Board considering the charges pertaining to agency and government-wide ethics regulations:

As noted above, the agency's first charge was that the appellant's conduct relating to the purchase of the Edgemont Street property violated the OGE and agency Standards of Ethical Conduct. The first specification was that the appellant's conduct violated 5 C.F.R. §§ 2635.101(b)(3) and 2635.703. Section 2635.101(b)(3) provides as follows:

Employees shall not engage in financial transactions using nonpublic Government information or allow the improper use of such information to further any private interest.

Similarly, section 2635.703(a) provides as follows:

An employee shall not engage in a financial transaction using nonpublic information, nor allow the improper use of nonpublic information to further his own private interest or that of another, whether through advice or recommendation, or by knowing unauthorized disclosure.

The second specification was that the appellant's conduct violated 5 C.F.R. § 2635.101(b)(8). That section provides:

Employees shall act impartially and not give preferential treatment to any private organization or individual.

The third specification was that the appellant's conduct violated 5 C.F.R. § 2635.101(b)(14). That section provides:

Employees shall endeavor to avoid any actions creating the appearance that they are violating the law or the ethical standards set forth in [OGE's regulations].

The fourth specification was that the appellant's conduct violated 5 C.F.R. § 7501.104(a)(4), which is contained in the agency's Standards of Ethical Conduct. That section provides that employees

shall not directly or indirectly receive, acquire, or own...stock or another financial interest in a...single family dwelling...which is owned or subsidized by [HUD], or which is subject to a note or mortgage or other security interest insured by [HUD], except to the extent that the stock or other interest represents the employee's principal residence.
