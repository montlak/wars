c. Performance Action

Performance-related adverse actions offer fertile ground for rehabilitation. The reasoning in Douglas is applicable to adverse actions taken for performance reasons under Chapter 75. Hatler v. Dept. of Air Force, 6 MSPR 724, 727, 6 MSPB 605 (1981). But the Douglas factors do not apply equally to performance and misconduct actions, McCoy v. DOD, 20 MSPR 284, 287 (1984):

[W]e note that although the Board has previously held that the reasoning in Douglas is generally applicable to performance-based actions taken under 5 USC Chapter 75, Springer v. U.S. Postal Service, 7 MSPB 63 (1981), the Board has also noted that such factors as the employee's past disciplinary record, past work record and the adequacy and effectiveness of alternative sanctions are not equally applicable to performance cases. Van Gilder v. Department of the Air Force, 9 MSPB 572 at 574 (1982). The Board has further held that a relevant factor in determining the appropriateness of an agency penalty of removal for unsatisfactory performance is whether the nature of appellant's deficient performance could adversely affect his performance in a different position. Hatler v. Department of the Air Force, 6 MSPB 605, 607 (1981). In the instant case, the record shows that despite appellant's 26 years of satisfactory service prior to the action involved in this case, he failed to exert sufficient effort to perform basic receiving procedures as a Warehouse Worker, including such simple tasks as counting items of property. Thus, the agency had reasonable grounds to believe that his performance would not change if he was suspended, reassigned or demoted to another position.

Parton v. FCC, 7 MSPR 236, 239-40, 7 MSPB 142 (1981), observed that In light of the legislative history of Merit System Principle 6, 5 USC 2301(b)(6), that "[e]mployees should be retained on the basis of the adequacy of their performance, inadequate performance should be corrected, and employees should be separated who cannot or will not improve their performance to meet required standards," agencies are encouraged to inform their employees of any deficient performance and provide them an opportunity to improve before initiating a removal. When a license examiner was removed for an excessive error rate in grading examinations, but was never told of his deficiency before the action was taken and given no chance to improve, the Board found the penalty excessive because of the relevant Douglas factor concerning the clarity of either the rules that were violated or warnings about the conduct in question. Believing that the appellant's problem was carelessness rather than inability, the Board substituted a 30-day suspension:

The appellant urged in his petition that his removal was unduly harsh because, inter alia, the agency never assisted the appellant to improve his performance in contravention of the Merit Systems Principles. Principle 6 provides at 5 USC § 2301(b)(6) that "[e]mployees should be retained on the basis of the adequacy of their performance, inadequate performance should be corrected, and employees should be separated who cannot or will not improve their performance to meet required standards." That statutory language evolved from the following Senate and House proposals:

Employees are to be retained on the basis of their performance, and those who cannot or do not meet the required standards should be separated. Training is to be provided to employees to improve organizational and individual performance.

S. Rep. No. 95-969, 95th Cong., 2d Sess. 19 (1978), U.S. Code Cong. & Admin. News 1978, pp. 2723, 2741.

Employees whose performance is inadequate should be encouraged to improve, and employees who cannot and will not improve their performance to meet established standards should be separated from the Federal service.

H. R. Rep. No. 95-1403, 95th Cong., 2d Sess. 16 (1978).

We conclude that, in light of the legislative history of Merit Systems Principle 6 as reflected in the House and Senate Reports, the Principle encourages agencies to inform their employees of any deficient performance and afford them an opportunity to improve before initiating a removal action, as opposed to another disciplinary action. We held in Wells v. Harris, 1 MSPB 199, 203, 1 MSPR 208, 214 (1979), that the Merit Systems Principles are not self-executing in that they cannot be the basis of an action unless they are implemented by a law, rule, or regulation, but according to the legislators' statement of purposes in enacting them, the Principles are "expressly stated to furnish guidance to Federal agencies in carrying out their responsibilities in administering the public business." H.R. Conf. Rep. No. 95-1717, 95th Cong., 2d Sess. 3 (1978), U.S. Code Cong. & Admin. News 1978, p. 2860.

As we held in Douglas, 5 MSPB 331-32, 5 MSPR 304-05 one relevant factor in determining the appropriateness of a penalty is "the clarity with which the employee was on notice of any rules that were violated in committing the offense, or had been warned about the conduct in question...." Analogously, the appellant in the case now before us presented uncontroverted evidence that the agency never assisted him to improve his performance, or even informed him of any deficiency, after it discovered his high error rate in grading examinations. We find that, because the agency did not provide the appellant an opportunity to improve his performance, the appellant's removal was a clearly excessive penalty. Demotion might have been a viable alternative, but since the appellant's high error rate in using a template scoring sheet indicates carelessness rather than inability on his part, we believe that the most appropriate penalty is a thirty (30) day suspension without pay to impress upon him the seriousness of his deficient performance.

On reconsideration of Parton at the behest of OPM, MSPB determined that some of the appellant's errors in Parton were the result of intent rather than carelessness. Removal was deemed appropriate. Parton v. FCC, 10 MSPR 330, 333, 9 MSPB 566 (1982), aff'd, Parton v. MSPB, 684 F.2d 530 (8th Cir. 1982); see Fairall v. VA, 33 MSPR 33, 46 (1987) (not all Douglas factors will apply to performance cases; when an absence of notice of performance deficiencies is raised as a mitigating factor, the Board will consider whether the employee received actual or constructive notice of performance deficiencies).

If an appellant defends a removal based on unacceptable performance in a job or training position, the appellant shows potential for rehabilitation by demonstrating an interest in learning job skills prior to, rather than after, the proposed removal so that the agency can assess that potential and incorporate it into its determination of whether to take adverse action and, if so, what action is appropriate. See Black v. Dept. of Navy, 23 MSPR 450, 453 (1984). A policy calling for retraining is not a "black letter rule that the agency is required to follow in all cases without regard to the circumstances of the particular situation before it." Brehmer v. FAA, 294 F.3d 1344, 1348 (Fed. Cir. 2002). An individual who suffers a severe demotion from a supervisory position for what amounts to performance-based reasons, involving no personal gain or malice, may succeed in convincing the Board that long service warrants mitigation to a less draconian demotion. See Botto v. USPS, 75 MSPR 471, 477 (1997) ("[W]hile the appellant may be unsuitable to perform at his current supervisory position, he is capable of performing satisfactorily as a lower-level supervisor or in a high-level non-supervisory position."), followed in Simpkins v. USPS, 79 MSPR 603, 607 ¶ 9 (1998), overruled in part, Doe v. USPS, 95 MSPR 493, 499-500 (2004) (Board accepted the demotion of a postal supervisor to a nonsupervisory position based on a finding of intentional failure to follow instructions concerning distribution of mail). But see Carter v. Dept. of Army, 23 MSPR 43, 44 n.1 (1984) (length of service was not a compelling mitigating factor when appellant "was significantly derelict in several areas and has shown no indication of potential rehabilitation"). The issue here is of unacceptable performance pursued as an adverse action. If unacceptable performance leads to action against an employee under Chapter 43, known as a performance action, there is no mitigation.
