broida mspb Chapter 11: Harmful Error Chapter 11: Harmful Error, Subchapter 03: Errors in Proposal Notice and Reply Process, Section G: Agency Decisional Procedures; Ex Parte Communications, Subsection 07: Information Pertaining to Aggravation or Mitigation of Penalty; Ward, Part a: Mitigating Information
a. Mitigating Information

Williams v. USPS, DE-0752-13-0322-I-1 (NP 4/8/2014), declared that Ward applies to consideration of new aggravating, not mitigating, factors:

The parties engaged in mediation pursuant to chapter 650 of the agency's Employee and Labor Relations Manual, but they were unable to resolve the matter. On May 15, 2013, the Senior Manager of Distribution Operations, J.M., issued a decision letter sustaining the charge, but mitigating the removal to a downgrade in craft. On appeal, the administrative judge found that the agency proved three of ten specifications of the charge. The appellant alleged as an affirmative defense that the agency denied him procedural due process because J.M. relied upon ex parte information developed during the mediation, at which J.M. was not present, when he decided to mitigate the penalty. Although the administrative judge could not determine the source of the information that J.M. received, the agency did not dispute that it failed to advise the appellant that statements he made during the mediation would be considered in the penalty determination. The administrative judge concluded that an ex parte communication had taken place. Because J.M. considered the communicated information as a mitigating factor, however, and not as an aggravating factor that enhanced the penalty, no due process violation occurred, "and thus the appellant was not prejudiced by its consideration."

On review, the appellant challenges only the findings regarding due process. The appellant argues that the information from the mediation session that J.M. received was new and material information received by ex parte communications as defined in Stone v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 179 F.3d 1368, 1376 (Fed. Cir. 1999). He argues that the analytical requirements set forth in Ward v. U.S. Postal Service, 634 F.3d 1274, 1279-80 (Fed. Cir. 2011) (applying the Stone factors to information introduced during penalty determinations), are not limited to the review of information supporting enhancement of the penalty and may include mitigating information. Ward, he argues, requires the agency to provide "a meaningful opportunity to address 'whether the level of penalty to be imposed is appropriate.'" There is no indication in Ward, he asserts, that ex parte communications are somehow permissible even if they are used as mitigating factors in penalty determinations. The appellant further argues that the agency's failure to notify him regarding its use of the information was prejudicial "because [he] was deprived of any meaningful opportunity to address whether the ultimate demotion, in light of the ex parte evidence considered, was appropriate." He argues that had he been able to respond, the agency might have imposed a less severe penalty or even allowed him to retain his supervisory position.

The appellant does not cite any authority supporting the specific proposition he advances, nor have we found any such authority. Ward explicitly states that an employee must receive "notice of any aggravating factors supporting an enhanced penalty" given to the deciding official. Ward, 634 F.3d at 1280 (emphasis added). The court referenced information "contributing to the enhancement of the penalty" more than once when explaining that there exists "no constitutionally relevant distinction between ex parte communications relating to the underlying charge and those relating to the penalty." Id. The Board has repeatedly cited Ward for the specific proposition that an employee must receive notice of aggravating or penalty-enhancing factors. See, e.g., Buelna v. Department of Homeland Security, 121 M.S.P.R. 262, ¶ 31 (2014); Hulett v. Department of the Navy, 120 M.S.P.R. 54, ¶ 11 (2013); Lopes v. Department of the Navy, 116 M.S.P.R. 470, ¶¶ 5-7 (2011). Ultimately, the Board's inquiry must be "whether the ex parte communication is so substantial and so likely to cause prejudice that no employee can fairly be required to be subjected to a deprivation of property under such circumstances." Stone, 179 F.3d at 1377. Although the agency failed to inform him of the communication, its omission was not by its very nature likely to cause prejudice against the appellant....

The Board discerned no improper ex parte consideration of information that led to mitigation of a penalty, Steele v. Dept. of Interior, SF-0752-12-0665-I-1 (NP 12/9/2013):

On review, the appellant disputes the administrative judge's finding that the agency did not deny the appellant due process because of ex parte communications between the deciding official and an Acting Deputy Ranger. The administrative judge found that, after the decision letter had been issued, the deciding official consulted the Acting Deputy Ranger to determine if the appellant was qualified for the GS-7 Dispatcher position that the agency had just decided to fill, and that, when the deciding official was advised that she was qualified, he amended his decision letter to state that she would be placed in that position, rather than the GS-5 position, as initially indicated. The administrative judge concluded that the deciding official was not provided with any new or material information relating to the appellant, the charge, or the penalty. The appellant asserts that the information provided by the Acting Deputy Ranger did relate to the penalty.

The information provided to the deciding official did technically relate to the penalty ultimately imposed upon the appellant, and, because she was not apprised of it, it was an ex parte communication. However, the communication, that influenced the deciding official's penalty determination did not contribute to the enhancement of the penalty. Ward v. U.S. Postal Service, 634 F.3d 1274, 1280 (Fed. Cir. 2011). On the contrary, it contributed to a lessening of the ultimate penalty, reducing the appellant in grade to GS-7 rather than to GS-5. We therefore agree with the administrative judge that the communication did not implicate due process because it was not so substantial or likely to cause prejudice that no employee could fairly be required to be subjected to a deprivation of property under such circumstances.

Similarly, consideration of mitigation information was not a due process violation in Backer v. Dept. of Interior, DE-0752-12-0315-I-1 (NP 7/1/2014):

The appellant further contends that the deciding official violated her due process rights by considering the appellant's performance beyond the date of the notice of proposed removal. As noted by the administrative judge, the deciding official testified that he only considered the appellant's post-notice of proposed removal performance as a possible mitigating factor that may have resulted in the appellant's retention in federal service. The deciding official testified that he did not consider the appellant's performance past the date of the notice of proposed removal as an aggravating factor. The appellant has presented no evidence or argument showing that the deciding official's consideration of such information was likely to result in undue pressure upon him to rule in a particular manner. The appellant, therefore, has failed to show that the deciding official's consideration of such information resulted in a due process violation under Ward. See Ward, 634 F.3d at 1280.

See Daellenbach v. VA, CH-0752-15-0318-I-2, n.7 (NP 10/19/2022) ("we note that favorable information about an employee is not the type of communication likely to result in undue pressure upon the deciding official to rule against the employee"); cf. ONeal v. Dept. of Air Force, AT-0752-15-0666-I-1 (NP 2/22/2023) ("The appellant, however, received the opportunity to examine the evidence the deciding official would consider and to present evidence of his own, including witness statements, and he failed to present such evidence. The fact that the deciding official drew adverse conclusions when the appellant did not present evidence to bolster his case for mitigation of the penalty does not support a finding that the agency violated his due process rights."); Savage v. Dept. of Army, AT-0752-11-0634-B-1 (NP 10/31/2022) ("the appellant has not identified any ex parte communication that was material to the deciding official's penalty selection. Rather, she alleges that the individuals with whom the deciding official conferred failed to communicate information concerning her whistleblowing activity. If so, it is not apparent how the alleged concealment of this information would implicate Ward or otherwise represent a due process violation.").
