f. Personal Delivery

If the appeal is filed through personal delivery, including a messenger service, it is filed when it is received at the Board's office. A good practice is to take along another copy of the appeal when filing at the regional or field office and to have the receptionist date and time stamp that copy, to be retained by the appellant or counsel as proof of filing.

Cohen v. Dept. of Commerce, 56 MSPR 578, 581, 581-82 (1993), determined that receipt at the regional office included delivery after normal business hours on the last day for filing:

The appellant contends on review that the administrative judge misinterpreted the Board's regulations by finding that her appeal was untimely filed because it was not delivered during the regional office's business hours. She argues that, while the Board's former regulations at 5 CFR 1201.22(d) (1988) specifically required that filing by personal delivery be made during normal business hours, the Board's current regulations impose no such requirement. She points out that the current regulations require only that "[f]iling must be made with the appropriate Board office by personal delivery, by facsimile, or by mail." 5 CFR 1201.22(d). She also points to the Board's current regulations at 5 CFR 1201.4(l), which provide that "[a] document that is filed with a Board office by personal delivery is considered filed on the date on which the Board office receives it." She contends that, because the Board's regulations no longer require filing during normal business hours, the date of receipt by the regional office was February 26, 1992, when, she claims, the appeal was delivered. We agree.

The current regulations at 5 CFR 1201.4(l) require only that a filing be made by the deadline date. The regulations do not require that filing be made within normal business hours. See id.; see also Jude v. Dept. of Treasury, 52 MSPR 5, 8 (1991) (even though the appellant's submission, which was filed by facsimile with the Washington, D.C., Regional Office on the deadline date, was filed after the close of business by that regional office, the submission was timely filed as evidenced by the date and time information automatically recorded on the facsimile's cover sheet by the receiving facsimile machine); Raphel v. Dept. of Army, 50 MSPR 614, 616-19 (1991) (a petition placed in the Postal Service mailstream on the deadline date is timely filed). Therefore, the administrative judge's finding here that the appellant's appeal was not filed because it did not come into the possession of a Board employee during regular business hours was error.

Further, we find that the appellant established below through her representative that her appeal was picked up and delivered to the regional office on February 26, 1992. In this regard, we note that the representative's statements that her messenger records showed that the messenger picked up the appeal from her office at 3:36 P.M. on February 26, 1992, and delivered it to the regional office at 5:50 P.M. are unrebutted. While the agency specifically denies that it has adopted the appellant's representative's account of the filing, it has submitted no evidence to the contrary.
