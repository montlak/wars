u. Awaiting Transcript, Other Document

The Board does not require that a party obtain a copy of the hearing transcript before it files a PFR, or that it cite specific pages of the transcript. Sadowski v. USPS, 28 MSPR 461, 462 n.* (1985). If one wants a transcript, the Board grants an extension request if the request is filed before the PFR deadline (because a stenographic firm does not timely prepare a transcript), and the request may be renewed if transcript preparation continues to be delayed for a short time. See Koury v. SBA, 40 MSPR 172, 175 (1989) (PFR was timely; additional time was allowed for supplemental documents based on the delayed receipt of a transcript from the former representative); Wenzel v. Dept. of Interior, 33 MSPR 344, 349 (1987) (granting one extension and denying another based on transcript delays and other problems). But lateness of a PFR caused by waiting for a transcript is an inadequate cause for delay. Steele v. USPS, 44 MSPR 327, 329 (1990); Murphy v. Dept. of Treasury, 91 MSPR 239, 244 ¶ 9 (2002) (once the Board stopped providing extensions to obtain hearing tapes, a late PFR was not excused for the failure to obtain tapes; a PFR need not include a copy of the hearing transcript); Green-Victory v. Dept. of Interior, 73 MSPR 272, 274 (1997) (good cause for a delayed PFR was not shown by an appellant who averred that she was waiting for the transcript to determine the conditions of her settlement agreement); cf. Harding v. VA, 115 MSPR 284, 289 ¶ 12, 2010 MSPB 241 (2010) ("Although the transcript of the statement made by the appellant's supervisor on March 8, 2010, was unavailable at the time the record closed, the information contained in the document (i.e., the supervisor's recollection of events that took place in 2008 and 2009) was available before the close of the record. We therefore find that the transcript is not new evidence satisfying the standard set forth at 5 C.F.R. § 1201.114(i), and we will not further consider the appellant's submission filed on May 5, 2010."), aff'd, Harding v. VA, 451 Fed. Appx. 947 (Fed. Cir. 2011 NP). [Refer to Chapter 3, "Hearing Recording Omissions; Transcript Errors."]

Although the Board may grant an extension to file a PFR while the transcript is being prepared, the Board is not likely to excuse a late PFR by reason of the interest in security a transcript by the party filing the PFR. See Cooper v. USPS, 70 MSPR 87, 90 n.2 (1996) (noting that a delay in the receipt of a transcript would not furnish good cause for the belated filing of a PFR because "the Board's regulations do not require a petition for review to be accompanied by a copy of the hearing transcript."); Berryman v. Dept. of Army, 42 MSPR 108 (1989) (good cause was not shown by an appellant who sought a transcript from the agency but who did not detail her efforts to obtain the transcript or provide any reason for failing to request an extension to file her response); Marie v. USPS, 28 MSPR 244 (1985) ("We find that appellant's decision to delay filing her petition for review because she wanted to forward a copy of the hearing transcript does not establish good cause for her untimely filing."); Vowell v. Dept. of Transp., 15 MSPR 646, 648 & n.2 (1983) (although the appellant argued that delay in filing the PFR was caused by difficulties obtaining hearing transcript, the appellant had not requested extension of time prior to the expiration of filing deadline). Nor is an untimely PFR excused by difficulty obtaining the transcript of the hearing if both the appellant and the representative were present at the hearing and can narrate in the PFR any specific testimony that the Board should examine. Blalock v. OPM, 22 MSPR 545, 547 (1984). A long-delayed PFR was not excused because the Board lost a tape of a prehearing conference. Wright v. Dept. of Air Force, 96 MSPR 1, 2 ¶ 6 (2003).

Although the Board may reopen a case to consider evidence that is material but could not have been discovered during the adjudication of the case before the regional office (a topic considered earlier in this chapter, "Review of New Evidence"), the need to obtain new documents to support a PFR will not serve as good cause to extend the filing period. Smith v. Dept. of Army, 27 MSPR 619, 621 (1985). A few days of delay by an individual awaiting some medical documentation warranted dismissal of a PFR of an appeal from OPM's termination of a disability retirement annuity. The appellant should have requested an extension from the AJ for time to obtain and submit the information. Snipes v. OPM, 32 MSPR 66, 67 (1987); see Raleigh v. VA, 110 MSPR 7, 10 ¶ 8, 2008 MSPB 207 (2008) ("a decision to delay filing in the hopes of procuring additional evidence does not provide good cause for the untimeliness of a petition"); Floyd v. OPM, 95 MSPR 260, 262 ¶ 6 (2003) ("the Board has held that delays caused by attempts to obtain additional evidence do not constitute good cause for waiving a filing deadline"); Salaz v. OPM, 91 MSPR 300, 303 ¶ 8 (2002) ("The appellant's delay in filing his petition to procure additional medical evidence of his alleged disabling condition does not warrant a waiver of the filing deadline."); Coles v. Dept. of Army, 85 MSPR 571, 574 ¶ 5 (2000) ("[N]either the appellant's inability to secure documentation to support her DOL claim or general personal difficulties constitute good cause for the untimely filing."); Burton v. Dept. of Army, 79 MSPR 136, 137 ¶ 5 (1998) ("[D]ifficulty contacting his former attorney and obtaining documents from the attorney regarding his appeal does not constitute a basis for waiving the [PFR] filing deadline."); Benroth v. Dept. of Army, 79 MSPR 15, 18 (1998) (PFR delay was not excused; the appellant did not explain why the PFR could not have been filed without the needed documents); Comer v. VA, 78 MSPR 633, 636 (1998) (declining to excuse a belated PFR accompanied by the documentation allegedly undermining the merits of the agency case; the case was not reopened: the appellant failed to show that the documentation could not have been discovered earlier); Noble v. USPS, 73 MSPR 59, 62-63 (1997) (delay of two days to obtain additional documentation was not excused and the PFR was untimely; the appellant could have contacted the Board for information on filing procedures if written procedures were unclear; the appellant did not request an extension of the filing deadline); Williams v. USPS, 71 MSPR 144, 147 (1996) (late PFR was not excused: the appellant failed to demonstrate the relevance of tapes when he first requested the tapes of the unemployment compensation hearing); Williamson v. VA, 61 MSPR 66, 68-69 (1994) (delay was not excused in seeking a review of a compliance initial decision on the basis of a delay in obtaining a copy of an official personnel file so that the appellant could determine whether the agency followed the proper procedures, established by settlement agreement, concerning documentation in that file); Gray v. USPS, 59 MSPR 142, 146 n.1 (1993) (agency's belated response to the PFR was not excused by a desire to present new and previously unavailable evidence; the agency should have requested an extension); Cababaro v. OPM, 57 MSPR 487, 489 (1993) (lack of documents reflecting service history was insufficient to excuse delay); Mendoza v. OPM, 53 MSPR 233, 236 (1992) (declining to excuse a belated PFR because the appellant could not locate civil service records that he asserted would establish title to an annuity); Baugh v. OPM, 49 MSPR 58 (1991) (reciting the rule, but the Board reopened the case on its own authority).

Other decisions conclude that a late PFR will not be excused because the filing party was awaiting documentation. See Zamot v. MSPB, 332 F.3d 1374, 1377 (Fed. Cir. 2003) ("The difficulty in obtaining evidence from the Navy may have been a circumstance out of Mr. Zamot's control, but the failure to file a document to serve as a petition for review was not."). Declining to excuse a considerably belated PFR, Linares-Rosado v. USPS, NY-3330-12-0223-I-1 (NP 4/14/2022):

...Indeed, he has not explained his attempts to obtain these documents or why he could not have submitted his petition for review without them. See Schuringa v. Department of the Treasury, 106 M.S.P.R. 1, ¶ 8 (2007); Benroth v. Department of the Army, 79 M.S.P.R. 15, ¶ 6 (1998). Of note, the record does not contain any discovery requests or motions seeking to compel discovery by the appellant. Moreover, the agency did respond with evidence and argument on the VEOA jurisdictional issue. Nonetheless, regardless of whether the appellant submitted any requests for evidence, he has not demonstrated how his alleged requests, or the absence of any responses, affected his ability to timely file his petition for review. See Benroth, 79 M.S.P.R. 15, ¶ 6 (finding no good cause when an appellant failed to explain why he could not have timely submitted a petition for review without certain documents). Even if the agency improperly failed to submit evidence and the administrative judge failed to respond to the appellant's requests, he has not explained how this prevented him from raising these issues following issuance of the initial decision in a timely petition for review....

OPM, 48 MSPR 535, 538-39 (1991) (inability to secure a complete medical report did not justify a filing delay; the appellant did not explain why she could not have requested an extension of the filing deadline or why her PFR could not have been submitted without the report); Kraning v. Dept. of Treasury, 43 MSPR 86, 88 (1989) ("Because the appellant's contention that the agency has interfered with his accumulating evidence is unsupported by any evidence, it does not stand as good cause for his untimely filing[.]"); Vitek v. Dept. of Navy, 40 MSPR 170, 171 (1989) (delay in obtaining paperwork from counsel did not excuse a late PFR); Wise v. Dept. of Treasury, 37 MSPR 314, 316-17 (1988) (PFR late by five days was not excused by the appellant's interest in securing a statement from his union in support of the PFR); Wilson v. OPM, 27 MSPR 628, 630 & n.2 (1985) (delay in the PFR to permit the appellant to secure power of attorney from her father was not excused when the document was not necessary and there was no explanation for the long delay in obtaining it); Fetter v. OPM, 27 MSPR 40, 41 (1985) (no excuse for a long-delayed PFR that appellant did not have "paper work" available); Stanford v. Dept. of Army, 19 MSPR 385, 386-87 (1984) (delay was not excused for the appellant to find an attorney and gather necessary information); Ferguson v. Dept. of Navy, 18 MSPR 689, 690 (1984) (the appellant's claim that he was waiting for a government document proving his veterans preference eligibility was no excuse for the year delay of a PFR); Washington v. GSA, 14 MSPR 173, 174 (1982) ("[A]ppellant has failed to show any difficulty in obtaining the submissions or that they were previously unavailable to him."); Sherwood v. DOJ, 46 MSPR 462, 464 (1990) (PFR delay was not excused because of the slow arrival of FOIA response; the information provided to the Board was not material to the issues on appeal); Webb v. USPS, 15 MSPR 522, 524 (1983) (delay was not excused because the appellant misplaced or lost some documents; a substantial delay occurred after the documents were again obtained); Sabornido v. OPM, 48 MSPR 66, 67 (1991) (misplacement of documents does not excuse the belated filing of a corrected PFR).

Since it appears that the appellant was diligent in gathering further statements in support of his petition but apparently was confronted in doing so with time delays occasioned by vacation and appointment schedules beyond his control, and since we can discern no prejudice resulting to the agency from the 28-day delay in filing, the Board finds good cause under the circumstances of the appellant's case to waive the regulatory deadline for filing a petition for review under 5 C.F.R.
