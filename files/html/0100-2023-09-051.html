broida mspb Chapter 02: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 02: Appeal and Discovery, Subchapter 01: Initiating an Appeal, Section E: Timeliness of Appeals, Subsection 01: Judge's Responsibility to Provide Notice Concerning a Timeliness Issue, Part a: Mental or Physical Illness; Lacy Notice
a. Mental or Physical Illness; Lacy Notice

Lacy v. Dept. of Navy, 78 MSPR 434, 437-38 (1998), remanded an appeal for further development by an AJ who failed to give to a pro se appellant with an apparent mental impairment precise information on how to demonstrate that his appellate deadline should be waived:

The Board will find good cause for waiver of its filing time limits where a party demonstrates that he suffered from an illness that affected his ability to file on time. See generally Hill v. Department of the Air Force, 54 MSPR 254, 256 (1992), aff'd, 991 F.2d 808 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table). To establish that an untimely filing was the result of an illness, the party must: (1) Identify the time period during which he suffered from the illness; (2) submit medical evidence showing that he suffered from the alleged illness during that time period; and (3) explain how the illness prevented him from timely filing his appeal or a request for an extension of time. See generally Andrews v. Department of the Army, 76 MSPR 147, 151 (1997).

Here, the appellant, who was pro se, alleged in his petition for appeal that it was untimely due to depression. Nevertheless, the administrative judge's acknowledgment order did not set forth the specific showing that an appellant must make if he alleges that an untimely filing is due to illness. The agency's motion to dismiss the appeal as untimely filed also discussed the Board's case law relating to timeliness only in general terms. Similarly, the initial decision failed to state the legal standard for waiver of an untimely filing when the alleged cause for the delay was illness. Thus, the appellant was never, at any stage of the Board's proceedings below, informed of the specific criteria he was required to meet to make a showing of good cause under such circumstances, even though he placed the administrative judge on notice that he was claiming depression as the cause of the delay.

When, as here, an appellant states that the reason for a filing delay is physical or mental illness, he must receive explicit information regarding the legal standard for establishing good cause on that basis, and he must be afforded a fair opportunity to submit evidence and argument to show that he met that standard.

In Hamilton v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 75 F.3d 639 (Fed. Cir. 1996), the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit construed an acknowledgment order identical to the one at issue here, finding that "[a]n appellant cannot be expected to fight a fog of generality," but must be given a "full and fair opportunity to litigate the [timeliness] issue." Id. The specific timeliness questions here and in Hamilton are different. Here, the administrative judge's acknowledgment order failed to inform the appellant of what he was required to show to establish good cause for an untimely filing on the basis of his alleged mental illness. In Hamilton, the acknowledgment order failed to advise the appellant of what she needed to submit to establish the date on which she received the agency's final decision on her formal complaint of discrimination. See Hamilton, 75 F.3d at 641, 646. The same principle that guided the court in Hamilton, however, applies with equal force in this case: an appellant should be told what the timeliness issue is, and what he must show to establish either that his appeal is timely, or that there is good cause for waiving the time limit. See also Pierce v. U.S. Postal Service, 60 MSPR 26, 30 (1993). Therefore, we find that the administrative judge should have advised the appellant of what he needed to submit to prove that a mental condition prevented him from timely filing his appeal.

In Williams v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 75 MSPR 144 (1997), the Board applied Hamilton, finding that the appellant should have received specific notice regarding the date on which the Board will presume receipt of a document that triggers the running of the filing period. Id. at 148. Similarly, in Williams v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 74 MSPR 472 (1997), the Board found that the appellant was unaware of the necessity of submitting evidence to show that her motion was timely filed, where neither the administrative judge's acknowledgment order, nor the agency's motion to dismiss, informed her of what was necessary to show that it was timely filed. Id. at 147; cf. Persons v. U.S. Postal Service, 75 MSPR 428, 432 (1997) (where the appellant filed an untimely appeal alleging involuntary retirement, the Board found that the appellant's burden of proof was adequately explained to him when the agency's motion to dismiss the appeal as untimely filed specifically stated that he had to show that "he put the agency on notice that he considered his retirement to be involuntary or that he otherwise lacked the capacity to make a voluntary decision and a timely filing"); Dotson v. U.S. Postal Service, 41 MSPR 412, 414-15 (1989), aff'd, 895 F.2d 1420 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (Table).

Moreover, it is well-settled that, for jurisdictional purposes, an order must provide the appellant with "explicit information" as to what is required to establish an appealable jurisdictional issue. See Burgess v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 758 F.2d 641, 643-44 (Fed. Cir. 1985). While timeliness and jurisdiction are different issues, the rules of fairness should apply to both inquiries.

We find that, because the appellant was never specifically informed of the three criteria he was required to meet to establish good cause for waiver of the Board's time limit on the basis of physical or mental illness, he was, in the words of the Federal Circuit, fighting a "fog of generality," and he was, therefore, denied a fair opportunity to address the timeliness issue. Accordingly, we find that the appeal must be remanded to provide the appellant with proper notice and a meaningful opportunity to file evidence and argument on the timeliness issue.

Lacy involved a mental condition that may have affected timeliness of the appeal. The rationale of the case applies to other illness. See, e.g., Vitale v. VA, 107 MSPR 501, 507-08 ¶¶ 14-15, 2007 MSPB 300 (2007) (diabetes). The Federal Circuit determined that the Board should apply to all pending cases the notice requirements imposed on AJs by Lacy when timeliness is an issue and mental or physical illness is the asserted reason for the tardy appeal. See Smith v. MSPB, 168 F.3d 1305 (Fed. Cir. 1999) (applying Lacy to a late PFR); Klages v. DOD, 80 MSPR 594, 596-97 ¶¶ 7-8 (1999) (applying Lacy retroactively to the belated appeal of an involuntary resignation).

Price v. Dept. of Navy, 101 MSPR 204, 206-07 ¶¶ 6-7, 2006 MSPB 33 (2006), summarizes application of Lacy to the assertion that an appeal was delayed because of medical problems:

Here, the appellant asserted below that he was "disabled" and undergoing "medical procedures." In addition, the agency removed the appellant for medical inability to perform the duties of his position, and there is some evidence in the record that the pro se appellant suffers from chronic pain and cognitive impairment. Under these circumstances, the appellant should have been provided with detailed and specific instructions concerning how to demonstrate that his medical condition constituted good cause for his apparent untimely filed appeal. See, e.g., Lacy v. Department of the Navy, 78 M.S.P.R. 434, 437-38 (1998). He should have been informed that, to establish that his apparent untimely filing was the result of an illness, he must: (1) identify the time period during which he suffered from the illness; (2) submit medical and/or corroborating evidence showing that he suffered from the alleged illness during that time period; and (3) explain how the illness prevented him from timely filing his appeal or a request for an extension of time. Chalom v. Department of the Navy, 86 M.S.P.R. 218, ¶ 5 (2000); Lacy, 78 M.S.P.R. at 437-38.

The AJ's show cause order, however, did not meet the requirements of Lacy. Thus, the appeal must be remanded for issuance of such a notice and an opportunity for the appellant to respond. See Santos v. Department of the Treasury, 94 M.S.P.R. 74, ¶ 7 (2003); Chalom, 86 M.S.P.R. 218, ¶ 6....

Colleran v. USPS, 112 MSPR 650, 655 ¶¶ 15-16, 2009 MSPB 225 (2009), also summarizes Lacy:

The Board will find good cause for an untimely filing when a party demonstrates that he was unable to timely file his petition due to illness. Lacy v. Department of the Navy, 78 M.S.P.R. 434, 437 (1998). Where the appellant states that the reason for a filing delay is illness, he must receive explicit information regarding the legal standard for establishing good cause on that basis. Id. at 438. He must specifically be informed that he must identify the time period during which he suffered from the illness, submit medical evidence showing that he suffered from the alleged illness during that time period, and explain how the illness prevented him from timely filing the appeal or requesting an extension of time. Id. at 437.

Although the AJ apprised the appellant of the general requirements to prove that he timely filed his appeal, he failed to provide the appellant with Lacy notice after the appellant alleged that the filing deadline should be waived due to mental incapacity. In the interest of justice and so the appellant is not "fighting a log of generality,'" his appeal should not be dismissed as untimely filed before he has a fair opportunity to address the timeliness issue. See Lacy, 78 M.S.P.R. at 439; McIver v. Office of Personnel Management, 70 M.S.P.R. 103, 106 (1996).

If the AJ fails to provide a Lacy-style notice to an appellant, the Board may decide to forego a remand to develop medical evidence and issue the notice to the appellant at the PFR stage. The Board then evaluates the evidence provided by the appellant as to timeliness. See Hawkins v. OPM, 96 MSPR 147, 151 ¶ 10 (2004).

"The broad purpose of the Lacy notice is to provide an appellant, especially one acting pro se, with detailed and specific instructions concerning how to demonstrate a relationship between illness and an untimely filing." Black v. DOJ, 87 MSPR 545, 549 ¶ 8 (2001). Typically, Lacy is applied when the appellant fails to respond, or responds inadequately, to a general show-cause order issued following what appears to be an untimely appeal. The Board may remand the case for issuance of a more specific show-cause order when the AJ has before her a response to the appeal from the agency suggesting a mental or physical condition that may have interfered with the appellant's timely filing. The AJ is responsible for reviewing the information in the agency response to ascertain whether a Lacy notice is warranted. See Moore v. OPM, 87 MSPR 104, 106 ¶ 6 (2000). The Board may remand when, with her PFR, the appellant first supplies information demonstrating that her condition may have hindered a timely appeal. Alternatively, after a PFR is filed, the Board may issue its own Lacy notice and itself evaluate the information supplied by the appellant. See Santos v. Dept. of Treasury, 94 MSPR 74, 76 ¶ 6 (2003) (appellant who stated in her PFR that she was suffering from hypertension, depression, and anxiety, making it difficult for her to understand procedures, was entitled to a Lacy notice to assist her in explaining the delayed filing of her appeal); Hanney v. DOD, 86 MSPR 420, 423 ¶ 7 (2000) (declining to waive a filing deadline for an appeal late by 37 days and lacking an explanation of how the appellant's medical conditions prevented his timely appeal).

When the AJ is presented with a claim that an appeal delayed because of mental or physical capacity, the AJ is to outline the specific applicable waiver requirements to the appellant, so that the appellant can intelligently respond to the assertion that his or her appeal is untimely. See Ferguson v. OPM, 88 MSPR 555, 558 ¶ 9 (2001) (statement in a letter accompanying the appeal that the appellant was very ill for two weeks should have led the AJ to issue a specific notice of Lacy factors rather than a general notice of the timeliness issue); Chalom v. Dept. of Navy, 86 MSPR 218, 220 ¶¶ 5-6 (2000) (general instructions by the AJ to the appellant concerning the necessity for supplying information on the timeliness of an appeal did not excuse the AJ from providing more explicit Lacy notice requirements when the appellant's initial response raised mental and physical illness as reasons for the untimely appeal); Stewart v. DOD, 84 MSPR 318, 321 ¶ 7 (1999) (appellant is entitled to notice of the requirements pertaining to a waiver of untimely filings for medical reasons); McKay v. USPS, 84 MSPR 152, 156 ¶ 9 (1999) (remanding for the development of further evidence concerning the effect of the appellant's brain injury on the ability to timely file an appeal); Leslie v. USPS, 83 MSPR 361, 364 ¶ 10 (1999) (AJ should have explicitly informed the appellant of requirements for establishing that an untimely filing resulted from illness: identify the time period she suffered from the illness; submit medical or corroborative evidence showing she suffered a specific illness during that time; and explain how the illness prevented her from timely filing her appeal or requesting an extension of time) (Leslie was overruled in Bradshaw v. VA, 113 MSPR 650, 654-56 ¶¶ 12-14, 2010 MSPB 97 (2010), on the matter of the appellant's duty to keep the agency informed of a change of address if there is a question of when an appeal should have been filed following receipt of an agency decision notice); Meyer v. USPS, 79 MSPR 667, 673-4 ¶ 14 (1998) (same; remanding for further evidence of proper notice of adverse action from the agency and due diligence by the appellant); Schorr v. Dept. of Navy, 79 MSPR 594, 599 ¶ 12 (1998) (employee who claims mental illness as the basis for a filing delay must be given a fair opportunity to submit evidence and argument showing that he met the standard); Cutrufello v. USPS, 79 MSPR 557, 562 ¶ 11 (1998) ("[W]hen a party claims that he was prevented from meeting a filing deadline because of illness, he must be specifically informed of the legal standard for establishing good cause, and he must be given a fair opportunity to submit evidence and argument relevant to that standard.").

Pro se status is significant when the Board assesses mental incapacity relative to a filing delay, but pro se status alone is not determinative. See Smith v. Dept. of Air Force, AT-0752-14-0339-I-1 (NP 2/10/2015) ("In Smith v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 84 M.S.P.R. 655 (2000), the Board qualified the rule that an appellant is responsible for the errors of his representative if an appellant makes specific and credible assertions that medical conditions prevented both him and his representative from filing an appeal on time. Here, the appellant has made no such assertion."); Smith v. VA, 84 MSPR 655, 658 ¶ 7 (2000) (remanding for the development of evidence concerning the appellant's mental incapacity: "While the instant appellant has not been entirely pro se, at the times he filed his first and refiled appeals he apparently was acting on his own or through his wife, who is not a professional representative."); cf. McIver v. OPM, 70 MSPR 103, 106 (1996) (remanding to the AJ for additional consideration of evidence of mental incapacity as to the untimely filing of an appeal; although the AJ advised the appellant of the burden of proof on timeliness, the AJ's order did not advise the appellant that to establish good cause, the appellant had to submit corroborating evidence and explain how his condition prevented him from timely filing).

In 2004 the Federal Circuit attempted to broaden or redefine Lacy to merge the analysis of the appellant's ability to file a timely appeal with the appellant's ability to file a request for an extension of time to file an appeal, Stout v. MSPB, 389 F.3d 1233, 1242 (Fed. Cir. 2004):

Further, given the facts of record here, we hold Stout's capacity to file an application for an enlargement of time to file his appeal stands or falls together with his capacity to file the appeal itself. At least on this record, any incapacity to do one establishes incapacity to do the other. If Stout's mental illness and physical condition rendered him unable to appreciate the process and deadline for appealing his removal, it rendered him equally unable to ask for an extension of that process and deadline; an applicant must comprehend a deadline in order to ask that it be extended or to request assistance in doing so. Thus, we reject the alternative reasoning of the Board that Stout failed to show he was unable to request an extension of time to file his appeal.

The standard cited by the AJ for showing good cause as a result of an illness was that set forth in Lacy v. Department of the Navy, 78 M.S.P.R. 434, 437 (1998), which requires, among other things, that a party "explain how the illness prevented him from timely filing his appeal or a request for an extension of time." (Emphasis added). An appellant's ability to request an extension of time to file his appeal, similar to his capacity to request assistance as discussed above, presumes cognizance of the filing deadline and method, and the mental wherewithal to file for an extension or to seek assistance in so doing. In addition, an appellant must understand that an extension may be available upon timely request. In cases alleging good cause due to mental incapacity, the ability to timely file an appeal and to file a request for an extension will often be coextensive; both presuppose cognition of one's state of affairs, potentially impaired by serious mental illness. The ability to perform these two actions--filing the appeal or requesting an extension to file by the thirty-day deadline--may prove more distinct in cases involving a physical, rather than mental, illness. For instance, an appellant recovering from a major surgery during the thirty-day appeal period, may have the physical capacity to draft a short request for an extension of time or ask another to do so yet, at the same time, lack the capacity to complete the more elaborate appeal form, or to request assistance in doing so, detailing specific grounds for challenging an agency's action. But in cases of mental illness, it is also possible that an appellant may have the capacity to file, or request assistance in filing, an extension request while lacking the capacity to file, or cooperate effectively with another in filing, the appeal itself. In either case, however, the requirement set forth in Lacy that an appellant explain how an illness prevented him from requesting an extension of time must necessarily turn on whether appellant knew of the filing deadline, knew that he could request an extension, and had the mental or physical capacity to do so. Absent such a three-part finding, an appellant should not be faulted for not seeking an extension to file his appeal. To the extent Lacy can be read to suggest otherwise, it is disapproved.

The problem with the Stout analysis is that the Board does not have a regulation providing for an extension of time for filing an appeal, with the possible exception of appeals that can be filed later than the normal time limit because the cases are the subject of mediation after the decision is issued and prior to the normal time for filing an appeal.
