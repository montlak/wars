1. Award Subject to Review

With the principal exception of appeals involving the Postal Service, the general rule is that the Board may review an arbitrator's decision only if an allegation of prohibited discrimination was raised in a case otherwise within its jurisdiction had the matter been originally appealed to the Board. Berry v. DOJ, 31 MSPR 676, 678 (1986); see Boyd v. USPS, DC-3443-11-0581-I-1 (NP 8/17/2012) ("to the extent that the appellant is appealing the arbitration award, the Board does not have jurisdiction over such a claim because 5 U.S.C. § 7121 does not apply to the Postal Service and, therefore, Postal Service employees do not have a right of Board review of an arbitration decision. See, e.g., Anderson v. U.S. Postal Service, 109 M.S.P.R. 558, ¶ 4 (2008)"); Morales v. DHHS, 67 MSPR 371, 374 (1995) ("The Board has jurisdiction to review an arbitrator's decision under 5 USC § 7121(d) when the subject matter of the grievance is one over which the Board has jurisdiction, the grievant alleges discrimination under 5 USC 2302(b)(1) [including discrimination on the basis of a disabling condition], and a final decision has been issued."). The Board lacks jurisdiction to review an arbitrator's award that remands an adverse action case back to the agency for reconsideration of the penalty when the arbitrator retains jurisdiction to review the agency's action on remand; the award is not final. Bettis v. Dept. of Labor, 112 MSPR 285, 287 ¶¶ 6-8, 2009 MSPB 183 (2009). Another exception to the Board's general jurisdiction to review arbitration awards involves appeals by employees to government corporations. Snead v. PBGC, 74 MSPR 501, 502-03 (1997) (claimed EEO reprisal; corporation excluded from coverage of 5 USC 2302(b)(1): "As a corporation owned by the Government of the United States, then, it is a 'Government corporation' for purposes of title 5 and is excluded from coverage under 5 U.S.C. § 2302 with the exception of subsection (b)(8). See 5 U.S.C. §§ 103, 2302(a)(2)(C)(i)."). The Board lacks jurisdiction to review a VA Title 38 medical professional's removal on appeal from an arbitrator's award, Alsdorf v. VA, CB-7121-13-0014-V-1 (NP 3/10/2014):

The key issue here is whether the Board has jurisdiction over the subject matter of the grievance, i.e., the appellant's removal. Under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(b)(10), the right to appeal an adverse action to the Board does not accrue to an individual "who holds a position within the Veterans Health Administration which has been excluded from the competitive service by or under a provision of title 38, unless such employee was appointed to such position under section 7401(3) of such title." See, e.g., Mfotchou v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 113 M.S.P.R. 317, ¶ 8 (2010). Thus, a nurse appointed under 38 U.S.C. § 7401(1) does not have the right to appeal 5 U.S.C. chapter 75 adverse actions to the Board. Evans v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 119 M.S.P.R. 257, ¶ 6 (2013).

The appellant argues that the Board has jurisdiction over his request for review because he could have appealed his removal to the Board under 5 U.S.C. § 7702. The agency asserts that it appointed the appellant to his CRNA position under the hiring authority in 38 U.S.C. § 7401(1) and that the Board therefore would have lacked jurisdiction over a direct appeal of his removal.

With its response to the request for review, the agency has submitted a copy of the Standard Form (SF) 50 documenting the appellant's removal, which shows that the appellant's position is excluded from the competitive service and that he was appointed to the position under 38 U.S.C. chapter 74. Although the SF-50 does not identify the statutory hiring authority for the appellant's appointment by section, the position of registered nurse is not listed under section 7401(3) of title 38, but rather under section 7401(1). See Falso v. Office of Personnel Management, 77 M.S.P.R. 207, 210 (1997) (registered nurses are among the Department of Veterans Affairs health-care professionals appointed under 38 U.S.C. § 7401(1)). Because the appellant held a position within the Veterans Health Administration as a registered nurse, he lacked the right to appeal the agency's removal action to the Board. Therefore, the Board does not have jurisdiction over the appellant's request for review.

The Board does not enforce arbitration awards. It reviews them, under limited circumstances, and only certain awards are subject to review, Fields v. USPS, PH-0752-09-0568-I-2 (NP 4/4/2011):

The administrative judge correctly found that the Board lacks jurisdiction to enforce the arbitration award as it is not an order or decision issued under the Board's jurisdiction. 5 U.S.C. § 1204(a)(2); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.181(a). The administrative judge also correctly found that Postal Service employees, unlike other employees, do not have a right to Board review of an arbitration decision. Anderson v. U.S. Postal Service, 109 M.S.P.R. 558, ¶ 4 (2008).

Assuming discrimination was properly raised, the Board reviews the case for violations of EEO law and for violation of civil service laws, rules, or regulation. See, e.g., Cosio v. DOJ, 65 MSPR 691, 695 (1994). Without an EEO allegation, the Board rejects the review request as beyond its jurisdiction. See Bass v. Dept. of Labor, CB-7121-11-0022-V-1 (NP 12/13/2011) ("Based upon our review of the record, the appellant has raised no claim of discrimination under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(1) in connection with the underlying action in his filing with the Board or before the arbitrator. Accordingly, in the absence of such a claim, the Board lacks jurisdiction over the appellant's request for review of the arbitration decision, and it must be dismissed. See American Federation of Government Employees, Local 1458 v. Department of Justice, 89 M.S.P.R. 120, ¶ 4 (2001)."); McClain v. VA, 58 MSPR 93, 95 (1993) ("The appellant has not raised an allegation of prohibited discrimination, either before the arbitrator or before the Board. Nor has he responded to the Clerk's show-cause orders with such an allegation."); cf. Miller v. SSA, CB-7121-15-0041-V-1 (NP 3/2/2016) (although the arbitrator imposed a time-served suspension, not ordinarily permissible under civil service law, the Board had no jurisdiction to correct the award because discrimination was not alleged to the arbitrator).

For the Board to review an arbitration award, the case must be one that would have been within the Board's appellate jurisdiction, Means v. Dept. of Labor, 61 MSPR 183, 186-87 (1994):

A federal employee who believes that he has been subjected to unlawful discrimination with respect to a matter that is covered by a negotiated grievance procedure "may raise the matter under a statutory procedure or the negotiated procedure, but not both." If such an employee selects the negotiated grievance procedure, however, he may request Board "review of the final decision pursuant to [5 USC] § 7702...in the case of any personnel action that could have been appealed to the Board."

An agency's decision not to promote an employee may not be appealed directly to the Board. Similarly, a performance evaluation may not be appealed directly to the Board. Finally, there is no statutory or regulatory authority for seeking Board relief from stress and pressure in the workplace. Thus, despite the fact that the appellant alleges that the agency discriminated against him on the basis of a disabling condition, he has no right of Board review of the arbitrator's decision because the three matters that he grieved could not have been appealed to the Board initially. Also, his allegation that the agency decided not to promote him because of his union activities provides no independent basis for Board jurisdiction.

Although an award sustaining a performance-based removal under Chapter 43 may be reviewed by the Board, Dobruck v. VA, 102 MSPR 578, 583 ¶ 10, 2006 MSPB 213 (2006), Lee v. Dept. of Labor, 110 MSPR 355, 2008 MSPB 252 (2008), an arbitration award on a performance appraisal is not appealable to the Board since the appellant could not have appealed the rating to the Board in the first place. See Ariza v. Dept. of Educ., 36 MSPR 54, 55 (1988) (citing Hardison v. Dept. of Army, 13 MSPR 175, 176 (1982)); cf. Doyle v. VA, 46 MSPR 615, 616 (1991) (no jurisdiction to review an arbitration award concerning a failure to promote); Perlmutter v. Dept. of Air Force, 41 MSPR 38, 39 (1989) (no jurisdiction to review an arbitration award involving a grievance over two performance ratings and a proposed notice of removal); Kirby v. USPS, 40 MSPR 229, 230 (1989) (grievance decision reviewing a seven-day suspension was not within MSPB jurisdiction). Conversely, if the agency action could have been originally reviewed by the Board, and goes to arbitration, it is reviewable by the Board if EEO challenges are made to the action. See Rupp v. DHHS, 51 MSPR 456, 462 (1991) (Board could review, following arbitration, a step increase denial and Chapter 43 removal for unsatisfactory performance). For the Board to consider an arbitration award, the employee must be subject to the personal jurisdiction of the Board. See Capriles v. Panama Canal Comm'n, 65 MSPR 221, 223-24 (1994) (employee was nonpreference eligible, an excepted service appointee of the Panama Canal Commission; the employee was expressly excluded from coverage under 5 USC 7511(b)(8)).

To be heard by the Board, the case must also have resulted in a final grievance decision by an arbitrator. The Board will not take jurisdiction over problems with or objections to a settlement reached in the grievance process but not resulting in an arbitration award. Even the filing of exceptions to an arbitration award with the FLRA appears to have the effect of precluding Board jurisdiction--at least until the exceptions are resolved. Mawson v. Dept. of Navy, 48 MSPR 318, 322 (1991); see Little v. Dept. of Treasury, 65 MSPR 360, 362 (1994) (no jurisdiction, despite EEO allegations, when a grievance protesting the denial of a step increase did not advance to arbitration but terminated at the fourth step of the grievance procedure); Leary v. Dept. of Navy, 60 MSPR 529, 531 (1994) (no jurisdiction when the appellant was not appealing a grievance decision but was appealing the grievance process); Garvin v. Dept. of Navy, 5 MSPR 372, 373-74 (1981) (declining jurisdiction when a group grievance was settled: "[T]he record reflects that the grievant did not exhaust his administrative remedies under the negotiated grievance procedure...a final decision within the meaning of Section 7121(d) was not rendered...grievant has failed to establish that the matter was initially appealable to the Board pursuant to 5 USC § 7702[.]"). The review process applies to grievances heard through collective bargaining grievance procedures; a decision on, or the implementation of, an agency grievance not involving arbitration is not subject to Board jurisdiction. See Loynachan v. VA, 14 MSPR 270, 271 (1982). If the arbitrator decides that there is no contract jurisdiction to consider a matter subject to a statutory appeal, the grievance is not arbitrable and, as a result, the appellant is not affected by a prohibited personnel practice falling "under the coverage of the [applicable] negotiated grievance procedure," a review criterion set by 5 USC 7121(d). The Board then lacks review jurisdiction over the resulting award. See Johnson v. Smithsonian Inst., 74 MSPR 94, 97 (1997). But see Perkins v. Dept. of Interior, CB-7121-12-0020-V-1 (NP 10/24/2013) (although the arbitrator determined that the grievant made an election of remedies to pursue her case through the EEO route, a jurisdictional impediment to arbitration, the arbitrator's ruling was not definitive and the arbitrator decided the grievance on the merits, establishing jurisdiction for the Board to review the resulting arbitration award).
