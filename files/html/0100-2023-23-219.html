broida mspb Chapter 16: Settlement, Remedies, and Enforcement Chapter 16: Settlement, Remedies, and Enforcement, Subchapter 03: Enforcement, Section B: Enforcement and Interpretation of Settlement Agreements, Subsection 11: Specific Settlement Provisions, Part k: Waiver of Protection of Confidentiality or Affirmative Reference Provision
k. Waiver of Protection of Confidentiality or Affirmative Reference Provision

Waiver of nondisclosure protections contained in a settlement was addressed in Lowe v. VA, 85 MSPR 219, 224-25 ¶¶ 11-12 (2000):

Assuming without deciding that the provision of the agreement pertaining to discussions with prospective employers prohibited the agency from sending a copy of the appellant's OPF to Ms. Denham, the issue is whether the release signed by the appellant waived the agency's obligations. A settlement agreement is a contract, the interpretation of which is a question of law. Greco v. Department of the Army, 852 F.2d 558, 560 (Fed. Cir. 1988). A waiver in contract law is the intentional relinquishment of a known contractual right or advantage, which, but for the such waiver, the party would have enjoyed. Hosey v. Interstate Commerce Commission, 74 MSPR 605, 609 (1997), aff'd, 132 F.3d 53 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (Table). A party may waive any provision of a contract intended for his benefit. Millmaster Intl, Inc. v. United States, 57 C.C.P.A. 108, 427 F.2d 811, 814 (CCPA 1970), modified in part on other grounds, Millmaster Int'l Inc. v. United States, 57 C.C.P.A. 108, 429 F.2d 985 (CCPA 1970). A waiver requires: 1) the existence at the time of the waiver of a right, privilege, advantage, or benefit that may be waived; 2) the actual or constructive knowledge thereof; and 3) an intention to relinquish such right, privilege, advantage, or benefit. Hosey, 74 MSPR at 609.

The appellant possessed a waivable right under the agreement: a right to limit the agency's discussion of the circumstances of her resignation to prospective employers. The appellant certainly knew of this right because she signed the settlement agreement. Further, the appellant showed her intent to relinquish this right by signing a "Release of Employment Information" which authorized "all of my past and current employers and all of my prior or current employees to release any and all information of any nature regarding that employment with them, including, but not limited to, my...work history, disciplinary records, and any records relating to loss of time from work, to J. Denham, Attorney at Law, or her representative." Ms. Denham was not, as the appellant herself agrees, a prospective employer. Thus, the authorization to release records to Ms. Denham modified the settlement agreement by waiving the requirement in paragraph 5(f) of the agreement limiting discussion of the circumstances of the appellant's resignation to prospective employers....

See Del Balzo v. Dept. of Interior, 60 MSPR 659 (1994) (addressing a limited release signed by the appellant authorizing disclosure of information to a prospective employer; agency should have provided, under the release, only information described in the agreement); Wexler v. Dept. of Interior, 48 MSPR 513, 518 (1991) (agreement contained clause waiving nondisclosure provision to the extent that disclosure was required to implement any part of agreement).

Hosey v. ICC, 74 MSPR 605, 607-11 (1997), made clear that an employee who settles for a neutral reference can waive the limitations of the settlement agreement, if the waiver is phrased with reasonable clarity:

The agreement...provided:

[T]he agency will designate a contact position in its Office of Personnel Services for employment references sought on behalf of Mr. Hosey. The person in that position will provide the following information only:

(a) Positions and grades held by Mr. Hosey;

(b) Dates of employment at the agency;

(c) The fact that Mr. Hosey was separated in October, 1994, by reason of a [RIF], which eliminated his position; and

(d) That Mr. Hosey's last rating of record was summary "commendable."

It is undisputed that the agency purged the appellant's OPF of the performance appraisal and other documents as required by the settlement agreement.... According to the agency, the only other record it maintained concerning the appellant was the litigation file of its representative, which was not so purged.

According to the appellant, he was selected for a position at another agency subject to his obtaining a "Top Secret" security clearance. An OPM investigator assigned to conduct a background investigation for the security clearance contacted the agency's Personnel Services Director, the employee designated by the agency to provide an employment reference concerning the appellant. The Personnel Services Director told the OPM investigator the information detailed in the settlement agreement, and allowed the investigator to review the appellant's OPF. The Personnel Services Director did not provide any other information or records to the investigator or permit the investigator to interview other agency employees concerning the appellant. Following persistent attempts to elicit further information, the investigator was referred to the agency representative, who informed the investigator that the agency would only provide further information or permit interviews with other employees if it received a signed, written authorization from the appellant.

Two weeks later, the OPM investigator presented to the agency a "Specific Release" form signed by the appellant which authorized the investigator to obtain from the agency

(ICC) permission to interview all previous supervisors and coworkers. Records included.

The agency allowed the investigator to interview the appellant's former supervisors and co-workers and to inspect the agency's litigation file.

...

This case turns upon whether the specific release signed by the appellant waived the agency's obligations under the neutral reference provision of the settlement agreement. A settlement agreement is a contract, the interpretation of which is a question of law. Greco v. Department of the Army, 852 F.2d 558, 560 (Fed. Cir. 1988). In contract law, a waiver is the intentional abandonment or relinquishment of a known contractual right or advantage which, but for such waiver, the party would have enjoyed.... Waiver requires: (1) the existence at the time of the waiver of a right, privilege, advantage, or benefit that may be waived; (2) the actual or constructive knowledge thereof; and (3) an intention to relinquish such right, privilege, advantage, or benefit....

We find that these requirements are met here. The appellant possessed a waivable right or benefit under the agreement: a right to a neutral reference consisting of only specified information from a specified individual, and to an OPF cleaned of all evidence of the challenged performance appraisal. The appellant knew of this right; he signed the settlement agreement. Moreover, the OPM investigator informed him that the agency refused to provide information outside of that specified in the agreement, i.e., information concerning his character or suitability for employment. The appellant intended to relinquish his right to the neutral reference set forth in the agreement. The specific release expresses this intent. So does his statement that he signed the release because the OPM investigator told him that he would not obtain a security clearance if he did not.

The scope of the appellant's waiver of the neutral reference provision of the settlement agreement depends upon the language of the specific release. The specific release stated:

(ICC) permission to interview all previous supervisors and coworkers. Records included.

We find that this language clearly and unambiguously instructs the agency to permit the OPM investigator to interview all of the appellant's former supervisors and coworkers, and to inspect all of the agency's records concerning the appellant. Thus, we find that the agency's actions in accordance with the appellant's waiver do not constitute a breach of the settlement agreement.

We agree with the agency that Del Balzo v. Department of the Interior, 60 MSPR 659 (1994), is distinguishable from this case. In Del Balzo, the parties entered into a settlement agreement with a neutral reference provision similar to the one in this case. Mr. Del Balzo was offered a position at another agency contingent on a suitability inquiry. He signed a general release form authorizing his former agency to provide to the new agency information regarding his "achievement, performance,...personal history, [and] disciplinary...records." Id. at 661. Upon receiving the release, the former employing agency released information outside that described in the settlement agreement, and the new agency withdrew its offer. The Board held that the agency breached the settlement agreement. The Board determined that the general release form merely authorized the agency to disclose the information described in the settlement agreement. Id. at 663. The agreement did not prohibit the disclosure of the information identified in the release, it merely prescribed the method for such disclosure. Id. Thus, the release did not modify the terms of the settlement agreement. Id. The Board stated that construing such a general authorization to seek an employment reference as a waiver of the neutral reference provision would be inconsistent with the purpose of the settlement agreement. Id. at 663-64.

In contrast to Del Balzo, the neutral reference provision in this case prohibited the disclosure of the information identified in the specific release. Under the neutral reference provision, all employment inquiries would be answered by a specific agency employee who would provide specific information. The specific release, however, permitted the OPM investigator to interview other agency employees and to inspect agency records concerning the appellant. Thus, the specific release modified the settlement agreement by waiving the neutral reference requirement.

In Soler v. Dept. of Treasury, 167 Fed. Appx. 188, 189 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP), a settlement called for nondisclosure of information concerning the removal that led to the MSPB appeal and settlement. But the protection against nondisclosure was waived, based on the employee's signature of an SF-86 form that provided:

I Authorize custodians of such records and other sources of information pertaining to me to release such information upon request of the investigator, special agent, or other duly accredited representative of any Federal agency authorized above regardless of any previous agreement to the contrary.

Similarly, from Torres v. DHS, DA-0752-07-0066-C-2 (NP 4/4/2022):

...the administrative judge also found that, even if the appellant established that CBP provided the information regarding his 2006 removal to ICE, his e-QIP waiver released CBP from its nondisclosure obligation. The appellant challenges this finding on review, arguing that he would not have signed the e-QIP waiver if he had known that the agency would "enhance" his personnel file and fail to provide him a "clean paper" record pursuant to the settlement agreement. However, the appellant has not provided any evidence on which to disturb the administrative judge's alternate finding that the appellant executed a valid and enforceable waiver releasing CBP from its nondisclosure obligation. U.S. Postal Service, 111 M.S.P.R. 551, ¶¶ 9-10 (2009) (finding that an appellant's unilateral mistake as to the scope of a waiver of rights in a settlement agreement did not provide a basis for finding the waiver unenforceable), aff'd per curiam, 367 F. App'x 137 (Fed. Cir. 2010).
