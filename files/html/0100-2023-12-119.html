1. Factual Findings; "Special Deference"

The Board, like any other agency, is entitled to substitute its judgment for that of its hearing examiners. McDonough v. USPS, 666 F.2d 647 (1st Cir. 1981). The Board reserves to itself the authority to make decisions on cases, giving only the weight it decides is due to initial decisions. An AJ acts as the Board's agent and has no independent statutory jurisdiction; however, the Board respects the AJ's opportunity to observe the demeanor of those who testify. Billingsley v. USPS, 4 MSPR 368, 370, 4 MSPB 428 (1980). The Board may decide to make factual findings itself based on the record, when the AJ has not done so, and when the findings do not require determinations based upon the demeanor (credibility) of witnesses. See Negron v. DOJ, 95 MSPR 561, 565 ¶ 9 (2004). Whoever finds the facts, if charges sustained by the agency and the AJ are not supported by preponderant record evidence, the Board will not sustain the action. Brooks v. FAA, 18 MSPR 178, 181 (1983).

When it reviews an initial decision, "the Board may freely substitute its own determinations of fact for those of the presiding official, giving the presiding official's findings only as much weight as may be warranted by the record and by the strength of the presiding official's reasoning," Weaver v. Dept. of Navy, 2 MSPR 129, 132-34, 2 MSPB 297 (1980):

In arguing that the presiding official incorrectly interpreted the evidence, appellant has merely stated his disagreement with the presiding official's conclusion. Although stating that the testimony is in conflict, and citing his own testimony as evidence of that conflict, appellant has pointed to nothing in the record that could explain why his testimony should be assessed as more credible than that of his supervisor. Apart from the bias allegation discussed below, appellant has identified no evidence and given the Board no reason tending to show that the presiding official's credibility determination was in error.

Under 5 U.S.C. § 7701, the Board's presiding officials act as the Board's agents under delegated authority, issuing "initial" decisions which become final between the parties only if the Board itself does not review or reopen those decisions. The presiding officials have not independent statutory jurisdiction. Under this statutory scheme, it would not be appropriate for the Board to apply customary standards of appellate review, such as "clearly erroneous" or "arbitrary or capricious," to the findings of fact set forth in the initial decisions of its own employees. Compare FCC v. Allentown Broadcasting Corp., 349 U.S. 358, 363-365 (1955); Universal Camera Corp. v. NLRB, 340 U.S. 474, 492-493 (1951). Consequently, in reviewing an initial decision, this Board is free to substitute its own initial decision, this Board is free to substitute its own determinations of fact for those of the presiding official, giving the presiding official's findings only so much weight as may be warranted by the record and by the strength of the presiding official's reasoning.

In undertaking such reviews, however, the Board must recognize that demeanor evidence cannot by its nature be reflected fully in the written transcript, with the result that the Board must depend heavily upon the presiding official's assessment of witness credibility, at least when there are not other, perhaps more persuasive, indicia of credibility included in the record. For this reason, when questions of credibility are presented, due deference must necessarily be given to the assessment of the presiding official who was present to hear and observe the demeanor of the witnesses. Universal Camera, supra, 340 U.S. at 496.

Such Board review of the presiding official's fact finding will ordinarily be undertaken only when the petitioning party makes a showing consistent with 5 C.F.R. § 1201.115(b), which requires a demonstration that the initial decision is based on an erroneous interpretation (or application) of the statutory requirements governing the weight of the evidence. While the language of the petition may be simply drawn, there must be at least enough specified in the petition to enable the Board to ascertain whether there may be a serious evidentiary question justifying a complete review of the record. A mere conclusory allegation of factual error cannot be sufficient to trigger such a review, in view of Congress' clear desire to restrict the former multi-level appeals process. Before the Board will undertake the burden of a complete review of the record, the petitioning party must, as the regulation plainly states, explain in the petition why the challenged factual determination is incorrect, and identify the specific evidence in the record which demonstrates the error.

When the alleged factual error relates to a credibility determination, as in this case, the petition for review must contain some specification of evidence or reasons warranting a review of the presiding official's credibility finding. But here the petitioner has not identified any internal inconsistencies or inherent improbability in his supervisor's testimony, or any contradiction of that testimony by independent witnesses or independently corroborated evidence, or any reliance by the presiding official on hearsay lacking in probative value, unsubstantiated rumor, surmise, conjecture or speculation. Nor has the petitioner identified anything in the record that could arguably justify attributing greater credibility to his own testimony, or identified any error in the reasoning on which the presiding official predicated his credibility finding, or referred to anything else in the record that suggests the presiding official's credibility assessment was erroneous. The presiding official devoted a substantial portion of the initial decision to his findings on the credibility question, which were clearly made with care.

In view of the deference due the presiding official's opportunity to observe the demeanor and hear the testimony of the witnesses, and the complete lack of anything in the record that appellant had referred us to or any other reasons advanced by appellant that could warrant reversal of the presiding official's determination, we find no basis for granting the petition to review that determination.

The Board described the process slightly differently in Dogar v. DOD, 95 MSPR 52, 58-59 ¶ 14 (2003): "in reviewing an initial decision we are free to substitute our own determinations of fact for those of the administrative judge, even when his credibility findings are based in part on demeanor evidence, if we can articulate a sound reason, based on the record, for a contrary evaluation of the evidence." The review standard was restated, slightly differently, in Richardson v. USPS, 96 MSPR 649, 651 ¶ 8 (2004): "[a]n administrative judge's finding of fact will not be disturbed unless a serious evidentiary question is raised." Moore v. EEOC, 97 MSPR 684, 690 ¶ 12 (2004), concluded that the Board may overturn demeanor-based credibility determinations if the AJ's findings are incomplete, inconsistent with the weight of the evidence, and do not reflect the record as a whole. See Smith v. VA, 93 MSPR 424, 430 ¶ 22 (2003) (citing Haebe v. DOJ, 288 F.3d 1288, 1301 (Fed. Cir. 2002), for the proposition that the Board "may overturn an administrative judge's demeanor-based findings if the Board's reasons for doing so are 'sufficiently sound'").

A summary of the Board's authority to overturn an AJ's credibility findings is taken from Velez v. DHS, 101 MSPR 650, 659 ¶ 17, 2006 MSPB 116 (2006):

...To resolve credibility issues, the trier of fact identifies the factual questions in dispute, summarizes the evidence on each disputed question, states which version he believes, and explains in detail why he found the chosen version more credible, considering such factors as: (1) The witness's opportunity and capacity to observe the event or act in question; (2) the witness's character; (3) any prior inconsistent statement by the witness; (4) a witness's bias, or lack of bias; (5) the contradiction of the witness's version of events by other evidence or its consistency with other evidence; (6) the inherent improbability of the witness's version of events; and (7) the witness's demeanor. Faucher v. Department of the Air Force, 96 M.S.P.R. 203, ¶ 8 (2004); Hillen v. Department of the Army, 35 M.S.P.R. 453, 458 (1987). The Board must give deference to an AJ's credibility determinations when, as here, they are based on the demeanor of the witnesses testifying at a hearing. Haebe v. Department of Justice, 288 F.3d 1288, 1301-02 (Fed. Cir. 2002); Moore v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 97 M.S.P.R. 684, ¶ 12 (2004); Faucher, 96 M.S.P.R. 203, ¶ 8. However, the Board may overturn an AJ's demeanor-based credibility findings when it has sufficiently sound reasons for doing so, such as when the AJ's credibility findings are incomplete, inconsistent with the weight of the evidence, and they do not reflect the record as a whole. Id.

The reliance on the AJ's findings of credibility assumes that findings are made. The Board will set aside factual findings if the AJ does not supply intermediate reasoning explaining credibility resolutions. If a witness is to be found incredible, reasons must be stated if the Board is to be prevented from substituting its own factfinding. See McInville v. USPS, 31 MSPR 297, 301 (1986).

SSA v. Long, 113 MSPR 190, 201-02 ¶ 25, 2010 MSPB 19 (2010), summarized the Board's approach to review of credibility findings:

The Board is not "free to overturn an administrative judge's demeanor based credibility findings merely because it disagrees with those findings." Leatherbury v. Department of the Army, 524 F.3d 1293, 1304 (Fed. Cir. 2008). Rather, when the findings are explicitly or implicitly based on demeanor, the Board may make determinations of fact different from those of the judge only where it "articulate[s] sound reasons, based on the record, for its contrary evaluation of the testimonial evidence." Haebe v. Department of Justice, 288 F.3d 1288, 1300 (Fed. Cir. 2002). An administrative judge's findings are due only as much weight as the evidence of record and the strength of the judge's reasoning require. Christopher v. Department of the Army, 107 M.S.P.R. 580, ¶ 16, aff'd, 299 Fed. Appx. 964 (Fed. Cir. 2008). Moreover, where, as here, the record is sufficiently developed and we do not rely upon witness demeanor, it is not necessary to remand the case to the judge, and we may adjudicate the case on the record. Aldridge v. Department of Agriculture, 111 M.S.P.R. 670, ¶ 11 (2009); Dogar v. Department of Defense, 95 M.S.P.R. 527, ¶ 4 (2004), aff'd, 128 Fed. Appx. 156 (Fed. Cir. 2005).

Setting aside factual findings in Special Counsel v. Lee, 114 MSPR 57, 66-67 ¶¶ 22-23, 2010 MSPB 89 (2010) (rev'd, sub nom Beatrez v. MSPB, 413 Fed. Appx. 298 (Fed. Cir. 2011 NP), post), the Board said of the AJ's credibility determinations:

The administrative law judge's analysis of intent here rests largely on credibility determinations made from the testimony of the respondents and their managers. Normally, the Board must defer to the credibility determinations of an administrative judge when they are based, explicitly or implicitly, upon the observation of the demeanor of witnesses testifying at a hearing, because the administrative judge was in the best position to observe the demeanor of the witnesses and determine which witnesses were testifying credibly. Haebe v. Department of Justice, 288 F.3d 1288, 1301 (Fed. Cir. 2002); Smith v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 93 M.S.P.R. 424, ¶ 4 (2003). Conversely, the Board may substitute its own determinations of fact for those of an administrative judge, even where his credibility findings are based in part on demeanor evidence, if the Board can articulate a sound reason, based on the record, for a contrary evaluation of the evidence. Haebe, 288 F.3d at 1300; Dogar v. Department of Defense, 95 M.S.P.R. 527, ¶ 4 (2004) ("Although we seldom exercise this prerogative, we could not ignore in the case before us the serious inconsistencies between the appellant's account and the documentary evidence, including the findings of the agency's extensive investigation."), aff'd, 128 Fed. Appx. 156 (Fed. Cir. 2005). The Board may overturn credibility determinations when the findings are incomplete, inconsistent with the weight of the evidence, and do not reflect the record as a whole. Faucher v. Department of the Air Force, 96 M.S.P.R. 203, ¶ 8 (2004); see, e.g., Wallace v. Department of Commerce, 106 M.S.P.R. 23, ¶¶ 14-16 (2007) (finding that the administrative judge failed to consider conflicting evidence); Velez v. Department of Homeland Security, 101 M.S.P.R. 650, ¶ 18 (2006) (finding that the appellant's purported exculpatory explanation was not only contradicted by the undisputed evidence, it was inconsistent with the appellant's and a subordinate employee's previous sworn statements, and it was also inconsistent with the record evidence as a whole), aff'd, 219 Fed. Appx. 990 (Fed. Cir. 2007); Moore v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 97 M.S.P.R. 684, ¶ 12 (2004) (rejecting the administrative judge's credibility determination in favor of the testimony of one agency witness, where the testimony conflicted with sworn statements by three impartial witnesses supporting the appellant's account of events).

Here, the administrative law judge did not ignore the evidence that would support a finding of preferential treatment, and indeed he acknowledged several times that some of their actions might imply or create the appearance of wrongdoing. His findings and credibility determinations are nevertheless inconsistent with the weight of the documentary evidence and do not reflect the record as a whole. It is our view that the administrative law judge gave a sizable body of particularly telling circumstantial evidence too little weight in favor of some direct testimony that was inconsistent with that body of evidence. As a result, he explained away serious contradictions between the testimony and the other less favorable evidence in the record, crafting an improbable account of the events leading up to Woodson's appointment to the vacant position. See Hillen v. Department of the Army, 35 M.S.P.R. 453, 458 (1987) (factors for assessment of credibility include the contradiction of the witness's version of events by other evidence or its consistency with other evidence, and the inherent improbability of the witness's version of events).

Lee involved two respondents, the other being Diane Beatrez, who took the case to the Federal Circuit. That court reversed the Board's decision because the Board improperly overturned the AJ's findings concerning the appellant's intent, Beatrez v. MSPB, 413 Fed. Appx. 298, 304-06 (Fed. Cir. 2011 NP):

Ms. Beatrez raises several arguments on appeal. We find it necessary to address only one of them: her contention that the Board erred in overturning the administrative judge's findings with respect to her intent. She argues that her actions were based on her belief that there was a legitimate management interest in expanding the announcement so that well-qualified merit promotion candidates at the place where the vacancy occurred could be considered. She argues that the Board erred because at worst, the evidence was as consistent with her having acted with an innocent intent as it was with her not having done so.

Both the administrative judge and the Board recognized that this case turns on intent. The administrative judge found that Ms. Beatrez and Mr. Lee did not intend to violate § 2302(b)(6) by granting an illegal preference to Mr. Woodson. This finding was supported by the testimony of Ms. Beatrez, CMDR Hogan, Ms. NelsonPottinger, and Mr. Kogut, all of whom the administrative judge found credible. Noting that the agency may rely on circumstantial evidence to establish intent, the Board took the position that the HR specialists' culpability was undermined by CMDR O'Hare's emails and by what it concluded Ms. Beatrez and Mr. Lee knew about CMDR O'Hare's wishes. According to the Board, the administrative judge failed to take these considerations into account.

The Board's reasons for substituting its own credibility determinations for that of the administrative judge are not sufficiently sound to justify overturning the administrative judge's finding as to Ms. Beatrez's intent. First, we note that while the Board's opinion addresses both HR specialists' culpability, the bulk of the considerations it points to for overturning the administrative judge pertain to Mr. Lee rather than Ms. Beatrez. For example, the Board stated that both Mr. Lee and Ms. Beatrez advised CMDR O'Hare regarding the language of the final vacancy announcement. The testimony does not support this assertion. Rather, it was Mr. Lee alone who emailed CMDR O'Hare and recommend cancelling the second set of vacancy announcements and advertising the position as a GS-09 with potential to GS-11. After receiving Mr. Lee's email, CMDR O'Hare emailed Mr. Lee and directed him to re-announce the position as a "GS-9/11 to expand the pool of qualified applicants with specific licensing experience." She also asked him to limit the announcement to the local commuting area. Mr. Lee passed this information to Ms. Beatrez, stating that she should re-announce the position as a GS-09/11 merit promotion vacancy limited to the Los Angeles commuting area. There is nothing in either the administrative judge's or the Board's decision indicating that Ms. Beatrez actually advised CMDR O'Hare regarding the language of the final vacancy announcement.[1]

[1] Thus, Ms. Beatrez did not advise regarding the language of any of the vacancy announcements. The first and the second set of announcements were issued before the staffing assignment for the action was transferred to Ms. Beatrez.

The Board further points to a "fairly significant paper trail of email messages" that "clearly document CMDR O'Hare's desire to select Woodson." The Board concluded that the HR specialists intentionally facilitated CMDR O'Hare's blatant attempt to violate § 2302(b)(6). The examples discussed in the Board opinion demonstrating CMDR O'Hare's blatant intent, however, are all emails between CMDR O'Hare and Mr. Lee. There is nothing in either the administrative judge's or the Board's decision indicating that Ms. Beatrez, who was located in Washington, D.C., had any direct contact with CMDR O'Hare. On the other hand, the testimony supports that Mr. Lee and CMDR O'Hare had frequent face-to-face conversations as well as email discussions at every stage of the process. In contrast, all of Ms. Beatrez's limited dealings were all with Mr. Lee.

Beyond the emails discussing CMDR O'Hare's desire to be able to consider Mr. Woodson and the advice provided regarding the vacancy announcements, the Board points to the "carefully tailored nature" of the third and final vacancy announcement. While the announcement expanded the experience level for potential applicants by advertising the open position at both the GS-09 and GS-11 levels, it limited consideration of applicants to the local commuting area. The Board expressed concern that such specifications precluded candidates from other Coast Guard REC facilities even though they were a precise match for the position in terms of their experience. A geographic limitation, however, is not inherently incompatible with an intent to expand the pool of well-qualified internal candidates. This court has previously concluded that 5 C.F.R. § 335.103(b)(2) provides that a vacancy announcement can be geographically limited so long as the area of consideration is sufficiently broad to ensure the availability of high quality candidates. The administrative judge recognized that it was entirely appropriate to use an announcement that would give the agency's internal candidates an opportunity to be considered for the position. Had the agency used this approach at the outset, the announcement would have been proper. It is only through the backdrop of the repeated postings and repostings that the announcements may have appeared at all suspicious--but Ms. Beatrez was not involved with those earlier postings. Her decision to issue the third vacancy announcement is consistent with her expressed intent to expand the announcement so that well-qualified merit promotion candidates at the place where the vacancy occurred could be considered. Ultimately, candidates from all three sets of announcements were considered.

Finally, to the extent that the Board is relying on Ms. Beatrez's decision to cancel the second set of vacancy announcements as circumstantial evidence of improper intent, Ms. Beatrez credibly explained: "I saw that there were just a limited number of applicants, most of which were applicants under the [DEU] or open competitive announcement, I made an assessment that the position was advertised in error so I cancelled that." It is undisputed that there were deficiencies in the first two sets of vacancy announcements and that re-announcing positions under such circumstances was a routine practice. The Board, in considering appropriate penalties, acknowledged this practice. It cited to Mr. Kogut's testimony stating that the Coast Guard frequently "reannounce[s] jobs to get the best quality candidates.... [Beatrez] would have seen this as something that was proper, appropriate versus inappropriate and wrong."

In sum, most of the evidence discussed by the Board goes to CMDR O'Hare's improper intent and Mr. Lee's understanding of that intent. Much of the remaining evidence is at least as consistent with Ms. Beatrez having acted with an innocent intent as it was with her having intended to aid and abet CMDR O'Hare grant an unauthorized preference to Mr. Woodson. Admittedly, Ms. Beatrez testified that Ms. House had told her that CMDR O'Hare wanted to "try and reach Mr. Woodson." But given the above-described problems with the sufficiency of the circumstantial evidence used to infer Ms. Beatrez's intent, the Board's reasons for substituting its own credibility determinations for that of the administrative judge are not sufficiently sound. Accordingly, the Board's decision does not survive substantial evidence review.

Suggesting (likely as dictum) greater deference to AJs' credibility findings, under Reeves v. USPS, 117 MSPR 201, 207 ¶ 15, 2011 MSPB 102 (2011):

The Board will defer to the credibility determinations of an administrative judge when they are based, explicitly or implicitly, upon the observation of the demeanor of witnesses testifying at a hearing because the administrative judge is in the best position to observe the demeanor of the witnesses and determine which witnesses were testifying credibly. Haebe v. Department of Justice, 288 F.3d 1288, 1300-01 (Fed. Cir. 2002); see Smith v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 93 M.S.P.R. 424, ¶ 4 (2003). The credibility determinations of an administrative judge are virtually unreviewable on appeal. Bieber v. Department of the Army, 287 F.3d 1358, 1364 (Fed. Cir. 2002); see Hambsch v. Department of the Treasury, 796 F.2d 430, 436 (Fed. Cir. 1986).

The citation to the "virtually unreviewable" standard, as reflected by the Federal Circuit cases cited in Reeves, is misleading. The court cases referred to a statutory judicial standard of review by a court charged with reviewing Board factual findings based on the substantiality of the evidence as a whole. The Board is not constrained by that statutory standard and probably did not intend to adopt it as its standard of review of decisions of its AJs, who are attorney examiners, not administrative law judges with limited statutory judicial independence.

Alexander v. USPS, CH-0752-09-0737-I-1 (NP 3/3/2011), provided a synopsis of the law and a reversal of the AJ's credibility findings:

Normally, the Board will defer to the credibility determinations of an administrative judge when they are based, explicitly or implicitly, upon the observation of the demeanor of witnesses testifying at a hearing because the administrative judge is in the best position to observe the demeanor of the witnesses and determine which witnesses were testifying credibly. Special Counsel v. Lee, 114 M.S.P.R. 57, ¶ 22 (2010); see Haebe v. Department of Justice, 288 F.3d 1288, 1301 (Fed. Cir. 2002); Smith v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 93 M.S.P.R. 424, ¶ 4 (2003). Conversely, the Board may substitute its own determinations of fact for those of an administrative judge, even where his credibility findings are based in part on demeanor evidence, if the Board can articulate a sound reason, based on the record, for a contrary evaluation of the evidence. Lee, 114 M.S.P.R. 57, ¶ 22; Dogar v. Department of Defense, 95 M.S.P.R. 527, ¶ 4 (2004), aff'd, 128 F. App'x 156 (Fed. Cir. 2005); see Haebe, 288 F.3d at 1300. The Board also may overturn credibility determinations when the findings are incomplete, inconsistent with the weight of the evidence, and do not reflect the record as a whole. Lee, 114 M.S.P.R. 57, ¶ 22; see Wallace v. Department of Commerce, 106 M.S.P.R. 23, ¶¶ 14-16 (2007) (rejecting an administrative judge's credibility determinations for a failure to consider conflicting evidence); Moore v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 97 M.S.P.R. 684, ¶ 12 (2004) (rejecting an administrative judge's credibility determination in favor of the testimony of one agency witness where the testimony conflicted with sworn statements by three impartial witnesses supporting the appellant's account of events); Faucher v. Department of the Air Force, 96 M.S.P.R. 203, ¶¶ 8, 12-13 (2004) (rejecting an administrative judge's credibility determinations where the administrative judge failed to identify and discuss the purported victim of sexual harassment's initial statement reporting the harassment and several evidentiary submissions that supported a finding that she was a credible witness).

In this case, the administrative judge failed to mention crucial evidence, mischaracterized other evidence, wantonly disregarded the testimony of important witnesses, and appeared to ignore a central question as to whether or not the appellant testified falsely at the hearing. We find that the administrative judge's credibility determinations are rife with errors, and, therefore, we now substitute our own. See Haebe, 288 F.3d at 1300.

Theoretically, under Weaver, supra, because credibility resolutions are not readily made on the basis of the transcript alone, deference is paid to the AJ's credibility resolutions. Until Jackson v. VA, 768 F.2d 1325 (Fed. Cir. 1985), post, the Board essentially determined that it could freely substitute its factual findings for those of the AJ. A complete factual review was conducted in Miller v. Dept. of Air Force, 8 MSPR 204 (1981). The case involved two appellants removed for alleged threats to coworkers. The Board went through an elaborate analysis of the testimony, with citations to the transcript, determined that the threats had not been made, and reversed the initial decision sustaining the removals. The Board's opinion does not indicate what triggered the extensive review, although it may be significant that an administrative law judge of the Federal Labor Relations Authority determined that the employer committed numerous unfair labor practices in the appellant's workplace. (FLRA ALJs normally write detailed opinions, replete with record and legal citation and analysis.) When, in a different appeal, the Board reinstated a removal reversed by the AJ, Board Member Wertheim dissented, noting that the AJ weighed the evidence and the Board failed to state any basis for reversing the factual findings. Hayden v. Bureau of Indian Affairs, 5 MSPR 92, 95, 5 MSPB 162 (1981); see Wilson v. DHHS, 5 MSPR 102, 107, 5 MSPB 172 (1981) (presiding officials (Ajs) have no independent statutory jurisdiction; the Board is free to substitute its determinations of fact, giving the findings made by the presiding official the weight warranted by the record and by the strength of the presiding official's reasoning). Member Wertheim also dissented when he felt the Board erred by disregarding credibility resolutions made by the AJ without articulation by the Board of the basis for its revisions. York v. USPS, 6 MSPR 450, 453-55, 6 MSPB 381 (1981). Member Devaney, critical of the Board's de novo review of the record, considered the appellant's testimony incredible. Beck v. Dept. of Transp., 19 MSPR 402, 407 (1984).

Courts recognize that if credibility issues are not critical, the Board is as capable of evaluating the evidence as the AJs, Dunning v. NASA, 718 F.2d 1170 (D.C. Cir. 1983), but restraint is to be exercised before credibility determinations can be disturbed, Connolly v. DOJ, 766 F.2d 507, 512 (Fed. Cir. 1985):

...A presiding official does not have an independent statutory basis for his jurisdiction. He hears only those cases referred to him by the board. The decision of a presiding official is merely "an initial (or recommended) decision." 5 USC 1201.111. Such decision becomes final if a petition for review is denied or if a party to the appeal or the Special Counsel or the Director of the Office of Personnel Management does not petition the board for review within 30 days after receipt of the decision, 5 USC 7701(e)(1), or within 35 days after issuance of the decision, 5 CFR 1201.113, 1201.114, unless time is waived, 5 CFR 1201.12, or if the board does not reopen and reconsider the case on its own motion. In any case reviewed or reopened, the "[b]oard may affirm, reverse, remand, modify or vacate the decision of the presiding official, in whole or in part." 5 CFR 1201.116(b). Given such a statutory and regulatory scheme, it is inappropriate to assume that the board is guided by customary standards of appellate review. (citing Weaver).... To the contrary, the board is free to substitute its judgment for that of one of its presiding officials, Curran v. Dept. of Treasury, 714 F.2d 913, 915 (9th Cir. 1983); McDonough v. USPS, 666 F.2d 647, 648 n.1 (1st Cir. 1981), with deference of course to the presiding official on any issues of credibility. Griessenauer v. Dept. of Energy, 754 F.2d 361, 364 (Fed. Cir. 1985).

...The initial (or recommended) decision of the presiding official is part of the record which this court reviews. When, as here, the decision of the full board differs from that of its presiding official, this court will engage in a more searching scrutiny of the record. See Curran, 714 F.2d at 915, and cases cited there.

Connolly was not mentioned but its theme was developed in Jackson v. VA, 768 F.2d 1325, 1332 (Fed. Cir. 1985), a sexual harassment case. The court there concluded that the Board could not disregard a AJ's credibility findings without articulating the reasons for its decision; disagreement alone was insufficient. As to credibility findings, "the presiding official is without question the better judge of who to believe." Special deference must be provided to the findings of the AJ who relies expressly or by necessary implication on the demeanor of witnesses. If the Board comes to a contrary evaluation of the testimonial evidence, the Board must articulate a sound reason for this result. [Jackson is discussed in Chapter 17, "Substantial Evidence; Factual Dispute."]

The necessity for the Board to analyze evidence carefully when it reverses credibility determinations was emphasized, Chauvin v. Dept. of Navy, 38 F.3d 563, 566 (Fed. Cir. 1994):

The AJ concluded that Chauvin did not intend to remove the plate from the shipyard without authorization. Specifically, the AJ found that scrap metal was commonly used for non-production and personal uses at the shipyard; that Chauvin did not attempt to conceal the plate in his truck, which was parked near a security gate in the shipyard parking lot; that Chauvin testified in a "consistent and credible" manner; and that Chauvin's explanation was corroborated by a written statement of a co-worker, who stated that prior to the date of the incident Chauvin informed him that he intended to obtain a property pass to borrow a piece of scrap metal for his truck. The board majority, disbelieving Chauvin's testimony, substituted its contrary finding. Chauvin argues that the board majority erred in reversing the AJ's credibility finding.

We agree. The board is not free simply to disagree with an AJ's assessment of credibility. Jackson v. Veterans Administration, 768 F.2d 1325, 1331 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Rather, the board must give special deference to the AJ's factual findings that are based, expressly or implicitly, on the demeanor of a witness. Id. When the board reverses such a finding, we cannot sustain the decision on appeal unless the board has articulated sound reasons, based on the record, for its contrary evaluation of the testimonial evidence. Id.

Here, the board majority cited the Navy's policy prohibiting personal use of government property, Chauvin's position as a supervisor, and his actions in cutting the plate and placing it in his truck. None of these factors, however, is inconsistent with Chauvin's explanation that he planned to remove the plate from the shipyard only if his supervisor granted him a property pass. They are not probative of the charge of attempted removal. Also, whether a property pass would have been granted does not show lack of intention to obtain one. The evidence cited by the board thus fails to rebut Chauvin's consistent and corroborated explanation for his having placed the plate in his truck. Moreover, the board majority's finding is inconsistent with the Navy's admission during discovery that it did not contend that Chauvin intended to remove the plate from the shipyard without a property pass. We conclude, as did the dissenting board member, and as the Navy now concedes, that the board majority did not articulate a sufficient reason for its disbelief of Chauvin's testimony. See Jackson, 768 F.2d at 1331 ("[E]vidence in the record which, when taken alone, may amount to 'substantial evidence' and therefore support the Board's decision, will often be insufficient when the trial examiner has, on the basis of the witnesses' demeanor, made credibility determinations contrary to the board's position.") (quoting Penasquitos Village, Inc. v. National Labor Relations Bd., 565 F.2d 1074, 1078 (9th Cir. 1977)).

Given the deference that should have been afforded the AJ's credibility-based finding concerning Chauvin's intent, and the Navy's admission that it did not allege that Chauvin intended to remove the plate from the shipyard without authorization, we cannot agree that a reasonable fact-finder would have found that Chauvin intended to take that action. See Jackson, 768 F.2d at 1332. Because the Navy failed to prove intent, an essential element of the attempted removal charge, the board's decision sustaining the charge is not supported by substantial evidence. See Burroughs v. Dept. of Army, 918 F.2d 170, 172 (Fed. Cir. 1990) ("If the agency fails to prove one of the elements of its charge, then the entire charge must fail."). We therefore reverse the board's decision insofar as it sustains the charge of attempted removal of government property.

Determinations by an AJ as to the appellant's rehabilitation potential may equate to credibility findings entitled to Board deference, Purifoy v. VA, 838 F.3d 1367, 1373 (Fed. Cir. 2016):

[F]indings about Mr. Purifoy's propensity for rehabilitation are necessarily intertwined with issues of credibility and an analysis of his demeanor at trial, and they deserved deference from the Board....

If an AJ makes findings that do not provide specific record references, the Board may independently review the evidence of record to determine if the AJ's findings are supported. See Blake v. DOJ, 81 MSPR 394, 401 ¶ 8 (1999).

The Board may also independently review findings not dependent on credibility assessments, Hale v. Dept. of Transp., 772 F.2d 882, 886 (Fed. Cir. 1985):

This case may be distinguished from another recent decision by this court, Jackson v. VA, 768 F.2d 1325 (Fed. Cir. 1985), in which this court reversed the board and concluded that the presiding official's determination that the agency had not proved its case by a preponderance of the evidence was correct. The court there reversed the board's decision because the decision of the presiding official was based on credibility of witnesses, an issue on which the presiding official should be accorded substantial deference. Here, the board's decision not to adopt the presiding official's initial decision was based entirely on a review of documentary evidence, the identical evidence presented to the presiding official and to this court. Where the final decision of the board is based entirely on a question of law, as is this case, there is no basis for according substantial deference to the presiding official's conclusion.

If an AJ's findings are not based on the AJ's observation of a witness and consideration of his or her demeanor, the Board need not defer to related findings by the AJ that are contrary to the preponderance of the evidence. Bledsoe v. DOJ, 91 MSPR 93, 101-02 ¶ 17 (2002); see Christopher v. Dept. of Army, 107 MSPR 580, 588 ¶ 16, 2008 MSPB 2 (2008) ("When an administrative judge's findings are not based on the observation of witnesses' demeanor, the Board is free to re-weigh the evidence and to substitute its own judgment on credibility issues. Haebe, 288 F.3d at 1302. In this case, the initial decision's findings do not indicate that the administrative judge relied upon the demeanor of the witnesses."). [Refer later in this chapter to "Decision Based on Affidavits; Documents."]

Caddell v. DOJ, 61 MSPR 670, 682 (1994), concluded that "remand is unnecessary where the record is sufficiently well-developed, and findings can be based on factors other than the witnesses' demeanor." See Rackers v. DOJ, 79 MSPR 262, 277 (1998) (same); Timberlake v. USPS, 76 MSPR 172, 177 (1997) ("The Board will resolve conflicts in the evidence and decide issues of credibility without remanding where the record is sufficiently well-developed to address these issues and the Board's findings are not based on the demeanor of witnesses."); Aiu v. DOJ, 70 MSPR 509, 517 (1996) ("Where those assessments are not based on witness demeanor and are contrary to a preponderance of the evidence of record, however, they do not merit the Board's deference. Uske v. USPS, 60 MSPR 544, 550, 557 & nn. 26, 77 (1994)."); Scott v. DOJ, 69 MSPR 211, 222 (1995) ("[W]e find the record sufficiently well-developed to address those issues without a remand because we need not base our findings on the demeanor of witnesses."); Staples v. OPM, 68 MSPR 570, 574 (1995) ("Because the record is sufficiently well-developed, however, we may decide the issue here without a remand."). The same result occurred in Frederick v. DOJ, 65 MSPR 517, 532 (1994):

Nevertheless, although resolving conflicts in the evidence and deciding issues of credibility is normally the province of the administrative judge, we find that the record is sufficiently developed to address this question without a remand, because we need not base our findings on the factor of demeanor. See Uske v. USPS, 60 MSPR 544, 557 (1994). Mr. Womack's demeanor is not at issue, because he did not testify at the hearing and the administrative judge thus had no opportunity to observe him. While the administrative judge certainly had the opportunity to observe Mr. Mayberry's demeanor during the latter's testimony, he did not record his observations in the initial decision and thus apparently did not conclude that they significantly influenced the result that he reached. We find that the issue thus can be resolved here based on factors other than demeanor and that a remand is unnecessary. See id.

As to its authority to find facts, under Deskin v. USPS, 76 MSPR 505, 510 (1997):

In reviewing an initial decision where the record is complete on the issues in conflict, the Board is free to substitute its own determinations of fact for those of the administrative judge, giving his findings only as much weight as may be warranted by the record and by the strength of his reasoning. See Marchese v. Department of the Navy, 65 MSPR 104, 109 (1994); Uske v. U.S. Postal Service, 60 MSPR 544, 557 (1994), aff'd, 56 F.3d 1375 (Fed. Cir. 1995), cert. denied, 116 S. Ct. 728 (1996); Weaver v. Department of the Navy, 2 MSPR 129, 133 (1980), review denied, 669 F.2d 613 (9th Cir. 1982) (per curiam). Further, the Board may substitute its own credibility determinations for an administrative judge's explicit or implicit credibility findings where the administrative judge's findings are not based on the demeanor of the witnesses. See Jackson v. Veterans Administration, 768 F.2d 1325, 1331 (Fed. Cir. 1985). The Board, however, must articulate sound reasons, based on the record, for its contrary evaluation of the testimonial evidence. See Kimm v. Department of the Treasury, 61 F.3d 888, 892 (Fed. Cir. 1995), citing Chauvin v. Department of the Navy, 38 F.3d 563, 566 (Fed. Cir. 1994).

As to Board authority to reverse the credibility findings of an AJ, the Board may modify those findings on a matter established by circumstantial evidence, e.g., theft, if it has a sound reason, based on the record, for its contrary evaluation of the testimonial evidence. Although the Board chose one set of inferences from the same facts that led the AJ to another set of inferences, the court sustained the Board's ruling, citing Jackson v. VA, 768 F.2d 1325, 1330 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Davis v. Dept. of Air Force (Fed. Cir. 1986 NP No. 85-2648).

When the Board wants to affirm the AJ, it rules that the administrative findings were not shown to have rested on testimony either inherently improbable or based on internal inconsistencies, or it rules that the evidence was "merely susceptible to varying interpretations." Carroll v. Dept. of Air Force, 34 MSPR 87, 89 (1987) (citing Sowers v. USDA, 24 MSPR 492, 495 (1984)); see Smith v. Dept. of Air Force, 34 MSPR 373, 375 (1987) ("The appellant has failed to establish that the administrative judge's credibility determinations, based on both demeanor and corroboration, actually rested on internal inconsistencies or inherent improbabilities."). Conversely, when documents or objective evidence contradict a witness's testimony or where the explanation itself is so internally inconsistent or implausible on its face that a reasonable factfinder would not credit it, the reviewing authority may find clear error in the purported credibility determination. Bledsoe v. DOJ, 91 MSPR 93, 108 ¶ 29 (2002) (considering the inherent improbability of events); Bradley v. VA, 39 MSPR 598, 602 (1989) (AJ did not rely specifically on demeanor of witnesses or individual credibility findings and he did not refer to factors under Hillen v. Dept. of Army, 35 MSPR 453 (1987), to determine the credibility of evidence); Marotta v. DHHS, 34 MSPR 252, 257-58 (1987) ("When documents or objective evidence contradict the witness' story or when the story itself is so internally inconsistent or implausible on its face that a reasonable factfinder would not credit it, the reviewing authority may find clear error in the purported credibility determination."). The Board may be less inclined to entertain objections to findings based on documents or testimony if that evidence was introduced by the party who makes objections to its interpretation. See Merchant v. USPS, 52 MSPR 330, 333 n.6 (1992).

Credibility findings of an AJ were set aside in an appeal involving six or so agency witnesses whose testimony was not consistent, against the testimony of the appellant, found to be consistent. The Board's decision may have been influenced by what appeared to be an attempt by the agency to prevent the appellant from securing some critical testimony and also by the agency's failure to file an opposition to the appellant's PFR. Madison v. Dept. of Air Force, 32 MSPR 465, 468-69 (1987). Credibility findings concerning the intent of the appellant to falsify a SF-171 employment application were set aside in Delessio v. USPS, 33 MSPR 517, 519-20 (1987), the Board concluding that the AJ did not consider the total evidence. The same result occurred in another SF-171 falsification case, Harmon v. GSA, 61 MSPR 327, 332 (1994), with the Board noting that "[w]hile we normally accord due deference to credibility determinations made by an administrative judge on the basis of a witness's demeanor, the question of the appellant's intent must be resolved not from demeanor evidence alone, but rather from the totality of the circumstantial evidence."

Assuming that the party filing the PFR provides precise citation to evidence, and given the Board's position on review of credibility issues, what does the Board review? Broughton v. DHHS, 33 MSPR 357, 359 (1987), suggested that without internal inconsistencies or inherent improbability of evidence, it is the credibility analysis that counts:

...Appellant's petition contains specific citations to the record in support of her contentions that the administrative judge erred.... However, the petition fails to identify any internal inconsistency or inherent improbability in the fact findings of the administrative judge or other basis sufficient to overcome the special deference which reviewing bodies must necessarily accord the factual determinations of the original trier of fact. See Jackson v. VA, 768 F.2d 1325 (Fed. Cir. 1985); Connolly v. Dept. of Justice, 766 F.2d 507 (Fed. Cir. 1985). The initial decision reflects that the administrative judge considered the evidence as a whole, drew appropriate inferences, and made reasoned conclusions on issues of credibility. Under these circumstances, the Board finds no reason to disturb those conclusions.

Broughton was followed in Kirkpatrick v. USPS, 74 MSPR 583, 588 (1997) ("[T]he Board will not disturb an administrative judge's conclusions where an appellant's petition for review fails to identify any internal inconsistency or inherent improbability in the administrative judge's fact findings, or other basis sufficient to overcome the special deference which reviewing bodies must necessarily accord the factual determinations of the original trier of fact."); see Iyer v. Dept. of Treasury, 95 MSPR 239, 243 ¶ 9 (2003) ("special deference must be given to the administrative judge's findings regarding credibility where those findings are based on the straightforward and candid demeanor of the witnesses").

A typical analysis calling into question an AJ's credibility analysis and examining the probative value of hearsay is provided by Womack v. USPS, 70 MSPR 285, 291-93 (1996):

While hearsay is admissible in proceedings before the Board, the assessment of the probative value of such evidence depends on the circumstances of each case. See, e.g., Reniere v. Dept. of Agric., 62 MSPR 648, 652 (1994). The determination of whether hearsay alone is sufficient to sustain a charge depends on its reliability and trustworthiness. See SSA v. Whittlesey, 59 MSPR 684, 692 (1993), aff'd, 39 F.3d 1197 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table), cert. denied, __U.S.__, 115 S. Ct. 1690, 131 L. Ed.2d 555 (1995). The probative value of such evidence must be evaluated in terms of the factors set forth in Borninkhof v. Dept. of Justice, 5 MSPB 150, 5 MSPR 77, 83-87 (1981), such as the availability of persons with firsthand knowledge to testify at the hearing, the agency's explanation for failing to obtain signed or sworn statements, whether the out-of-court declarant was a disinterested witness to the events, the consistency of the declarant's account with other information, whether corroboration for statements can be found in the agency's record, the absence of contradictory evidence, and the credibility of the declarant when he made the statement attributed to him.

We find that the administrative judge did not address information in the record that is material in determining the weight to be accorded the hearsay statement of Ingram.

First, the agency offered no explanation for its failure to call Ingram to testify at the hearing. Instead, the agency called Horger, who testified that the appellant approached Ingram and questioned Ingram regarding whether he deserved to be back at work on limited duty. Horger testified that the appellant called the injury compensation office in Cleveland, representing herself as the injury compensation specialist, and asked about Ingram's medical condition and whether Ingram should have been returned to work. Horger stated that his knowledge of the incident was based on what Ingram told him, and that he reported the incident to Draper. Although the hearing in this appeal occurred over six years after the appellant's demotion, the agency did not show, or even assert, that Ingram was unavailable, and did not obtain a written statement or explain why it did not obtain such a statement.

Second, corroboration for Ingram's account of the incident cannot be found in the agency's record. The only documentary evidence relating to this charge is a January 24, 1989 memorandum from Draper to the appellant, instructing the appellant to "refrain from further interference in, and misrepresentation of the Youngstown MSC Injury Compensation Unit." The memorandum also states that, should further problems arise, including unauthorized telephone calls to outside agencies, the appellant will be subject to adverse action. While this memorandum may establish that Horger reported Ingram's account of the incident to Draper, it does not corroborate Ingram's account of what occurred.

Third, Horger's testimony regarding what Ingram told him is contradicted by the appellant's live testimony. See Robinson v. DHHS, 39 MSPR 110, 115 (1988) (hearsay evidence may not be sufficiently probative, in light of contradictory live testimony, to sustain an agency's burden by preponderant evidence). The appellant testified that she did call the Department of Labor after Ingram told her that he could not get through to his claims examiner. The appellant testified that, after reaching the claims examiner, she handed Ingram the telephone and left the office so he could talk to the examiner in private. She testified that she never questioned Ingram or asked him anything; rather, Ingram told her what he needed and she made the call, handed him the telephone, and left the office. She testified that did not talk to Ingram's claims examiner that day.

Fourth, Ingram is not a disinterested witness to the events and his credibility is cause for concern. The agency's specification suggests that Ingram had filed an Equal Employment Opportunity complaint against the appellant before this incident occurred. Ingram's statement could therefore be tainted by any bias he may have felt toward the appellant....

Walker v. Dept. of Navy, 59 MSPR 309, 325 (1993), conducted a complete analysis of the testimony and evidence in a sexual harassment case to justify setting aside the AJ's credibility findings favoring the appellant. The Board found no legal fault in the AJ's analysis, but the Board drew different conclusions as to who should be believed after examining the testimony and evidence in the appeal. The explanation for the result was that the Board was justified under Weaver v. Dept. of Navy, 2 MSPR 129, 2 MSPB 297 (1980), in giving the AJ's findings only the weight warranted by the record and the strength of her reasoning, and that its approach was justified because a critical piece of evidence consisted of a tape recording of a telephone conversation that the Board determined could be analyzed by the AJ no better than by the Board.
