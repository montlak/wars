broida mspb Chapter 15: Attorney Fees Chapter 15: Attorney Fees, Subchapter 02: Procedures for Obtaining Fees, Section E: Fee Motion, Subsection 05: Format of Motion, Part a: What to Include
a. What to Include

A fee request or motion has no prescribed format, but defined information must be included, 5 CFR 1201.203 (2022):

Proceedings for attorney fees.

(a) Form and content of request. A request for attorney fees must be made by motion, must state why the appellant or respondent believes he or she is entitled to an award under the applicable statutory standard, and must be supported by evidence substantiating the amount of the request. Evidence supporting a motion for attorney fees must include at a minimum:

(1) Accurate and current time records;

(2) A copy of the terms of the fee agreement (if any);

(3) A statement of the attorney's customary billing rate for similar work, with evidence that that rate is consistent with the prevailing community rate for similar services in the community in which the attorney ordinarily practices; and

(4) An established attorney-client relationship.

As a practical matter, the following should be included: (1) a motion asking for a sum certain; (2) a supporting memorandum explaining the calculation of the fees demanded and the factual and legal basis for the award of fees; (3) an affidavit or sworn declaration from counsel explaining his hourly rate, nature of practice, experience, education, retainer agreement with his client, system for keeping track of time and expenses, and information supporting his hourly rate; perhaps a statement about the relative complexity of the case; a summary of the major components of time invested in the appeal, a statement about expenses, and a statement that the time and expenses are reasonable; and (4) copies of the retainer agreement, time sheets, and itemizations or summaries of recoverable expenses. If counsel does not have an established record of fee awards from the Board or other adjudicators involving employment litigation, affidavits should be submitted from other counsel, with comparable experience in roughly comparable litigation, who can state their own rates and, when possible, explain whether they view as reasonable the rate of petitioning counsel.

Under Mitchell, it is not enough to state the hourly rate set in the fee agreement; the fee agreement should be attached to the motion, or its terms stated, and the other requirements of the case must be established to demonstrate the reasonableness of the fee requested. Francis v. Dept. of Commerce, 23 MSPR 443, 446 (1984); see Key v. USPS, 33 MSPR 142, 151-52 (1987) ("while it is preferable that the appellant's counsel submit the terms of their fee arrangement with the appellant, it was not required that the appellant's counsel submit the fee arrangement itself...appellant's counsel need only submit with his motion for fees a statement of the terms of the agreement").

There is no requirement that a fee agreement be written (although state bar rules may require a different result). See Jones v. Dept. of Navy, 46 MSPR 616, 622 (1991) (counsel's affidavit asserted that he was under a monthly retainer to the union and that he and the union agreed he could keep any fees awarded; the agency failed to request discovery on the nature of the agreement or rates). Nor, under some decisions, is there a requirement that a copy of an agreement be submitted to the Board as a part of a fee request. Wilkins v. Dept. of Navy, 51 MSPR 290, 293 (1991) (affidavit from the attorney describing the fee arrangement was sufficient); see Miller v. VA, 84 MSPR 692, 696 ¶ 6 (2000) (record contained evidence of an oral fee agreement and work performed by counsel under that agreement: "The agency's substantive rights are not prejudiced where an administrative judge ignores the appellant's failure to submit a copy of the fee agreement if the appellant establishes that fees were incurred and the agency does not argue that fees were not incurred[.] "); Williams v. Dept. of Air Force, 64 MSPR 37, 41 (1994) ("[t]he appellant submitted sufficient evidence of a fee agreement. The attorney stated in affidavit form that the appellant had incurred attorney fees and that he had a written contract with the appellant for $5,000 in fees."). But see Neal v. DOJ, 65 MSPR 207, 213 (1994) ("In order to establish the appropriate hourly rate, a fee application must contain a copy of the fee agreement, if any, as well as evidence of the attorney's customary billing rate for similar work."); Lizut v. Dept. of Army, 51 MSPR 549, 553-54 (1991) (suggesting that if a written fee agreement exists it should be submitted to the Board); Swanson v. DLA, 35 MSPR 115, 119 (1987) ("In order to establish the appropriate hourly rate, the fee application must contain a copy of the agreement, if any.").
