a. Language Difficulties

An untimely appeal may be excused if it is delayed by the appellant's difficulty with the English language and related problems encountered in having materials translated for the Board. Sadian v. OPM, 45 MSPR 100, 103 (1990); see Koyner v. OPM, DC-0831-12-0065-I-1 (NP 5/3/2013) ("The appellant noted that she is not fluent in English and that all English correspondence is translated for her. The Board has previously held that an appellant's knowledge of English is another factor to be considered in determining good cause to waive the time limits for an appeal. Bocanegra, 52 M.S.P.R. at 117. The appellant is appearing pro se which is also a factor for consideration. Moorman v. Department of the Army, 68 M.S.P.R. 60, 62-63 (1995), aff'd, 79 F.3d 1167 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table)."); Chang-Hung Wang v. OPM, 36 MSPR 28, 30 (1987) (following a court remand, a delay of ten days in filing was excused when the appellant was ill, not living in her usual residence; she did not understand English; she had difficulty arranging for a translation; the translator did not understand that the appellant should have described the reasons for delay in filing the appeal). The same holds for the illiterate appellant not made aware of his appeal rights through verbal instruction from agency officials (who knew of the appellant's illiteracy) or from information from another source. Scott v. Dept. of Navy, 40 MSPR 579, 582 (1989) (evidence did not demonstrate that the appellant had representation or that he would have learned of appeal rights from other employees who were suspended at the same time for the same offense); Ott v. EPA, 14 MSPR 158, 160 (1982) (excusing a short delay: "While the appellant was notified of the time limits, it is apparent that he did not appreciate the significance or the terms of the deadline requirement. The record establishes that the appellant is functionally illiterate and may easily have been confused."). But see Ford v. OPM, 59 MSPR 66, 69 (1993) ("Mr. Ford's alleged inability to read and write is not significant since he had assistance at all times, before both OPM and the Board's regional office.").

Everage v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1991 NP No. 91-3133), found no legal authority for the proposition that a sixth-grade reading ability should excuse noncompliance with Board rules governing the filing of an appeal. Casiana v. MSPB, 70 Fed. Appx. 562 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP), declined to excuse an appeal, late by some years, filed by an individual who was deaf, for whom English was not her first language, whose ability to understand written English was stated to be at the third grade level, and whose counsel allegedly provided less than competent representation. But OPM should have forwarded to the Board, and the AJ should have accepted as an appeal timely filed, a letter to OPM by an appellant with limited English ability who sought further reconsideration from OPM of a retirement decision. Lapenas v. OPM, 44 MSPR 303, 306 (1990).

The Board summarized law concerning the effect of claimed illiteracy on a late appeal, Teamer v. VA, CH-0752-10-0735-I-1 (NP 4/5/2011):

The appellant's excuse, as expressed solely by his brother, was that he could not read or write and was unable to understand the dismissal letter. He therefore had to wait for his brother to return from "out of town" in order to file his appeal. However, the Board has held that an assertion that an appellant was illiterate and had to wait for someone to return from out of town to help did not provide good cause to waive the filing deadline for a petition for review. See Besinio v. Office of Personnel Management, 75 M.S.P.R. 187 (1997) (3-year delay).

The only support the appellant provided for his excuse is his brother's unsworn statement. The administrative judge correctly noted that the appellant failed to provide any evidence regarding when his brother was actually "out of town" including whether his absence occurred between the issuance of the agency decision and the filing deadline and when he returned. Thus, even if the Board were to consider the appellant's excuse, he has failed to provide any evidence in support of his assertion. He has also not submitted any evidence to show that he is indeed illiterate.

Even assuming that the appellant is illiterate, however, he designated his brother as his representative around the same time that the administrative judge issued the show-cause order on jurisdiction. The agency served its response, which notes the timeliness issue, on the appellant's brother, but the appellant did not respond. Moreover, based on the appellant's brother's initial statement filed with the appellant's appeal, it is clear that he was aware that the appeal was untimely filed. Because the agency's response notes the untimeliness of the appeal and states that the appellant failed to offer any justification for the delay, the appellant's brother was put on notice by that time, if not much sooner, that the timeliness of the appeal was at issue.

The Board has found that in a situation where the record establishes that an appellant is illiterate, and the agency was aware of that fact, the agency's failure to make that appellant aware of his appeal rights and the applicable time limits may provide good cause for waiver of the filing deadline. Scott v. Department of the Navy, 40 M.S.P.R. 579, 581-82 (1989) (the parties stipulated, in accordance with a professional evaluation, that the appellant could not read or write); Ott v. Environmental Protection Agency, 14 M.S.P.R. 158, 159-60 (1982) (clinical psychologist's report indicated that the appellant was functionally illiterate). However, as noted above, there is no evidence in this matter to establish that the appellant actually is illiterate, much less that the agency was aware of his alleged illiteracy. Thus, those cases are distinguishable from the instant matter.

Further, the appellant addresses neither the administrative judge's finding that a reasonable person would have found someone to help him read the decision letter, nor his failure to specify the timeframe that his brother was unavailable. Indeed, the appellant has not provided any evidence or argument on the issue of timeliness in his petition for review. Accordingly, the appellant has failed to show good cause for waiving the filing deadline for his appeal.
