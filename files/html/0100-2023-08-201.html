c. Calculating Duration of Suspension

Although the Board only has jurisdiction over suspensions exceeding 14 days, Klimek v. Dept. of Army, 3 MSPR 139, 142, 3 MSPB 248 (1980), determined that the Board had jurisdiction over a suspension for 14 working days when that suspension exceeded 14 calendar days and covered more than one full pay period. Klimek was cited by Clerman v. ICC, 35 MSPR 190, 193 (1987):

[F]or the purposes of determining adverse action appeal rights...the length of an action imposed on intermittent days should be judged by the number of work days which generally would be included in an action of the same number of calendar days. Thus, in that case, the Board concluded that the suspension imposed on 14 non-consecutive days had to be regarded as the equivalent of a suspension of more than 14 calendar days and was, therefore, an appealable adverse action.

The Board has since determined that because a suspension is measured in calendar days, a suspension within the Board's jurisdiction may encompass 10 workdays but 14 or more calendar days. See Piercy v. Dept. of Transp., 42 MSPR 73, 76 (1989) (remanding for a determination of the duration of a suspension).

Nonworkdays do not add to a 14-day suspension that is otherwise properly calculated, Cremeans v. USPS, 88 MSPR 277, 279-80 ¶¶ 6-7 (2001):

Where an agency suspends an employee for 14 days and does not immediately return him to work on the fifteenth day because that day is one of his regularly scheduled days off, the suspension cannot be deemed to have lasted more than 14 days. Perez v. U.S. Postal Service, 52 MSPR 248, 250 n.2 (1992). The undisputed time and attendance records show that the appellant's predetermined tour of duty for the relevant time period ran from Monday through Friday. Thus, as the agency states, his regularly scheduled days off were Saturday and Sunday. Because the 2 days immediately preceding the appellant's return to duty status on June 12 were his regularly scheduled days off, the suspension cannot be deemed to have extended beyond Friday, June 9. See Perez, 52 MSPR at 250 n.2.

Moreover, where notice of a suspension clearly indicates that the suspension will not last beyond 14 days and that the employee will return to work on the next working day following his regularly scheduled days off, the regularly scheduled days off do not extend the suspension beyond the 14 day period. Jennings v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 59 F.3d 159, 160 (Fed. Cir. 1995). The agency's notice here informed the appellant that the suspension would not extend beyond 14 days starting May 27, 2000, and it advised him that if, for any reason, the suspension period extended beyond 14 days, he would be placed in administrative leave status and paid for those days. The appellant therefore was plainly on notice that his suspension would not be longer than 14 days, and that his non-duty, non-pay status would not continue under any circumstances beyond Friday, June 9, 2000, which was the fourteenth day from May 27. For this reason as well, the Board finds that Saturday and Sunday, June 10 and 11, cannot be deemed to extend the suspension.

See Ahuruonye v. Dept. of Interior, DC-0752-15-0509-I-1 (NP 6/29/2015) ("We therefore find that Saturday and Sunday, December 27-28, 2014, and Saturday and Sunday, January 10-11, 2015, would not constitute periods of suspension. See Cremeans v. U.S. Postal Service, 88 M.S.P.R. 277, ¶ 6 (2001) (where an agency suspends an employee for 14 days and does not immediately return him to work on the fifteenth day because that is one of his regularly scheduled days off, the suspension cannot be deemed to have lasted more than 14 days). At best, the period of AWOL from December 29, 2014, to January 9, 2015, could only constitute a 12-day suspension. Thus, even taking into account January 20, 2015, the appellant could only at most show that he was suspended for 13 days.").

If, in addition to a ten-day suspension, the employee is also required to shift a regular day off under a flexible schedule, the suspension is not over 14 days, Engler v. Dept. of Army, 121 MSPR 547, 548 ¶¶ 2, 8, 2014 MSPB 71 (2014):

The agency suspended the appellant from his GS-13 Logistics Management Specialist position for 10 working days to be served from Monday, April 29, 2013, through Friday, May 10, 2013, for conduct unbecoming a federal employee. In the decision letter, the agency ordered the appellant to move his regular day off (RDO) from May 3, 2013, to April 26, 2013, the Friday immediately preceding the suspension period. Id. at 154 of 169. Pursuant to the agency's instruction, the appellant was off work from Friday, April 26, 2013, through Monday, May 13, 2013, although he was returned to a duty status on Saturday, May 11, 2013.

...

We find that the switching of an optional day off under a compressed work schedule does not equate to involuntarily placing an employee in a leave status. Here, the appellant is not losing any pay or benefit of employment by having to take an alternate day off. Thus, the situation here, where management merely advised the appellant to change his day off under the Federal Employees Flexible and Compressed Work Schedules Act, Pub. L. No. 95-390, 95 Stat. 755 (1978), as amended, codified at 5 U.S.C. §§ 6120-6133, is not tantamount to forcing an employee to take leave against his will. See generally Worthington v. United States, 168 F.3d 24, 27 (Fed. Cir. 1999) (being forced to work a compressed work schedule is not an adverse action within the Board's jurisdiction).
