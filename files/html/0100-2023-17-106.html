a. Reorganization Defined; Single Position RIF

A reorganization is a planned action by management at any level that eliminates, adds to, or redistributes functions or duties of one or more positions. Calhoon v. Dept. of Treasury, 90 MSPR 375, 378-79 ¶¶ 6-7 (2001) (although the agency did not characterize the action as a RIF, an employee who was downgraded through restructuring that eliminated her job was subjected to a RIF); Holmes v. Dept. of Army, 41 MSPR 612 (1989) (citing 5 CFR 351.203); Di Biase v. Dept. of Energy, 15 MSPR 180, 182 (1983) (reorganization occurred when the supervisory level between the director and the bulk of staff no longer existed, the mission of the office was redefined and the number of employees in the office was reduced); Killingsworth v. DHHS, 11 MSPR 273, 275 (1982) (reorganization included the abolition of agency temporary or term excepted service positions and replacement with permanent, competitive service positions); Cummings v. DOD, 6 MSPR 202, 204, 6 MSPB 178 (1981) (reorganization encompassed a change in the appellant's position from a twelve-month status to a school-year status). An agency's decision to eliminate an employee's position because the function can be performed by others is a reorganization that justifies invoking RIF regulations. O'Connell v. DHHS, 21 MSPR 257, 260 (1984); Farmer v. Dept. of Transp., 13 MSPR 384, 387 (1982) (reorganization encompasses the abolition of two positions and the combination of their duties into a single new position).

Reorganization through planned elimination and redistribution of functions or duties is a legitimate basis for a RIF. Carson v. USDA, 21 MSPR 774 (1984) (Table). A realignment of work due to a change in workload conditions requiring a work force realignment describes a reorganization. Blanshan v. Dept. of Air Force, 23 MSPR 84, 86-87 (1984); see Sokol v. Dept. of Educ., 19 MSPR 466, 468 (1984) (reorganization included the redistribution of duties to implement a "team" concept for agency programs); Dante v. Nat'l Science Found., 16 MSPR 314, 316 (1983) (reorganization was established by the consolidation of branches, abolition of positions, and large number of reassignments designed to effectuate a change from a technological to functional management approach); Holbrook v. Dept. of Air Force, 4 MSPR 247, 249, 4 MSPB 322 (1980) (RIF was appropriate to downgrade a position when action was necessitated by a decrease in the number of employees serviced by the appellant's office).

A process of replacing one organization with another constitutes a reorganization. See Hanson v. USPS, 58 MSPR 413, 415 (1993). A RIF conducted to remedy an imbalance in skills and workload is within the ambit of a RIF conducted due to lack of work or for purposes of a reorganization. Bacon v. DHUD, 20 MSPR 408, 414-15 (1984), aff'd, Bacon v. DHUD, 757 F.2d 265 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (decision on appeal is discussed in the next section); see Horst v. DHHS (Fed. Cir. 1998 NP No. 98-3197) (affirming a RIF separation for lack of work of an individual properly placed in a one-person competitive level); Rutledge v. TVA, 19 MSPR 77, 79 (1984) (projected diminution of concrete production warranted RIF separating mixing plant foreman). Exercise of reemployment rights leading to an over complement of employees is a legitimate basis for a RIF under the governing OPM regulation. Coleman v. Dept. of Navy, 24 MSPR 426, 427-28 (1984).

A reorganization may affect just a single position. See Barton v. Dept. of Educ., 20 MSPR 451, 460 (1984); Caviness v. Dept. of Army, 12 MSPR 564 (1982) ("the fact that appellant was the only person affected as a result of a reorganization does not render the RIF invalid"); Killingsworth v. DHHS, 11 MSPR 273, 276 (1982) ("The fact that the appellant was the only person separated as a result of the RIF does not render the reorganization invalid."); Copeland v. Dept. of Army, 9 MSPR 348, 350 (1981) (RIF occurred through the planned management action of conversion of a nurse training position to a registered nurse position with increased duties: "such action clearly meets the regulatory definition of a reorganization, even though only one person was affected thereby"). An agency may rerun a RIF to correct an earlier RIF that improperly placed an employee at a level higher than that justified by the employee's circumstances. The late FPM provided this authority to agencies at Chapter 335, § A-4 (May 7, 1981). Martin v. Dept. of Education (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-811).

A reorganization is a planned event, but planning need not be by the book. In Crambert v. Dept. of Educ., 22 MSPR 628, 631-32 (1984), the Board upheld a RIF based on a reorganization even though that reorganization was not the product of a study that was apparently suggested by an agency report.
