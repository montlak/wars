7. Unfamiliarity With MSPB Procedures and Law; Ambiguous Agency Communications

Even for a short delay of three days, "[l]ack of experience with the Board's appeal process is not a valid excuse for allowing a clearly stated time limit to elapse." Bonefas v. FAA, 12 MSPR 65, 67 (1982); see Teesateskie v.Goellner v. DHS, SF-0752-16-0757-I-1 (NP 11/29/2022) ("Further, despite the appellant's pro se status and his professed inexperience with Board matters, it is well established that lack of familiarity with the Board's practices does not constitute good cause for waiver of its timeliness requirements, Mata v. Office of Personnel Management, 53 M.S.P.R. 552, 554-55, aff'd, 983 F.2d 1088 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table); see Caballero v. Department of the Army, 59 M.S.P.R. 298, 302 (1993) (finding that inexperience with legal matters and unfamiliarity with Board procedures do not warrant waiver of the Board's deadlines)."); Teesateskie v. Dept. of Interior, 87 MSPR 618, 621 ¶ 6 (2001) ("A general inability to understand instructions and procedures does not provide a basis for a waiver of the time limit for filing a petition for appeal."); Chacon v. FAA, 12 MSPR 112, 114 (1982) (same rule; an appeal one day late was untimely). If the record demonstrates notice was given of appeal rights, unsupported allegations that an appellant is unaware of the Board or adverse action procedures will not support waiver of an untimely appeal. Williams v. Dist. of Columbia Gov't, 2 MSPR 234, 235, 2 MSPB 397 (1980). A delayed appeal is not excused by the appellant's contention that he did not realize he had good grounds for an appeal. Solomon v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1994 NP No. 93-3565) ("[T]he failure to recognize that one possesses grounds to appeal does not constitute good cause for excusing an untimely filing of an appeal to the Board. See Duff v. Dept. of Army, 68 MSPR 146, 149 (1995) ("The chance receipt of information regarding the alleged reestablishment of the appellant's pre-RIF position, even acted upon promptly, is insufficient to excuse the appellant's failure to act at the time of the RIF to determine whether the RIF was personal to him."); see also Mozee v. USPS, 56 MSPR 602, 604-05 (1993); Pearce v. Dept. of Air Force, 52 MSPR 251, 253 (1992); Caney v. Dept. of Treasury, 48 MSPR 101, 103 (1991), aff'd, 956 F.2d 1172 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table); Hall v. Dept. of Navy, 44 MSPR 274, 276-77, aff'd, 914 F.2d 270 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (Table)."); cf. Slominske v. OPM, CH-0845-16-0335-I-1 (NP 9/21/2016) (delayed appeal from OPM overpayment determination: "OPM also provided the appellant with clear and straightforward instructions for filing an appeal with the Board. A general inability to understand instructions and procedures does not provide a basis for a waiver of the time limit for filing an appeal. Burgess v. U.S. Postal Service, 78 M.S.P.R. 484, ¶ 7 (1998); Holloway v. Office of Personnel Management, 54 M.S.P.R. 507, 509 (1992). If the appellant had difficulty in understanding the Board's appeal form, it would have been reasonable for him to contact the Board for assistance rather than OPM. Burgess, 78 M.S.P.R. 484, ¶ 7; Holloway, 54 M.S.P.R. at 510. His failure to do so weighs against a finding of due diligence or ordinary prudence. Burgess, 78 M.S.P.R. 484, ¶ 7.").

Unfamiliarity with Board procedures can support a waiver of the appeal period when coupled with unusual circumstances. See Beaird v. OPM, 9 MSPR 515, 517 (1982) (appellant's delay in filing an appeal from OPM's denial of a disability annuity was excused when the appellant explained that he expected the employing agency to permit him to return to work; the appeal was filed when the agency told him he would not be returned); cf. Stevens v. OPM, 11 MSPR 437, 439 (1982) (short delay was waived for an appellant who stated she did not appreciate the significance of the deadline requirement and that she had to consult with her doctor to get a medical opinion in support of the retirement appeal). Lack of sophistication, or confusion of an appellant, may be taken into account for waiving a late appeal, but the confusion should be related to an ambiguity in either the instructions the appellant received from the agency or in a Board procedure. Jones v. USPS, DC-0353-11-0501-B-1 (NP 3/18/2013) (appeal was untimely when the filing time was calculated from receipt by the appellant's representative of the agency's final EEO decision); cf. Swearingen v. Dept. of Transp., AT-0752-13-0167-I-1 (NP 7/25/2014) (Board waived a brief delay in an appeal when the agency wrote to the appellant to deny the appellant's request to extend a decision date and then included language that the appellant had 30 days to appeal from "this" action, phrasing the Board considered to be ambiguous).

Discovery of case law that could furnish the basis for a successful appeal will not excuse filing delays. [Refer to Chapter 5, "Ignorance of Law; Late Discovery of Legal Defense; Subsequent MSPB Decision in Another Case," for decisions considering whether discovery of new case law excuses a late PFR.] Wright v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 96-3119), sustained the Board's refusal to entertain a belated appeal from an OPM reconsideration decision when the appellant belatedly discovered a decision that could affect the outcome of the appeal:

The Board may waive the time limit for filing an appeal when good cause is shown. 5 CFR § 1201.22(c) (1994). The petitioner bears the burden of demonstrating such good cause. 5 CFR § 1201.56(a)(2)(ii) (1994). The decision regarding whether to waive the time limit for filing an appeal is within the discretion of the Board and this court may not substitute its own judgment for that of the Board. Mendoza v. MSPB, 966 F.2d 650, 653 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (in banc); Rowe v. MSPB, 802 F.2d 434, 437 (Fed. Cir. 1986). Thus, as Mrs. Wright concedes her appeal was untimely, our review in this case is limited to determining whether the Board's decision was arbitrary or capricious, an abuse of discretion or otherwise not in accordance with the law. See Mendoza, 966 F.2d at 653; Rowe, 802 F.2d at 437.

Applying this standard in view of her failure to argue legal error, we hold that the Board did not abuse its discretion because Peggy Wright has presented no excuse which the Board would have to accept as justifying her delay of over three years in filing her appeal. Peggy Wright admits she did not appeal within the allotted time because she had been unaware of any facts which could change the outcome. The only truly new fact presented by Peggy Wright, either to the AJ in her submission regarding timeliness, to the Board on her appeal of the AJ's dismissal or on appeal to this court, was her discovery of the Brush case while working in a library.[1] However, as we have recently held, the discovery of new legal arguments or the discovery of a precedential decision which may affect the outcome of an appeal does not, by itself, establish good cause for the untimely filing of a petition for review. Nelson v. Federal Deposit Ins. Corp., 83 F.3d 1375, 1376 (Fed. Cir. 1996). This rule applies even where, as here, the new decision issued after the due date of an appeal. See id. ("Even if Fox had issued between...the due date of Nelson's petition [and]...the date he actually filed it, its discovery by the petitioner cannot constitute good cause for untimeliness...."); see also Romero, 66 MSPR at 111-12; Acree v. USPS, 64 MSPR 566, 569 (1994). It is particularly applicable where the decision relied upon by a petitioner does not contain any hint that it was meant to be applied retroactively to alter the outcome of final decisions that were not timely appealed. Brush is such a decision.

[1] Peggy Wright also suggests that she talked to unidentified individuals at OPM who did not give her "any hope that there was anything that could be done." However, she fails to identify either the individuals she allegedly spoke to at OPM or the content of their discussions, other than the fact that OPM correctly informed her that Mr. Wright had failed to fill out the proper papers. In any event, Peggy Wright does not make an estoppel argument here, but rather relies solely on her recent discovery of the Brush decision.

Nor does an appellant's lack of awareness of Board laws or the procedure involved in adverse actions excuse a late appeal. Maxham v. Dept. of Navy, 6 MSPR 188, 189, 6 MSPB 166 (1981); see Small v. Dept. of Navy, 6 MSPR 366, 367, 6 MSPB 312 (1981) (declining to extend the time for a fee motion: "The fact that appellant did not read the material [Board regulations] provided him, standing alone, does not support a finding of good cause for waiving the regulation."). If confusion results through no fault of the appellant--as with the employee who reasonably believed that a suspension and removal would be treated together--there is good cause for delay in the appeal if the appellant filed a separate appeal after the region announced the two actions would be separately treated. Bascom v. Dept. of Transp., 20 MSPR 295, 298 (1984). But if the appellant is furnished with explicit advice concerning appeal rights in a removal notice or if the appellant is furnished with a copy of Board regulations with the removal notice, long (and short) delays are likely to be unforgiven. See Blue v. USPS, 65 MSPR 370, 377-78 (1994) (appellant's alleged confusion concerning the filing requirements was not excused; he earlier filed appeals on time; the agency's reissued notice of removal stated the time for filing the appeal and placed the appellant on notice of what to do to receive a Board hearing); Smith v. Dept. of Navy, 37 MSPR 560, 563-64 (1988). If the appellant is provided specific advice concerning the right to a Board appeal, substantial delay will not be excused if the appellant relies on a coworkers' bad advice. See Little v. GPO, 99 MSPR 292, 295-96 ¶ 9-11 (2005). [Refer later in this chapter to "Lack of Notice of Appeal Rights."]
