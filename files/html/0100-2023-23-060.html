7. Breach of Settlement: Election Between Rescission or Enforcement

Despite a few contrary implications noted in decisions discussed below, if one party breaches a settlement agreement, the other has the option of either enforcing the terms of the settlement or rescinding the settlement (through enforcement proceedings) and going forward with the original appeal. McClain v. USPS, 40 MSPR 66, 70 (1989); see Shaw v. Dept. of Air Force, 79 MSPR 446, 452 (1998) (unilateral error by the agency, even if not in bad faith, gives rise to the appellant's right of rescission; alternatively, the appellant may decide to accept the agreement without complete enforcement when the remedy specified in the agreement could not be accomplished); Esteves v. VA, 66 MSPR 586, 589 (1995) (rescission option is available even if agency's material breach is inadvertent); Betterly v. VA, 47 MSPR 63, 67 (1991) (remanding for an evaluation of whether the confidentiality provision of the agreement was breached by the agency: "If the agreement has been materially breached, the appellant's appeal should be reinstated."); Fuller v. USPS, 45 MSPR 611, 614 (1990) (remanding to determine whether the agency breached a nondisclosure provision). But see Hammond v. Dept. of Navy, 46 MSPR 313, 319 (1990) ("Because there was a mutual breach of the settlement agreement, and because the agency acted in good faith to correct its breach, we find that there is no additional relief that the Board can grant to the appellant[.]").

[Refer to Chapter 5, "Challenges to Settlement."]

The option to rescind an agreement for a material breach by the other party is ordinarily exercised through enforcement proceedings, Lenco v. USIA, 64 MSPR 187, 189 (1994):

When an administrative judge dismisses an appeal pursuant to a settlement agreement, and the appellant believes that the agreement is invalid because it was obtained by fraud or coercion, or because it was based on mutual mistake, he may file a petition for review of the initial decision dismissing his appeal. See Frizzell v. Department of the Air Force, 53 MSPR 413, 415 (1992); Strange v. US Postal Service, 48 MSPR 674, 676 (1991). If the employee is not challenging the validity of the agreement, however, but instead believes that the agency has failed to comply with the provisions therein, he may file a petition for enforcement with the regional or field office that issued the initial decision on his appeal. See 5 CFR § 1201.182(a). If the appellant substantiates his allegation that the agency has breached the agreement, he is entitled to either enforcement of the agreement or rescission of the agreement and reinstatement of his claim. See Murphy v. US Postal Service, 54 MSPR 202, 206 (1992).

In assessing an allegation of breach, the Board examines the terms of the agreement itself. An appellant's allegations of improprieties by the agency that do not concern the terms of the agreement will not invalidate the agreement. Smith v. VA, 47 MSPR 297, 303 (1991) ("[a]ppellant's allegations of agency error and his request for compensation for 'mental and emotional distress' do not concern matters included in the terms of the settlement agreement[.]"); Clay v. Dept. of Army, 47 MSPR 406, 408 (1991) (disputes concerning the appellant's allegations of prohibited discrimination are issues not involved in the settled appeal) (citing Colonel v. Dept. of Air Force, 38 MSPR 285, 291 (1988)).

Only material breaches of a settlement permit the nonbreaching party to avoid the agreement. Materiality was defined, Leeds v. USPS, 108 MSPR 113, 115-16 ¶ 4, 2008 MSPB 30 (2008):

A settlement agreement is a contract and the Board will adjudicate an enforcement proceeding relating to a settlement according to contract law. Greco v. Department of the Army, 852 F.2d 558, 560 (Fed. Cir. 1988). Pursuant to contract law, substantial performance by one party is sufficient to require the other party to perform, and a party's performance is considered substantial when there is no material failure of performance. Muzzipapa v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 63 M.S.P.R. 559, 562 (1994), citing Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 237, cmt. d (1981). The material failure determination depends upon the extent to which the injured party is deprived of the benefit which was reasonably expected from the breach. Id. citing Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 241 (1981). The party's breach is material when it relates to a matter of vital importance or goes to the essence of the contract. Thomas v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 124 F.3d 1439, 1442 (Fed. Cir. 1997). A material breach of a contractual promise by one party discharges the other party from his contractual duty to perform what was exchanged for the promise. Id. Mere delay in performance, however, will not necessarily establish a material breach sufficient to discharge the other party's obligation to perform. See Farrero v. National Aeronautics & Space Administration, 78 M.S.P.R. 382, 388 (1998), rev'd on other grounds, 194 F.3d 1328 (Fed. Cir. 1999) (Table) (a material breach by the appellant of his obligations under the settlement agreement could have precluded his obtaining enforcement of a provision of the agreement violated by the agency, but his delay in performance did not show such bad faith here as to deny him a remedy for the agency's breach).

If breach is established, the breaching party has no right belatedly to attempt to cure the problem; if there has been a material breach, the nonbreaching party may seek rescission of the agreement. See Mullins v. Dept. of Air Force, 79 MSPR 206, 210-12 ¶¶ 8-13 (1998) (violation of nondisclosure provisions of an agreement). If the agency breached, and the appellant moves to rescind the agreement through enforcement proceedings, the agency's later compliance does not retroactively negate the appellant's right to rescind the settlement. See Murphy v. USPS, 62 MSPR 206, 211 (1994). But see Williams v. DOJ, 66 MSPR 366, 368 (1995) (party cannot exercise the right to rescind the agreement once he has requested and obtained enforcement of the agreement) (relying on Holmes v. VA, 57 MSPR 115, 119 (1993)).

The right to rescind, if established, is the appellant's option if the agency is in breach. "[t]he agency's belief regarding what is best for the appellant is no basis for disallowing the appellant to exercise the rights provided to him by law and through the agreement." Reinert v. USDA, 74 MSPR 60, 64 (1997). If there is a mutual breach of the agreement and the agency acts in good faith to correct its breach, the Board is not likely to rescind the agreement. Hammond v. Dept. of Navy, 46 MSPR 313, 319 (1990).

To avoid the problems caused when one party or another (usually the appellant) seeks to rescind an agreement based upon a contract breach, some agreements are written with a provision that neither party will seek to set the agreement aside. This provision is usually sought by the agency when the settlement agreement calls on the appellant to resign or retire. Gonzales v. USPS, 93 MSPR 391, 393 ¶ 5 (2003), determined that an AJ correctly declined to consider setting an agreement aside when that agreement specified that neither party would seek to set it aside. If a settlement agreement provides that any breach results in reinstatement of the appeal, and if the appellant seeks enforcement of the specific provisions of the agreement, the Board pursues the enforcement route. See Soffer v. Dept. of Army, 44 MSPR 402, 406-07 n.5 (1990).

The Board ordinarily does not create solutions when the agreement is breached. The Board provides the appellant a choice of enforcing the agreement (if possible), waiving the breach, or rescinding the agreement and litigating the appeal. The Board will not select an alternative suggested by the appellant, although the parties may work out their differences and arrive at an agreed-upon alternative to the options identified in the underlying agreement. See Carson v. Dept. of Energy, 79 MSPR 502, 503-04 (1998) (when the agency did not create the dispute resolution mechanism called for in the agreement, the appellant could rescind the agreement, enforce the remaining terms of the agreement, or agree upon a substitute procedure). Although the Board ordinarily will not modify a settlement agreement upon an allegation of breach or lack of mutual assent, agreements have occasionally been modified. [Refer later in this chapter to "Modification of Settlement Provision."]

If a settlement agreement is set aside in its entirety, the agreement is considered void and the appeal of the action leading to the agreement should be reinstated. Fredendall v. VA, 38 MSPR 366, 373 (1988) (suggesting that to reinstate the underlying appeal on the basis of an invalid settlement agreement, the appellant should, on remand, file a motion with the regional office to vacate the agreement). Should the settlement agreement not be filed with the Board, the Board cannot rescind it, but the Board could invalidate withdrawal of the appeal if the appellant demonstrated that he was coerced into settling. Cranfield v. TVA, 44 MSPR 384, 387 (1990). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Unfiled Settlement Agreement; Preappeal Settlement."]

The Board was earlier reluctant to set aside agreements based on material breaches. The Board preferred a different route. Ortega v. Dept. of Interior, 35 MSPR 158, 161 (1987), determined that if the complaint was that the agency breached the agreement, the remedy would lie in enforcement proceedings; there was no suggestion of the possibility of abandonment of the agreement and continuation of the adverse action appeal. Reinstatement of the appeal was appropriate only if the agreement was invalid. Grube v. Dept. of Air Force, 41 MSPR 494, 498-99 (1989). When the employee sought to set aside a settlement, arguing that the agency had not complied with the agreement, the Board's course was to remand the case for determination of the compliance issues. Valentine v. Dept. of Air Force, 36 MSPR 507, 508-09 (1988); see Ferry v. Dept. of Army, 67 MSPR 229, 232 (1995) (appellant, confused about the process of addressing compliance problems, filed both an enforcement petition and a PFR from AJ's decision accepting the settlement; AJ dismissed the enforcement petition because a PFR was pending; Board denied the PFR and remanded appellant's arguments concerning agency noncompliance to region for adjudication as enforcement petition); Johnson v. FEMA, 41 MSPR 561, 567 (1989) (no basis for setting aside agreement despite allegation of noncompliance when there was no contention that the agreement was for fraud or mutual mistake); McNamee v. VA, 39 MSPR 530, 533-34 (1989) (Board would not set aside an agreement that was allegedly unfair without a demonstration that the agreement was invalidated by fraud or mutual mistake). But if the settlement agreement called for reinstatement of the appeal as a remedy for failure to comply, the Board could "enforce" the agreement by requiring the reinstatement of the appeal should the agency fail to abide by a compliance order following a finding of noncompliance. Graff v. Dept. of Air Force, 39 MSPR 639, 643 (1989). The wording had to be precise. If the agreement called for the agency (employer) to reinstate the appeal when the agency failed to abide by the agreement, the agreement did not require the Board to reinstate the appeal. Grube v. Dept. of Air Force, 41 MSPR 494, 498 (1989).
