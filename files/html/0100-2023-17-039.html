8. Transfer of Function

The transfer of function may involve relocating an activity from one agency to another or from one unit to another in the same agency. 5 USC 3503 provides the broad definition of a transfer of function:

Transfer of functions

(a) When a function is transferred from one agency to another, each competing employee in the function shall be transferred to the receiving agency for employment in a position for which he is qualified before the receiving agency may make an appointment from another source to that position.

(b) When one agency is replaced by another, each competing employee in the agency to be replaced shall be transferred to the replacing agency for employment in a position for which he is qualified before the replacing agency may make an appointment from another source to that position.

OPM regulations implement the statute. A transfer of function is defined at 5 CFR 351.203 (2022) to mean:

[T]he transfer of the performance of a continuing function from one competitive area and its addition to one or more other competitive areas, except when the function involved is virtually identical to functions already being performed in the other competitive area(s) affected; or the movement of the competitive area in which the function is performed to another commuting area.

Subsection (a) of Section 3503 requires that the receiving agency make the appointment of a qualified individual before the receiving agency appoints a person not from the transferring agency to the position. An implementing regulation, 5 CFR 351.302(a) (2022), provides:

Before a reduction in force is made in connection with the transfer of any or all of the functions of a competitive area to another continuing competitive area, each competing employee in a position identified with the transferring function or functions shall be transferred to the continuing competitive area without any change in the tenure of his or her employment.

See Biter v. ICC, 76 MSPR 82, 88-89, 94 (1997) (reviewing OPM regulations governing the identification of employees with transfer rights; noting that employees are identified for transfer based on their duties, not their duty stations).

There is no right to an appeal from a transfer of function per se; only an adverse action or RIF resulting from the transfer is appealable. See Biter v. ICC, 76 MSPR 82, 88 (1997) (transfer of function was not appealable, but the Board may review the transfer process if it directly results in an appealable RIF action). Because a transfer of function is one of several permissible reasons for a RIF, an appellant's separation may still be proper as a RIF even if the agency improperly characterizes the transaction as a transfer of function. See Hasler v. Dept. of Air Force, 48 MSPR 207, 213 (1991) (remanding for assessment of the appellant's assignment rights); cf. Qatsha v. Dept. of Navy, 73 MSPR 561, 563 (1997) (no jurisdiction over a transfer of function, per se; only an adverse action or RIF action resulting from a transfer is appealable).

Mullen v. Dept. of Navy, 76 MSPR 590, 596 (1997), explained appeal rights arising from a transfer of function:

A "function" is "all or a clearly identifiable segment of an agency's mission (including all integral parts of that mission), regardless of how it is performed." 5 CFR § 351.203. A "transfer of function," in relevant part, is "the transfer of the performance of a continuing function from one competitive area and its addition to one or more other competitive areas, except when the function involved is virtually identical to functions already being performed in the other competitive area(s) affected." Id.; see, e.g., Blevins v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 46 MSPR 239, 242-43 (1990). A TOF per se is not appealable, but whether the agency afforded an employee his transfer rights is within the scope of a RIF appeal. Jackson v. National Transportation Safety Board, 18 MSPR 626, 628 (1984).

If the Board has jurisdiction over an appeal submitted by an employee adversely affected by a transfer of function, it has jurisdiction to examine the transfer of function process applied to the employee. Jackson v. NTSB, 18 MSPR 626, 628 (1984) (employee was improperly transferred; the Board directed the transferor agency to rescind the transfer and reinstate the employee to the original position).

A function is a clearly identifiable activity of the agency that consists of substantial authorities, powers, and duties authorized by law combining to form a segment of the agency's mission. Whitehurst v. TVA, 43 MSPR 486, 491 (1990). A transfer of function occurs if the work of one or more employees moves from one competitive area to another, or if the competitive area performing the function is moved to another commuting area. See 5 CFR 353.301 (2022), et seq.; Feldman v. Herter, 276 F.2d 485 (D.C. Cir. 1960) (transfer of a function occurred when the old agency was liquidated and a new agency was created within a different government department to perform the same function); Casman v. Dulles, 129 F. Supp. 428 (D.D.C. 1955) (same principle). Reorganizations that shift functions out of one competitive area and into another are transfers of functions. Seidel v. USDA, 22 MSPR 293, 295 (1984) (relying on FPM Ch. 351-S-3; Att. to FPM Letter 351-14(17)), followed, Hasler v. Dept. of Air Force, 48 MSPR 207, 211 (1991) (broadening of an activity's scope of authority was not a transfer of function). Employees are identified for transfer based on their duties, not on their duty stations. Biter v. ICC, 76 MSPR 82, 94 (1997).

A transfer of function occurs when the function is transferred to another competitive area and the gaining area undertakes a class of activity it did not previously perform, Kentner v. NTSB, 20 MSPR 595, 597-98 (1984):

[W]e find no merit to appellants' contention that because the agency's Chicago and Atlanta Field Offices performed investigations only for separate specified geographical areas a transfer of function occurred within the context of this case. It has been held that "[i]dentity of function does not require identity of work." See Childress v. United States, 650 F.2d 285, 222 Ct. Cl. 557, 558 (1980). When a function is transferred to another competitive area, the gaining competitive area 'undertakers a class of activity it did not have before." Id. Here, the Atlanta Field Office had previously performed railroad accident investigation functions, albeit in a different geographical area. Thus, when it was assigned railroad accident investigation responsibilities for the geographical area formerly held by the Chicago Field Office, it was simply assuming another part of a single function, i.e., railroad accident investigations, and was not acquiring a separate and independent agency function. See McNamara v. Dick, 323 F.2d 276, 281 (D.C. Cir. 1963). Thus, we find that no transfer of function occurred and that appellants were therefore not entitled to transfer of function rights under RIF regulations. See Prince v. Department of Transportation, 10 MSPB 505 at 506 (1982).

Alternatively, a transfer of function occurs if a function ceases in one competitive area and reappears in an identifiable form in another competitive area. O'Connell v. DHHS, 21 MSPR 257, 261-62 (1984); see Metivier v. Dept. of Interior, CH-0351-14-0772-I-1 (NP 12/21/2016) ("No transfer of function occurred in this case because the agency's Salt Lake City office was already performing an adjudication function, including processing WELSA cases. [S] ee Mullen v. Department of the Navy, 76 M.S.P.R. 590, 597 (1997)."); Dowd v. Dept. of Energy, 21 MSPR 312, 315 (1984) ("[A]ppellants' function did not reappear in another competitive area because their audit work, as the agency had defined it, no longer existed."); Prince v. Dept. of Transp., 11 MSPR 584, 586 (1982) (no transfer of function occurred when the appellant's work was already being performed in the allegedly new competitive area). Under 5 CFR 351.302(c) (2022), an employee has the right to transfer with his or her function if the alternative in the competitive area losing the function is separation or demotion. Seidel, supra. If the transfer of function causes the employee to move to another office involved in a RIF, the employee competes in the competitive area of the gaining office. Demoret v. CPSC, 24 MSPR 466, 467-68 (1984) (AJ incorrectly required the agency to offer the appellant a vacancy in losing competitive area during a transfer of function); see Colbert v. Dept. of Army, 54 MSPR 492, 496 (1992) ("In a transfer of function, if the gaining area does not have positions available for all affected employees, selections are made based on a retention list made up of employees of the gaining area and the incoming employees.").

The transfer of a function is an event of some formality, not to be implied, Caviness v. Dept. of Army, 39 MSPR 548, 553 (1989):

A transfer of function is defined as the movement of work from one competitive area to another. See 5 CFR 351.301. A function is a clearly identifiable activity of the agency which consists of substantial authorities, powers, and duties authorized by law which combine to form a segment of the agency's mission. See Former Community Services Administration Employees v. Department of Health and Human Services, 21 MSPR 379, 383 (1984), aff'd, 762 F.2d 978 (Fed. Cir. 1985). In addition, there must be a quantitative or qualitative method of identifying the activity as a function to ensure traceability when the function is transferred from one entity to another. Id. The agency's enabling legislation, organization manuals, and delegations of authority are the most significant sources of identifying functions. Id.

Blevins v. TVA, 46 MSPR 239, 242-43 (1990), added:

A "transfer of function" is defined as the movement of work from one competitive area to another. 5 CFR §§ 351.203, 351.301. See Caviness v. Department of the Army, 39 MSPR 548, 553 (1989). A "function" is a clearly identifiable activity of the agency which consists of substantial authorities, powers, and duties authorized by law which combine to form a segment of the agency's mission. 5 CFR § 351.203. See Former Community Services Administration Employees v. Department of Health and Human Services, 21 MSPR 379, 383 (1984), aff'd, 762 F.2d 978 (Fed. Cir. 1985). A transfer of function occurs only when a function ceases in one competitive area and reappears in identifiable form in another competitive area. See O'Connell v. Department of Health and Human Services, 21 MSPR 257, 262 (1984). See also Walsh v. Environmental Protection Agency, 25 MSPR 460, 465 (1984) (a transfer of function occurs when "functions," as opposed to "duties," are moved from one competitive area to another); Seidel v. Department of Agriculture, 22 MSPR 293, 295 (1984) (a transfer of function occurs when a reorganization shifts functions out of one competitive area and into another). A transfer of function does not occur merely because an agency decides that the duties of one position could be more efficiently performed as part of another position. See O'Connell, 21 MSPR at 262. Further, no transfer of function occurs when the function involved is virtually identical to functions already being performed in another competitive area. 5 CFR § 351.203. Rather, a function is transferred only when the gaining competitive area undertakes a class of activity that it did not previously perform. See Hayes v. Department of Health and Human Services, 829 F.2d 1092, 1096 (Fed. Cir. 1987).

But see Seidel v. USDA, 22 MSPR 293, 295-96 (1984) (analysis of historical shifts in work may be required to determine if a RIF resulted from an earlier transfer of function that was not so characterized).

If two different offices perform identical functions and one has a decrease in workload calling for a reduction in force, while the second in a different geographical location has an increase in workload calling for new hiring, no transfer of function occurs. If there is no transfer of function, the appellant has no right to follow the work to the gaining office that will continue with the work. See Neilson v. Fed. Highway Admin., 21 MSPR 178, 180 (1984) (when function is transferred to another competitive area, the gaining area undertakes a class of activity it did not have before (citing Childress v. United States, 222 Ct. Cl. 557, 558 (1980))). A transfer of case work to another office doing the same type of cases is not a transfer of function. Davidson v. Dept. of Energy, 22 MSPR 531, 537 (1984); see Hayes v. DHHS, 829 F.2d 1092, 1096 (Fed. Cir. 1987) ("[a]n employee has no right to transfer with his function if the gaining office already performs the function and simply adds the employee's duties."). Seidel v. USDA, 26 MSPR 605, 606-07 (1985), summarized the decisions:

The presiding official's finding on remand that no transfer of function occurred when the agency transferred the supervision function for the states of Georgia, North Carolina, and South Carolina from the Savannah (Georgia) Sub-office to the Mobile (Alabama) Field Office is correct. As the presiding official reasoned, the supervision function was at all times performed either in Mobile or in both Savannah and Mobile. When Savannah relinquished responsibility for supervision of Georgia, North Carolina and South Carolina, and Mobile resumed full responsibility for those states in addition to the ones already covered, no transfer of function occurred because Mobile had performed the Supervision function in some form all along. The function in question is supervision for a specified geographic area. The Board has held that, where an agency realigns geographic boundaries so that an installation already performing a particular function begins to handle that function for a wider area, no transfer of function occurs. The receiving installation merely assumes responsibility for another part of a single function. See Childress v. United States, 650 F.2d 285, 222 Ct. Cl. 557, 558 (1980). See also Neilson v. Federal Highway Administration, 21 MSPR 178 (1984); Kentner v. National Transportation Safety Board, 20 MSPR 595 (1984).

Broadening of an activity's scope of authority is not a transfer of function. See Hasler v. Dept. of Air Force, 48 MSPR 207 (1991). There is no transfer of function if the new office already has the function but picks up the appellant's duties. Walsh v. EPA, 25 MSPR 460, 464-65 (1984). A transfer of function does not occur only because an agency decides that the duties of one position could be more efficiently performed as part of another position. Blevins v. TVA, 46 MSPR 239, 243 (1990) (citing O'Connell v. DHHS, 21 MSPR 257, 262 (1984)). If an agency realigns geographic boundaries so that an installation already performing a particular function begins to handle that function for a wider area, no transfer of function occurs. The receiving installation assumes responsibility for another part of the single function. See Seidel v. USDA, 22 MSPR 293 (1984).

As to the movement of an activity within an agency, the concept of transfer of function was described by McLean v. Dept. of Army, 55 MSPR 414, 417-18 (1992):

Under 5 CFR 351.301, a TOF occurs when the work of one or more employees is moved from one competitive area to another. A competitive area may consist of all or part of an agency. The minimum competitive area in the departmental service is a bureau, major command, directorate or other equivalent major subdivision of an agency within the local commuting area. In the field, the minimum competitive area is an activity under separate administration within the local commuting area. A competitive area must be defined solely in terms of an agency's organizational units and geographical location, and it must include all employees within its limits. See 5 CFR 351.402(b). In this case, the administrative judge determined that the transfer of the career development function within Fort Lee was a TOF. She did not, however, specifically find that the career development function was moved from one competitive area to another.

Our review of the record reveals no basis to conclude that the career development function was transferred from one competitive area to another.... [A]t the time that the career development function was moved, both DIT and DET were located at Fort Lee, were directorates within the Army Logistics Management College, and were within the Army Material Command. Thus, the administrative judge erred in finding that the 1990 transfer of the career development function from DIT to DET was a TOF. Further, because that transfer was not a TOF, the agency was not obligated to follow TOF procedures, and thus the appellant has shown no harmful error.

A transfer of function need not entail a geographical relocation. Lee v. Dept. of Interior, 6 MSPR 669, 670, 6 MSPB 561 (1981) ("[t]here is no requirement that a function physically move, and it is proper for an agency to have more than one competitive area within one geographical area.").

An agency has the discretion to plan both a RIF and a transfer of function and to implement the RIF first. Kurtz v. DHHS (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 85-998); see Holcomb v. United States, 146 F. Supp. 224 (Ct. Cl. 1956) (RIF preceding a transfer of function was permissible). However, an agency need not conduct a RIF before transferring a function to another agency, even though the RIF might better protect employees' assignment rights, Cross v. Dept. of Transp., 127 F.3d 1443, 1448 (Fed. Cir. 1997):

...According to Petitioners, when a RIF is conducted in conjunction with a transfer of function, the RIF should be conducted first, followed by the transfer. Otherwise, petitioners argue, civil service employees who would have superior retention rights during a RIF are given less favorable treatment than employees holding positions covered by the transferred functions, thus contravening the principles behind the merit system.

We disagree. Of course, once a RIF is initiated, it must be conducted pursuant to the procedures outlined in the statutes and regulations. Such procedures are intended to protect the civil service retention rights or preferences of competing employees. On the other hand, an employee occupying a position that is not being transferred to another agency does not have the right to "bump" an employee who is being transferred. Contrary to Petitioners' contentions, the regulations provide that when a RIF is conducted in conjunction with a transfer of function, the transfer of function should occur first, followed by the RIF. The pertinent regulation states:

Before a reduction in force is made in connection with the transfer of any or all of the functions of a competitive area to another continuing competitive area, each competing employee in a position identified with the transferring function or functions shall be transferred to the continuing competitive area without any change in the tenure of his or her employment.

5 CFR 351.302(a) (1997). Therefore, once the agency determined that it would conduct a RIF at the time of the agency's demise, a determination that was within the agency's broad discretion, see Gandola, 773 F.2d at 313, it was compelled by the regulation to transfer those employees whose positions were being transferred before conducting the RIF.

The Federal Circuit discussed the structure of transfers of function between federal agencies in Certain Former CSA Employees v. DHHS, 762 F.2d 978 (Fed. Cir. 1985). After determining that transfer rights were not extended to some employees by the receiving agency, the court decided that the Board correctly ordered individual assessment of transfer rights rather than ordering cancelation of the separations and awards of back pay for all employees who were separated without full compliance with transfer of function regulations. CSA Employees rejected the argument that failure to follow regulations automatically invalidated the separations of the employees, affirming instead the Board's approach of ordering remedial action only if the violation of regulations actually resulted in loss of substantive job rights in the transferee agency. The CSA Employees case continued, on remand and PFR, sub nom. Belton v. DHHS, 31 MSPR 414 (1986), discussing the specifics of assignment rights of various employees. See also Gearan v. DHHS, 860 F.2d 426 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (concerning a remedy); Hayes v. DHHS, 829 F.2d 1092 (Fed. Cir. 1987); Ahlberg v. DHHS, 804 F.2d 1238 (Fed. Cir. 1986); Menoken v. DHHS, 784 F.2d 365 (Fed. Cir. 1986), cert. denied, 107 S. Ct. 273 (1986); Putnam v. DHHS, 31 MSPR 427, 430 (1986) ("[t]he agency was required to utilize the method of assignment it had adopted--unless it could demonstrate that not doing so would not abridge the rights of another employee who may challenge the result--and the appellant should have been offered the position to which he had established superior entitlement under the mandate of the Board's court-approved remand decision. We can discern no basis for finding that the appellant should be required, at the hearing or in his petition for review, to rebut arguments and evidence that neither the agency not competing employees ever advanced, either expressly or by implication.").
