1. Prima Facie Case

The burden of establishing a prima facie case of discrimination is on the appellant. Polite v. Dept. of Navy, 3 MSPR 410, 412, 3 MSPB 486 (1980). That burden, summarized in Thomas v. OPM, 47 MSPR 369, 373 (1991), requires showing that (1) the appellant is a member of a protected class; (2) she was the object of an adverse employment action; and (3) there was a causal relationship between membership in the protected class and the adverse action that she suffered. The Board restated the analysis in Miller v. OPM, 59 MSPR 539, 545 (1993) (retirement appeal), to include a burden to prove the following factors to show disparate treatment: that the appellant is a member of a protected group; that she was similarly situated to an individual who was not a member of the protected group; that she was treated more harshly or disparately than the individual who was not a member of her protected group; and that the difference in treatment was based on an intent to discriminate. See Marbray v. Dept. of Navy, 25 MSPR 224, 225 n.2 (1984) ("We conclude that the appellant established a prima facie because he produced evidence that he is a member of a minority group (black) and he was treated differently for the same offense when compared to the treatment given a white supervisor. See Metcalf v. Omaha Steel Castings Co., 507 F. Supp. 679, 25 FEP 16 (D.C. Neb. 1981). Otis v. Inland Container Corp., 25 FEP 1280 (N.D. Ill. 1981)."). Applying the McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792 (1973), formulation to allegations of substandard performance, under Baqir v. Principi, 434 F.3d 733, 742 (4th Cir. 2006), a prima facie case may be established by the plaintiff's showing that (1) he is a member of a protected class, (2) he suffered an adverse employment action (such as discharge), (3) he was performing his job duties at a level that met the employer's legitimate expectations at the time of the adverse employment action, and (4) his position remained open or was filled by a similarly qualified applicant outside the protected class.

Although "a prima facie case of discrimination varies depending on the circumstances, and an appellant's allegations should be broadly and liberally construed, particularly if the appellant is pro se," Crawford v. USPS, 70 MSPR 416, 424 (1996), the mere assertion of discrimination is insufficient to constitute a prima facie case of discrimination. See Banez v. DOD, 69 MSPR 642, 649 (1996); Mascarenas v. DOD, 54 MSPR 303, 308 (1992) (restating the analytical approach); Brown v. Dept. of Air Force, 67 MSPR 500, 508 (1995) ("The administrative judge found that the appellant's mere assertion of race discrimination was insufficient to constitute a prima facie case of discrimination."). The appellant who raises a defense of mental disability discrimination must go beyond the bare allegation of discrimination in the appeal if he is to establish a prima facie case. Graham v. Dept. of Army, 41 MSPR 347, 348 (1989); see Romero v. EEOC, 55 MSPR 527, 539 (1992) ("It is well-settled that an employee's mere assertion that he was treated differently from other employees, and that he believed it was due to discrimination, is insufficient to constitute a prima facie case of such discrimination."); Gamble v. VA, 31 MSPR 649, 653 (1986) (a bare allegation was insufficient to establish a prima facie case that action against the appellant was the result of "prejudice, discrimination and a plot against the last old employees in the file room"); Montgomery v. VA, 28 MSPR 386, 388 (1985) ("The appellant has not, however, submitted evidence beyond his bare allegation to support his claim of discrimination, and such a bare allegation is insufficient to make out a prima facie case of handicap discrimination.").

An employee's own opinions, without more, are insufficient to establish a prima facie case of discrimination. Mackey v. Shalala, 360 F.3d 463, 469-70 (4th Cir. 2004). Snell v. USPS, 82 MSPR 674, 676-77 ¶¶ 6-8 (1999), considered a conclusory discrimination claim:

In his petition for appeal, the appellant stated that he was "replaced with a young female." He submitted no further evidence or argument either before or at the hearing about this employee, and he did not submit evidence or argument regarding any other employees. The only testimony given by the appellant at the hearing on his age discrimination claim was his statement that he was born on March 23, 1946.

An appellant who claims discrimination based on disparate treatment must establish that: (1) He is a member of a protected class; (2) he was similarly situated to an individual who was not a member of his protected group; and (3) he was treated more harshly or disparately than the individual who was not a member of his protected group. Farmer v. U.S. Postal Service, 80 MSPR 205 ¶ 16 (1998). Where, as here, the record is fully developed, the inquiry shifts from whether the appellant made a prima facie case of discrimination to whether he met his ultimate burden of proving his affirmative defense. Jackson v. U.S. Postal Service, 79 MSPR 46, 53 (1998).

Given his undisputed date of birth, the appellant has shown that he is a member of a protected class (over age 40). 29 USC § 631. The appellant, however, failed to show that he was similarly situated to any comparison employee or that he was treated more harshly than any such employee. Thus, we find that he did not prove his affirmative defense of age discrimination based on disparate treatment. See Farmer, 80 MSPR at 212 ¶ 17.

Beamon v. Dept. of Labor, 41 MSPR 525, 537 (1989), summarized the elements of the prima facie case:

When an agency establishes a legitimate nondiscriminatory reason for its personnel action by meeting its burden of proof on the merits, the issue of discrimination may be resolved by determining whether the appellant has shown that the agency's asserted reason for the action was not the true reason but merely pretext for discrimination.... An employee may meet this burden by showing that a discriminatory reason, more likely than not, motivated the employer or that the employer's explanation was unworthy of credence....

King v. USDA, AT-0752-18-0079-I-1 (NP 10/20/2022), commented on analysis of discrimination and reprisal defenses:

When an appellant asserts an affirmative defense of discrimination or retaliation under 42 U.S.C. § 2000e-16, the Board first will inquire whether, considering the evidence as a whole, the appellant has shown by preponderant evidence that the prohibited consideration was a motivating factor in the contested personnel action. Gardner v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 123 M.S.P.R. 647, ¶ 28 (2016), clarified by Pridgen v. Office of Management & Budget, 2022 MSPB 31, ¶¶ 23-24; Savage v. Department of the Army, 122 M.S.P.R. 612, ¶ 41 (2015), overruled in part by Pridgen, 2022 MSPB 31, ¶¶ 23-25. Such a showing is sufficient to establish that the agency violated 42 U.S.C. § 2000e-16, thereby committing a prohibited personnel practice under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(1). Savage, 122 M.S.P.R. 612, ¶ 51.

There must be a causal relationship between membership in the protected class and the adverse action to establish a prima facie case of discrimination. Faust v. Smithsonian Inst., 29 MSPR 496, 499 (1985). The McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792 (1973), analysis is followed if there is no direct evidence of discrimination. See Hazen Paper Co. v. Higgins, 113 S. Ct. 1701 (1993). Once a prima facie case of discrimination is established, the agency's stated reason is examined; if the reason is unpersuasive, the result will be reversal of the discharge as discriminatory and, in a willful case, a finding of a prohibited personnel practice under 5 USC 2302(b). Mahurin v. Dept. of Air Force, 4 MSPR 302, 305-06, 4 MSPB 372 (1980).

A prima facie case may be established by inference. To establish the prima facie case, the employee need only raise an inference that some factor other than the one given was involved in the decision to take disciplinary action. Once the employer provides an explanation, the employee attempts to prove that the explanation is a pretext. Evidence of pretext may include facts concerning the employer's treatment of the employee and the employer's general policy relative to minority employment. Person v. USDA, 5 MSPR 1, 3-4, 5 MSPB 85 (1981). But see Pace v. VA, 12 MSPR 259, 261 (1982) ("[A]lthough evidence may support the proposition that there was racial tension in appellant's department, unsubstantiated opinions do not establish that the employer's general policy with respect to minority employment was such that the agency's reasons for removing appellant were pretextual."). An appellant establishes a prima facie case, subject to rebuttal by the agency, by proof that he is African-American, that he was qualified for his position, that he was removed from his position, and that his position was filled by another employee. O'Reilly v. CSA, 16 MSPR 44, 47 (1983); see Haney v. USPS, 12 MSPR 135, 138 (1982) (accepting an EEOC determination of religious discrimination arising out of the failure of the agency to accommodate the appellant by shifting his schedule so he did not have to work on Saturdays). The failure to provide a preference under an affirmative action plan does not appear to be supportive of an allegation of discrimination in an employment decision. See Motley v. TVA, 43 MSPR 1, 2 (1989) (citing Liao v. TVA, 867 F.2d 1366 (11th Cir. 1989)). "The supervisor's insistence on having the tasks completed in the manner he desired does not establish a prima facie case of discrimination." Davies v. USDA, 5 MSPR 251, 256, 5 MSPB 291 (1981).

The prima facie case does not require identification of a comparator employee. Any evidence suggesting discriminatory motive will establish the basic prima facie case, according to Davis v. Dept. of Interior, 114 MSPR 527, 530-31 ¶¶ 6-8, 2010 MSPB 161 (2010) (adverse action reversed and case remanded for assessment of compensatory damages following EEOC review, Davis v. Dept. of Interior, AT-0752-09-0860-E-1 (NP 8/15/2014)):

In an Order dated August 19, 2009, the administrative judge directed the appellant to identify the factual bases for her discrimination claims and identified the following elements as defining her prima facie burden of proof:

(a) that the appellant is a member of a protected group; (b) that she was similarly situated to an individual who was not a member of the protected group; and (c) that she was treated more harshly or disparately than the individual who was not a member of his [sic] protected group.

However, as discussed below, comparator evidence is not essential to establish a prima facie case of disparate treatment discrimination.

In the absence of direct evidence[2] of discrimination, the Board typically uses the "pretext standard" of analysis set forth in McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792, 802-04 (1973). Under this framework, an employee may establish a prima facie case of prohibited discrimination by introducing evidence to show that she (1) is a member of a protected group; (2) suffered an appealable adverse employment action; and (3) that the unfavorable action gives rise to the inference of discrimination. 411 U.S. at 802. As to the third element, an employee may rely on any evidence giving rise to an inference that the unfavorable treatment at issue was due to illegal discrimination. See, e.g., Chappell-Johnson v. Powell, 440 F.3d 484, 488-89 (D.C. Cir. 2006); Stella v. Martin, 284 F.3d 135, 145 (D.C. Cir. 2002) (no requirement in a failure to hire case that the plaintiff show that the employer filled the position with a person outside the protected class) In light of the foregoing, we find that the administrative judge erred in requiring the appellant to demonstrate that she was treated more harshly than a similarly situated individual who was not a member of her protected class and in summarily dismissing her discrimination claims prior to a hearing when she failed to do so. Thus, a prima facie case of disparate treatment discrimination can be established by any proof of actions taken by the employer that shows a "discriminatory animus," where "in the absence of any other explanation it is more likely than not that those actions were bottomed on impermissible considerations." Schoenfeld, 168 F.3d at 1268.

[2] Direct evidence may be any statement made by an employer that: (1) reflects directly the alleged discriminatory attitude; and (2) bears directly on the contested employment discrimination. Arredondo v. U.S. Postal Service, 85 M.S.P.R. 113, ¶ 13 (2000). However, such evidence is composed of "only the most blatant remarks, whose intent could be nothing other than to discriminate" on the basis of some impermissible factor. Schoenfeld v. Babbitt, 168 F.3d 1257, 1266 (11th Cir. 1999) (quoting Carter v. City of Miami, 870 F.2d 578, 582 (11th Cir. 1989)). If an alleged discriminatory statement at best merely suggests a discriminatory motive, then it is only circumstantial evidence. Id.

If an appellant meets her prima facie burden, the burden of production shifts to the employer to "articulate some legitimate nondiscriminatory reason" for its action. McDonnell Douglas, 411 U.S. at 802. If the employer does so, the appellant must then be given an opportunity to demonstrate that, based on all of the evidence, the stated reason is a pretext and that the action was taken for a discriminatory reason. Id; see also Holcomb v. Powell, 433 F.3d 889, 897 (D.C. Cir. 2006). With regard to this ultimate burden, an appellant can rely on "any combination of (1) evidence establishing the plaintiff's prima facie case; (2) evidence the plaintiff presents to attack the employer's proffered explanation for its action; and (3) any further evidence of discrimination that may be available to the plaintiff, such as independent evidence of discriminatory statements or attitudes on the part of the employer." Holcomb, 433 F.3d at 897 (citing Aka v. Washington Hospital Center, 156 F.3d 1284, 1289 (D.C. Cir. 1998)) (en banc).

Stewart v. USPS, 9 MSPR 71, 74 n.1 (1981), explained the application of shifting burdens of proof under Texas Dept. of Community Affairs v. Burdine, 450 U.S. 248, 101 S. Ct. 1089 (1981), in adverse action cases:

The order and allocation of proof on discrimination issues is well settled. First the employee has the burden of proving a prima facie case of discrimination by a preponderance of the evidence. If the employee succeeds, the burden shifts to the agency to articulate a legitimate, non-discriminatory reason for the contested action. If the agency meets its burden, the employee then has the opportunity to prove by a preponderance of the evidence that the agency's articulated reasons are a pretext for discrimination. See McDonnell Douglas v. Green, 411 U.S. 792, 93 S. Ct. 1817, 36 L. Ed.2d 668 (1973). Issues of discrimination may also be analyzed in these terms, but it is not necessary to follow this order of analysis in all cases. For example, in adverse action cases, such as this one, the Board typically decides whether the preponderance of the evidence supports the charges before considering issues or prohibited personnel practices. See 5 USC § 7701(c). Therefore, before the issue of discrimination is analyzed, the Board has already determined whether the agency has articulated a legitimate, non-discriminatory reason for its action. In cases where the agency has met its burden, it is generally unnecessary to make a finding on the existence of a prima facie case. Under these circumstances, the issue may be resolved by determining whether the appellant has shown pretext.

In actions before the Board, the agency is required to prove by the preponderance of evidence the reasons for appellant's removal before the issue of discrimination is reached. A 1989 decision, Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins, 490 U.S. 228, 239, 242-44, 252, 109 S. Ct. 1775 (1989), defined the proper analysis of a mixed motive EEO case, that is, a case involving an action triggered by discriminatory and legitimate motivations. The case involved the failure of an accounting firm to promote or select an accountant for partnership in the firm. The plaintiff alleged sex discrimination. The Court declared, condemning sex stereotyping during the hiring process:

In passing Title VII, Congress made the simple but momentous announcement that sex, race, religion, and national origin are not relevant to the selection, evaluation, or compensation of employees. Yet, the statute does not purport to limit the other qualities and characteristics that employers may take into account in making employment decisions....

...

...The critical inquiry...is whether gender was a factor in the employment decision at the moment it was made.... Title VII [was] meant to condemn even those decisions based on a mixture of legitimate and illegitimate considerations....

...

...It is difficult for us to imagine that, in the simple words "because of," Congress meant to obligate a plaintiff to identify the precise causal role played by legitimate and illegitimate motivations in the employment decision she challenges. We conclude, instead, that Congress meant to obligate her to prove that the employer relied upon sex-based considerations in coming to its decision.

...

To say that an employer may not take gender into account is not, however, the end of the matter, for that describes only one aspect of Title VII. The other important aspect of the statute is its preservation of an employer's remaining freedom of choice. We conclude that the preservation of this freedom means that an employer shall not be liable if it can prove that, even if it had not taken gender into account, it would have come to the same decision regarding a particular person....

...

[O]nce a plaintiff in a Title VII case shows that gender played a motivating part in an employment decision, the defendant may avoid a finding of liability only by proving that it would have made the same decision even if it had not allowed gender to play such a role....

...

As to the employer's proof, in most cases, the employer should be able to present some objective evidence as to its probable decision in the absence of an impermissible motive.... An employer may not...prevail in a mixed-motive case by offering a legitimate and sufficient reason for its decision if that reason did not motivate it at the time of the decision. Finally, an employer may not meet its burden in such a case by merely showing that at the time of the decision it was motivated only in part by a legitimate reason. The very premise of a mixed-motives case is that a legitimate reason was present.... The employer instead must show that its legitimate reason, standing alone, would have induced it to make the same decision.

The courts below held that an employer who has allowed a discriminatory impulse to play a motivating part in an employment decision must prove by clear and convincing evidence that it would have made the same decision in the absence of discrimination. We are persuaded that the better rule is that the employer must make this showing by a preponderance of the evidence.

The distinction between a mixed motive case depending on direct evidence of discrimination and a case based on circumstantial evidence leading, if unrebutted, to an inference of discrimination, was described in Haddon v. Executive Residence at the White House, 313 F.3d 1352, 1357-59 (Fed. Cir. 2002):

Mr. Haddon alleges that the Executive Residence discharged him and subsequently declined to rehire him in retaliation for his filing of an EEO complaint. He claims that the AJ erroneously evaluated this issue under the prima facie case/burden shifting approach set forth in McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Greene, 411 U.S. 792, 93 S. Ct. 1817, 36 L. Ed.2d 668 (1973), rather than the standard for mixed motive cases set forth in Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins, 490 U.S. 228, 109 S. Ct. 1775, 104 L. Ed.2d 268 (1989), and that the EEOC erred by failing to address this issue. Mr. Haddon argues that he presented "direct evidence of retaliation" and thus the mixed motive approach rather than the prima facie case approach was required. See Trans World Airlines, Inc. v. Thurston, 469 U.S. 111, 121, 105 S. Ct. 613, 83 L. Ed.2d 523 (1985) (stating that "the McDonnell Douglas test is inapplicable where the plaintiff presents direct evidence of discrimination" (citing Teamsters v. United States, 431 U.S. 324, 358 n.44, 97 S. Ct. 1843, 52 L. Ed.2d 396 (1977))).

In Price Waterhouse, the Supreme Court held that Title VII was "meant to condemn even those decisions based on a mixture of legitimate and illegitimate considerations." 490 U.S. at 241, 109 S. Ct. 1775. The Court further held, however, that in mixed motive cases "an employer shall not be liable if it can prove that, even if it had not taken [the prohibited factor] into account, it would have come to the same decision regarding a particular person." Id. at 242, 109 S. Ct. 1775. Different courts have taken various approaches to the question of how much evidence a plaintiff is required to submit before he is entitled to rely on Price Waterhouse. The Second Circuit has stated that a "plaintiff will be entitled to a burden-shifting instruction on the Price Waterhouse defense where the evidence is sufficient to allow the trier to find both forbidden and permissible motives." Rose v. New York City Bd. of Educ., 257 F.3d 156, 161 (2d Cir. 2001) (quoting Ostrowski v. Atlantic Mut. Ins. Cos., 968 F.2d 171, 181 (2d Cir. 1992)). Other courts have set out more stringent tests for direct evidence. In Damon v. Fleming Supermarkets of Florida Inc., 196 F.3d 1354, 1358-59 (11th Cir. 1999), the Eleventh Circuit stated that direct evidence must show a discriminatory or retaliatory attitude and "must indicate that the complained-of employment decision was motivated by the decision-maker's" discriminatory or retaliatory attitude toward the claimant. The court went on to note that "'only the most blatant remarks, whose intent could be nothing other than to discriminate'...will constitute direct evidence of discrimination." Id. at 1359 (quoting Earlay v. Champion Int'l Corp, 907 F.2d 1077, 1081-82 (11th Cir. 1990)). The Eleventh Circuit has also defined direct evidence as a statement by the decisionmaker/employer that "reflects a discriminatory attitude" and "ties the discriminatory attitude to the relevant employment decision...." Wright v. Southland Corp., 187 F.3d 1287, 1294 (11th Cir. 1999). The Fifth Circuit has defined direct evidence as including "any statement or written document showing a discriminatory motive on its face." Portis v. First Nat'l Bank of New Albany, 34 F.3d 325, 329 (5th Cir. 1994). Although this court has never squarely addressed the issue, we have noted that direct evidence is rarely available in retaliation cases and that it is usually necessary to rely on circumstantial evidence. See Webster v. Dept. of Army, 911 F.2d 679, 689 (Fed. Cir. 1990).

The evidence that Mr. Haddon sets forth as "direct evidence" of retaliation is not terribly direct at all, and does not qualify under any of the definitions of direct evidence....

...All the evidence pointed to by Mr. Haddon requires some inferences to get at a retaliatory motive for his discharge and does not qualify as direct evidence. Therefore it was not error to apply the McDonnell Douglas approach rather than the Price Waterhouse approach.

Under the McDonnell Douglas framework, the plaintiff must first make out a prima facie case of discrimination. McDonnell Douglas, 411 U.S. at 802, 93 S. Ct. 1817. In the context of a retaliation claim, this is done by showing that: (1) the plaintiff participated in a protected activity, (2) the defendant had knowledge of this participation, (3) the defendant took adverse action against the plaintiff, and (4) there was a "causal connection" between the plaintiffs protected activity and the defendant's adverse action against the plaintiff. Hollins v. Atlantic Co., 188 F.3d 652, 661 (6th Cir. 1999) (citing Christopher v. Stouder Mem'I Hosp., 936 F.2d 870, 877 (6th Cir. 1991)). See also Webster, 911 F.2d at 689. If the plaintiff is successful in showing a prima facie case, the burden then shifts to the defendant to show that there was a legitimate nondiscriminatory reason for the rejection. McDonnell Douglas, 411 U.S. at 802, 93 S. Ct. 1817. Finally, the plaintiff is given the opportunity to show by a preponderance of the evidence that the stated reason is a mere pretext, and the real reason was prohibited discrimination. McKenna v. Weinberger, 729 F.2d 783, 790 (D.C. Cir. 1984).

Buckler v. Fed. Retirement Thrift Inv. Bd., 73 MSPR 476, 496-98 (1997), determined that a prima facie case of discrimination does not require proof of intentional discrimination, although the appellant must ultimately prove that discrimination was the real reason for the challenged action:

The administrative judge found, in reliance on Board precedent, that the appellant was required to show the following in order to establish a prima facie case of age and sex discrimination: (1) He is a member of a protected group; (2) he was similarly situated to an individual who was not a member of his protected group; (3) he was treated more harshly or disparately than the individual who was not a member of his protected group; and (4) the difference in treatment was based on an intent to discriminate. See Richard v. Dept. of Defense, 66 MSPR 146, 152 (1995); Mascarenas v. Dept. of Defense, 54 MSPR 303, 308 (1992). This rule of law dates back at least to the Board's decision in Fenn v. Dept. of the Army, 35 MSPR 362, 366 (1987). Fenn in turn relied on the decision of the United States Supreme Court in McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792, 93 S. Ct. 1817, 36 L. Ed.2d 668 (1973).

However, a closer reading of McDonnell Douglas reveals that the Court did not establish a rule of law requiring an employee to make a showing of discriminatory intent as part of his prima facie case. McDonnell Douglas, 411 U.S. at 801-03, 93 S. Ct. at 1824. Moreover, subsequent Supreme Court decisions confirm that an employee need not make a showing of intent to establish a prima facie case of discrimination. See O'Connor v. Consolidated Coin Caterers, Corp., __ U.S. __, __, 116 S. Ct. 1307, 1309, 134 L. Ed.2d 433 (1996); St. Mary's Honor Center v. Hicks, 509 U.S. 502, 506-08, 113 S. Ct. 2742, 2747, 125 L. Ed.2d 407 (1993). We find, therefore, that an appellant may establish a prima facie case of prohibited discrimination by introducing preponderant evidence to show that he is a member of a protected group, he was similarly situated to an individual who was not a member of the protected group, and he was treated more harshly or disparately than the individual who was not a member of his protected group. A showing of discriminatory intent is not required to establish a prima facie case of discrimination. To the extent that Fenn and its progeny are inconsistent with this holding, those decisions are hereby clarified.

Nevertheless, the Supreme Court has made it clear that it remains the appellant's ultimate burden of proof to show that the agency's reason for taking an alleged discriminatory employment action is pretextual. In this regard, the appellant must show both that the agency's purported reason for the action was false or not the real reason for the action, and that discrimination was the real reason for the action. St. Mary's Honor Center, 509 U.S. at 515-17, 113 S. Ct. at 2752; see, e.g., Boatman v. Dept. of Justice, 66 MSPR 58, 63-64 (1994).

Here, the administrative judge found that the appellant failed to introduce evidence showing that the agency's action was based on discrimination and that all evidence of record showed that the agency's reasons for taking action were non-discriminatory in nature, although its determination concerning the appellant's assignment rights ultimately proved to be incorrect. The appellant does not challenge the administrative judge's findings of fact in this regard. Moreover, he does not identify evidence of discrimination that the administrative judge failed to consider, and we discern none in the record. Therefore, the administrative judge's error in requiring the appellant to prove intent as part of his prima facie case of discrimination did not prejudice the appellant's substantive rights because the appellant failed to carry his ultimate burden of proof of discrimination....

The appellant's failure to introduce any evidence in support of an allegation of discrimination results in a finding that he did not meet his burden of proof. Monahan v. USPS, 41 MSPR 153, 158 (1989); see Evans v. USPS, 70 MSPR 450, 451 (1996) ("An appellant's failure to introduce evidence supporting an allegation of prohibited discrimination does not satisfy his burden of proof to make out a prima facie case of discrimination based on disparate treatment.").

If the employee offers no evidence to support allegations of discrimination and reprisal, the defenses will not be sustained. See Spencer v. Dept. of Navy, 73 MSPR 15, 20-21 (1997) ("When an appellant fails to introduce evidence supporting his allegations of prohibited discrimination, he does not satisfy his burden of proof to make out a prima facie case of discrimination based on disparate treatment."); Richards v. Dept. of Navy, 24 MSPR 50, 53 (1984) ("It is well settled that the appellant has the burden of proof with respect to claims of discrimination and reprisal."); cf. Boatman v. DOJ, 66 MSPR 58, 64 (1994) ("The appellant has presented nothing further to rebut the agency's showing or otherwise to prove that discrimination was the real reason for her suspension.").

In some appeals the Board condenses the allocation of proof, determining that its AJs need not follow the established order of analysis in EEO cases: if an agency meets its burden on the merits by establishing a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its personnel action, consideration of a prima facie case is unnecessary; instead, the allegation of discrimination may be resolved by determining whether the appellant has shown the agency's reason to be pretextual. Vincent v. FDIC, 41 MSPR 637, 640 (1989) (age discrimination); see Nevarez v. Dept. of Army, SF-0752-14-0538-I-1 n.9 (NP 2/27/2015) (with a complete record, a burden shifting analysis is not required and the question is whether the appellant demonstrated by preponderant evidence that the reason for taking the challenged action was pretextual: "The Board has applied this principle when an appellant requests a hearing and when an appellant requests an adjudication on the written record. See Prather v. Department of Justice, 117 M.S.P.R. 137, ¶ 37 (2011) (assessing whether an appellant carried the ultimate burden on a discrimination defense after the administrative judge affirmed the charge of misconduct upon a review of the written record)."); Jackson v. USPS, 79 MSPR 46, 50-53 (1998) (applying the totality of evidence approach to an affirmative defense of disability discrimination and interpreting United States Postal Service Board of Governors v. Aikens, 103 S. Ct. 1478 (1983)); Nestro v. USPS, 34 MSPR 242, 244 (1987) (citing Stewart v. USPS, 9 MSPR 71, 74 (1981)); Howard v. USPS, 26 MSPR 393, 397-98 (1985) (race discrimination allegation in appeal from a dismissal for gambling: the appellant did not show that the persons who recommended or took action against him acted out of a discriminatory motive or intent; nor did the appellant show that there were white employees named as operators against whom the agency did not proceed); cf. Considine v. NCUA, 366 Fed. Appx. 157 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP) (as to an allegation of reprisal, through a performance-based removal, in reprisal for grievances filed against the appellant's supervisor: when the performance action was substantiated, the inquiry was whether the appellant established, by preponderant evidence, that her performance was a "mere pretext" for removal); Spruill v. USPS, 84 MSPR 36, 50 ¶ 40 (1999) (agency's failure to present additional evidence supporting charges did not relieve appellant of burden of proving EEO affirmative defense).

Reduction in force appeals may present issues of discrimination that are not completely analogous to those raised in adverse action cases. In a RIF appeal asserting the appellant was discriminated against racially because, unlike others, qualification requirements were not waived for a job she sought through assignment, the McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792 (1973), analysis was adopted, requiring the following elements: the individual belongs to a minority group; she applied for and was qualified for a job the employer sought to fill through applications; she was rejected; and after the rejection, the position remained open and the employer continued to seek applications with the appellant's qualifications. Here the evidence demonstrated that although the agency waived qualification requirements for higher-level positions requiring experienced personnel, it did not waive the requirements as to personnel filling GS-7 positions. There was no showing that the agency sought to fill the position the appellant sought through assignment with applicants with qualifications similar to hers. A prima facie case was not established. Taylor v. DHUD, 6 MSPR 177, 180, 6 MSPB 157 (1981).
