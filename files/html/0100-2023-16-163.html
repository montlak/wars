c. Lawsuit; Threats of Litigation

FLRA will not interfere with a person's right to litigate their claims in court by condemning the exercise of that right as a ULP; nor will FLRA seek to determine whether the lawsuit is reasonably founded. CPSC and AFGE Local 3705, 4 FLRA 803, 843 (1980). FLRA concluded in CPSC that there was no ULP if a supervisor filed a lawsuit for libel, without agency involvement, against an employee.

Threats of lawsuits by supervisors against employees in the unit were considered in Dept. of Treasury, Louisville Dist., IRS and NTEU, 20 FLRA 660, 664-65 (1985). The threat of a lawsuit by a supervisor, contained in a lawyer's letter to the union and a unit employee, explicitly recognized the right to pursue grievances and objected to disparaging statements being made about the supervisor outside normal channels. The concerns expressed in the letter stemmed from what FLRA found to be serious allegations of misconduct that the supervisor would want to refute. Those allegations were made both outside and inside normal channels to members of the supervisory hierarchy and to fellow employees of the supervisor. The allegations against the supervisor had a detrimental effect on the workplace. The threat of the lawsuit did not constitute a ULP. A decision of the NLRB, Clyde Taylor Co., 127 NLRB 103 (1960), was distinguished; there the threat to file a lawsuit was conditioned on the continuation of a ULP charge and was direct interference with protected activity. See Dept. of Army, Aviation Ctr. and AFGE Local 1815, 46 FLRA 535, 545-46 (1992) (ALJ Decision) (threat to sue employee for slander was not ULP when threat was not conditioned on employee's withdrawal of charge; there was no actual discouragement of employee's pursuit of protected activity).

Additionally, a central point in the Judge's reasoning is incorrect. Statements by union representatives signifying an intent to sue management officials are not beyond the protection of the Statute. See, e.g., Mullican Lumber Co., 310 NLRB 836, 842 (1993) (employer's termination of employee for statement, in context of protected activity, that he would sue supervisors and hold them personally liable found to violate the Act); Wayne W. Sell Corp., 281 NLRB 529, 533 (1986) (NLRB "precedent shows that warnings by employees to resort to legal procedures are protected activity").[6] In this regard, the Authority has held that management officials do not commit an unfair labor practice by threatening private civil suits against employees or union representatives for, e.g., libel. See Dep't of Treasury, Internal Revenue Serv., Louisville Dist., 20 FLRA 660, 661 (1985) (threat to file suit). See also Consumer Product Safety Comm'n, 4 FLRA 803 (1980) (actual filing of suit). It would be anomalous, in light of this precedent, to conclude that threats by union representatives to engage in similar litigation, related to and based in their representational activities, against their supervisors or other management officials, are not protected by the Statute.
