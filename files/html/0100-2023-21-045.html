10. Necessity for Cessation of Pay or Separation From the Federal Service

Young v. OPM, 97 MSPR 202, 204-06 ¶¶ 5-9 (2004), addressed whether an employee has to be separated from the federal service to obtain disability retirement. Nonpay status sufficed:

OPM's argument that the appellant is not entitled to an annuity because she has not been separated from her position is based on its interpretation of Riggs v. Office of Personnel Management, 709 F.2d 1486 (Fed. Cir. 1983). In that case, the plaintiff was seeking to have his disability annuity begin as of the date he filed his application. Id. at 1487. In sustaining OPM's denial of this request, the court stated that the "fatal defect in the plaintiff's] position is that by express provision of [the applicable statutory section] the annuity cannot start until [the plaintiff] separates from his civil service position." Id. at 1488.

The plaintiff in Riggs evidently was employed subject to the Civil Service Retirement System (CSRS), rather than FERS, and the statutory provisions applicable to him accordingly were somewhat different from those applicable in the present case. See id. (citing a provision in 5 U.S.C. ch. 83, which is applicable to CSRS employees). CSRS and FERS statutory provisions are essentially the same, however, as they concern the date on which a disability retirement annuity may begin. Both 5 U.S.C. § 8345(b)(2), the CSRS-related provision applicable in Riggs, and 5 U.S.C. § 8464(a)(1)(C), the corresponding provision for FERS employees, provide that a disability annuity may commence "on the day after separation from the service."

The statement in Riggs that the plaintiff's disability "annuity cannot start until [the plaintiff] separates from his civil service position" appears, when viewed in isolation, to support OPM's position in this case. We note, however, that neither 5 U.S.C. § 8345(b)(2) nor 5 U.S.C. § 8464(a)(1)(C) provides that a disability annuity never commences until after the employee has been separated. Both sections provide more specifically that such an annuity commences on the day after separation "or the day after pay ceases and" other requirements for the annuity are met.[1] In Riggs, the court did not address the latter alternative, apparently because the plaintiff's pay did not cease at any time prior to his separation. See Riggs, 709 F.2d at 1487 (after applying for a disability annuity, the plaintiff "remained on active duty pending confirmation by the Office of Personnel Management that he was disabled"). Moreover, it defined the issue it was addressing in the statement quoted above as "whether Mr. Riggs was entitled to disability annuity on the date of his application or any subsequent date while he was still on active service," Id. at 1487-88. In the present case, in contrast to Riggs, the appellant's pay, along with her "active service," appears to have ceased some time before she even filed her disability retirement application. (individual retirement record, showing that the appellant was in leave-without-pay status as of Jan. 14, 2002). Accordingly, we find that Riggs does not support a conclusion that the appellant in this case is not entitled to a disability retirement annuity.

[1] CSRS-related provision indicates that the "disability requirements for title to annuity" must be met before the annuity commences, while the FERS-related provision indicates that "the requirements for title to [a disability] annuity are met." 5 U.S.C. §§ 8345(b)(2), 8464(a)(1)(C).

It could be argued that the appellant's pay did not "cease," for purposes of 5 U.S.C. § 8464(a)(1)(C), at the end of her last day in a pay status prior to April 2003, and that it instead was only suspended temporarily, pending the appellant's return to a pay status. Relevant statutory and regulatory provisions, however, indicate that an individual may be entitled to a disability retirement annuity even though her disabling conditions eventually prove to be temporary. Under 5 U.S.C. § 8454, for example, a disability retirement annuitant is to be examined, beginning 1 year after the date of retirement and continuing annually thereafter, "unless the disability is permanent in character." Moreover, OPM's regulations indicate that an individual may be entitled to receive a disability annuity even if her disabling medical condition is expected to continue only for 1 year. See 5 C.F.R. § 844.103(a)(3) (providing that an individual may not receive a disability annuity unless the "disab[l]ing medical condition [is] expected to continue for at least 1 year from the date the application for disability retirement is filed"). Finally, we note that in Dudar v. Office of Personnel Management, 93 M.S.P.R. 292, ¶¶ 2, 8-10, 12 (2003), the Board found that an appellant was entitled to a disability retirement annuity beginning on the date of his separation, even though the appellant had subsequently returned to work and requested termination of the annuity.

For the reasons stated above, we find that the appellant, if she is otherwise entitled to a disability annuity, would be entitled to receive such an annuity starting after her pay status ended, provided that the appellant was otherwise eligible at that time for a disability annuity.
