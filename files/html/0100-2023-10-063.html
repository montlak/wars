F. CONDUCT OF HEARING; RESPONSIBILITIES OF JUDGE

The Board considers evidence introduced at a hearing or through proper pleadings and other submissions. Precious v. TVA, 15 MSPR 176, 178 (1983). The AJ is paid to run the hearing, develop the record, and issue an initial decision. The Board vests control of the hearing process in the AJ. The discretion of the AJ is considerable. See Gottlieb v. VA, 39 MSPR 606, 609 n.* (1989) (implying it was within the discretion of the AJ to hear the opening or closing argument). But discretion is not to be whimsical. A decision calling for the exercise of discretion hardly means that it is unfettered by meaningful standards or shielded from appellate review. United States v. Taylor, 108 S. Ct. 2413, 2419 (1988). Rulings governing the conduct of hearing and prehearing procedures are reviewed by the Board and eventually by courts.

Board hearings are informal in comparison with most court proceedings. AJs are authorized to wear robes in hearings; some do. There is no need to stand as the AJ walks in or out of the hearing room, although some people stand. Opening statements are taken at the discretion of the AJ, Logan v. OPM, 38 MSPR 612, 614 (1987), but most AJs do not take opening statements since the facts and issues are developed through prehearing submissions. Questions are asked from one's seat, although most AJs do not seem to mind if you rise and stalk about a bit while asking questions--it may help you and the others to keep awake during the long afternoon hours of an intricate performance case. Coffee and colas are routinely sipped during the proceedings, interspersed with breaks for more substantial refreshment, logistical arrangements (to locate lost witnesses), or regrouping of documents. The privilege of light liquid refreshment is not an excuse to turn the hearing into an indoor barbecue. Smoking is prohibited in hearing rooms. Witnesses and representatives should not read newspapers in the hearing room during the proceedings. AJs may also require cellphones to be placed in Board mode (off) during the hearing.

In the hearing, as in any other phase of any litigation, civility among counsel makes the proceedings tolerable--even enjoyable. Stipulations are helpful if they save time and avoid prejudice to a case. A few steps involving paperwork--clearly marking documents with exhibit numbers, paginating individual exhibits, and agreeing to their admissibility during the prehearing conference or during the hearing--save time. If a document is in evidence--as it will be if it has been approved as an exhibit during the prehearing conference--do not have a witness read the document aloud during testimony (except for the unusual occasion requiring special emphasis). Direct the attention of the witness to the document and the particular portion of interest and ask the questions.

Witnesses and counsel should be dressed to fit the climate and the formality of the hearing. Witnesses are frequently unfamiliar with hearings. They should be told that they will be subjected to cross-examination (this seems to come as a surprise to witnesses in some hearings). They should be told that they are to answer the questions put to them and not provide expositions on everything they know and think they know about the issues. It helps for them to understand in advance that the stenographer or audio recorder taking down their answers cannot pick up nods, gestures, grunts, thumps, or sobs. It helps, too, if they understand that opposing counsel has a job to do and should not be fenced with, berated, lied to, or given anything other than a direct and honest answer to a question properly put. Witnesses should be informed that if an objection is made to a question, it should not be answered until the AJ rules on the objection. It never hurts to tell the witness that he can briefly pause after a question from opposing counsel either to think about the answer or (if that is not possible) to see if an objection will be posed before a reply is irretrievably offered. Have your witness look at the AJ as he testifies. The AJ is supposed to assess the credibility of your witness by watching him and listening to him testify. It helps to be able to see something other than his ear. If your witness happens to have been convicted of several recent crimes of violence, the witness should be cautioned not to watch the AJ with too fixed a stare. Witnesses must understand that the hearing is not a game show--they get no points for guesses and speculation. If they do not know or cannot remember, it is appropriate to say as much in response to a question.

Next, the appellant cites two specific objections to the way in which the administrative judge conducted the hearing. First, he contends that she advised him, prior to the hearing, that he could not refer to himself in the third person when he questioned witnesses, but that she later reversed her position on that matter, causing him confusion. Second, the appellant alleges that, at the hearing, he was seated furthest from the witnesses and was required by the administrative judge to remain seated during the questioning of witnesses. As to these matters we note that the administrative judge has authority to regulate the course of the hearing. See 5 CFR § 1201.41(b)(6). The appellant has not shown that the administrative judge abused her authority in this regard. Even if the administrative judge's conduct could be considered error, the appellant has shown no prejudice to his substantive rights. In the absence of prejudice, such errors are of no legal consequence. See Karapinka v. Department of Energy, 6 MSPB 114, 6 MSPR 124, 127 (1981) (the administrative judge's procedural error is of no legal consequence unless it is shown to have adversely affected a party's substantive rights).
