b. Disabled Veteran

Under Bell v. USPS, DC-0752-14-0633-I-1 (NP 1/23/2015):

...A preference-eligible veteran includes an individual who served on active duty in the armed forces during specific periods of time, and who has been discharged or released from active duty in the armed forces under honorable conditions. 5 U.S.C. § 2108(1)(C), (3)(A)-(B). A disabled veteran is also preference eligible. 5 U.S.C. § 2108(3)(C). A disabled veteran means an individual who served on active duty in the armed forces, was separated under honorable conditions, and has an established service-connected disability. 5 U.S.C. § 2108(2).

The definition of a "disabled veteran" for purposes of application of veterans preference was provided by Downs v. VA, 110 MSPR 139, 144-45 ¶ 10, 2008 MSPB 230 (2008):

On petition for review, the appellant argues that he is a preference eligible because he qualifies as a "disabled veteran." A "disabled veteran," as defined in 5 U.S.C. § 2108(2), qualifies as a preference eligible. 5 U.S.C. § 2108(3)(C). "Disabled veteran" is defined as

an individual who has served on active duty in the armed forces, has been separated therefrom under honorable conditions, and has established the present existence of a service-connected disability or is receiving compensation, disability retirement benefits, or pension because of a public statute administered by the Department of Veterans Affairs or a military department.

5 U.S.C. § 2108(2). In 2005, the agency certified that the appellant was entitled to compensation for service-connected disabilities rated at 30 percent or more. The agency also certified that, according to its records, the appellant had served on active duty in the armed forces and was separated under honorable conditions. We therefore find that the appellant has nonfrivolously alleged that he is a preference eligible.

A service-connected disability may establish the veterans preference necessary for a Board appeal right in the excepted service, and it may establish a preference for the veterans who applies for a position with an agency or who competes for retention in a reduction in force, Carey v. USPS, 50 MSPR 359, 361 (1991):

The appellant now presents evidence which shows that she is a preference eligible. The letter from the Veterans Administration states that she receives disability compensation because of a service-connected disability, and the appellant has previously submitted evidence proving that she was honorably discharged from service in the United States Marine Corps. Under 5 USC 2108(3)(C), a disabled veteran is a "preference eligible," and a disabled veteran as defined in 5 USC 2108(2) is "an individual who has served on active duty in the armed forces, has been separated under honorable conditions, and...is receiving compensation." Thus, the appellant qualifies as a preference eligible employee as defined in 5 USC 2108(2) and (3)(C), so the Board would have jurisdiction over her removal appeal under 5 USC 7511 and 39 USC 1005....

Badana v. Dept. of Air Force, 104 MSPR 182, 185-86 ¶ 10, 2006 MSPB 345 (2006), stated that preference eligible status may depend on the rank of the appellant, not to exceed that of major or the equivalent, at the time of separation from the service, but service-connected disability may establish preference eligibility without regard to the rank of the appellant at the time of separation:

Preference eligibility does not attach to a member of the armed forces who retires at the rank of major or above unless the individual in question is a disabled veteran. 5 U.S.C. § 2108(4). "Disabled veteran" is defined as an individual who has served on active duty in the armed forces, has been separated from the armed forces under honorable conditions, and has established the present existence of a service-connected disability or is receiving compensation, disability retirement benefits, or a pension because of a public statute administered by the DVA or a military department. 5 U.S.C. § 2108(2). The appellant provided documentation below demonstrating that he retired on May 1, 1994 at the rank of major. Thus, he is only entitled to a veterans' preference if he establishes that he is a "disabled veteran." The appellant provided a DVA rating decision dated November 19, 2002 establishing that he was granted service-connected status for several claimed disabilities, and he contends that the rating decision shows that he is entitled to veterans' preference. To establish that the Board has jurisdiction over his appeal, the appellant need not prove that he is entitled to veterans' preference, and we need not decide under the circumstances of this case whether the rating decision constitutes definitive proof that he is entitled to veterans' preference. We are persuaded, however, that the appellant has made a nonfrivolous allegation that he is entitled to veterans' preference. Consequently, we find that the Board has jurisdiction over this appeal.
