b. Election Between EEO and Direct MSPB Appeal; Treatment of EEO Complaint

A binding election follows an informed choice among options. Agencies are supposed to assist employees in identifying appeal rights and the impact of election of remedies. Under 5 CFR 1201.21 (2022):

When an agency issues a decision notice to an employee on a matter that is appealable to the Board, the agency must provide the employee with the following:

(a) Notice of the time limits for appealing to the Board, the requirements of § 1201.22(c) [relating to the timeliness of the appeal], and the address of the appropriate Board office for filing the appeal;

(b) A copy, or access to a copy, of the Board's regulations;

(c) A copy, or access to a copy, of the MSPB appeal form available at the Board's Web site (http://www.mspb.gov), and

(d) Notice of any right the employee has to file a grievance or seek corrective action under subchapters II and III of 5 U.S.C. chapter 12, including:

(1) Whether the election of any applicable grievance procedure will result in waiver of the employee's right to file an appeal with the Board;

(2) Whether both an appeal to the Board and a grievance may be filed on the same matter and, if so, the circumstances under which proceeding with one will preclude proceeding with the other, and specific notice that filing a grievance will not extend the time limit for filing an appeal with the Board;

(3) Whether there is any right to request Board review of a final decision on a grievance in accordance with § 1201.155 of this part; and

(4) The effect of any election under 5 U.S.C. 7121(g), including the effect that seeking corrective action under subchapters II and III of 5 U.S.C. chapter 12 will have on the employee's appeal rights before the Board.

(e) Notice of any right the employee has to file a complaint with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission or to grieve allegations of unlawful discrimination, consistent with the provisions of 5 U.S.C. 7121(d) and 29 CFR 1614.301 and 1614.302.

(f) The name or title and contact information for the agency official to whom the Board should send the Acknowledgment Order and copy of the appeal in the event the employee files an appeal with the Board. Contact information should include the official's mailing address, email address, telephone and fax numbers.

[Refer to Chapter 2, "Lack of Notice of Appeal Rights."]

If an agency fails to inform an employee of the option to file an EEO complaint, and the employee files an untimely Board appeal and also files an EEO complaint, the Board apparently will accept the case when it has completed its cycle through the EEO process, the result in Peartree v. USPS, 66 MSPR 332, 341-42 (1995):

For mixed cases, 5 USC 7702(a)(1)-(2) and its implementing regulations provide for an election between (1) filing a direct Board appeal, and (2) filing a formal EEO complaint with the agency, followed by a right to file a Board appeal upon exhaustion of the agency EEO procedure. See 29 CFR 1613.403 (effective before October 1, 1992); 29 CFR 1614.302(b), (d) (effective from October 1, 1992); 5 CFR 1201.154(a), (b). Regardless of whether an employee elects to file a direct Board appeal under 5 CFR 1201.154(a), or elects to file a Board appeal after exhausting the agency EEO procedure under 5 CFR 1201.154(b), she is entitled to the same de novo review before the Board, including the right to a hearing. See generally 5 USC 7702(a)(1)-(2). However, an employee may not file a Board appeal under 5 CFR 1201.154(b), upon exhausting the agency EEO procedure, if she has previously made a valid election of the direct Board procedure. See Wilson v. United States Postal Service, 45 MSPR 499, 500, 502 (1990); Dawson v. United States Postal Service, 45 MSPR 194, 196-97 (1990).

The issue then is whether this appellant made a valid election of the direct Board procedure, when she untimely filed the prior Board appeal. Regarding this issue, the EEOC has determined that the appellant's prior Board appeal did not constitute a valid election because the agency had not adequately notified the appellant of the election requirement and the consequences of such an election.... As the Board held in Dawson, 45 MSPR at 196-97, we find it appropriate to defer to the EEOC's determination on this issue. We therefore find that the appellant's prior Board appeal did not constitute a valid election of the direct Board procedure precluding a subsequent Board appeal under 5 CFR 1201.154(b). See id.

The result in Peartree was replicated by McCoy v. USPS, 108 MSPR 160, 166-67 ¶ 13-14, 2008 MSPB 37 (2008):

Even though the appellant filed a Board appeal before filing a mixed-case complaint with his agency, the election requirement set forth at 29 C.F.R. § 1614.302(b) does not bar this appeal. The appellant did not respond, either orally or in writing, to the notice of proposed removal, and the agency's decision letter did not, in any event, provide him with notice of a choice between filing a mixed-case complaint and a mixed-case appeal. The appellant did not allege discrimination in his first Board appeal. (Summary of Telephonic Prehearing Conference). In fact, as set forth above, the appellant has alleged that the facts underlying his discrimination claim did not come into existence until 4 months after he withdrew his Board appeal. Shortly thereafter, the appellant sought EEO counseling and filed a mixed-case discrimination complaint with his agency. Under these circumstances, we find that the election requirement set forth at 29 C.F.R. § 1614.302(b) does not apply because the factual circumstances giving rise to the appellant's discrimination claim had not occurred when he filed his first Board appeal, and the appellant was not, therefore, informed that filing a Board appeal would preclude him from subsequently filing a mixed-case complaint with the agency.

This determination is consistent with Board and EEOC decisions issued under somewhat similar circumstances. In Gomez-Burgos v. Department of Defense, 79 M.S.P.R. 245, ¶¶ 2-4 (1998), the appellant filed a Board appeal of his removal, withdrew the appeal, and then filed a formal discrimination complaint regarding his removal. An EEOC AJ held that, because the agency's removal decision notice failed to apprise the appellant of his mixed-case rights of filing an appeal with the Board or a discrimination complaint with the agency, and the appellant filed his Board appeal without notice of his right to elect between forums, the appellant never made a valid election to appeal his removal to the Board, and the agency was required to issue a final agency decision affording the appellant an opportunity to file a new Board appeal. Id., ¶ 4. After the appellant filed a new appeal, the Board deferred to the EEOC's determination and held that the appellant's first appeal did not constitute a valid election of the direct Board procedure that would preclude his subsequent appeal of his removal under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.154(b). Id., ¶ 10; see Peartree v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 M.S.P.R. 332, 341-42 (1995). Similarly, the EEOC has held that, when an appellant is not informed of the election requirement, and was unaware that he could not file both a complaint and an appeal or that whichever he filed first would constitute an election of that forum, the appellant did not make an informed election to pursue an appeal to the Board, and an agency's rejection of a subsequent discrimination complaint is improper. See Zuniga v. Runyon, EEOC Request No. 05920857, 1992 WL 1371933 (Apr. 26, 1993); Lauderdale v. Stone, EEOC Request No. 05910033, 1991 WL 1189009 (Feb. 15, 1991). Just as the appellants in the above cases did not make valid elections because their agencies did not notify them of their right to file either a mixed-case complaint or a mixed-case appeal, the appellant in this case could not have made an informed, valid election under 29 C.F.R. § 1614.302(b) because the facts underlying the basis of his discrimination claim had not yet come into existence.

See Carter v. Dept. of Navy, 87 MSPR 373, 377-78 ¶ 9 (2000) (an agency decision letter did not inform the appellant of the mixed case election requirement; the election to appeal directly to the Board instead of pursuing the EEO process is not binding if it was not informed); French v. USPS, 80 MSPR 171, 173 ¶ 6 (1998) ("[F]or a complaint or appeal to be considered a valid election, the complainant or appellant must have been informed of his right to appeal to both forums"; the case was remanded for development of evidence concerning the election of remedies); Peltier v. DOJ, 79 MSPR 674, 677-79 ¶¶ 9-12 (1998) (considering the chronology of events concerning the initiation of EEO counseling and an MSPB appeal to determine if the appellant made an informed election to select MSPB as the route of appeal).

Gomez-Burgos v. DOD, 79 MSPR 245, 249 ¶ 10 (1998), considered the plight of an appellant who did not receive notice of EEO rights, appealed to the Board, withdrew his appeal to pursue an EEO complaint, received an order from an EEOC judge remanding the case to the agency for issuance of a mixed case decision, then received an unfavorable final agency decision, and filed another MSPB appeal:

The Board has previously determined in cases such as this that, based on policy considerations of comity and cooperation with the EEOC as co-equal tribunals in the mixed-case process, the Board will defer to the EEOC's procedural determination of whether an appellant made a valid election between the Board appeal process and the EEO process. See Crawford v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 MSPR 416, 423 n.5 (1996); Peartree, 66 MSPR at 342; Dawson v. U.S. Postal Service, 45 MSPR 194, 196-97 (1990). We thus find that the appellant's prior Board appeal did not constitute a valid election of the direct Board procedure precluding his subsequent Board appeal of his removal under 5 CFR § 1201.154(b). See Peartree, 66 MSPR at 342; Dawson, 45 MSPR at 196-97. Therefore, this case is not a reopening of the appellant's prior Board appeal, but is properly docketed as a new appeal within the Board's jurisdiction. See Peartree, 66 MSPR at 342; Dawson, 45 MSPR at 196-97.

If the employee is misinformed that the initiation of EEO counseling is a binding election of remedies and withdraws an MSPB appeal based on that incorrect belief, the Board may, upon the appellant's request, reinstate the Board appeal if the AJ failed properly to inform the appellant of the proper elements of the election scheme, depending upon the filing of an EEO complaint rather than upon the initiation of EEO counseling, Shapiro v. VA, 114 MSPR 585, 589-90 ¶ 9, 2010 MSPB 174 (2010):

In light of the above, it appears the appellant withdrew his appeal based on the belief that what had thus far been informal EEO activity constituted a binding election to proceed before the agency. Had the administrative judge inquired whether the appellant filed a formal mixed case complaint prior to his Board appeal, he could have corrected the appellant's misapprehension, but the administrative judge did not take that opportunity. Under these circumstances, we find that reinstatement of the appeal is appropriate. See Brooks, 67 M.S.P.R. at 554 (reinstating a withdrawn appeal where the administrative judge was in a position to correct appellant's mistaken belief that she could appeal to the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission); Scarboro, 55 M.S.P.R. at 498 (reinstating a withdrawn appeal where the administrative judge had an opportunity to correct the appellant's mistaken belief that the Board lacked jurisdiction); Dodd v. Department of the Interior, 48 M.S.P.R. 582, 585 (1991) (reinstating a withdrawn appeal where the administrative judge had an opportunity to correct the appellant's mistaken belief that his choices were limited to an oral hearing or withdrawal).

See Maxwell v. USPS, CH-0353-10-0809-I-1 (NP 3/15/2011) ("[I]t appears that the appellant withdrew her Board appeal based on the mistaken belief that what had thus far been informal EEO activity constituted a binding election to proceed before the agency. While this mistake could potentially have been discovered and corrected below, it was not. Under these circumstances, we find that reinstatement of the appeal is appropriate.").

In Bligen v. USPS, 100 MSPR 303, 306-07 ¶¶ 8-10 (2005), the appellant filed a timely appeal from an adverse action, withdrew his appeal to pursue an EEO complaint, the agency rejected the EEO complaint because of the prior Board appeal, and the Board declined to reinstate the appeal. The Board noted that the appellant did not attempt to rescind the withdrawal of the Board appeal soon after the agency rejected the EEO complaint. The Board declined to reinstate the appeal or reopen the appeal based on the appellant's arguments concerning negligence of his representative.

A binding election assumes that if the election to proceed with an MSPB appeal (rather than an EEO complaint) was made by a representative, the representative was authorized to proceed on the appellant's behalf. See Thomas v. USPS, 71 MSPR 474, 477 (1996) (the appellant did not sign the appeal or the designation of representative, took no action to pursue an MSPB appeal, and retained a different representative to pursue his EEO complaint). The Board discounted the effect of a class-action EEO complaint on the Board's jurisdiction over a reemployment rights appeal, Luna v. USPS, 114 MSPR 273, 279-80 ¶¶ 14-15, 2010 MSPB 124 (2010):

When an appellant raises a claim of disability discrimination in connection with an otherwise appealable action, the Board generally has jurisdiction to decide both the discrimination issue and the appealable action. 5 U.S.C. § 7702(a)(1); Hardy v. U.S. Postal Service, 104 M.S.P.R. 387, ¶ 29, aff'd, 250 Fed. Appx. 332 (Fed. Cir. 2007). In this case, however, the agency argued that the appellant's disability discrimination claim should be held in abeyance because that claim is covered under McConnell v. Potter, EEOC Hearing No. 520-2008-00053X (May 30, 2008), a class complaint pending before the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC). Specifically, the agency argued that the appellant fits the definition of a McConnell class member,[5] the appellant cannot opt out of the class, and the appellant should therefore be deemed to have made a binding election to proceed with her claim through the equal employment opportunity (EEO) process rather than through the Board. Id.

[5] The EEOC administrative judge recommended defining the McConnell class as "All permanent rehabilitation employees and limited duty employees at the Agency who have been subjected to the NRP from May 5, 2006 to the present, allegedly in violation of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973." McConnell, EEOC Hearing No. 520-2008-00053X at 23. During the pendency of the appellant's petition for review, the EEOC Office of Federal Operations issued a decision certifying the class as defined in the administrative judge's recommended decision. McConnell v. Potter, EEOC DOC 0720080054, 2010 WL 332083 at *9-*10 (January 14, 2010); see generally 29 C.F.R. §§ 1614.204, .403-.405.

We find the agency's argument unpersuasive because it presumes that McConnell is a mixed case, which it is not. See Hay v. U.S. Postal Service, 106 M.S.P.R. 151, ¶ 13 (2007) (an individual who claims prohibited discrimination in connection with an action otherwise appealable to the Board, i.e., a mixed case, may pursue her claim by filing an appeal with the Board or an EEO complaint with her employing agency, but not both); 29 C.F.R. § 1614.302(b) (same). Nothing in the EEOC's certification of the class complaint discusses denial of restoration or any other action that may be otherwise appealable to the Board. McConnell v. Potter, EEOC DOC 0720080054, 2010 WL 332083 (January 14, 2010). Nor do the claims at issue in McConnell, as defined by the EEOC, encompass any such action.[6] Id. at *9. Furthermore, the EEOC is not processing McConnell as a mixed case. Upon certifying the McConnell class, the EEOC remanded the matter to the agency instructing it to request that an administrative judge be appointed to hear the certified class claim. Id. at *10. If McConnell were a mixed case, the EEOC would have remanded the matter for further proceedings before the agency without a hearing. Compare 29 C.F.R. § 1614.108(f) with 29 C.F.R. § 1614.302(d); see also Throckmorton v. Norton, EEOC DOC 01A03994, 2003 WL 21145345, *5 (May 6, 2003) (mixed case class complaints are processed the same as mixed case individual complaints). We therefore find that the appellant's alleged membership in the McConnell class does not divest the Board of jurisdiction over any aspect of her Board appeal. See Coleman v. Department of the Treasury, 22 M.S.P.R. 519, 520-21 (1984) (because the appellant's EEO complaint did not pertain to any action appealable to the Board, it was not a mixed case complaint sufficient to divest the Board of jurisdiction over the appeal under 5 U.S.C. § 7702(a)(2)). Because the Board has jurisdiction over the appellant's restoration claim, 5 U.S.C. § 7702(a)(1) requires that the administrative judge adjudicate the appellant's disability discrimination claim on remand. See Barrett, 107 M.S.P.R. 688 ¶ 8.

[6] The claims at issue in McConnell are: (1) The NRP fails to provide a reasonable accommodation; (2) the NRP wrongfully discloses medical information; (3) the NRP creates a hostile work environment; and (4) the NRP has an adverse impact on disabled employees. McConnell, 2010 WL 332083 at *9.

See White v. USPS, 114 MSPR 386, 391-92 ¶¶ 15-17, 2010 MSPB 135 (2010) (analysis similar to Luna, supra); Mubdi v. USPS, 114 MSPR 559, 563-65 ¶¶ 13-17, 2010 MSPB 166 (2010) (similar to Luna, remanding to the AJ and requiring consideration of the discrimination allegation as a matter of disability discrimination based on lack of accommodation rather than an allegation of disparate treatment).
