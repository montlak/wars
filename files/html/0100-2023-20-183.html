5. Hearing by Administrative Law Judge; MSPB Review

Disciplinary cases brought by OSC are assigned to an administrative law judge (now an ALJ under contract to the Board from another federal agency) rather than an AJ at a regional office for a hearing. The right to a hearing by an ALJ is guaranteed by statute. 5 USC 1215(a)(2):

(2) Any employee against whom a complaint has been presented to the Merit Systems Protection Board under paragraph (1) is entitled to--

...

(C)  a hearing before the Board or an administrative law judge appointed under section 3105 and designated by the Board.

5 CFR 1201.125(a), (b) (2022) states:

Administrative law judge.

(a) An administrative law judge will hear a disciplinary action complaint brought by the Special Counsel.

(b) The administrative law judge will issue an initial decision on the complaint pursuant to 5 U.S.C. 557. The applicable provisions of §§ 1201.111, 1201.112, and 1201.113 of this part govern the issuance of initial decisions, the jurisdiction of the judge, and the finality of initial decisions. The initial decision will be subject to the procedures for a petition for review by the Board under subpart C of this part.

Through 1997, the ALJ issued a "recommended decision" that the full Board reviewed before the decision became a final decision of the MSPB. Nothing in the CSRA precludes the delegation scheme the Board has established or its reservation of the right to review an ALJ Decision in an OSC disciplinary action. See Special Counsel v. Nichols, 36 MSPR 445, 451 n.2 (1988).

If an employee in a complaint case waives a hearing, the denial of a subsequent request for a hearing is within the ALJ's proper exercise of authority. Special Counsel v. Seastrunk, 28 MSPR 51, 53 (1985). If the ALJ determines that a preliminary ruling raises substantial grounds for a difference of opinion and immediate review would materially advance the proceeding, the ruling may be certified for interlocutory review by the Board. See Special Counsel v. Russell, 28 MSPR 162, 170 n.7 (1985).

The Board may disagree with the factual findings of the ALJ. Special Counsel v. Starrett, 28 MSPR 372 (1985). The power of the Board to review and revise the ALJ's decision was affirmed on appeal. Starrett v. Special Counsel, 792 F.2d 1246 (4th Cir. 1986). However, deference was given to the ALJ's findings concerning witness credibility. Special Counsel v. DeFord, 28 MSPR 98, 101 (1985). To obtain that deference, the ALJ's decision, in the same manner as a decision of an administrative judge, must "identify all material issues of fact and law, summarize all the evidence on each disputed question of fact sufficiently to disclose the evidentiary basis for the presiding official's findings of fact, resolve issues of credibility, and include the administrative judge's conclusions of law and his legal reasoning, as well as the authorities on which that reasoning rests." Credibility resolutions must "explain in detail why the ALJ found the chosen version of the facts more credible than OSC's version of the facts." Special Counsel v. Eubanks, 76 MSPR 405, 413-14 (1997).
