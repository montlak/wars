b. Enforcement of the Act

The district court performs its usual function as a trial court and, although it may inspect agency records in camera, it is not confined to an appellate review of the agency's actions. Under the Act the court has the authority to order the correction or amendment of records, to order the release of records, to award damages, and to grant reasonable attorney fees to successful litigants. The Act provides that if a plaintiff shows that a Privacy Act violation was intentional or willful, the court may award "actual damages," but in no event less than $1,000. 5 USC 552a(g)(4)(A). The phrase "actual damages" has no accepted meaning in the legal community. The circuits have split on the meaning of the phrase. Compare Fitzpatrick v. IRS, 665 F.2d 327, 331 (11th Cir. 1982) (actual damages means pecuniary loss and does not encompass mental suffering, embarrassment, or damage to reputation), with Cooper v. FAA, 622 F.3d 1016, 1034 (9th Cir. 2010) (pecuniary and nonpecuniary damages are available); and Johnson v. Dept. of Treasury, 700 F.2d 971, 986 (5th Cir. 1983) (actual damages includes proven physical and mental injuries). Multiple concurrent disclosures of information (e.g., broadcast faxes) are compensable as one violation of the Act. See Tomasello v. Rubin, 167 F.3d 612, 617-18 (D.C. Cir. 1999). Although the Supreme Court did not define the phrase "actual damages," a plaintiff must suffer some actual damages to recover the $1,000 statutory minimum in damages. Doe v. Chao, 124 S. Ct. 1204, 1212 (2004) ("entitle[ment] to recovery" necessary to qualify for the $1,000 minimum is not shown merely by an intentional or willful violation of the Act producing some adverse effect. The statute guarantees $1,000 only to plaintiffs who have suffered some actual damages."). The Supreme Court also concluded that the Privacy Act damages provision does not encompass mental emotional distress. FAA v. Cooper, 132 S. Ct. 1441 (2012). Reinstatement is not a remedy available under the Privacy Act. An employee who claims that he was terminated on the basis of inaccurate agency records may be entitled to correction of the records and monetary damages. Reinstatement would have to be sought through other means. A court's authority over claims for injunctive relief and damages was extensively reviewed in Doe v. Dept. of Labor, 451 F. Supp.2d 156 (D.D.C. 2006).
