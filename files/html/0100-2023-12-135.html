3. Materiality of New Evidence

If new evidence is offered to the Board and if it is relevant, the Board reopens only if the new evidence is of sufficient weight to warrant an outcome different from the initial decision. Russo v. VA, 3 MSPR 345, 349, 3 MSPB 427 (1980); see Brown v. DHS, AT-315H-17-0696-I-1, n.2 (NP 5/10/2022) ("he does not specify what evidence he intends to submit, when he obtained the evidence or learned of the information contained therein, or the nature of the information. Further, even if the motions could be construed to contain or lead to new and material evidence, the appellant has not argued that such evidence is of sufficient weight to warrant an outcome different from what was ordered by the administrative judge. See Russo v. Veterans Administration, 3 M.S.P.R. 345, 349 (1980); see also 5 C.F.R. § 1201.114(a)(5), (k)."); Fealhaber v. OPM Fealhaber v. OPM, 93 MSPR 143, 146 ¶ 6 (2002) (stating rule); Lachiewicz v. DHS, PH-0752-10-0490-I-1 n.3 (NP 9/30/2013) ("Where the initial decision dismissed the appeal as settled, newly-discovered evidence would constitute good cause for untimely filing a petition for review, if the newly discovered evidence establishes that the settlement agreement is invalid. Dickson v. General Services Administration, 85 M.S.P.R. 7, ¶ 6 (1999), aff'd, 243 F.3d 566 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (Table)."); Dickson v. GSA, 85 MSPR 7, 10 ¶ 6 (1999) (new evidence is material if it has enough weight to affect the outcome of the initial decision or a settlement challenged by PFR); Wright v. R.R. Retirement Bd., 76 MSPR 330, 332 (1997) (documents provided by the agency after the initial decision in a RIF case were new but not material because they did not change the appellant's adjusted service computation date); Melendez v. VA, 73 MSPR 1, 4 (1996); Grant v. Dept. of Air Force, 61 MSPR 370, 378 (1994) (in an IRA case it was not new and material evidence that, after the record was closed at the regional level, the agency transferred an individual accused by the appellant of taking reprisal against her: "whether the agency took disciplinary action against Mouzon is irrelevant as to why the agency took disciplinary action against the appellant."); Madrid v. Dept. of Interior, 37 MSPR 418, 421 (1988) ("the agency did not show that [the evidence] is material because it is not of sufficient weight to warrant an outcome different from that reached in this Opinion and Order"); Edwards v. Dept. of Army, 24 MSPR 162, 164-65 (1984) (evidence that after the record was closed the agency corrected a RIF assignment error was considered new and material, mooting the remedy ordered by the AJ); Murnin v. Dept. of Educ., 9 MSPR 600, 601-02 (1982) ("For evidence to be material, it must be 'directly relevant to and probative of the charges' and have the potential of affecting the outcome of the decision, Powell v. Interior, 3 MSPB 35, 2 MSPR 513 (1980)"; a statement from a witness was immaterial because "the witness has never worked with appellant, does not know appellant's background, and her opinion is based entirely on speculation regarding her own personal situation only.").

Evidence is material when it is directly relevant to, and probative of, the charges and is of so controlling a nature that it changes the substantive equities between the parties. Callahan v. OPM, 6 MSPR 332, 334, 6 MSPB 284 (1981) (citing Powell v. Dept. of Interior, 2 MSPR 513 (1980)). Cumulative evidence is not new and material evidence, Fleming v. DHHS, 4 MSPR 197, 199, 4 MSPB 277 (1980). Evidence supplied with a PFR is not material if it concerns an issue that the appellant decided not to pursue at a hearing, e.g., discrimination. See Evcic v. Dept. of Navy, 9 MSPR 656, 661 (1982).

If a PFR challenges, on the basis of new evidence, the fairness of a settlement accepted by an AJ, the new evidence should be material to whether the settlement is invalid as a result of fraud, coercion, or mutual mistake, or that it has otherwise become an improper resolution. Shoemaker v. Dept. of Navy, 44 MSPR 396, 401 (1990). An OWCP determination was material that was received after the issuance of an initial decision sustaining the appellant's removal for violation of a last chance settlement when the OWCP finding undercut the agency's allegation that the employee had prohibited unscheduled absences during the period covered by the settlement agreement. Brent v. USPS, 104 MSPR 56, 58, 2006 MSPB 332 (2006). The Board expects a specific showing of how new evidence would make a material difference in an initial decision, noting in Edwards v. Dept. of Army, 63 MSPR 680, 683 (1994), as to the appellant's discovery that a supervisor said he had been reluctant to remove the appellant, but that he would proceed with removal to avoid "trouble" with his supervisors: "[H]e has not explained how the alleged conversation bears on the result reached in the initial decision, much less how it would warrant a different outcome." See Harrison v. OPM, 76 MSPR 442, 445 (1997) ("When a request for reopening is based on a claim of new evidence, the evidence must be of such weight to warrant a different outcome.").

In Waters v. OPM, 83 MSPR 553, 555-56 ¶¶ 5-7 (1999), evidence of a change in appellant's medical status was new when the change occurred after the issuance of the initial decision. The evidence was material because it tended to prove that appellant's condition, the basis for her disability retirement application, was not controlled by the medication evaluated by the AJ in the initial decision. A state unemployment compensation decision was not of sufficient weight to warrant an outcome different from that ordered by the AJ. See Upshaw v. DOD, 56 MSPR 94, 97 (1992); Henscheid v. USPS, 14 MSPR 422, 425 (1983) (unemployment compensation decision was not a reason to set aside the Board's decision). A Tax Court decision affecting the existence or magnitude of an employee's tax deficiencies had sufficient potential probative value to warrant remand to an AJ to reconsider allegations of failure to file tax returns and failure to pay taxes leading to an adverse action against a an IRS tax specialist. See Tawadrous v. Dept. of Treasury, 110 MSPR 475, 2009 MSPB 18 (2009).

A court remanded a RIF appeal to the Board based on evidence from a witness who evaluated the appellant's assignment rights and learned, after the appeal was decided, that the position description he used for his review may have been inaccurate. The reviewing court declined to discount the new evidence as a "recantation" in Bergquist v. Dept. of Interior (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 99-3468), following remand from court, Bergquist v. Dept. of Interior, 87 MSPR 605 (2001):

The government argues that "recantations" are disfavored and viewed with skepticism, citing Olson v. United States, 989 F.2d 229, 231 (7th Cir. 1992) ("It is a truism that our courts treat recantations with skepticism."), and United States v. Provost, 969 F.2d 617, 620 (8th Cir. 1992) ("[N]ew trial motions based on recanted testimony are immediately suspect"). In Olsen, a man convicted of homicide sought a new trial based on recantation by an eye-witness. In Provost, the recanting witness was a ten year old victim of a sexual assault. Each case involved recantation of testimony based on the witness' actual observation of events, the witness simply changing its story about what had happened. Dr. Christiansen's affidavit, however, is based on his discovery, as an impartial observer, of the true nature of the Wan position. Dr. Christiansen did not simply change his mind; he learned that he had been misled by an inaccurate document. Such forthcoming correction is favored by the law.
