1. Personal Work on Government Time

Agency toleration of outside pursuits markedly diminishes if the work occurs on government time or uses federal resources. Essential to the good of the service is that employees devote government time to government work. Green v. Baughman, 243 F.2d 610, 613 (D.C. Cir. 1957). "To conduct personal business when the agency presumes you are performing the official duties of your position violates the trust that the agency has placed in its employee." Cohen v. IRS, 7 MSPR 57, 61, 6 MSPB 662 (1981). The Board sustained removal of a GS-12 Revenue Officer for conducting personal business on government time, stating that to conduct personal work when an agency presumes one is performing the government's work violates the trust that the agency places in its employee. The conduct destroyed the confidence established in the employee-employer relationship, a result not outweighed by 13 years of service, a clean disciplinary record, and an absence of progressive discipline. Cohen v. IRS, 7 MSPR 57, 61, 6 MSPB 662 (1981); see Crostic v. VA (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-886) (adverse action was sustained against an employee who engaged in an outside business during working hours); Howard v. Dept. of Air Force, 114 MSPR 482, 485 ¶ 8, 2010 MSPB 154 (2010) ("for an employee to conduct personal business while he is presumed to be performing the official duties of his position violates the trust the agency has placed in the employee and destroys the confidence established in the employer-employee relationship"); Patten v. Dept. of Army, 26 MSPR 214, 216-17 (1985) (mitigating, pursuant to table of penalties, a suspension to a reprimand of a procurement clerk who briefly and without complaint distributed some advertising materials at work); Furlick v. EEOC, 20 MSPR 71, 75 (1984) (removal of an EEO specialist was sustained for charges that included conducting a personal business while on government time); cf. Lewis v. USDA, 268 Fed. Appx. 952 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP) (sustaining charge that a supervisor failed to prevent subordinates from performing work on outside business ventures on government time).

There are degrees of intolerance as to outside activities. Use of a government desk to store papers related to an outside business is a minor infraction if the appellant is unaware of its prohibition. Forrester v. DHHS, 27 MSPR 450, 458 (1985). At times it may be difficult to distinguish approval of outside work from inappropriate deviation from government work orders for purposes related to the approved outside business. See Crane v. Dept. of Air Force, 240 Fed. Appx. 415 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP) (reversing removal and remanding for further penalty consideration as a result of the knowledge of the employee's supervisors for most of the charged conduct and the lack of any warning to the employee; compare majority and dissenting opinions as to the nature of the actual misconduct). Agencies issue their own regulations and guidance on personal use of government equipment (computers, emails, phones, copiers), and those regulations should be examined for restrictions on the use of resources for outside commercial or other enterprises.
