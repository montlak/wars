3. Rehabilitation

On the subject of rehabilitation, a matter of some significance in cases involving persons deemed unsuitable for present government work because of past criminal conduct, Buhl v. OPM, 37 MSPR 305, 312-14 (1988), concluded that the appellant's relatively recent past record outweighed rehabilitative efforts:

In reaching her determination that the agency failed to prove that the appellant's prior criminal misconduct could reasonably be expected to interfere with or prevent the effective performance in a position in the Federal service or prevent the effective performance by an employing agency of its duties and responsibilities, the administrative judge considered the appellant's past criminal record and concluded that his offenses showed a lack of honesty and integrity. She also found that the appellant's latest offense occurred just three years prior to the appellant's application for employment. She next found that the appellant had established, by preponderant evidence, that he had made an effort towards rehabilitation and that he had achieved rehabilitation to a considerable degree. She, therefore, found that the appellant was suitable for Federal employment. We disagree.

Criminal, dishonest, infamous, or notoriously disgraceful conduct, and intentional false statements are among the reasons the agency may use in determining that an applicant is unsuitable for Federal employment under 5 CFR § 731.202(b). If the applicant is found unsuitable for Federal employment based upon criminal, dishonest, infamous, notoriously disgraceful conduct, or false statements, then the factors set forth at 5 CFR § 731.202(c)--to the extent they are deemed pertinent in an individual case--must be considered. Thus, absence or presence of rehabilitation or efforts towards rehabilitation is but one of the factors to be considered. See 5 CFR § 731.202(c)(7).

In our view, the other relevant factors to be considered in this case are: (1) The nature and seriousness of the misconduct; (2) the recency of the misconduct; and (3) the kind of position for which the appellant was applying. Our consideration of these factors leads us to conclude that the appellant's past criminal misconduct and his intentional falsification of two government documents could reasonably be expected to interfere with or prevent his effective performance in a position in the Federal service or prevent the effective performance by an employing agency of its duties and responsibilities.

The appellant's past criminal record showed a lack of honesty and integrity. In this regard, we note that most of the appellant's past arrests and convictions were for theft offenses. Further, the appellant's intentional falsification of his SF-171 and the supplemental information form submitted to the Puget Sound Naval Shipyard raises serious doubts as to his honesty and fitness for employment. See McCreary v. Office of Personnel Management, 27 MSPR 459, 462-63 (1985) (the employee's falsification of a government document raises serious doubts as to the employee's honesty and fitness for employment). The appellant's past criminal record and his intentional falsification of two government documents demonstrate a pattern of dishonest conduct.

The appellant's misconduct was also quite recent. His latest criminal offense occurred only three years prior to the agency's finding him unsuitable for Federal employment. Even more recently, the appellant submitted a SF-171, in December 1985, and a supplemental form, in July 1986, in which he omitted any reference at all to most of his past criminal record.

Finally, as found by the administrative judge, the position for which the appellant was applying, a Marine Machinery Mechanic at a Naval shipyard, was one which necessarily required honesty and integrity. The appellant's criminal misconduct and falsification of government documents are incompatible with the requirements of honesty and integrity.

Our consideration of the factors relevant in this case leads us to conclude that, notwithstanding the appellant's efforts toward rehabilitation,[6] the appellant's employment in the Federal Service could reasonably be expected to interfere with or prevent the effective performance in a position in the Federal service or prevent the effective performance by an employing agency of its duties and responsibilities. We also conclude that the agency's determination finding that the appellant was unsuitable for Federal employment promotes the efficiency of the service.

[6] The appellant's failure to fully disclose his past criminal record in his SF-171 and supplemental information form seriously denigrates the value of his attempts to rehabilitate himself for purposes of determining his suitability for Federal employment.
