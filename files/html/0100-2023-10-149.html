a. Questioning by Judge

It is the duty of the AJ to develop a record. The AJ may develop evidence on her own initiative. See 5 CFR 1201.41(b)(10) (2022). AJs often ask questions, some more than others (AJs and questions). This is not bias (it may be the only way the AJ can remain alert). Some appellants charge bias because the AJ does not ask enough questions. The Board ruled that an unrepresented employee unskilled in hearing procedures received a fair hearing when the AJ questioned witnesses to ensure a complete record, including clarifying questions to elicit information that the appellant sought to obtain during cross-examination of agency witnesses. Johnson v. GSA, 2 MSPR 47, 49 (1980); see Blain v. VA, 36 MSPR 322, 328 (1988) ("Because the appellant was unrepresented and unfamiliar with the hearing procedures, the administrative judge properly intervened in order to ensure that the issues were thoroughly explored."). As to a challenge to questions by the AJ, Zebeau v. Dept. of Army, 12 MSPR 85, 87-88 (1982):

[W]e note the requirement set forth in the Board's regulations at 5 CFR § 1201.111(b)(1). Pursuant to that regulation, each initial decision must contain "[f]indings of fact and conclusions, as well as the reasons or bases therefore, upon all the material issues of fact and law presented on the record." See Rosenberg v. Commodity Futures Trading Commission, 7 MSPB 491, 7 MSPR 664 (1981), and cases cited therein. Thus it is incumbent upon the presiding official to understand and comprehend fully all of the evidence presented, no matter how technical its nature. The Board has held that if the presiding official was unable to understand technical evidence, that official should have directed the agency to provide explanatory material so that he or she could make the requisite findings of fact. Id. at 491, 7 MSPR at 665.

Thus given the presumption of integrity and the need for comprehension, we turn to a consideration of the hearing transcript. Although we have carefully reviewed the entire transcript, the appellant has directed our attention to numerous pages allegedly illustrative of the presiding official's "advocacy" of the agency position. Therefore, we have reviewed these pages with particular care. We find that in every instance the presiding official was attempting to elucidate and clarify his own understanding of the situation and evidence presented or was otherwise engaged in discretionary functions related to conducting a fair and impartial hearing pursuant to 5 CFR § 1201.41(b).

As the agency asserted in its response to the PFR, the problem in this case is that the evidence is based on computer output and is in supply language and format. Therefore, the evidence does not lend itself to ready comprehension by those unfamiliar with the computerized Army Supply System. Nevertheless, there is no evidence that the presiding official overstepped his discretionary authority in attempting to clarify matters.

The Federal Circuit noted what it considers to be the 's "obligation" to assume an active role in assisting (through questions or advice on hearing procedure) to help a pro se appellant. Yanopoulos v. Dept. of Navy, 796 F.2d 468, 471 (Fed. Cir. 1986), citing Sims v. Harris, 631 F.2d 26, 28 (4th Cir. 1980).

The Hearings Procedures Guide advises AJs on the subject of their examination of witnesses. The Judges' Handbook has superseded the Hearings Procedures Guide, but the Guide remains a source for guidance and it is still used for training and orientation. The Guide provides, at 17-18:

e. Examination by the Administrative Judge

(1) The AJ is responsible for conducting a fair and impartial hearing, and insuring that the record is fully developed.

(2) The Board has noted its approval of the AJ's active participation in the hearing by assisting parties, especially unskilled pro se appellants, with opening and closing statements, questioning witnesses, and clarifying questions on cross-examination. Johnson v. GSA, 2 MSPR 47 (1980). See also Yanopoulos v. Dept. of Navy, 27 MSPR 647, aff'd 796 F.2d 468, 471 (Fed. Cir. 1986), (citing Sims v. Harris, 631 F.2d 26, 28 (4th Cir. 1980)).
