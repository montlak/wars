b. Hardship

Economic hardship caused by termination of employment is insufficient reason to set aside a settlement. To successfully plead force or duress, it must be shown that the party who benefited constrained or forced the action of the injured party. Gunn v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 2001 NP No. 01-3219) ("Gunn made the difficult choice of continued employment over pursuing his appeal. But this inherently unpleasant situation does not make his decision less voluntary."); see Calhoun v. GSA, 636 Fed. Appx. 571, 574 (Fed. Cir. 2016 NP) ("Ms. Calhoun's allegation that "[m] entally, physically, and financially, after more than twenty years, [she] could not afford to fight GSA any longer," does not amount to sufficient duress to invalidate the agreement. Long v. U.S. Postal Serv., 229 Fed. Appx. 919, 921 (Fed. Cir. 2007)."); Marshall v. USPS, 402 Fed. Appx. 521, 523 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP) ("Marshall appears to argue that he was coerced into the settlement agreement because the mediator knew that Marshall's house was being foreclosed, and recommended that Marshall "take what [he] can get." There is no evidence in the record that Marshall's decision to follow-through on the recommendation and proceed with the settlement was involuntary.... Second thoughts about the terms of a settlement agreement do not provide a legally cognizable basis on which to set aside the agreement."); Springs v. Dept. of Treasury (Fed. Cir. 2001 NP No. 01-3196) ("That she was unhappy with the choices presented to her, and therefore executed the agreement 'under protest,' does not make the agreement unenforceable. Although Ms. Springs asserts that she was coerced into signing the agreement because she would have been removed from her position if she had not signed it, the fact that the alternative to signing was removal does not constitute coercion."); Perez v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 96-3172) (citing Asberry for the following proposition: "Every loss of employment entails financial hardship. If that alone were sufficient to establish economic duress, no settlement involving it would ever be free from attack."); Asberry v. USPS, 692 F.2d 1378 (Fed. Cir. 1982); Cook v. USDA, PH-0752-14-0492-I-1 (NP 8/15/2014) ("[t]he appellant's petition reflects a change of heart due to financial difficulties. This is not a basis for granting his petition for review or setting aside the settlement agreement. See Hinton, 119 M.S.P.R. 129, ¶ 4."); Bowie v. USPS, 72 MSPR 42, 46 (1996) (economic distress due to possible foreclosure of home did not render settlement invalid); Riley v. Dept. of Army, 55 MSPR 671, 677 (1992) (allegations of pain, duress, and stress were insufficient to set aside addendum to settlement); Souza v. VA, 54 MSPR 107, 110 (1992) ("[T]hat...appellant was faced with unpleasant choices, i.e., to face criminal charges while represented by an individual who was unprepared to represent him thereon (assuming arguendo that that consideration was relevant to his pending Board appeal), or to enter into the settlement agreement, does not affect the voluntariness of the appellant's ultimate decision to enter into the agreement settling his appeal."); Kramer v. USPS, 53 MSPR 318, 319 (1992) (apparently considering an allegation that disability discrimination had a bearing on the voluntariness of a settlement, but finding no jurisdiction for AJ to consider a race discrimination claim once AJ found the agreement valid); Moran v. VA, 43 MSPR 547, 553 (1990) (finding immaterial allegations of agency threats of possible criminal prosecution); Yeto v. USPS, 41 MSPR 402, 405 (1989) (settlement of a WIGI appeal called for some relief, coupled with the appellant's resignation and an agreement not to appeal the resignation to the Board; allegation of hardship and stress placed upon appellant's family insufficient to invalidate agreement); Hightower v. Dept. of Transp., 39 MSPR 538, 541 (1989) ("Although the appellant has claimed that she was under economic duress to accept the terms of the settlement agreement, such claim does not rise to the level of coercion. See Asberry v. U.S. Postal Service, 692 F.2d 1378, 1381 (Fed. Cir. 1982)."); Ortega v. Dept. of Interior, 35 MSPR 158, 160 (1987) (duress not established by appellant's statements that he was uncertain whether AJ would permit him to present his case; concerns about his future; statements by agency representative that agency did not want him back as an employee and, if he returned, agency would take action against him for other misconduct)); cf. Choroszy v. DHS, PH-315H-16-0458-I-1 (NP 4/27/2022) ("Yet, because the appellant's appeal is a petition for enforcement, the Board may only decide whether the agency has breached the agreement entered into the record and may not decide a claim of discrimination that is alleged to be a basis in whole or in part of the agency's noncompliance. See King v. Reid, 59 F.3d 1215, 1218-19 (Fed. Cir. 1995)."). [Refer in this chapter to "Allegation of Discrimination; Whistleblowing Reprisal."]
