broida mspb Chapter 15: Attorney Fees Chapter 15: Attorney Fees, Subchapter 02: Procedures for Obtaining Fees, Section B: Compensable Services, Subsection 11: Related Proceeding, Part a: MSPB
a. MSPB

On (rare) occasion, one Board case may have an effect upon another, leading to interesting issues as to fees. The situation presented in Nadolney v. EPA, 30 MSPR 561, 563-67 (1986), involved an unusual sequence of events. The appellant was fired for misconduct consisting of poor performance and of what amounted to insubordination. At the time the removal proceedings were being considered at the agency level, the agency also denied the appellant's step increase. The removal decision came first and was appealed. The step increase reconsideration process was exhausted, and another Board appeal ensued. The two appeals proceeded separately in the same regional office. The removal decision was decided first and resulted in mitigation of the removal to a suspension. The removal hearing was more than a week long. Because the WIGI and removal charges were so similar, in order to avoid a repeat performance of the hearing held on the removal, it was agreed by all concerned that the transcript of the removal hearing would serve as the record for the Board's decision in the WIGI appeal. Appellant won the WIGI appeal and petitioned for fees, claiming time both for the efforts directly involved in the WIGI case and for the time put into the removal hearing (no fee petition had been filed as to the outcome of the removal case). The AJ awarded fees for the time spent on the WIGI but denied the claim for time spent at the Board's removal hearing. The Board awarded fees on PFR in Nadolney:

[A]ppellant contends that the presiding official erred in disallowing approximately 70 hours for the time spent on the removal action because the two actions are related and legal work done on the removal appeal was used to represent appellant with regard to his appeal of the agency's denial of his within-grade salary increase. In support of this position, appellant contends that he waived his right to a hearing and stipulated to the use of the record from the removal action in the interest of judicial economy and that he is now being penalized for his cost-saving litigation strategy.

In King v. United States Postal Service, 20 MSPR 467 (1984), the Board denied a request for fees incurred for representation in a state unemployment compensation hearing.... The Board held that although compensable time should not be limited to the hours expended within the four corners of the litigation, there must be a showing that the time was expended in pursuit of a successful resolution of the case in which fees are being claimed.... [T]he appellant had not established that the unemployment compensation proceeding was in any way connected with or necessary to the successful prosecution of his appeal before the Board.

In contrast, the present case involves two separate Board proceedings based on a "common core of facts."...

...In Webb v. Board of Education of Dyer County, 105 S. Ct. 1923 (1985), the Supreme Court addressed the issue of awardability of time spent pursuing optional administrative proceedings.... The Court clarified its earlier holding in Hensley v. Eckerhart, 461 U.S. 424 (1983), and ruled that compensable time...is that "reasonably expended on the litigation," but that fees may also be awarded for a "discrete portion" of work in a related proceeding to the extent that it was "useful and of a type ordinarily necessary" to the successful outcome of the subsequent litigation....

The Board's holding in King is consistent with Webb.... [w]here an attorney fee petition includes a request for an award of fees for time spent on a separate but factually related Board proceeding, the standard for determining whether such time is "useful and of a type ordinarily necessary" to the successful outcome of the subsequent Board proceeding will be evaluated within the framework of the following factors:

(1) whether the claimed portion of work done in the prior Board proceeding is reasonable under the Board's standard set forth in Kling...and

(2) whether the work done in the prior Board proceeding, or some "discrete portion" of it, significantly contributed to the success of the subsequent Board proceeding and eliminated the need for work that otherwise would have been required in connection with that subsequent proceeding.

...

Inasmuch as the presiding official relied almost exclusively on the record in the adverse action hearing to resolve the WIG appeal and the two actions were based on substantially the same charges, we conclude that counsel expended time in pursuit of a successful resolution of a WIG appeal.... Thus, we find under the facts presented, that it would be incongruous to disallow the requested fees on the sole basis that the work was expended for another Board proceeding when such work was equally valuable in adjudicating the WIG appeal.

Finally, it is clear from the record that the presiding official's reliance on the evidence in the adverse action appeal meant that any evidence that would have been submitted in the WIG appeal would most likely be duplicative. By stipulating to submission of the record in the adverse action appeal, the parties avoided the need for a second hearing which could have resulted in calling the same witnesses as well as submitting identical exhibits and pleadings. Thus, given the factual relationship between these two proceedings, we find that submission of the record in the prior Board proceeding was cost-effective and that counsel should be compensated for development of this record for use in the WIG appeal.
