b. Rebuttal Witness

A rebuttal witness is called to refute testimony already presented. Board practice contemplates an opportunity for the agency to present evidence and an opportunity for the appellant to present evidence. Within that scheme, all witnesses and all evidence is presented, subject to cross-examination, and there the hearing ends. Board practice in an adverse action case does not contemplate a session for the agency to present its evidence to support the case, a session for the appellant to provides his or her own evidence opposing the agency case, and then another session for the agency to refute or rebut the appellant's presentation. Through discovery the agency should determine the appellant's issues and evidence and the agency counters that evidence or those issues during its presentation in a hearing (or through cross-examination during the appellant's case). A true rebuttal witness in a Board case would ordinarily be approved based on some evidence or issue that arises during the hearing that could not reasonably be anticipated by the agency or the appellant.

Assuming a legitimate need for a rebuttal witness, an appellant (or agency) will most likely not know the particular need for a rebuttal witness until the hearing, and the rebuttal witness cannot practically be included in a prehearing witness list. The Board explained this concept in Jolley v. Dept. of Navy, 36 MSPR 174, 177 (1988), commenting on the nature of rebuttal testimony:

Our review of the record discloses and we find that the denial of Mr. Jarrett as a witness violated the principles of fundamental fairness. See 5 CFR § 1201.41(b); cf. Kennedy v. Military Sealift Command, 24 MSPR 96 (1984); Rucker v. Department of the Treasury, 6 MSPB 80, 6 MSPR 82, 83 (1981). Although surprise to the agency was the reason given for not having Mr. Jarrett's live testimony at the hearing, there is no merit to this contention. The record establishes that the agency was well aware of the duties which the appellant claimed that he could perform even with his limitations. The appellant had specifically identified, at the prehearing conference, seven positions which he alleged that he could perform. We find that, under the circumstances of this case, the appellant's request for a rebuttal witness was appropriate. It was not necessary for the appellant to identify Mr. Jarrett, as a rebuttal witness, in advance. This is especially true in a case such as this where the burden of proof is on the appellant to show that the agency's action constituted a violation of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, as amended, and it is the appellant who is entitled to rebut the agency's claim of a legitimate nondiscriminatory basis for its action. Cf. McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792, 802-03, 93 S. Ct. 1817, 1824, 36 L. Ed.2d 668 (1973). The administrative judge's acquiescence in the denial of Mr. Jarrett's live testimony based upon alleged surprise effectively deprived the appellant of his witness and, therefore, was error.

We further find that it was error for the administrative judge to rely on the statements which the agency submitted from witnesses whom the appellant could not cross-examine. The agency used these statements more in an attempt to bolster Mr. Nyswonger's testimony than to refute Mr. Jarrett's statement. Because these statements relate to matters which the agency knew were in issue before Mr. Jarrett made his statement for the record, we find that these statements constitute improper surrebuttal and it was error for the administrative judge to consider them.

It was improper to deny the appellant a chance to call a previously unlisted rebuttal witness to counter expert testimony from an agency witness that arguably went beyond the scope of what that witness was called to prove. Miller v. Dept. of Navy, 38 MSPR 377, 379 (1988). But Gonzales v. Dept. of Navy, 101 MSPR 248, 253 ¶ 14, 2006 MSPB 41 (2006), determined that it may be necessary to make a showing of some likely harm through the denial of rebuttal evidence:

We note that the administrative judge did not permit the appellant to offer rebuttal evidence after the agency concluded its case at the hearing. However, the appellant did not object to this ruling below, and is therefore precluded from doing so now. See Tarpley v. U.S. Postal Service, 37 M.S.P.R. 579, 581 (1988). In any event, the appellant has not established that he was prejudiced by the denial, as he has not indicated with any specificity what the content of his rebuttal would have been. Accordingly, we find that the denial of rebuttal testimony does not constitute grounds for reversing the decision. See Karapinka v. Department of Energy, 6 M.S.P.R. 124, 127 (1981).

The privilege of using rebuttal testimony should not be abused. In Lateef v. Dept. of Interior, 6 MSPR 664, 667, 6 MSPB 557 (1981), the agency did not seek to call the appellant as a witness until after both parties rested their cases. The Board determined that it would be unfair to permit the agency to present part of its direct case during its portion of the hearing and then to present additional nonrebuttal evidence after the appellant completed his case.

If a party is to claim error through PFR concerning treatment of rebuttal witnesses, the request for those witnesses must be properly handled before the AJ, Chaney v. USPS, SF-0752-09-0834-I-1 (8/25/2010):

The appellant's vague allegation regarding rebuttal witnesses does not provide a basis for review because it does not contain sufficient specificity to enable the Board to ascertain whether there is a serious evidentiary challenge justifying a complete review of the record. King v. Department of Justice, 81 M.S.P.R. 435, ¶ 3 (1999). In any event, the appellant did not request a rebuttal witness. Having failed to do so at the appropriate time, the appellant may not now be heard to complain. Alaniz v. U.S. Postal Service, 100 M.S.P.R. 105, ¶ 9 (2005). To the extent the appellant is blaming his attorney for not requesting a rebuttal witness, the appellant is responsible for the errors of his chosen representative. Sofio v. Internal Revenue Service, 7 M.S.P.R. 667, 670 (1981).

If the AJ allows the appellant to request rebuttal witnesses after the agency's witnesses complete their testimony and the appellant does not call them, any objections to the AJ's earlier rulings on those witnesses are not preserved for purposes of a PFR challenging those rulings. Gonzalez v. Dept. of Transp., 109 MSPR 250, 256-57 ¶ 13, 2008 MSPB 143 (2008) (some of the witnesses were listed on the prehearing submission and denied subject to a request for them as rebuttal witnesses); see Lee v. EPA, 115 MSPR 533, 544 ¶ 16, 2010 MSPB 240 (2010) (appellant was not deprived of his opportunity to question witnesses when the AJ denied the witness requests but stated she would entertain a request at the hearing to call the individuals as rebuttal witnesses and the appellant did not make that request).
