a. Critical Elements

The emphasis in Chapter 43 cases is usually on performance standards--are the standards reasonably objective, did the appellant meet them, have an opportunity to improve if he did not, and what must an agency establish to prevail if the employee who failed to improve her performance is removed or reduced in grade?

But critical elements are, well, critical. The law requires them. Under 5 USC 4302(c):

(2) as soon as practicable, but not later than October 1, 1981, with respect to initial appraisal periods, and thereafter at the beginning of each following appraisal period, communicating to each employee the performance standards and the critical elements of the employee's position[.]

OPM regulations define a critical element (and its relative, a noncritical element) at 5 CFR 430.203 (and 5 CFR 432.103(b)):

Critical element means a work assignment or responsibility of such importance that unacceptable performance on the element would result in a determination that an employee's overall performance is unacceptable. Such elements shall be used to measure performance only at the individual level.

Non-critical element means a dimension or aspect of individual, team, or organizational performance, exclusive of a critical element, that is used in assigning a summary level. Such elements may include, but are not limited to, objectives, goals, program plans, work plans, and other means of expressing expected performance.

Agencies may use subelements, and agencies and the Board treat discrete subelements as critical elements.

The OPM regulation, 5 CFR 430.203, explains that "performance standard":

...means the management-approved expression of the performance threshold(s), requirement(s), or expectation(s) that must be met to be appraised at a particular level of performance. A performance standard may include, but is not limited to, quality, quantity, timeliness, and manner of performance.

The same regulation requires that critical elements and performance standards be spelled out in a performance plan for each employee:

Performance plan means all of the written, or otherwise recorded, performance elements that set forth expected performance. A plan must include all critical and non-critical elements and their performance standards.

Unacceptable performance, the lynchpin of a Chapter 43 action, is described by 5 USC 4301:

(3) "unacceptable performance" means performance of an employee which fails to meet established performance standards in one or more critical elements of such employee's position.

Then, under 5 USC 4303, for action to be taken against an employee (after the opportunity to improve required by 5 USC 4302(c)(6)):

(b)(1) An employee whose reduction in grade or removal is proposed under this section is entitled to--

(A) 30 days' advance written notice of the proposed action which identifies--

(i) specific instances of unacceptable performance by the employee on which the proposed action is based; and

(ii) the critical elements of the employee's position involved in each instance of unacceptable performance[.]

The statutory provisions are implemented by OPM regulations at 5 CFR Part 432 and by agency performance management systems submitted to and approved by OPM.

In sum, the statutes and regulations require the agency to establish one or more critical elements for each employee's position; those elements describe the major components of the individual's job. Agencies then establish performance standards to establish measures of performance of employees against their positions' critical elements. Elements and standards appear in employees' performance plans, however they the plans are denominated by agency performance management regulations. Those plans are usually issued annually. Against those elements and standards is an employee's performance measured. Deficient performance is then addressed through steps that may culminate in performance-based removals and grade reductions. The discussion that follows concentrates on performance standards and actions taken to address substandard performance. Critical elements are often mentioned in Board and Federal Circuit decisions, but they are not often closely analyzed, with the exception of the occasional case that requires analysis of how a performance standard forming the basis of the action against the employee relates to the critical element relied on by the agency.
