2. Stipulations Directed by Judge

As the Board attempts to shorten its hearings, it is encouraging its AJs to use stipulations to avoid the necessity of proof through testimony. The authority of an AJ to press for stipulations--sometimes as a component of prehearing submissions--was explored in D'Iorio v. DHUD, 34 MSPR 351, 354-55 (1987):

...Under 5 CFR § 1201.41(b)(12), administrative judges have the authority to "[h]old prehearing conferences for the settlement and simplification of issues." This is similar to the power a federal court has under Fed. R. Civ. P. 16 to order pretrial conferences. It is well established that a federal judge may compel stipulation to undisputed facts. See 6 C. Wright & A. Miller, Federal Practice & Procedure, sec. 1527 (1971).

In Holcomb v. Aetna Life Insurance Co., 255 F.2d 577, 580 (10th Cir.)...the court stated that "[T]he purpose of pre-trial conferences is to simplify the issues and eliminate waste of time and money by avoiding unnecessary proof of facts at the trial...[It] is more than a mere conference at which the court seeks to eliminate groundless allegations or denials and the court has the power to compel the parties to agree to all facts concerning which there can be no real issue."

While the Federal Rules are not binding, the Board has recognized that they are to be considered instructive. Further, the authority to compel such stipulations promotes the mandate of 5 CFR § 1201.41(b), that an administrative judge "shall take all necessary action to avoid delay in the disposition of all proceedings."

Moreover, such a stipulation will satisfy a party's burden of proving said facts. 5 CFR § 1201.66. However, an administrative judge may not expand the scope of such a stipulation beyond the purpose for which it was made, nor relieve an agency of its overall burden to support an adverse action by preponderant evidence based on a stipulation that does not prove the essential elements of the charge. See Morris v. Dept. of Air Force, 30 MSPR 343 (1986).

Stipulations are useful if they save time and effort during the hearing. They may also waste time. Some AJs expect the parties to arrive at written stipulations of fact when all of the significant evidence is in dispute. It may be simpler to allow people to testify and let the AJ determine the facts than it is to have the parties waste time arguing the scope of supposedly undisputed facts. The AJ should not attempt to bully the parties into predetermining the appeal through stipulations. If the AJ insists on a stipulation of uncontested facts in a hotly contested case, agree to the essentials, e.g., the name of the employing organization, the grade of the appellant, his work location and chain of command, and leave the remainder of the facts for the hearing. [Refer to Chapter 3, "Prehearing Submission."]
