broida flra Chapter 02: Unit Determinations and Elections Chapter 02: Unit Determinations and Elections, Subchapter 01: Unit Determinations, Section B: Statutorily Excluded Positions, Subsection 02: Supervisors; 5 USC 7112(b)(1), Part a: Guard Units
a. Guard Units

GSA Region 4, Atlanta, Ga. and NFFE, Local 1766, 3 FLRA 175, 176 (1980), considered whether employees classified as GS-6 Supervisory Federal Protective Officers (corporals) were supervisors within the meaning of § 7103(a)(10). The title was not controlling. Some corporals exercised more authority than others:

[S]ignificant differences exist in the authority exerted by Corporals serving as senior officers [where there were no sergeants] as compared to their counterparts stationed at installations headed by Sergeants. Corporals acting as senior officers consistently exercise independent judgment in performing their work. They have significant impact upon the hiring decisions of the Activity, in that they regularly interview prospective employees, (often traveling to conduct these interviews), and their recommendations effectively determine the final hiring decision of the agency. Senior Corporals are responsible for assigning and directing duties of the FPO's (GS-5) and Guards under their command. They regularly visit other offices within their jurisdiction for the purpose of reviewing ongoing operations. Furthermore, the Senior Corporals have a major role in administering the grievance procedure and the authority to initiate discipline. They have the final authority to implement a settlement of a grievance if the settlement is acceptable to the employee. In addition, the Senior Corporals recommend disciplinary actions directly to the supervisory lieutenants. The record indicates that these recommendations are regularly accepted and followed. They also have a major role in determining promotions. Both their personnel evaluations and recommendations are effective in determining the promotional decisions of the Activity. Accordingly, the Authority finds that Corporals serving as senior officers at their particular installations...are supervisors....

[S]upervisory authority of Corporals situated in installations headed by senior Sergeants is extremely limited. There is no indication that they consistently exercise independent judgment in the performance of their duties. These Corporals have no role in the hiring procedure. Additionally, they have no authority to promote, little if any jurisdiction over assignments, no role in the grievance procedure, and their authority to discipline is limited to making recommendations to the Senior Sergeant. Their administrative duties are few, and are routine and clerical in nature. There is no evidence in the record to indicate whether they conduct training sessions for other FPO's and guards. Moreover, the record reveals that the Activity itself recognizes that the supervisory authority of Corporals serving under Sergeants exists only in the absence of the Senior Sergeant and is not consistently exercised. Accordingly the Authority finds that Corporals serving at installations...in which a Sergeant is stationed and acts as the senior officer are not supervisors....

Guard sergeants were excluded from one unit and included in another. Those excluded conducted some hiring interviews, recommended some hiring decisions to the guard captain, directed work through the consistent use of independent judgment, approved leave, assigned and approved overtime, prepared performance evaluations, and recommended promotions and disciplinary actions. Not supervisors were Guard sergeants having no part in employees' hiring or discipline, exercising only routine authority as to assigning work, having only occasional involvement in recommending promotions, and ordinarily not assigning overtime. Their work involved more technical direction of work than supervision of subordinates. Naval Undersea Warfare Eng'g Station, Keyport, Wash. and AFGE Local 48, 7 FLRA 526, 530-31 (1981). GS-6 police supervisors (sergeants) were supervisors. They had authority to assign and remove patrol officers to and from posts or assignments, adjust their work priorities for unsatisfactory performance, direct them in the assignment of their duties, approve or disapprove their shift swaps and requests for sick or annual leave, counsel and reprimand patrol officers, and recommend more severe disciplinary action. Dept. of Navy, Portsmouth Naval Shipyard and AFGE Local 2024, 38 FLRA 764, 766 (1990).
