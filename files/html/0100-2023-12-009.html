broida flra Chapter 04: Subjects of Bargaining: Procedures for Negotiability Determinations Chapter 04: Subjects of Bargaining: Procedures for Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 04: Framework for Negotiability Appeals, Section B: Development of Factual Record, Subsection 01: Hearings

1. Hearings

FLRA has discretion under 5 CFR 2424.31 (2022) to hold a hearing in a negotiability case, but the request will be denied if the record contains sufficient evidence to permit a reasoned decision. Fed. Firefighters Ass'n and Air Force, Otis Air Nat'l Guard Base, 34 FLRA 855, 858 (1990); see NAIL Local 7 and Dept. of Air Force, Seymour Johnson AFB, 64 FLRA 1194, 1194 (2010) ("the Union does not raise disputed issues of material fact necessitating a hearing, and we deny the Union's request"); MEBA and Dept. of Navy, Military Sealift Command, 60 FLRA 828, 829 (2005) ("Here the Union has not set forth any disputed issues of material fact for the Authority to consider at a hearing."); cf. AFGE Local 1030 and DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, FDC Houston, 57 FLRA 901, 902 (2002) (request for hearing denied; no disputed issues of material fact). A hearing on negotiability was held on referral to an FLRA ALJ in ACT Roughrider Chap. and DOD, N.D. NG, 56 FLRA 256, 256-57 (2000) (ALJ noting "The purposes of the hearing the Authority directed to be held was to develop a full and complete record concerning the meaning and context of each provision in dispute and to clarify any other matters regarding the parties' arguments as deemed necessary by the Judge.").

Before changes of the negotiability regulations in 1999, negotiability appeals rarely involved hearings. Although hearings were infrequently employed in the past, see NFFE Local 1418 and USIA, Voice of Am., 49 FLRA 1262, 1264-65 (1994) (involving a hearing on a complex negotiability issue concerning duties of employees prior to 1972 as those duties pertained to current craft jurisdiction of Prevailing Rate Act employees), they do occur. ACT Old Hickory Chap. and DOD, N.C. NGB, 55 FLRA 811, 811 (1999), noted as to a negotiability dispute that FLRA had "transferred the case...for assignment to an administrative law judge...for factfinding with respect to one of the provisions." See NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., 55 FLRA 1174, 1174 (1999) (negotiability dispute referred to ALJ to conduct hearing on meaning and content of provisions); cf. ACT Wichita Air Capitol Chap. and DOD, Civilian Personnel Mgmt. Serv., 57 FLRA 50, 51 (2001) ("On occasion, when confronting questions as to the interpretation of a provision agreed to by the parties and disapproved on agency head review,...the Authority has referred the question of interpretation to an Administrative Law Judge.... Where the Authority can determine the meaning of a provision, however, the Authority proceeds to resolve whether the provision was properly disapproved.").

A party's request for a hearing in a negotiability appeal will not be granted if facts concerning the proposal are immaterial to resolution of the negotiability dispute, AFGE Local 997 and Dept. of Air Force, Maxwell AFB, 66 FLRA 499, 499 (2012):

The Union requests a hearing. The Union asserts that a hearing would allow the Authority to ask questions about the uniform requirement's technical and procedural effects on civilian employees. Under § 2424.31 of the Authority's Regulations, the Authority may order a hearing "[w]hen necessary to resolve disputed issues of material fact." 5 C.F.R. § 2424.31. But facts about the uniform requirement's technical and procedural effects are not material to resolving the negotiability of the Union's proposal. Because the Union does not raise an issue of material fact necessitating a hearing, we deny the Union's hearing request. See NAIL, Local 7, 64 FLRA 1194, 1194 (2010) (Local 7), rev'd as to another matter, U.S. Dep't of the Air Force v. FLRA, 648 F.3d 841 (D.C. Cir. 2011).

Similarly, AFGE Council of Prison Locals 33 and DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, 65 FLRA 142, 145 (2010), explained:

The Union requests a hearing to resolve certain issues in this case. Under 5 C.F.R. § 2424.31 of the Authority's Regulations, a hearing is appropriate "[w]hen necessary to resolve disputed issues of material fact...." Here, the Union has not set forth any disputed issues of material fact for the Authority to consider at a hearing. Therefore, we deny the Union's request for a hearing. See NFFE, Local 1998, IAMAW, Fed. Dist. 1, 60 FLRA 141, 143 (2004); Prof'l Airways Sys. Specialists, 59 FLRA 25, 25 n.2 (2003).

See NFFE Local 1998 and Dept. of State, Passport Serv., 60 FLRA 141, 143 (2004) ("Here, the Union has not set forth any disputed issues of material fact for the Authority to consider at a hearing. Therefore, we deny the Union's request for a hearing."); PASS and Dept. of Transp., FAA Kansas City, 59 FLRA 485, 486 (2003) ("We find that the Union's claim presents no disputed issues of material fact. Consequently, we conclude that a hearing is not necessary to resolve the negotiability of the proposal and we deny the Union's request for a hearing."); AFGE Local 1827 and DOD NIMA, 58 FLRA 344, 344 n.3 (2003) ("In addition, we deny the Union's request for a hearing because nothing in the request or in the record raises a factual issue that needs to be resolved to determine the negotiability of the proposals at issue. See 5 C.F.R. § 2424.31 (a hearing is appropriate '[w]hen necessary to resolve disputed issues of material fact').").

The standard practice is that FLRA develops the record through written position statements. Many negotiability disputes are resolved through the post-petition conference, [discussed later in this chapter, "Post-Petition Conference; Alternative Dispute Resolution"]; as to cases that continue past the conference stage, there are no real indications in most reported cases of exactly how FLRA resolves disputed factual issues presented in the position statements. Some decisions reflect agreements reached at conferences held by FLRA representatives with the parties as part of the negotiability appeal process. See 5 CFR 2424.23. Past resolution of factual disputes involved in negotiability issues was more by implication than resolution. In IAM and Dept. of Treasury, Bureau of Engraving & Printing, 33 FLRA 711, 727-29 (1988) (Proposal 8), concerning the negotiability of a proposal governing overtime distribution, the implied factual issue was whether the employees were interchangeable--or whether the proposal was nonnegotiable because it interfered with management's right to select for overtime those employees whose qualifications best matched the work to be performed. FLRA noted that the union asserted that the employees were generally interchangeable. It then observed that the agency "does not acknowledge" interchangeability. Finally, FLRA stated that "the Agency's denials of the Union's assertions were not challenged by the Union." From all of that, FLRA appeared to operate on the basis that the employees differed in their skills and held the proposal nonnegotiable.
