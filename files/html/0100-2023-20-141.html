6. Promotion, Appointment

"[A]ny agency actions in filling positions for which the appellant applied under a vacancy announcement that has not been canceled and was not selected would constitute 'personnel actions' within the meaning of 5 USC 2302(a)(2)." Chianelli v. EPA, 81 MSPR 141, 145 ¶ 12 (1999). Nonselection for promotion is a covered personnel action. Monasteri v. MSPB, 232 F.3d 1376, 1380 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (WPA provides Board jurisdiction over a claim that an individual was denied an appointment or promotion because of a protected disclosure); Horton v. Dept. of Navy, 66 F.3d 279 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (IRA covers the nonselection of applicants and the termination of probationers); Pulcini v. SSA, 83 MSPR 685, 693 ¶ 15 (1999) (nonselection for an announced vacant position is a covered personnel action); Gilmore v. Dept. of Army, 83 MSPR 16, 19 ¶ 5 (1999) (promotion or "position upgrade" constituted a covered personnel action concerning pay, benefits, or awards), followed, Boughton v. USDA, 94 MSPR 347, 351 ¶ 10 (2003); McVay v. Arkansas Nat'l Guard, 80 MSPR 120, 123 (1998) (promotion was covered under an IRA appeal); Pashun v. Dept. of Treasury, 74 MSPR 374, 381-82 (1997) ("Nonselection for a position under a vacancy announcement that has not been canceled is a 'personnel action' under the WPA[.]"); Ward v. Dept. of Army, 67 MSPR 482, 485 (1995) ("Here, the nonselection for promotion challenged by the appellant is a personnel action covered by 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(A)(ii) and 5 C.F.R. § 1209.4(a)(2)"); Paul v. USDA, 66 MSPR 643, 648-49 (1995); Niswonger v. Dept. of Air Force, 60 MSPR 655, 658 (1994) (nonselection for temporary promotion was a covered action). Under Ruggieri v. MSPB, 454 F.3d 1323, 1325-27 (Fed. Cir. 2006), supra, the retaliatory decision to cancel a vacancy is actionable as an IRA case. An appointment at an incorrect grade level is a covered personnel action. Cambric-Gumbs v. Dept. of Air Force, DA-3443-14-0183-I-1 (NP 11/4/2014); cf. Weed v. SSA, 113 MSPR 221, 226-30 ¶¶ 8-12, 2010 MSPB 23 (2010) (IRA appeal could be based on the retaliatory determination of an agency to avoid hiring the appellant by using the FCIP appointing authority to avoid open competition for a position).

The failure to promote an individual noncompetitively qualified for IRA protection, Briley v. Nat'l Archives and Records Admin., 71 MSPR 211, 221-22 (1996):

On petition for review, the agency reasserts that the classification of the appellant's position and the failure to create a GS-12 Archivist position are not "personnel actions" under the WPA. Among the personnel actions listed at 5 USC § 2302(a)(2)(A) is "a promotion." 5 USC § 2302(a)(2)(A)(ii). In previous cases in which the Board has considered whether a failure to promote was a personnel action under the WPA, the agency had announced a vacancy and filled it with another individual. See Costin, 64 MSPR at 528-30; Di Pompo v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 62 MSPR 44, 47-48 (1994); Slake v. Department of the Treasury, 53 MSPR 207, 210-14 (1992). In this appeal, the agency issued no vacancy announcement and has argued that it did not intend to create a GS-12 Archivist position and that the appellant's duties did not support a promotion to GS-12. The appellant has argued that her duties as a GS-11 Archivist support a GS-12 grade level and that other agency Archivists in other presidential libraries performing the same tasks occupy GS-12 Archivist positions.

An employee in the Federal service may receive an appointment to a higher graded position by competitive appointment, through a career-ladder promotion program, or by a reclassification of her position to a higher grade than the one she occupies. See 5 CFR part 335. Under 5 CFR § 335.103(c)(2)(i), an agency need not apply competitive procedures to "[a] promotion resulting from the upgrading of a position without significant change in the duties and responsibilities due to issuance of a new classification standard or the correction of an initial classification error."

According to the appellant, her duties as a GS-11 Archivist are the same as those performed by GS-12 Archivists in other Presidential libraries, and those Archivists had had their positions reclassified from a GS-11 to a GS-12 Archivist shortly before the appellant's request for a similar reclassification. Thus, the appellant maintains that her position should also have been reclassified at the GS-12 level.

Thus, the appellant must show that, at the time of her request for reclassification, she was entitled to a promotion via reclassification because the GS-11 Archivist positions comparable to hers had been recently reclassified elsewhere by the agency at a GS-12 level because of a change in the classification standards or a classification error; the appellant must also prove that she would have met the legal and qualification requirements for promotion. See Spicer v. Department of Defense, 59 MSPR 359, 362-63 (1993). We find under the circumstances of this appeal that, if the appellant's allegations, as set forth above, are proven on remand, the agency's failure to reclassify her position as a GS-12 Archivist position would constitute a "personnel action" for IRA jurisdictional purposes.

Moreover, viewing the same circumstances under 5 USC § 2302(a)(2)(A)(ix), as "a decision concerning pay," we find that the agency's refusal to reclassify the GS-11 position at the GS-12 level, if the appellant's allegations are proven on remand, would constitute a "personnel action" under this subsection for IRA jurisdictional purposes. See, e.g., Houlihan v. Office of Personnel Management, 909 F.2d 383, 384 (9th Cir. 1990) (a misclassification of a position in the Federal service can be a "prohibited personnel practice" under 5 USC § 2302(a), (b)). As the rate of basic pay for a GS-12 position is higher than that for a GS-11 position, the decision not to reclassify the appellant's position, if her allegations are proven on remand, would constitute "a decision concerning pay." See, e.g., Carter v. Department of the Army, 62 MSPR 393, 400 (1994) (the agency's failure to waive qualification requirements for reduction in force purposes, resulting in the employee's separation, concerned pay or benefits and was a "personnel action" under 5 USC § 2302(a)(2)(A)(ix)), aff'd, 45 F.3d 444 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table).

Failure to correct an erroneous appointment by promoting, or properly appointing, the appellant to the proper grade level is a personnel action in the form of an appointment or a decision concerning pay, benefits, or awards. See Orr v. Dept. of Treasury, 83 MSPR 117, 123 ¶ 13 & n.3 (1999).

Cassidy v. DOJ, DA-1221-11-0365-B-1 (NP 8/30/2013) (aff'd, Cassidy v. DOJ, 581 Fed. Appx. 846 (Fed. Cir. 2014 NP)), applied the knowledge/timing test and Carr v. SSA, 185 F.3d 1318, 1323 (Fed. Cir. 1999), to a nonselection:

To obtain corrective action, the appellant must meet his initial burden of establishing by preponderant evidence that his whistleblowing activity was a contributing factor in the personnel actions in dispute. 5 U.S.C. § 1221(e)(1); McCarthy v. International Boundary & Water Commission, 116 M.S.P.R. 594, ¶ 39 (2011), aff'd, 497 F. App'x 4 (Fed. Cir. 2012). The administrative judge found, based on the knowledge/timing test, that the appellant met such burden here, and we discern no reason to disturb this finding. See Schnell v. Department of the Army, 114 M.S.P.R. 83, ¶ 21 (2010) (once an appellant has satisfied the knowledge/timing test, he has demonstrated that a protected disclosure was a contributing factor in a personnel action, even if a reasonable fact finder could not conclude that the whistleblowing was a contributing factor in the personnel action after a complete analysis of all of the evidence). The burden of proof then shifts to the agency to establish by clear and convincing evidence that it would have taken the same personnel actions absent the protected activity. See McCarthy, 116 M.S.P.R. 594, ¶ 42. We agree with the administrative judge's determination that the agency demonstrated by clear and convincing evidence that it would not have selected the appellant for either position absent his protected disclosures.

In determining whether an agency has shown by clear and convincing evidence that it would have taken the same personnel actions in the absence of whistleblowing, the Board will consider the following factors: (1) the strength of the agency's evidence in support of its action; (2) the existence and strength of any motive to retaliate on the part of the agency officials who were involved in the decision; and (3) any evidence that the agency takes similar actions against employees who are not whistleblowers but who are otherwise similarly situated. Carr v. Social Security Administration, 185 F.3d 1318, 1323 (Fed. Cir. 1999). The first two of these factors appear to be the most pertinent in this appeal.[4] Here, upon consideration of these factors, the administrative judge found that: (1) the agency had legitimate reasons for not selecting the appellant for either position, mainly that two Immigration Judges had raised concerns regarding the appellant's temperament and that his interview for the Houston position was not overly impressive; (2) other than the circumstantial evidence with respect to the knowledge/timing test, the evidence of record failed to show that any of the individuals involved in the selection process had any motive to retaliate against the appellant for making his protected disclosures; and (3) the agency also failed to select a similarly situated non-whistleblower for the Houston position.

[4] The third factor in the Carr formulation has obvious relevance where the action in question is a disciplinary action, i.e., the appellant claims he was punished or punished more harshly than non-whistleblowers who were not punished or punished more leniently. This factor has much less relevance where the contested action is a nonselection, as it would be highly unlikely that the selectee would also have made protected disclosures.
