2. Improper Personnel Recommendation; 5 USC 2302(b)(2)

Under 5 USC 2302:

(b) Any employee who has authority to take, direct others to take, recommend, or approve any personnel action, shall not, with respect to such authority--

...

(2) solicit or consider any recommendation or statement, oral or written, with respect to any individual who requests or is under consideration for any personnel action unless such recommendation or statement is based on the personal knowledge or records of the person furnishing it and consists of--

(A)  an evaluation of the work performance, ability, aptitude, or general qualifications of such individual; or

(B)  an evaluation of the character, loyalty, or suitability of such individual[.]

A defense was raised that this section precluded an adverse action based upon a proposal and a decision issued by anyone without personal knowledge of the events leading to the action. The Board rejected the defense. The law was intended to avoid use of improper influence to obtain positions for favored individuals. The law did not preclude the agency from taking an action because the proposing and deciding official lacked personal knowledge of the basis of the action. Middleton v. DOJ, 23 MSPR 223, 228 (1984). Similarly, under Mattison v. VA, 123 MSPR 492, 498 ¶ 11, 2016 MSPB 27 (2016):

Section 2302(b)(2) prohibits agency officials from soliciting statements from individuals about a person under consideration for a personnel action unless the statement is based on personal knowledge or the records of the person providing it and concerns matters such as the performance, qualifications, character, or suitability of the individual at issue. However, as our reviewing court has explained, section 2302(b)(2) "relates to statements or recommendations by outsiders, like senators or congressmen; the legislative objective was to forestall political or partisan interference in personnel actions." Depte v. United States, 715 F.2d 1481, 1484 (Fed. Cir. 1983), overruled on other grounds by Stone v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 179 F.3d 1368, 1376 n.4 (Fed. Cir. 1999). As a result, the appellant's reliance upon section 2302(b)(2) is misplaced. Despite his conclusory assertion that the proposing official had no factual information to support his analysis of two Douglas factors, his allegations do not support a prohibited personnel practice claim under section 2302(b)(2). The proposing official was not an outsider; he was an agency manager. Accordingly, we find that the appellant failed to establish a violation of 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(2).

See Sadowski v. USPS, 28 MSPR 461, 462 n.* (1985) (citing Williamson v. DHHS, 3 MSPR 18, 22, 3 MSPB 142 (1980) ("[t]he 'recommendation or statement' considered in taking action against appellant was 'based on the personal knowledge or records of the person furnishing it,' and...this information clearly concerns appellant's 'general qualifications' for retention in his position.")); Roane v. DHHS, 8 MSPR 339, 343 (1981) ("[t]he sparse legislative history of the statutory provision indicates that the section was intended to prevent the use of improper influence to obtain a position or promotion.").

Nor did the section preclude a deciding official from considering, as to the ability of the appellant to get along with supervisors, statements of a former supervisor that were based on the supervisor's personal knowledge. These statements concerned "appellant's character and suitability" and "do not fit within the statutory prohibition of 5 USC 2302(b)(2)." Woodward v. Dept. of Interior, 40 MSPR 649, 654 (1989); see Bize v. Dept. of Treasury, 3 MSPR 155, 168, 3 MSPB 261 (1980) ("The clear language of the statute does not prohibit hypothetical discussions to give an official broader knowledge on which to base a personal decision or recommendation.... There is no indication in the record that the proposing official solicited or considered any recommendation or statement with respect to the appellant individually."). The section prohibits solicitation or consideration of recommendations based on political considerations. Acting Special Counsel v. Sullivan, 6 MSPR 526, 529, 6 MSPB 442 (1981).

Wenzel v. Dept. of Interior, 33 MSPR 344, 351-52 (1987), discerned nothing objectionable, and no prohibited personnel practice, in agency interviews of individuals with information necessary for evaluating and proposing an adverse action:

Appellant contends that the agency committed a prohibited personnel practice when, in deciding on this removal action, it considered ex parte communications with his co-workers and other persons. Specifically, the agency considered summaries of telephone interviews and affidavits solicited from agency employees and other persons outside the agency who had a professional affiliation with appellant in his capacity as a Wildlife Management Biologist. The agency conducted these interviews for the purpose of determining what impact appellant's alleged misconduct would have on his ability to function with those individuals. Appellant contends that these interviews were in fact illegal ex parte communications amounting to a prohibited personnel practice under 5 USC § 2302(b)(2)(A) and (B). We disagree.

The legislative history of this provision indicates that it was designed to preclude an agency's reliance on statements or recommendations by outsiders and to specifically avoid partisan or political interference in effecting a personnel action. See Depte v. United States, 715 F.2d 1481, 1484 (Fed. Cir. 1983). However, this provision does not apply to situations where an agency takes statements from persons who are somehow involved in the incident giving rise to the disciplinary proceeding. Id. at 1484-85.

In the present case, appellant's responsibilities included serving as a liaison with state agencies, wilderness groups, and universities, concerning enforcement of federal wildlife endangered species laws and the trading of confidential information pertinent to the protection of endangered species, which includes the peregrine falcon. The agency charged that appellant's alleged misconduct adversely affected the agency's ability to fulfill its mission and thus irreparably damaged appellant's ability to carry out that mission effectively. We find that in order to determine what damage appellant's misconduct had done, the agency had a legitimate right to contact these persons and consider their statements in its decision on appellant's removal. Such action does not amount to a prohibited personnel practice within the meaning of 5 USC § 2302(b)(2)(A) and (B). The agency has demonstrated that the statements were taken for the specific purpose of investigating the incidents giving rise to the removal action and in no way reflect any outside political or partisan interference in personnel actions. Depte at 1484. Thus, we conclude that appellant's removal was not based on a prohibited personnel practice.
