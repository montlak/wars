6. Scope of Competition

Woolery v. OPM, 4 MSPR 142, 143-45 (1980), disclaimed jurisdiction over a claimed employment practice challenge by an individual who complained both of incorrect information pertaining to eligibility for a position and of an agency's decision to limit competition for a position to agency employees:

Tom B. Woolery (the appellant) petitioned the Atlanta Field Office of the Merit Systems Protection Board for appeal of his non-selection for the position of Automotive Equipment Inspector, WG-11, with the General Services Administration (GSA). The appellant alleged that, although he intended to apply for the position, he submitted no application because employees of the Office of Personnel Management (OPM), whom he contacted about the position, informed him that he did not have the necessary civil service status and that the position was restricted to GSA employees. He alleged, further, that he later learned that he did have civil service status at the time of his inquiry and that the position in question had been filled by a recently retired veteran, like himself, who was not a GSA employee when he applied for the position. The copy of the vacancy announcement for the position in question, attached to the original petition for appeal, bore no indication that consideration would be restricted to GSA employees.

The presiding official dismissed the appeal for lack of jurisdiction, concluding that a selection for a particular vacancy does not constitute an appealable "employment practice" under 5 CFR 300.101 and 300.104. The appellant petitioned for review of this dismissal, and OPM opposed the petition.

OPM has asserted in its response to the petition that administrative errors in its dissemination of information concerning civil service employment do not represent appealable "employment practices" under 5 CFR part 300. We agree that such errors are not appealable "employment practices" as that term is circumscribed at 5 CFR 300.101.

OPM has contended further in its response that restriction of the scope of competition for a vacant position does not constitute an appealable "employment practice" because it is a management tool rather than a measurement device, and that, if GSA did impose such a restriction in this instance, it is not an appealable "employment practice" in any event because it was not "applied" to the appellant by OPM, as it must be for the Board's appellate jurisdiction to attach under 5 CFR 300.104(a). While intimating no opinion on the former contention, we find that the Board lacks jurisdiction in this appeal because OPM "applied" no restrictions on the position in question. Id. If any restriction was applied to that position, GSA was the imposing authority.

OPM's regulations relating to selection for appointment or promotion, 5 CFR parts 300, 302, 332, and 335, make no mention of restricting the scope of competition for a position. OPM's Federal Personnel Manual (FPM) does discuss such restriction to an agency's employees in the context of merit promotion plans. However, while mandating that "[a]gencies must develop, publish, and administer merit promotion plans which apply the policies and requirements of their promotion guidelines to positions to be filled through promotion," FPM ch. 335, subch. 3-1(a) (October 25, 1973), the FPM only offers guidance for administering those merit promotion plans developed by the agencies.

The FPM does not require agencies to limit competition for a position to agency employees under any particular circumstances. That discretion is to be exercised by the agency, as indicated by the following provision:

The decision whether to restrict consideration to agency employees or to consider concurrently persons from outside the agency should be based on the nature of the job to be filled and on the number of highly qualified candidates available within the agency.

...It is entirely proper that positions be filled within the organization as a general rule.

...An agency, however, has to guard against the dangers of inbreeding in positions above the entrance level where fresh viewpoints and new ideas are necessary....

...Concurrent consideration also may be used in filling positions for which not enough highly qualified candidates can be located within the agency....

FPM ch. 335, subch. 3-3(e) (October 25, 1973). Thus, although OPM generally supports an agency's use of such restriction on the scope of competition for a position in administering a merit promotion plan, OPM cannot be considered to "apply" such restrictions to a candidate for a position in another agency, like the appellant, as must be the case for the Board to have appellate jurisdiction under 5 CFR 300.104(a).
