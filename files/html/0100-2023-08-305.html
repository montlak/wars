b. Length of Service Before Loss of Restoration Right

Under 38 USC 4312(a)(2) the maximum length of military service before restoration rights are lost is five years. Erickson v. USPS, 571 F.3d 1364, 1366-72 (Fed. Cir. 2009). [Erickson is discussed in this chapter, "Dismissal From Employment; Demotion; Abandonment of Civilian Career."] Under a prior statute setting the maximum time at 4 1/2 years, Sheldon v. Dept. of Navy, 46 MSPR 88, 90-92 (1990):

Subsection 2024(b)(1) sets out a general four-year limitation on the period a Reservist can perform active military duty and retain restoration rights to his civilian employment. Subsection 2024(b)(2) extends this four-year eligibility period in limited circumstances, as follows:

(2) Any [Reservist] who...enters upon active duty...or whose active duty is...extended during a period when the President is authorized to order [the Reserves] to active duty shall have the [4-year] service limitation governing eligibility for reemployment rights under subsection (b)(1) of this section extended by such member's period of such active duty, but not to exceed that period of active duty to which the President is authorized to order [the Reserves] . With respect to a member who voluntarily enters upon active duty or whose active duty is voluntarily extended, the provisions of this subsection shall apply only when such additional active duty is at the request and for the convenience of the Federal Government.

(Emphasis supplied.)

There is no dispute as to the meaning of the first sentence of subsection 2024(b)(2). It extends the four-year eligibility period when a Reservist's active duty occurs during a period when the President is authorized to call up the Reserves to active duty, but only by the maximum period that the President can call up the Reserves, which is presently 180 days. The issue is whether the second sentence was intended to abrogate the otherwise applicable time limits when a Reservist's active duty is "at the request and for the convenience of the Federal Government." We find that the plain meaning of that language simply creates an additional requirement that a Reservist must satisfy in order to qualify for restoration, and that the time limits of section 2024(b) remain applicable.

Our construction of subsection 2024(b)(2) is supported by the Senate committee report accompanying the 1968 amendments to the statute now codified at 38 U.S.C. § 2024(b):

[T]his bill would provide reemployment rights for reservists whose service after August 1, 1961, is for more than 4 years if such additional service (1) occurs during a period when the President is authorized to order the Ready Reserve to active duty; (2) is not longer than the period for which the President is authorized to order the Reserve to active duty; and (3) is at the request, and for the convenience, of the Federal Government in the case of those reservists who voluntarily enter upon or extend their active duty.

S. Rep. No. 1477, 90th Cong., 2d Sess., reprinted in 1968 U.S. Code Cong. & Admin. News 3421, 3422 (emphasis added). The Senate report also reflects that, concurrent with the 1968 amendments, Congress amended 5 U.S.C. § 3551, because the latter provision

apparently [gave] Government employees...an unqualified right of restoration to their jobs upon release from active duty, irrespective of how long they have been on active duty. Consequently the Comptroller General was required to hold recently that a former employee of the Civil Aeronautics Authority was entitled to back pay as a consequence of a failure to reemploy him promptly under section 3551...after he had served more than 12 years on active duty and had been retired for length of service. Such as requirement obviously was unintended and the committee has amended section 3551 to limit the time that reservists and National Guardsmen who are Government employees...may serve without losing reemployment rights...[to] the same as that which applies to other persons...

S. Rep No. 1477, 1968 U.S. Code Cong. & Admin. News at 3423.

This Senate report leaves no doubt that the requirements of subsection 2024(b)(2) are to be read in the conjunctive, not in the alternative. A pertinent Congressional committee report is highly persuasive evidence of legislative intent. See Zuber v. Allen, 396 U.S. 168, 186 (1969); Hanson v. Office of Personnel Management 33 MSPR 581, 588, aff'd, 833 F.2d 1568 (Fed. Cir. 1987). We thus conclude that the maximum period that a Reservist can serve on active duty and retain restoration rights under 38 U.S.C. § 2024(b)(2) is 4 years and 180 days.

The appellant here served more than 11 years on active duty before seeking restoration to his civilian employment. Indeed, the appellant's situation is strikingly similar to that of the employee cited in the above committee report, who sought restoration to his civilian employment after more than 12 years of active duty and his retirement from the military. Accordingly, we find that the appellant does not have restoration rights under 38 U.S.C. § 2024(b)(2), and conclude that the administrative judge properly dismissed his appeal for lack of jurisdiction.
