b. Revocation of Driver's License

An individual removed because his driver's license was revoked for refusing to take a breathalyzer test did not demonstrate that removal for his subsequent inability to drive his work vehicle was a result of alcoholism. The appellant did not show that his refusal to take the test was caused by alcoholism or intoxication. Nor did he show that he lacked control over his actions when he refused to take the test, Malbouf v. Dept. of Army, 43 MSPR 588, 592-93 (1990):

To establish an affirmative defense of handicap discrimination, an appellant must prove not only that he is a "qualified handicapped person," but also that his handicap caused his misconduct or that the misconduct was entirely a manifestation of his handicap. See Brinkley v. Veterans Administration, 37 MSPR 682, 684 (1988). Even assuming arguendo that the appellant here was a "qualified handicapped person," we find that he did not prove that his failure to maintain a valid driver's license, which resulted in his removal, either was "caused" by or was "entirely a manifestation of" his handicap of alcoholism.

The appellant was not removed because he was an alcoholic or even as a result of his arrest on suspicion of driving while intoxicated, which one could argue is a manifestation of alcoholism. Rather, he was removed because he failed to maintain a current driver's license, which was a direct result of his own action in refusing to take a breathalyzer test after his arrest. The appellant has adduced no argument or evidence that his refusal to take the test was caused by alcoholism or even intoxication at the time. Nor did he show that he lacked control over his actions when he refused to take the test. Hence, the appellant's failure to meet an essential condition of his employment and his resulting removal were due to his own intentional and volitional actions and not solely because of his alcohol abuse. See Richardson, 613 F. Supp. at 1215-16. Cf. Spragg v. Campbell, 466 F. Supp. 658, 660-61 (D.S.D. 1979) (an electrician whose job required driving and who was terminated for having an automobile accident while intoxicated was not removed solely because of alcohol abuse, but for an accident showing that he was unable to perform all of the essential duties of his electrician position).

Finally, even assuming arguendo that the appellant was a "qualified handicapped person" and that his handicap caused his misconduct or that the misconduct was entirely a manifestation of his handicap, we find that the agency satisfied any obligation that it may have had under Ignacio v. United States Postal Service, 30 MSPR 471, 486-87 (Spec. Pan. 1986), to consider reassignment as a reasonable accommodation of the appellant's condition by attempting to find vacant positions for him that would not require a valid driver's license. That the agency was unsuccessful in its search for a suitable vacant position does not detract from the sufficiency of its efforts in that regard. The requirement that an agency accommodate a handicapped employee is not infinite--while an agency must search its vacancies and take other potentially fruitful steps, it need not consider reassignment ad infinitum or create a position where none exists. See Patrick v. Department of the Air Force, 39 MSPR 392, 396 (1988).

See Rednall v. Dept. of Army, 54 MSPR 343, 346 (1992) ("The fact that the appellant's license was suspended because of his conviction for driving while intoxicated does not mean that his concealing the suspension from the agency and driving agency vehicles without a license were related to alcohol abuse.").
