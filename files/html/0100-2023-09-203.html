1. Acknowledgment of Appeal

Once the appeal is filed, the Board's regional director decides the AJ to be assigned to the appeal. An acknowledgment order is sent (or issued by e-filing) to the appellant (and any representative) to inform him of the major steps in processing the appeal. The acknowledgment order could be signed by the regional director of the Board office where the appeal is filed (and the AJ assigned to the case will later identify himself or herself) or the acknowledgment order will be signed by the assigned AJ. At the same time, the agency is provided a copy of the appeal and acknowledgment order and is directed to respond to the appeal in the format required by the order. The time for discovery formally begins within 30 days after the agency is issued an order, although nothing prevents the appellant from initiating discovery sooner, i.e., between the date the appeal is filed and the date the order is issued directing the agency to respond. 5 CFR 1201.73(d)(1) (2022). If the appellant amends his appeal to include a different basis for invoking the Board's jurisdiction and that basis arose after the filing of the original appeal, the AJ may need to issue a second acknowledgment or jurisdictional order, Delalat v. Dept. of Navy, 86 MSPR 455, 461 ¶ 14 (2000), but ordinarily an appeal is amended, as permitted by Board rules, without issuance of another acknowledgment order. Another acknowledgment order might be issued if there were a new claim presented through an amendment, e.g., a performance-based removal is added to a WIGI denial, or a USERRA allegation is added to an adverse action appeal. It will be in the discretion of the AJ as to how to treat the amendment or new claim, and the agency may have something to say about the amendment or claim, should there be a question of jurisdiction or timeliness, through a motion to a dismiss.

Acknowledgment orders are assembled in sections by grouping word-processing files. Some AJs are not careful in the compilation of these orders. Form sometimes trumps substance: clauses occasionally conflict; some clauses are inapplicable or incorrectly imply the existence of a jurisdictional or timeliness problem; at times the guidance provided by these orders is incorrect. Since the acknowledgment order is the first contact of the parties with the AJ, a poorly assembled order provides an unfortunate introduction to the Board. Judge Newman noted in her dissent to Mendoza v. MSPB, 966 F.2d 650, 656 (Fed. Cir. 1992), "[t]he issuance of form 'orders' in inapplicable circumstances can only confuse the public that OPM and the Board are designed to serve." See Hamilton v. MSPB, 75 F.3d 639, 645-46 (Fed. Cir. 1996) ("The Order is a formidable document to digest, particularly as some stock paragraphs may be inapplicable. For example, there was no basis for raising a question respecting the Board's jurisdiction in this case, an issue on which the AJ ordered evidence and argument."). It may occur that an appeal is clearly timely and that the appellant clearly designated counsel in his or her appeal, but the acknowledgment order is issued with a paragraph asking for a submission on timeliness and another paragraph telling the appellant how to designate a representative. It all gets worked out in the end, but the confusion, aggravation, and time wasted through the use of form acknowledgment orders could and should be avoided by AJs paying attention to appeals and appropriately crafting their acknowledgment orders. The Board should require AJs to tailor their acknowledgment orders to the contours of the appeal. Until that day comes--if ever--should portions of the order be confusing, conflicting, or incomprehensible, ask the AJ for clarification or object to provisions that are improper, burdensome, or prejudicial.

Chapter 3 of the Judges' Handbook provides the Board's guidance to its AJs concerning preparation of acknowledgment orders:

7. ACKNOWLEDGMENT AND SHOW CAUSE ORDERS. Prior to the assignment of an appeal, the CAJ (or designee) should make every effort to ensure that the parties receive clear and relevant information related to MSPB's processing of the appeal. A special effort should be made to inform the parties of the burdens and standards of proof applicable to their appeal because an appellant must receive explicit information on what is required to establish an appealable jurisdictional issue. Burgess v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 758 F.2d 641 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Further, the Board requires that even beyond jurisdiction, the AJ must explain the burdens and methods of proof of any claim as to which the appellant has some or all of the burden of methods of proof of any claim as to which the appellant has some or all of the burden of proof or production in an appeal:

[W]hen an appellant raises an affirmative defense in an appeal either by checking the appropriate box in an appeal form, identifying an affirmative defense by name such as "race discrimination," "harmful procedural error," etc., or by alleging facts that reasonably raise such an affirmative defense, the AJ must address the affirmative defense(s) in any close of record order or prehearing conference summary and order. If an appellant expresses the intention to withdraw such an affirmative defense, in the close of record order or prehearing conference order the AJ must, at a minimum, identify the affirmative defense, explain that the Board will no longer consider it when deciding the appeal, and give an appellant an opportunity to object to withdrawal of the affirmative defense. Wynn v. U.S. Postal Service, 115 M.S.P.R. 146, ¶ 10 (2010).

Where a standard acknowledgment order is available in HotDocs, it should be used. In the absence of such standard language, the AJ should provide the necessary information. If there are repetitive situations which an AJ, CAJ, or RD believes should be covered by a standard acknowledgment order, ORO should be notified.

a. Time Requirement. If the CAJ (or designee) determines that the appeal may be processed by the RO, an acknowledgment or show cause order must be issued to the parties within 3 workdays of receipt of the appeal except as provided above with respect to premature and consolidated appeals.

b. Tailoring of Standardized Orders. The standardized acknowledgment orders are a general guide to language that may be applicable to a specific case. These orders were designed to provide notice of the general procedures that will apply to the adjudication of the appeal. They cover a wide range of possibilities including information on hearings, discovery, settlement, designation of representative, issues of timeliness and jurisdiction, general instructions and forms (Privacy Act statements and designation of representative forms), and schedules. The AJ should modify the standard order, only if necessary, to adjust it to the circumstances of the appeal. Regarding timeliness and jurisdiction issues, tailoring may require inclusion of specific dates or jurisdictional issues in question (e.g., see chapter 2, section 2b). As noted above, any applicable substantive law should be set out in the acknowledgment or subsequent show cause orders to avoid a remand based on a finding that the parties had not been provided explicit instructions regarding their burdens and responsibilities.

The AJ must provide the necessary notice. The various possibilities for an initial order range from a show cause order addressing timeliness and/or jurisdiction to a complete acknowledgment order covering all aspects of guidance including information on hearings, discovery, etc. Sometimes, follow-up orders may be necessary to clarify issues or to provide guidance not provided in the previous order before the matters being appealed became clear. In any event, any time an issue is raised that may be acceptable for consideration, the AJ must provide the appropriate notice to the appellant. Even when the potentially jurisdictional matter cannot properly be addressed in the current appeal, the appellant should be informed of the possible appealability of the issue as a separate case.

c. Attachments (Schedules). Any documents attached to the acknowledgment order must be included in the appeal file.
