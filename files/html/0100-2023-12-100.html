c. Delay in Payment Following Certification or Prima Facie Evidence of Interim Relief

The agency has the entire period for filing a PFR to choose between complying fully with an initial decision or filing a PFR and complying with the initial decision on an interim basis. "It would be unreasonable to rule that the agency may delay this decision until the thirty-fifth day, but that, if it decides to file a petition for review on the thirty-fifth day, it must actually issue a paycheck on that same day." Salazar v. Dept. of Transp., 60 MSPR 633, 638-39 (1994). The agency is only to take appropriate administrative action by the deadline for filing the PFR that will result in the issuance of a paycheck for the interim relief period and is not required to have paid the appellant by the deadline. Tisdell v. Dept. of Air Force, 94 MSPR 44, 49 ¶ 8 (2003).

If the SF-50 or SF-52 is issued in a manner demonstrating prima facie evidence of interim relief, there may still be a question of whether the agency unduly delays paying the appellant and fails to provide proper interim relief. Delays of two to three weeks in payment are apparently acceptable, Lebray v. Dept. of Navy, 62 MSPR 468, 472 (1994):

The appellant's contention that the agency was required to provide him with pay for the interim relief period no later than the deadline for filing its petition for review is incorrect. All that an agency must accomplish by this deadline is to take appropriate administrative action, such as executing an SF-50 or SF-52, that will result in the issuance of a paycheck for the interim relief period. Salazar v. Dept. of Transp., 60 MSPR 633, 638-39 (1994). The agency accomplished this when it executed an SF-50 on August 26, 1992, well before the filing deadline.

The remaining question is whether the agency unreasonably delayed in implementing the SF-50 by not issuing a paycheck for the interim relief period until September 25. In Salazar, we determined that the passage of 22 days from the execution of the SF-50 to the issuance of a paycheck was not an unreasonably long period of time under the circumstances. Id., at 639. Given the unrebutted statements in the affidavit submitted by the agency's representative in the instant case, we find that the agency did not unduly delay in providing the appellant with pay for the interim relief period. We also find that the 15-day period between the issuance of the initial decision and the appellant's restoration to duty does not show noncompliance with the interim relief order. See Johnson v. Dept. of the Navy, 58 MSPR 386, 389 (1993). Accordingly, we deny the appellant's motion to dismiss the agency's petition for review.

As to payment delays involving interim relief, in Robinson v. VA, 67 MSPR 334, 338 (1995):

The appellant also alleged that the agency delayed until December 21, 1994, paying him under the interim relief order for the period between the issuance of the initial decision on November 8 and his return to work on December 12, 1994. The agency stated in its affidavit that it began processing the pay due to the appellant under the interim relief order as soon as possible after determining to file a petition for review. According to the agency, the first pay date following the filing of its December 9, 1994 petition for review was December 20, 1994, and the appellant received a check for $1,888.56 as pay for the period commencing November 8 on December 21, 1994. We find that the agency's sworn statement is sufficient to show it has paid the appellant in compliance with the interim relief order and that any delay was reasonable. See Lebray v. Dept. of the Navy, 62 MSPR 468, 472 (1994).

Although the Board tolerates short delays in payment of interim relief, eight months of delay resulting from DFAS delays was too much. The agency's PFR was dismissed even though the problem was with its finance agent, DFAS, rather than with its own efforts to obtain payment for the appellant. Bradstreet v. Dept. of Navy, 83 MSPR 288, 293 ¶ 12 (1999) ("Nor does any failure on the part of DFAS 'to follow through' or its 'extraordinary' processing requirements excuse the agency's substantial delay."). In Bradstreet, the Board did not fault the agency for declining to pay the appellant for a period that he refused to return to work after the agency implemented interim relief in a manner that apparently left the appellant unwilling to return to the job the agency had assigned to him.
