k. Correction of Case Processing Error

Although the Board may decline to grant a PFR on the basis of an issue belatedly raised by a party, the Board may on its own motion reopen the case to correct reversible error committed by the AJ. In Jordan v. OPM, 6 MSPR 389 (1981), the decision was reopened and remanded after the AJ advised the Board that certain evidence was misplaced and that the initial decision was issued without consideration of that evidence. The effect may then be that the Board reviews issues not properly preserved by the party filing a PFR. See Lawson v. OPM, 68 MSPR 672, 675 (1995) (PFR was timely but OPM failed to raise the issues before the AJ, so it could not properly raise those issues in its PFR; the Board determined the errors to constitute reversible error, however, and reopened the case on its own motion to treat these errors); Gevaert v. Dept. of Navy, 74 MSPR 303, 306 (1997) (reopening a compliance case to consider submissions by the appellant that responded to arguably belated submissions from the agency); Branch v. DOD, 51 MSPR 537, 539 n.* (1991) (under the facts of the case, involving a semi-literate appellant who was confused with aspects of processing of the case, the AJ erred by precluding a hearing: "[I]t is necessary for the Board to exercise its discretion to reopen the appeal in order to insure that the appellant receives the even administration of justice."); Hernandez v. Dept. of Navy, 4 MSPR 62, 63, 4 MSPB 152 (1980) (appeal was reopened, despite an untimely PFR, to cure the AJ's error in failing to properly consider a change in the hearing site that the appellant requested); cf. Washington v. Dept. of Navy, 77 MSPR 525, 528-29 (1998) (Board declined to consider the argument that should have been offered in response to the AJ's show cause order when the appellant offered no good cause for a delayed response to the order); Golden v. USPS, 60 MSPR 268, 272 (1994) (rather than treating as a new appeal a submission from the appellant suggesting prior jurisdictional error by the Board, the submission should have been forwarded to the Board for consideration as a motion to reopen the prior appeal). The Board reopened an appeal when, shortly after it was dismissed by the AJ, the agency submitted evidence to the AJ showing that the appeal was timely filed because the appellant filed a complaint of discrimination concerning the removal and the agency had not yet issued a final decision on the complaint. Elliott v. Dept. of Army, 106 MSPR 326, 327-28 ¶¶ 3-7, 2007 MSPB 184 (2007).

The Board may reopen an appeal when relevant material was inadvertently omitted from the case file at the time of headquarters review. See Mandel v. OPM, 86 MSPR 299, 300 ¶¶ 2-5 (2000) (region belatedly forwarded to the Board material filed by the appellant immediately after the initial decision was issued; the Board reopened on its own motion and sustained an earlier denial of the PFR); Marren v. DOJ, 50 MSPR 474, 475 (1991) (denying a PFR and reopening the appeal to consider the appellant's response, inadvertently omitted from appeal file, to the AJ's jurisdictional order); Johnson v. Dept. of Army, 10 MSPR 161, 162 (1982) (vacating a Board decision and remanding the case for the AJ to consider a document considered by the Board that was "decisionally significant" that was not earlier served on the agency); Elias v. VA, 7 MSPR 66, 67 (1981) (vacating prior decision when the Board "discovered that certain relevant documents were inadvertently omitted from the file at the time of preparation of the Board's previous decision in this case"); cf. Stratton v. USPS, 89 MSPR 337, 339 ¶ 6 (2001) (regional office misplaced evidence concerning an employee's AWOL; on a PFR the Board considered evidence to issue "decision based on the written record as it should have been at the time the record closed"). An appeal may be reopened if, quickly following the issuance of the Board's final decision, the appellant demonstrates that he filed a timely response to the agency's PFR but the Board failed to consider it because, apparently, it was not received by the Board or did not find its way into the case file. See Beaudoin v. VA, 100 MSPR 507, 510 ¶¶ 4-5 (2005) (reaffirming the Board's earlier result).

The Board may grant a PFR and, if necessary, remand a case if the initial decision was "improvidently issued" because the AJ issued the decision before the appellant had a proper opportunity to file a jurisdictional submission. See Hankerson v. USPS, 93 MSPR 452, 455 ¶ 8 (2003). Without characterizing its action as reopening, the Board sent a retirement appeal back to the regional office for adjudication when the AJ received a material jurisdictional submission from the appellant after the initial decision was issued. Appellant's submission was delayed in the mail from the Philippines. The regional office sent the material to the Board and the Board, noting the acquiescence of OPM, sent the case back to the region. Exala v. OPM, 84 MSPR 277, 279 ¶ 3 (1999). The Board may also reopen an appeal on its own motion when it discovers that it relied upon a clerical mistake in one of the appellant's filings with the Board. See Biter v. ICC, 86 MSPR 438, 442-43 (2000). [Refer to Chapter 16, "Settlement After Initial Decision," for treatment of settlements submitted to AJ after record closes or initial decision has been issued.]

Clear error was sufficient to persuade the Board to reopen, despite an untimely PFR, when the issue was the AJ's dismissal of an appeal based on the AJ's incorrect assumption that the case was settled, Woods v. Dept. of Treasury, 87 MSPR 557, 560 ¶ 6 (2001):

Although the Board generally will not reopen an appeal to cure the untimeliness of a party's petition for review, the Board will not turn a blind eye to clear and material errors that appear to have prejudiced an appellant's substantive rights. See, e.g., Robinson v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 72 MSPR 444, 447-50 (1996); McFalls v. Office of Personnel Management, 72 MSPR 252, 258-59 (1996). When cognizance of an error that implicates an employee's basic procedural rights is necessary to prevent manifest injustice, the Board will reopen a case sua sponte to address that error. See, e.g., Robinson, 72 MSPR at 447-50 (reopening the case sua sponte to address the administrative judge's clear error of law that prevented the appellant from showing that the Board has jurisdiction over his appeal); Carey v. U.S. Postal Service, 50 MSPR 359, 361 (1991) (reopening the case sua sponte to address claim of new evidence that would provide the Board with jurisdiction over the appeal).

The Board considers reopening as a means of correcting case processing errors by an AJ after the initial decision is issued. If an AJ announces the result that will likely be achieved, but then revises her assessment before issuing the initial decision, the AJ commits no case processing error. The Board reviews the case through a PFR based on the strength of the AJ's analysis as expressed in the initial decision. See Williams v. DLA, 34 MSPR 54, 57-58 (1987).
