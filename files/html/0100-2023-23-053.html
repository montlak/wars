o. Lack of Consideration

McGowan v. DHUD, PH-0752-10-0572-I-1 (NP 4/1/2011), rejected an argument that a settlement lacked consideration that substituted for removal a resignation without clean paper:

The appellant's remaining contentions are essentially interrelated--that he received no benefit from the settlement because the agency was not required to expunge negative information, that agency counsel misrepresented that his future employment prospects would be better with the resignation, and that he did not understand that the settlement would effectively bar him from future employment--all of which implicitly reflect the appellant's assessment that there is no difference between a removal and a resignation in lieu of termination. The appellant's assessment is inaccurate.

Contrary to the appellant's view, the Board has approved of settlement agreements essentially identical to this one. See Crook v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 102 M.S.P.R. 340, ¶ 8 (2006). Significantly, this settlement was not a "clean record" agreement, it contained no provision requiring the agency to expunge any negative information, and it did not preclude the agency from including the reasons for the proposed termination in the SF-50 documenting the appellant's "resignation in lieu of termination." See id. Moreover, the appellant did receive a benefit--he was permitted to resign rather than be removed, an option he did not have before the settlement.

On that score, agency counsel's alleged general comments that the appellant's future employment prospects would be "better" with a resignation are true. The appellant can now tell potential employers that he resigned from his prior employment, which, as a matter of common sense, will raise fewer questions with a potential employer than a removal. See generally Harvey v. Veterans Administration, 2 M.S.P.R. 517, 519 (1980) (a removal may adversely effect the appellant's future employment prospects). While the appellant is justifiably concerned that there is negative information in his file that may become known to potential employers, the fact remains the appellant had the choice of contesting the validity of the agency action before the Board, see generally Axsom v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 110 M.S.P.R. 605, ¶ 17 (2009), or bargaining for a clean record resignation, see generally Vance v. Department of the Interior, 114 M.S.P.R. 679, ¶ 8 (2010) (the agency agreed to expunge the removal-related materials and not disclose the removal-related information to third parties). At the end of the day, that the settlement was not more generous to him appears to be a reflection of the strength of the agency's case, which included the appellant's response to the proposed removal that essentially conceded that he engaged in the (serious) charged misconduct, and the appellant's choice not to proceed with his appeal.

The appellant also presents nothing beyond one rejected application (by the same agency) in support of his conclusion that he is now barred from all future employment. Notably, nothing in the settlement suggests that he was debarred from federal employment, and he does not suggest how a removal would have left him in a better competitive position in the private sector job market. In sum, there is simply no evidence to support the appellant's bare assertions that the settlement resulted from fraud or that any other basis exists for invalidating the settlement. See Wofford, 115 M.S.P.R. 468, ¶ 8.

Consideration supported a settlement in Andersen v. DOD, DC-1221-19-0058-W-1 (NP 11/17/2022):

Under the law of contracts, an enforceable contract does not exist unless it is based on a bargained-for exchange, known as "consideration." Pappas v. Office of Personnel Management, 76 M.S.P.R. 152, 158 (1997), aff'd per curiam, 155 F.3d 565 (Fed. Cir. 1998) (Table). A very small consideration (the proverbial "peppercorn") can support an enforceable contract; convenience, avoidance of troublesome details and efforts, and forbearance to institute proceedings against the other party, for example, can be proper elements of consideration. Id. As found by the administrative judge, the appellant received valuable consideration in the form of promises by the agency to extend his employment by 3 months even though his appointment was expiring in December 2011, positively amend his employment evaluation records, provide a letter of recommendation, and remove derogatory information from his personnel file. The appellant's assertion of noncompliance by the agency regarding derogatory information in his personnel file after the parties entered into the settlement agreement does not demonstrate a lack of consideration sufficient to invalidate the agreement. See Zemco Manufacturing, Inc. v. Navistar International Transportation Corp., 270 F.3d 1117, 1121 n.3 (7th Cir. 2001) (explaining the difference between a lack of consideration in the formation of a contract and a breach of a contract). Moreover, regardless of the letter of recommendation writer's beliefs, the letter still represented a thing of value to the appellant in exchange for other consideration received by the agency.

Choroszy v. DHS, PH-315H-16-0458-I-1 (NP 4/27/2022), did not credit an assertion that a settlement was a contract of adhesion:

The appellant also argues on review that the settlement agreement should be invalidated because it is an adhesion contract, as the agency was in a position of power and gave him no choice but to sign the agreement or to never again be employed with the Federal Government. To establish that a settlement was a result of coercion or duress, a party must prove that he involuntarily accepted the other party's terms, that circumstances permitted no alternative, and that such circumstances were the result of the other party's coercive acts. Candelaria v. U.S. Postal Service, 31 M.S.P.R. 412, 413 (1986). The record demonstrates that the appellant voluntarily accepted the terms of the agreement, as evidenced by the language of the agreement itself, which states that the parties "entered into it voluntarily and of their own free will, without duress or coercion." Furthermore, the agreement provides that "[t]he parties acknowledge that they have both participated in the drafting of the terms of this [a]greement." The agreement also provides that the parties agreed that they had "an opportunity to consult with counsel."

The appellant had an alternative to the agreement, as he could have continued with the appeal of his termination. The appellant's contention that if he did not sign the agreement he would not be employable with the Federal Government is not supported by any evidence. The appellant then apparently argues the converse, by stating that entering the agreement has prevented him from all Federal employment. We find unpersuasive the appellant's arguments to invalidate the agreement on these grounds.
