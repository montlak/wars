1. Necessary Findings

Evidence of demeanor cannot be reflected fully in the written transcript. The Board must rely heavily on the AJ's credibility determinations if there are no other more persuasive indications of credibility in the record. For that reason, the AJ should make detailed findings on the credibility issues. Ray v. Dept. of Army, 2 MSPR 565, 567, 3 MSPB 86 (1980). Ibrahim v. Dept. of Army, 30 MSPR 531, 536 (1986), articulated the qualified "special deference" provided to AJs' credibility findings:

...Although special deference must be given to the presiding official's findings regarding credibility where such findings are based on the demeanor of the witness, Jackson v. Veterans Administration, 768 F.2d 1325 (Fed. Cir. 1985), such deference by the Board must necessarily be tempered by any weaknesses or errors in the presiding official's legal analysis of the facts before him. If it appears that the presiding official's credibility determinations are based on improper criteria, they cannot be sustained. See Breeden v. Weinberger, 493 F.2d 1002, 1010 (4th Cir. 1974).

The "failure to resolve contradictory evidence is error." Morgan v. Dept. of Army, 62 MSPR 380, 382 (1994); cf. Ware v. VA, 76 MSPR 427, 432 (1997) ("We find that the administrative judge erred in her analysis of the weight that this evidence deserved and in ignoring other evidence of record that supports the specification."). The Board discourages findings that are essentially hypothetical, that is, findings that are assumed or made arguendo for the purpose of further analysis of misconduct charges. See Wilson v. DOJ, 68 MSPR 303, 309 n.4 (1995) ("We find that the administrative judge erred in assuming arguendo that the appellant made the statements instead of determining whether, in fact, he made the statements.").

The Board will not provide the special deference generally reserved for AJs' credibility findings if those findings suffer from paucity of explanation. Garza v. Dept. of Army, 57 MSPR 214, 218 (1993) (brief conclusions of the AJ were inadequate when they did not explain what aspect of the witnesses' demeanor was relied upon by the AJ or why it contributed to or detracted from their credibility); see Special Counsel v. Eubanks, 76 MSPR 405, 416 (1997) (noting as to the inadequate credibility findings of an ALJ based on the demeanor of a witness that "the ALJ did not explain anything about her manner and appearance that warranted this conclusion."). Although an initial decision that resolves factual conflicts through references to testimony and evidence contains implicit credibility determinations, Brown v. Coast Guard, 10 MSPR 573, 577-78 (1982), it is insufficient to recount the evidence adduced at the hearing and draw a general conclusion without either providing the supporting reasons or including any discussion of how and why credibility questions were resolved. Pierce v. USPS, 3 MSPR 533, 535-36, 4 MSPB 52 (1980); cf. Corriveau v. Dept. of Navy, PH-1221-14-0377-W-1 (NP 9/4/2015) ("Although credibility determinations may be based implicitly upon demeanor evidence, Little v. Department of Transportation, 112 M.S.P.R. 224, ¶ 4 (2009) (recognizing that, where an administrative judge has heard live testimony, her credibility determinations must be deemed to be at least implicitly based upon the demeanor of the witnesses), the administrative judge here cited no reason why she found R.G. to be a credible witness, see Hillen v. Department of the Army, 35 M.S.P.R. 453, 458 (1987). In that respect, we find that the initial decision is incomplete."); Hicks v. Dept. of Army, DE-0432-10-0472-I-2 (NP 12/26/2012) ("While the administrative judge did not make explicit credibility determinations, he did find, for example, that agency witnesses straightforwardly confirmed during their testimonies that the appellant was told to concentrate on producing one error-free estimate per day, and that their testimony as to the concentration on quality versus quantity was confirmed by their interactions with the appellant during the period in question. Because the administrative judge was present to hear the live testimony, such a finding is an implicit credibility determination. Haebe v. Department of Justice, 288 F.3d 1288, 1300 (Fed. Cir. 2002); Hillen v. Department of the Army, 35 M.S.P.R. 453, 458 (1987)."); But cf. Brown v. OPM, CH-844E-16-0273-I-1, n.6 (NP 6/13/2022) ("Although the administrative judge's found the appellant's friend 'credible,' he also found her testimony regarding specifically recalling the appellant telling her about mailing her application in October 2012, 'dubious.' These two conclusions are difficult to reconcile. In any event, the Board may overturn demeanor-based credibility determinations when, as here, the administrative judge's findings are incomplete, inconsistent with the weight of the evidence, and do not reflect the record as a whole. See Rapp v. Office of Personnel Management, 108 M.S.P.R. 674, ¶ 13 (2008).").

An example of an AJ's detailed credibility findings credited by the Board is Cook v. Dept. of Army, 105 MSPR 178, 186 ¶¶ 18-19, 2007 MSPB 76 (2007):

In the present case, the administrative judge expressly stated that his findings were based on his observation of witness demeanor, specifically on the demeanor of the witness to whom the appellant allegedly made his statement. Moreover, he thoroughly explained his reasons for finding that witness's testimony less credible than the appellant's testimony. For example, he noted that the witness's account changed over time, while the appellant's did not; that the witness could not recall key details of the incident; that her testimony regarding some aspects of the incident was vague; that parts of her account seemed "fabricated after the fact"; and that there was evidence that she was motivated to make a false accusation against the appellant because she personally disliked him. The administrative judge found further that the other agency witness who was present during the appellant's remarks was unable to corroborate the account of the witness mentioned above because he testified only that he heard the appellant use the word "nigger." As noted earlier, the appellant has not denied using that word, and has maintained consistently that he was referring to remarks by a police officer who had been quoted as using that term. The administrative judge therefore concluded properly that the testimony of this second agency witness did not corroborate that of the first.

Under the circumstances described above, we find that the administrative judge provided persuasive reasons for his credibility determinations and for his conclusions regarding the merits of the charge against the appellant. We note further that, even if the agency's evidence were equally worthy of belief as that of the appellant, the appellant would be entitled to prevail. See Knudsen v. Department of Health & Human Services, 35 F.3d 543, 550 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (when the evidence is in "equipoise," the party with the burden of persuasion has failed to meet its burden). For these reasons, we conclude that the agency has not presented sufficiently sound reasons for overturning the administrative judge's credibility determinations. See Haebe, 288 F.3d at 1301. The part of the initial decision in which the administrative judge found that the agency had not proven its charge against the appellant therefore is AFFIRMED.

If the Board determines that credibility findings are lacking or insufficient, it remands if credibility is material to resolution of the appeal. Migliori v. Dept. of Army, 47 MSPR 448, 451 (1991), sent a case back to the AJ with the observation that:

In the instant case, the agency presented two witnesses who testified regarding the appellant's alleged unacceptable performance. On the other hand, the appellant and her four witnesses testified at the hearing in support of her alleged acceptable performance and provided explanations for her actions. The initial decision does not adequately assess the credibility of the witnesses, and it does not state which of the witnesses the administrative judge found credible or explain her reasons for apparently finding that the agency's witnesses were more credible than the appellant and her witnesses. It merely notes that the testimony of the appellant's supervisor "was consistent with the deficiencies that were noted in the letter denying the within-grade increase." Because the administrative judge apparently credited the testimony of the agency's witnesses over the testimony of the appellant and her witnesses, the administrative judge was required to make credibility determinations consistent with Hillen. Therefore, we find that the administrative judge erred by failing to do so, and the Board is unable to give due deference to her findings in this regard. See Cordioli v. Department of the Navy, 38 MSPR 374, 377 (1988); cf. Berkey v. United States Postal Service, 38 MSPR 55, 57 (1988) (the administrative judge correctly resolved the credibility determinations in accordance with Hillen because he first identified the factual questions in dispute, identified the agency's charges, and then summarized the evidence that the agency and the appellant presented with respect to the charges, explaining why he found the appellant's version to be incredible).

Cf. Miller v. DOD, 85 MSPR 310, 319 ¶ 22 (2000) (although some credibility findings should have been made, a remand was not required when the appellant did not show that a different outcome in the appeal was warranted despite deficiencies in the credibility determinations).

The requirement of credibility determinations extends through the full range of the Board's docket. Credibility determinations must be made in Chapter 43 cases as well as in cases brought under Chapter 75. Generalized factual conclusions following conflicting testimony in a performance case will require a remand for the entry of proper findings of fact. Cordioli v. Dept. of Navy, 38 MSPR 374, 377 & n.4 (1988) ("The administrative judge did not adequately explain the bases for his acceptance of the testimony of the appellant's first and second-level supervisors as more credible than that of the appellant....The administrative judge stated, in sum, that the agency's witnesses 'gave consistent and responsible testimony, which stood up well under cross examination.' This is insufficient to explain why the administrative judge credited these witnesses over the appellant where their testimony raised a factual dispute."); see Hayes v. USPS, 42 MSPR 651, 656 (1989) (remand when the AJ failed to provide an explanation of why he found the appellant's live testimony less credible than agency hearsay evidence offered through an affidavit). The Board observed in Cohen v. GSA, 48 MSPR 451, 458 (1991), that "[m]erely stating that a witness was credible with respect to three quarters of the specifications, or portions of his testimony is not sufficient." Under Cohen, the AJ must explain why documentary hearsay evidence is entitled to greater probative value than the appellant's testimony regarding the charges.
