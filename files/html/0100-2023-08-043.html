b. Performance-Based Action

A few percent of Board appeals involve performance actions, described in their rich complexity in Chapter 9. Principal structure for appeal rights from performance actions is offered by 5 USC 4303(e), 5 USC 7701(a), and 5 CFR 432.102, .106 (2022). 5 USC 4303(f), limiting the scope of employees who may appeal performance-related actions to the Board, does not incorporate by reference the definitions or limitations of 5 USC 7511, The 1990 Civil Service Due Process Amendments extended MSPB appeals from performance actions to excepted service nonpreference eligibles with two years of service in the same or similar position. See 5 USC 4303(e)(3). Previously, 5 USC 4303(e), (f) specifically limited appeal rights from § 4303 (unacceptable performance) actions to preference eligibles and employees in the competitive service who survived their trial or probationary periods. Rodriguez-Villafane v. Dept. of Air Force, 20 MSPR 156, 158 (1984); see Butler v. Dept. of Labor, 4 MSPR 452, 453, 4 MSPB 501 (1980) (employee who has no MSPB appeal rights may be covered, through OPM regulations, by a performance appraisal system and have procedural rights within the agency under § 4303(b-d)). Until 1990, a nonpreference eligible employee in the excepted service was excluded from the class of employees entitled to appeal under 5 USC 4303(e), 7511(a)(1)(A). Garrow v. Gramm, 856 F.2d 203, 205 (D.C. Cir. 1988) ("Chapter 43 protections are provided to non-preference members of the Excepted Service (such as Garrow) but denies them the right to an administrative or judicial review of the agency's final action--a right afforded to other categories of employees under Chapter 43."); Smith v. MSPB, 813 F.2d 1216, 1219 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (OPM does not confer jurisdiction over Chapter 43 actions by approving appraisal plans that include nonpreference eligible attorneys of the NLRB); cf. Horne v. ICC, 5 MSPR 208, 211, 5 MSPB 259 (1981) ("As non-preference eligible employees in the excepted service, appellants are specifically excluded from the class of 'employees' entitled to appeal to the Board under the provisions of 5 U.S.C. 7511(a)(l)(A) and 5 U.S.C. 4304(c). See also Henderson v. NSA, 2 MSPB 189 (1980)."). An employee could, however, complain to OSC should her termination constitute a prohibited personnel practice. Harrison v. Bowen, 815 F.2d 1505 (D.C. Cir. 1987).
