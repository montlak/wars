broida flra Chapter 04: Subjects of Bargaining: Procedures for Negotiability Determinations Chapter 04: Subjects of Bargaining: Procedures for Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 04: Framework for Negotiability Appeals, Section D: Requirement of Specific Proposals; Format, Subsection 03: Examples of Rulings Concerning Specificity of Proposals

3. Examples of Rulings Concerning Specificity of Proposals

A collection of decisions offers examples of proposals that have failed, and a few that have passed, the specificity challenge. AFGE Local 738 and Dept. of Army, Ft. Leavenworth, 30 FLRA 794, 795 (1987) (concerning impact bargaining proposal lacking in specificity that required new management study and revalidation of organizational plan); NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, IRS, Midwest Region, 2 FLRA 470, 470-71 (1980) (proposals lacked requisite specificity that requested negotiations on "the substance, impact, and implementation" of (a) decision to assign work to appeals officers outside of their position descriptions, and (b) the assignment of legal research assistants); NFFE Local 1363 and Hq., Army Garrison, Yongsan, Korea, 2 FLRA 374, 375 (1979) (insufficient to request negotiability determination as to seat-belt requirement and its impact); NFFE Local 1750 and Dept. of Interior, Fish and Wildlife Serv., 2 FLRA 371, 371-72 (1979) (denying union petition requiring determination of negotiability of "the matter of standby pay" as not sufficiently specific). NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, IRS, Birmingham Dist., 2 FLRA 466, 467 (1980), declined to consider, for lack of specific proposals, a negotiability appeal predicated on an agency statement that any potential proposals concerning certain changes in work practices were nonnegotiable. A proposal that the unit members be entitled to all benefits granted military personnel at Ft. Bragg was not specific enough for a negotiability determination. Ft. Bragg Unit of N.C. Ass'n of Educators and Ft. Bragg Dependents Sch., 12 FLRA 519, 527 (1983) (Proposal 20). Also nonnegotiable was a proposal that the union "requests negotiations with the [Employer] over salary and fringe benefits for the certified staff." The proposal did not permit the parties to determine whether subsequent negotiations would be within the limits of negotiability under controlling law permitting negotiation over salary, subject to certain limitations. Ft. Knox Teachers Ass'n and Ft. Knox Dependents Sch., 27 FLRA 750, 755 (1987). A proposal to distribute parking spaces on a system "such as seniority" was not sufficiently specific for a negotiability appeal. AFGE Local 3525 and DOJ, Bd. of Immigration Appeals, 10 FLRA 61, 66 (1982). Nonnegotiable was a proposal that an agency should take an action. The proposal required nothing, but if bargaining resulted in impasse, the FSIP could impose a specific contractual requirement, changing a proposed "should" to a contractual "must." See DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, FCC Coleman v. FLRA, 737 F.3d 779, 786-88 (D.C. Cir. 2013).

A proposal calling on the agency to eliminate discriminatory provisions of its travel regulations, permitting some employees higher rates of compensation than others, was not specific enough for a negotiability appeal. The union had not specified any provisions of agency regulations that appeared discriminatory. Maritime/Metal Trades Council and Panama Canal Comm'n, 18 FLRA 326, 328-29 (1985) (Proposal 2). A proposal that employees be reimbursed for additional expenses incurred as a result of changes in schedule was not specific enough for FLRA review, despite a union statement that the proposal concerned day care expenses. FLRA declined to consider a statement of intent that was contrary to the express language of the disputed proposal. NAGE Local R14-32 and Hq., Army Training Ctr. Eng'g, 20 FLRA 501, 502 (1985). A proposal was nonnegotiable that requested a delay in the implementation of transfers resulting from a reorganization until the employees and union were supplied "full information" about the changes and until all talks had been completed as required by law. The union did not explain what was meant by the phrase "full information." The proposal was too vague for a negotiability determination. AFGE Local 12 and Dept. of Labor, 26 FLRA 768, 776 (1987) (Proposal 13) (citing AFGE Local 12 and Dept. of Labor, 25 FLRA 987 (1987)). A union request for negotiation over an agency job contingency plan was not particular; the union offered no proposals. AFGE Local 1482 and Marine Corps Logistics Base, Barstow, Cal., 11 FLRA 351, 352 (1983).

The proposal need be no more specific than circumstances require. A union proposal that responded to regulatory changes in travel and per diem advance practices, by requesting that there be no changes in the existing procedures, was sufficiently precise. IFPTE Local 12 and Dept. of Navy, Puget Sound Naval Shipyard, 24 FLRA 178, 179 (1986). A proposal was sufficiently specific that required management to continue a past practice rather than employ a new procedure under a regulation it intended to issue. Nat'l Ass'n of Agric. Employees and USDA, APHIS, 22 FLRA 451, 454 (1986). A negotiable proposal requested that management eliminate specified portions of agency regulations. IBEW Local 1245 and Dept. of Interior, Bureau of Reclamation, 25 FLRA 201, 204 (1987) (although appeal was dismissed because union did not provide the text of regulations). A proposal that competitive areas be negotiated was negotiable. No mention was made as to the specificity of the proposal. AFGE and GSA, 11 FLRA 261, 266 (1983).
