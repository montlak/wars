broida mspb Chapter 08: Substantive Offenses Chapter 08: Substantive Offenses, Subchapter 01: Status, Section G: Strikes, Subsection 05: Fear for Personal Safety, Part b: Duress, Coercion Established
b. Duress, Coercion Established

Duress was established in Hanson v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 210, 212 (1983). Appellant's wife was called and told their house would be burned if appellant crossed the picket line. Finding immaterial the fact that others crossed the line because they had not received this threat, the Board determined that a reasonable person of ordinary firmness, under the appellant's circumstances, could not have been expected to resist the intimidation, harassment, and threats. Although duress through verbal threats is more readily established and corroborated if instances of violence associated with the strike take place at a particular location, and although the danger posed by a threat may have been speculative, it is not incumbent upon an employee to go forward in the face of the threat (to be returned to the hospital in an ambulance) to establish its validity. Arndt v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 221, 228 (1983). Physical duress, along with implied threats, constituted coercion in Bambury v. Dept. of Transp., 17 MSPR 417, 426-28 (1983); see Hutchins v. Dept. of Transp., 17 MSPR 199, 202 (1983) (threatening phone calls and anonymous notes threatening bodily injury were sufficient to constitute coercion); Hoyer v. FAA, 17 MSPR 184, 187 (1983) (serious and repeated threats to an appellant and his wife). Threats sufficiently specific and physically menacing to an appellant and his family need not suggest immediate harm to be coercive. Hennelly v. Dept. of Transp., 18 MSPR 360, 362-63 (1983). Harassment on the job, coupled with anonymous phone calls at home over a four-month period, intimation of harm to the appellant's young child, and the failure of the agency to assure the appellant of steps that would be taken to ensure his safety in crossing the picket line, were sufficiently coercive, even without direct specific threats on the life or health of the appellant, to support a defense of coercion in Last v. Dept. of Transp., 18 MSPR 446, 451-52 (1983). Direct threats to family members through phone calls established coercion. Albert v. Dept. of Transp., 19 MSPR 62, 63-65 (1984). In another strike situation, participation was disproved by a reasonable fear of injury from participants and demonstration of reasons other than striking for being seen among picketers. Duckett v. TVA, 10 MSPR 304, 307-08 (1982).
