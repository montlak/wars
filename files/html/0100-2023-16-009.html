2. Flexibility Permitted by Appraisal System; Skeletal Plans

OPM may approve an agency appraisal system that allows the agency flexibility to select among options described in the plan for performance appraisal, allowing an agency to use different approaches for the evaluation of work of different units or employees. Salmon v. SSA, DC-0432-09-0732-I-1 (NP 9/30/2010) (aff'd, Salmon v. SSA, 663 F.3d 1378 (Fed. Cir. 2011)), post), discussed a flexible or skeletal plan:

The appellant also argues that the agency illegally implemented 2006 changes in its performance appraisal system without obtaining OPM approval. However, an agency human resources specialist testified at the hearing that the 2006 changes were to one of three programs within the performance appraisal system, and that such changes do not require OPM approval. We have reviewed the agency's OPM-approved 1995 performance appraisal system and its 2006 Performance Assessment and Communication System (PACS), and find that the PACS did not constitute a significant change in the performance system such as to require additional OPM approval. Rather, the approved appraisal system gave the agency flexibility to exercise a number of different options in evaluating employee performance. The PACS program merely identifies the agency's specific election of options under that system for bargaining unit employees. For example, the agency has authority under its performance appraisal system to use appraisal programs using from a 2-tier to a 5-tier assessment program. The PACS program indicates that it is a 3-tier assessment program. Similarly, the agency's performance appraisal system states that employees with unacceptable performance will be provided an opportunity to improve period lasting at least 90 days. The PACS program provides an employee with a 30-day opportunity to improve during a Performance Assistance period and then a formal 120-day Opportunity to Perform Successfully period. We therefore agree with the administrative judge that the 2006 changes did not constitute a new performance appraisal system requiring OPM approval.

Salmon v. SSA, 663 F.3d 1378, 1384 (Fed. Cir. 2011), post, noted that under current OPM regulations, an agency may obtain approval from OPM for a skeletal description of plans yet to be issued, what the court described as a "high-level" plan providing a "framework of policies and parameters," and the agency later issues detailed operating performance appraisal plans without further OPM approval. The framework-type review was noted in Ortegel v. NASA, DC-0432-15-0715-I-1 (NP 1/3/2017):

The appellant argues that the three-tier rating system applied to him (i.e., "Substantially Exceeds Expectations," "Meets Expectations," and "Fails to Meet Expectations") was a significant change to the agency's prior five-tier system (used until the 2012-13 performance cycle) and was never approved by OPM. We agree with the administrative judge's finding that the three-tier rating system was within the parameters of the performance appraisal system that had been approved by OPM. As correctly noted by the administrative judge, the performance appraisal system approved by OPM in 1996 was a "framework-type overview" similar to the one discussed in Salmon v. Social Security Administration, 663 F.3d 1378, 1384 (Fed. Cir. 2011), and not a "detailed implementation." OPM approved a system providing for employee performance plans that use a minimum of two levels to appraise each critical element but did not require a specific number of levels to be used. Because the three-tier rating method under which the appellant was reviewed was consistent with the OPM-approved appraisal system, we find that the change to three-tier ratings for each element was not a significant change in the system requiring additional OPM review. See Salmon, 663 F.3d at 1384.
