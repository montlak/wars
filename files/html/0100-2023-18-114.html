h. Harmful Error Analysis Before Ward

The harmful error doctrine was not abolished by Ward. Instead, Ward created a different and determinative constitutional due process analysis. If no due process error is established under Ward, there may still be consideration of harmful error under 5 USC 7701(c): "harmful error in the application of the agency's procedures in arriving at such decision." Under law prevailing before 2011, it was not error should the deciding official consider past discipline not mentioned in the proposal letter if the current sustained charge warrants the penalty imposed. Underwood v. DOD, 53 MSPR 355, 360 (1992) ("The appellant has not shown to date what evidence or argument she would have proffered had she known that her prior past record was being considered in assessing the penalty.... Moreover, on the record before us, without considering the past record, the penalty of removal is within the bounds of reasonableness."). If the proposing official considered past conduct but did not mention it in the removal proposal, there was no harmful error if the deciding official sustained the removal on the basis of the information in the proposal and did not consider the past misconduct in his decision. Lavezza v. VA, 27 MSPR 105, 107 (1985); see Deutsch v. USPS, 39 MSPR 386, 390 n.7 (1989) ("even if factors not mentioned in the removal notice were considered in determining an appropriate penalty, the error is not fatal where the serious nature of the sustained charge justifies the penalty imposed"; citing Plath v. DOJ, 12 MSPR 421, 424-25 (1982)). Were the agency to rely on uncharged past similar misconduct in its proposal to justify a penalty, the agency may have to prove that misconduct on appeal, at hearing, if the appellant denied it. See Thomas v. USPS, 96 MSPR 179, 184-85 ¶¶ 9-12 (2004). [Refer to Chapter 7, "Agency Consideration of Past Discipline."]

If an agency removed an employee for one specific charge, e.g., falsification of a Form 171, the agency could take into account the employee's marginal performance, a factor properly considered in determining the severity of the penalty, and under law prior to 2011 it was not reversible harmful error for the agency to fail to state in its decision letter that it considered the marginal performance. If the agency also considered abuse of leave privileges and unapproved absences, conduct that could support a penalty against the petitioner, the agency must provide notice of these allegations to the appellant and permit him the opportunity to respond. When the agency did not do so, it erred in considering and relying on the allegations in arriving at its final decision. But to warrant reversal, demonstrable prejudice was to be demonstrated. Should the record disclose that despite the procedural error, the charged offense (of falsification), standing alone, would support removal under the agency's regulations, there was no harmful error as to the procedurally deficient adverse action notice or decision. See Payne v. Dept. of Army, 6 MSPR 693, 6 MSPB 581 (1981). If the agency considered issues not contained in a proposal notice, the Board could independently assess the propriety of the penalty. See Howard v. USPS, 72 MSPR 422, 426 (1996) (deciding official's error in considering her personal knowledge of the appellant's work performance as a factor in the penalty determination caused the Board to mitigate a removal for AWOL when other mitigating factors were present).

Under earlier law, an agency deciding official should not consider charges or aggravating factors relating to a penalty that were not embraced by the proposal for adverse action. Yet it was not harmful error for the deciding official to learn of charges or events not included in the proposal when the deciding official reviewed materials for information that would mitigate the penalty proposed. As long as there was no reliance on the extraneous information, there was no harmful error. Kimm v. Dept. of Treasury, 64 MSPR 198, 209 (1994) (no error where the agency considered the appellant's "failure to accept responsibility" and other factors in assessing a penalty, when the factors were not mentioned in the proposal notice; those factors were not used to determine whether the adverse action should be sustained); Filson v. Dept. of Transp., 7 MSPR 125, 129, 7 MSPB 50 (1981) (deciding official reviewed a report of investigation to determine if it supported the charges, demonstrated that the investigation was fairly conducted, or offered mitigating factors; exclusion of the matters referenced in the ROI from the advance notice was proper since those matters were not used as aggravating factors).

The decisions issued before 2011 were uniform in approach. Although an agency should include in the advance notice of an adverse action the aggravating factors relied on to impose a severe penalty, and the failure to do so was error, the error was not harmful if the appellant had notice of the aggravating factors, and was prepared to, and did, defend against those matters at hearing. Daniel v. USPS, 16 MSPR 486, 490 & n.* (1983); cf. Ruiz v. USPS, 59 MSPR 76, 80 (1993) ("The administrative judge found that the agency had failed to establish that the appellant acted improperly in connection with this incident and, therefore, did not consider it in his findings."). Failure of an agency to inform an appellant of the specific past discipline relied on to enhance a penalty was not harmful error. The appellant learned the specifics in time to address them at the Board hearing. MacGillivray v. Dept. of Air Force (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-990); cf. Phillips v. GSA, 38 MSPR 206, 213 (1988) (no error when the agency did not disclose to the appellant during the adverse action process an internal memorandum discussing the Douglas factors as they applied to the appellant's case; the agency did not rely on the factors to impose an enhanced penalty). If the agency proved less than all charges and the Board then mitigated the penalty, the agency would not be heard to complain when the Board declined to consider a past disciplinary record that was not cited in the notice of proposed removal. Sternberg v. DODDS, 52 MSPR 547, 559 n.6 (1992).

Under earlier law, and a nonprecedential Federal Circuit decision, Cadenhead v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 98-3305), an agency properly relied on an employee's past conduct, even although it was not specifically charged, if the proposal letter referenced the employee's past record as one determinant for the penalty. The reference to the past record, according to Cadenhead, was sufficient to place the employee on notice that past incidents may be relied upon to support the penalty imposed:

Mr. Cadenhead argues that the deciding official improperly relied on factors not set forth in the Removal Notice. More specifically, Mr. Cadenhead points to the fact that the deciding official testified at the hearing that he considered incident reports in rendering his decision.[1] He cites precedent for the proposition that factors not set forth in the Removal Notice cannot be used by a deciding official to determine the penalty. See Kimm v. Department of Treasury, 64 MSPR 198, 208-209 (1994); Lavezza v. Veterans Administration, 27 MSPR 105, 107 (1985). However, the Removal Notice specifically recites the factors the deciding official was to consider in the determination of the penalty. These factors were the nature and seriousness of the offense, past work record and length of service, other penalties imposed on others for the same offense, potential for rehabilitation and past discipline record. The Removal Notice put Mr. Cadenhead on notice that the deciding official was to consider his past work record. Incident reports are part of an employee's past work record and therefore were proper factors for the deciding official to rely upon in determining the penalty. Thus, there was no error made by the deciding official. Further, we see no abuse of discretion in the Board's ultimate reliance on the deciding official's Removal Notice.

[1] Incident reports are written statements by other employees of any incidents they feel are threatening, hostile or affect their safety at work.

If the appellant essentially placed his past conduct at issue through a claim that he is the victim of disparate treatment, past discipline could be considered (although it was not earlier considered as to the penalty determination), Bess v. Dept. of Navy, 46 MSPR 583, 591 (1991):

[W]here...the appellant raises the issue of disparate treatment, the agency is required to present evidence to refute such a challenge.... Thus, if the appellant contends that a penalty exceeds that imposed upon other, similarly-situated employees, the agency may present rebuttal evidence of prior discipline imposed on the appellant, despite its failure to have provided such information in the advance or final notices.
