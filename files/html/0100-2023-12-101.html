d. Withdrawal of Interim Relief

The Board allows an appellant to challenge interim relief that is granted and then withdrawn by the agency, but the appellant must act quickly, Johnson v. DOJ, 71 MSPR 59, 65-66 (1996):

The appellant argues, however, in his renewed motion to dismiss, that the agency thereafter improperly revoked its full compliance with the interim relief order when, on April 12, 1995, after 42 days, it stopped paying him the 25% differential. Before addressing the merits of that argument, we must first consider whether the Board may entertain an allegation from a prevailing appellant that the agency, which admittedly complied with the administrative judge's interim relief order before the record on petition for review closed, subsequently withdrew that compliance. A clear reading of the interim relief statute suggests that the Board may indeed entertain such an allegation. Specifically, 5 USC § 7701(b)(2)(B) provides that, if an agency makes an undue disruption determination that prevents the employee's return or presence at the place of employment, the employee shall receive pay, compensation, and all other benefits as terms and conditions of employment during the period pending the outcome of any petition for review. See also 5 CFR § 1201.115(b)(2). Since the statute clearly provides that interim relief remains in effect until a final decision by the Board, see McLaughlin v. OPM, 62 MSPR 536, 550-51 (1994), aff'd, 47 F.3d 1181 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table), it follows that an employee who believes that interim relief, properly granted, has been withdrawn prior to issuance of the Board's final decision may raise such an allegation to the Board.

If, however, as in this case, the allegation is raised after the record on petition for review has closed, the appellant must make a further showing. The Board's regulations provide, at 5 CFR § 1201.114(i), that, once the record closes, no additional evidence or argument will be accepted unless the party submitting it shows that the evidence was not readily available before the record closed. Here, the appellant states, in the affidavit accompanying his renewed motion to dismiss, that he did not regularly receive any differential in his pay check after the initial decision of February 28, 1995, and that he was never informed in writing by the agency, prior to the time he reviewed its May 26, 1995, response to his motion to dismiss, that his differential had been revoked. In its response arguing for dismissal of the appellant's renewed motion on the grounds of untimeliness, the agency counters that he acknowledged, in the affidavit accompanying his first motion to dismiss, that he was told, during an April 27, 1995, phone conversation with the Supervisory Payroll Services Specialist that, since he was on detail, he would receive post differential only for the first 43 [sic] days of his detail. The agency asserts that the appellant cannot now claim that he was unaware until May 26, 1995, of this revocation, and that his renewed motion to dismiss was, therefore, not timely filed. However, in that same affidavit, the appellant also states that the Payroll Specialist told him he would get a 15% post differential and no cost-of-living allowance, and that, later that same day, another Staffing Specialist told him that he would not get a post differential at all but would get a 22 1/2% cost-of-living allowance. Apparently, neither individual gave the appellant wholly correct information because the agency ultimately determined that he was entitled to, and retroactively granted him, a 25% post differential. Moreover, the appellant's sworn statement that, prior to May 26, 1995, he never received any written documentation showing that his differential had been revoked, stands unrebutted. Under the circumstances, we do not find that the appellant was on notice, based on his phone conversation, that his differential had been revoked. We find, therefore, that the agency's statement, in its response to the appellant's motion to dismiss, that his differential had been revoked was new evidence, not available to him prior to the close of the record on petition for review, and that his renewed motion to dismiss, filed within 30 days of the agency's statement, was not untimely.
