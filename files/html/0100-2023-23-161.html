d. Other Post-Reinstatement Action; Medical Certification Requirement

Agencies are not precluded from taking personnel actions pending Board disposition or after reversal of appealable actions, as long as a valid reason exists for the action. Reclassification of a job is appropriate based upon a change of duties. Higashi v. Dept. of Army, 26 MSPR 330, 331 (1985) (reclassification action was instituted prior to the Board decision on the appellant's demotion). An agency may seek to remove the appellant, as long as a legitimate reason exists for the subsequent action. Mascarenas v. DOD, 57 MSPR 425, 430-31 (1993). The agency whose adverse action is reversed for procedural reasons may file a PFR from the initial decision reversing the action while it starts a new, procedurally valid, action based on the substantive basis leading to the first action appealed. Litton v. DOJ, 118 MSPR 626, 629-31 ¶¶ 9-13, 2012 MSPB 124 (2012). An agency could properly change the appellant's shift based on bona fide management concerns, particularly if a reasonable period of time passed following reinstatement. Cook v. USPS, 30 MSPR 671, 674 (1986) (appellant's shift changed when the office discontinued daytime mail distribution). The agency could adjust the appellant's days off following reinstatement, Smith v. USPS, 62 MSPR 417, 423 (1994):

Regarding the agency's action changing the appellant's work schedule when it reinstated him, the administrative judge correctly found that, in the absence of a loss of pay differential or other evidence of harm, an agency has discretion to change a returning appellant's work schedule, and that the appellant had failed to show a loss of pay differential or other evidence of harm. See Harris v. Department of Agriculture, 53 MSPR 78, 82 (1992), aff'd, 988 F.2d 130 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table); Wittl v. Department of the Air Force, 36 MSPR 20, 24-25 (1987). Although the appellant has cited facts in his petition for review which he contends show that he "suffered a loss" by having his days off changed, he has made no showing that he could not have introduced evidence of the alleged harm in the regional office proceeding. See 5 CFR § 1201.115; Avansino v. US Postal Service, 3 MSPB 308, 3 MSPR 211, 214 (1980).

See Conaway v. USPS, 93 MSPR 6, 12-13 ¶ 13-14 (2002) (when it reinstates an employee, an agency may change the employee's work schedule if there is no loss of pay or other harm, but "evidence of punitive or improper reasons for a shift change will constitute an abuse of discretion"; finding no abuse of discretion in a shift change that involved 30 employees, including the appellant); Rose v. USPS, 77 MSPR 139, 146-47 (1997) (noting agency discretion but considering the propriety of reassignment to a new schedule four months after reinstatement); Unhoch v. USPS, 66 MSPR 651, 657 (1995) ("[i]t is within the agency's discretion to change the appellant's work schedule, absent a loss of pay differential or other evidence of harm.").

Despite the holdings disfavoring the imposition of conditions upon reinstatement, the Board implied in Butanowicz v. Dept. of Treasury, 48 MSPR 550, 557 (1991), that an agency may impose the requirements of its internal regulations applying to reinstatement actions resulting from settlements:

...Under IRM Section 0736.534:(3)...which antedates the settlement agreement, "an [i]nvestigation is [r] equired" when a former employee is appointed following a break in service of more than 1 year (emphasis supplied).... There is no indication that the regulation is discretionary, and by its plain language it is mandatory. Therefore, we agree with the administrative judge that the requirement for the completion of appointment papers is not tantamount to a modification of the settlement agreement or equivalent to a precondition to employment.... Thus, we reject the appellant's argument that the agency violated the settlement agreement by following the requirements set forth in its own regulations for reinstating him.

Refusal to restore a government driver's license to an appellant, reinstated after a successful alcoholism defense, was proper when his regular license was suspended as a result of state court action. Marren v. DOJ, 32 MSPR 285, 286-87 (1987). An agency could not, however, impose medical restrictions on an employee fired for physical inability to perform a job when the decision of the AJ restoring the employee to duty refuted the basis for the restrictions. Wittl v. Dept. of Air Force, 36 MSPR 20, 25 (1987) (appellant objecting to harassment in the form of a required medical examination had to show that the annual exam he was directed to take differed from that required of other employees).

An agency may not, without good cause, delay the reinstatement of the appellant pending an agency-imposed requirement that the appellant present medical evidence showing his fitness for duty. Greaves v. USPS, 55 MSPR 337, 341 (1992) (agency could not impose additional requirements for medical documentation upon a settlement agreement calling for a successful fitness for duty examination; the majority opinion was joined by Chairman Levinson's concurring opinion, stating that additional medical documentation could have been required as part of a fitness examination); Gottlieb v. VA, 44 MSPR 298, 302 (1990) (agency's unjustified requirement of a medical examination turned a 30-day suspension into a two-year suspension).

Nor could an agency impose a requirement that an employee report for a medical evaluation that was not contemplated by a settlement agreement restoring the employee to a position. Grube v. Dept. of Air Force, 41 MSPR 494, 499 (1989). followed in Sternberg v. Dept. of Transp., 56 MSPR 520, 526 (1993) ("To add such a limitation would amount to a unilateral modification of a material term of the agreement, an action which the Board has no authority to take."). But see Connor v. USPS, 50 MSPR 389, 393 (1991) (involving an unclear opinion suggesting that an agency could require a psychiatric fitness exam as a precondition to reinstatement when its regulations called for the exam; the reversal of the removal was based on a procedural point rather than a decision on the fitness for duty issue involved in the removal decision and the Board order required the appellant to "provide all necessary information requested by the agency in furtherance of compliance"); cf. Coffey v. USPS, 60 MSPR 533, 538 (1994) (agency offered "compelling reasons" for not returning the appellant to his former position when a physician mutually agreed upon by the parties recommended "long term psychiatric treatment" and stated that "the appellant could function at his job if he obtained the necessary treatment"; "[a]s long as the appellant refuses to follow Dr. Ahmad's recommendation, we find that the agency has a strong overriding interest for not placing the appellant in his former position"). Yokley v. USPS, 57 MSPR 482, 485-86 (1993), clarified the right of the Postal Service, under its internal regulations, to require as a prerequisite to reinstatement that an employee take a fitness examination if the employee was absent from work (including time off during the overturned adverse action) for more than a year:

The administrative judge determined that the agency had demonstrated that its regulations mandate that an employee who has been absent from work for more than a year must take a fitness for duty examination before the employee returns to work. Accordingly, he found that the agency properly ordered the appellant to undergo a medical examination based on legitimate agency concerns and in accordance with the applicable agency regulations.

...

It has been established that the agency's regulation requires employees, who have been away from work for more than one year, to undergo a fitness for duty examination. The appellant was absent from the work site for some thirteen months when his removal was overturned. The agency has a legitimate interest in requiring him to pass a fitness for duty examination before returning him to work. See Connor v. US Postal Service, 50 MSPR 389 (1991) (although removal, based on the results of a psychiatric fitness for duty examination, was reversed due to procedural error, the administrative judge correctly found that the agency's insistence that the appellant undergo a fitness for duty examination before returning to duty did not constitute non-compliance with the Board's Order). In addition, the appellant was clearly informed by the agency that he would have to submit to a medical examination. In light of these circumstances, the appellant was obligated to undergo the medical examination before the agency restored him to his position. See also Daniel v. United States Postal Service, 16 MSPR 486, at 490 (an employee was required to undergo a fitness for duty examination after an absence of more than two years because the agency could not be expected to place the employee in a duty status until it had evaluated his fitness for duty).

We find therefore that the appellant has not established that the agency was in noncompliance with the Board's Order when it required him to report for a fitness for duty examination before he was returned to duty....
