broida mspb Chapter 16: Settlement, Remedies, and Enforcement Chapter 16: Settlement, Remedies, and Enforcement, Subchapter 03: Enforcement, Section A: Enforcement of MSPB Decision, Subsection 01: Authority to Order Compliance; Sanctions, Part a: Sanction Against Agency
a. Sanction Against Agency

Under 5 USC 1204(e)(2)(A), the Board may order that the salary be withheld of any official who interferes with compliance. Cherry v. Dept. of Army, PH-0752-18-0225-X-1 (NP 8/22/2022), explained:

Pursuant to 5 U.S.C. § 1204(e)(2)(A) and 5 C.F.R. § 1201.183(c), the Board has the authority to impose sanctions against the agency official responsible for noncompliance with a Board order. Such sanctions may include a ruling adverse to the agency and certification to the Comptroller General of the United States that no payment is to be made to certain agency employees found to be in noncompliance with the Board's order. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.183(e). Under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.183(a)(2), if the agency fails to submit the name, title, grade, and address of the agency official charged with complying with the Board's order, the Board will presume that the highest-ranking appropriate agency official who is not appointed by the President by and with the consent of the Senate is charged with compliance.

The Board has threatened but never stopped the salary of a noncompliant agency official. Essentially the Board operates on a "last chance" basis as it attempts to define areas of noncompliance and then allows the agency a given time to bring itself into compliance. See, e.g., Short v. USPS, 69 MSPR 141 (1995) (agency was found in noncompliance and given 20 days to comply; if agency had not then fully complied, it would have been required to show cause why salary withholding sanctions should not be imposed).

Although the Board threatened to withhold the salaries of agency officials if the agency does not achieve compliance, there are generally delays in compliance, enforcement proceedings, and then eventual compliance. One case noted that a period of noncompliance of about four months did not warrant the suspension of salary as a sanction once the agency complied with the Board order. Alexander v. USPS, 86 MSPR 434, 437 ¶ 10 (2000) (same result despite "lengthy delay in effecting compliance"); Fryer v. Dept. of Air Force, 27 MSPR 496 (1985) (Table). Four months or so of delay again passed without sanction in another case; the Board did not even manage to compel the agency to provide it with the name of the agency official who had made a determination not to comply with the Board's final decision. Simons v. DHHS, 89 MSPR 685, 686-87 ¶¶ 1-2 (2001) (Board General Counsel found the agency in compliance; no sanctions were ordered); see Suggs v. VA, SF-0752-09-0734-X-1 (NP 3/27/2014) (eventual compliance; no sanctions, but noting: "Even though DFAS computes back pay and interest on the agency's behalf, it is the agency's responsibility to ensure that these calculations are correct before submitting them to the Board. The agency cannot 'pass the buck' merely because it finds the computation process onerous or difficult to understand."); Day v. USPS, 113 MSPR 482, 485 ¶ 8, 2010 MSPB 63 (2010) (Board identifies the agency official responsible for compliance and warns of possible sanctions under 5 USC 1204(e)(2)(A) if compliance was not achieved); Readout v. OPM, 78 MSPR 637, 644 (1998) (on a request to withhold the salary of an agency official following noncompliance with a settlement: "the agency's actions to date have been woefully inadequate...we agree that such action may be appropriate if the agency fails to submit evidence of compliance in response to this order"); Zinno v. USPS, 77 MSPR 290, 297 (1998) (after agency failed to submit to the Board the name of the individual responsible for compliance, the Board reminded the agency of the possibility of sanctions); Ferry v. Dept. of Navy, 35 MSPR 12, 14 (1987) (finally determining that compliance had been achieved, Board bravely stated that "the agency is admonished concerning its failure to provide the name of the responsible official."); cf. Hood v. OPM, DA-0831-11-0336-X-1 (NP 12/23/2013) (sanctions threatened against an OPM official in a retirement case as a result of OPM's delays and incomprehensible submissions as to Board-ordered annuity payments); Bruton v. VA, 111 MSPR 489, 497-98 ¶ 21, 2009 MSPB 110 (2009) (following remand, Bruton v. VA, 112 MSPR 313, 2009 MSPB 149 (2009)) (Board named a hospital director as the agency official responsible for compliance and directed him to appear before the MSPB General Counsel to show cause why the Board should not impose sanctions for noncompliance).

Negligence or less than speedy relief alone will not be a basis for the penalty sanction; bad faith appears to be a prerequisite. Shirley v. OPM, 31 MSPR 640, 642 (1986); see Rivas v. USPS, 76 MSPR 584, 588-89 (1997) (compliance delay was excused and sanctions were not awarded when there was no agency bad faith: two employees working on the case were "out of office on Family and Medical Leave for extended periods of time"). Partial compliance seems to negate the element of intent to avoid compliance. Camastro v. DOJ, 80 MSPR 326, 327 (1998) ("Because the agency has already made some payment and has provided the additional explanation of its calculations requested by the administrative judge, we find that sanctions would be inappropriate."); Gay v. USPS, 41 MSPR 476, 482-83 (1989). The same for belated compliance. See, e.g., Connett v. USPS, 51 MSPR 18 (1991) (Table); Morones v. DOJ, 42 MSPR 642, 643 n.1 (1989) (declining to impose sanctions for failure of the agency to abide by the settlement because the agency brought itself into compliance with the settlement).

When an agency partially complies with a Board order and legitimate issues are raised that cannot be resolved without additional evidence being taken, sanctions are inappropriate. Hooks v. USPS, 59 MSPR 20, 23 (1993); see Smith v. Dept. of Army, 72 MSPR 676, 679 (1996) ("[t]he provisions of section 1204 are designed to obtain compliance, and...once compliance is achieved, sanctions are inappropriate."), followed in Martin v. DOJ, 86 MSPR 13, 15 ¶ 2 (2000); Hansson v. Dept. of Navy, 62 MSPR 228, 232 (1994) ("When an agency has partially complied with a Board order and legitimate issues have been raised that cannot be resolved without additional evidence being taken, sanctions would be inappropriate.").

The Board tells us that it is inappropriate to impose sanctions when compliance is complete, because sanctions are a means to enforce compliance. Mavronikolas v. USPS, 53 MSPR 113, 116 (1992). The notion that unnecessary sanctions should not be applied again found expression in Bales v. Dept. of Transp., 54 MSPR 187, 192 (1992):

Appellant also requests sanctions against the agency for the period of noncompliance, claiming the requested sanctions are necessary to overcome the stigma placed upon him by the agency by the demotion and to make him whole. The Board has the authority under 5 USC 1204(a)(2) to order any federal employee to comply with its orders and to enforce compliance with those orders. Under 5 USC 1204(e)(2)(A) the Board also has the authority to order that an employee "shall not be entitled to receive payment for service as an employee during any period that the order has not been complied with." Procedures for implementing these sanctions are set out at 5 CFR 1201.183. As we noted in Norris v. United States Postal Service, 29 MSPR 434, 435 (1985), however, "[t]he language of these statutory provisions makes it clear that they are to be implemented as mechanisms to enforce compliance." (Emphasis added.) Where an agency has complied with a Board order, sanctions pursuant to these sections are therefore inappropriate. Here, the agency has now submitted satisfactory evidence of compliance. We find, therefore, that the agency has complied with the Board's order of February 21, 1990. Because compliance has been achieved, sanctions are not appropriate.

O'Keefe v. VA, 69 MSPR 567, 569 (1996), added, concerning the sanction of withholding salaries of agency officials responsible for past noncompliance once compliance was achieved:

Under 5 USC § 1204(a)(2) the Board is authorized to order any federal employee to comply with its orders and to enforce compliance with those orders. To enforce compliance, the Board has authority under 5 USC § 1204(e)(2)(A) to order that an employee responsible for compliance with an order "shall not be entitled to receive payment for service as an employee during any period that the order has not been complied with." It is clear from the language of section 1204(e)(2)(A) that it is a mechanism to obtain compliance and that sanctions pursuant to this provision are not appropriate after the agency has complied with the Board's order. See Holtgrewe v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 65 MSPR 137, 141 (1994). It is not disputed that the agency has issued the appellant a police badge and restored him to full police officer duties. The appellant is therefore not entitled to an order imposing sanctions under section 1204(e)(2)(A), and he has cited no other basis for the Board to award him damages....

See Tubesing v. DHHS, 115 MSPR 327, 337, 340 ¶¶ 21, 33-34, 2010 MSPB 242 (2010) (when the agency failed to name a responsible official who could be sanctioned for continued noncompliance, the Board determined the name and title of an agency official; the Board lacked legal authority to take other sanctions relative to compliance, e.g., drawing adverse inferences, precluding introduction of evidence, or disregarding portions of a pleading).

An appellant's request for sanctions may be defeated if the appellant is partially responsible for the lack of full implementation of a settlement agreement approved by an initial decision. Raphel v. Dept. of Army, 54 MSPR 596, 608 (1992). Assuming any necessity for sanctions, the Board is limited to its statutory compliance sanction. It may not sanction an agency by directing the agency to pay the appellant back pay that the appellant is not entitled to receive under statute or regulation. Lavelle v. Dept. of Navy, 37 MSPR 86, 89-90 (1988), overruled in part on other grounds by Mattern v. Dept. of Treasury, 88 MSPR 65, 72 ¶ 16 (2001); see Czubinski v. Dept. of Treasury, 85 MSPR 278, 279-80 ¶ 3 (2000) (agency would not be sanctioned for an initial failure to pay TSP contributions by reissuing a back pay check without reimbursement by appellant for the amount initially received; appellant was required to reimburse the agency as a condition of the agency's reissuance of corrected payments), aff'd, Czubinski v. Dept. of Treasury (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 00-3219). The agency cannot be subjected to monetary sanctions for noncompliance with a Board remedial order. See Patterson v. OPM, DC-0841-12-0399-C-1 (NP 7/3/2014) ("Moreover, although the Board has the authority to impose sanctions for failure to comply with a Board order, it lacks the authority to impose monetary damages as a sanction for noncompliance. Cunningham v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 91 M.S.P.R. 523, ¶ 3 (2002)."); cf. Suttles v. OPM, 37 MSPR 282, 283-84 (1988) (no authorization for an award of interest against OPM for a delay of several months in issuing a retirement refund; appellant had already received statutory interest for the sum).
