6. Appeal Right if Termination Occurs Before End of Expiration of Limited Appointment

Williams v. DOD, 76 MSPR 270, 274 (1997), explained that a statutory appeal right is available to a term employee who survives his trial period but who is terminated before the expiration date of the term appointment:

Here, as to the issue of whether the appellant made below a nonfrivolous allegation of the Board's jurisdiction over his appeal, 5 USC 7511(a)(1) provides that an "employee," for purposes of entitlement to coverage under the adverse action procedures set forth in 5 USC chapter 75, subchapter II, is defined as "(A) an individual in the competitive service--(i) who is not serving a probationary or trial period under an initial appointment; or (ii) who has completed 1 year of current continuous service under other than a temporary appointment limited to 1 year or less[.] " The Board has held that the appeal rights of an individual serving under a term appointment in the competitive service are governed by 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(A)(i). Youngs v. Department of the Army, 73 MSPR 551, 557 (1997). Thus, such an individual who completes a trial period under a term appointment meets the definition of "employee" under 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(A)(i). Youngs, 73 MSPR at 560. Thus, if a term employee is separated for cause after completion of the trial period, but before the expiration of the term appointment, such employee has been subjected to a removal under 5 USC 7512(1). A term employee against whom an adverse action listed in 5 USC 7512 is contemplated, is entitled to the procedural rights set forth in 5 USC 7513(b) (including 30 days advance written notice stating the reasons for the proposed action, a reasonable time to respond, and a written decision stating the reasons for the agency's action), and a right of appeal to the Board under 5 USC 7513(d). in Youngs, 73 MSPR at 560.

Anderson v. GSA, 56 MSPR 316, 318-19 (1993), alluded to circumstances allowing an appeal to the terminated temporary employee:

The administrative judge's finding that the Board lacked jurisdiction over the appellants' appeals appears to have been based on his belief that the Board lacked jurisdiction over any termination of a temporary appointment.... We know of no basis for such a broad holding. While one of the decisions on which the administrative judge relied supports the proposition that a separation on the expiration date of an appointment does not constitute a removal appealable to the Board, Soehngen v. Dept. of Justice, 47 MSPR 169, 172 (1991), that decision does not indicate that a separation before that date may not be appealed under any circumstances.[2]

[2] An authority cited in that decision indicates that the reason a separation on the expiration of an appointment is not appealable is that the separation simply carries out the terms of the appointment. See Berger v. Department of Commerce, 3 MSPB 297, 3 MSPR 198, 199-200 (1980), cited in Soehngen, 47 MSPR at 172.

For the reasons stated below, however, we concur in the administrative judge's conclusion that the Board lacks jurisdiction over the appellants' appeals.

First, we note that, under 5 USC 7512(1) and 7513(d), the Board has jurisdiction over an appeal from the removal of an "employee," as that term is defined at 5 USC 7511(a)(1). An individual in the competitive service is an "employee" if (1) he is not serving a probationary or trial period under an initial appointment and (2) he has completed 1 year of current continuous service under other than a temporary appointment limited to 1 year or less. 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(A).

The appellants were employed in the competitive service, and there is no indication in the record that any of them was serving a probationary or trial period at the time of his separation. Furthermore, the record indicates that each appellant had been continuously employed by the agency for more than a year when he was separated. None of the appellants, however, has shown that he had a year of current continuous service other than service under a temporary appointment limited to a year or less. The service of appellant Anderson appears to have been performed entirely under two temporary appointments, each of them limited to 1 year; and, except for a period lasting well under a year, the service of the other two appellants was performed under two temporary appointments, each of them limited to 1 year. We therefore find that the appellants have failed to show that they met the definition of "employee" described above. See Fish v. Department of Navy, 29 MSPR 595, 598 (1986).

Care must be taken to determine whether a term employee's prior service furnishes the service credit necessary to provide an appeal right, as was noted in Williams v. DOD, 76 MSPR 270 (1997), supra, and more extensively in Todd v. DOD, 76 MSPR 184, 188-90 (1997):

Here, as to the issue of whether the appellant made below a nonfrivolous allegation of the Board's jurisdiction over his appeal, 5 USC 7511(a)(1) provides that an "employee," for purposes of entitlement to coverage under the adverse action procedures set forth in 5 USC chapter 75, subchapter II, is defined as "(A) an individual in the competitive service--(i) who is not serving a probationary or trial period under an initial appointment; or (ii) who has completed 1 year of current continuous service under other than a temporary appointment limited to 1 year or less[.] " The Board has held that the appeal rights of an individual serving under a term appointment in the competitive service are governed by 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(A)(i). Youngs v. Department of the Army, 73 MSPR 551, 557 (1997). Thus, such an individual who completes a trial period under a term appointment meets the definition of "employee" under 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(A)(i). Youngs, 73 MSPR at 560. Thus, if a term employee is separated for cause after completion of the trial period, but before the expiration of the term appointment, such employee has been subjected to a removal under 5 USC 7512(1). A term employee against whom an adverse action listed in 5 USC 7512 is contemplated, is entitled to the procedural rights set forth in 5 USC 7513(b) (including 30 days advance written notice stating the reasons for the proposed action, a reasonable time to respond, and a written decision stating the reasons for the agency's action), and a right of appeal to the Board under 5 USC 7513(d). Youngs, 73 MSPR at 560.

The appellant asserted below, both in his response to the administrative judge's acknowledgment order and in a supporting affidavit, that the Board has jurisdiction over his appeal because he worked as a Supply Worker under a temporary appointment prior to the conversion of that appointment to a term appointment. In support of his assertion, he referred to the SF-50 that documented the conversion.

A review of the SF-50 in question shows that, effective July 7, 1996, the appellant's prior unspecified appointment was converted to a term appointment in the competitive service (not the excepted service as the appellant claims), not to exceed September 30, 1997, and that his appointment was for more than 1 year and was subject to the completion of a 1-year trial period beginning July 7, 1996. The Board has held that prior temporary service will be credited towards the completion of a trial period under a term appointment provided that certain conditions are met, i.e., the service under the temporary and term appointments are continuous (without a break in service of more than 30 days), the appointments are in the same line of work and in the same agency, and, together, the appointments exceed 1 year. Youngs, 73 MSPR at 559.

In the present case, the appellant made below nonfrivolous allegations that he met these requirements. The appellant's assertions and the SF-50 show that: The appellant had service prior to the conversion of his appointment to a term appointment; the fact that the appellant's prior appointment was converted indicates that there was no break in his service; the appellant continued to occupy the position of Supply Worker after the conversion of his appointment; and he continued to work in the same agency. Further, we note the appellant's sworn and unsworn assertions that he served for more than 1 year under his temporary and term appointments. The SF-50 that documents the conversion of the appellant's prior appointment to a term appointment lists the appellant's service computation date as January 3, 1995. It cannot be determined from the documentary evidence of record, however, the length of the appellant's prior temporary service that preceded the conversion of his appointment to a term appointment and, thus, whether these two appointments together exceeded 1 year of service. See Youngs, 73 MSPR at 559-60.

Competitive service term employees may have the appellate rights of a probationer if they are terminated before the end of the first year or "trial period" of their employment. See Jordan v. Dept. of Air Force, 61 MSPR 388, 392 (1994) (appeal rights of probationary employees).
