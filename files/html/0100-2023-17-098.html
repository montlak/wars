1. Agency Responsibility

The burden of proving the legitimacy of a RIF is on the agency; it is not a burden of proof that falls upon the appellant in the nature of an affirmative defense. Waters v. Dept. of Navy, 5 MSPR 509, 5 MSPB 504 (1981). The agency must show by a preponderance of evidence the proper invocation of RIF regulations as well as the specific application of those regulations to the individual employee. Smith v. Dept. of Air Force, 7 MSPR 366, 368, 7 MSPB 246 (1981); Shockro v. FCC, 5 MSPR 113, 117, 5 MSPB 181 (1981). Although an agency may initially establish that the regulations were properly invoked, shifting to the employee the burden of going forward with rebuttal evidence, the burden of persuasion concerning the propriety of the RIF never shifts from the agency. Brandon v. Dept. of Army, 4 MSPR 38, 40, 4 MSPB 129 (1980). The Board relies on the RIF notice itself to show that the RIF was undertaken for a valid reason, but the notice will not suffice to prove the agency's case if there is a challenge to the validity of the reason asserted in the notice. Then the agency must offer evidence. Conclusory statements offered by the agency's personnel director in the response to the appeal will not sustain the agency's burden of proof. See Rosen v. ICC, 20 MSPR 574, 578 (1984); cf. Flores v. USPS, 75 MSPR 546, 551 (1997) (rejecting conclusory agency statements concerning the appellant's specific evidence of qualifications in support of assignment rights); Buckler v. Fed. Retirement Thrift Inv. Bd., 73 MSPR 476, 494-95 (1997) (rejecting the vague and conclusory testimony of agency witnesses offered to demonstrate the appellant's purported lack of qualifications for a position sought through assignment rights; the witnesses failed to identify specific flaws in the appellant's writing; the agency introduced no examples of the appellant's allegedly poor writing to buttress its claim that he lacked writing skill). An agency cannot rely on OPM recommendations based upon a verbal description to OPM of the circumstances (for example, whether a particular action is a reclassification or a RIF). Nichols v. DOD, 19 MSPR 471, 474 (1984).

The agency must present a full record in each case appealed. If employees who make claims to common jobs present separate appeals and the agency does not request and obtain consolidation of those appeals, the agency may end up with inconsistent results. It is too late, once decisions are issued in individual cases, for the agency to seek to consolidate them through PFRs and sort out the inconsistencies in allocation of assignment rights to various jobs. See Brooks v. DHHS, 31 MSPR 576, 578 (1986). [Refer to Chapter 5, "Requested by Agency" for stay requests submitted by agencies desiring to avoid inconsistent results in related appeals.]
