a. Wiretap

The Board showed some willingness to consider exclusion of intercepted telephone calls under the standards of the Omnibus Crime Control and Safe Streets Act of 1968 [18 USC 2510], in Bernstein v. Dept. of Transp., 18 MSPR 191, 194 n.5 (1983). The manager of a facility listened in on a telephone conversation between the appellant and his supervisor, without the consent of either. The evidence came in under the "business extension" exemption to a statute prohibiting telephonic communications intercepts:

Appellant asserts that the presiding official erred in admitting testimony concerning the telephone conversation between appellant and Mr. Wingard, since Mr. Walters, the manager of Napa Tower, admittedly listened to the conversation on an extension phone, without either party's consent or knowledge. Appellant contends that the entire conversation must be excluded from evidence under 18 USC § 2515 and California Penal Code Section 632.

Title III of the Omnibus Crime Control and Safe Streets Act of 1968 (18 USC § 2510 et seq.) prohibits the judicially unauthorized interception of wire communications. The statute, however, recognizes an exception to the definition of intercepting device for equipment being used in the ordinary course of business. See 18 USC § 2510(5)(a)(i).

We find that this business extension exemption applies to appellant's case. Although appellant's counsel emphasizes the personal relationship between appellant and Mr. Wingard, there can be no doubt that Mr. Wingard's communication with appellant was solely a business call. Mr. Wingard phoned appellant from their place of business and engaged in a conversation limited in topic to instructing appellant to report to work the next day, in tone to a curt exchange, and in time to a few minutes. Moreover, the objective of the call and appellant's reaction to it were obviously of legitimate and vital interest to the agency as a whole, and to Mr. Walters, as the manager of the tower. See generally Watkins v. L.M. Berry & Company, 704 F.2d 577 (11th Cir. 1983), Annot., 58 A.L.R. Fed. 594 (1982). Therefore, we find that, since the telephone used by Mr. Walters did not fall within the statutory definition of an intercepting device, Title III does not apply to appellant's case. Furthermore, we find that appellant's assertion of error based on a state law of evidence does not warrant review of the presiding official's rulings in this federal administrative agency proceeding. Thus, the presiding official did not commit error by admitting the conversation into evidence.

Wissner v. United States, 176 Ct. Cl. 1372, 1375 (1966), said of a challenge to evidence used to support dismissal of a contract specialist alleged to have solicited and accepted gifts from a contractor:

The other basic complaint of plaintiff here appears to be the contention that his constitutional rights were violated when FBI agents monitored his telephone conversations without his consent or knowledge. However, we are of the opinion that this contention is disposed of adversely to plaintiff by the case of Rathbun v. United States, 355 U.S. 107 (1957), where the Supreme Court held that each party to a telephone conversation takes the risk that the other party may have an extension telephone and may allow another person to overhear the conversation.

If the challenged evidence was not relied on by the AJ, the constitutional issue need not be reached. See Sabie v. USPS, 27 MSPR 220, 221 (1985) (no harmful error when the AJ denied a motion to strike evidence concerning the surveillance of the appellant through the use of an electronic beeper; the record contained other evidence supporting the result reached by the AJ); cf. King v. Dept. of Labor, 37 MSPR 110, 113 (1988) (if an investigation required self incrimination, the error was harmless; an adverse action was supported by other sustained charges).
