broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 16: Facilities, Benefits for Employees, Section I: Housing

I. HOUSING

Government-owned housing supplied for employees as a result of a lack of available housing is a condition of employment. DOJ, INS and AFGE Local 2509, 14 FLRA 578, 591-93 (1984) (ALJ Decision). But if a proposal concerning provision of housing is inconsistent with a treaty between this and another country (the Panama Canal Treaty) it is nonnegotiable. It is inconsistent with federal law. Int'l Org. of Masters, Mates and Pilots and Panama Canal Comm'n, 13 FLRA 508, 511-13 (1983) (Proposal 5). A proposal creating a quarters entitlement consistent with civilian grade was nonnegotiable. A statute controlled the assignment of the type of quarters used by employees. AFGE Local 3004 and Dept. of Army and Air Force, Nat'l Guard Bureau, 15 FLRA 270, 271 (1984).

A government-wide regulation blocked negotiation on a proposal relating to the quality of accommodations that employees were required to use during travel. The regulation consisted of a congressional "mandate" in the form of a House Report accompanying an appropriations bill that expressed a desire that agencies establish uniform rules relating to the adequacy of government-supplied housing. The need for uniformity precluded variations through the collective bargaining process. The Conference Report was an "outside authority" within the meaning of § 2424.11(c) of Authority regulations. NFFE Local 561 and Army Corps of Eng'rs, Mobile, 17 FLRA 759, 766-67 (1985).

Within the duty to bargain was a proposal allowing employees attending an agency school to arrange for their housing rather than requiring them to stay at agency-arranged housing and compelling the agency to pay to the employee that money the agency would otherwise pay for the lodging it arranged, in addition to regular per diem. The proposal did not violate the agency right to determine its budget, a determination that will only be made if an agency demonstrates that a proposal requires a significant and unavoidable increase in costs not offset by compensating benefits. The proposal did not interfere with the right of the agency to contract for property or services under then-controlling 41 USC 252(a) and the proposal was not inconsistent with the obligation of the agency head under 5 USC 5911(e) not to require employees to occupy quarters on a rental basis absent certain conditions. The proposal did not violate agency rights concerning requirements contracts under the regulation then in effect. Nat'l Weather Serv. Employees Org. and Dept. of Commerce, Nat'l Weather Serv., NOAA, 21 FLRA 590, 590-93 (1986); see NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., 46 FLRA 696, 754-56 (1992) (Provision 29 negotiable: allowing employees to select their lodging while on official travel except under certain circumstances); NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., 31 FLRA 181, 195-98 (1988) (Proposals 2 and 3 were negotiable, allowing choice of government or private housing for students attending training center at Glynco, with reimbursement at same rate as students residing at facility; students residing at facility would not be required to share room), aff'd, Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv. v. FLRA, 873 F.2d 1473, 1475-76 (D.C. Cir. 1989); see NFFE Local 405 and Dept. of Army, Army Info. Sys. Command, St. Louis, 42 FLRA 1112, 1147, 1149 (1991) (negotiable, Proposal 28: "When management or supervisory personnel are on the same or similar missions at that site and there are not enough quarters available to accommodate all individuals, employees will be allocated quarters or lodging after the management individuals are accommodated.").

A proposal was nonnegotiable that prevented management from assigning unit employees on temporary duty at a military installation to government quarters unless all management personnel on the same or similar mission were assigned to government quarters. The proposal conflicted with joint travel regulations and associated DOD regulations requiring that, when adequate government quarters are not used by an employee the quarters portion of the per diem will not be paid without a statement of nonavailability or nonutilization. The joint travel regulations were supported by a compelling need--a demonstrated interest by Congress in avoiding disbursement of funds for lodging employees on temporary duty if government quarters were available but not occupied. NFFE Local 405 and Army Aviation Sys. Command, 33 FLRA 604, 612-15 (1988) (Provision 4). Also nonnegotiable in NAGE, SEIU Local R1-34 and Dept. of Navy, Naval Underwater Sys. Ctr., 43 FLRA 1526, 1527, 1529-32 (1992), was the proposal that, upon the determination that use of government-furnished quarters is not "unequivocally essential to the TDY mission," an employee using other suitable accommodations, despite the availability of suitable government quarters, shall be authorized the full quarters portion of per diem or actual expense of the rate he would have been paid had he been residing in government quarters. The proposal was contrary to a DOD joint travel regulation supported by a compelling need requiring uniform rules concerning use of government quarters.
