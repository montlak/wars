a. Employee on LWOP

Early Board decisions determined that an employee has no right of appeal if he requests and takes LWOP because of physical inability to do the job, but contends that he would be able to perform a different job that does not have the same physical requirements; the leave status not being the result of disciplinary action, is not subject to adverse action appeal. Tyndall v. Dept. of Navy, 5 MSPR 194, 196, 5 MSPB 247 (1981). The appellant who has a temporary, documented medical restriction precluding his working in his assigned position is not on enforced leave when he is placed on LWOP at his request after he declines to return to work because of the medical restriction. Finn v. VA, 39 MSPR 658, 661-62 (1989) ("While the appellant's failure to return to work was perhaps medically justifiable, it was entirely voluntary and...it was not at the behest, or under the control, of the agency. The appellant's absences were not contingent upon agency review and his return was not dependent upon the agency's changing its mind."), overruled in part, Rivas v. USPS, 61 MSPR 121 (1994); see Connor v. USPS, 52 MSPR 583, 587 (1992) (employee took leave and the agency never specifically denied the appellant the right to return to duty; Pittman was distinguishable "where an employee, rather than the agency, initiates the leave"). Under early law, an employee on leave because of a physical impairment who awaits OPM approval of his own disability retirement application has no right to an adverse action appeal should the agency place him on leave without pay after all his leave is exhausted; the nonduty status is not a suspension and it is not for disciplinary reasons if the employee's inability to perform his duties is undisputed. Varner v. Dept. of Navy, 5 MSPR 17, 18-19, 5 MSPB 99 (1981). If the appellant states that he is ready and able to work in a prior position that is no longer his, but claims that he is unable to work in his currently assigned position, the resulting leave status is not enforced leave. Kinsella v. DHHS, 41 MSPR 643, 649 (1989); cf. Jennings v. SSA, AT-4324-11-0442-B-1 (NP 4/10/2023) ("agency's action retroactively placing the appellant on LWOP to eliminate his improper dual compensation, did not constitute a 'suspension' because the agency did not place him in a 'status without duties' during the period in question. Rather, the appellant chose to be absent from his agency workplace during his scheduled work hours."). [Refer below to "Light Duty Cases."]

A lot of law has come and gone since the early LWOP appeals decisions. If an employee is barred from the workplace, or not permitted to return once medical restrictions are met, an enforced leave or constructive suspension analysis would likely be applied even if the employee were not carried on annual or sick leave and instead was placed on LWOP.
