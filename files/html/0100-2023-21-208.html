e. Separation by Settlement Agreement

If an employee who has received a retroactive award of workers' compensation is separated through a settlement agreement rather than on the basis of absence from the job (later covered by the OWCP finding), restoration rights may be precluded, Polk v. Dept. of Army, 40 MSPR 539, 543-46 (1989):

The Board has held that removal actions based on agency charges of AWOL cannot be sustained when the OWCP determines that the employee was entitled to compensation benefits during the time period charged by the agency, as a result of a work-related injury. See, e.g., Crawford v. Veterans Administration, 22 M.S.P.R. 380, 382-83 (1984); Stith v. Department of Housing and Urban Development, 21 M.S.P.R. 328, 331-32 (1984); McGilberry v. Defense Mapping Agency, 18 M.S.P.R. 560, 563 (1984).

However, the appellant in the present case was not removed for AWOL. Rather, as the administrative judge found, she resigned from her position under the terms of the settlement agreement entered into by the parties in the prior abandoned removal action.

Pursuant to the regulations issued by the Office of Personnel Management under the authority of 5 U.S.C. § 8151(b), an injured employee whose recovery takes longer than one year from the date compensation began is entitled to priority consideration for restoration to the position he left or an equivalent one if he "was separated because of a compensable injury...." 5 C.F.R. § 353.307 (emphasis supplied). See 5 C.F.R. § 353.103(c)(1) (the provisions of 5 C.F.R. Part 353 cover employees who were "separated or furloughed from a position without time limitation as a result of a compensable injury") (emphasis supplied). Accordingly, the Board has determined that the right to restoration applies only to a fully recovered employee whose separation either resulted from or was substantially related to a compensable injury. See Rishavy v. United States Postal Service, 35 M.S.P.R. 528, 530 (1987; Miner v. United States Postal Service, 31 M.S.P.R. 369, 372 (1986), aff'd, 819 F.2d 280 (Fed. Cir. 1987); Cox v. Department of Transportation, 31 M.S.P.R. 148, 151, aff'd, 795 F.2d 1013 (Fed. Cir. 1986); Ruppert v. United States Postal Service, 8 M.S.P.R. 593, 596 (1981).[11]

[11] We have previously noted that, although the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit has commented that the "substantially related" criterion applied by the Board does not appear in 5 U.S.C. §§ 8101 or 8151, or 5 C.F.R. Part 353, the court has not rejected its use. See Brown-Cummings v. Department of Health and Human Services, 36 M.S.P.R. 94, 100 n.7 (1987), appeal after remand, 39 MSPR 627 (1989)).

An employee who is removed for cause or who is separated from his position by resignation or retirement does not become entitled to restoration under 5 C.F.R. Part 353 simply by receiving an OWCP award after his separation. The separation must have resulted from or been substantially related to the compensable injury. See Miner, 31 M.S.P.R. at 372-73; Cox, 31 M.S.P.R. at 151-52; Johnson v. United States Postal Service, 29 M.S.P.R. 138, 140 (1985), appeal after remand, 30 M.S.P.R. 188 (1986), aff'd sub nom. Johnson v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 812 F.2d 705 (Fed. Cir. 1987); Miller v. United States Postal Service, 3 M.S.P.R. 336, 337 (1980).

The appellant was separated from her position by her voluntary resignation under the settlement agreement entered into by the parties in the prior removal action. The administrative judge refused to go beyond the terms of the settlement agreement to analyze the reasons for the abandoned removal action. Under the circumstances of this case, we find that determination was, at least, premature.

A settlement agreement is a contract between the parties, the interpretation of which is a question of law. See Wethington v. Department of the Army, 39 MSPR 285, 286-87 (1988); Fredendall v. Veterans Administration, 38 M.S.P.R. 366, 371 (1988). In construing the settlement agreement, the Board looks first to the terms of the agreement itself in order to determine the intent of the parties at the time that they entered into the contract. See Wethington, at 287. The Board will consider parol evidence only if the agreement is ambiguous. See id. We find in the instant case that the terms of the settlement agreement are ambiguous, and that a remand for further development of the record is necessary.

The law is clear that a waiver of a right in a settlement agreement must be comprehensive, freely made and fair. McCall v. United States Postal Service, 839 F.2d 664, 668 (Fed. Cir. 1988), citing Ferby v. United States Postal Service, 26 M.S.P.R. 451, 456 (1985). Moreover, in order to be enforceable, a waiver of a statutory right must constitute the informed, intentional relinquishment of a known right. McCall, 839 F.2d at 668; Ferby, 26 M.S.P.R. at 455. See, e.g., Rhoades v. United States Postal Service, 36 M.S.P.R. 639, 642-45 (1988) (a settlement agreement cannot be deemed to encompass the statutory entitlement to attorney fees unless the language of the agreement itself, or the underlying circumstances, demonstrate the intent of the prevailing party to waive that right).

The settlement agreement in the instant ease does not provide for an explicit waiver of the appellant's right to request priority consideration for reemployment under 5 U.S.C. § 8151(b)(2) and 5 C.F.R. Part 353. Nor does the record indicate that the parties intended such a waiver to be included in the settlement agreement. The terms of the settlement agreement disclose that the intent of the parties at the time that they entered into the contract was to resolve the entire appeal before the Board. See Wethington, at 287.

The agreement here was entered into the record on April 9, 1987. In her petition for review, the appellant alleges that, at the time the settlement agreement was concluded, the agency was aware that the appellant had applied to OWCP for workers' compensation. On July 23, 1987, after the settlement agreement was reached, OWCP notified the appellant that her claim had been approved for her compensable injury. Based on these facts and allegations, it is unclear whether the parties intended, at the time they contracted, that the appellant waive her right to request priority consideration for restoration under the settlement agreement. Thus, we find it necessary to remand the appeal for further inquiry into the matter.

On remand, the administrative judge is to determine consideration for restoration to duty under 5 U.S.C. § 8151 (b) (2) and 5 C.F.R. Part 353, pursuant to the settlement agreement. If so, the administrative judge is to determine whether the waiver constituted an informed, intentional relinquishment of a known right. See McCall, 839 F.2d at 668; Ferby, 26 M.S.P.R. at 455-56; Wethington, at 287. If the administrative judge finds that the appellant did not knowingly and intentionally waive this right, he should then determine whether the appellant's removal resulted from a compensable injury, in light of the fact that failure to follow leave procedures was also part of the charge against her.
