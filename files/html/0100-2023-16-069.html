g. Harmful Error Rule

The Board's decisions on the application of the harmful error rule in performance cases are inconsistent. It was determined that the substantive requirements of 5 USC 4302(c)(1), (2), and (6) are not subject to harmful error analysis. Jimenez-Howe v. Dept. of Labor, 35 MSPR 202, 208 (1987). Although Sandland suggested that the lack of opportunity for improvement is not just procedural error, the Board does appear to use a harmful error analysis in some of its Chapter 43 decisions. In one case, a 30-day improvement period rather than the 90-day period for improvement required by agency regulation was error, but it was subject to the harmful error test. A possibility of prejudice was insufficient to warrant removal. The appellant would have to show that additional time would have permitted her to improve and would have caused the agency not to take the removal action. Wood v. Navy, 27 MSPR 659, 663 (1985) (military personnel clerk was removed for failure to meet a critical element calling for a determination of the promotional eligibility of officers to be considered for selection); cf. Melnick v. DHUD, 42 MSPR 93, 101-02 (1989) (no error was shown when the PIP was shorter than the time allowed to the appellant to demonstrate an acceptable performance for her WIGI). In Allen v. DOD, 4 MSPR 465, 5 MSPB 5 (1981), a counsel fee decision, failure to allow the appellant any reasonable time to improve his performance constituted error sufficiently harmful to warrant reversal of the removal and a fee award.

Wood, supra, assumes that some point exists demarcating an improvement period as too short, yet makes it impossible for the employee to prove the demarcation. If an agency regulation cannot be used to provide at least a rebuttable presumption of harmful error, the regulation is truly without import, as is the employee's substantive right to a meaningful improvement period. The Board continues to apply the harmful error test to problems occurring during the improvement period, declaring in Jones v. Nat'l Gallery of Art, 36 MSPR 602, 605 n.* (1988), that "[A]lthough the Board has held that the opportunity to improve is a substantive right...we have found it appropriate to analyze certain issues relating to the improvement period under the harmful procedural error rule":

Although the Board has held that the opportunity to improve is a substantive right, see Sandland, 23 MSPR at 587, we have found it appropriate to analyze certain issues relating to the improvement period under the harmful procedural error rule. The Board's decision in Wood v. Department of the Navy, 27 MSPR 659 (1985), is directly on point and suggests that a harmful procedural error analysis should be applied to this case. In Wood, the Board rejected the appellant's claim that the agency's provision of a thirty-day improvement period, as opposed to the ninety-day period required by the agency's regulations, was harmful error, finding that the appellant had failed to show that the additional time would have permitted her to improve her performance. See also Green v. Department of Labor, 26 MSPR 96, 98 n.3 (1985) (the appellant did not show that the agency's failure to provide him with weekly counseling, as required by his performance improvement plan, was harmful error). Moreover here, the appellant has not contended that he was harmed by the agency's failure to give him a full ninety days to complete the performance plans for his staff.

If a performance plan makes a promise, e.g., of weekly counseling, and there is some counseling but not strict adherence to the schedule, there is no reversible error without proof of harm under the harmful error standard. Green v. Dept. of Labor, 26 MSPR 96, 98 n.3 (1985); see Griggs v. Dept. of Army, 53 MSPR 597, 601 (1992) (failure of a supervisor to meet with the appellant on a biweekly basis was not prejudicial; evidence demonstrated that acceptable performance was not jeopardized by the supervisor's failure to comply with that provision of the PIP). If it is required by agency regulations that during the appraisal period the employee receive counseling or performance reviews, the case against the employee will not be set aside for a technical violation of those regulations, such as a requirement that the counseling or interim appraisal be documented, as long as the substance of the requirement was met. Martin v. FAA, 795 F.2d 995 (Fed. Cir. 1986). Should a collective bargaining contract require the agency to perform some task concerning performance appraisal, e.g., rewriting a position description to match work requirements, a violation of that provision is examined under the harmful error standard as to its effect on a Chapter 43 action. DeSousa v. AID, 38 MSPR 522, 525-26 (1988). DeSousa was followed in Manock v. Dept. of State, PH-0432-13-0293-I-1 (NP 7/23/2015):

We interpret the appellant's argument that the performance standards were in violation of law as an allegation of harmful procedural error.[9] See Slavich v. Social Security Administration, 102 M.S.P.R. 171, ¶ 8 (2006) (applying a harmful error standard to an appellant's claim that her performance standards were invalid because the agency did not comply with the terms of a collective bargaining agreement in creating the standards); see also DeSousa v. Agency for International Development, 38 M.S.P.R. 522, 526 (1988) (applying a harmful error standard to an appellant's claim that an agency violated a collective bargaining agreement in establishing his performance standards). To establish harmful error, the appellant must demonstrate that it is likely that the error affected the outcome of the action before the agency. See Jimenez-Howe v. Department of Labor, 35 M.S.P.R. 202, 205-06 (1987); see also 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(c)(1) (an appellant bears the burden of proving harmful error).
