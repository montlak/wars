6. Promotion Claim

With the caveat that promotion issues are the subject of IRA (whistleblower reprisal) [Chapter 13 of the Guide], USERRA or VEOA claims [Chapter 1 of the Guide], and that they may surface in claims for retroactive relief in successful adverse action appeals [Chapter 16 of the Guide] as well in situations when a promotion is granted and then canceled [Chapter 6 of the Guide], the Board does not have ordinary appellate jurisdiction to decide challenges to merit promotion actions. Geisinger v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1995 NP No. 95-3352) (citing Ellison v. MSPB, 7 F.3d 1031, 1034 (Fed. Cir. 1993)); see Sambrano v. MSPB, 240 Fed. Appx. 420 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP) (stating the jurisdictional rule and adding that "[l]ack of standard procedure in the selection process is not an 'employment practice' within the meaning of 5 CFR 300.104(a)"); Latimer v. Dept. of Air Force, 657 F.2d 235, 238 (8th Cir. 1981) (examining the facts relating to the claimed promotion: "There being no promotion, it is clear that there could be no subsequent reduction in grade or removal. Therefore, the MSPB properly concluded it had no appellate jurisdiction to review Latimer's appeal."); Williams v. Dept. of Army, 651 F.2d 243, 244 (4th Cir. 1981) ("The Board is charged with the hearing or adjudication of such matters as are placed within its jurisdiction 'under any law, rule, or regulation.' 5 U.S.C. §§ 7701(a) and 1205(a)(1). By law, 'adverse actions' are subject to review by the Board. 5 U.S.C. § 7513(d). These actions, however, are narrowly defined and include only removal, suspension for more than 14 days, reduction in grade, reduction in pay, and furlough of 30 days or less. 5 U.S.C. § 7512(1-5).... Appellant's assertion that she was denied a promotion falls within none of these categories and is, thus, not reviewable by the Board under this authority."); James v. DOD, DA-3443-14-0073-I-1 (NP 5/7/2014) ("It is well settled that the Board generally lacks jurisdiction to review an agency's decision not to select a particular applicant for a vacant position. Sapla v. Department of the Navy, 118 M.S.P.R. 551, ¶ 8 (2012). Although exceptions to this general rule exist in the context of an individual right of action appeal under the Whistleblower Protection Act and when the unsuccessful candidate claims that the agency's decision was in violation of his rights under the Veterans Employment Opportunities Act of 1998 or the Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act of 1994, id., the appellant has not presented any argument or evidence that these exceptions apply."); Santos v. Dept. of Transp., 48 MSPR 579, 580 (1991) (no jurisdiction over a claim that the agency had, over the course of the appellant's tenure, denied him the opportunity to receive pay commensurate with the difficulty of his work); Headds v. EEOC, 5 MSPR 347, 348 (1981) (claim to the position of acting supervisor); Langster v. SSA, 2 MSPR 32, 33, 2 MSPB 206 (1980) (the agency rejected an application for promotion to a position that it announced was to be filled by reassignment only).

Denial or delay of promotion is not a change in the basic rate of pay, and it is not appealable. Segovia v. Dept. of Treasury (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 00-3016) (the Board correctly determined that it lacked jurisdiction over a "demotion" when the appellant was tentatively selected for promotion but, prior to the effective date, the promotion was canceled because of a pending disciplinary investigation into the appellant's conduct); Ford v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1986 NP No. 86-1045) (because it is not within the Board's appellate jurisdiction, nonpromotion may be pursued to the Board, if at all, through OSC); McVay v. Arkansas Nat'l Guard, 80 MSPR 120, 123 (1998) ("An agency's decision not to promote an employee is not directly appealable to the Board, but it is subject to challenge in an IRA appeal."); Pritchett v. USPS, 72 MSPR 35, 38 (1996) (without an IRA appeal, a promotion or nonselection for promotion is not appealable as an adverse action or as a RIF); Harper v. Customs Serv., 38 MSPR 364, 365 (1988) (a claim that the appellant was entitled to a promotion to GS-7 rather than GS-6); Fair v. FAA, 4 MSPR 493, 496, 5 MSPB 28 (1981) ("[D]enial of a promotion does not involve a reduction in an employee's basic rate of pay and therefore does not fall within the subject matter areas reviewable by the Board.").

Hundley v. USPS, 64 MSPR 598, 602 n.2 (1994), noted the availability of its review procedures for IRA cases alleging nonselection for promotion:

A nonselection for promotion may be appealed as an individual-right-of-action (IRA) appeal, based on the provisions of the Whistleblower Protection Act (WPA), Pub. L. No. 101-12, 103 Stat. 16 (1989). See 5 USC 1214(a)(3), 1221(a); Slake [v.] Department of the Treasury, 53 MSPR 207, 210 (1992). Postal Service employees may not file an IRA appeal, however, because they are not covered by the WPA. See Patton v. US Postal Service, 53 MSPR 21, 22-23 (1992).

Claims of nonpromotion prosecuted through the grievance process of a collective bargaining agreement are not reviewable by the Board. The Board only reviews final decisions under a grievance process when there is an otherwise appealable action resolved by the grievance and an allegation of discrimination has been made. See Harper, supra; Pittsenbarger v. Dept. of Air Force, 5 MSPR 462, 463, 5 MSPB 464 (1981); see Harper v. Customs Serv., 38 MSPR 364, 365 (1988) (for the Board to secure jurisdiction of a grievance protesting nonpromotion, three elements are required: a final decision through a negotiated grievance procedure, a prohibited personnel practice alleged under 5 USC 2302(b)(1), and a matter that is otherwise appealable to the Board); (asserting that nonselection was the result of physical disability discrimination). [Refer to Chapter 5 concerning Board review of arbitration awards.]

It is immaterial that the position sought has promotion potential; there is still no appellate jurisdiction over denial of a position unless there are statutory reemployment rights. See Humphries v. VA, 2 MSPR 559, 561, 3 MSPB 80 (1980). Jurisdiction does not lie from removal of the appellant from a list of eligibles qualified for promotion. Maskell v. Panama Canal Comm'n, 19 MSPR 643 (1984) (Table); Salzmann v. Dept. of Army, 15 MSPR 583, 586 (1983) (allegation that a higher graded position was improperly filled; no involvement of OPM). The Board lacked jurisdiction over a claim that the agency failed to advance the appellant through a career ladder promotion in Gershfeld v. MSPB, 232 Fed. Appx. 972, 975 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP):

Section 300.604 places a minimum requirement for time in grade. It does not, however, mandate that an employee be promoted upon meeting this minimum. Further, Mr. Gershfeld has not provided any evidence that the Agency improperly delayed his promotions. Because promotions under section 300.604 are discretionary and Mr. Gershfeld did not show that the Agency improperly followed its own rules, the Board lacks jurisdiction over Mr. Gershfeld's claim for delayed promotion. For these reasons, this court affirms the Board's decision.

See Kolliker v. VA, CH-3443-11-0425-I-1 (NP 12/7/2011) ("The appellant appealed the agency's failure to give her a career ladder promotion and to select her for a position after the agency informed her that her appointment was illegal and it may have to initiate action to terminate her employment. The administrative judge properly found that the appellant failed to show that the agency took an adverse action within the Board's jurisdiction.").
