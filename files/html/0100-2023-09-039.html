a. Appeal To or From Congressional Office

The proper MSPB regional or field office for filing is the one in the area of the appellant's duty station at the time the appealed personnel action was taken. 5 CFR 1201.4(d) (2022). The Board will not consider an appeal to be timely filed if it is lodged with a congressional office, although that practice was permitted previously by the Civil Service Commission. In re Burleson, 1 MSPR 745, 746, 2 MSPB 152 (1980). If a referral from a congressional office is received by the Board, the response from the region should explain that only the employee or his representative may file. See Gray v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1994 NP No. 94-3168) (appeal was untimely when it was belatedly submitted by a congressman on behalf of the appellant; the notice from OPM to the appellant clearly stated where and when the appeal should have been filed); Williams v. OPM, 71 MSPR 597, 606 (1996) (untimely appeal from an OPM reconsideration decision was not excused when the appellant did not follow explicit filing instructions: "The appellant's actions cannot be found diligent merely because he wrote to his legislators or other agencies to resolve his disability retirement claim outside of an appeal to the Board"); Gipson v. VA, 1 MSPR 435, 437, 1 MSPB 420 (1980) ("the field office erroneously accepted as the initial filing of the appeal what in fact was only a Congressional inquiry in behalf of the appellant"). Regarding the practice of considering congressional inquiries as appeals, the Federal Circuit wryly observed in Edgerton v. MSPB, 768 F.2d 1314, 1316 (Fed. Cir. 1985), partially explaining but not wholly excusing the late appeal of an employee not otherwise aware of an appeal right: "Unfortunately, Edgerton followed the practice of so many in taking up his problem with Senators and Congressmen (really their staff aides) who knew no more than Edgerton about the intricacies of Civil Service law and procedure, and were less in a position to find out."

The Board added internal guidance concerning congressional referrals in Chapter 2 of the Judges' Handbook:

9. CONGRESSIONAL INQUIRIES AND REFERRALS.

a. Initial Inquiries. The referral of a constituent's complaint or inquiry by a Member of Congress or other individual is not an appeal unless it is accompanied by an appeal form or other documents sufficient to meet the requirements of 5 C.F.R. § 1201.24. If the Congressional referral does not meet these requirements, the CAJ should respond to the referral in writing, or at the CAJ's discretion, by telephone, explaining that the forwarded documents are not an appeal, that the employee/retiree or his or her designated representative must personally file, and that the appellant or the representative must sign an appeal. If the Congressional referral meets the filing requirements, the response should indicate that, although generally only an appellant or his or her designated representative may file an appeal, because the material forwarded by the Member meets the Board's filing requirements, including correspondence raising an appeal, signed by the individual (or representative), the Board will treat the correspondence as an appeal. A copy of the referral and any written response should be sent to the potential appellant with a blind copy to the Board's Legislative Counsel in OGC and to OCB. Finally, the RO should retain a copy of the referral and response so that the AJ assigned to the appeal is aware of it in deciding any potential timeliness issues.

b. Subsequent Submissions. If a Member of Congress, not designated as a representative of an individual, submits evidence or argument on behalf of a constituent after the Board's processing of an appeal has begun, such a submission is not...routinely entered into the record. It should be treated as an ex parte communication and handled as required by 5 C.F.R. § 1201.101. The AJ should make the communication part of the record, notify the parties in writing of the communication, and give the parties 10 days to file a response. If the agency has no objection to the submission, the AJ may still reject the submission as evidence on the basis of relevance, materiality, or repetitiousness. The CAJ should inform the Member of Congress (or staff member) of the disposition of the submission and advise the Member, for future reference, of the Board's requirements for the designation of representatives and for accepting submissions. A blind copy of any such correspondence with a Member of Congress should be sent to OCB and the Legislative Counsel.

Rodriguez v. USPS, 96 MSPR 189, 190 ¶ 3 (2004), involving not an appeal but a petition for review, noted:

[B]y letter dated May 31, 2003, the appellant contacted his congressman and requested assistance with his case. The congressman's office forwarded the appellant's letter to the Board, which docketed the letter as a petition for review.

Efforts to obtain congressional assistance may factor into allowance of an otherwise late appeal. DiCastro v. OPM, 105 MSPR 47, 49-50 ¶¶ 9-11, 2007 MSPB 56 (2007), excused an appeal 10 days late when the appellant earlier sought congressional assistance and when the pro se appellant was preoccupied with the care of her ill spouse and may have thought that the congressional office would handle the annuity dispute for her:

...The evidence shows that the appellant did not intend to accept OPM's reconsideration decision but, rather, contacted her Congressman prior to the filing deadline and expressed a clear objection to the decision. While OPM's reconsideration decision informed the appellant that she could file an appeal with the Board within 30 days after the date of the decision, because of her husband's serious illness, her extensive responsibilities as his primary caregiver, and the Congressman's prior assistance in responding to OPM's initial decision regarding this matter, the pro se appellant may have understandably failed to realize that her next step lay with the Board and not with her Congressman.

Further, although ordinarily seeking relief from a member of Congress instead of filing a Board of appeal is not a basis for excusing a late filing, e.g., Waldon v. Department of the Army, 63 M.S.P.R. 478, 479, review dismissed, 36 F.3d 1112 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table), this case is not ordinary. Based on her Congressman's assistance during the reconsideration process and the appellant's express request for additional assistance in responding to OPM's reconsideration decision, made before the Board filing deadline had passed, the pro se appellant may have believed that she could focus her attention on caring for her husband and that her Congressman would pursue her case.

Under the unique facts and circumstances of this appeal, we find that he appellant has shown good cause for waiver of the time limit for filing her appeal. See Shiflett v. U.S. Postal Service, 839 F.2d 669, 670-74 (Fed. Cir. 1988).
