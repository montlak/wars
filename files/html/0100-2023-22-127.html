e. Criminal Case

Boese v. Dept. of Air Force, 33 MSPR 410, 414 (1987), followed in Burrell v. Dept. of Navy, 40 MSPR 494 (1989), declined to award fees for time spent in criminal proceedings related to the Board appeal:

...In construing a statute waiving the sovereign immunity of the United States, great care must be taken not to expand liability beyond that which was explicitly consented to by Congress....

The appellant...requested a fee award under the authority of 5 USC 7701(g)(1).... [T]he clarity of waiver required to meet the strict construction standard for sovereign immunity waiver does not here exist....

In support of this legal conclusion, we note that the criminal proceedings did not grow from the Board or agency proceedings. Awarding fees for them would result in a windfall to counsel; he would receive an award for fees he would have had no entitlement to, absent the removal action. Although the Board has awarded fees for work done before other bodies, these situations have all involved related administrative proceedings and are distinguishable from the case before us on that ground alone. Further, in all of these other cases in which the Board has awarded fees for attempts at resolution of the personnel action, the agency brought the charges or created the situation that appellant was entitled to challenge. In the case of criminal proceedings, the action is initiated by the U.S. attorney's office or other authority over which the agency has no control.

The rule against fees for time spent in criminal proceedings was reaffirmed, Richards v. DOJ, 67 MSPR 46, 49-51 (1995):

[W]e agree with the agency that the appellant is not entitled to attorney fees that were incurred as a result of the criminal investigation. As we held in Boese v. Department of the Air Force, 33 MSPR 410, 414 (1987), the Board lacks jurisdiction under 5 USC 7701(g)(1) to award attorney fees for services rendered by counsel in criminal proceedings. See also McWilliams v. Department of the Treasury, 51 MSPR 422, 426-27 (1991); Burrell v. Department of the Navy, 40 MSPR 494, 496 (1989).

In Boese, 33 MSPR at 412, the administrative judge awarded fees to counsel for his criminal representation of the appellant based on what the administrative judge termed the "Nadolney-Blumenson" test. The administrative judge's test required a showing that work done in a criminal proceeding significantly contributed to success before the Board and eliminated the need for work in the Board proceeding. See id. The Board, however, rejected this test.

The Board found that, absent a clear waiver of sovereign immunity, attorney fees are not awardable to a litigant who prevails against the United States. See Boese, 33 MSPR at 413 (citations omitted). With respect to services performed by counsel in a criminal proceeding, the Board found that the clarity of the waiver at 5 USC 7701(g) required to meet the strict construction standard for a sovereign immunity waiver did not exist. Id. at 414; see Burrell, 40 MSPR at 496 (5 USC 7701(g) cannot be construed as a waiver of sovereign immunity to award attorney fees for services rendered in criminal proceedings).

In support of its legal conclusion, the Board distinguished those cases where it had awarded fees for work done before other bodies on the ground that they involved related administrative proceedings. See Boese, 33 MSPR at 414. The Board noted that, in the case of criminal proceedings, the action is initiated by the U.S. attorney's office or other authority over which the agency has no control. Id. Here, although the Marshals Service initially discovered what appeared to be evidence of criminal wrongdoing by the appellant, it forwarded that information to the DOJ-IG, which conducted a criminal investigation over which the Marshals Service had no control. Thus, despite the appellant's contention in this appeal that the work done in the criminal investigation significantly contributed to his success before the Board and eliminated the need for work in the Board proceeding, we find that the Board lacks jurisdiction to award fees for services performed in connection with the criminal investigation into the appellant's misconduct.[*] Although the administrative and criminal investigations may have arisen out of a common core of facts, this does not warrant an award of attorney fees. See Burrell, 40 MSPR at 495-96.

[*] The appellant contended below that attorney fees incurred in conjunction with the DOJ-IG criminal investigation should be permitted because the Marshals Service was negligent in conducting its investigation of the incident and in forwarding a report of investigation of the matter to the DOJ-IG. As previously noted, however, the Board has found that 5 USC 7701(g) cannot be construed as a waiver of sovereign immunity to award attorney fees for services rendered in criminal proceedings. Therefore, even assuming that the attorney fees in the criminal investigation resulted from alleged negligence by the Marshals Service, the appellant is not entitled to an award of such fees.

The administrative judge relied upon the Board's decision in McGovern v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 42 MSPR 399, 407 (1989), to support her finding that the appellant was entitled to fees arising out of the criminal investigation. She referred to that portion of McGovern in which the Board cited to the Supreme Court's implied holding in Webb v. Board of Education of Dyer County, 471 U.S. 234, 105 S. Ct. 1923, 85 L. Ed.2d 233 (1985), that fees may be awarded for a "discrete portion" of work done in prior optional proceedings, outside the litigation in which the fees are requested, to the extent that it was "useful and of a type ordinarily necessary" to the successful outcome of that subsequent litigation. McGovern, 42 MSPR at 407. We find, however, that McGovern does not support the administrative judge's finding.

The Board noted in McGovern that the courts of appeals and the district courts had split in their interpretation of the Supreme Court's decision in Webb, with some courts construing it narrowly to allow fees only for work done in required prior proceedings, and some courts giving it a broader interpretation. See McGovern, 42 MSPR at 407-08. The Board found that it had adopted the broader interpretation of Webb, holding in Nadolney v. Environmental Protection Agency, 30 MSPR 561, 565 (1986), that fees may be awarded for time spent on a separate and optional, but factually related, proceeding if: (1) The claimed portion of work done in that proceeding is reasonable under the standard set forth by the Board in Kling v. Department of Justice, 2 MSPB 620, 2 MSPR 464 (1980); and (2) the work, or some "discrete portion" of it, done in the other proceeding, significantly contributed to the success of the subsequent Board proceeding and eliminated the need for work that otherwise would have been required in connection with that subsequent proceeding. McGovern, 42 MSPR at 408. The Board found that the time spent by McGovern's attorney on marshalling the facts in regard to the appellant's discrimination claims was "useful and of a type ordinarily necessary" to the successful outcome of the subsequent Board proceeding, and was properly compensable under Webb and Nadolney. Id. at 408-09. Thus, the administrative judge's reliance on McGovern in this appeal is misplaced because McGovern relied upon Nadolney, which the Board held does not apply to fees for services performed in connection with criminal proceedings. See Boese, 33 MSPR at 413-14.

Cf. Davis v. Dept. of Army, 49 MSPR 445, 450 (1991) (appellant was not entitled to the reimbursement of counsel fees incurred to defend criminal proceedings when, under SOFA, governing statutes and regulations, payment was discretionary with the agency).
