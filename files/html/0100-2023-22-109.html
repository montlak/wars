B. COMPENSABLE SERVICES

Far less controversy occurs over the nature of compensable services than over underlying fee entitlement. A few decisions indicate the broad nature of services supporting compensation claims.

There is one cardinal rule (so far): to obtain fees it is necessary that the corrective action obtained be causally related to the Board appeal and not be completely the result of action taken by other agencies. Pope v. Dept. of Transp., 21 MSPR 756, 759 (1984); cf. Morley v. DLA, 15 MSPR 486, 487 (1983) (fees denied when the relief was obtained through a district court case over a nonpromotion--an action outside the Board's jurisdiction). Once the MSPB connection is made--assuming an attorney-client relationship and that the interest of justice standard has been met--there need only be a fee petition demonstrating that work was done.

If there is a right to fees, the manner it was incurred is not likely to bar the claim. Fees are reimbursable for time spent consulting with an attorney concerning a Board case even if counsel does not enter an appearance and involve himself in the proceedings. The consultation creates a fiduciary, attorney-client relationship. Naekel v. Dept. of Transp., 32 MSPR 497, 502 (1987); Dungan v. USPS, 25 MSPR 349, 351 (1984); Johnson v. Dept. of Interior, 24 MSPR 209, 211-12 (1984); cf. Mudrich v. USDA, 92 MSPR 413, 417-18 ¶¶ 9, 10 (2002) (consult fee was recovered for one attorney based on a canceled check and a letter from the law firm; a consult fee was denied for another attorney because there was no documentation demonstrating payment for legal services); Trachy v. Defense Communications Agency, 20 MSPR 104, 106 (1984) (disallowing $100 consult fee charged by counsel when there was no indication in record of his qualifications or customary billing rate). The Board requires that the attorney perform the work to claim counsel fees; however, it is not necessary that the attorney actually sign a particular document that he prepared or reviewed for him to claim compensation for work performed. Logan v. DHUD, 23 MSPR 345, 349 (1984), followed, Mudrich v. USDA, 92 MSPR 413, 421-22 ¶ 19 (2002).

Of fees paid to lawyers who provide consultation but whose appearance is not entered in the Board appeal, Boyd v. Dept. of Labor, AT-0752-10-0687-P-1 (NP 9/1/2011), explained:

Based upon our review of the record in the Chapter 75 action, there is not a scintilla of evidence to support that the appellant designated an attorney or any other representative, that an attorney for the appellant filed an entry of appearance before the Board, or that an attorney filed submissions on behalf of the appellant. The undisputed record shows that the appellant proceeded pro se throughout the appeal. Although the appellant submits a spreadsheet referring to "legal consultation" costs that correspond to her credit card charges incurred with the Vaughn Law Firm LLC, Tulley Rinckey PLLC, and Just Answer Experts, this evidence is insufficient to show the existence of an attorney-client relationship. See Mudrich v. Department of Agriculture, 92 M.S.P.R. 413, ¶ 10 (2002) (finding that an appellant was not entitled to recover fees for consulting with an attorney in the absence of documentation such as a cancelled check and a letter acknowledging payment for legal services performed in connection with the case). Based on the foregoing, we DENY the appellant's motion for attorney's fees.

The services described in the sections that follow describe controversies putting entitlement issues to rest. Some services are so obviously compensable that there have been no challenges concerning payment. Examples include preparation of the Board appeal and time spent in the hearing. Appellants' counsel should keep time records of work as it is performed. The preparation of the fee petition later is easier, the result more predictable. If time claimed for events collateral to the appeal is to be compensated, it must be itemized and presented to the AJ with an explanation of how it related to the appeal. The AJ's findings will then have to be detailed and reasoned. The Board said, as it remanded Smit v. Dept. of Treasury, 61 MSPR 612, 621-22 (1994):

...On remand, the administrative judge should make more explicit findings regarding the hours disallowed because they were not spent on furtherance of this appeal, particularly in regard to whether the hours spent on newspaper interviews, Congressional subcommittee hearings, and an Office of Workers Compensation Programs claim were deducted. The administrative judge may find it necessary to allow the appellant's representatives an opportunity to supplement their fee request with more detail.
