broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section B: Interim Relief, Subsection 05: Dismissal of PFR for Failure to Provide Interim Relief, Part d: Effect of Jurisdictional Issue
d. Effect of Jurisdictional Issue

The Board carves out an exception to the general use of a dismissal to punish an agency's failure to provide proper interim relief or evidence of interim relief: if the Board lacks jurisdiction over the appeal, the Board may dismiss the underlying appeal as beyond the Board's jurisdiction. This occurred in O'Clery v. USPS, 67 MSPR 300, 302-03 (1995), involving a claim that a retirement was involuntary. Applying law developed after the initial decision was issued, the Board concluded in O'Clery that the retirement found involuntary by the AJ was not involuntary after all. Even though the agency failed to provide timely evidence of interim relief, the Board dismissed the appeal on the jurisdictional point. Cooley v. USPS, 68 MSPR 353, 358 (1995); see Gussie v. USPS, 69 MSPR 654, 659 ¶ 6 (1996) (same analysis on a petition to reopen).

The Board decided in Lovoy v. DHHS, 94 MSPR 571, 585 ¶ 28 (2003), that it might overlook interim relief disputes when the underlying appeal presented issues of Board jurisdiction. In Lovoy the jurisdictional issue was whether the pay reduction suffered by the appellants was a reduction in grade or pay within the Board's jurisdiction, or whether the agency was correcting the effects of an unlawful appointment, an action that the agency argued was outside the Board's jurisdiction:

The appellants argue that the agency failed to comply with the interim relief order by failing to adjust their pay in accordance with the pay increase or "COLA" the Director of NIH authorized for special experts in January 2002. Although the agency certified that it provided the appellants with interim relief when it filed its petition for review, it did not file a response to the arguments the appellants raised in their motions to dismiss the agency's petition. However, the Board did not order the agency to submit additional evidence of compliance with the interim relief order. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.115(b)(3). In any event, for the reasons set forth below, it is not clear that the Board has jurisdiction over these appeals, and it is not clear that the agency properly exercised its authority with respect to setting the appellants' pay in their positions as special experts. Under these circumstances, even if the agency has failed to fully comply with the administrative judge's interim relief order, the Board exercises its discretion not to dismiss the agency's petition for review on that basis. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.115(b)(4); Byers v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 89 M.S.P.R. 655 ¶ 13 (2001) (the Board may exercise its discretion and not dismiss an agency's petition for review even if the agency is in noncompliance with an interim relief order).

See Cruz v. DOD, NY-0752-20-0029-I-1 (NP 4/18/2023) ("Nevertheless, in light of our dismissal for lack of jurisdiction, we exercise our discretion not to dismiss the agency's petition for review regardless of whether the agency complied with the administrative judge's interim relief order.").
