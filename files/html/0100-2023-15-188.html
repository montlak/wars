h. Threat Against a Supervisor

Removal was sustained for threatening to hit a supervisor with a handheld radio. Perry v. Dept. of Air Force (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 87-3038). A threat against the life of a supervisor, although somewhat guarded, was grounds for removal in Hayden v. USPS, 15 MSPR 296, 298 n.1 (1983) ("[Y]ou stated to Acting Supervisor, E. Collins, 'I could just kill you, I could just kill you, I could just kill you, I could just take you into my hands right now and just kill you. I will come to your house and kill you and take you and dump you into Redondo Beach.' You said this while clinching [sic] your hands and giving supervisor Collins a strange look."). The less-guarded threat to put a bullet through a supervisor's head warranted discharge in Carbone v. USPS, 19 MSPR 460, 462 (1984), and Pulley v. DHHS, 14 MSPR 121, 125 (1982) ("An employee's threat to shoot his supervisor and a coworker in the head is, without question, a serious offense. Such behavior affects the agency's obligation to maintain a safe work place for its employees, thus impinging upon the efficiency of the service."); see Vernon v. USPS, 87 MSPR 392, 394 ¶ 3, 400 ¶ 21 (2000) ("the appellant stated to a coworker that, when he found out who was responsible for trying to suspend him, if he saw that person in the supermarket he would throw a can so hard as to take the person's head off...the appellant stated that he had been a drill sergeant, had been on a rifle range before, and knew how to use a weapon"; removal mitigated to a suspension for misconduct that "strikes at the very heart of an agency's obligation to maintain a safe and productive workplace"; the "agency should not be required to assume the risk that one of its employees will jeopardize the safety of its other employees"); Groeber v. USPS, 84 MSPR 646, 653 ¶ 16 (2000) (removal; "swinging tire chains and brandishing an open knife at a supervisor, and telling a nurse that he would go after his supervisor, her, and the rest of the medical staff constitute serious misconduct") (overruled on other grounds, Thomas v. USPS, 116 MSPR 453, 458-61 ¶¶ 8-13, 2011 MSPB 62 (2011), and Pickett v. USDA, 116 MSPR 439, 440-44 ¶¶ 2-12, 2011 MSPB 58 (2011)); Hutson v. Dept. of Interior, 67 MSPR 432, 447 (1995) (allegations of threats against lives of coworkers or supervisors: "Threatening a supervisor affects the agency's obligation to maintain a safe workplace for its employees and is without question a serious offense. See Battle, 63 M.S.P.R. at 410; Robinson, 30 M.S.P.R. at 679. Moreover, the appellant was specifically warned against such conduct."); Cochran v. INS, 16 MSPR 343, 353 (1983) (removal was mitigated to a 90-day suspension for threatening a supervisor with a knife). Discharge for threats of bodily harm on the person of a supervisor was judicially approved. Hunt v. DHHS, 758 F.2d 608, 609 (Fed. Cir. 1985) ("on two occasions petitioner suggested to a management official investigating the case that he 'might have to hurt' his supervisor").

The Board set aside an initial decision mitigating removal of an employee whose past record was not satisfactory and whose attendance was unreliable. The employee's verbal threat to shoot his supervisor, whether or not a gun was in his hand at the time of the threat, was without question a serious offense. Webb v. USPS, 21 MSPR 493, 494 (1984); see Smith v. USPS, 30 MSPR 107, 107 n.* (1986) (specific threat that the appellant "would get his gun and do what was necessary to shoot the two [supervisors]" was repeated numerous times to numerous individuals and was taken seriously by the agency and the supervisors who were threatened); Romero v. Dept. of Army, 10 MSPR 56, 59 (1982) (sustaining removal for pointing a gun at a supervisor as a threat). A statement over the telephone by an appellant to his supervisor that his "career and his family are going to suffer as a result of his actions" was not deemed a serious threat in Mendez v. Dept. of Air Force, 38 MSPR 659, 663 n.3 (1988). Although the supervisor reported the statement to his superior, the supervisor waited a week before submitting his statement in writing, and the agency took no precautions to ensure the appellant did not carry out the supposed threat. A delay of three months took place between the time of the appellant's statement and the agency action against him. Appellant did not make a "true threat."

Removal for a threat was appropriate, the appellant's conduct being "particularly egregious because it was directed at his third-level supervisor, occurred at the agency's facility in a public area, and was repeated in a short period of time despite a warning to backoff and not do something he might regret," in Chatman v. Dept. of Army, 73 MSPR 582, 584, 589 (1997), commenting that "the appellant approached Cho in the agency's fitness center, placed his face very close to Cho's face, and cursed, berated, and threatened him," and noting:

The U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit and the Board have repeatedly found that removal is a reasonable penalty for threatening co-workers and supervisors with bodily harm, even where there are mitigating factors. Meehan v. U.S. Postal Service, 718 F.2d 1069, 1075 (Fed. Cir. 1983) (removal was reasonable for threatening bodily harm to two managers despite employee's lack of special warning that removal penalty could be imposed); Hutson, 67 M.S.P.R. at 447-48 (removal was a reasonable penalty for insubordination and threatening supervisors and co-workers despite the fact that the employee was undergoing psychological treatment); Battle, 63 M.S.P.R. at 410-11 (removal was reasonable for threatening supervisors during a conversation with a co-worker); Oglesby, 51 M.S.P.R. at 205-06 (removal was a reasonable penalty for threatening and abusive behavior toward co-workers and a supervisor despite the fact that the employee was undergoing psychological treatment); Gardner v. U.S. Postal Service, 44 M.S.P.R. 565, 569-71 (1990) (penalty of removal was reasonable for threatening a supervisor and use of profanity despite the employee's eight years of service), aff'd, 983 F.2d 1087 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table); Facas v. U.S. Postal Service, 35 M.S.P.R. 426, 430-31 (1987) (removal penalty was reasonable for threatening a coworker despite 16 years of discipline-free service); Gunn v. U.S. Postal Service, 34 M.S.P.R. 509, 511-12 (1987) (removal was reasonable for disrespectful and discourteous conduct and threatening a supervisor even though the employee had undergone rehabilitation treatment for alcoholism); Robinson, 30 M.S.P.R. at 679-80 (removal was a reasonable penalty for threatening a supervisor, insubordination, and failure to perform duties); Pulley v. Department of Health & Human Services, 14 M.S.P.R. 121, 125-26 (1982) (penalty of removal was reasonable for threatening to shoot a supervisor and co-worker despite the employee's over seven years of service). We discern no reason to abandon our well-established precedent in this appeal.

The Federal Circuit found no abuse of discretion in removal of an employee for a threat against a supervisor in Meehan v. USPS, 718 F.2d 1069, 1071 (Fed. Cir. 1983) ("'I only want to work here until December and then I'm going to go to Florida anyway. And I'm going to take one of the m...f...s out before I go.'"). An employee was discharged who threatened to kill a supervisor (not present) after the employee learned of an adverse action against him. The file included several past incidents characterized as insubordination, an altercation with another employee, and threats against others in authority. Broden v. Dept. of Army, 2 MSPR 339, 340, 2 MSPB 502 (1980). A threat, reported through another employee, to butcher the postmaster, warranted removal in Facas v. USPS, 35 MSPR 426 (1987), despite long past service and an absence of prior warnings or discipline. Agency management reacted by placing the appellant on emergency suspension, escorting him off the premises, instructing supervisors to call the police if he came to work, filing a police report, and requesting that a police cruiser be in the area when appellant was scheduled for work. A threat, reported through other employees, to a military supervisor that "[i]f I see that pussy-ass lieutenant out in town, I'll kick his ass," was grounds for dismissal in Sims v. DOD, 58 MSPR 131, 134 (1993). The supervisor, on hearing of the threat, was concerned and asked his neighbors to watch his house while he was at work; the statement was not conditional; and others believed the statement to be a threat. A threat by an appellant who strode out of an office in anger saying he had "to take a walk before 'I kill your goddamn ass,'" apparently not taken seriously by the supervisor, was not considered a significant threat. It resulted from mutual antagonisms. Threats of that nature were commonplace at the workplace. Moye v. VA, 10 MSPR 448, 452 (1982).
