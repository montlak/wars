b. Preservation of Issue

The appellant cannot claim denial or obstruction of discovery for the first time in the PFR. The assertion that the agency did not respond to discovery or responded improperly or at a time that did not permit hearing preparation must be made in the proper manner to the AJ before the hearing. Smith v. OPM, 31 MSPR 406, 409-10 (1986); see Johnson v. VA, DA-3330-12-0214-I-1 (NP 11/1/2013) ("Issues relating to discovery must be raised before the administrative judge or they are precluded on review. Smith v. Office of Personnel Management, 31 M.S.P.R. 406, 409-10 (1986)."); Chaney v. USPS, SF-0752-09-0834-I-1 (8/25/2010) ("To the extent the appellant is attempting, by this claim, to raise an issue regarding discovery, his failure to have filed a motion to compel below precludes his raising the issue for the first time on petition for review. Szejner v. Office of Personnel Management, 99 M.S.P.R. 275, ¶ 5 (2005), aff'd, 167 F. App'x 217 (Fed. Cir. 2006)."); cf. Markland v. OPM, 73 MSPR 349, 354-57 (1997), aff'd, Markland v. OPM, 140 F.3d 1031 (Fed. Cir. 1998) (appellant inappropriately abandoned a hearing request based on discovery problems). An appellant cannot complain of failure to obtain discovery when no specific attempts are made to pursue discovery procedures. See White v. SSA, 76 MSPR 447, 463-64 (1997).

To preserve an objection to the agency's discovery responses, or lack of responses, a motion to compel discovery is necessary. See Gardner v. VA, 123 MSPR 647, 657 ¶ 26, 2016 MSPB 36 (2016) ("However, we find that the appellant fully utilized the Board's discovery procedures on appeal and there is no evidence that she filed a motion to compel below. Therefore, she is precluded from raising this issue on review. See Szejner v. Office of Personnel Management, 99 M.S.P.R. 275, ¶ 5 (2005) (finding that an appellant's failure to file a motion to compel below precludes him from raising a discovery dispute for the first time on petition for review), aff'd, 167 Fed. Appx. 217 (Fed. Cir. 2006)."). To preserve for PFR an objection to an AJ's ruling on a motion to compel discovery, there should be a request for reconsideration and an objection to the unfavorable reconsideration decision, Figueroa v. DHS, 119 MSPR 422, 425-26 ¶¶ 4-6, 2013 MSPB 33 (2013) (overruled on other grounds, Singh v. USPS, 2022 MSPB 15 (2022)):

The appellant subsequently filed a motion to compel discovery with respect to the document request. The appellant argued, among other things, that he was entitled to the documentation he sought because the Board found in Lewis v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 113 M.S.P.R. 657 (2010), that comparator employees no longer needed to be in the same work unit, have the same supervisor, or have the same deciding official. The agency filed a response to the motion to compel arguing, in relevant part, that supervisors are not valid comparators to the appellant under Lewis, 113 M.S.P.R. 657, ¶ 6, because "their circumstances are not substantially similar."

The administrative judge granted in part and denied in part the appellant's motion to compel. Specifically, the administrative judge found that the appellant was entitled to receive information regarding discipline administered to employees who were in circumstances substantially similar to those of the appellant. Id. The administrative judge therefore ordered the agency to produce the disciplinary records of Immigration Enforcement Agents, GL-1801-09, who were charged with the same or similar offenses within the same geographic region within the 3-year period preceding the appellant's removal.

The appellant filed a request for reconsideration of the administrative judge's ruling, seeking the production of documents relating to Immigration Enforcement Agents nationwide, as opposed to within the same geographic region. After reviewing the appellant's request for reconsideration, the administrative judge granted the request and ordered the agency to produce the disciplinary records of Immigration Enforcement Agents, GL-1801-09, who were charged with the same or similar misconduct nationwide during the 3-year period preceding the appellant's removal.

The appellant filed a second request for reconsideration of the administrative judge's ruling, seeking the production of documents relating to all Immigration Enforcement Agents and Deportation Officers, including supervisors. During a telephonic prehearing conference, the administrative judge ruled that the appellant was not entitled to discovery with respect to materials relating to supervisory employees because such information did not appear reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. The appellant filed an objection to the administrative judge's ruling, thus preserving it for review by the Board.

If a motion to compel discovery is untimely filed, the Board may decline to fault the AJ's discovery management, Saulter v. SSA, AT-3330-10-0369-I-1 (NP 6/29/2011):

The appellant also complains on review that the administrative judge rendered his initial decision without ruling on the appellant's motion to compel discovery. The Board's regulations require that a motion to compel discovery be filed within 10 days of the date of service of objections. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.73(f)(4). The record reflects that the agency served its discovery response, including its objections, on March 17, 2010. The appellant's motion to compel would therefore be due by Monday, March 29, 2010. The appellant filed his motion to compel discovery on April 5, 2010. Thus, the appellant's motion was late by 7 days. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.73(f)(4). The Board's regulations provide that an administrative judge may deny a motion to compel discovery if a party fails to comply with 5 C.F.R. § 1201.73(f)(4). 5 C.F.R. § 1201.74(a). Because the appellant's motion to compel discovery was untimely filed, the administrative judge's failure to rule on it did not prejudice the appellant's rights and was therefore harmless error. E.g., Ludlum v. Department of Justice, 87 M.S.P.R. 56, ¶ 7 (2000) (administrative judge's failure to rule on motion to compel discovery filed 6 days late was harmless error).

Assuming that discovery is initiated, the need for a motion to compel discovery [discussed earlier in this chapter, "Motion to Compel"] before the date for prehearing submissions was emphasized in Ioannou v. OPM, 56 MSPR 426, 431-32 (1993):

[T]he appellant reiterates his allegation below that OPM has ignored his requests for documents under the Board's discovery procedures and the Freedom of Information Act (FOIA). However, while the appellant complained below about OPM's alleged lack of cooperation with his requests for documents, he never filed a motion to compel discovery despite being informed by the administrative judge of the procedure for doing so. Thus, the appellant's contention regarding discovery lacks merit.... Furthermore, the appellant has not shown any jurisdictional basis that permits the Board to hear disputes involving an agency's failure to grant a FOIA request. See Cortright v. Dept. of Transp., 37 MSPR 565, 570 (1988).

Similarly, in Choroszy v. DHS, PH-315H-16-0458-I-1 (NP 4/27/2022):

In his petition for review, the appellant takes issue with the agency's responses to his discovery requests and claims that he was not given enough time to raise such issues prior to the issuance of the initial decision. The administrative judge granted the appellant's request for discovery on June 12, 2017, and set July 3, 2017, as the closure of discovery date. According to the appellant, he did not receive the agency's response to his discovery requests until July 13, 2017. There is no evidence, however, that he raised the agency's purported delay in responding to discovery with the agency representative, as required by the Board's regulations, and he did not file a motion to compel with the administrative judge. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.73(c), (d)(3). These failures preclude the appellant from raising a discovery dispute on review for the first time. Boston v. Department of the Army, 122 M.S.P.R. 577, ¶ 12 (2015). Even if the appellant had filed a motion to compel below, he has not shown how the information he sought would have changed the result of his appeal. See Russell v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 110 M.S.P.R. 557, ¶ 15 (2009).

If information sought during discovery is not forthcoming, in order to protect himself, the appellant should request a postponement of the hearing until the material is produced. After the record closes, the material may not be offered as new evidence. Forrester v. DHHS, 27 MSPR 450, 455-56 (1985). Even with a request for a postponement, however, the appellant's prior actions must be prudent. He cannot wait until "almost the day of the hearing" to initiate discovery and fail to file a motion to compel to place any discovery problems before the AJ. The Board will then deem the appellant to have failed to exercise due diligence in the pursuit of discovery, meaning that the appellant "is responsible for the absence of evidence to support his claims." Walton v. TVA, 48 MSPR 462, 468 (1991). If the appellant does not receive a copy of the acknowledgment order containing directives governing the initiation of discovery, the appellant will be deemed to have waived his discovery rights if he knows through other means (other pro se appeals by the same appellant) of the time limits for discovery and then waits until prehearing submissions are due to request an extension of time to initiate discovery. See Fisher v. DOD, 59 MSPR 165, 170 (1993). If the appellant believes that the discovery period allowed by the AJ is insufficient to permit follow-up discovery, the appellant should preserve the point for PFR by objection to the AJ. See Vores v. Dept. of Army, 109 MSPR 191, 196 ¶ 14, 2008 MSPB 135 (2008) (also requiring a demonstration of how the error affected the result reached in the appeal). Under DeJohn v. Dept. of Army, 106 MSPR 574, 582 n.2, 2007 MSPB 215 (2007), the Board will not review the AJ's denial of a motion to compel discovery unless the moving party objects to that ruling--overkill by the Board:

The appellant asserts that the AJ erred in finding unpersuasive his statistical evidence concerning the agency's failure to hire veterans after the AJ had denied his motion to compel, which had sought that evidence. The record does not show, however, that the appellant preserved an objection to the AJ's discovery ruling. Thus, the Board does not need to consider the appellant's assertion on review. See, e.g., Langford v. Department of the Treasury, 73 M.S.P.R. 129, 138 (1997).

Despite the rulings concerning the necessity of raising and preserving discovery disputes before the AJ, the Board may sustain an objection concerning discovery in a PFR if the Board finds another reason to remand the case to the AJ for further evidentiary development. See McCray v. DOD, 68 MSPR 186, 196 (1995).
