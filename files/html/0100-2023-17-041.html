b. Employee Who Declines to Move

An employee who refuses to transfer with his function may be removed under adverse action procedures. Although the agency may also apply RIF procedures to the employee, it is not obligated to do so. Stewart v. DOJ, 5 MSPR 321, 323 (1981) ("[a]n agency may properly remove an employee under adverse action procedures when an employee fails to transfer with his or her position."); Brown v. Dept. of Air Force, 4 MSPR 221, 223, 4 MSPB 298 (1980) ("When an employee refuses to transfer with his function, the agency may remove that employee under adverse action procedures. Although the agency may also apply reduction in force procedures to the employee, it is not obligated to do so. See Baldwin v. United States, 175 Ct. Cl. 264, 270 (1966)."). It has since been determined that if the employee fails to go along with a transfer of function, the agency should use a RIF if the transfer occurs during a RIF in the losing competitive area. If no RIF occurs, the agency should use adverse action. See Smith v. Dept. of Commerce, 19 MSPR 589, 590-91 (1984). Kimsey v. Dept. of Interior, 24 MSPR 528, 532 (1984), noted that the (now defunct) FPM stated that a RIF may be used if a RIF accompanies the transfer of function. But if there is no concurrent RIF, adverse action procedures should be used. The prerequisite to the employee's separation, however, is that he be validly identified for transfer of function. If the employee's new functions appear materially different, the agency obligations under 5 CFR 351.303 (2022) are not met and the separation must be canceled for lack of a valid transfer of function because the appellant's position was not correctly identified for transfer. In Brown, supra, 4 MSPR 221, 223, the Board suggested that the appellant's claim that he had been improperly identified in the transfer of function could have been adjudicated in two instances: if he accepted the transfer and then undergone a reduction in grade; or, if he refused the transfer and also refused the lower-graded position that he accepted in lieu of adverse action. Once he voluntarily accepted the lower-graded position that was offered by the agency in lieu of separation, the Board lacked jurisdiction over his appeal because he was not the object of an adverse action.
