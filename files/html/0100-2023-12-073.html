h. Reopening in Lieu of Excusing Untimely or Defective PFR

If a PFR is untimely, or even if no PFR is filed, the Board provides itself the regulatory authority under 5 CFR 1201.118 (2022) to reopen and reconsider an initial decision. If the reopening procedure is followed, 5 CFR 1201.117 (2022) provides the framework for processing the case upon the Board's decision to reopen the appeal.

The Board treats a request to reopen an initial decision that became the final Board decision when neither party petitioned for review as an untimely filed PFR. Williams v. DOD, 83 MSPR 519, 521 ¶ 4 (1999). Setevage v. DOD, 77 MSPR 120, 124 (1997), provides an example of the Board's treatment of a request for reopening of an initial decision that was not challenged through PFR:

When a party files a request to reopen in a case in which, as here, no party has filed a petition for review, the Board processes the filing as a petition for review, and has docketed the request as a petition for review of the June 19, 1995 initial decision. Further, because the appellant filed his petition in this case more than 35 days after the issuance of that initial decision, the petition is untimely. See 5 CFR § 1201.114(d). The Board afforded the appellant an opportunity to file a motion for waiver of the applicable time limits for filing a petition for review in his first appeal, and to establish that there is good cause for the untimely filing of almost nineteen months. In his response to the notice on timeliness, the appellant requests that the Board waive the filing time limit based on his submission of new evidence....

See Shannon v. VA, 110 MSPR 365, 368 ¶ 5, 2009 MSPB 2 (2009) (same as Williams).

Generally the Board does not reopen a case in order to cure the untimeliness of a party's PFR. Evans v. OPM, 85 MSPR 36, 39 ¶ 10 (1999); see McMullen v. Dept. of Treasury, 83 MSPR 448, 450 ¶ 5 (1999) (restating rule); LaMotta v. Dept. of Navy, 80 MSPR 225, 228 ¶ 7 (1998) (stating the conditions for reopening and noting that the Board does not reopen "merely to cure the untimeliness" of a PFR); Wolfe v. Dept. of Army, 77 MSPR 175, 181 n.2 (1998) (Board will not reopen an appeal to cure the untimeliness of a PFR); Kimble v. USPS, 74 MSPR 539, 543 (1997) (the Board will not reopen an appeal to cure the untimeliness of a party's PFR); Sessions v. Dept. of Army, 74 MSPR 343, 347 (1997) ("[W]e generally will not reopen an appeal to cure an untimely filing, absent unusual circumstances."); McFalls v. OPM, 72 MSPR 252, 258 (1996) (same); Brown v. Dept. of Navy, 71 MSPR 479, 483 (1996) ("we generally will not reopen an appeal to cure an untimely filing"); Choco v. OPM, 69 MSPR 638, 641 (1996) ("[I]t is well-settled that the Board will not reopen an appeal merely to cure the untimeliness of a petition for review."); Romero v. OPM, 66 MSPR 109, 112 (1995) (stating rule); Oliveras v. USPS, 64 MSPR 74, 76 (1994) (stating rule); Rivers v. Dept. of Navy, 61 MSPR 385, 387-88 (1994) ("The Board will generally not reopen an appeal to cure an untimely filing.... More than a reasonable time has passed since the initial decision was issued, and there are no 'unusual circumstances' present necessitating that we reopen and reconsider this decision."); Stanley v. USPS, 61 MSPR 225, 228 (1994) ("the Board generally does not reopen a case in order to cure the untimeliness of party's petition for review"); Santos v. OPM, 58 MSPR 408, 409 (1993) (same analysis as Stanley, supra); cf. Harnish v. USPS, CH-0752-14-0303-I-1 (NP 7/14/2015) ("Ordinarily, an appellant's withdrawal of an appeal is an act of finality and, in the absence of unusual circumstances such as misinformation or new and material evidence, the Board will not reinstate an appeal once it has been withdrawn merely because the appellant wishes to proceed before the Board or to cure an untimely petition for review. Potter v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 116 M.S.P.R. 256, ¶ 7 (2011). The appellant states that she was confused at the time of her withdrawal, that she was not provided information concerning the withdrawal, and that she is disabled. However, she has presented no argument or evidence of unusual circumstances concerning the withdrawal of her appeal. We therefore decline to exercise our discretion to reopen the appeal. See Lincoln, 113 M.S.P.R. 486, ¶ 9.").
