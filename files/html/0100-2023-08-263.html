9. Appeal Right With Arbitration Award; Employees of Agencies Other Than Postal Service

An earlier section concerned the use of EEO allegations to place jurisdiction in, and the ability to obtain a hearing from, the Board for appealable actions that would otherwise be relegated to a contract grievance procedure. For cases going the arbitration route, EEO allegations preserve the right of Board appellate review (through a review of the record and ordinarily without a hearing) of cases that are first pursued through the contract grievance procedure through to an arbitration award. 5 USC 7121(d) provides:

...Selection of the negotiated procedure in no manner prejudices the right of an aggrieved employee to request the Merit Systems Protection Board to review the final decision pursuant to section 7702 of this title in the case of any personnel action that could have been appealed to the Board, or, where applicable, to request the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission to review a final decision in any other matter involving a complaint of discrimination of the type prohibited by any law administered by the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission.

The implementing regulation, 5 CFR 1201.155 (2022), provides:

Requests for review of arbitrators' decisions.

(a) Source and applicability.

(1) Under paragraph (d) of 5 U.S.C. 7121, an employee who believes he or she has been subjected to discrimination within the meaning of 5 U.S.C. 2302(b)(1), and who may raise the matter under either a statutory procedure such as 5 U.S.C. 7701 or under a negotiated grievance procedure, must make an election between the two procedures. The election of the negotiated grievance procedure "in no manner prejudices" the employee's right to request Board review of the final decision pursuant to 5 U.S.C. 7702. Subsection (a)(1) of section 7702 provides that, "[n]otwithstanding any other provision of law," when an employee who has been subjected to an action that is appealable to the Board and who alleges that the action was the result of discrimination within the meaning of 5 U.S.C. 2302(b)(1), the Board will decide both the issue of discrimination and the appealable action in accordance with the Board's appellate procedures under section 7701.

(2) This section does not apply to employees of the Postal Service or to other employees excluded from the coverage of the Federal labor management laws at chapter 71 of title 5, United States Code.

(b) When filed. The appellant's request for Board review must be filed within 35 days after the date of issuance of the decision or, if the appellant shows that he or she received the decision more than 5 days after the date of issuance, within 30 days after the date the appellant received the decision.

(c) Scope of Board review. If the negotiated grievance procedure permits allegations of discrimination, the Board will review only those claims of discrimination that were raised in the negotiated grievance procedure. If the negotiated grievance procedure does not permit allegations of discrimination to be raised, the appellant may raise such claims before the Board.

(d) Contents. The appellant must file the request with the Clerk of the Board, Merit Systems Protection Board, 1615 M Street NW., Washington, DC 20419. The request for review must contain:

(1) A statement of the grounds on which review is requested;

(2) References to evidence of record or rulings related to the issues before the Board;

(3) Arguments in support of the stated grounds that refer specifically to relevant documents and that include relevant citations of authority; and

(4) Legible copies of the final grievance or arbitration decision, the agency decision to take the action, and other relevant documents. Those documents may include a transcript or recording of the hearing.

(e) Development of the record. The Board, in its discretion, may develop the record as to a claim of prohibited discrimination by ordering the parties to submit additional evidence or forwarding the request for review to a judge to conduct a hearing.

(f) Closing of the record. The record will close upon expiration of the period for filing the response to the request for review, or to the brief on intervention, if any, or on any other date the Board sets for this purpose. Once the record closes, no additional evidence or argument will be accepted unless the party submitting it shows that the evidence was not readily available before the record closed.

[Refer to Chapter 5, "Review of Arbitration Awards"; refer to Chapter 5, "MSPB Review Procedure" for the use of a hearing by a Board AJ to develop information concerning an allegation of discrimination when the arbitrator's discussion was nonexistent or inadequate.]

The right to secure Board review of an arbitration award requires that the action reviewed be a personnel action otherwise within the Board's appellate jurisdiction. See Sanders v. Dept. of Army, 38 MSPR 74, 77 (1988) (no jurisdiction if discrimination is alleged but the action under challenge is not within the Board's appellate jurisdiction: a complaint that the agency assigned additional duties and failed to amend the position description); Ariza v. Dept. of Educ., 36 MSPR 54, 55 n.2 (1988) (the Board lacked jurisdiction over a grievance of a performance appraisal; other conditions required for Board review were that "[t]he appellant also must allege discrimination as stated in 5 USC 2302(b)(1) in connection with the underlying action, and a final decision must have been issued.").

The Board's authority to review grievance decisions is limited. Martinez v. Dept. of Air Force, 12 MSPR 251, 252 (1982). There must be a final grievance (arbitration) decision (award) involving a claim within the Board's jurisdiction. The Board lacked jurisdiction to consider an arbitrator's grievance decision concerning two negative performance ratings and a proposed removal notice. Perlmutter v. Dept. of Air Force, 41 MSPR 38, 39 (1989); see Morris v. Dept. of Interior, 11 MSPR 126, 128 (1982) (dispute over a premium pay claim was not within the Board's jurisdiction and there was no final arbitration decision). Although the Board retains the authority to consider a prohibited personnel practice under 5 USC 2302(b)(1) (discrimination) on appeal from a final grievance decision, there is nothing for the Board to review on the appeal if no underlying allegation of discrimination exists. Usova v. Dept. of Educ., 20 MSPR 75, 77-78 (1984); see Krajewski v. DHHS, 27 MSPR 673, 675 (1985) ("5 USC 7121(d) allows arbitrators' decisions to be reviewed by the Board in cases in which the employee has been affected by a prohibited personnel practice as set forth in 5 USC 2302(b)(1) where the underlying action could have been appealed to the Board in the absence of the negotiated procedures."); Fach v. Dept. of Army, 10 MSPR 509, 510 (1982) (appeal dismissed for lack of an issue of prohibited discrimination); Ogden Air Logistics Ctr. v. AFGE Local 1592, 6 MSPR 630, 636, 6 MSPB 531 (1981) (even if an arbitration award reviewing a challenge to an agency employment practice were ordinarily reviewable by the Board, the Board lacked jurisdiction to review the award when there was no claim of discrimination cognizable under 5 USC 7702). Stroud v. VA, 2022 MSPB 43, ¶ 25 (2022), recognized but did not decide the question of whether the Board has jurisdiction to review arbitrators' awards involving cases of VA employees under 38 USC 714.

Through 2012, when the regulatory structure changed to require, 5 CFR 1201.155 (2022), that an allegation of discrimination that can be raised under the negotiated grievance procedure first be raised with the arbitrator, the allegation of discrimination leading to Board review of an arbitration award could be raised for the first time with the Board. See Jones v. Dept. of Navy, 898 F.2d 133 (Fed. Cir. 1990), post; Keller v. Dept. of Army, 113 MSPR 557, 559 ¶ 5, 2010 MSPB 81 (2010) (Board could review an issue of prohibited discrimination even if the appellant did not raise the issue before the arbitrator); Hidalgo v. DOJ, 93 MSPR 645, 651 ¶ 8 (2003) ("The appellant did not raise this matter below. An appellant, however, may raise a claim of discrimination for the first time in his request for review of the arbitration decision."); cf. Hunter v. DOJ, 110 MSPR 219, 222 ¶ 5, 2008 MSPB 236 (2008) (reprisal for filing an EEO complaint as an EEO allegation sufficient to sustain MSPB review authority); Crawford-Graham v. VA, 99 MSPR 389, 398 ¶ 24 (2005) (permitting a reprisal claim to be raised for the first time with the Board). [Chapter 5, "Review of Arbitration Awards," discusses the process permitting review at the headquarters level of the Board of an arbitrator's award on an action otherwise within the Board jurisdiction when an allegation of discrimination is made.]

For employees whose cases are reviewed through arbitration but are not subject to MSPB review, further review may be available through the courts, EEOC, or exceptions to FLRA, depending on the nature of the case. If the parties arbitrate a matter that appears to be within the Board's jurisdiction and exceptions to the award are filed with the FLRA, the award is not final until the FLRA decides the exceptions. Until the award becomes final, it is not reviewable by the Board. See Fierro v. Dept. of Treasury, 35 MSPR 618, 620-21 (1987) (exceptions filed with FLRA by agency). [See 37 MSPR 609 (1988), for the continuation of Fierro, discussed below.] [For discussion of the considerable complexities of FLRA jurisdiction over arbitration awards in matters arguably subject to Board jurisdiction, refer to Chapter 9 in Broida's A Guide to Federal Labor Relations Authority Law and Practice (Dewey Publications, Inc. 2022).]

The Board's jurisdiction in cases other than those filed by preference-eligible postal employees is invoked by allegations of discrimination in cases decided by arbitrators involving personnel actions that could be heard by the Board under its normal appellate jurisdiction. The types of discrimination encompassed by this review authority include discrimination prohibited by Title VII of the Civil Rights Act, the Fair Labor Standards Act, the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, the Age Discrimination In Employment Act, and implementing rules, regulations, or policy directives. Under 5 USC 7121(d), that review is initiated by the aggrieved employee. Robinson v. DHHS, 30 MSPR 389, 398 (1986), reconsideration denied, 31 MSPR 479, 481 (1986) [30 MSPR 389 is discussed in Chapter 5], determined that once the Board has jurisdiction to review an award, it may review the award for errors in civil service law as well, although the extent of the review is limited. Review is conducted at the Board Headquarters. The appeal is addressed to the Clerk. 5 CFR 1201.155(d) (2022). [Refer to Chapter 5, "Review of Arbitration Awards," for procedures governing review of arbitration awards and Chapter 12, "Procedures in Mixed Cases," for law governing mixed cases (those appeals that include EEO affirmative defenses).]

If the employee elects the grievance procedure, but it turns out that the grievance procedure does not cover the employee, the employee may lose the ability to secure MSPB review of his EEO or annexed allegations. [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Premature Elections" for discussion concerning ineffective elections of grievance/arbitration.] In Fierro v. Dept. of Treasury, 37 MSPR 609 (1988), an arbitrator accepted and ruled on a grievance challenging a RIF. The arbitrator determined that the contract reserved to the Board exclusive jurisdiction to consider a RIF appeal. The arbitrator directed the agency to permit the grievants to file appeals with the Board without objection to the timeliness of those appeals. The Federal Labor Relations Authority then ruled on exceptions to the award that claimed that the issues resolved by the arbitrator were neither grievable nor arbitrable and that the arbitrator had no authority to direct the agency to waive timeliness objections to direct appeals to the Board. The Board earlier declined to waive the timeliness of the ensuing MSPB appeals. Fierro v. Dept. of Treasury, 35 MSPR 618, 620 (1987). The Board then said of the appellants' later attempts to secure Board review of the arbitrator's award on the EEO or other issues, 37 MSPR at 612-13:

Because the appellants did not have a right to elect the grievance procedure, the arbitrator could not issue a decision on both the merits of their RIF appeal and their allegations of discrimination, i.e., a "mixed" grievance decision, and the Board does not have jurisdiction under 5 USC 7121 to review their discrimination claims. The language of section 7121(d), that "[s]election of the negotiated procedure in no manner prejudices the right of an aggrieved employee to request the Merit Systems Protection Board to review the final decision pursuant to section 7702...," suggests a broader jurisdiction than we have found here. Nevertheless, we construe that sentence to apply only to final "mixed" grievance decisions. Any other construction would be inconsistent with the purpose of section 7121(d)....

Fierro commented on the possibility of an independent Board appeal, 37 MSPR at 613 n.2:

An appellant's proper exercise of his right to elect the grievance procedure in effect tolls, until issuance of a final grievance procedure decision, the 20-day time limit for his exercise of Board review under 5 USC 7702 of his allegation that the appealable action on the merits was tainted by a prohibited personnel practice under 5 USC 2302(b)(1).

When, however, an employee has no right to elect the grievance procedure, he cannot preserve a right of Board review of any discrimination claim that he raises in the grievance procedure. To grant such review would...be inconsistent with the limited scope of the Board's jurisdiction under section 7121 and, in effect, be waiving the Board's time limits for filing an appeal. See Jones v. IRS, 34 MSPR 364, 366-67 (1987) (filing in another forum does not constitute good cause for waiver of the Board's time limit for appeal).

Preference eligible Postal Service employees may pursue both a contract grievance and a Board appeal through a Board hearing. These employees do not have the right to secure Board review of an arbitration award through the appellate process described in 1201.154(d). See Epstein v. USPS, 36 MSPR 662, 664 (1988) (citing Hall v. USPS, 26 MSPR 233, 236 (1985): MSPB does not have jurisdiction to review appeals from USPS arbitration decisions under 5 USC 7121; preference eligible postal employees may avail themselves of both Board appeal and negotiated grievance procedures).
