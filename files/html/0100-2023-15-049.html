c. Unreliable Attendance

Although periods of approved leave will not support an adverse action, and a prior disciplinary record based on approved leave does not warrant an enhanced penalty, in an otherwise appropriate case involving some current AWOL, an agency may use an employee's record of approved leave to show that the employee is of marginal value to the agency because of his or her unreliable attendance. The agency should prove that the excessive absenteeism as a whole has been disruptive to the agency. Dunnock v. USPS, 14 MSPR 382, 385-86 (1983); see New v. VA, 40 MSPR 212, 215 (1989) ("An agency...is not precluded from using a poor leave record to show that an employee is of marginal value to the agency because of her unreliable attendance."); McClarty v. USPS, 15 MSPR 199, 202 n.* (1983) ("an agency may...use an employee's record of approved leave to establish that an employee is of marginal value as the result of his unreliable attendance...an agency may consider an employee's past work record, performance on the job, and overall dependability in determining the appropriateness of the penalty"); Edwards v. USPS, 13 MSPR 471, 474 (1982) ("a prior disciplinary record based on approved leave may not be treated as a disciplinary record warranting an enhanced penalty...[n]onetheless, the...in an otherwise appropriate case, an agency is not precluded from showing that the employee is of marginal value to the agency because of his unreliable attendance, albeit that leave was approved for those absences").

Washington v. Dept. of Army, 813 F.2d 390, 394 (Fed. Cir. 1987), noted that "although an employee's prior use of approved leave may not constitute a ground for disciplinary action or a basis for increasing the penalty, 'this principle need not preclude an agency, in an otherwise appropriate case, from showing that the employee is of marginal value to the agency because of his unreliable attendance.' Cade v. USPS, 8 MSPR 717, 720-21 (1981)." Washington added:

[T]he petitioner was the only permanent secretary in her branch of the agency, and because of her extended absence, higher level employees were required to perform the administrative tasks that the petitioner was assigned to perform.... The petitioner's supervisor testified that he was unable to hire a permanent replacement for the petitioner because he was concerned that the petitioner would return to work and there would be two employees occupying the same position.

The decision relied on in Washington, Cade v. USPS, 8 MSPR 717, 720-21 (1981), explained:

As to appellant's past disciplinary record, the record indicates that all of the absences for which appellant was disciplined were approved by the agency. There is no showing that any of the absences involved were unapproved (AWOL). The Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit has held that a period of approved absence may not be considered along with a period of unapproved absence as a basis for a removal action. See Bond v. Vance, 327 F.2d 901 (D.C. Cir. 1964); but see Morgan v. Manganaro, No. C75-593S (W.D. Wash. 1975) (unpublished slip opinion). This Board has held, in a case arising prior to the Civil Service Act of 1978, that a removal action for unsatisfactory attendance in which all of the absences were approved cannot constitute a valid cause of action. In the Matter of Kibort, Dkt. No. ARB752B901418 (June 29, 1979). In Kibort, the Board reasoned that:

given an agency's authority to deny leave under many circumstances when it must have the services of an employee, when an agency approved an employee's request for leave, it thereby released him/her from the obligation to report for duty. Consequently such an absence was not considered to be a breach of the employer-employee relationship, and an adverse action based on a record of approved leave normally would not be deemed to be for such cause as promoted the efficiency of the service.

The Board, based upon its reasoning in Kibort, now holds that prior disciplinary actions for unsatisfactory attendance which, as here, are based wholly upon approved absences may not be considered as "aggravating factors," Douglas, 5 MSPB at 331, 5 MSPR at 304, for the imposition of an enhanced penalty in an action appealed to this Board. While the Board does not conduct a de novo review of the merits of an employee's past disciplinary actions where certain criteria are met, Howard v. Department of the Army, 6 MSPB 180, 6 MSPR 205 (1981), the fact remains that "the ultimate burden is upon the agency to persuade the Board of the appropriateness of the penalty imposed." Douglas, at 333, 5 MSPR at 306. Accordingly, in deciding whether an agency has met that burden in an appealed action the Board will not consider, as a relevant factor, a prior disciplinary action which shows on its face that it was imposed on an employee for his or her use of approved leave.

Although we have made it clear that a prior disciplinary record based on approved leave may not be treated as a disciplinary record warranting an enhanced penalty, we also point out that this principle need not preclude an agency, in an otherwise appropriate case, from showing that the employee is of marginal value to the agency because of his unreliable attendance. This evidence would be pertinent to an "employee's past work record, including length of service, performance on the job, ability to get along with fellow workers, and dependability...," which is a factor relevant to the appropriateness of a penalty. Id. at 332, 5 MSPR at 305. The underlying facts relating to such a factor, of course, must be proven by the agency. Id. at 324-25, 5 MSPR at 296-97. An agency's reliance upon the employee's "past work record" is thus very different from its reliance on an employee's "past disciplinary record" as a factor relevant to the penalty, because the latter normally requires proof only of the disciplinary action and not of the underlying facts. In this case the agency did not present evidence, other than the disciplinary records themselves, tending to show that appellant was an unreliable employee due to excessive absenteeism.

See Ulrich v. USPS, 20 MSPR 433, 435 (1984) (removal for AWOL; the agency properly considered the employee's marginal value because of past unreliable attendance); LeFlore v. USPS, 11 MSPR 277, 278 (1982) (sustaining removal on the basis of charges of tardiness, although a charge of AWOL could not be sustained because the instances of tardiness were charged to approved leave rather than to AWOL: "The Board noted in Cade, however, that the principle of disallowing an employee's use of approved leave as a basis for an adverse action 'need not preclude an agency, in an otherwise appropriate case, from showing that the employee is of marginal value to the agency because of his unreliable attendance.'").
