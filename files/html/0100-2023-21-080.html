broida mspb Chapter 14: Retirement and Restoration Rights Chapter 14: Retirement and Restoration Rights, Subchapter 01: Retirement, Section D: Disability Retirement, Subsection 19: Earnings Recovery, Part a: Current Rate of Pay
a. Current Rate of Pay

Barbarino v. OPM, 33 MSPR 441, 444-46 (1987), reviewed the measure of "current rate of pay":

The "current rate of pay of the position occupied at the time of retirement" under 5 U.S.C. § 8337(d) means the current rate of pay of the particular grade level and step of the position formerly held by an annuitant immediately before his or her retirement.

In his petition for review, appellant reiterates his argument that, under 5 U.S.C. § 8337(d), he is entitled to a continuation of his disability retirement annuity because his income during the calendar year in question did not exceed eighty percent of the higher salary of the Air Traffic Control Specialist position as it is presently constituted. We disagree.

OPM is charged with implementing the statutes relating to retirement under the Civil Service Retirement System. With regard to 5 U.S.C. § 8337(d), OPM interprets the "current rate of pay of the position occupied at the time of retirement" to mean the current rate of pay of the particular grade level and step of the position formerly held by an annuitant immediately before his or her retirement. We find this interpretation to be eminently reasonable.

The statute plainly states that "the current rate of pay" used to determine whether an annuitant has been restored to his earning capacity is that "of the position occupied immediately before retirement." 5 U.S.C. § 8337(d). The position that appellant occupied immediately before his retirement was that of an Air Traffic Control Specialist, GS-12, Step 2. The current rate of pay for that position (as of December 31, 1985) was $32,673.00. Since appellant's reported income for 1985 was $26,669.00, he exceeded the eighty percent limitation of $26,138.40, and his disability retirement annuity was properly discontinued by OPM pursuant to the explicit directions set forth in 5 U.S.C. § 8337(d).

In determining whether an annuitant's earning capacity has been restored, the Board will not consider the "current rate of pay" of a position that the annuitant might have held had he not retired.

Appellant asks the Board to interpret the "current rate of pay" requirement of a 5 U.S.C. § 8337(d) to mean the rate of pay of the Air Traffic Control Specialist position as it has been reclassified upward since the time of his retirement. However, the fact that appellant's former position was reclassified after he retired, first to a GS-13 in 1968, and then to a GS-14 in 1977, cannot affect the situation at hand, since appellant never occupied either of those reclassified positions.

In Smith v. Office of Personnel Management, 760 F.2d 244, 246 (Fed. Cir. 1985), an appeal from a Board decision sustaining OPM's discontinuance of a disability retirement annuity, the petitioner contended that OPM erred in finding that her earning capacity had been restored. Smith argued that OPM ought to have considered that, had she continued to work, she may have received promotions or salary increases. The court, in determining whether Smith had earned more than eighty percent of the current rate of pay for her former position, held that the Board had properly looked to the current rate of pay for the position occupied immediately before the retirement rather than considering an increased salary that the retiree might have received due to promotions or within-grade step increases had she continued to work.

The Board was faced with a similar factual situation and argument in Foley [v. OPM, 28 MSPR 291, 291 (1985)] . There, the appellant contended that, for the purposes of determining the "current rate of pay" of the annuitant's former position, the Board should consider that, had he not retired but continued to work with a performance rating of satisfactory or better until the statute revising his wage grade system (to include five rather than three steps) became effective, he might have received within-grade increases to the fifth step of his position by operation of statute. The Board rejected the appellant's argument, finding that 5 U.S.C. § 8337(d) did not permit the Board to speculate as to a "current rate of pay" for a position to which an annuitant may have been promoted had he not retired. Id. at 295.

Accordingly, the Board will not presume that, had appellant here not retired in 1965, he would have continued to work and receive satisfactory or better performance ratings until 1968 (when the Air Traffic Control Specialist position was reclassified to the GS-13 grade level) and then until 1977 (when the position was again reclassified upward to the GS-14 grade level).

See Smith v. OPM, 760 F.2d 244, 246 (Fed. Cir. 1985) ("Smith also says that OPM erred in failing to consider that, had she continued work, she may have received promotions or salary increases. The board, however, correctly looked to the current rate of pay for the 'position occupied immediately before retirement.' 5 U.S.C. Sec. 8337(d). Nothing in the statute permits the board to speculate as to a 'current rate of pay' for a position to which an annuitant may have been promoted had she not retired. Cf. Lancellotti v. Office of Personnel Management, 704 F.2d 91, 99-100 (3d Cir. 1983).").

The income limitation is measured by actual income, not a discounted figure based on the employee's part-time position with the government at the time he was granted a disability annuity. See Crockett v. OPM, 783 F.2d 193, 195 (Fed. Cir. 1986) ("Contrary to Mr. Crockett's analysis, the statute requires a calculation based on income from wages or self-employment in the years following retirement, not the rate of pay for that work. 'Rate of pay' enters into the calculations only in determining the current wages of his former position. In substance, Mr. Crockett urges this court to interpret the statute as requiring comparison on a pro rata basis. To do so, however, would require that we add to the statutory language which speaks of 'income' earned 'in each of 2 succeeding calendar years,' a further requirement that the actual income figure be reduced to the portion of a full-time position worked by the annuitant in his or her former job. However, such a reduction is not specified in the statute, nor can it reasonably be implied. OPM properly used actual income figures for 1980 and 1981.").
