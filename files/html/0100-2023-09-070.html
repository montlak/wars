4. Incarceration; Criminal Proceeding

Incarceration may furnish good cause for delay in filing an appeal, depending upon the circumstances. See Nicholson v. VA, CH-0752-15-0452-I-1 (NP 11/9/2015) ("While the appellant was apparently incarcerated at that time, he does not suggest, nor does it otherwise appear, that he so advised the agency. It is his responsibility to ensure the timely forwarding of his own mail. See D'Aquin v. Office of Personnel Management, 65 M.S.P.R. 499, 503 (1994)."); Cordero v. VA, AT-0752-13-0986-I-1 n.7 (NP 3/28/2014) ("The appellant has not alleged that he did not receive the agency's letter of decision regarding his removal, or that he was unable to file his appeal due to his incarceration. See Burton v. Office of Personnel Management, 93 M.S.P.R. 402, ¶ 6 (2003) (appellant seeking to excuse untimely filing of a Board appeal due to incarceration must submit evidence that he did not receive the letter of decision or that he could not file his appeal while in prison); Carbarloc v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 110 M.S.P.R. 695, ¶ 11 (2009) (the appellant's statement on his appeal form that he did not receive the removal decision letter while in jail, coupled with other circumstantial evidence of nonreceipt, was sufficient to overcome the standard presumption of receipt of mail)."); Cabarloc v. VA, 110 MSPR 695, 699-700 ¶¶ 10-11, 2009 MSPB 52 (2009) (excusing delay in filing the appeal: the agency knew of the appellant's incarceration, but did not make an attempt to serve him there, instead serving a family member); Alcantar v. Dept. of Army, 33 MSPR 214, 217 (1987) (appellant believed he still had a job when he was released; his agency gave him misleading instructions when he returned to work; he appealed promptly when he learned he was fired); Belton v. Dept. of Navy, 3 MSPR 479, 480, 4 MSPB 3 (1980) (appellant was entitled to have evidence evaluated relative to the effect of his incarceration and claimed functional illiteracy on his ability to file a timely appeal). But see D'Aquin v. OPM, 65 MSPR 499, 503 (1994) (declining to excuse an untimely appeal when incarceration followed the filing deadline); cf. Defoggi v. DHHS, DC-0752-14-0307-I-3 (NP 6/23/2015) ("Although the appellant claims that he did not receive the administrative judge's order because he was incarcerated at the time, it was sent to the appellant's address of record, and he was responsible for notifying the Board of any change of address as well as for the timely forwarding of his own mail. See Jacks v. Department of the Air Force, 114 M.S.P.R. 355, ¶ 8 (2010); see also 5 C.F.R. § 1201.22(b)(3)."; still reinstating an appeal for lack of any bad faith in appellant's failure to respond to an AJ's procedural order).

The Federal Circuit concluded that incarceration alone was not sufficient to waive a late appeal, Hinga v. MSPB, 415 Fed. Appx. 230, 232-33 (Fed. Cir. 2011 NP):

...The appellant bears the burden of showing that he exercised "diligence or ordinary prudence" in the prosecution of an appeal to justify waiver of the timeliness requirement. In this case, the Board did not find evidence of diligence or prudence by Mr. Hinga. As the administrative judge noted, incarceration alone is insufficient to explain Mr. Hinga's failure to respond to the notices sent to his address of record. See, e.g., McCoy v. U.S. Postal Serv., 112 M.S.P.R. 256, 259 (2009). Mr. Hinga was clearly informed of the factors used by the Board in assessing whether waiver of timeliness is proper, yet he failed to produce evidence indicating that he was unable to file a timely appeal. The administrative judge credited Mr. Hinga's admission that he had received the letter of decision in January 2009 and noted that, even knowing that he had been removed, Mr. Hinga failed to file his appeal for more than nine months. The administrative judge added that even if Mr. Hinga's appeal were deemed to have been filed as of the date in August 2009 when he attempted to file an electronic appeal, that attempt was made more than six months after Mr. Hinga received the notice of removal and 23 days after he was released from confinement, further indicating a lack of diligence in appealing his removal. Under these circumstances, we conclude that the Board did not abuse its discretion in finding that Mr. Hinga failed to show good cause for his belated filing.

Cummings v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1990 NP No. 89-3437), determined that the pendency of criminal proceedings may not necessarily excuse a late appeal:

Cummings argues that his appeal was late because he was fighting a companion criminal indictment in federal court. According to Cummings, he would have abandoned his Fifth Amendment privilege against self-incrimination had he contemporaneously appealed the service's removal decision to the MSPB. This, he asserts, is "good cause" for not filing his appeal on time. We find this argument unpersuasive....

The proper course in Cummings was for the appellant to timely file the appeal and then move for dismissal of the appeal without prejudice to its refiling [a procedure discussed later in this chapter, "Dismissal Without Prejudice to Refile ('DWOP')"] or to move for proceedings on the appeal to be continued pending resolution of the criminal charges [a procedure discussed in Chapter 3, "Pending Criminal Proceeding; Indefinite Suspension"] . See Dunn v. OPM, 60 MSPR 426 (1994) (discussing continuances for those faced with criminal proceedings); Williams v. Dept. of Navy, 4 MSPR 427, 430, 4 MSPB 479 (1980) (dismissal without prejudice was appropriate for an appellant who was to be incarcerated for six months; as to prejudice asserted to the agency, although witnesses who were military officers were subject to transfer on short notice, "the officers here concerned are under the agency's control and can therefore be produced, within minimal difficulty, to testify on its behalf; [i]n the alternative, depositions or affidavits of those officers could easily be obtained and utilized").

In Depierro v. USPS, 54 MSPR 251, 253 (1992), the Board was not persuaded that confusion resulting from concurrent indefinite suspension, removal, and criminal proceedings was good cause for a delayed appeal. But Cole v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 99-3389), excused delay in an appeal from an allegedly involuntary retirement, apparently prompted by the possibility of criminal proceedings, until the actual proceedings were concluded favorably to the appellant:

Mr. Cole states that he delayed filing his Merit Systems claim because he believed that the controversy surrounding his retirement could not be resolved until after the criminal charges had been disposed of, for they arose from the same factual nucleus. Even if this belief were legally incorrect, it was not unreasonable. Further, despite the agency's assertions of prejudice, no showing was made of prejudice to the agency. See Pyles v. Merit Sys. Protection Bd., 45 F.3d 411, 414 (Fed. Cir. 1995) ("[W]aiver of the filing deadline is appropriate absent a showing of substantial prejudice to the agency caused by the delay in filing."). Concerning the prejudice factor, Alonzo explains that:

The presiding official should take into account whether the agency's ability to defend its action has been impaired by the failure of the appellant to appeal in a timely fashion. Such matters as the disposal schedule for pertinent documentation and the availability of employees with knowledge of the action are factors to be considered. Likewise, there may be legitimate management considerations mitigating against undertaking the adjudication of a case which the agency reasonably has considered closed.

Alonzo, 4 MSPB at 264 n.3, 4 MSPR at 184 n.3...

The Federal Circuit affirmed dismissal of an untimely appeal when the Board declined to accept the appellant's explanation that he incorrectly believed he could not appeal until he knew in what manner a U.S. Attorney intended to charge him for an alleged criminal offense. Grijalva v. MSPB, 306 Fed. Appx. 607 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP).
