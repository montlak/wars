5. Jurisdictional Effect of Lapse of Term or Temporary Appointment

5 CFR 752.401(b) (2022) excludes from adverse action procedures:

(11) Termination of appointment on the expiration date specified as a basic condition of employment at the time the appointment was made;

(12) Action that terminates a temporary or term promotion and returns the employee to the position from which temporarily promoted, or to a different position of equivalent grade and pay, if the agency informed the employee that it was to be of limited duration[.]

Under the regulation, the Board lacks adverse action jurisdiction when employment ends by lapse of a term appointment. Kenney v. MSPB, 356 Fed. Appx. 394 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP). Jurisdictional difficulties are not ordinarily encountered when a term appointment simply lapses. The jurisdictional and due process issues arise when the employee is terminated before expiration of the term appointment. The question of whether temporary appointees secure Board appeal rights is complicated, depending in part on whether the appointees are in the competitive or excepted service and whether or not they are preference eligibles. The significance of a proper determination of the employee's status and tenure, for purposes of Board appeal rights, is apparent once it is understood that a termination will be set aside if the term employee is entitled to, but does not receive, notice of charges and an opportunity to respond to those charges before the employee's adverse separation prior to the expiration date of the term appointment. See Cunningham v. VA, 86 MSPR 519, 523 ¶ 7 (2000). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Current Continuous Service."]

Smith v. DHS, DC-0752-13-2302-I-1 (NP 2/25/2014), determined that there was no jurisdiction over the appellant's separation at the conclusion of her term appointment:

The appellant's argument that she was terminated for misconduct, not due to the expiration of her employment term, is immaterial. The Board has held that an agency may consider a term employee's work performance in determining whether to reappoint her without creating Board jurisdiction, provided that her employment does not end before its specified term. See Scott, 113 M.S.P.R.434, ¶ 9; Shelton v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 38 M.S.P.R. 303, 306-08 (1988). There is no basis to treat employee conduct differently than performance. Similarly, the appellant's assertions of reprisal for filing a grievance under the collective bargaining agreement, harassment, and unlawful discrimination do not establish Board jurisdiction. Penna v. U.S. Postal Service, 118 M.S.P.R. 355, ¶ 13 (2012) (a claim of unlawful discrimination is not a basis for a finding of Board jurisdiction); Wren v. Department of the Army, 2 M.S.P.R. 1, 2 (1980), aff'd sub nom. Wren v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 681 F.2d 867, 871-73 (D.C. Cir. 1982) (prohibited personnel practices under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b) are not an independent source of Board jurisdiction).

Finally, regarding the appellant's claim of reprisal for whistleblowing, we note that the appellant did not raise this claim below and thus we need not consider it now. See Banks v. Department of the Air Force, 4 M.S.P.R. 268, 271 (1980) (the Board generally will not consider an argument raised for the first time in a petition for review absent a showing that it is based on new and material evidence not previously available despite the party's due diligence). In any event, although an allegation of reprisal for whistleblowing may constitute an independent cause of action before the Board, the Board has jurisdiction over such an allegation only if the employee has exhausted her administrative remedies before the Office of Special Counsel (OSC)--and there is no evidence here suggesting that the appellant has exhausted her remedies before OSC. 5 U.S.C. § 1214(a)(3); Cassidy v. Department of Justice, 118 M.S.P.R. 74, ¶ 5 (2012).

The termination of a term appointment at its scheduled expiration date is not within the Board's RIF or adverse action appellate jurisdiction. See Webb v. MSPB, 307 Fed. Appx. 454, 455 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP) ("Though the Board has jurisdiction over adverse actions by the agency, 5 C.F.R. § 752.401(b)(11) specifically excludes from the definition of an adverse action a '[t]ermination of appointment on the expiration date specified as a basic condition of employment at the time the appointment was made.'"); MacIntyre v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2001 NP No. 00-3381) ("When the expiration date of an appointment is specified as a basic condition of employment, the employee's separation on the expiration date is not an adverse action appealable to the Board."); Townsend v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 98-3330) (citing Mullins v. DOJ, 57 MSPR 496, 499 (1993) (IRA appeals excepted, "when the expiration date of an appointment is specified as a basic condition of employment, the employee's separation from the service on the expiration date is not an adverse action appealable to the Board"), and Shelton, post; the period of OWCP continuation of pay did not extend the term appointment for restoration rights); Leonard v. Dept. of Army, 78 MSPR 492, 494 (1998) ("When an appointment is subject to a definite expiration date, the termination of the appointment on that date is not an adverse action appealable to the Board."); Davis v. SBA, 74 MSPR 281, 287 (1997) (expiration of temporary excepted service appointments, none exceeding three months at a time, did not constitute an appeal of an adverse action or RIF; however, the separation might be appealable as IRA); Soehngen v. DOJ, 47 MSPR 169, 172 (1991) (no appeal right from expiration of term appointment of Presidential Management Intern); Meade v. FDIC, 41 MSPR 191, 193 (1989) (when the expiration date of an appointment is specified as a basic employment condition, expiration of the appointment is not an appealable adverse action); Hume v. Dept. of Navy, 29 MSPR 221, 222-23 (1985) (FPM Chapter 351-2-9(c) (1981) provided that competitive service employees with temporary appointments with specific time limits, as well as excepted service employees who have not completed one year of current continuous service, were not "competing employees" to whom RIF protections under 5 CFR 351.201(a) apply); Deans v. Dept. of Labor, 15 MSPR 326, 328 (1983) ("neither reduction in force nor adverse action procedures apply to termination of a term appointment at its scheduled expiration date and such actions are not within the Board's jurisdiction"); cf. Cooper v. VA, AT-3443-16-0421-I-1 (NP 9/29/2016) ("On review, the appellant contends that the agency's decision to shorten her term appointment constitutes a performance-based removal appealable under 5 U.S.C. § 4303(e). However, the appellant has not as yet been separated, and the date of her separation is not itself an appealable matter. See Langford v. Department of the Treasury, 73 M.S.P.R. 129, 140 (1997) (timing of the agency's decision to proceed with the appellant's removal when it did was not an action within the Board's jurisdiction). Moreover, the appellant has not articulated any other basis for finding jurisdiction over her appeal."); Endermuhle v. Dept. of Treasury, 89 MSPR 495, 498 ¶ 8 (2001) (no appeal over the termination of the appointment of an "American Family Member" serving an overseas limited appointment that depended upon, and ended with the completion of, a tour of duty of sponsor spouse; no Board jurisdiction over a claim of constructive removal through resignation allegedly forced by discriminatory conditions).

An employee who is not reappointed following the expiration of a term-limited appointment is not entitled to an adverse action appeal. He has not been removed; his appointment has lapsed of its own accord. Berger v. Dept. of Commerce, 3 MSPR 198, 3 MSPB 297 (1980); see Nuri v. MSPB, 695 Fed. Appx. 550, 552 (Fed. Cir. 2017 NP) ("[A] public employee's 'federal constitutional due process claim depends on his having a property right in continued employment' and where 'the public employee is hired for a limited appointment or is at will, then the employee does not have a property interest in continued employment.' Stone v. F.D.I.C., 179 F.3d 1368, 1375 (Fed. Cir. 1999)"); Schafer v. Dept. of Interior, 88 F.3d 981, 985 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (as to an excepted service employee serving under a teaching contract, constitutional due process protection arises only if there is "something beyond the contract, e.g., a statute, rule, or policy [that] secures the person's interest in reemployment or creates a legitimate claim to reemployment"); Surdo v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 96-3248) (relying upon Starling v. DHUD, 757 F.2d 271 (Fed. Cir. 1985): "It is clear that Mr. Surdo had a temporary appointment. Thus his discharge was not an appealable reduction in force action. Nor is the failure to renew a temporary appointment an adverse action."); Rowell v. Dept. of Educ. (Fed. Cir. 1983 NP No. 83-785) (agency's failure to select a term employee for a position after his appointment expires is not among the actions appealable to the MSPB); Hall v. Dept. of Army, 78 MSPR 222, 224 (1998) ("When an appointment is subject to a definite expiration date, the termination of the appointment on that date is not an adverse action appealable to the Board.... Further...the agency's failure to select the appellant for a permanent appointment before the expiration of her term appointment is not directly appealable to the Board."); cf. Reddick v. FDIC, 809 F.3d 1253 (Fed. Cir. 2016) (no adverse action when an agency withdrew an extension of a term appointment before the extension began); Edwards v. Dept. of Air Force, 120 MSPR 307, 312 ¶ 7, 2013 MSPB 84 (2013) ("Whether an employee meets the definition of employee under 5 U.S.C. § 7511 is irrelevant to whether an employee can appeal the expiration of a term position. Scott, 113 M.S.P.R. 434, ¶ 9.") (Edwards overruled as to election of remedies for managers, Requena v. DHS, 2022 MSPB 39 (2022)); cf. Shik v. GSA, NY-3443-16-0034-I-1 (NP 4/18/2016) (continuation of assignment of higher-level duties following cessation of a temporary promotion does not equate to an appealable grade reduction; it constitutes a classification dispute beyond the Board's appellate jurisdiction).

If the appellant's SF-50 establishes a definite ending date for a term appointment, a memo from the agency to the appellant explaining that her appointment will last from one to no more than four years does not extend the appointment beyond the date set in the SF-50. See Murdock-Doughty v. Dept. of Air Force, 74 MSPR 244, 252-53 (1997). It is immaterial that the reason for not renewing the appointment is the agency's dissatisfaction with the appellant's performance. Shelton v. FDIC, 38 MSPR 303, 306 (1988), distinguishing Stevens v. TVA, 687 F.2d 158 (6th Cir. 1982). The Board looks to the totality of circumstances to determine the nature of an appointment and to determine the duration of a temporary appointment. See Prather v. Dept. of Army, DC-0752-14-0327-I-1 (NP 8/15/2014) ("The exact date of an appointment's expiration need not be predetermined in order for the expiration of the appointment to be outside of the Board's adverse action jurisdiction. Endermuhle v. Department of the Treasury, 89 M.S.P.R. 495, ¶ 9 (2001). The Board looks to the totality of the circumstances to determine the nature of an employee's appointment; an employee's SF-50, although the customary document used to memorialize a personnel action, is not controlling. Scott, 113 M.S.P.R. 434, ¶ 8."); Although nonrenewal of a temporary appointment is not an adverse action, it may be a prohibited personnel practice subject to relief through OSC or, if the nonrenewal results because of whistleblowing reprisal, through an IRA appeal. See Suzal v. Director, USIA, 32 F.3d 574 (D.C. Cir. 1994). [Refer to Chapter 13, "Whistleblowing Reprisal."]

Scott v. Dept. of Air Force, 113 MSPR 434, 437-39 ¶¶ 6-10, 2010 MSPB 58 (2010), summarized the law concerning termination of an appellant's employment at the end of a three-year overseas term appointment:

The undisputed evidence shows as follows: Effective April 3, 2006, the appellant was appointed to the position of Historian at the Yokota Air Base in Japan. Prior to his appointment, he had signed a Department of Defense (DOD) Transportation Agreement, Transfer of Civilian Employees Outside CONUS [Continental United States] . The agreement stated, inter alia, that it was an initial agreement; that the appellant was a new appointee; that his "prescribed tour of duty" was 36 months; and that, when he had completed his prescribed tour of duty, he would be eligible for return travel and transportation allowances to his actual residence at the time of his appointment "for purpose of separation from the service." He also signed a document, entitled "Overseas Employment Agreement (Without Return Rights)," in which he acknowledged that his initial period of Foreign Service was limited to the prescribed tour of duty for the area to which he was assigned and that any extension beyond that period was subject to both his and management's approval. He agreed as follows:

When notified that my foreign service will not be extended beyond that initial or subsequent tour of duty, or when advised that my return is required because of the DOD rotation program, I agree to return to the United States. I understand that I will need to register for return placement to Continental United States (CONUS) on the Priority Placement Program (PPP), until I am offered or decline a valid, continuing position. I understand that if I do not receive a valid offer within 90 days of PPP registration, the registration must be amended to include one grade interval below my current grade. Otherwise I may resign or be separated from the Air Force.

He further agreed that he did "not have return rights back to a position in the United States[,] " and that to continue employment in the civil service, he could apply for positions after his tour expired.

On February 23, 2009, Colonel Dan Rogers issued the appellant a Notice of Proposed Separation based on the expiration of his 3-year overseas tour of duty and his ineligibility to register in the DOD PPP due to an overall Level 1 performance rating. On March 17, 2009, Brigadier General Salvatore Angelella upheld the decision to remove the appellant. As previously noted, the appellant's separation was effective April 2, 2009.

We find that the evidence establishes that the appellant received a term appointment. Admittedly, the Standard Form (SF) 50 documenting the appellant's appointment stated that he received a career-conditional appointment in the competitive service, and the Office of Personnel Management's regulations differentiate between such appointments and term appointments, 5 C.F.R. § 315.201(a). An SF-50, however, is not a legally operative document controlling on its face an employee's status and rights. Grigsby v. U.S. Department of Commerce, 729 F.2d 772, 776 (Fed. Cir. 1984). Although the issuance of an executed SF-50 is the customary documentation for a federal personnel action, it does not constitute the personnel action itself. Scott v. Department of the Navy, 8 M.S.P.R. 282, 287 (1981). Rather, the Board looks at the totality of the circumstances in determining the nature of the appointment. See, e.g., Scott, 8 M.S.P.R. at 287 (looking at the totality of the circumstances in determining whether an appointment has been effected). Here, despite any indication in the SF-50, the appellant's specific employment agreements, as described above, showed that he was appointed to a 3-year term and that he was separated at the expiration of that term.

The expiration of a term appointment is not an action appealable to the Board. See, e.g., Williams v. Department of the Army, 109 M.S.P.R. 206, ¶ 2 (2008); Endermuhle v. Department of the Treasury, 89 M.S.P.R. 495, ¶ 9 (2001); Law v. Department of the Navy, 77 M.S.P.R. 474, 476 (1998); 5 C.F.R. § 752.401(b)(11). That the appellant met the definition of "employee" at 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1) and had completed his probationary period is irrelevant to that determination. See, e.g., Endermuhle, 89 M.S.P.R. 495, ¶¶ 6-7. Further, that the agency considered the appellant's performance in deciding not to reappoint him fails to establish jurisdiction. See Shelton v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 38 M.S.P.R. 303, 306 (1988). Moreover, the agency's admitted error in notifying the appellant of a right of Board appeal, does not serve to confer jurisdiction on the Board, see Miles-Townsend v. U.S. Postal Service, 37 M.S.P.R. 405, 407 (1988).

Similarly, the agency's failure to place the appellant in the PPP is not an adverse action appealable to the Board. See, e.g., Hill v. Department of the Air Force, 309 Fed. Appx. 413, 416 (Fed. Cir. 2009); Wilson v. Department of the Navy, 3 M.S.P.R. 295, 298 (1980); cf. Sturdy v. Department of the Army, 440 F.3d 1328, 1335-36 (Fed. Cir. 2006) (the Board has jurisdiction over a reemployment priority rights claim asserted by an individual who registered for a reemployment priority list or an Office of Personnel Management-approved PPP during a reduction-in-force). Absent an appealable action, the Board also lacks jurisdiction to adjudicate the appellant's affirmative defense. See, e.g., Law, 77 M.S.P.R. at 477; Wren v. Department of the Army, 2 M.S.P.R. 1, 2 (1980), aff'd, 681 F.2d 867, 871-73 (D.C. Cir. 1982).

Characterization of expiration of a term appointment, one in a series of teaching appointments, as a performance-based removal did not vest jurisdiction in the Board, Colburn v. Dept. of Army, SF-3443-14-0446-I-1 (NP 6/4/2015):

The appellant next argues that the termination of his appointment was actually a performance-based removal, over which the Board has jurisdiction, and that the agency has "deliberately mischaracterized" the action as a nonrenewal of his appointment in order to avoid its obligations under Title V. The Board has explained, however, that the fact that an agency considers an employee's performance in deciding not to reappoint him fails to establish jurisdiction. Scott, 113 M.S.P.R. 434, ¶ 9 (citing Shelton v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 38 M.S.P.R. 303, 306 (1988)).

Lastly, the appellant argues that the agency is abusing its authority under 5 C.F.R. § 213.3101, which allows agencies to make excepted service appointments under certain circumstances, by "subjecting its employees to an endless cycle of time-limited appointments, while also asserting a blanket right not to renew those employees for any reason whatsoever, including for performance." The appellant did not raise this argument below, however, and has provided no reason why we should consider it for the first time on review. See Banks v. Department of the Air Force, 4 M.S.P.R. 268, 271 (1980) (stating that the Board generally will not consider an argument raised for the first time in a petition for review absent a showing that it is based on new and material evidence not previously available despite the party's due diligence). Moreover, even if the appellant's allegation were true, he has failed to point to any authority that would vest the Board with jurisdiction to consider this claim in the absence of an otherwise appealable action.

Law v. Dept. of Navy, 77 MSPR 474, 476-77 (1998), considered the appeal by a term employee who sought to litigate a restoration claim involving a term appointment:

We find that the administrative judge correctly determined that the Board does not have jurisdiction over the appellant's March 29, 1997 separation under 5 USC Chapter 75. See 5 USC § 7512; Youngs v. Department of the Army, 73 MSPR 551, 560 (1997) (the separation of a term appointee at the expiration of the term specified at the time of appointment is not an appealable adverse action). In addition, the administrative judge correctly found that the Board does not have jurisdiction over the appellant's claim that the agency failed to restore her following her partial recovery from a compensable injury. An employee who was "separated or furloughed from an appointment without time limitation, or from a temporary appointment pending establishment of a register (TAPER) as a result of a compensable injury" may have restoration rights under 5 CFR Part 353. 5 CFR § 353.103(b). The record evidence, however, shows that the appellant was separated from an appointment with a time limitation and there is no evidence that her appointment to that position was a TAPER. Accordingly, the Board does not have jurisdiction over the appellant's restoration claim. See 5 CFR § 1201.3(a)(12).
