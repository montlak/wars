o. Other Entities

The International Boundary and Water Commission is an entity within the coverage of the WPA IRA provisions. Wilcox v. IBWC, 103 MSPR 73, 2006 MSPB 237 (2006).

A Farm Service Agency employee was not a federal employee for purposes of MSPB IRA jurisdiction. Levering v. MSPB, 259 Fed. Appx. 321 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP).

Although the decision was not specific on the point, the appellant in Drake v. AID, 543 F.3d 1377 (Fed. Cir. 2008), may have been a foreign service officer.

Bethel v. Dept. of State, DC-1221-10-0528-W-1 (NP 11/12/2010), indirectly described a State Department employment or contracting relationship that did not qualify for IRA coverage:

The appellant filed an individual right of action (IRA) appeal alleging that the agency terminated him during his probationary period from the position of Protective Security Agent, Foreign Service National (FSN) 6, step 6, at the United States Embassy, Nassau, Bahamas, for making a disclosure protected under the Whistleblower Protection Act (WPA)....

After the appellant responded to her show-cause order, the administrative judge dismissed the appeal, concluding that he was not an "employee" covered by the WPA with a right to file an IRA appeal because he did not meet the definition of "employee" at 5 U.S.C. § 2105(a). She found that the documentation showed that the agency hired the appellant under a Personal Services Agreement (PSA), and, therefore, he was not "appointed in the civil service" as required by 5 U.S.C. § 2105(a)(1). She acknowledged that the appellant cited authorities suggesting that, for many purposes, individuals serving under PSAs are treated like employees, but found that they did not show that the appellant was "appointed in the civil service."

In his petition for review, the appellant cites Ang v. Department of State and Department of Homeland Security, 103 M.S.P.R. 324 (2006), in which the Board found that an FSN-9 Customs Investigator was "appointed in the civil service," and, thus, was an "employee" covered by the WPA. He asserts, in relevant part, that his documentation includes indicia of appointment to a position in the civil service. He further asserts that he was hired by an "agency" and that his position was not considered a part of "the uniformed services."

The appellant has not shown that the administrative judge erred under Ang in finding that he is not an "employee" covered by the WPA because he was not "appointed in the civil service." That he was hired by an "agency" as defined in 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(C) is insufficient to show that he has a right to file an IRA appeal because 5 U.S.C. § 2302 does not grant jurisdiction to the Board. Clark v. Army and Air Force Exchange Service, 57 M.S.P.R. 43, 46 n.1 (1993). Similarly, that his position was not excluded from the "civil service" by being in the "uniformed services," see 5 U.S.C. § 2101(1), does not mean that he was "appointed in the civil service."

Unlike Ang, the appellant's documentation does not include indicia such as a standard form 50 showing that he was appointed to a position in the excepted service or evidence that he took an oath of office. See Ang, 103 M.S.P.R. 324, ¶¶ 13, 17-18. In addition, it does not show that he was paid through the civil service system or subject to social security withholding. See Werley v. Office of Personnel Management, 39 M.S.P.R. 686, 689-90 (1989) (citing Watts v. Office of Personnel Management, 814 F.2d 1576, 1579 (Fed. Cir.), cert. denied, 484 U.S. 913 (1987)). In that regard, the agency's May 5, 2008 letter offering the appellant employment stated that he would be provided a salary "plus benefits as contained in our Local Compensation Plan." Further, the record does not include a standard form 50; rather, it includes a form identified as "JF-62-A" and entitled "Personal Services Agreement Action." Although the form refers to the appellant's "appointment," it lists the "Agreement Number" as "PSA-50"; the "Authority" as "State/Program"; the "Leave Plan" as "Local," as opposed to "U.S. Style"; the "Retirement(s)" as "Local Retirement Plan"; the "Annuitant" as "N/A"; and the "Pay Plan" as "L." It further states that the employer and employee health contribution should be deducted for the "National Insurance Board." Given those differences, we find that Ang does not warrant a finding that the appellant was an "employee" covered under the WPA with a right to file an IRA appeal.

The Office of Special Counsel was not within the IRA jurisdiction of the Board when, although it was essentially charged with mismanagement of statutory whistleblowing disclosure protections, it was not the employer of the appellant and took no personnel action as to the appellant. Carson v. OSC, AT-1221-14-0620-W-1 (NP 3/25/2015), aff'd, Carson v. MSPB, 680 Fed. Appx. 1016, 1017 (Fed. Cir. 2017 NP) ("[W]e find that Carson failed to allege that a cognizable personnel action was taken against him and that, in the absence of such allegations, the Board lacked jurisdiction to review Carson's claims.").

Employees of the Office of Administration, Executive of the President, are covered employees for purposes of an IRA appeal. Maloney v. Executive Office of the President, Office of Admin., 2022 MSPB 26 (2022) (extensive discussion of what constitutes an agency, an Executive agency, and an establishment, and an independent establishment for purposes of IRA jurisdiction and 5 USC 2302, with particular reference to the Office of Administration of the Executive Office of the President (with an extensive discussion of the history of the origin and development of the EOP and OA).

Commissioned officers of the Public Health Service do not have Board adverse action or Individual Right of Action appeal rights. Gjovik v. DHHS, DE-1221-11-0136-W-1 (NP 9/1/2011) ("Due to his status as a commissioned officer of the Commissioned Corps of the Public Health Service (PHS), the appellant was a member of a uniformed service and, as such, did not qualify as an employee under 5 U.S.C. § 2105(a) as he was not 'appointed in the civil service.' 5 U.S.C. §§ 2101, 2105(a)(1).").

The President, as an office-holder, is not a covered "agency." Carson v. Dept. of Energy, AT-1221-15-0073-W-1 n.4 (NP 7/20/2015).
