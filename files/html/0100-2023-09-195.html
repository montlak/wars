C. INTERVENTION

According to 5 CFR 1201.34(a) (2022), intervenors are:

...organizations or persons who want to participate in a proceeding because they believe the proceeding, or its outcome, may affect their rights or duties. Intervenors as a "matter of right" are those parties who have a statutory right to participate. "Permissive" intervenors are those parties who may be permitted to participate if the proceeding will affect them directly and if intervention is otherwise appropriate under law. A request to intervene may be made by motion filed with the judge.

5 CFR 1201.41(b)(13) (2022) provides that AJs will:

Require that all persons who can be identified from the record as being clearly and directly affected by a pending retirement-related case be notified of the appeal and of their right to request intervention so that their interests can be considered in the adjudication.

The Board's internal guidance on intervention is in Chapter 3 of the Judges' Handbook:

5. INTERVENTION. See generally 5 C.F.R. § 1201.34.

a. Intervenors as Matter of Right. The Director of OPM and the OSC may intervene as a matter of right in a proceeding before the Board, but the OSC may not intervene in an action brought by an individual under 5 U.S.C. § 1221 or in an appeal brought by an individual under 5 U.S.C. § 7701 and § 7702 without the consent of the individual.

(1) Before Intervention. If a representative of OPM or the OSC asks to review a file to determine whether to intervene, the request must be granted, subject to the caveat as to OSC in section 5.a. The CAJ or AJ should invite the representative to visit the RO to conduct the review. The fact of the review will be documented (by a memorandum placed in the file) as required by the Privacy Act. A request by OPM or OSC for a copy of the appeal file, or a portion thereof, to decide whether to intervene should be granted unless compliance would constitute an undue hardship on the resources of the RO. That may not generally be a problem, though, because most files are available in electronic form, regardless of whether they are designated as Electronic Case Files (ECFs).

(2) After Intervention. When OPM or OSC has intervened in an appeal, it may have access to the file. Upon request, copies of documents in the file are to be provided to OPM and/or the OSC. Also after intervention, the intervenor must be added to the certificate of service.

b. Permissive Intervenors. The AJ is delegated the authority to rule on motions for permissive intervention. The AJ must invite any person directly affected by the outcome of the proceeding to intervene, especially in retirement cases involving competing beneficiaries. Any employee alleged to have committed a prohibited personnel practice may, upon request, be granted status as an intervenor. Once a motion for intervention has been granted, the AJ must provide copies of the specific documents requested and/or all of that part of the appeal file that concerns the issue(s) affecting the intervenor. Because permissive intervenors may only participate on the issues affecting them, it may not be necessary or appropriate to provide a copy of the entire file, depending on the circumstances.

c. Amicus curiae. Any person or organization, including those who do not qualify as intervenors, may be granted permission to file a brief as an amicus curiae, in the discretion of the AJ. See also chapter 10, section 7, infra. E-filing may not be used to file a request to participate as an amicus curiae or file a brief as amicus curiae. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.14(c)(6).

The intervention rights discussed in this section concern proceedings before regional offices. Intervention at the PFR level, if intervenor status is not been earlier secured, is addressed through 5 CFR 1201.114(i)(3) (2022). [Refer to Chapter 5, "Who May Obtain Review" and "Others; Intervenors."]

If an individual seeks to intervene in an adverse action proceeding, the intervention motion should be identified with the same case number as the underlying appeal. The intervenor should establish in his motion how he is affected by the outcome of the appeal. It is not sufficient that the putative intervenor filed complaints with the Office of Special Counsel or that he filed unfair labor practice complaints or is considering other litigation against the appellant. At issue in an intervention motion is the effect of the outcome of the case on the intervenor; mere interest is not a sufficient basis for intervention. Crumbaker v. Dept. of Labor, 7 MSPR 84, 91-92, 7 MSPB 15 (1981). To justify intervention, the intervenor must allege or show that she would be directly affected by the outcome of the Board's adjudication of the appeal or some issue in the appeal. Dunlap v. USPS, 51 MSPR 432, 433 (1991) (union did not allege or show that it would be directly affected by the outcome of Board adjudication). The Board may announce that a person achieves the equivalent of intervenor status by informal participation in Board proceedings, without the blessing of a formal order permitting intervention. Alexander v. OPM, 34 MSPR 697, 698 n.2 (1987). The Board may rule on an intervenor's interest if that interest is fully presented by a party before the Board. See Gensburg v. VA, 85 MSPR 198, 209 ¶¶ 17-18 (2000) (denying a nonattorney representative intervenor status in a fee proceeding, but based on briefing by appellant's counsel, the Board ruled on the representative's right to counsel fees).

Intervention will most often occur in appeals involving distribution of or entitlement to annuity proceeds--most commonly when there is a claim to a former spouse survivor annuity (and disputes as to entitlement between or among past spouses) and claims for distribution of lump sum death benefits (and claims by competing family members). One person starts the appeal; other individuals are added as potential claimants learn of the appeal or when interests of third parties become known to the AJ entrusted with adjudication of the appeal. [Refer to Chapter 14 for annuity interests.]

An AJ must at least consider and rule on a motion for permissive intervention; it is improper to receive and then fail to rule on the motion. McGowan v. Dept. of Air Force, 28 MSPR 314, 319 (1985). Although there is no regulatory right to reply to an intervenor's filing, the Board routinely provides parties the opportunity to do so. See Warren v. Dept. of Transp., 19 MSPR 560, 562 n.2 (1984). The intervention motion must be timely. In a proceeding brought by OSC on behalf of an employee, the Board would not permit the employee's last-minute motion for intervention after discovery concluded. See Acting Special Counsel v. Dept. of State, 6 MSPR 398, 399, 6 MSPB 338 (1981).

In an appeal involving competing claimants to the annuity rights of a deceased employee, the Board stated that in considering a motion for intervention it reviews all circumstances surrounding the presentation of that motion, including, but not limited to, the timeliness of the motion, its purpose, the status of the person seeking intervention, the contents of the motion as to the facts presented for resolution and, most importantly, the rights of all parties to the case and the effect upon the parties of the acceptance of the motion to intervene. Jones v. OPM, 31 MSPR 622, 624 (1986) (relying on Adakai v. Dept. of Interior, 20 MSPR 196, 199-200 (1984)), followed, Freeman v. OPM, 44 MSPR 317, 320 (1990) (the most important factor the Board considers in reviewing an intervention motion is the right of all parties to the case and the effect that intervention would have on those parties). In annuity claims, an AJ must invite to intervene any person or organization directly affected by an appeal. Holser v. OPM, 72 MSPR 247, 251 (1996) (no harm involving processing of annuity and forced retirement claims: "we find that the appellant has not shown that the administrative judge erred by not combining his appeal of OPM's denial of his application for disability retirement with his involuntary resignation claim, by not inviting the Army to intervene in the proceedings, or by choosing, instead, to advise the appellant to file a separate appeal of his involuntary resignation if he desired to appeal that action"). The AJ should invite intervention when a nonparty's interest in an annuity may be adversely affected by the outcome of a Board appeal. See Baugh v. OPM, 49 MSPR 58, 63 (1991) (directing the AJ to provide an opportunity to intervene to potential annuitant beneficiaries); Freeman v. OPM, 44 MSPR 317, 320 (1990) (remand ordering that attempts be made to provide a possible beneficiary the opportunity to intervene); Wiggins v. OPM, 43 MSPR 579, 582 (1990) (survivor benefit payable on a qualifying court order requires the corresponding reduction of the annuity of the retired employee; the current annuitant should have been informed by the AJ of the right to intervene). [Refer to Chapter 14, "Amendment to Court Order Affecting Former Spouse Annuity; Construction of Annuity Interest Following Ineffective Amendment."]
