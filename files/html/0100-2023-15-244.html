4. Records of Time, Attendance, Work, and Pay; Negligence

False statements concerning work now require an element of material gain. The proof in falsification cases changed as a result of Boo v. DHS, 122 MSPR 100, 107-109 ¶¶ 11-12, 2014 MSPB 86 (2014):

Although the Board previously has stated that it is unaware of any case holding that financial gain is required to prove a falsification charge, see Seas v. U.S. Postal Service, 73 M.S.P.R. 422, 427 (1997);[2] see also Schoeffler v. Department of Agriculture, 47 M.S.P.R. 80, 84, vacated in part, 50 M.S.P.R. 143, enforcement dismissed, 51 M.S.P.R. 20 (1991), in Leatherbury, the Federal Circuit subsequently explained that "the intent element [of falsification]...requires two distinct showings: (a) that the employee intended to deceive or mislead the agency; and (b) that he intended to defraud the agency for his own private material gain." Leatherbury, 524 F.3d at 1300 (citing Bradley v. Veterans Administration, 900 F.2d 233, 237 (Fed. Cir. 1990)). Notwithstanding the Board's suggestions in Seas and Schoeffler, the vast majority of the Board's cases addressing falsification--including some of its earliest case law discussing the contours of this charge--either expressly or implicitly considered whether an employee's misrepresentation was made for personal gain. See, e.g., Lacapurcia v. Department of the Army, 27 M.S.P.R. 514, 519 (1985) (finding that the agency failed to demonstrate that the employee intentionally falsified time records for personal gain); Allen v. U.S. Postal Service, 2 M.S.P.R. 420, 426 (1980) (finding that there was not a scintilla of evidence to suggest that the appellant completed his timecard with the intent to obtain 10 or 15 minutes of undeserved pay).

[2] Seas was last cited by the Board for this specific proposition in Gebhardt v. Department of the Air Force, 99 M.S.P.R. 49, ¶ 8 (2005), aff'd, 180 Fed. Appx. 951 (Fed. Cir. 2006). Each of these decisions predates the Federal Circuit's decision in Leatherbury, which was issued in 2008. Although the Board also cited Seas in Prather v. Department of Justice, 117 M.S.P.R. 137, ¶¶ 17-21 (2011), and Crump v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 114 M.S.P.R. 224, ¶¶ 6-9 (2010), in both cases there was no dispute that the appellants had personally benefited from their misrepresentations concerning, inter alia, outside employment activities and educational qualifications.

In light of the Federal Circuit's articulation of the elements of falsification in Leatherbury, and in keeping with the Board's historical approach to the elements of falsification, we MODIFY Seas and Schoeffler to the extent that they suggest that an agency does not have to establish that an employee personally benefited or gained from his misrepresentation when assessing the elements of a charge of falsification, misrepresentation, or lying. See Leatherbury, 524 F.3d at 1300. Accordingly, we find that in order to establish a charge of falsification, misrepresentation, or lying, the agency must establish by a preponderance of the evidence that, inter alia, the employee "intended to defraud the agency for his own private material gain."[3] See id.; see also Harrison v. Department of Agriculture, 411 Fed. Appx. 312, 315 (Fed. Cir. 2010) (citing and relying on Leatherbury) ("To prevail on [a charge of falsification], the agency was required to prove that the statement was false, that it was material, and that the employee acted with the intent to deceive the agency for her private gain.").

[3] We do not believe that the Federal Circuit intended to develop a unique set of elements for a charge of falsification based only on an allegation of fraud, and we find that the test set forth in Leatherbury applies to instances of falsification, misrepresentation, or lying involving an employee's attempt to defraud, deceive, or mislead an agency for his own private material gain. See Leatherbury, 524 F.3d at 1300. We note, moreover, that the Board has historically used the terms defraud, deceive, and mislead interchangeably when discussing a charge of falsification, misrepresentation, or lying, and there is nothing in Leatherbury to suggest that the court's articulation of the elements of falsification was limited to instances of fraud. Compare Eilertson v. Department of the Navy, 23 M.S.P.R. 152, 156 (1984) (using deceive or defraud in explaining standard), aff'd, 770 F.2d 179 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (Table), with Allen, 2 M.S.P.R. at 425 (only reciting defraud as part of standard).

Boo was applied to work reports, O'Lague v. VA, 123 MSPR 340, 343-45 ¶¶ 6, 10, 2016 MSPB 20 (2016):

To sustain a falsification charge, the agency must prove by preponderant evidence that the employee knowingly supplied incorrect information and that he did so with the intention of defrauding the agency. Naekel v. Department of Transportation, 782 F.2d 975, 977 (Fed. Cir. 1986). The intent element, in turn, requires two distinct showings: (a) that the employee intended to deceive or mislead the agency; and (b) that he intended to defraud the agency for his own private material gain. Leatherbury v. Department of the Army, 524 F.3d 1293, 1300 (Fed. Cir. 2008); Boo v. Department of Homeland Security, 122 M.S.P.R. 100, ¶¶ 11-12 (2014). The intent to defraud or mislead the agency may be established by circumstantial evidence and also may be inferred when the misrepresentation is made with a reckless disregard for the truth or with conscious purpose to avoid learning the truth. Boo, 122 M.S.P.R. 100, ¶ 10. Whether intent has been proven must be resolved by considering the totality of the circumstances, including the appellant's plausible explanation, if any. Id.

Removal is an appropriate penalty if an employee substantially misstates the amount of work that he has done. If a lesser penalty were imposed, the agency would mistrust future work reports and be required to subject the appellant to oversight not required of other employees. Southers v. VA, 36 MSPR 213, 215-16 (1988); see Davis v. USPS, 257 Fed. Appx. 320 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP) (demotion of supervisor for improperly altering subordinates' clock ring entries); Grover v. Dept. of Treasury (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 99-3214) (demotion for telling coworkers, if asked, to make a false or misleading statement concerning a work-related incident: "Encouraging coworkers to make a false or misleading statement seriously undermined the agency's trust and confidence in Grover's integrity and the confidence his future crew members would have in his command decisions"); Good v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1997 NP No. 96-3210) (demotion from a management position when an employee falsified productivity rate reports); Wood v. USPS, SF-0752-12-0787-I-1 (NP 12/31/2013) ("appellant intentionally and repeatedly manipulated the data entries in order to meet his mail processing goals and he never received authorization to leave work during the times in question"); Brown v. DLA, 65 MSPR 436, 445 (1994) (by creating a bogus work certification that procedures were followed, the appellant had the requisite intent to mislead the agency; by falsely certifying that customary procedures were followed, the appellant indicated she could manage a workload that apparently was overwhelming the system; whether the appellant intended to gain personally from the falsification is immaterial to finding intent), aff'd, Brown v. DOD (Fed. Dept. of Air Force, 25 MSPR 411, 418-19 (1984) (mitigating removal to a demotion to a nonsupervisory position for an assistant commissary manager who entered into an agreement with a vendor to minimize the reporting responsibilities caused by food spoilage); Paclibare v. VA, 13 MSPR 450, 455 (1982) (removal of a social worker on charges of false reports of work performed); Rhodes v. Dept. of Labor, 8 MSPR 257, 259, 7 MSPB 719 (1981) (1981) (removal was sustained for falsification of mine inspection and other reports); Melillo v. USPS, 4 MSPR 483, 5 MSPB 20 (1981) (removal of a postmaster for submitting false production reports to increase the post office pay level); Greene v. DHHS, 3 MSPR 379, 380-81, 3 MSPB 459 (1980) (suspension for overstating the amount of work completed); Croken v. USPS, 3 MSPR 128, 134-35 (1980) (removal of a customer service manager for the falsification of the amount of stamps destroyed at the post office in an effort to hide shortages in the clerks' accounts).

Before Boo and O'Lague, supra, charges of fraud or intentional misstatement were not limited to claims resulting in monetary gain for the appellant. Under O'Lague, supra, the definition of material gain was broad. Removal was appropriate for a supervisor with an excellent work record who deliberately omitted information from a report that was to be submitted to Congress and failed to report a significant event concerning a program under his control. Yiakis v. GSA, 25 MSPR 661, 667 (1985) (removal for deliberate omission of important information from an official report intended for congressional use and for failure to report a significant even affecting a regional leasing program: "appellant's failure to report the error promptly to his superiors, and his actions regarding the deletion of information from the prospectus, were not based entirely on the "benign purpose" to which the presiding official attributed them. Instead, we find that they were motivated largely by his desire to conceal the error, and the extent of harm caused by it, from his superiors."); see Blake v. DOJ, 81 MSPR 394, 418-19 ¶ 47 (1999) (mitigation was appropriate when a manager should have presented to a congressional delegation an undistorted view of agency operations; there was no active, malevolent design to mislead Congress); Stein v. USPS, 57 MSPR 434, 438 (1993) (adverse action based on the appellant allegedly "manipulating production reports 'to falsely increase productivity'"); Hernandez v. Dept. of Educ., 42 MSPR 61, 69 (1989) (no showing that an investigator provided incorrect information on investigative reports with an intent to deceive the agency or government prosecutor; no showing that the appellant acted with reckless disregard for the truth).

Removal was sustained of a mine inspector who falsified reports of mine inspections. Despite job tensions and family problems, the misconduct went to the heart of the duty appellant owed to perform his job. The actions eroded the trust lodged in the appellant to conduct proper inspections of mines. Rodgers v. Dept. of Labor, 8 MSPR 257, 259, 7 MSPB 719 (1981). The same result followed for a meat-cutting foreman who, for reasons having nothing to do with personal gain, falsified inventory records involving more than $19,000. The Board considered that because it was incumbent on the appellant to report inventory losses, appellant's special position of trust as a supervisor served as an aggravating factor requiring removal rather than the mitigated demotion ordered by the AJ on the basis of a long and otherwise unblemished service record. Pitts v. Dept. of Air Force, 29 MSPR 108, 110 (1985); see Tidwell v. Dept. of Air Force, 29 MSPR 111, 114 (1985) ("The record reflects that the agency reassigned the appellant to the Maxwell Air Force Base Commissary in order to find out why inventory problems were occurring. However, rather than reporting the irregularities when they surfaced, the appellant attempted to cover up the irregularities by falsifying the meat inventory.").

Alteration of a subordinate's performance appraisal with the intent to harm the employee's career was cause for removal in Chambers v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 2001 NP No. 00-3265); see Cross v. Dept. of Army, 89 MSPR 62 (2001) (removal for charges relating to material alterations of subordinates' performance ratings); cf. Clarke v. DHUD, 49 MSPR 421 (1991) (Table) (employee was removed for submitting a subordinate's performance rating to a new supervisor and obtaining approval of the rating at a level higher than authorized by the previous supervisor). Another removal was sustained of an employee who made false statements concerning the employment status of a coworker with the apparent effect of enhancing the coworker's recovery of lost wages for an off-duty auto accident. The appellant occupied a position of trust with the agency. Removal was appropriate because of the agency's loss of trust in the employee after the incident. Allison v. TVA, 32 MSPR 251, 253-54 (1987); see Glover v. USPS, 23 MSPR 306, 307 (1984) (noting adverse action taken when supervisor improperly allowed clerks to be compensated for hours they did not work). Removal was sustained of lead police officers who either qualified or caused another officer to qualify in firearms tests for other officers who should themselves have taken the tests. The appellants were also charged with having failed to respond in candor to agency inquiries on the matter. Mitigation was inappropriate because, among other reasons, "by permitting unqualified personnel to carry weapons, the appellants compromised the very purpose of their position: the preservation of peace at Ft. Benning." Jackson v. Dept. of Army, 99 MSPR 604, 607 ¶ 6 (2005).

Proof that an employee intentionally made and asserted as true a statement on timecards known to be false is sufficient to support a charge of falsification of government records. There is no need for further proof of intent to deceive or defraud because it is implicit. See Washington v. USDA, 22 MSPR 374, 376 (1984) (nexus between the falsification of time and attendance records, resulting in personal gain, and the efficiency of service); Minyard v. DHHS, 21 MSPR 229, 233 (1984) (demotion of a branch manager for charging as official duty on time records absences for the pursuit of golfing); Schwender v. Dept. of Labor, 19 MSPR 522, 525 (1984) (office supervisor was removed for falsifying a timecard, instructing a subordinate to prepare a false timecard, and making false statements to a superior regarding the timecard; weighing against mitigation were a past disciplinary record and the appellant's position of trust as an area office director).

A charge of making a false statement may be lodged against a supervisor who, seeking to reward efficient employees, incorrectly credited more hours than they worked. See Whelan v. USPS, 103 MSPR 474, 2006 MSPB 288 (2006). Removal of a law enforcement officer was sustained for entering inaccurate information into an official report and instructing a subordinate to enter inaccurate information into an official report. Guajardo v. DHS, 337 Fed. Appx. 892, 897 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP). Removal was warranted after an employee falsified timecards for three years at a cost to the agency of about $2,300. The offense was not mitigated by the employee's long and otherwise unblemished service. The appellant, a secretary, held a position of "trust and confidence" in an intelligence unit and handled classified information. Watson v. Dept. of Air Force, 34 MSPR 656, 663 (1987). The Board considered the seriousness of falsification of time and attendance records to be great when the employee could have used annual leave to accomplish his personal business. Affsa v. USDA, 7 MSPR 446, 454, 7 MSPB 314 (1981).

Not every misstatement on a timecard or record is a false or fraudulent statement. The Board set aside the removal of some employees who put certain incorrect entries on their time records because their supervisor wanted their time records to reflect line watch duties regardless of whether the duties were actually performed, i.e., the incorrect entries appeared as a matter of administrative convenience condoned or directed by the supervisor, not as a means of defrauding the government. Gray v. DOJ, 30 MSPR 635, 639 (1986); see Wusstig v. Dept. of Navy, 49 MSPR 335, 338-39 (1991) (no culpability in the timekeeping practices used by supervisors for subordinates that followed longstanding shop practices); Coulter v. Dept. of Navy, 12 MSPR 431, 435 (1982) (setting aside an adverse action: "[A]lthough appellants did falsify government documents, apparently for reasons of administrative convenience, their actions have not been shown to have been motivated by a desire to receive more pay than they were due, as alleged by the agency."). But see Miller v. Dept. of Navy, 11 MSPR 518, 521, 10 MSPB 445 (1982) (demotion of a supervisor for ignoring the repeated, blatant leave abuse of subordinates). A charge of falsification of time and attendance reports was not made out when a certain portion of the employee's workday was spent commuting. Although the employee may have been AWOL, his supervisor was aware of how he was using his time. There was no intent to defraud the agency. Boscoe v. USDA, 54 MSPR 315, 325 (1992) (agency must prove the employee knowingly supplied incorrect information with the intention of defrauding the agency). Some minor discrepancies in recording credit hours did not warrant removal and the penalty was mitigated to a suspension in Raco v. SSA, 117 MSPR 1, 4-5 ¶ 7, 2011 MSPB 87 (2011). The Board discounted the agency's argument that the appellant falsified information when falsification was not fairly embraced in the narrative supporting the "conduct unbecoming" charge.

If the charge is in the nature of a fraudulent claim, intent to defraud must be shown; unintentional error is insufficient to show fraud. If fraud is the charge, the penalty cannot be sustained because should have monitored paychecks unless negligence is also explicitly charged. See Skinner v. Dept. of Army, 32 MSPR 586, 588 & n.2 (1987) (charge of false statements on time and attendance reports); Lacapurcia v. Dept. of Army, 27 MSPR 514, 519 (1985), post; cf. Wells v. DOD, 53 MSPR 637, 643 (1992) (agency timekeeping records did not establish the fraudulent receipt of overtime by the appellant; "It is not for the appellant to prove that he worked the hours claimed, particularly when the agency has not specified which hours it disputes."). The agency bears the burden of proving fraud; it is not for the appellant to prove that he worked the hours claimed, particularly when the agency has not specified the hours it disputes. As to alleged falsification of timecards involving the accounting for time by a subordinate who appeared to have some personal differences with his supervisor, the Board discounted the evidence as unreliable and would not sustain the falsification charge because of the appellant's inability to explain his whereabouts or because he may have made some errors as to his timecards, particularly when the appellant's duties called for him to be at other locations. Lacapurcia v. Dept. of Army, 27 MSPR 514, 519 (1985); see Conaway v. Dept. of Commerce, CH-0752-16-0166-I-2 (NP 9/22/2022) (charge of providing false information not sustained for possible failure to follow correct procedures in filling out a survey form: "A charge of providing false information is considered to be a charge of falsification. See, e.g., Leatherbury v. Department of the Army, 524 F.3d 1293, 1297, 1300 (Fed. Cir. 2008) (construing charges of 'filing a false claim against the government' and 'filing a false travel voucher' as charges of falsification); Rackers v. Department of Justice, 79 M.S.P.R. 262, 266, 278 (1998) (considering a charge of 'providing false information in official documents' as one of falsification), aff'd, 194 F.3d 1336 (Fed. Cir. 1999). To establish a charge of falsification, the agency must prove the following by preponderant evidence: (1) the appellant supplied incorrect information; and (2) she did so knowingly with intent to defraud, deceive, or mislead the agency for her own private material gain. Boo v. Department of Homeland Security, 122 M.S.P.R. 100, ¶¶ 10-12 (2014).").

There is a distinction between a false claim, negligence in treating matters pertaining to timekeeping, and a violation of regulations. A charge that an employee failed to follow regulations pertaining to timekeeping does not depend on proof of the appellant's intent to violate the regulation or the intent to mislead the agency. Wells v. DOD, 53 MSPR 637, 643 (1992) ("Whether he intended to violate the regulation or did so unwittingly and with no intent to mislead is not relevant....[K]nowledge is not an element of the offense."). As to negligence, employees have an obligation to review the accuracy of their paychecks and leave and earnings statements that their agencies provide to them. They have an obligation to report errors they discover in the wages paid or in the leave deducted from their leave accounts. They cannot relieve themselves of this responsibility by arranging to have their leave and earnings statements mailed to addresses where they do not reside. But the Board concluded that removal for unintentional failure to monitor these payments exceeded the limits of reasonableness. A suspension for 20 days was warranted. Broussard v. Dept. of Navy, 27 MSPR 382, 385-86 (1985); see Lee v. Dept. of Treasury, DC-0752-13-0157-I-1 (NP 4/25/2014) ("Even if the appellant's supervisor entered his time incorrectly, the appellant remained ultimately responsible for ensuring that his time was reported accurately. Cf. Luther v. Department of Commerce, 107 M.S.P.R. 616, ¶ 14 (2008) (the fact that the appellant's supervisor certified his time and attendance record did not indicate the supervisor's approval of the appellant's performance of unauthorized work during the relevant pay period); cf. Broussard v. Department of the Navy, 27 M.S.P.R. 382, 385 (1985) (finding that disciplinary action was warranted where an employee negligently received wage overpayments and leave credits because employees have an obligation to review the accuracy of their paychecks and of the leave-and-earnings statements which their agencies provide to them, as well as an obligation to report errors which they discover in the wages paid or in the leave which is deducted from their leave accounts)."); Skinner v. Dept. of Army, supra. For negligence concerning time and payment records, involving about $155 for night differential received but not earned, removal was too extreme. The penalty was reduced to 60 days' suspension. White v. Dept. of Army, 32 MSPR 600, 604 (1987) (citing Broussard, supra). [Refer in this chapter to "Negligence."]
