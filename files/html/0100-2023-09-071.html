5. Need to Obtain Documentation

An appellant is not required to have evidence available concerning the merits of the agency's action when he files an appeal. Lack of access to documents should not prevent timely filing of the appeal. Hollingsworth v. Defense Commissary Agency, 82 MSPR 450 (1999) ("An appellant's filing delay is not excused based on the bare claim that he needed to obtain additional evidence."); Holt v. USPS, 36 MSPR 240, 243-44 (1988) (given the availability of discovery procedures, the unavailability of documents from the agency did not excuse either an untimely appeal or an untimely PFR). Time limits will not be waived on the basis of unspecific allegations that the appellant lacked material to prepare his appeal. Trask v. FAA, 2 MSPR 208, 210, 2 MSPB 373 (1980); cf. Arredondo v. Dept. of Treasury, 9 MSPR 90, 92 (1981) (appeal time was not tolled because the agency official refused to discuss the case with the appellant's counsel without prior receipt of a designation of representative; there was no explanation of how the need to speak with an agency official delayed the filing of an appeal).

Failure to receive the SF-50 formally documenting an appealable action did not excuse delay in filing the appeal, Paradiso v. Dept. of Army, 65 MSPR 324, 327-28 (1994):

The appellant did not need the SF-50 to prepare her appeal. First, the charge on which she was removed, making threats of bodily harm, was stated clearly and consistently in the proposal notice and the decision letter. Second, even if there had been some inconsistency between those documents, the SF-50 would be unlikely to resolve the matter. Furthermore, even if the instructions on the appeals form led the appellant to believe that she was required to attach a copy of the SF-50 to her appeal before filing it, there is no basis in the record for finding either that she required two weeks after receiving that form to do so or that she believed she had an additional twenty days for this purpose. We note further that, in view of the clearly set forth twenty calendar day deadline of which the appellant was aware, she might have contacted the Board to inquire as to the necessity for having the SF-50 or to request an extension of time in which to file her appeal. The record, however, does not reflect that she did either. See Caldwell v. Dept. of the Interior, 58 MSPR 54, 57 (1993).

We also find that Walls v. MSPB, 29 F.3d 1578 (Fed. Cir. 1994), and Loui v. MSPB, 25 F.3d 1011 (Fed. Cir. 1994), cases relied upon in the Dissenting Opinion, are distinguishable from the present appeal. In Walls, the court excused an appeal that was filed two days late on the basis that the appellant was confused by a requirement of the Board's regulations and because of the minimal nature of the delay. Id. at 1582-83. While the one-week filing delay in this case is only slightly longer than the delay in Walls, the appellant in this case did not allege that her ability to act in a timely manner was adversely affected by the emotional problems suffered by the appellant in the Walls case. Moreover, there is no basis, in Walls or elsewhere, for considering the lack of prejudice to the agency as a factor in determining whether good cause has been shown; instead, that factor is to be considered only after a good-cause determination has been made. See Walls, 29 F.3d at 1583-84 ("[u]pon petitioner's showing of good cause for the untimely filing of his appeal, waiver of the filing deadline is appropriate..., 'absent a showing of substantial prejudice to the agency caused by the delay in filing'").

In Loui, where the agency failed to provide the employee with notice of his removal on or before its effective date, the court held that he should be allowed the full appeal period to file an appeal from the time of receipt of notice of the agency's action, regardless of the effective date of the action. Loui, 25 F.3d at 1014. The delayed receipt of an SF-50, however, does not have the same effect on an employee's ability to file a timely appeal that the delayed receipt of a decision notice has. In the instant case, the appellant knew of the basis of the removal, and of her appeal right, by the time she was removed. For this reason, and because she did not need a copy of the SF-50 in order to prepare her appeal, Loui does not support a finding that the appellant has shown good cause for her untimeliness.

The need to obtain documentation from an employee's union did not excuse an untimely appeal, Schuringa v. Dept. of Treasury, 106 MSPR 1, 4 ¶ 8, 2007 MSPB 116 (2007):

On review, the appellant reasserts that she acted diligently when she attempted to gather "copies of [her] oral replies and other documentation" from the union pertaining to her suspension and by contacting the Board in an effort to request an extension of time. On this point, we agree with the finding of the administrative judge that these circumstances do not constitute good cause for the filing delay. The appellant's alleged lack of access to information from which she could develop a meaningful appeal does not establish good cause for her failure to timely file her appeal. See Hollingsworth v. Defense Commissary Agency, 82 M.S.P.R. 450, ¶ 6 (1999); Persons v. U.S. Postal Service, 75 M.S.P.R. 428, 432 (1997) (an appellant's filing delay is not excused based on the bare allegation that he needed to obtain additional evidence). Moreover, the record indicates that, on June 20, 2006, the day before the filing deadline, the appellant e-mailed a union official to request copies of the transcripts from her oral replies to the proposed suspension and stated that she was submitting her Board appeal "before the end of this week." The appellant apparently had, in her possession, some of the documents she wanted to file with her appeal, but continued to request additional documentation even in light of the looming filing deadline. Id. The appellant has not explained why she was unable to obtain these documents before the filing deadline or why she could not have submitted her petition for appeal without them. See, e.g., Benroth v. Department of the Army, 79 M.S.P.R. 15, ¶ 6 (1998).
