1. Default Judgment

A default judgment imposes an adverse adjudication against an agency that fails to defend the appeal. The default is entered without regard to the merits of the case. The Board observed in Miller v. OPM, 7 MSPR 469, 474-75, 7 MSPB 333 (1981), that a default judgment cannot be entered against the United States under Fed. R. Civ. P. 55 unless the claimant establishes his right to relief. Miller concluded that it was inappropriate to find in favor of an appellant in a disability retirement appeal as a result of OPM's failure to comply with discovery requests. See Burnett v. DHUD, 114 MSPR 1, 2 n.1 2010 MSPB 68 (2010) (Board lacks authority to award a default judgment against an agency that fails to respond to a PFR); Hayes v. Dept. of Treasury, 74 MSPR 613, 615 (1997) (Board is without authority to issue default judgments against agencies); Head v. OPM, 53 MSPR 421, 422 (1992) ("It is well settled that the Board does not have the authority to enter a default judgment as a sanction against OPM."); Jeffery v. OPM, 37 MSPR 356, 359 (1988) ("[T]he Board itself lacks the authority to enter a default judgment as a sanction against OPM in this case. Such a ruling would, in effect, grant the appellant retirement benefits where it has not been determined that she is legally entitled to them."); Lavelle v. Dept. of Navy, 37 MSPR 86, 90 (1988) ("The Board is without authority to issue a default judgment against an agency.").

Mittendorf v. OPM, 9 MSPR 484, 486 (1982), summarized the bar against default judgments:

Section 1205(a)(2) of 5 USC provides the board with the authority to order a federal agency to comply with any order or decision issued and to enforce compliance. Furthermore, the Board's regulations at 5 CFR § 1201.43 allow presiding officials to impose sanctions upon the parties as necessary to serve the ends of justice. However, the grant of a default judgment in favor of a claimant against the United States is prohibited by the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, Rule 55(c), "unless claimant establishes his claim or right to relief by evidence satisfactory to the court." Thus, the rule prohibits the entry of default judgment as a sanction against an agency of the United States for failure to comply with an order for the production of evidence or for failure to comply with an agency's procedures for defending an administrative action. See Miller v. Office of Personnel Management, 7 MSPB 333, 336, 7 MSPR 469, 474 (1981).

Therefore, in the instant case, the Board finds that the presiding official erroneously ruled for the appellant solely as a sanction against OPM. A ruling to the contrary would in effect grant appellant disability retirement benefits where it has not been determined whether she is legally entitled to them under 5 USC § 8331(6).

The Board again addressed the use of a default judgment in Crisler v. FEMA, 32 MSPR 578, 580 (1987). The agency failed to respond to the appeal. The AJ reversed the appellant's RIF as a sanction against the agency. The Board reversed the AJ:

The Board has held that an administrative judge may impose sanctions on a party as necessary to serve the ends of justice.... However, an administrative judge's sanctioning authority does not include the authority to rule against an agency on the merits as a sanction for not responding to Board orders. See Miller v. OPM, 7 MSPB 333 (1981). The Board has held that reversing an agency action as a sanction for failure to comply with a Board order amounts to the entry of a default judgment against the agency and that such action is beyond the Board's statutory authority. See Mittendorf v. OPM, 9 MSPB 120 (1982). Thus, inasmuch as the administrative judge erred in ruling against the agency as a sanction for not responding to his orders, the case must be remanded to the Denver Regional Office for issuance of a new acknowledgment order and for further processing on the merits of this appeal.

Crisler was reprised by Mozqueda v. DOD, 54 MSPR 152, 157 (1992) (AJ's sanctioning authority does not include the authority to rule against an agency on the merits as a sanction for not responding to the Board's orders). The Board's reluctance to impose sanctions against OPM by striking its defenses for late filings was also apparent in Swenson v. OPM, 8 MSPR 73, 76, 7 MSPB 565 (1981). The Board noted but, as a practical matter, did nothing about OPM's failure to comply with Board orders in Hansen v. OPM, 35 MSPR 1, 1 (1987), although at least the Board suggested that in the future it might consider striking OPM's pleadings or evidence as a sanction. Even though a default judgment is not entered in Board litigation, it is emphasized that the same practical effect is accomplished if the agency appeal response is excluded as belated in an unacceptable performance or adverse action case. If the agency is procedurally barred from supplying its proof, the appellant must win if he stands on a record involving disputed matters the agency must prove.

Nothing in the Reform Act precludes a default against the government. EEOC AJs often effectively default agencies for failure to follow Commission rules governing the processing of federal sector complaints. If an agency fails to respond to an unfair labor practice complaint issued by an FLRA regional director, the allegations in the complaint are deemed admitted and summary judgment against the agency is likely. Appellants are often effectively defaulted for filing their appeals a few days late, yet no statute compels that result. As the Board often states, when an appellant relies on a provision of the Federal Rules, those rules are instructive, not controlling. Striking an agency's evidence or appeal response strips the agency of the record necessary to defend against the appeal if the appellant decides to forego a hearing. Because an order striking evidence has the same effect as a default, it would make more sense to permit the entry of defaults against agencies that have the burden of proof in adverse actions.

Complaints against ALJs are adjudicated under the Administrative Procedure Act, and if the AJ fails to timely respond to a complaint she may find himself the recipient of an adverse default judgment. [Refer to Chapter 6, "Administrative Law Judges."] The same might occur in a Special Counsel disciplinary case or Hatch Act case brought through an OSC complaint to the Board.
