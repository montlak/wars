d. Time Pressure

A reassignment and downgrade accepted by the employee may be an adverse action if the employee was under extreme time pressure to accept the downgrade and if the appellant was provided no viable alternatives. White v. Dept. of Treasury, 3 MSPR 488, 490, 4 MSPB 12 (1980) (determining involuntary a downgrading that occurred after a brief meeting with the appellant, who was given "no viable alternatives"). Although unreasonable time pressure may suggest duress, if the appellant had at least a week to consider the action proposed by the agency, and if he had time to consult with his family and union representative before he actually made his decision to request a demotion, no duress is evident. Ryals v. Dept. of Army, 14 MSPR 409, 412 (1983). A downgrading requested by an appellant after being threatened with an adverse action is not involuntary if the employee had three days to think it over, the employee had an alternative--fighting any removal action taken--and there was no misrepresentation of material facts by the agency. Grant v. Dept. of Air Force, 15 MSPR 288, 291 (1983). A downgrading is not involuntary if an employee is assisted by a representative during discussions with management, knows the available options, and has the opportunity to discuss the decision with others. Andrews v. DHHS, 10 MSPR 226, 228 (1982).

The voluntariness of a downgrading was considered in O'Brien v. MSPB (9th Cir. 1981 NP No. 80-7132). The employee agreed to a reassignment and downgrading after he was both confronted with his conduct and threatened by the agency with suspension of his security clearance if the agency was required to direct a reassignment and terminate his overseas appointment. He had less than a day to think about his decision. Substantial evidence supported the conclusion that the employee was not under coercive time pressure. The personnel action was not effective for two more months; the employee did nothing to rescind his agreement to reassignment during that time. The court also concluded that the agency had a legitimate basis for threatening the suspension of the employee's security clearance:

The inquiry here involved does not bear upon O'Brien's state of mind; he would be as motivated to seek a transfer by a justified security clearance suspension as an unjustified one. Rather the inquiry relates to the appropriateness of the threatened personnel action. It is entirely proper to afford an employee a choice between taking voluntary action, such as a resignation or transfer, and facing an adverse personnel action, so long as the threatened action could reasonably be justified.

...

O'Brien was faced with an unpleasant choice. He could have contested a directed reassignment as well as any suspension of security clearance. He chose to avoid the confrontation and instead to utilize the 60 days afforded him to find another position in the United States. Such a choice, if there is a reasonable and good faith basis for the proposed personnel action, does not constitute coercion. See Taylor v. United States, 591 F.2d 688 (Ct. Cl. 1979); Autera v. United States, 389 F.2d 815 (Ct. Cl. 1968).

A day to think a downgrading over, with the help of a union representative, deflected an allegation of a constructive adverse action through downgrading, Rethaber v. MSPB, 233 Fed. Appx. 1001, 1004 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP):

Ms. Rethaber also argues that she was coerced into accepting the demotion because the agency did not give her time to consider her options. Ms. Rethaber is correct in her statement that forcing an employee to make an immediate decision can be improper and coercive. Schultz, 810 F.2d at 1136 (holding that agency's requirement that resignation be submitted immediately was coercive). However, Ms. Rethaber was not forced to make an immediate decision. She was given until end of the next business day to return her response to the transfer proposal. During the time period between receiving the proposal and returning her decision, Ms. Rethaber was able to meet with her union representative. Under these circumstances, we determine Ms. Rethaber has not met her burden of proving that the agency coerced her into accepting the transfer.
