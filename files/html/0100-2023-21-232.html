1. Light Duty

Placement on light duty is not the equivalent of full recovery for purposes of restoration. The appellant may invoke the right to priority consideration, Beltran v. USPS, 50 MSPR 425, 429-30 (1991):

The appellant contends that he is entitled to mandatory restoration because he recovered from a compensable injury within 1 year from the time the compensable injury recurred. In this regard, he asserts that the administrative judge erred in finding that his limited duty position did not constitute regular employment. The appellant's assertion is without merit. The Board recently held in Denny v. Department of the Navy, 48 M.S.P.R. 241, 244-46 (1991), that regular employment under 5 U.S.C. § 8151(b)(l) and 5 C.F.R. § 353.301(b) does not include light duty employment because the term "regular" contemplates that an employee is able to perform all the duties of the position he left or of an equivalent one. The same analysis applies to limited duty employment. Id. at 245-46; 5 C.F.R. § 353.304. Thus, the appellant does not meet the criteria for immediate restoration to his position.

We have granted the appellant's petition for review because we agree with his assertion that his right to priority consideration should begin the date he first applied for restoration, November 6, 1986, after his OWCP benefits were retroactively terminated on the basis of his full recovery. See, e.g., Metsopulos v. United States Postal Service, 33 M.S.P.R. 536, 541 (1937); Hogg v. United States Postal Service, 29 M.S.P.R. 153, 157 (1985); Withers v. Department of the Air Force, 28 M.S.P.R. 379, 382 (1985); McKoy v. Department of the Army, 18 M.S.P.R. 636, 639-40 (1984); see also Cox v. United States International Trade Coiwnission, 15 M.S.P.R. 455, 459 (1983).[4] We are aware that it may be burdensome for the agency to have to make a position available to the appellant. Since the appellant was not responsible for OWCP's delay, however, the delay cannot defeat his right to priority consideration for restoration. See Gavette v. Department of the Treasury, 44 M.S.P.R. 166, 177 (1990); Cox v. United States International Trade Commission, 6 M.S.P.R. 336, 338 (1981).

[4] Cf. Hetrick v. Department of the Interior, 39 M.S.P.R. 426, 428-30 (1989) (in case involving mandatory restoration where the appellant recovered within 1 year, the appellant was entitled to be restored on date his compensation ended despite his late request, which was due to the agency's failure to notify him of his right to restoration).

If an employee is injured, goes on light duty, and retires, his later receipt of OWCP benefits does not entitle him to reemployment if there is no evidence of recovery after he ceased working for the agency. See Oswald v. USPS, 5 MSPR 385 (1981).
