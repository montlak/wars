2. Additional Appealable Action

Despite ambiguous regulatory language--lacking definition of a claim--there is a distinction between an issue, e.g., whether a penalty is disparate, and an entirely new appealable matter that could be styled as a claim--essentially a new appeal. One might appeal the denial of a within-grade increase and then seek to tack on a performance-based removal that followed the WIGI appeal. However, the removal is not a new issue; it is a new, or at least a different, appealable matter. On issues concerning the same claim, the matter, perhaps a claims for damages, can normally be raised at any time through the prehearing submissions unless there is prejudice or delay to the opposing party. 5 CFR 1201.24(b) (2022). An appealable matter tacked on to an existing appeal, in contrast, should ordinarily be filed by appeal within the Board's regulatory deadline. If sufficiently related, the two appealable matters could then be joined.

An employee was notified of the right to appeal a suspension during a notice period and did not do so, waiting until the hearing of the removal case to challenge the suspension. The Board sustained the AJ's refusal to consider the suspension claim without a demonstration of good cause for delay in presenting the matter. Thomson v. Dept. of Navy, 16 MSPR 474, 475 n.1 (1983); see McMillian v. OPM, 36 MSPR 259, 262 (1987) (appellant appealed an OPM determination that he did not have sufficient service credit for an annuity; he would be required to file a separate appeal as to the agency-employer that allegedly misled him concerning annuity entitlement).

In Smith v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 363, 366 n.4 (1983), an appealable matter--as opposed to a legal issue--an improper emergency suspension, was raised for the first time during the hearing. The agency argued that the AJ and the Board should not have considered the suspension. The Board applied the Alonzo standards, noting that the agency did not give the employee notice of his adverse action appeal rights concerning the suspension, and concluded that an inference that the appellant should have known he could appeal because he lost pay was unwarranted since the appellant was not trained in law and was not represented by legal counsel until several months after filing his appeal. The agency demonstrated no prejudice; it was notified of the suspension challenge at the time of the hearing and before the close of the record, and the agency could defend against the challenge to the suspension. But see Thomson, supra. Smith noted that the agency should take care to preserve its position during adjudication:

Moreover, there is no evidence in the record that the agency objected to the Board's consideration of the suspension issue, or sought a continuance to respond to the claim, before the record closed or at any time prior to the issuance of the initial decision three months later, nor does it now assert that it did so. Rule 15(b) of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure provides that issues not raised in the pleadings, but tried by express or implied consent of the parties, shall be treated in all respects as if they had been raised in the pleadings. Although the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure are not binding on the Board, we have previously consulted them for guidance as to procedural matters, and do so here.... We emphasize, however, that it is the agency's inability to demonstrate prejudice, and not its failure to object per se that is of determinative significance in this regard.
