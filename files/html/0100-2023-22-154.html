5. Reasonableness of Time Invested

An adverse action does not an adverse action equal. Cases are dissimilar. Lawyers' approaches to similar cases are dissimilar. An appeal that takes one lawyer half an hour to dictate may take another lawyer three hours to compose. Who judges whether the time is reasonable? First, the attorney responsible for the case. Second, the Board. The rule is that the "burden of establishing the reasonableness of the hours claimed in any attorney fee request is on the party moving for an award of attorney fees." Casali v. Dept. of Treasury, 81 MSPR 347, 353 ¶ 13 (1999).

Perhaps there are some outer limits. The Board signaled administrative panic in SSA v. Goodman, 33 MSPR 325, 335 (1987), remanding the case after stating:

...We note that the large number of individuals performing legal services--26--and the large number of hours expended--approximately 2400--are unprecedented in Board proceedings. Based on the record before us, our preliminary assessment is that the hours claimed by the petitioner are excessive, if not extravagant.[10]

[10] By comparison, attorneys in a comparable case, SSA v. Balaban, 33 MSPR 309 (1987), sought fees for a total of 499.30 hours for the Board proceeding.

The proceedings in this case have been protracted, and the request for attorney fees has been zealously litigated. In this regard, we find it appropriate to repeat the Supreme Court's admonishment that "[a] request for attorney's fees should not result in a second major litigation. Ideally, of course, litigants will settle the amount of a fee." Hensley v. Eckerhart, 461 U.S. 424 (1983).

The Board examines fees from the perspective of the time records submitted by counsel and its AJs' assessments of the quality and efficiency of representation, tempered by whatever evidence is offered by the agency that pays the bill as to the reasonableness of time invested. The Board seems disinclined to read into counsel's financial arrangements with his client any reflection on the fees claimed by counsel and adequately supported by counsel's time records. See Wiatr v. Dept. of Air Force, 50 MSPR 441, 450 (1991). The Board may be impressed with counsel's attempts to police his own billings to avoid what might be considered as unreasonable charges to the government. See Green v. Administrators of Tulane Educ. Fund., 284 F.3d 642, 662 (5th Cir. 2002) (Title VII case: "Billing judgment is usually shown by the attorney writing off unproductive, excessive or redundant hours."; "[t]he remedy for failing to exercise billing judgment is to reduce the hours awarded by a percentage."); Taylor v. DOJ, 69 MSPR 299, 306 (1996) ("We also note that the appellant's primary attorney, Mr. Gaffney, claimed compensation for less than 95% of the hours he spent on the appeal. He made his own '5% billing judgment reduction,' applied to the total number of hours spent, and his statement also contains entries for activities that appear to have been related to the appeal, but for which he did not seek recovery.").

At times the Board provides little deference to counsel or to the AJ's rulings and instead undertakes intensive analysis of an AJ's rulings on components of fee claims, examining the claim (and the allowance of fees by the AJ) on a line-item basis. See Schultz v. USPS, PH-0752-94-0233-M-1 (NP 12/16/2016) ("Because no hearing was held on the attorney fees motion, however, we need not defer to the administrative judge's determination that all of the claimed hours are reasonable. Driscoll, 116 M.S.P.R. 662, ¶ 30 (citing Howard v. Office of Personnel Management, 79 M.S.P.R. 172, ¶ 7 (1998)). Accordingly, we have thoroughly reviewed the relevant documentary evidence to determine if the claimed hours are reasonable."; "In assessing the reasonableness of the amount of time spent on these submissions, we have considered the level of complexity of the issues they address and the amount of time necessary to review the record."); Gubino v. Dept. of Transp., 85 MSPR 518, 521-30 ¶¶ 6-27 (2000); cf. Kilpatrick v. VA, CB-7121-13-0181-A-1 (NP 1/16/2015) (small reduction in fees occurred when a small amount of time was charged for correction of a case processing error); Grant v. Dept. of Treasury, DC-0752-11-0884-A-1 (NP 2/10/2014) (denying fees for time spent on a motion to compel a response to belatedly-initiated discovery). But see Freeman v. VA, 66 MSPR 125, 131 (1995) (suggesting that a minute examination of charges will generally not be required or encouraged). In Ruble v. OPM, 96 MSPR 44, 49 ¶ 11 (2004), the Board seemed concerned that counsel billed .2 hours for leaving each in a group of telephone messages. The Board said "the appellant's attorney reasonably should have taken no more than .1 hours for each message." If counsel bills some time to a client that is not included in a fee petition, the AJ has no cause to evaluate that time and should not rely upon that time to reduce the fee claimed in the motion. Robinson v. DHHS, 32 MSPR 683, 688 (1987). In Matthews v. SSA, 104 MSPR 130, 2006 MSPB 325 (2006), the Board determined that the AJ was too restrictive in evaluating the time spent by counsel immediately prior to hearing. The Board noted that counsel had not become involved in the appeal until shortly before the hearing and that the time spent analyzing the record, researching legal issues, and preparing for the hearing was not duplicative or excessive. Counsel was awarded a little more than $9,000 for the work that went into a day-long hearing with about nine witnesses and the time for preparing the fee petition. Rumsey v. DOJ, 123 MSPR 502, 515-16 ¶ 25, 2016 MSPB 28 (2016), considering a reduction in fees for an issue the Board determined was not properly compensable, compared the briefing space spent on that issue with counsel's entire briefing: "By our calculation, Mr. Burka devoted approximately 11% of his argument in the petition for review and reply (4 pages out of 36) to this issue. Accordingly, we reduce the number of hours for preparation of those two documents by approximately 11% from 71.67 to 63.79 hours (71.67 x .89 = 63.79)."
