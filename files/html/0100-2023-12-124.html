4. Issue of Mental Competence During Appeal Adjudication

A challenge to all the factual findings may be presented if an appellant proceeded pro se before the AJ during a period when the appellant was mentally unable to represent himself. Connelly v. USPS, 35 MSPR 614, 615, 617-18 (1987), sent the case back to the region when the appellant, through retained counsel, demonstrated that the appellant was incapable of representing himself pro se during the earlier regional adjudication:

...The removal was based on the charge that the appellant's emotional and mental condition rendered him unable to fulfill the requirements of his position.

...

In his petition for review, the appellant, through counsel, challenges virtually all of the findings of the administrative judge. The appellant's counsel contends that, assuming for purposes of argument that the appellant suffered from the mental disorders alleged by the agency, he certainly could not represent himself adequately at a hearing on appeal.

As we have noted above, the appellant was unrepresented in the proceeding below, and there are indications that he was suffering from a psychiatric disorder that is likely to have affected his ability to pursue his appeal effectively.[4] We note further that the appellant's removal, which was effected only 3 months and 2 days before the hearing was convened, was based solely on the agency's charge that a psychiatric condition rendered him "unfit for duty," and that, according to an unrefuted statement in the initial decision, the agency has stated that the reason it did not file a disability retirement application in the appellant's behalf, instead of removing him, was that the appellant did not meet the service requirements for a disability retirement annuity.

[4] The administrative judge stated in her initial decision that, at the time the hearing in this case was held, the appellant was "still under the impression [despite testimony to the contrary] that someone at the Post Office 'blew up' his automobile," and that his testimony that he entered a psychiatric center "to hide from a possible 'assassination"' was "difficult to follow."

In light of these particular circumstances, it appears that the appellant was not capable of representing himself before the regional office. For that reason, and because the appellant now is represented by an attorney, we find it appropriate to provide the appellant with a full opportunity to pursue his allegations.[6] Accordingly, we hereby VACATE the initial decision and REMAND the appeal to the regional office for a new adjudication.

[6] See Bergstein v. United States Postal Service, 27 MSPR 56, 58-60 (1985) (Board remanded appeal for new adjudication, based in part on evidence that appellant's physical and mental problems prevented him from properly representing himself at the hearing).

USPS, 27 MSPR 56, 59-60 (1985), cited in Connelly, reversed an AJ who failed to continue a hearing for an appellant who asserted that he needed more time to retain counsel because his mental state did not permit him to adequately represent himself.
