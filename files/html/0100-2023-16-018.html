a. Timeliness of Work

As an example of the leeway managers are allowed in interpreting the performance standards requiring timely work by professional employees, consider the discussion of standards for a supervisory trial attorney, Romero v. EEOC, 55 MSPR 527, 536-37 (1992):

The administrative judge noted that Alston, the appellant's supervisor, was able to quantify the requisite figure for the "Exceeds" (sic, "Highly Effective") level, and that he testified that he used a "sliding scale" to "determine what was an appropriate level of untimeliness." Alston testified that he had advised the appellant that "in terms of 'Highly Effective,' as opposed to 'Fully Successful,' you've got to be talking less than 15 percent, or 10 percent [of untimely cases submitted]." He also testified, however, that a determination of the number of documented instances of untimeliness that would render an employee's performance "Fully Successful" under the critical elements:

[W]as probably a discretion of mine, and I tried to implement that standard by looking at the amount of work he had, and the time frames with which he had to do the work, and incorporating his entire workload at that period of time.

The fact that a performance standard could be more objective than it is does not, per se, make it impermissibly subjective. See Bronfman v. General Services Administration, 40 MSPR 184, 190 (1989). Further, we have held that the Board should give some deference to an agency's judgment as to whether performance standards for a professional employee can feasibly incorporate specific deadlines and numeric measures. See Siegelman v. Department of Housing and Urban Development, 14 MSPR 326, 330-31 (1983). We have also held that an agency is not required to include in each performance standard specific indicators of quantity, quality, and timeliness that are used to evaluate work. See Coleman v. Department of the Army, 27 MSPR 305, 309 (1985).

Thus, we find that, under the performance standards at issue here, Alston could appropriately use his managerial discretion to determine the permissible level of untimely submissions for a supervisory attorney's legal work. This exercise of managerial discretion does not render the "Fully Successful" standards under the two critical elements at issue in this appeal impermissibly vague. Here, as in Siegleman, "[w]e cannot find the agency unreasonable in determining that work at [the] appellant's level is not susceptible to the kind of mechanical, judgment-free rating which [he] argues is required." Siegleman, 14 MSPR at 331.

As to a "special projects" standard for a secretary, the Board opined in Melnick v. DHUD, 42 MSPR 93, 96 n.1, 99 (1989), first stating the standard and then noting the ability to fill in the standard's nuances through the PIP:

...Non-routine special tasks are carried out accurately, professionally and timely. Results are well thought out, displayed and presented, with minimum input from the immediate supervisor.

...

..."Non-routine special tasks" are, by definition, ad hoc assignments that vary widely in nature and that occur on an intermittent basis, along with the appellant's regularly-scheduled and anticipated job duties. Extreme specificity in the written performance standard relating to such special, non-routine assignments therefore cannot be expected, and is unnecessary so long as the agency provides the appellant with additional, specific information about her job requirements adequate to apprise the appellant of what is expected of her. See Donaldson, 27 M.S.P.R. at 298.

Here, the agency communicated what was required of the appellant both prior to and during the PIP. Indeed, as the administrative judge noted, "the agency clearly reminded the appellant of four specific special tasks which remained incomplete at the outset of the performance improvement period...." Accordingly, because the agency provided the appellant with a detailed description of the standards against which she was being measured, and because it was not unreasonable for the agency to expect completion of the assigned tasks by the end of the PIP, we find that the performance standard relating to special tasks met the statutory requirements for validity under 5 U.S.C. § 4302. See Wilson, 770 F.2d at 1055-56.
