6. USERRA Appeal

There is no time limit for filing a USERRA appeal directly with the Board if resort was not earlier made to the Labor Department. 5 CFR 1201.22(b)(2) (2022). [Refer to Chapter 1, "Time for USERRA Appeal" and "Permissive Exhaustion of Labor Department Remedy."] If an individual has an otherwise appealable action, e.g., a removal, and a USERRA claim, and if that individual fails to file a timely appeal of the removal, then the Board will adjudicate the USERRA complaint concerning the removal under only USERRA standards, Holmes v. DOJ, 92 MSPR 377, 381-82 ¶¶ 10-12 (2002):

An individual who believes that he has been the victim of a violation of the prohibitions discussed above may choose to file a complaint with the Board, 38 U.S.C. § 4324(b), or with the Secretary of Labor, 38 U.S.C. § 4322. 5 C.F.R. § 1208.11. See Yates, 145 F.3d at 1483; Petersen v. Department of the Interior, 71 M.S.P.R. 227, 233 (1996). Here, the appellant elected to file his USERRA complaint directly with the Board. There is no time limit to file a USERRA appeal with the Board. Muse v. U.S. Postal Service, 82 M.S.P.R. 164 ¶ 14 (1999).

In Muse, the Board found that the appellant's appeal of his removal was untimely filed. Id. ¶ 11. Nonetheless, the Board found that the administrative judge erred by not considering as a new appeal the appellant's allegation, made in his untimely petition for appeal of an otherwise appealable action under 5 U.S.C. § 7701, that the agency's removal action was taken in violation of USERRA. Id. ¶ 12. The Board found that the appellant had not established USERRA jurisdiction because, although his allegation that he was a disabled veteran was undisputed, nothing in the record affirmatively showed that the appellant performed in the uniformed service. Id. The Board remanded for a jurisdictional determination. The Board, however, found that, if on remand the administrative judge found that the Board had jurisdiction and that the appeal was ripe for adjudication, the appeal was timely filed. Id. ¶ 14. The situation in this appeal does not materially differ from that in Muse. The appellant filed an untimely petition for appeal of an otherwise appealable removal action, and alleged that in taking the removal action, the agency discriminated against him on the basis of his status as a veteran in violation of USERRA. Thus, we find that the appellant's USERRA appeal is timely filed, and remand to allow the appellant to establish jurisdiction under USERRA. See Muse, 82 M.S.P.R. 164 ¶ 14.

Here, as in Muse, the administrative judge should on remand apply the jurisdictional test adopted by the court in Yates, 145 F.3d at 1485. The requirements of that jurisdictional test are that the appellant: (1) show performance of duty in a uniformed service of the United States; (2) allege a loss of a benefit of employment; and (3) allege that the benefit was lost due to the performance of duty in the uniformed service. Id. If the appellant establishes the Board's jurisdiction, the administrative judge should adjudicate only whether the agency has violated USERRA. Under the circumstances of this appeal, where the appellant's otherwise appealable action was untimely filed, the Board is exercising its jurisdiction over the appeal as a complaint under 38 U.S.C. § 4324(c)(1)-(2), not as an appeal under 5 U.S.C. § 7701. Thus, the Board's jurisdiction in adjudicating the appellant's USERRA complaint is no different from the jurisdiction it exercises in a pure USERRA case, i.e., a case that alleges a USERRA violation but does not raise matters that may otherwise be appealed to the Board. See Bodus v. Department of the Air Force, 82 M.S.P.R. 508 ¶¶ 11-17 (1999). Accordingly, the Board's authority with regard to this complaint does not extend beyond the alleged USERRA violation and does not allow for a decision on the merits of the underlying matter except as necessary to address the appellant's military status discrimination claims. Metzenbaum v. Department of Justice, 89 M.S.P.R. 285 ¶ 15 (2001).

It was again stated that because there is no time limit for the USERRA appeal, an adverse action appeal that contains a USERRA affirmative defense will be heard by the AJ on the basis of the USERRA claim even if the adverse action appeal is untimely, Aguilar v. USPS, 102 MSPR 102, 107 ¶ 10, 2006 MSPB 132 (2006):

Under the circumstances of this appeal, where the appellant's otherwise appealable action was untimely filed, the Board is exercising its jurisdiction over the appeal as complaints under 38 U.S.C. §§ 4324(c)(1)-(2) and 5 U.S.C. § 3330a. See Henderson, 95 M.S.P.R. 454, ¶¶ 8-9; Holmes, 92 M.S.P.R. 377, ¶ 12. Thus, the Board's jurisdiction to adjudicate the appellant's USERRA and VEOA complaints is no different from the jurisdiction it exercises in pure USERRA and VEOA cases where the appellant does not raise matters that may otherwise be appealed to the Board. Holmes, 92 M.S.P.R. 377, ¶ 12. Accordingly, the Board's authority with regard to this complaint does not extend beyond the alleged USERRA and VEOA violations and does not allow for a decision on the merits of the appellant's removal, except where examination of those issues is necessary to address the appellant's USERRA and VEOA claims. Id.

Aguila was expanded on by Brown v. USPS, 106 MSPR 12, 22-23 ¶¶ 19-21, 2007 MSPB 136 (2007):

In a removal appeal, the Board can consider an appellant's USERRA claim as an affirmative defense under 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)(2)(C). Aguilar v. U.S. Postal Service, 102 M.S.P.R. 102, ¶ 8 (2006). Further, the Board has found that, where an appellant raises a USERRA claim as an affirmative defense in an appeal of an adverse action that is either untimely or not within the Board's jurisdiction, the Board shall consider the appellant's allegations that the adverse action was taken in violation of USERRA as a separate claim. Id. Since there is no time limit for filing an appeal with the Board under USERRA, an appellant who has filed an untimely petition for appeal of an otherwise appealable action and alleged that, in taking that action, the agency discriminated on the basis of his status as a veteran in violation of USERRA, has filed a timely USERRA appeal. Holmes v. Department of Justice, 92 M.S.P.R. 377, ¶ 11 (2002). Thus, in this case, even if the appellant's adverse action appeal is dismissed on remand as untimely filed, or, alternatively, as not within the Board's adverse action jurisdiction, the Board may still consider his USERRA claim as a separate appeal. See id.

If the administrative judge finds on remand that the appellant's adverse action appeal will be dismissed as untimely filed or not within the Board's jurisdiction, then the administrative judge must examine whether the Board's USERRA jurisdiction provides an alternative basis to consider the appellant's allegation that the agency's actions violated USERRA. See Holmes, 92 M.S.P.R. 377, ¶ 8. To establish jurisdiction over a USERRA claim, an appellant must: (1) show performance of duty in a uniformed service of the United States; (2) allege a loss of benefit or employment; and (3) allege that the benefit was lost due to the performance of duty in the uniformed service. Yates v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 145 F.3d 1480, 1485 (Fed. Cir. 1998); Aguilar, 102 M.S.P.R. 102, ¶ 9; Holmes, 92 M.S.P.R. 377, ¶ 12.

If the administrative judge finds that the Board has jurisdiction over the appellant's USERRA claim, then the Board is exercising its jurisdiction to adjudicate the appeal as a complaint under 38 U.S.C. § 4324(c)(1)-(2). See Holmes, 92 M.S.P.R. 377, ¶ 12. Thus, the Board's jurisdiction to adjudicate the appellant's USERRA complaint is no different from the jurisdiction it exercises in a pure USERRA case where the appellant does not raise matters that may otherwise be appealed to the Board. Id. Therefore, the Board's authority with regard to this complaint would not extend beyond the alleged USERRA violations and would not allow for a decision on the merits of the appellant's removal, except where examination of those issues is necessary to address the appellant's USERRA claim. See id.

The Aguilar analysis, allowing a USERRA or VEOA appeal to proceed even if an adverse action appeal was untimely, was reconfirmed in Nahoney v. USPS, 112 MSPR 93, 98-99 ¶¶ 16-19, 2009 MSPB 150 (2009). [Refer to Chapter 1 for USERRA and VEOA appeals.]
