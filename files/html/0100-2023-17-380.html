4. Failure to Discuss All Charges, Evidence, Issues

FLRA takes the approach under NTEU, Chap. 24 and Dept. of Treasury, IRS, 50 FLRA 330, 332 (1995), that it reviews awards for legal error de novo, and to accomplish that task FLRA expects sufficient factfinding by an arbitrator to support the legal conclusions embraced by the award. See NFFE Local 1437 and Dept. of Army, ARDEC, 53 FLRA 1703, 1709-10 (1998). As a result of NTEU, NFFE Local 1437, and kindred cases, some earlier decisions of FLRA accepting extremely abbreviated factual and legal analyses by arbitrators would likely not pass muster if reviewed today. [Refer later in this chapter to "Contrary to Law; Award is Inconsistent With Law, Rule, or Regulation" and "Nature of the Exception."]

Assuming that the exceptions do not present legal issues requiring complex factual analysis, and assuming that a monetary award is not involved, FLRA traditionally defers to awards that resolve claims with or without complex analysis. An award is not deficient because the arbitrator fails to make specific findings of fact. PTO and POPA, 32 FLRA 1168, 1174 (1988); see Dept. of Army, Pine Bluff Arsenal and AFGE Local 953, 34 FLRA 293, 295 (1990) ("The Arbitrator was not obligated...to set forth findings or rationale more extensive than he did to support the award denying the grievance."). An arbitrator's failure to set forth specific findings or to specify and discuss all allegations in a grievance does not provide a basis for finding an award deficient. Int'l Org. of Masters, Mates and Pilots, Marine Div., ILA, Canal Pilots Branch and Panama Canal Comm'n, 39 FLRA 707, 715 (1991); see NLRB and NLRB Union, 48 FLRA 1337, 1346-47 (1994) ("Further, we note that "[a]n arbitrator's failure to set forth specific findings, or to specify and discuss all allegations in a grievance, does not provide a basis for finding an award deficient....In this regard, an arbitrator is not required to specify or discuss specific items of evidence on which an award is based or which otherwise were considered by the arbitrator."); NFFE Local 259 and Dept. of Army Corps of Eng'rs, Memphis Dist., 45 FLRA 773, 779 (1992) ("In making the overall finding...the Arbitrator was not required to address specifically every argument that the Agency violated the agreement..."); cf. DOD, DCMA and AFGE Local 2128, 59 FLRA 396, 404 (2003) (arbitrators are not obligated to provide rationale for their findings unless required to do so by contract, submission of parties, or law); accord, AFGE Nat. Border Patrol Council Local 2455 and DHS, C&BP, 62 FLRA 37, 39 (2007) (same as AFGE Local 2128). "[A]n arbitrator is not required to specify or discuss particular items of evidence that were considered." AFGE Local 1867 and Dept. of Air Force, Air Force Academy, 59 FLRA 737, 738 (2004).

Factual and legal findings may be implied from other findings, as FLRA determined concerning the assertion that the arbitrator failed to determine a claim of discrimination as to challenged performance rating, AFGE Local 3911 and EPA Region 2, 64 FLRA 686, 687 (2010):

When an exception involves an award's consistency with law, the Authority reviews any question of law raised by an exception and the award de novo. See NTEU, Chapter 24, 50 FLRA 330, 332 (1995) (citing U.S. Customs Serv. v. FLRA, 43 F.3d 682, 686-87 (D.C. Cir. 1994)). In applying the standard of de novo review, the Authority assesses whether an arbitrator's legal conclusions are consistent with the applicable legal standard. See U.S. Dep't of Def. Dep'ts of the Army & the Air Force, Ala. Nat'l Guard Northport, Ala., 55 FLRA 37, 40 (1998) (DOD). In this regard, an arbitrator's failure to apply a particular legal analysis "does not render [an] award deficient because...in applying the standard of de novo review, the Authority assesses whether the arbitrator's legal conclusions are consistent with law, based on the underlying factual findings." AFGE, Nat'l Border Patrol Council, 54 FLRA 905, 910 n.6 (1998). In making that assessment, the Authority defers to the arbitrator's underlying factual findings. DOD, 55 FLRA at 40.

The Arbitrator made factual findings that the Union had not demonstrated that the grievant's performance was rated improperly. As noted above, in analyzing whether the award is contrary to law, we defer to the Arbitrator's underlying factual findings. See DOD, 55 FLRA at 40. The Arbitrator's finding that the grievant's rating was not improper establishes that the Agency had a legitimate basis for its rating of the grievant. In this context, it follows that the Arbitrator's statement that the discrimination claims were "moot" is, effectively, a conclusion that the rating was not discriminatory. The Union provides no basis for finding this conclusion contrary to law. Accordingly, we deny the exception.

See Dept. of Navy, Naval Undersea Warfare Ctr. and NAGE Local R1-134, 64 FLRA 1136, 1140 (2010) ("The Authority has held that an arbitrator's failure to apply a particular legal analysis 'does not render [an] award deficient because...in applying the standard of de novo review, the Authority assesses whether the arbitrator's legal conclusions are consistent with law, based on the underlying factual findings.' AFGE, Nat'l Border Patrol Council, 54 FLRA 905, 910 n.6 (1998).").

Although the arbitrator's reasoning need not be lengthy, there must be some reasoning to explain whether a grievance has been sustained based on a violation of contract or law or regulation and, if so, what is the appropriate remedy. Even expedited arbitrations must conform to that requirement, SSA and AFGE Local 1923, 64 FLRA 516, 519 (2010):

We acknowledge that the parties' agreement does not require the Arbitrator in an expedited arbitration to state explicitly all of his findings as part of his decision. Thus, it is possible that the Arbitrator had a basis for setting aside the suspension that is not specified in his written award. However, we also note that Article 25, Section 7(C) of the parties' agreement states that the arbitrator "shall include a brief written explanation of the decision." It seems reasonable to infer that this requirement for a written explanation includes an unstated corollary requirement that such explanation be internally consistent, which this award is not. Because we are unable to determine whether the award is deficient in this regard, we remand the award to the parties for resubmission to the Arbitrator, absent settlement, for clarification of the basis for the remedy.

If the arbitrator discusses and disposes of the principal charge in a removal case against an employee, there is no requirement that he address expressly secondary charges without some requirement in the negotiated agreement. That his opinion accompanying the award does not mention the secondary charges does not establish that he did not consider or rule on them. OPM and AFGE Local 32, 9 FLRA 676, 679 (1982); see NAGE Local R5-66 and VAMC, Memphis, 40 FLRA 504, 509 (1991) (that arbitrator sustained discipline on basis of only one of several charges against grievant was not basis for setting award aside). The failure of an arbitrator to specifically discuss all bases of discipline concerning the disposition of an award contesting that discipline does not render his award deficient. It does not mean that he did not consider those issues. IRS, Philadelphia Region and NTEU, 12 FLRA 196, 197 (1983); see NAGE Local R5-66 and VAMC, Memphis, 40 FLRA 504, 508-10 (1991) (no basis to set aside the award when arbitrator did not issue specific findings and a rationale to support failure to find discipline untimely).

That an arbitrator does not specifically cite all the provisions of a contract argued to be applicable by a party does not invalidate the award. However, if an arbitrator fails to consider a critical provision of a contract, and that provision could reasonably require an opposite result from that reached in the award, the award will be remanded for resubmission to the arbitrator, AFGE Council 220 and SSA, Baltimore, 54 FLRA 156, 159-60 (1998):

In reviewing an arbitrator's interpretation of a collective bargaining agreement, the Statute provides that the Authority apply the deferential standard of review that Federal courts use in reviewing arbitration awards in the private sector. See 5 U.S.C. § 7122(a)(2). Under this standard, the Authority will find that an arbitration award is deficient as failing to draw its essence from the collective bargaining agreement when the appealing party establishes that the award: (1) cannot in any rational way be derived from the agreement; (2) is so unfounded in reason and fact and so unconnected with the wording and purposes of the collective bargaining agreement as to manifest an infidelity to the obligation of the arbitrator; (3) does not represent a plausible interpretation of the agreement; or (4) evidences a manifest disregard of the agreement. See United States Department of Labor (OSHA) and National Council of Field Labor Locals, 34 FLRA 573, 575 (1990). The Authority and the courts defer to arbitrators in this context "because it is the arbitrator's construction of the agreement for which the parties have bargained." Id. at 576.

Here, the Union does not claim that the arbitrator misinterpreted a controlling provision of the agreement. Rather, the Union claims that the issue resolved by the Arbitrator under Article 4 of the agreement is actually controlled by Article 7 of the agreement, which was not interpreted by the Arbitrator.

Article 7 of the agreement provides that negotiations to "add to, amend, or modify" the agreement may be "conducted only by mutual consent" of the parties. This requirement that negotiation over mid-term changes to the agreement be "only by mutual consent" would appear to limit "management-initiated changes" proposed under Article 4. However, without discussing Article 7, the Arbitrator found that management-initiated changes were "not limited."

It appears that there may be a conflict between the Arbitrator's interpretation of Article 4 and the terms of Article 7. At the very least, any interpretation of Article 4 must take into account the "mutual consent" provision of Article 7. The Arbitrator's decision fails to take this language into account. Absent findings by the Arbitrator on this matter, we are unable to determine whether the award is deficient.

In the private sector, where "the arbitrator fails to discuss critical contract terminology, which terminology might reasonably require an opposite result, the award cannot be considered to draw its essence from the contract." Cannelton Industries v. District 17, UMWA, 951 F.2d 591, 594 (4th Cir. 1991) (quoting Clinchfield Coal Co. v. District 28, UMWA, 720 F.2d 1365, 1369 (4th Cir. 1983)) (Cannelton Industries); Young Radiator Co. v. International Union, UAW, 734 F.2d 321, 326 n.5 (7th Cir. 1984) (Young Radiator). The practice of the courts in such circumstances of "critical ambiguity," Cannelton Industries, 951 F.2d at 594, is to remand the dispute to the arbitrator, despite the "general rule...that a reviewing court should either enforce or vacate an arbitration award," Young Radiator, 734 F.2d at 326.

The Authority has not previously decided a case where it appeared that there was an inconsistency between the agreement and the award at issue, but the relevant contract language was not interpreted by the arbitrator. However, no basis to depart from the private sector approach is argued or apparent. Accordingly, in this case and similar future cases, we will follow the practice in the private sector and remand such awards for the arbitrator to address the contract provision in dispute. A remand in such cases permits the arbitrator, who was the parties' choice to interpret and apply their agreement, to interpret in the first instance the provision that may be dispositive of the grievance.

In remanding the Arbitrator's award in this particular situation, we do not alter our practice of deferring to an arbitrator's interpretation of contract language and finding awards deficient when they fail to draw their essence from the parties' collective bargaining agreement. A remand is only appropriate in the unusual situation where it appears that the agreement and award are inconsistent, and the Authority is unable to assess the arbitrator's interpretation of relevant contract provisions.

The decisions just discussed concern allegations that the arbitrator failed to discuss some factual issues or that the arbitrator failed to resolve some subsidiary legal issues. What occurs when the arbitrator fails to resolve a principal claim that is before him, i.e., one of two or more disciplinary actions against a grievant? If the parties have stipulated that the arbitrator has jurisdiction over the claim, the arbitrator is not to disregard the stipulation, as Dept. of Treasury, IRS, Oklahoma City Dist. and NTEU Chap. 45, 37 FLRA 775, 781-82 (1990), remanding a case to the parties for resubmission to the arbitrator, explained:

The record in the case shows that the parties clearly stipulated the issues to be arbitrated, including the validity of the second 14-day suspension.... The Arbitrator had no authority to look behind the stipulation of the parties or to ignore the stipulation in deciding the case before him....

...

The Arbitrator was required to render his award on the issues that were properly stipulated by the parties.... [I]n light of the parties' express stipulation, the Agency's reference in its post-hearing brief to the issue of arbitrability regarding the second suspension...is insufficient to put the matter before the Arbitrator....
