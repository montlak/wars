h. Reclassification

305th AMW, McGuire AFB and AFGE Local 1778, 54 FLRA 1243, 1248-52 (1998), presented the interesting issue of whether "conditions of employment" included the classification of a union official who was downgraded when he switched shifts to handle union business. The job held during the shift the employee obtained did not, because of a difference in the complexity of the work, support the grade of the position on the shift that the employee earlier worked. FLRA decided that it could offer no remedy because the classification of the official's job was not an employment condition under the Statute:

[U]nder section 7116(a)(2), it is an unfair labor practice for an agency to "discourage membership in any labor organization by discrimination in connection with hiring, tenure, promotion, or other conditions of employment[.]" This prohibition is expressly limited to discrimination with respect to conditions of employment. Thus, it is clear that section 7116(a)(2) does not, in and of itself, prohibit discrimination with respect to matters other than "conditions of employment."

The Statute defines the term "conditions of employment" in section 7103(a)(14)(B). This definition expressly excludes matters "relating to the classification of any position." Based on this definition, the Statute, by its terms, excludes classification matters from the reach of section 7116(a)(2).

In construing section 7103(a)(14)(B), the Authority relies on the definitions of the term "classification" that appears in 5 C.F.R. § 511.101. See, e.g., International Federation of Professional and Technical Engineers, Local 49 and U.S. Department of the Army, Army Corps of Engineers, South Pacific Division, San Francisco, California, 52 FLRA 665, 667-68 (1996) (Corps of Engineers). Pursuant to this definition, "classification" means: "[t]he analysis and identification of a position and placing it in a class under the position-classification plan established by OPM [Office of Personnel Management]." Under the system established by OPM, classification entails the identification of the appropriate title, series and grade of a position. See 5 C.F.R. § 511.701(a) (emphasis added).

There is no dispute that, as found by the Judge below, the vice president's "downgrading...resulted from a reclassification." Further, the finding is consistent with the foregoing definition of the term "classification" and the record in this case. With respect to the latter, undisputed record evidence shows that the disputed action occurred after a supervisory personnel management specialist reviewed the position descriptions of the WG-10 and WG-11 boiler plant operator positions and compared them to the position classification standard issued by OPM for the position of boiler plant operator. Based on the OPM standard, the personnel specialist determined that the WG-11 duties were predicated on the occupant of that position performing operator-in-charge duties. The personnel specialist also determined that the vice president was not performing the grade-controlling duties of the WG-11 position. Thus, the disputed downgrade was based on examining the vice president's permanent position, and classifying the position at the WG-10 grade level. Consistent with Corps of Engineers, this was a classification action.

The General Counsel argues, nevertheless, that despite the statutory roadblock, the Authority's Letterkenny framework for establishing a violation of section 7116(a)(2) has been satisfied....

...In particular, the General Counsel contends that the burden under Letterkenny was satisfied by establishing that the vice president's invocation of his contractual right to a day schedule and his reclassification are "inextricably intertwined."

The plain wording of section 7116(a)(2) places on the General Counsel the obligation to establish discriminatory treatment of an employee involving a "condition of employment." Letterkenny does not alter this requirement. If the General Counsel does not identify a condition of employment that has been affected by discrimination, then a prima facie case has not been established, and any consideration of the agency's motivation is unnecessary.[3] The relationship, if any, between the invocation of contractual rights and the disputed downgrade does not alter the connection required by the Statute between the alleged discrimination and a condition of employment. The relationship also does not change the character of the disputed action from one that does not concern a condition of employment to one that does.

[3] In this regard, we need not decide whether to follow the General Counsel's suggestion that the Authority adopt the private sector doctrine that certain actions by an employer are considered "inherently destructive" of employee rights. That doctrine, which was described by the Supreme Court in Great Dane, 388 U.S. at 34, provides a method of establishing an unfair labor practice without proving that the employer acted with an improper motivation. In this case, the dispositive issue is not the motivation of the Respondent, but whether the Respondent's action falls within the statutory definition of conditions of employment. As such, Chair Segal and Member Cabaniss express no opinion on whether application of the doctrine would yield a different result in this case. In Member Wasserman's view, application of the doctrine would not yield a different result.

The General Counsel also contends that a conclusion that a classification matter does not fall within section 7116(a)(2) is inconsistent with prior Authority precedent holding that parties may advance bargaining proposals which address the impact and implementation of classification actions. See General Counsel's Opposition at 5 (citing March Air Force Base, Riverside, California, 13 FLRA 255 (1983) (March)). However, March specifically held that such bargaining could involve only "matters not directly related to the actual classification of a position[,]" id. at 260, and could not involve "the specific duties or any other matter to be considered in classifying such positions." Id. at 259. Here, the matter at issue is the Respondent's classification decision, itself. Thus, March is inapplicable here.

Finally, the General Counsel argues that matters relating to classification should not be excluded from the reach of section 7116 because Congress only intended to exclude such matters from the scope of contract negotiation. However, the General Counsel offers no support for finding that the definition of "conditions of employment" in section 7103(a)(14) of the Statute, either in whole or in part, does not apply to the defined term where it appears in section 7116(a)(2).

In this regard, we reject the General Counsel's contention that the legislative history of this exclusion "is a clear statement the Congress never intended to exclude an agency's decision to reclassify a particular individual" from the reach of section 7116(a)(2). The General Counsel points to Representative Udall's remark that "[t]he effect of this exclusion would be to remove the classification of positions from collective bargaining." 124 Cong. Rec. 29,183 (1978). There is no indication in this comment that the removal of classification matters from the scope of contract negotiations was the only effect of the exclusion. Further, as the Judge found, "[n]either Representative Udall's remarks nor the Authority's previous interpretation of these remarks negate the plain meaning of the statutory language."

Based on the foregoing, we hold that the Respondent's disputed action downgrading the vice president in this case concerns a "matter...relating to the classification of any position," within the meaning of section 7103(a)(14)(B). Accordingly, the Respondent's action does not concern a condition of employment and as such, does not constitute an unfair labor practice under section 7116(a)(2).[4]

[4] We note that the General Counsel did not allege that the Respondent's conduct constituted an independent violation of section 7116(a)(1). Accordingly, we make no finding as to whether such a violation might be established under the facts of this case. We also do not address whether the particular matter involved in this case would be excluded from the scope of the parties' negotiated grievance procedure under section 7121(c)(5) of the Statute, separate from an allegation regarding section 7116(a)(2).
