B. FORMAT

The formalities of the appeal are established at 5 CFR 1201.26 (2022):

§ 1201.26 Number of pleadings, service, and response.

(a) Number. The appellant must file two copies of both the appeal and all attachments with the appropriate Board office, unless the appellant files an appeal in electronic form under § 1201.14.

(b) Service

(1) Service by the Board. The appropriate office of the Board will mail a copy of the appeal to each party to the proceeding other than the appellant. It will attach to each copy a service list, consisting of a list of the names and addresses of the parties to the proceeding or their designated representatives.

(2) Service by the parties. The parties must serve on each other one copy of each pleading, as defined by § 1201.4(b), and all documents submitted with it, except for the appeal. They may do so by mail, by facsimile, by commercial or personal delivery, or by electronic filing in accordance with § 1201.14. Documents and pleadings must be served upon each party and each representative. A certificate of service stating how and when service was made must accompany each pleading. The parties must notify the appropriate Board office and one another, in writing, of any changes in the names, or addresses on the service list.

(c) Paper size. Pleadings and attachments must be filed on 8 1/2 by 11-inch paper, except for good cause shown. This requirement enables the Board to comply with standards established for U.S. courts. All electronic documents must be formatted so that they will print on 8 1/2 by 11-inch paper.

If the appellant has not filed a copy along with the original, the AJ may do the copying at the region and serve that copy. Before the 2012 regulation limiting attachments to an appeal, If the appeal and attachments were voluminous and the second copy of the appeal (with attachments) was not filed with Board, the AJ would likely reject the appeal and return it to the appellant with leave to refile (with the copy) within 15 days; or the AJ could decide, particularly if it is determined that it would be a personal or financial hardship for the appellant to provide copies, to copy and serve those materials. See Board Order 1050.10 (Oct. 27, 1988). The regulation does not say that one fax of an appeal is sufficient. In the author's experience, one fax is enough--the regulatory requirement of two copies of the appeal is not applied to fax filing.

Agencies usually give employees against whom they take action a copy of a Board form for filing an appeal. Although completion of the appeal form constitutes compliance with the regulatory provision, use of the form is not essential, and the form may be confusing because it is not tailored to the precise type of appeal that is being filed. The regulations state what information is required to initiate an appeal. Agencies often supply employees with copies of the Board's regulations along with their decision notices. The Board's regulations, as well as guidance to Board operations, are found on the Internet at www.mspb.gov. Although the appeal must contain the necessary information under the regulations, no appeal may be rejected for failure to use the MSPB form. An appeal can be submitted by letter (or a pleading with a caption) to the Board if it includes the regulatorily-prescribed information and attaches a copy of any pertinent decision notice and a signed designation of representative (if there is a representative). If an appeal lacks some regulatory information, it should not be rejected; rather, supplementation should be requested through an order directing the appellant to cure the defects in the appeal. If some information needed for the appeal is missing, avoid missing the deadline: file the appeal on time, although it may be incomplete; supplement it later. In recent years electronic filing is ever more widely used and it obviates the need to worry about the format and means of filing paper appeals and documentary attachments. All that the appellant needs to file electronically is a PDF file of the proposal and decision letters in an adverse action. Even then, if the documents are not then available, the electronic appeal can be filed and the associated documents can later be filed electronically, by fax, mail, or commercial or personal delivery to the regional office where the appeal is assigned.
