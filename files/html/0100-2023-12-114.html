g. Mitigation of Demotion to Suspension

As to demotions mitigated to suspensions, Grover v. Dept. of Treasury, 55 MSPR 622, 624 (1992), first determined that interim relief should have been the suspension of the employee starting on the effective date of the demotion, followed by return of the employee at his original grade level as of the date of the initial decision--a date that surpassed the length of the suspension imposed when the initial adverse action was imposed. The Board reopened Grover and reformulated the required interim relief, Grover v. Dept. of Treasury, 59 MSPR 58, 60 (1993):

In its Opinion and Order of November 25, 1992, the Board found that, to show that it has granted the appellant the interim relief ordered where the administrative judge mitigated the appellant's demotion to a suspension, as here, the agency must present evidence that it has returned the appellant to full pay status at his original grade and pay level by the date of the initial decision because the suspension would be retroactive to the date of the demotion. Grover v. Dept. of Treasury, 55 MSPR 622 (1992). This holding is in conflict with the holding in the compliance case of Schoeffler v. Dept. of Agric., 50 MSPR 143 (1991). We held in Schoeffler that it is not possible to retroactively suspend an employee whose demotion has been mitigated to a suspension, because he has been working for the agency during the duration of his demotion. 50 MSPR at 146. The suspension must occur prospectively.[2] Thus, we cannot conclude that the agency here failed to comply with the interim relief order by not simply returning the appellant to full duty status, or its interim relief equivalent, as of the date of the initial decision.

[2] In Schoeffler, 50 MSPR at 146, the Board ordered the agency to effect the suspension 20 days after the issuance of the Board's final order. An administrative judge must allow an agency 35 days from the date of an initial decision within which to effect a suspension, however, if the decision requires the agency to cancel a demotion, to substitute a suspension therefor, and to provide interim relief in the event that it seeks review of the decision. In order to comply fully with such a decision, the agency would have to cancel the demotion and then effect a suspension. If, however, the agency wished to seek review of the initial decision, it would instead be required to effect a temporary promotion and a suspension in order to fulfill its interim relief obligations; the agency would lose its right to seek review if it cancelled the demotion. See Edney v. Dept. of Treasury, 56 MSPR 248 (1993). Because an agency has 35 days within which to seek review of an initial decision, see 5 CFR 1201.114(d), it must be given the entire 35-day period within which to choose between complying fully with an initial decision that mitigates a demotion to a suspension, or filing a petition for review of the decision and complying with it on an interim basis. Thus, the suspension must be effective no later than 35 days after the date of the initial decision.

See Moore v. USPS, 63 MSPR 23, 25-26 (1994) (if a demotion is mitigated to a suspension that is then challenged through a PFR, the agency petition will be dismissed if the agency cannot prove that its interim relief to the appellant places him at a base pay level equal to the level of pay prior to the demotion. The agency must demonstrate to the Board, upon appellant's challenge, that it restored appellant's pay to his prior grade and step).
