a. Preservation of Objection

There is another wrinkle in procedures that counsel should understand if the record is to be adequately protected once an AJ refuses to approve a witness. The party whose witness is denied may be required to lodge one and possibly two separate objections to the unfavorable ruling. The appellant or agency should lodge an objection with the AJ (most frequently made during the prehearing conference) as to excluded witnesses. The appellant may not wait until adjudication is complete to object for the first time to the AJ's hearing-related rulings. White v. Dept. of Army, 32 MSPR 600, 602 (1987). Twine v. DHHS, 36 MSPR 388, 393 (1988), reiterated that after the AJ disapproves a witness, an objection must be made to the ruling if the disapproval is to be preserved for appellate review through a PFR. See Harris v. USPS, 100 MSPR 613, 619 n.7 (2005) ("The appellant also contends on review that the AJ wrongly disapproved witnesses who would have testified concerning her harmful error claim. Because the appellant did not preserve an objection to the AJ's ruling on witnesses, this issue is not properly before the Board."); see Biswas v. VA, DA-1221-15-0471-W-2, n.2 (NP 1/4/2023) ("On review, the appellant argues that the administrative judge improperly excluded the testimony of one of her proposed witnesses. After the administrative judge's ruling, he afforded the appellant an opportunity to submit a written objection, but she did not do so. Therefore, she failed to preserve the issue for review. Sanders v. Social Security Administration, 114 M.S.P.R. 487, ¶ 9 (2010). The appellant also argues on review that she was denied the opportunity to call a rebuttal witness. Again, the appellant did not object to the administrative judge's ruling and therefore she did not preserve that ruling for review. Sanders, 114 M.S.P.R. 487, ¶ 9."); Fulton v. Dept. of Army, 95 MSPR 79, 87 n.7 (2003) ("The appellant preserved his objection to the AJ's ruling on witnesses by filing a motion for reconsideration, which the AJ overruled at the beginning of the hearing. See Tarpley v. U.S. Postal Service, 37 MSPR 579, 581 (1988) (a party's failure to timely object to rulings on witnesses precludes his doing so on petition for review)."); Hanna v. Dept. of Labor, 80 MSPR 294, 302 ¶ 11 (1998) ("Given the appellant's failure to timely submit arguments regarding the administrative judge's rulings on his witnesses, we find that he is precluded from doing so on petition for review."); Wilson v. VA, 63 MSPR 63, 65 (1994) (when the appellant did not object to the AJ's decision not to permit a witness to testify, the appellant was precluded from arguing abuse of discretion on review); Faucher v. USPS, 36 MSPR 517, 520 (1988) ("To the extent that other witnesses requested by the appellant would have provided additional testimony in this vein, the appellant did not object to the administrative judge's refusal to hear them, and therefore, cannot now raise this issue as a basis for review.").

The party whose witnesses were denied (through a prehearing ruling) and who preserved an objection to the AJ's rulings should, if offered the opportunity, renew the request for the witness at the hearing to avoid abandoning the request. Yarborough v. DOJ, 32 MSPR 691, 694 (1987) (AJ informed the appellant that she could renew her request for witnesses following the presentation of other testimony at the hearing; "[b]ecause the appellant did not renew her request at the hearing, she is deemed to have abandoned it"); see Alaniz v. USPS, 100 MSPR 105, 109-110 ¶ 9 (2005) (appellant failed to preserve a witness request for consideration on PFR when the AJ disapproved two witnesses, invited the appellant to request reconsideration of those rulings during the hearing and the appellant did not make the reconsideration request at the hearing). The appellant should then object at the hearing to the exclusion or absence of the requested witness. Vires v. Dept. of Navy, 38 MSPR 569, 572 (1988).

As a practical matter, an AJ may deny a witness during the prehearing conference but state that, on request, the AJ will reconsider the ruling during the hearing. For a party to take advantage of that opportunity (or to request reconsideration even if the offer has not been made), counsel will need to remind himself or herself to make the request during the hearing. It is a good point to note in the well-organized representative's hearing notebook.

It is necessary to preserve objections to an AJ's refusal to approve a witness and to the exclusion of some testimony of an approved witness. If a party examines or cross-examines a witness and testimony is precluded because an objection is sustained, according to Valverde v. Dept. of Army, 40 MSPR 380, 384 (1989), the questioner is then supposed to object to the rulings prohibiting the line of inquiry. The party seeking to conduct the questioning "cannot wait until after the adjudication is complete to object for the first time to the judge's hearing-related rulings." Presumably the party seeking to obtain answers must proffer a reason for the attempted examination, in addition to an objection to the ruling made on the objection.

The Board may excuse the failure to object to the AJ's exclusion of witnesses if the AJ does not inform the party calling the witnesses that an objection may be lodged to the AJ's ruling, Milner v. DOJ, 77 MSPR 37, 45-46 (1997):

The administrative judge issued two prehearing conference orders in which she excluded some of the witnesses who were requested by the appellant and who the appellant alleged had relevant testimony concerning his discrimination claim. In neither order did the administrative judge afford the appellant an opportunity to object to those rulings. In his petition for review, the appellant contends that the administrative judge abused her discretion in excluding the requested witnesses. Inasmuch as there is no indication that the administrative judge afforded the appellant an opportunity below to object to her prehearing rulings, we will consider that contention. See Phillips v. Department of the Navy, 74 MSPR 53, 58 (1997); cf. Germino v. Department of Defense, 61 MSPR 683, 690 (1994) (the employee did not object to the administrative judge's ruling in his prehearing order excluding the requested witnesses "even though he was afforded an opportunity to do so"; therefore, the Board did not consider his untimely objection on petition for review), aff'd, 52 F.3d 345 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table).

Once in a while, the Board dispenses with all the procedural hurdles and allows the agency a remand to develop evidence on the strength of the agency's protestations of mistreatment presented in its PFR. In Clarke v. DHHS, 48 MSPR 566, 571 n.10 (1991), a Chapter 43 case, the issue was whether an agency was unduly restricted in the development of its case on remand. The Board again remanded the case on the basis of its observation:

The agency proffers with its petition for review a November 7, 1988 affidavit by its representative at the remand hearing. The representative states that he would have elicited testimony from the appellant's supervisor and submitted documentary evidence...on these issues, but for the administrative judge's ruling.

It was left unsaid in Clarke whether the agency protected its record through an offer of proof and objections when the proffered proof was rejected, or whether the Board relied upon the belated statements of what the representative really wanted to do at the hearing had he the chance.
