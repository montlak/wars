B. SCOPE OF REVIEW

5 USC 7703 sets the standard of judicial review:

(c) In any case filed in the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, the court shall review the record and hold unlawful and set aside any agency action, findings, or conclusions found to be--

(1) arbitrary, capricious, an abuse of discretion, or otherwise not in accordance with law;

(2) obtained without procedures required by law, rule, or regulation having been followed; or

(3) unsupported by substantial evidence;

except that in the case of discrimination brought under any section referred to in subsection (b)(2) of this section, the employee or applicant shall have the right to have the facts subject to trial de novo by the reviewing court.

Jones v. USPS, 216 Fed. Appx. 986, 987-88 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP), provides a straightforward, unelaborated statement of the review functions of the Federal Circuit and the Board in an adverse action case:

An agency must establish three things to sustain an adverse action against an employee. First, the agency must prove, by a preponderance of the evidence, that the charged conduct occurred. 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)(1)(B). Second, the agency must show a nexus between that conduct and the efficiency of the service. 5 U.S.C. § 7513(a); Hayes v. Department of Navy, 727 F.2d 1535, 1539 (Fed. Cir. 1984). Third, the penalty imposed must be reasonable. See Douglas v. Veterans Admin., 5 M.S.P.R. 280, 306-07 (1981). The Board gives plenary review to the agency's decision. We, in turn, must affirm the Board's decision unless it was arbitrary, capricious, an abuse of discretion, or otherwise not in accordance with law; obtained without procedures required by law, rule or regulation; or unsupported by substantial evidence. 5 U.S.C. § 7703(c); Rosete v. Office of Personnel Management, 48 F.3d 514, 516 (Fed. Cir. 1995).

See deLeon v. VA, ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2021 NP) ("An agency abuses its discretion when it renders a decision 'based on an erroneous interpretation of the law, on factual findings that are not supported by substantial evidence, or represents an unreasonable judgment in weighing relevant factors.' Star Fruits S.N.C. v. United States, 393 F.3d 1277, 1281 (Fed. Cir. 2005) (citation omitted).").

Whatever type of Board case is under review, 5 USC 7703(c) sets the standards for review of Board decisions by the court of appeals: "(1) arbitrary, capricious, an abuse of discretion, or otherwise not in accordance with law; (2) obtained without procedures required by law, rule, or regulation having been followed; or (3) unsupported by substantial evidence." Discrimination or mixed cases result in trials de novo before district courts, complete with juries on EEO issues. An appellate court does not engage in factfinding. See Middleton v. DOD, 185 F.3d 1374, 1383 (Fed. Cir. 1999) ("[A]s an appellate court, we may not find facts or assess credibility."); Doe v. Hampton, 566 F.2d 265 (D.C. Cir. 1977) (considering an appeal prior to the Reform Act: "Our review... is limited to scrutinizing the administrative record accreted as the adverse action proceeds along its tortuous course up through the various levels of appeal."), followed in Ward v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 00-3187) ("Judicial review of Board decisions is limited to a review of the administrative record."). A reviewing court may decide a particular result is necessary, without remand, if it is satisfied that the facts developed below require that result and that a remand for further factfinding is not necessary. See Delgado v. DOJ, 966 F.3d 556, 567 (7th Cir. 2020) (amended on petition for rehearing, Delgado v. DOJ, 977 F.3d 1295 (7th Cir. 2020)) (whistleblower reprisal appeal following earlier remand to Board: "Reviewing the record as a whole, we conclude that a remand on the agency's affirmative defense would waste both judicial and the parties' resources." [remanding for determination of relief due to the appellant]). When the parties apparently agreed that VA's response to an appeal should have included some information pertaining to claimed military leave, the court remanded to the Board for production and consideration of that evidence. See Colicelli v. VA, ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2021 NP).

For appeals involving VA employees brought under the Department of Veterans Affairs Accountability and Whistleblower Protection Act of 2017, the standard for review is set by 38 USC 714(d):

(5)(A) A decision of the Merit Systems Protection Board under paragraph (3) may be appealed to the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit pursuant to section 7703 of title 5 or to any court of appeals of competent jurisdiction pursuant to subsection (b)(1)(B) of such section.

(B) Any decision by such Court shall be in compliance with section 7462(f)(2) of this title.

Under 38 USC 7462(f):

(2) In any case in which judicial review is sought under this subsection, the court shall review the record and hold unlawful and set aside any agency action, finding, or conclusion found to be--

(A) arbitrary, capricious, an abuse of discretion, or otherwise not in accordance with law;

(B) obtained without procedures required by law, rule, or regulation having been followed; or

(C) unsupported by substantial evidence.

The Federal Circuit is not a finder of fact, although it may draw inferences from facts of record. See Webster v. Dept. of Army, 911 F.2d 679, 684 n.4 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (an appellate court may infer the findings necessary to arrive at the explicit findings of the prior tribunal); cf. Parodi v. OPM, 19 MSPR 233, 234 (1984) (in what appeared to be a compliance context, a Board opinion referenced the appointment of a Special Master by the Ninth Circuit to determine issues of fact raised by an OPM report to the court concerning an offer by the employing agency of a suitable position called for by a decision of the court). If the court believes that there is some matter of fact that needs to be, but was not, resolved by the Board, the court may remand the case for the necessary factual determination. In the words of the court: "As an appellate court, we do not and cannot engage in fact-finding or second-guessing about the record." Miller v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 85-764); Raxter v. Dept. of Labor, 248 Fed. Appx. 177, 179 n.1 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP) ("Congress has not permitted us, a court of appeals with limited jurisdiction--to engage in fact-finding in the first instance on appeal."). The Supreme Court explained in United States v. Taylor, 108 S. Ct. 2413, 2422 (1988), as to review of district court factual findings: "It is always difficult to review a cold appellate record and acquire a full understanding of all the facts, nuances, and attitudes that influence a trial judge's decisionmaking, and we undertake such close review with reluctance."

On occasion an appellate court may suggest a statutory deficiency and a legislative remedy, without intruding on the legislative province, as the D.C. Circuit noted in Polcover v. Secretary of Treasury, 477 F.2d 1223, 1228 (D.C. Cir. 1973), involving an analysis of the duplicative nature of district and circuit court judicial review prior to the Reform Act:

The chapeau of a legislator is not one that we, as members of the judiciary, can or should have stashed away in our wardrobe. We may, however, express concern over matters directly affecting our prompt and efficient dispensation of justice. Finding that Judge McGowan's marginal references of the past have been less than marginally successful in generating study, we have elevated them to the text.

If the MSPB detects a material distinction between two panel decisions of the Federal Circuit, it follows the first in time. Fatigato v. USPS, CH-0353-10-0972-I-1 (NP 12/15/2011), observed at n.4:

In Bledsoe v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 659 F.3d 1097, 1104 (Fed. Cir. 2011), the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit recently stated that the appellant must prove these elements by preponderant evidence to establish jurisdiction. The court previously found, however, that jurisdiction in a 5 C.F.R. § 353.305(c) appeal is established by nonfrivolous allegations. See Walley v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 279 F.3d 1010, 1019 (Fed. Cir. 2002), abrogated on other grounds by Garcia v. Department of Homeland Security, 437 F.3d 1322 (Fed. Cir. 2006). Because Walley preceded Bledsoe, we are required to follow the Walley standard. See, e.g., Newell Companies, Inc. v. Kenney Mfg. Co., 864 F.2d 757, 765 (Fed. Cir. 1988) ("This court has adopted the rule that prior decisions of a panel of the court are binding precedent on subsequent panels unless and until overturned in banc. ... Where there is direct conflict, the precedential decision is first.").

Cf. MacLean v. DHS, 754 Fed. Appx. 950, 954 (Fed. Cir. 2018 NP) ("This panel, however, does not have the authority to grant Mr. MacLean the relief that he desires. A panel cannot simply overrule its precedent. George E. Warren Corp. v. United States, 341 F.3d 1348, 1351-52 (Fed. Cir. 2003). To overrule a precedent, the court must rule en banc. Id.").
