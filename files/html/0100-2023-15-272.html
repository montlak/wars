b. Photocopier; Facsimile

Minor misuse of a photocopying machine warranted a 60-day suspension, not removal. The employee should have known that use of the equipment was unauthorized; the appellant was on notice of the policy because five months earlier he reviewed the agency's standards of conduct that prohibited unauthorized duplication. Moore v. Dept. of Army, 32 MSPR 277, 279 (1987) (potential for rehabilitation; 11 copies were made while off duty). Use of a photocopier by an employee who did not know that it was owned by the government, or who did not know that he needed permission to use it for copying personal documents, could establish a disciplinary offense. See Sternberg v. DODDS, 41 MSPR 46, 61-62 (1989), following remand, Sternberg v. DODDS, 52 MSPR 547, 558 (1992) (charge of unauthorized use of government property does not require a demonstration of intent; the lack of notice concerning permitted use of the property is to be considered in has to the penalty); Perrotti v. Dept. of Army, 18 MSPR 548, 550-51 (1984) (mitigating removal of an employee charged with misusing and allowing others to misuse government video equipment; the appellant was not on clear notice of the policy concerning use of the equipment and ceased use once he learned of the policy).

In a decision that discounted the significance of an agency's authorization of a practice that the agency apparently retrospectively challenged as improper, Rogers v. DOJ, 60 MSPR 377, 388-89 (1994), discipline was sustained for personal use of a government fax:

We find that the administrative judge did err in not sustaining the second charge regarding the improper use of the facsimile machine. Essentially, the appellant argued, and the administrative judge found, that the appellant had no reason to know that his conduct would be considered improper and a basis for charges of wrongdoing, because the practice had been to allow the personal use of such government equipment as long as the employees reimbursed the government. The uncontradicted testimony showed that the policy concerning the occasional, personal use of the government fax machine or the phones, in place at the time of these actions, allowed an employee to reimburse the government for personal messages or calls. Therefore, the appellant would not have been aware that his use was improper.

We find, however, that the administrative judge incorrectly related the issue of notice to the proof of the charge rather than as a factor in mitigation of the penalty. An agency is not required to prove intent to sustain a charge of misuse of the property. Sternberg v. Department of Defense Dependents Schools, 52 MSPR 547, 558-59 (1992). The lack of notice that such conduct will be considered wrongdoing is to be considered in assessing the reasonableness of the penalty imposed, not whether the charge has been proven. Id. Furthermore, the fact that the appellant either did, or intended to, ultimately pay for the use of the fax and the phone does not negate his improper usage. See Wenzel v. Department of the Interior, 33 MSPR 344, 353 (1987) (a charge of misusing government property by conducting personal business on government equipment may still be sustained regardless of whether the employee paid for such usage); Davis v. Department of the Army, 33 MSPR 223, 226-27 (1987) (charges of misuse sustained despite supervisory authorization of certain of the misuse). Therefore, we sustain the second charge of improper use of government property, relating to the fax machine.
