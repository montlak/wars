broida mspb Chapter 10: Reductions in Force Chapter 10: Reductions in Force, Subchapter 01: RIF Process, Section D: Competitive Level, Subsection 02: Challenge to Competitive Level, Part b: Testimony Concerning Duties
b. Testimony Concerning Duties

Although position descriptions are important determinants of competitive levels, they are not the only evidence that will be considered, Simonton v. Dept. of Army, 62 MSPR 30, 35-36 (1994):

...We find that whether positions are in the same competitive level is mainly determined by the official position descriptions, even though other evidence may also be relevant in light of the circumstances. In Schroeder v. Department of Transportation, 60 MSPR 566, 575-76 (1994), we found that there were apparently significant differences in the duties, qualification requirements, and working conditions to the extent that an incumbent of one position may not be able to successfully perform the critical elements of the other position without any abnormal loss of productivity. In making that determination, we relied solely on our review of the pleadings and of the position descriptions. Id. at 575-76. Similarly, we have relied mainly on a comparison of the official position descriptions in determining whether a position was actually abolished. See Holmes, 41 MSPR at 616 ("The Board will examine all of the record evidence, particularly the position descriptions, in making this determination").

That we have relied on the official position descriptions, however, does not mean that we have excluded other relevant evidence, including testimony, especially where such evidence sheds light on the position descriptions. See Marcinowsky, 35 MSPR at 10 (the applicable FPM provision includes agency examination of the critical elements and the performance standards, as well as the position descriptions; the administrative judge properly supported his finding, that the positions required different subject matter and technical expertise that could not be readily acquired, based on the statements of the agency official whom he credited); Lover, 23 MSPR at 409 (the position review contained a position description indicating that the appellant supervised three employees rather than one as claimed by the agency); Burbridge, 13 MSPR at 362, citing Wright v. Department of Commerce, 9 MSPB 109, 9 MSPR 472, 475 (1982) (the testimony and the agency admissions can form a sufficient basis for deciding the propriety of the competitive level absent the official position description). And, as noted above, the relevant FPM provision, subsection S-5-5d, states that the "essentially identical" determination is made "on the basis of the information available." Thus, it does not appear to limit consideration solely to the position descriptions.

See McKenna v. Dept. of Navy, 105 MSPR 373, 379 ¶ 13, 2007 MSPB 102 (2007) ("Position descriptions are significant evidence in determining whether positions should be in the same competitive level, but other evidence also may be relevant under the circumstances if it sheds light on the position descriptions. See Evans v. Department of the Navy, 64 M.S.P.R. 492, 495-96 (1994).").

Bateman v. Dept. of Navy, 64 MSPR 464, 468-69 (1994), again demonstrated the Board's willingness to consider testimony (and other pertinent position descriptions) when a competitive level is challenged:

We find, however, that the inquiry in these appeals requires consideration of more than the official position descriptions. While, in most instances, the official position description will serve as the primary evidence of the propriety of a competitive level, it is not the only evidence that the Board will consider. See Simonton v. Department of the Army, 62 MSPR 30, 35-36 (1994); Schroeder, 60 MSPR at 574-78.

...

...The administrative judge did not examine the position descriptions for the other Supervisory P & E positions and in fact refused to admit them into evidence. The administrative judge further failed to make any credibility determinations regarding the testimony of Baker, Beard, and Brimer, or reconcile their testimonies in any way. See Spithaler v. Office of Personnel Management, 2 MSPB 2, 1 MSPR 587, 589 (1980). For this reason, we find it necessary to remand this appeal to the regional office for a determination of whether the Supervisory P & E (General) competitive level of appellants Bateman and Disney was properly drawn or whether it should have included the other Supervisory P & E positions.

The Board provided another example of analysis of testimony and position descriptions, as well as historical evidence concerning establishment of positions alleged to be in the same competitive level, Anderson v. TVA, 77 MSPR 271, 277-78 (1998):

Whether positions are in the same competitive level for reduction-in-force purposes is determined mainly by the official position description, even though other evidence may also be relevant in light of the circumstances. Simonton v. Department of the Army, 62 MSPR 30, 35-36 (1994). Such other evidence is especially relevant where it "sheds light on" the position description. Id. A comparison of the position descriptions for the SC4 Project Control Engineer/SD-4 Project Control Specialist position and the appellant's SD-4 Project Control Specialist (Discipline Scheduler) position shows that the positions are not distinguished by pay schedule or working conditions. Both are SD-4 positions, and were thus in the same pay schedule, and both are performed under the same working conditions....

Further, the qualifications for the positions are substantially similar. Both positions require operational plant experience. The SC-4 Project Control Engineer/SD-4 Project Control Specialist position requires five years' experience in a nuclear power plant; the SD-4 Project Control Specialist (Discipline Scheduler) position requires seven years' experience, preferably in a nuclear power plant. Both positions require planning experience, and both have the medical and security requirements for unescorted access at a nuclear plant and the requirement that an incumbent be able to negotiate in confined and congested work areas in the plant. The conclusion that the qualifications of the two positions may be essentially the same is corroborated by evidence in addition to the position descriptions. During the hearing below, Andrew VanOsdale acknowledged that Discipline Schedulers met the qualifications for the SC-4 Project Control Engineer/SD-4 Project Control Specialist position through the duties of their position.

Finally, we compare the duties of the two positions to determine whether they are sufficiently similar that the agency may reassign the incumbent of the SD-4 Project Control Specialist (Discipline Scheduler) position to the SC-4 Project Control Engineer/SD-4 Project Control Specialist position without undue interruption. As noted above, the duties of the Discipline Scheduler position and the duties of the SC-4 Project Control Engineer/SD-4 Project Control Specialist position are not identical. However, the duties of these two positions appear to be substantially similar. All of the duties of the Discipline Scheduler position were incorporated into the duties of the SC-4 Project Control Engineer/SD-4 Project Control Specialist position. A comparison of the position descriptions of the two jobs shows that both require expertise regarding nuclear power plant system outages and the site integrated schedule. Both require the ability to do computerized planning scheduling. Both require the incumbent to work closely with diverse engineering disciplines, and to make recommendations to managers.

As noted above, the administrative judge found that the duties of the SC-4 Project Control Engineer/SD-4 Project Control Specialist position and the duties of the appellant's position were not so substantially similar that he could find that the Discipline Scheduler position existed both before and after the RIF. This finding, however, does not address whether the incumbent of the appellant's position could perform the duties of the SC-4 Project Control Engineer/SD-4 Project Control Specialist position "without undue interruption." See Disney, 67 MSPR at 567-68. Thus, the administrative judge's finding does not support a determination of whether the positions at issue should be in the same competitive level.

The agency's submissions and testimonial evidence do not identify the distinguishing features of the Discipline Scheduler position and the SC-4 Project Control Engineer/SD-4 Project Control Specialist position that it relied on to place the two positions in separate competitive levels. Thus, based on the record before us, we are unable to determine whether the agency met its burden to show that these two positions are sufficiently distinguishable as a matter of law to find that these positions were properly placed in separate competitive levels. As we explained earlier, however, before the agency presented its hearing testimony, the administrative judge stated that the parties had stipulated that the appellant's competitive level was established in accordance with 5 CFR § 351.403. Thus, the parties' evidence of record was not tailored to addressing the issue of whether the appellant's competitive level was properly drawn. Under these circumstances, we must remand the appeal to allow the parties to develop the record on this issue.

See Salazar v. Dept. of Transp., 60 MSPR 633, 645 (1994) ("Although the agency argues on review that the administrative judge failed to afford sufficient weight to the position descriptions in determining whether the positions should have been assigned to separate competitive levels, the administrative judge properly considered all of the evidence of record, including both the testimony and the position descriptions."); cf. Knight v. TVA, AT-0351-15-0817-I-1 (NP 9/30/2016) ("We credit both the testimony of the H.R. Generalist and the Director that the MES and MEFS positions differed in their fundamental responsibilities. Neither witness made prior inconsistent statements, had any apparent bias, and their testimony is not contradicted by other evidence. See Hillen v. Department of the Army, 35 M.S.P.R. 453, 458 (1987).").
