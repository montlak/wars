b. Service on Representative

The Board's regulations on service of pleadings and other documentation are somewhat indirect, but the agency should serve materials on both the appellant and his representative. The appellant serves material upon the agency's designated representative. E-filing makes the process automatic if everyone is an e-filer.

Under 5 CFR 1201.26(b) (2022):

(2) Service by the parties. The parties must serve on each other one copy of each pleading, as defined by § 1201.4(b), and all documents submitted with it, except for the appeal. They may do so by mail, by facsimile, by commercial or personal delivery, or by electronic filing in accordance with § 1201.14. Documents and pleadings must be served upon each party and each representative. A certificate of service stating how and when service was made must accompany each pleading. The parties must notify the appropriate Board office and one another, in writing, of any changes in the names, or addresses on the service list.

Pleadings are defined at 5 CFR 1201.4 (2022):

(b) Pleading. Written submission setting out claims, allegations, arguments, or evidence. Pleadings include briefs, motions, petitions, attachments, and responses.

Under earlier regulations (requiring service "on each party") service on the designated representative was deemed service on the appellant, Smallwood v. OPM, 21 MSPR 302, 304 (1984):

...Since the applicable regulation at 5 CFR § 1201.26(b)(1) provides for service on the parties or their designated representatives, we find no error in the presiding official's failure to serve the hearing notice on the appellant. In considering the issue of whether service upon an appellant's designated representative was sufficient to start the running of the thirty-day period specified in 5 USC § 7703(b)(1), the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit held that "the board does not appear to be obligated by its own regulations to serve anyone other than an appellant's representative if one has been properly designated and such designation has not been revoked." Gragg v. United States, 717 F.2d 1343, 1345 (Fed. Cir. 1983). That court further held that a party's designation of a representative is binding on the designating party for service purposes unless and until expressly changed in writing by that party. Id. In light of the fact that the appellant in the instant case designated a representative and did not revoke that designation, we find that service of the hearing notice on the appellant's representative was binding on the appellant.[6] ...

[6] In Page v. Federal Aviation Administration, 3 MSPB 295 (1980), the Board found that service of the hearing notice on the appellant's representative was not binding on the appellant. This decision does not govern the instant case, however, since it was based on the Board's prior regulations, not applicable to this appeal, which the Board interpreted as requiring service on both the appellant and his designated representative.

If an AJ does not precisely follow the designation of representative and instead sends material to the original representative, no harm occurs unless there is demonstrable prejudice. See Williams v. Dept. of Army, 38 MSPR 496, 498 (1988) (original union representative transferred the case to another representative, who represented the appellant during a prehearing conference call and hearing; no error when the AJ served the initial decision on the latter representative).

Although the situation does not often occur, in Pace v. OPM, 117 MSPR 49, 54-55 ¶¶ 15-17, 2011 MSPB 86 (2011), the Board admonished OPM for dealing directly with an appellant in a disability retirement appeal and bypassing the appellant's counsel:

It is undisputed that the appellant was covered by life insurance at the time of his termination. In OPM's view, its October 22, 2010 letter to the appellant was sufficient notice that, if the appellant no longer wanted the insurance, he must make a written election to cancel it. OPM states that "because the appellant did not respond to this request by January 7, 2011, OPM processed the appellant's annuity with life insurance coverage." Id. We disagree with OPM's assessment. The appellant, through his attorney, contacted OPM prior to January 7, 2011, with phone calls, letters, and eventually the instant petition for enforcement which was filed on December 22, 2010. OPM disregarded all of these communications, which were relevant to the question of whether the appellant desired to carry life insurance, and did not even respond to the petition for enforcement until the case was referred to the full Board.

Pursuant to the Board's final order and its regulations, OPM was required to inform the appellant promptly of the actions it had taken to comply with the Board's order, and to notify the appellant when it believed it had completed its compliance. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.181(b). Where, as here, the appellant has a representative, documents must be served on the representative as well as the appellant. Id. at §§ 1201.26(b)(2), 1201.183(a). The service requirement is especially important when an appellant is mentally incompetent and relying on the assistance of counsel. "[I]t is patently unreasonable and fundamentally unfair to require or allow an incompetent to act as advocate in such a setting where even a sane attorney would be confronted with a difficult task." French v. Office of Personnel Management, 810 F.2d 1118, 1119 (Fed. Cir. 1987). OPM was on notice from two final Board decisions that found the appellant to be mentally incompetent and suffering a debilitating brain disease, as well as the regular correspondence from the appellant's attorney, that the appellant required the assistance of his chosen representative. OPM nevertheless failed to provide the appellant's representative with vital documents relating to the election of life insurance during the appellant's retirement, and it ignored the representative's diligent attempts to communicate with OPM about the computation of the annuity.

Under these circumstances, we find that OPM erred by not contacting the appellant's representative before computing the appellant's disability retirement annuity. If OPM had contacted the appellant's representative, it would have found, as the administrative judge did in her recommendation, that the deductions for life insurance were "contrary to the appellant's wishes of not carrying life insurance into his retirement."
