B. PRIMA FACIE CASE; ACCOMMODATION REQUIREMENTS

Discrimination based on a disabling condition is an affirmative defense to be pleaded and proved. See Kosanowsky v. Dept. of Army, 659 F. Supp. 872, 876 (S.D.N.Y. 1987) (in an MSPB case, disability discrimination is an affirmative defense to be pleaded and proved by the party having the burden of presenting the defense; MSPB had no authority to raise the defense sua sponte). But see McKowen v. DLA, 10 MSPR 98, 101 (1982) (principle of sua sponte consideration under circumstances that would otherwise work manifest injustice against the appellant applies to the affirmative defense of disability discrimination; the circumstances were not present, however, in appeal).

Pridgen v. OMB, 2022 MSPB 31, ¶¶ 35-37, 40, 42 (2022), outlined the elements of a disability discrimination affirmative defense in a Chapter 43 appeal (an analysis distinct from an assertion that reasonable accommodation was not provided to the disabled person). Pridgen also explained that the remedies available for disparate treatment disability discrimination will vary based on the level of causation; when disability discrimination is a but-for cause of the personnel action, full relief, including reinstatement, back pay, and damages, is available. When disability discrimination is merely a motivating factor, but not a but-for cause, injunctive or other forward-looking relief is available:

The Board adjudicates claims of disability discrimination raised in connection with an otherwise appealable action under the substantive standards of section 501 of the Rehabilitation Act. The standards under the ADA, as amended by the Americans with Disabilities Act Amendments Act of 2008 (ADAAA), have been incorporated by reference into the Rehabilitation Act, and the Board applies them to determine whether there has been a Rehabilitation Act violation. 29 U.S.C. § 791(f); Miller v. Department of the Army, 121 M.S.P.R. 189, ¶ 13 n.3 (2014). The ADA originally prohibited discrimination "because of" an individual's disability; Congress changed this language in the ADAAA to prohibit discrimination "on the basis of disability." ADAAA, Pub. L. No. 110-325, § 5(a), 122 Stat. 3553, 3557. Because the ADAAA went into effect prior to the events in this matter, we apply the standards set forth in the ADA as amended.

The administrative judge found that the appellant proved she was regarded as disabled, and thus was an individual with a disability for purposes of the ADA. We clarify that the appellant met her burden to prove she was an individual with a disability by proving that she was actually disabled.

To prove disability discrimination under the ADA, the appellant must establish that she is an individual with a disability as that term is defined in the ADA and Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) regulations. Thome v. Department of Homeland Security, 122 M.S.P.R. 315, ¶ 24 (2015). She may prove that she has a disability by showing that she: (1) has a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities; (2) has a record of such impairment; or (3) is regarded as having such an impairment. 42 U.S.C. § 12102(1); Thome, 122 M.S.P.R. 315, ¶ 24; 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(g)(1). Major life activities include the operation of major bodily functions, such as bowel functions. 42 U.S.C. § 12102(2)(B); 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(i)(1)(ii). The determination of whether a condition is substantially limiting is made by comparing the ability of the allegedly disabled individual to the abilities of "most people in the general population." 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(j)(1)(ii). The "substantially limits" standard is not "demanding," and is intended to "be construed broadly in favor of expansive coverage." 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(j)(1)(i).

...The EEOC and Federal circuit courts have disagreed regarding the level of causation an employee must meet to prove discrimination "on the basis of disability." See, e.g., Natofsky v. City of New York, 921 F.3d 337, 350 (2d Cir. 2019) ("We conclude that ‘on the basis of' in the ADA requires a but-for causation standard."); Monroe v. Indiana Department of Transportation, 871 F.3d 495, 503-04 (7th Cir. 2017) (using the "but-for" causation standard for a case arising after the ADAAA); Oehmke v. Medtronic, Inc., 844 F.3d 748, 757 n.6 (8th Cir. 2016) (declining to resolve whether a motivating factor or "but-for" causation standard applies to disability discrimination claims under the ADA as amended); Gentry v. East West Partners Club Management Co., Inc., 816 F.3d 228, 235-36 (4th Cir. 2016) (applying a "but-for" causation standard to a disability discrimination claim); Hoffman v. Baylor Health Care System, 597 F. App'x 231, 235 n.12 (5th Cir. 2015) (applying a motivating factor causation standard); Renee L. v. Department of Commerce, EEOC Appeal No. 0120141032, 2017 WL 1315387, at *17 (Mar. 29, 2017) (applying a motivating factor causation standard). The Board generally defers to the EEOC on issues of substantive discrimination law unless the EEOC's decision rests on civil service law for its support or is so unreasonable that it amounts to a violation of civil service law. Southerland v. Department of Defense, 119 M.S.P.R. 566, ¶ 20 (2013). In light of the split among the circuit courts, we find it appropriate to defer to the EEOC's use of a motivating factor causation standard. However, as explained below, to obtain full relief, an appellant must show that disability discrimination was a but-for cause of the personnel action.

...

In analyzing the appellant's disparate treatment disability discrimination claim, the administrative judge shall apply the same standards of proof set forth above regarding the appellant's Title VII claims, and the appellant may use the same methods of proof applicable to such claims. See Hoffman v. Caterpillar, Inc., 256 F.3d 568, 572 (7th Cir. 2001); Davis v. Department of the Treasury, EEOC Appeal No. 01A10591, 2002 WL 31014612, at *2 (Aug. 30, 2002). Also, as with Title VII claims, the remedies available for disparate treatment disability discrimination will vary based on the level of causation. When disability discrimination is a but-for cause of the personnel action, full relief, including reinstatement, back pay, and damages, is available. When disability discrimination is merely a motivating factor, but not a but-for cause, injunctive or other forward-looking relief is available. See Southerland, 119 M.S.P.R. 566, ¶¶ 23-25; Brenton W. v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 2020000957, 2021 WL 3792957, at *3 (Aug. 4, 2021); see also 42 U.S.C. § 12117(a) (incorporating, by reference, the remedy provisions of Title VII into the ADA).

Pridgen v. OMB, 2022 MSPB 31, ¶¶ 23-26 (2022), supra, described the forms of proof used to establish discrimination:

One may prove discrimination under these different standards of proof by various methods. No one method is the exclusive path to a finding of liability. We take the opportunity to explain the methods of proof by which an appellant may prove discrimination as an affirmative defense, and clarify Savage v. Department of the Army, 122 M.S.P.R. 612 (2015), and Gardner v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 123 M.S.P.R. 647 (2016), to the extent that they could be read to suggest otherwise.

The methods by which an appellant may prove a claim of discrimination under Title VII are: (1) direct evidence; (2) circumstantial evidence, which may include (a) evidence of "suspicious timing, ambiguous statements oral or written, behavior toward or comments directed at other employees in the protected group, and other bits and pieces from which an inference of discriminatory intent might be drawn," also known as "convincing mosaic"; (b) comparator evidence, consisting of "evidence, whether or not rigorously statistical, that employees similarly situated to the plaintiff other than in the characteristic...on which an employer is forbidden to base a difference in treatment received systematically better treatment"; (c) evidence that the agency's stated reason for its action is "unworthy of belief, a mere pretext for discrimination" (i.e., the burden-shifting standard under McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792, 802-04 (1973)); and (3) some combination of direct and indirect evidence. Troupe v. May Department Stores Co., 20 F.3d 734, 736 (7th Cir. 1994); see also Ortiz v. Werner Enterprises, Inc., 834 F.3d 760, 764-65 (7th Cir. 2016) (stating that "the use of disparate methods and the search for elusive [convincing] mosaics has complicated and sidetracked employment-discrimination litigation for many years" and explaining that Troupe used "mosaic" as a metaphor that was designed to displace the direct and indirect methods, rather than add a separate legal test to them). None of the above types of evidence, i.e., direct, "convincing mosaic," comparator, or pretext, will be needed in every case. "Each type of evidence," the Seventh Circuit explained in Troupe, "is sufficient by itself...to support a judgment for [the employee]; or they can be used together." Id. When an appellant raises an affirmative defense of disparate treatment discrimination under Title VII, the administrative judge should notify her of the various standards and methods of proof, including the respective levels of relief available under each standard.[5] See Alarid v. Department of the Army, 122 M.S.P.R. 600, ¶ 17 (2015).

[5] In particular, appellants must be instructed that to obtain the full panoply of relief under Title VII, they must prove "but-for" discrimination regarding the end result of an employment decision, and it may be proven through any of the methods stated above.

Cf. Walton v. Dept. of Army, CH-0752-15-0550-I-1 (NP 4/27/2023) ("'convincing mosaic' merely describes one type of evidence that, either alone or in combination with other evidence, an appellant may introduce in support of his claim. See Pridgen, 2022 MSPB 31, ¶ 24.").

As explained above, one way an appellant may establish a discrimination claim is through comparator evidence, or evidence relating to the treatment of similarly situated employees. See Fox v. Bond v. Dept. of Energy, 82 MSPR 534, 539-40 ¶¶ 13-14 (1999), presents a general analysis of a disability discrimination affirmative defense tied to the EEOC regulations:

To prove disability discrimination, the appellant must show, inter alia, that he is disabled or perceived to be disabled; and that he is otherwise qualified, with or without reasonable accommodation, for the position in question. See, e.g., Hunter v. Department of the Air Force, 77 MSPR 589, 595 (1998). An appellant may show that he is disabled by showing, inter alia, that he suffers from a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more of his major life activities. See, e.g., Robinson v. Department of the Air Force, 77 MSPR 486, 490 (1998); see also Bragdon v. Abbott, 118 S. Ct. 2196, 2202 (1998); 2 EEOC Compl. Man. (BNA) § 902 at 3-5 (1995).[1]

[1] Bragdon and the EEOC Compliance Manual specifically address discrimination under the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 (ADA). However, the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission has noted that, under the Rehabilitation Act Amendments of 1992, the ADA definition of the term "disability" also applies to sections 501, 503, and 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973. See 2 EEOC Compl. Man. (BNA) § 902 at 1 n.3.

"Major life activity" means a function such as caring for one's self, performing manual tasks, walking, seeing, hearing, speaking, breathing, learning, working, sitting, standing, lifting, and reaching. See, e.g., Patterson v. Department of the Air Force, 74 MSPR 648, 659-60 (1997). A person is substantially limited in a major life activity if he is unable to perform the activity as compared to the average person in the general population; or is significantly restricted as to the condition, manner, or duration under which he can perform a particular major life activity as compared to the condition, manner, or duration under which the average person in the general population can perform that same major life activity. Id. at 660. Three factors are considered in determining whether a person's impairment substantially limits a major life activity: its nature and severity; how long it will last or is expected to last; and, its permanent or long term impact, or expected impact. See, e.g., Bell v. Babbitt, EEOC No. 01964882 at 3 (Sept. 4, 1998).

Morrison v. Dept. of Navy, DC-0752-09-0758-I-2 (NP 3/9/2010), provided an analysis of the appellant's burden of proof as to an affirmative defense of disability discrimination under the ADA Amendments Act of 2008 (ADAAA) based on a failure to accommodate claim:

As a federal employee, the appellant's claim arises under the Rehabilitation Act of 1973. However, the regulatory standards for the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) have been incorporated by reference into the Rehabilitation Act, and they are applied to determine whether there has been a Rehabilitation Act violation. See 29 U.S.C. § 791(g); Pinegar v. Federal Election Commission, 105 M.S.P.R. 677, ¶ 36 n.3 (2007); 29 C.F.R. § 1614.203(b). Further, the ADA regulations superseded the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission's (EEOC's) regulations under the Rehabilitation Act. See Collins v. U.S. Postal Service, 100 M.S.P.R. 332, ¶¶ 7-8 (2005) (stating that 29 C.F.R. § 1614.203(g) and other portions of the regulations at 29 C.F.R. § 1614.203 were repealed on June 20, 2002, and the ADA regulations at 29 C.F.R. part 1630 were made applicable to cases under the Rehabilitation Act); 29 C.F.R. § 1614.203(b).

In order to prove a failure to accommodate a disability discrimination claim, the appellant would have to prove that he is a disabled person, that the action appealed was based on his disability and, to the extent possible, he must articulate a reasonable accommodation under which he believes he could perform the essential duties of his position or of a vacant funded position to which he could be reassigned. Sanders v. Social Security Administration, 114 M.S.P.R. 487, ¶ 16 (2010); Henson v. U.S. Postal Service, 110 M.S.P.R. 624, ¶ 6 (2009). The ADA Amendments Act of 2008 (ADAAA), which liberalized the definition of disability, became effective on January 1, 2009. See P.L. No. 110-325, 122 Stat. 3553 (2008), codified at 42 U.S.C. § 12101 et seq. The appellant was removed effective July 10, 2009, and the provisions of the ADAAA are therefore applicable to this case. The ADAAA defines "disability" to mean: "a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities of such individual; a record of such an impairment; or being regarded as having such an impairment (as described in paragraph (3))." See 42 U.S.C. § 12102(1)(A)-(C). "Major Life Activities" is defined in general to include, but not limited to, "caring for oneself, performing manual tasks, seeing, hearing, eating, sleeping, walking, standing, lifting, bending, speaking, breathing, learning, reading, concentrating, thinking, communicating, and working." See 42 U.S.C. § 12102(2)(A).

The ADAAA prohibits discrimination "against a qualified individual on the basis of disability in regard to job application procedures, the hiring, advancement, or discharge of employees, employee compensation, job training, and other terms, conditions, and privileges of employment." See 42 U.S.C. § 12112(a). The ADAAA defines "qualified individual," in part, to mean "an individual who, with or without reasonable accommodation, can perform the essential functions of the employment position that such individual holds or desires." See 42 U.S.C. § 12111(8). A reasonable accommodation is intended to allow an employee with the necessary job skills to overcome his disability so as to be able to fully perform the essential function of his position. See Gonzalez-Acosta v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 113 M.S.P.R. 277, ¶¶ 13-17 (2010); Clemens v. Department of the Army, 104 M.S.P.R. 362, ¶ 13 (2006). Guidance regarding the terms reasonable accommodation and undue hardship is provided by the EEOC's regulations at 5 C.F.R. § 1630(2)(o)&(p). Those regulations have not been the subject of the EEOC's revision as a result of the ADAAA. See 74 Fed. Reg. 48431 (Sept. 23. 2009).

The employee must show that he is a qualified individual with a disability, under (the current) regulation, 29 CFR 1630.2(m) (2022). Under Board definitions, and under preexisting EEOC regulatory definitions, the appellant asserting this status must prove: that he could perform the essential functions of the job with or without accommodation; and that, if appropriate, he can articulate reasonable accommodation that the agency should have made in order to enable him to perform successfully without endangering the health and safety of himself or others. The burden of proof to establish the defense is on the employee and it does not shift to the employer. "The burden of proof is on the employee to inform the employer of his needs, and he cannot shift to the employer all responsibility for accommodation." Clancy v. Dept. of Navy, 6 MSPR 196, 200, 6 MSPB 173 (1981); see Morgan v. USPS, 38 MSPR 676, 677 (1988) (failure to articulate an accommodation--the permanent restructuring of a position or the modification of a work schedule in a manner consistent with medical restrictions); Gallagher v. USPS, 31 MSPR 53, 55 (1986) (appellant must articulate, to the extent possible, a reasonable accommodation that would allow him to perform the essential functions of his position without endangering himself or others). Although knowledge of a disability, and the need for accommodation, must somehow be imparted to the employer to establish an accommodative requirement, the employer's knowledge of a condition is not necessary to establishing the existence of a disability under the ADAAA. McNab v. Dept. of Army, 121 MSPR 661, 666-67 ¶ 8, 2014 MSPB 79 (2014) (Vice Chairman Wagner concurring and dissenting in part).

As to a failure to accommodate claim, the employee must show that the requested accommodation will enable him to perform essential job functions or duties. West v. USPS, 44 MSPR 551, 559 (1990) (the provision of an assigned parking space, requested by an employee, was insufficient to address the condition, multiple sclerosis, that was determined by the agency to prevent the appellant from carrying out his law enforcement duties as a Postal Inspector). Emphasizing the need for the employee to perform the essential functions of his job, with or without accommodation, the Board noted in Byrne v. Dept. of Labor, 106 MSPR 43, 46 ¶ 7, 2007 MSPB 143 (2007), reviewing an arbitration award that sustained a performance-based removal involving a disability defense based on an alleged mental disability:

...Specifically, reasonable accommodation does not require an agency to lower production or performance standards, as the appellant had requested, and, upon determining that the appellant was not capable of meeting the productivity requirements of his position, the arbitrator correctly concluded that the appellant was not a qualified individual with a disability under the Americans with Disabilities Act because he could not perform the essential functions of his position with or without reasonable accommodation. See Clemens v. Department of the Army, 104 M.S.P.R. 362, ¶¶ 13, 27 (2006).

See SSA v. Abrams, CB-7521-13-0008-T-1 (NP 11/17/2022) (SSA not required to lower its production requirements for an ALJ to accommodate a disability).

Worthen v. VA, SF-0432-14-0539-I-1 (NP 6/26/2015), concluded the appellant was properly accommodated during a performance improvement period for the effects of injuries:

...An agency is required to make a reasonable accommodation to the known physical and mental limitations of an otherwise qualified individual with a disability unless the agency can show that accommodation would cause an undue hardship on its business operations. 29 C.F.R. § 1630.9(a). Reasonable accommodation includes modifications to the manner in which a position is customarily performed to enable a qualified individual with a disability to perform the essential job functions. Miller v. Department of the Army, 121 M.S.P.R. 189, ¶ 13 (2014). To establish disability discrimination, an employee must show that: (1) he is an individual with a disability, as defined by 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(g); (2) he is a qualified individual with a disability pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(m); and (3) the agency failed to provide a reasonable accommodation. Id.

The employee need not be certified as disabled by a state agency. See Vaughn v. USPS, 33 MSPR 496, 501 (1987). A designation of disability, as may occur when an individual has a veterans disability rating, does not establish a basis for an accommodation unless the accommodation is shown to be necessary under the ADA, Penn v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-11-0166-I-1 (NP 10/27/2011):

The appellant disagrees with the administrative judge's finding that he voluntarily retired. Among other things, he reasserts that the agency failed to help him maintain his employment and that, as a veteran with a service-connected disability, his request for a reasonable accommodation should have been evaluated differently than requests made by "civilians." Contrary to the appellant's assertions, neither the Board's decision in Hesse v. Department of the Army, 104 M.S.P.R. 647 (2007), nor Executive Order 13,164, nor any other documents that he cited support this proposition. While agencies must provide reasonable accommodation to the known limitations of a qualified individual with a disability unless doing so would create an undue hardship, 42 U.S.C. §§ 12112(a), (b)(5)(A); Simpson v. U.S. Postal Service, 113 M.S.P.R. 346, ¶ 13 (2010); 29 C.F.R. § 1630.9, agencies are not required to automatically approve all accommodation requests made by veterans with service-connected disabilities or to treat such requests differently than those made by individuals without a service-connected disability.

Assessment of what constitutes the "essential functions" of a position will include, for a job that can be rotated among employees, the number of other employees available to fill in for the appellant, the appellant's position description, and the question of whether all employees in the appellant's position actually rotate among the various duty stations. Trammell v. VA, 60 MSPR 79, 86 (1993). A decision casting disability in terms of employability within "the geographical area to which the employee has reasonable access" is Macaranas v. USPS, 48 MSPR 323, 326 (1991).

Discrimination issues may involve physical or mental inability to perform work. The question of who is a "disabled" person is, of necessity, an individualized one, resolved case by case. Fuentes v. USPS, 54 MSPR 4, 10 (1992); Stanley v. DOJ, 48 MSPR 1, 6 (1991). The existence of a mental or physical impairment alone is not sufficient to constitute a disabling condition. Kelly v. DHHS, 46 MSPR 358, 360 (1990). According to Stanley, 48 MSPR at 6-7:

...Relevant to the inquiry are "the number and type of jobs from which the impaired individual is disqualified, the geographical area to which the individual has reasonable access, and the individual's job expectations and training."... Further, an employee's inability to meet the demands of a particular job because of an actual or perceived impairment does not necessarily mean that the employee is handicapped. The impairment must foreclose generally the type of employment involved....

Cf. McAlexander v. DOD, 105 MSPR 384, 390 ¶ 14, 2007 MSPB 103 (2007) ("Although Dr. Cook did not examine the appellant herself, her opinion is based on reports from two audiologists who did examine him. Thus, her assessment is 'individualized'; it was not a summary determination that he should be excluded from the Police Officer job merely because he failed the hearing test.").

Although the Pridgen decision, supra, attempted to simplify, or better articulate, the analysis the Board's definitions of the order and burden of proof in disability discrimination cases have become convoluted, so it may be instructive, or at least more succinct, if before the Board law is tackled, mention is made of the discussion of disability discrimination in Lynch v. Dept. of Educ., 31 MSPR 519 (Spec. Pan. 1986), on appeal from 31 MSPR 627 (1986), vacated, Lynch v. Bennett, 665 F. Supp. 62 (D.D.C. 1987), a mixed case, performance-based decision of the Special Panel. Lynch provides an analytical model followed in Board litigation, although the structure of the model, as will be seen, changes somewhat during the course of the litigation as one result or another is announced and justified by adjudicators. Of disability discrimination involving an attorney, afflicted by epilepsy, whose performance and attendance problems led to his removal, the Special Panel majority determined in Lynch, 31 MSPR at 527-30, over a dissent of the EEOC representative concerning application of the principles stated by the majority:

To establish a handicap discrimination defense, the appellant must show that he was a qualified handicapped employee, i.e., that he was able to perform the essential functions of his position with or without reasonable accommodation of his handicapping condition.... The burden then shifts to the employing agency to prove inability to accommodate.... Once credible evidence of such inability to accommodate is presented, the appellant must advance "evidence concerning his individual capabilities and suggestions for possible accommodations" in rebuttal....

The existence of a handicap alone is insufficient to establish a prima facie case that an applicant or employee is a qualified handicapped employee...." To sustain [a] prima facie case, there should also be a facial showing or at least plausible reasons to believe that the handicap can be accommodated...." The EEOC's implementing regulations concerning the term "qualified handicapped employee" require that such an employee be able to perform the essential functions of the job, with or without reasonable accommodation. [29 CFR 1614.203(a)(6)].A qualified handicapped employee who performs the essential functions of the job is one who can fully perform the required work given accommodation....

Assuming that an employee establishes status as a qualified handicapped employee, an agency is then required to provide reasonable accommodation unless the agency can show undue hardship.... Of course, the appellant is not required to suggest all possible reasonable accommodation in order to establish that he is a qualified handicapped employee. The employing agency is often in a much better position to suggest such accommodations.... An agency is not required to provide accommodation to a qualified handicapped employee if it can prove that to do so would create an undue administrative or financial burden, or substantial modification of job requirements....

...The agency not only suggested and considered alternative reasonable accommodations, it implemented them. The agency's actions prior to proposing removal of liberally granting leave, significantly reducing appellant's workload, and providing close supervision of appellant's work are evidence of this....

...

Appellant contends that he never received substantive training in areas of law that comprised the major areas of his work responsibility.... [S]uch training was offered by the agency on a general basis to appellant and other attorneys in his office. Appellant failed, however, to request the opportunity to attend specific training sessions. More importantly, the agency was under no duty to provide training as an accommodation where no indication existed that training would have improved appellant's performance....

Appellant offered no evidence, medical or otherwise, demonstrating that training would or could have improved his work performance....

Even if the appellant had established such a prima facie case, the agency established that the suggested accommodation was not likely to result in improved performance. An agency is not required to spend its money and exert its resources pursuing every conceivable accommodation advanced by an employee when there is little or no likelihood that the accommodation will correct the performance or conduct deficiencies claimed to be caused by the handicap.... This result especially applies when the handicapped employee...fails in any way to assist the agency by showing how the accommodation might enable the employee to perform the essential functions of his position.

The next accommodation suggested was reassignment.... No vacancies existed in positions appellant would have been qualified to fill, in part because a freeze on filling positions was in effect at the time reassignment was considered. Despite this, a diligent effort was made to locate a non-legal programmatic position...an offer...was made.... The offer was declined. The appellant may not force consideration of reassignment ad infinitum....

The final accommodation suggested by appellant is the excusing of the AWOL charge that partially supported the agency's sustained third reason for his removal.... The Rehabilitation Act was not designed to excuse handicapped employees for acts of misconduct.

Mr. Lynch took his case to district court. The court vacated the order of the Special Panel, finding that it had overstepped its jurisdiction. Lynch v. Bennett, 665 F. Supp. 62 (D.D.C. 1987) (the district court decision is discussed earlier in comments about Special Panel operations). On remand to the Board, the analysis of the prima facie case changed slightly as the Board sought to justify a different result in the appeal, Lynch v. Dept. of Educ., 37 MSPR 12, 15-16 (1988):

The appellant was an epileptic during all of his employment with the government. However, in 1979, appellant's condition worsened, such that his memory and ability to concentrate were adversely affected. Appellant's treating physician changed appellant's medication...which, while providing relief from the seizures, caused insomnia and further exacerbated his difficulties with concentration. It is appellant's contention that his epileptic condition and the medication required to treat that condition, combined with his inexperience in the legal duties assigned him, adversely affected his ability to work at the desired level.

On numerous occasions, the appellant advised his supervisors of the effects of his epilepsy and medication and he provided medical documentation of same. On each occasion he requested accommodation. Specifically, the appellant asked that he be provided training in legal drafting and in Titles VI and IX of the Civil Rights Act of 1964; specific structured assignments, including clear written assignment directions and increased supervisory assistance; and the opportunity to make up the hours of work missed because of late arrivals. Although the agency allowed appellant to work a flexible schedule, he was not provided the other accommodations he requested.

Appellant's protection against discrimination on account of his handicap is derived from section 501 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, 29 USC 791, and implementing regulations.

The regulations define "handicapped person" as one who has a physical or mental impairment which substantially limits one or more of such person's major life activities. 29 CFR 1603.702(a). There is no question that appellant is a handicapped person. Ample evidence is in the record to show that appellant has a history of epilepsy and that the disease and its treatment affect his ability to remember and to concentrate.

The appellant must show, however, that with or without reasonable accommodation, he can perform the essential functions of the position in question without endangering the health and safety of himself or others. [29 CFR 1614.203(a)(6)].That burden, in our opinion, has been met. Appellant has presented evidence that he had previously performed the duties of an attorney in an outstanding manner. In addition, his testimony and the medical evidence demonstrates that it is very likely that the difficulties he eventually experienced on the job were caused by the effects of epilepsy and the drugs required to control the disease. Finally, he has articulated an accommodation which quite possibly would ameliorate the adverse effects that his epilepsy had on his ability to perform. Accordingly, the appellant has established a prima facie case of handicap discrimination....

An agency's obligation is to make reasonable accommodation to the known physical or mental limitations of a qualified handicapped employee unless it can demonstrate that the accommodation would impose an undue hardship on the operation of its program....

The agency has never asserted that the requested accommodation would cause it undue hardship, but instead argued that it had adequately accommodated appellant by allowing him to come in late and leave late and by excusing his typos.
