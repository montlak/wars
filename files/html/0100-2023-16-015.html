2. Scope of Review

Performance standards are not directly appealable to the Board. See De Maio v. MSPB, 107 Fed. Appx. 897 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP). Standards are reviewable in a Chapter 43 performance-based action. To determine whether performance standards are improper, e.g., too subjective, an AJ must allow an appellant an opportunity to challenge their content. Evans v. Dept. of Treasury, 24 MSPR 571, 576-77 (1984). An appellant's pleadings should be liberally construed. A performance standard is placed at issue by a pro se appellant's complaint that it was "impossible and unreasonable" for her to complete assigned tasks under a critical element within the time limits set by the agency. Melnick v. DHUD, 42 MSPR 93, 98 (1989). On challenge by the appellant, the agency has the burden of showing that its performance standard meets the requirements of the statute and does not constitute an abuse of its discretion. The Board has also held, however, that when, in a Chapter 43 action, the appellant attacks the performance standards, the appellant must show how they were lacking in objectivity, specificity, and accuracy, and the appellant must show the harm that resulted from a lack of those qualities. Hoesl v. Dept. of Navy, 19 MSPR 553, 554-55 (1984). But see Callaway, post (rejecting a harmful error approach to the adequacy of standards).

So significant are performance standards in a Chapter 43 case that the Board accepted without comment a finding by an AJ, stemming from concerns that the AJ raised sua sponte, that standards were improper because they were "backwards," i.e., they set a level of performance that allowed job retention but the standards described unacceptable rather than acceptable performance. That finding led to the agency's loss of its case. Ortiz v. DOJ, 46 MSPR 692, 695 (1991), observed:

In Chapter 43 performance-based actions, the validity or adequacy of the standards never lies very deep below the surface. An agency cannot complain of "surprise" or "prejudice" when the standards it seeks to enforce are so nondescript as to be unintelligible and unenforceable.

As to a violation of agency procedures governing creation or modification of performance standards, the appellant will be required to demonstrate harmful error if the agency's standards otherwise comply with the law, Mouser v. DHHS, 32 MSPR 543, 548 (1987):

Moreover, we find that even if the performance indicators constituted modifications and the agency did not follow prescribed procedures for implementing them, the appellant did not show that the agency committed harmful error. The appellant argues that the agency was required to follow the procedures set forth in the agency's Personnel Guide for Supervisors in modifying his performance standards and that it failed to do so. Although the Board has held that an agency is obligated to conform to the procedures it adopts, it applies a harmful error standard to determine whether any failure by the agency to follow its procedures warrants reversing an agency action. See Cross, 25 MSPR at 359. Here, appellant has not even alleged that any error by the agency in this regard harmed him, and as previously noted, the agency clearly put the appellant on notice that it was using the performance indicators to evaluate his performance.

However the issues are raised and however the burden of proof is defined, the scope of Board review is limited to determining whether the agency abused its discretion in establishing the standards so as to cause harm to the employee against whom the standards are applied. Siegelman v. DHUD, 14 MSPR 326, 330 (1983). Callaway v. Dept. of Army, 23 MSPR 592, 597-98 (1984), expanded on the "abuse of discretion" review standard as it rejected a reading of Siegelman that seemed to establish a "harmful error" rule for challenges to the substance of performance standards:

...The statute requires the use of objective job-related criteria enabling the performance rater to make an "accurate evaluation of job performance." Moreover, it requires that these criteria will be provided "to the maximum extent feasible" by the performance standards. Therefore, although the legislative history indicates that Congress intended agencies to have substantial flexibility in establishing performance standards, the statutory language requires that the performance standards be based on objective criteria and meet the other requirements of § 4302. Siegelman v. Department of Housing and Urban Development, 13 MSPB 27 at 29, 14 MSPR 326 at 330 (1983); Wells, supra, 1 MSPB at 202, 231, 1 MSPR at 213, 244.

An employee, of course, may still challenge her performance standards as failing to meet the requirements of 5 USC 4302(b)(1). Siegelman, supra, at 29, 14 MSPR at 330.... [T]he Board's scope of review of...all performance standards, is limited to determining whether the agency has abused its discretion in establishing the performance standards. Siegelman, supra, at 29, 14 MSPR at 330.[6]

[6] Some of the interested parties who submitted amicus briefs assumed that in Siegelman, supra, the Board established a harmful error standard for reviewing performance standards that do not comply with 5 USC § 4302(b)(1). The standard established in Siegelman, however, dealt only with the scope of review the Board would exercise to determine whether the performance standard violated the statute.

Further, we find that if an agency bases its action on an invalid performance standard, it is inappropriate to consider the effect of the error under the harmful procedural error standard. The establishment of such a performance standard does not constitute procedural error, but violates an important substantive right of the employee.... As OPM stated in its amicus brief:

...the utilization of performance standards which comply with 5 USC § 4302(b)(1) is at the very heart of the performance appraisal system contemplated in the CSRA.... Violation of the statutory criteria, determined under the Siegelman approach, can hardly be described as an error in the application of some procedural requirement. The performance standard is the substance of the appraisal system. If the standard used to determine performance does not comply with the statutory requirements the performance rating is substantively flawed and represents far more than a procedural error. Id. at 123.

Moreover, given the substantive nature of performance standards, we find that in response to an appellant's challenge, proof of the validity of the performance standard more properly represents an element of the agency's case than a matter on which appellant should bear the burden of proof. Thus, the agency retains the burden of showing that its performance standard meets the requirements of the statute and does not constitute an abuse of its discretion. To the extent that Siegelman can be read to apply a harmful error test, it is hereby clarified in accordance with our holding in the instant case.

Although the establishment of performance standards is within the agency's discretion, the exercise of that discretion is not unlimited, but must be reviewed in light of the purpose of the CSRA as reflected by its statutory provisions. The Board's review of the legislative history of 5 USC § 4302(b)(1) supports this conclusion. In discussing the statutory requirement for OPM review of each agency performance appraisal system, the Board noted Congress's intent that "employees not be subject to the streamlined procedures of § 4303 on the basis of subjective performance standards established at the unreviewed discretion of agencies." Wells, supra, 1 MSPB at 223, 1 MSPR at 236. See also Siegelman, supra. (Congress intended to protect employees from arbitrary labeling of their work as unacceptable). Consequently, when the Board reviews an agency performance standard, we must determine whether, under the particular circumstances of the case, the standard accords with the statute and promotes the statutory purpose.
