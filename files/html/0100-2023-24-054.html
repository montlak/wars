3. MSPB Decision

The actions of the Board are entitled to considerable deference when it acts to implement its statutory authority in a reasonable manner. It is not the court's function to substitute its own judgment in the matter unless the action is unreasonable or clearly unlawful. Bennett v. Dept. of Navy, 699 F.2d 1140, 1143 (Fed. Cir. 1983) (interpretation of the counsel fee provisions of the Reform Act); see Debose v. USDA, 700 F.2d 1262, 1266 (9th Cir. 1983) (Board interpretations are entitled to "great deference"). If the Board has issued conflicting opinions, it receives no deference from a court called on to provide statutory interpretation. See Lovshin v. Dept. of Navy, 767 F.2d 826, 840 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Deference to administrative statutory interpretation is to be distinguished from the effect of an administrative decision. Prior decisions of the MSPB are not binding precedent for an appellate court, although the reasoning contained in those decisions may be helpful guidance. Pettis v. DHHS, 803 F.2d 1176, 1178 (Fed. Cir. 1986).

Review more strenuous than that according "considerable deference" to a Board decision is appropriate if the disputed order or decision directly involves the construction of the organic statute defining agency operations. On review of an agency's attempt to reconcile arguably conflicting statutory provisions, the proper judicial role is to ensure that the agency effected an appropriate balance of the conflicting provisions and that the agency remained within the bounds of its statutory authority. Atwell v. MSPB, 670 F.2d 272, 282 (D.C. Cir. 1981). If there are no prior or consistent interpretations by the agency, and if it appears that Congress would not have blessed the interpretation and result advocated by the agency, deference will not be given to the administrative interpretation of a statute. Lichtman v. OPM, 785 F.2d 299, 303 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (retirement benefits dispute). But "if Congress intends for legislation to change the interpretation of a judicially created concept, it makes that intent specific." Martin v. Dept. of Air Force, 79 MSPR 380, 385 (1998) (citing Midlantic Nat. Bank v. New Jersey Dept. of Env'tl Protection, 474 U.S. 494, 501, 106 S. Ct. 755 (1986)). The court said of its review of a Board determination concerning collateral estoppel, "We review the Board's application of the doctrine here without deference to the Board, Johnson v. MSPB, 690 Fed. Appx. 683, 684 (Fed. Cir. 2017 NP), citing Morgan v. Dept. of Energy, 424 F.3d 1271, 1274 (Fed. Cir. 2005)."

If the Federal Circuit issues a precedential decision, that decision binds the Board in future cases, and the Board is not free to deviate from the law set by the circuit until that law is reconsidered by the circuit or revised by the Supreme Court. See Cobert v. Miller, 800 F.3d 1340, 1349 (Fed. Cir. 2015) ("Thus, contrary to what the Board said in Miller II, we did not merely 'endorse' the Ketterer framework. Rather, in clear and certain terms, we made it the 'law of the circuit.' Ketterer's approach thereby became law that must be followed by the Board and panels of this court until overruled by either the Supreme Court or by this court en banc.").
