broida flra Chapter 09: Review of Arbitration Awards Chapter 09: Review of Arbitration Awards, Subchapter 04: Particular Matters Excluded or encompassed by Contract

IV. PARTICULAR MATTERS EXCLUDED OR ENCOMPASSED BY CONTRACT

Under 5 USC 7121(a)(2), the parties "may exclude any matter from the application of the grievance procedures...." Section 7103(a)(9) of the Statute defines the term "grievance" for purposes of the Statute; that provision does not specify or mandate the scope of a negotiated grievance procedure. Dept. of Air Force, Air Force Flight Test Ctr., Edwards AFB and Air Traffic Controllers Org., 44 FLRA 1242, 1245 (1992); see AFGE Local 3911 and EPA Region II, 56 FLRA 480, 481-82 (2000) (concerning whether a grievance procedure excluded selections for positions outside of unit). The parties may exclude particular matters from their grievance procedure, and if they do the method chosen to resolve those matters will not be part of FLRA review procedure relating to arbitration awards. Should the contract exclude a matter, FLRA may honor an exception to an award that fails to honor the contract exclusion. When the issue was whether a contract permitted an arbitrator to exercise jurisdiction over a RIF appeal, FLRA set the award aside when the arbitrator concluded that he lacked jurisdiction but then directed the grievant to file an appeal with the MSPB and directed the agency to lodge no timeliness objection with the Board. Once the arbitrator determined that he lacked jurisdiction over the grievance, he lacked jurisdiction to fashion a remedy. AFGE Local 51 and U.S. Mint, U.S. Assay Office, San Francisco, 30 FLRA 1266, 1268-69 (1988). That a contract exclusion effectively leaves a separated employee without a forum does not deprive the grievant of constitutional due process. See Indian Educators Fed'n and Bureau of Indian Affairs, 53 FLRA 352, 361-62 (1997) ("Neither the Fifth Amendment nor the Statute compels that an arbitration hearing is the means by which an employee's right to due process must be satisfied.").

If a subject is asserted to be outside the scope of the grievance procedure because of a contractual restriction, FLRA has jurisdiction to review the award of the arbitrator who considers the exclusion. That occurred in Naval Air Station, Kingsville and AFGE Local 1735, 35 FLRA 841, 842-43 (1990), involving a dispute over contracting out procedures:

Section 7121(a)(2) of the Statute provides that a collective bargaining agreement "may exclude any matter from the application of the grievance procedures which are provided for in the agreement." The parties may agree to exclude any matter, including disputes concerning contracting out, from the scope of their negotiated grievance procedure....

...Because the Union has no statutory right to arbitrate contracting-out disputes, its assertion that the collective bargaining agreement does not constitute a clear and unmistakable waiver of its right is also incorrect....

The contractual exclusion of a topic from the grievance procedure may be established through the parties' history of negotiations, as in Dept. of Treasury, IRS, Omaha Dist. and NTEU, Chap. 3, 36 FLRA 273, 276 (1990). FLRA rejected the argument that "in order for an arbitrator to find a matter excluded from the coverage of the [parties'] negotiated grievance procedure, the exclusion must be clear and unmistakable."

In DOD, DMA, Hydrographic/Topographic Ctr. and AFGE Local 3407, 34 FLRA 44, 45-46 (1989), the contract excluded performance rating appeals from arbitration. The ratings could be considered only by a performance rating appeal panel, whose decision was final. When the union attempted to secure review of a decision of the panel, FLRA declined to provide the requested review:

Section 7121(b)(3)(C) of the Statute requires negotiated grievance procedures to subject unsettled grievances to binding arbitration.... Section 7121(a)(2), however, permits the parties to exclude certain matters from their negotiated grievance procedure....

The Authority does not have jurisdiction under section 7122(a) to review exceptions to decisions which did not result from binding arbitration within the meaning of section 7121.... Consequently, because the Panel decision did not result from binding arbitration under section 7121, the Authority has no jurisdiction to review the Union's exceptions to the Panel's decision under section 7122(a).

An arbitrator was not faulted who determined he lacked jurisdiction over a discrimination claim that was precluded by a contract provision relegating EEO complaints to the EEOC regulatory procedure. Dept. of Air Force, Carswell AFB and AFGE Local 1364, 43 FLRA 1266, 1269-70 (1992).

If the exclusion from grievance coverage is defined by contract, it is the contract that will be examined to determine the bounds of the exclusion. Dept. of Air Force, Scott AFB and NAGE Local R7-23, 40 FLRA 421, 423 (1991), set aside an award of an arbitrator who decided that the agency administrative grievance procedure defined exclusions from the negotiated grievance procedure. Not so, the Authority held: "Under the Statute, collective bargaining agreements, rather than agency rules and regulations, govern the disposition of matters to which they both apply and the agency rule or regulation." See Dept. of Air Force, Seymour Johnson AFB and NAGE Local R5-188, 55 FLRA 163, 165 (1999) ("[C]ollective bargaining agreements, rather than agency-wide regulations, govern the disposition of matters to which they both apply."), followed in VA Gulf Coast VHCS and AFGE Local 1045, 57 FLRA 77, 79 (2001). The exclusion need not be explicit. FLRA will review de novo an exception asserting that an award is contrary to law because the arbitrator improperly concluded that the contract excluded the subject matter of a grievance, but FLRA's interpretation of the contract will consider the contract as a whole. Indian Educators Fed'n and Bureau of Indian Affairs, 53 FLRA 352, 359 (1997).
