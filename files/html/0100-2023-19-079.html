a. Dangerousness; Risk Assessment; Direct Threat

Potential for dangerousness may render an employee disabled but not qualified for accommodation. Although the Board did not identify how it would quantify "dangerousness," in Mazzarella v. USPS, 56 MSPR 47, 54-55 (1992), the employee was fired for disruptive conduct and some property damage at the office, constituting a violation of agency standards of conduct. Medical evidence established that the appellant was afflicted with an "explosive personality disorder, intermittent type." That led to a determination by the AJ, undisputed by the agency, that the employee was disabled. Mazzarella examined the evidence involving the incident leading to the adverse action, and characterized it as "an episode fraught with danger to agency employees and property" that reflected, consistent with medical literature describing the appellant's condition, aggressiveness that was "grossly out of proportion to any precipitating psychosocial stressors." The Board discounted the AJ's evaluation of appellant's demeanor during the hearing by noting a reference to DSM-III-R indicating that "there are no signs of generalized impulsivity or aggressiveness between the episodes." The appellant's medical expert conceded that another violent outburst "could recur." From this possibility the Board concluded that the appellant was not a qualified disabled employee, that is, "a handicapped person who, with or without reasonable accommodation, can perform the essential functions of the position in question without endangering the health and safety of the individual or others...." See 29 CFR 1614.203 (2002) [now 29 CFR 1630.2 (2022)].The Board's analysis was followed in federal court. Mazzarella v. USPS, 849 F. Supp. 89 (D. Mass. 1994).

When assessing an adverse action involving an individual with a disability, the agency may assert that the individual does not meet medical qualifications and that his or her retention on the job would be a direct threat to the appellant or to others in the workplace. Or, without involving medical standards, the individual's behavior may constitute a danger or threat of danger in the workplace sufficient to warrant adverse action. Refer to OPM regulations on medical qualification determinations at 5 CFR Part 339 (2022). Johnson v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-12-0074-I-1 (NP 1/30/2014), explained application of a "direct threat" analysis when the appellant's position involves a medical standard:

On review, the appellant complains that the administrative judge improperly concluded that the appellant was not a "direct threat" and that the relevant EEOC guidelines should have been applied, which require that an individualized assessment be performed. We are not persuaded by this argument. The administrative judge noted in the initial decision that an agency may defend against a claim of disability discrimination by asserting that an individual poses a "direct threat" to the health or safety of herself and others in the workplace, and he noted that such a situation may arise when an agency asserts that an individual fails to meet the medical standards established for a position. ID at 24-25 & n.12 (citing, among other things, Simpson v. U.S. Postal Service, 113 M.S.P.R. 346, ¶ 14 (2010); Cano v. U.S. Postal Service, 107 M.S.P.R. 284, ¶ 14 (2007); Boots v. U.S. Postal Service, 105 M.S.P.R. 500, ¶¶ 5-6 (2007); McAlexander v. Department of Defense, 105 M.S.P.R. 384, ¶ 2 (2007); 29 C.F.R. §§ 1630.2(r), 1630.15(b)(2)). The administrative judge found that the agency was not obligated to prove that the appellant posed a "direct threat" in this case because it did not allege that she failed to meet the medical standards of her position. We agree with the administrative judge that the appellant's removal for unacceptable behavior does not implicate any type of qualification standard, and thus, the direct threat defense is not applicable. We therefore discern no error with his analysis of this issue.

The "direct threat" test was restated, Hernandez-Torres v. Dept. of Army, AT-0752-15-0664-I-1 (NP 9/12/2016):

If, as here, an agency asserts that an individual fails to meet the minimum standards established for a position, it may defend against a claim of disability discrimination by asserting that an individual poses a "direct threat" to the health or safety of himself or others in the workplace. 42 U.S.C. § 12113(a)-(b); Simpson v. U.S. Postal Service, 113 M.S.P.R. 346, ¶ 14 (2010); 29 C.F.R. § 1630.15(b)(2). "Direct threat means a significant risk of substantial harm to the health or safety of the individual or others that cannot be eliminated or reduced by reasonable accommodation." 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(r). Discrimination law requires an agency to conduct an individualized assessment of an employee's qualifications in determining whether an employee poses a direct threat because of a disability. McAlexander v. Department of Defense, 105 M.S.P.R. 384, ¶¶ 14-15 (2007). An individualized assessment is "based on a reasonable medical judgment that relies on the most current medical knowledge and/or on the best available objective evidence." 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(r).

The appellant claims that the direct threat defense is not available to the agency because it required a "100% guarantee...that there would be no further problems related to [the] [a]ppellant's epilepsy." We disagree. The agency's physician considered such factors as the potential for "sudden incapacitation," as well as the risk of recurrence, which he assessed at between 18% to 60%, depending on the amount of time that had passed since the last seizure. See Cano v. U.S. Postal Service, 107 M.S.P.R. 284, ¶ 14 (2007) (indicating that factors relevant in determining whether an individual with a disability poses a direct threat include the nature of the risk, the severity of the potential harm, the probability that the potential harm will occur, and the imminence of the harm); 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(r) (stating the same factors slightly differently). He also considered the possible side effects of the appellant's medication and the nature of the appellant's job duties. Ultimately, the administrative judge concluded that there was "a risk of substantial harm to the officer and the public" from a seizure "occur[ing] while driving, directing traffic, or result[ing] in loss of control of a weapon."

If medical standards are not in place for the employee, the Board considers the risk to the employee and to the workplace, or to the agency mission, to determine if the impact of an employee's medical condition supports adverse action, Fox v. Dept. of Army, 120 MSPR 529, 543-44 ¶¶ 24-26, 2014 MSPB 6 (2014):

In her analysis of the first charge, the administrative judge cited Slater v. Department of Homeland Security, 108 M.S.P.R. 419, ¶ 11 (2008), for the proposition that, in order to remove an employee for physical inability to perform, the agency must show that the disabling condition itself is disqualifying, its recurrence cannot be ruled out, and the duties of the position are such that a recurrence would pose a reasonable probability of substantial harm. The holding of Slater is derived from OPM's regulation at 5 C.F.R. § 339.206, which concerns disqualifications based on medical history from positions with medical standards or physical requirements or subject to medical evaluation programs. The appellant did not occupy such a position. See 5 C.F.R. §§ 339.202, .203, .205. Consequently, Slater does not govern this appeal.

Where, as here, the appellant does not occupy a position with medical standards or physical requirements or subject to medical evaluation programs, in order to establish a charge of physical inability to perform, the agency must prove a nexus between the employee's medical condition and observed deficiencies in her performance or conduct, or a high probability, given the nature of the work involved, that her condition may result in injury to herself or others. Marshall-Carter v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 94 M.S.P.R. 518, ¶ 10 (2003), aff'd, 122 F. App'x 513 (Fed. Cir. 2005). In other words, the agency must establish that the appellant's medical condition prevents her from being able to safely and efficiently perform the core duties of her position. See id., ¶¶ 10, 13. In determining whether the agency has met its burden, the Board will consider whether a reasonable accommodation exists that would enable the appellant to safely and efficiently perform those core duties. See id., ¶ 10. However, for the limited purposes of proving the charge, the agency is not required to show that it was unable to reasonably accommodate the appellant by assigning her to a vacant position for which she was qualified; whether it could do so goes to the affirmative defense of disability discrimination and/or the reasonableness of the penalty. Id., ¶¶ 12-14.

The core duties of a position are synonymous with its essential functions, i.e., the fundamental job duties of the position, not including marginal functions. See 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(n)(1). A job duty may be considered essential for any of several reasons, e.g., because the reason the position exists is to perform that function, because of the limited number of employees available among whom the performance of that job function can be distributed, or because the function is highly specialized so that the incumbent is hired for his or her expertise or ability to perform the particular function. 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(n)(2). Evidence of whether a particular function is essential includes, inter alia, the employer's judgment as to which functions are essential, written position descriptions, and the consequences of not requiring the incumbent to perform the function. 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(n)(3).

Cf. Jakes v. DOD, AT-0752-14-0614-I-1 (NP 4/1/2015) ("Evidence of whether a particular function is essential includes, inter alia, the employer's judgment as to which functions are essential, written position descriptions, the amount of time spent performing the function, and the consequences of not requiring the incumbent to perform the function. Fox, 120 M.S.P.R. 529, ¶ 26 (citing 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(n)(1))."). ...Here, the Special Panel deferred to the EEOC's finding that the agency discriminated against the appellant on the basis of his disability by relying on DOT regulations to remove him rather than conducting an individualized assessment of him to determine whether he posed a direct threat that could not be eliminated or reduced by reasonable accommodation. Boots, 100 M.S.P.R. 513 ¶¶ 11, 26-27. The finding of discrimination in this case, therefore, does not initially involve the issue of whether the agency failed to provide a reasonable accommodation. Instead, the discrimination finding is based on the conclusion that the agency failed to establish the defense of "direct threat as a qualification standard," under 29 C.F.R. § 1630.15(b)(2). See Fellows-Gilder v. Chertoff, EEOC Appeal No. 01A33476, 2005 WL 3452280, recon. denied, 2006 WL 1464810 (Where employee who was removed because she experienced seizures, was performing her job without an accommodation, and had not requested accommodation, the issue was "whether or not [the employee] posed a direct threat to herself or others at the workplace." Id.).

Nevertheless, the issue of reasonable accommodation arises in the direct threat defense. The definition of direct threat in 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(r) is: "a significant risk of substantial harm to the health or safety of the individual or others that cannot be eliminated or reduced by reasonable accommodation." In discussing this section of the regulations, the EEOC's Interpretive Guidance on Title I of the Americans with Disabilities Act states:

An employer may require, as a qualification standard, that an individual not pose a direct threat to the health or safety of himself/herself or others. Like any other qualification standard, such a standard must apply to all applicants or employees and not just to individuals with disabilities. If, however, an individual poses a direct threat as a result of a disability, the employer must determine whether a reasonable accommodation would either eliminate the risk or reduce it to an acceptable level. If no accommodation exists that would either eliminate or reduce the risk, the employer may refuse to hire an applicant or may discharge an employee who poses a direct threat.

Appendix to 29 C.F.R. Pt. 1630, § 1630.2(r).

To establish the direct threat defense, an employer must show that no reasonable accommodation exists that would either eliminate or reduce the threat. See, e.g., Lovell v. Department of Justice (Federal Bureau of Investigation), EEOC Appeal No. 01A41642, 2006 WL 3877373 ("Where the agency concludes that an individual poses a direct threat as a result of a disability, the agency must determine whether a reasonable accommodation would either eliminate the risk or reduce it to an acceptable level. If no such accommodation exists, the agency may refuse to hire an applicant."); Ganson v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01A01214, 2004 WL 321029 ("If it is determined that an individual does pose a direct threat because of a disability, the employer must determine whether a reasonable accommodation would eliminate the risk of harm or reduce it to an acceptable level."). In Love v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01A11561, 2002 WL 1590129, the Commission noted that the record contained documentation showing that complainant was not medically fit to work. It also took cognizance of the restrictions imposed by Associate Area Medical Officer after reviewing the medical impairments considered by the VA in giving complainant an 80% compensable disability as well as non-military impairments documented in complainant's medical records. On the basis of this assessment of complainant, the Commission concluded that "the agency acted reasonably when it determined it could not reasonably accommodate complainant's disability without posing a direct threat to her or other employees, and assessed her as a moderate risk." Similarly, in Parker v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 01981917, 2001 WL 1561930, the Commission noted that the record contained documentation from complainant's physician advising that complainant might become violent in the workplace, that his condition persisted despite treatment with anti-psychotics, and that transferring him to another facility would not ameliorate his symptoms. On the basis of this assessment of complainant, the Commission concluded that "the agency acted reasonably when it determined it could not reasonably accommodate complainant's disability without posing a direct threat to other employees, and issued complainant the Letter of Exclusion."

Slater v. DHS, 108 MSPR 419, 423-29 ¶¶ 4, 7-17 2008 MSPB 73 (2008) (overruled, Haas v. DHS, 2022 MSPB 36 (2022), to the extent that the Board applied 5 C.F.R. § 339.206 to a charge of medical inability when the appellant was removed based on his current medical condition or impairment) attempted to define, with reference to OPM regulations and past Board decisions, the standard for assessing the degree of risk created by a medical impairment that will permit retention, or require removal, of an employee. Mr. Slater was removed from his job as a Federal Protective Service police officer because he was afflicted with complications of diabetes, including a limited range of motion in the left wrist and a decrease in grip strength in the left hand. The question before the Board--one that it previously dealt with inconsistently--was how to enumerate or measure for decisionmakers (agency officials or AJs) the job-related impairment in terms of risk of an individual who previously performed a job at an acceptable level but who may not be able to perform the job safely over a long time due to a continuing or episodic impairment. Without addressing accommodative requirements, under Slater:

A removal as "medically disqualified" is equivalent to a removal for inability to perform for medical reasons. Cheers v. Office of Personnel Management, 87 M.S.P.R. 591, ¶ 11 (2001); see also Justice v. Department of the Navy, 89 M.S.P.R. 379, VII 2-3 (2001) (the appellant was medically disqualified from his position and subsequently removed for medical inability to perform his duties); Cunningham v. Department of the Air Force, 26 M.S.P.R. 599, 600-01 (1985) (the appellant was "disqualified from his position because of medical reasons," could not be reassigned, and did not apply for disability retirement; the Board upheld his removal on the charge of being "medically unable to perform the duties of his position"). The record evidence demonstrates that the agency removed the appellant solely on the basis of his alleged medical disqualification. The proper standard for evaluating an employee's fitness to perform the duties of his position, for positions with medical standards or physical requirements, or positions subject to medical evaluation programs, is 5 C.F.R. § 339.206. Lassiter v. Department of Justice, 60 M.S.P.R. 138, 141-42 (1993).

Under 5 C.F.R. § 339.206, a history of a particular medical problem may be the basis of a medical disqualification only if "the condition at issue is itself disqualifying, recurrence cannot medically be ruled out, and the duties of the position are such that a recurrence would pose a reasonable probability of substantial harm." The ID, citing Yates v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 M.S.P.R. 172, 176 (1996), stated that, in order to carry its burden of proof, the agency must "establish a nexus between [the appellant's] medical condition and observed deficiencies in his performance or conduct, or a high probability of hazard when his condition may result in injury to him or others because of the kind of work he does. As there was no evidence of observed deficiencies in the appellant's performance or conduct, the AJ applied the high probability of hazard standard and concluded that the agency failed to meet its burden of proof.

The first Board case that set forth the high probability of hazard standard was Owens v. Department of the Air Force, 8 M.S.P.R. 580, 584 n.10 (1981). The language in the ID quoted above, citing Yates, was the same language used by the Board in Owens. Owens, 8 M.S.P.R. at 584 n.10. Owens took that language almost verbatim from a quotation of the 1972 edition of the Federal Personnel Manual (FPM) in a D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals case reviewing a decision of the Board's predecessor agency, the U.S. Civil Service Commission. See Doe v. Hampton, 566 F.2d 265, 273 (D.C. Cir. 1977).

On March 8, 1989, however, OPM issued new regulations amending 5 C.F.R. Part 339, which regulates the medical qualification and disqualification of federal employees. See 54 Fed. Reg. 9761 (Mar. 8, 1989). The purpose of these regulations was "to allow agencies greater flexibility in setting appropriate medical standards and requirements." Id. OPM also simultaneously issued a comprehensive revision of chapter 339 of the FPM to reflect the new regulations. Id. The new regulations included section 339.206, quoted in paragraph 6. In contrast with the language of the 1972 FPM quoted in Doe in 1981, and then in Owens and Yates, the 1989 FPM, reflecting the revised chapter 339, read as follows:

A history of a medical condition may be considered disqualifying only if the condition itself is normally disqualifying, a recurrence cannot medically be ruled out, and the duties of the position are such that a recurrence would pose a reasonable probability of substantial harm.

...Generally speaking, so long as the candidate is presently able to do the job, he or she is qualified unless the possibility that the condition might recur would present a substantial health and safety risk.

Federal Personnel Manual, Chapter 339, Subchapter 2-1d. (April 28, 1989) (emphasis in original).[1] The Board first applied 5 C.F.R. § 339.206 in Lassiter, 60 M.S.P.R. 138, finding that a removal based upon medical disqualification was appropriate by applying the standard that the medical condition posed "a reasonable probability of substantial harm." Lassiter, 60 M.S.P.R. at 141-42 (quoting 5 C.F.R. § 339.206). Lassiter did not address the Owens line of cases, however, and subsequent to Lassiter, the Board has applied the Owens standard in five appeals.[2]

[1] While the FPM was abolished on December 31, 1993, it may be relied upon for guidance in appropriate circumstances. See Wallace v. Department of Commerce, 106 M.S.P.R. 23, ¶ 11 n.2 (2007).

[2] Simpson v. Department of the Navy, 95 M.S.P.R. 370 (2004); Schrodt v. U.S. Postal Service, 79 M.S.P.R. 609 (1998); Spencer v. Department of the Navy, 73 M.S.P.R. 15 (1997); Yates v. Considering that the basis for the Owens standard, the 1972 FPM, was amended, prior to its abolishment, to reflect 5 C.F.R. § 339.206, and that such regulation remains in force and applies to this field, we conclude that the Owens standard of "a high probability of hazard" is no longer appropriate to apply in cases such as this. Rather, the standard promulgated by OPM in its regulations is the proper one: To justify disqualification based upon a medical condition alone, the agency must show that the condition itself is disqualifying, its recurrence cannot be ruled out, and the duties of the position are such that a recurrence would pose a reasonable probability of substantial harm. 5 C.F.R. § 339.206. Such a finding is a legitimate reason for an adverse action under title 5 chapter 75. See Lassiter, 60 M.S.P.R. at 147-48. To the extent that they applied the "high probability of hazard" standard, the Board's five prior decisions, issued after the OPM regulations came into effect on March 8, 1989, are overruled.

The diagnoses of S.H. Sabnani, M.D. and Bijan Zardouz, M.D. established that the appellant suffered from polyneuropathy in his hands, legs, and feet, caused by his diabetes mellitus. Dr. Zardouz stated that diabetes can cause polyneuropathy to progress over time and that the appellant's problems with his hands and feet would likely worsen over time. Dr. Sabnani also diagnosed the appellant with an old fracture in his left wrist, which has caused a significant reduction in range of motion and grip strength in the left wrist and hand. Dr. Saladino did not examine the appellant but reviewed all the medical reports and concluded that the appellant could not safely perform his duties. Dr. Saladino testified that the appellant's neuropathy appeared fairly serious and that neuropathy of that severity generally persists and progresses. He also testified that the appellant's lost range of motion in his wrist due to an old fracture is not recoverable.

The appellant produced contrary evidence first in brief letters signed by Physician's Assistant James Garmon, Luzmin Inderias, M.D., and Paul Morales, M.D. These brief letters, with almost identical language, summarily state that the authors examined the appellant and reviewed his position description and believe the appellant is medically qualified to perform his duties. These letters are devoid of any medical information or detail concerning the appellant's medical examination and the appellant admitted that he actually drafted each of these letters for Mr. Garmon, Dr. Inderias, and Dr. Morales to sign. The appellant also produced medical reports from Jin Kim, M.D. and Johanna Rosenthal, M.D. Dr. Kim concluded that the appellant has a slight deficit in range of motion in his left wrist that is not significant or limiting and that the appellant does not have diabetes. Dr. Rosenthal provided a more comprehensive report stating that the appellant has diabetes but that it is controlled by diet and that he has a slight history of peripheral neuropathy. She characterizes the neuropathy as "very minimal," states that the appellant responds to the pinprick and tuning fork tests, and asserts that the prior nerve conduction test results were at the lower limits of normal, but were not abnormal. Dr. Rosenthal states that the appellant has a prior wrist fracture but that he retains full range of motion. Dr. Rosenthal also calls into question the reliability of the prior results and conclusions concerning the appellant's neuropathy. In his testimony, Dr. Saladino called into question the objectivity and reliability of Dr. Rosenthal's report, which he characterized as advocating for the appellant's return to duty rather than as an objective medical assessment.

In assessing the probative weight of medical opinion, the Board considers whether the opinion was based on a medical examination, whether the opinion provided a reasoned explanation for its findings as distinct from mere conclusory assertions, the qualifications of the expert rendering the opinion, and the extent and duration of the expert's familiarity with the treatment of the appellant. Lassiter, 60 M.S.P.R. at 142. None of the doctors who examined the appellant testified at the hearing. Neither party sought the testimony of Drs. Inderias, Morales, Sabnani, or Zardouz, and the AJ denied the appellant's request for Drs. Rosenthal and Kim to testify because it was after the deadline he had set for requesting witnesses. Accordingly, the assessment of the probative value of these medical reports, as hearsay evidence, also depends on the circumstances of the case. See Borninkhof v. Department of Justice, 5 M.S.P.R. 77, 83-87 (1981).

First, the letters of Dr. Morales, Dr. Inderias, and P.A. Gorman have very little probative value. They are entirely conclusory, devoid of any medical documentation or explanation in support of their conclusions. In addition, they were not written by their signatories, but rather by the appellant. The report of Dr. Kim is brief and lacking in explanation to support its conclusions, although it is appended with medical documentation. Dr. Kim's conclusion that the appellant does not have diabetes is questionable because it is in stark contrast to the other evidence and medical opinions in the record. The reports of Drs. Zardouz and Sabnani are corroborating with regard to their conclusions that the appellant suffers from peripheral polyneuropathy. Dr. Rosenthal concurs that the appellant suffers from neuropathy, but concludes that it is minimal and would not affect his ability to perform his duties. Dr. Rosenthal contradicts Dr. Sabnani with regard to the appellant's range of motion in his left wrist but does not address the appellant's grip strength. Despite Dr. Saladino's characterization of Dr. Rosenthal's report as a piece of advocacy, that Dr. Rosenthal highlighted in her report the conclusions that differed significantly from those of the agency's doctors does not undermine the report's credibility or reliability; rather, Dr. Rosenthal's report is a thorough, detailed, and relevant medical opinion addressing the medical issues of the agency's removal action.

The contradictory reports of Drs. Kim and Rosenthal call into question the conclusions of Drs. Sabnani, Zardouz, and Saladino regarding the extent of the appellant's neuropathy and wrist ailments. Given these inconsistent medical reports and the absence of direct testimony by any examining doctor, we find that the agency did not prove by preponderant evidence that the appellant has a disqualifying medical condition or that it poses a reasonable probability of causing substantial harm. The agency therefore failed to meet its burden of proof and the ID reversing the agency's action is affirmed as modified by this Opinion and Order.

The agency also alleges that the appellant has failed to articulate a reasonable accommodation of her handicapping condition. For the reasons stated below, we agree with the agency.

Although the four medical experts who testified in this case viewed the appellant as a good candidate for rehabilitation, each expressed the opinion that she needed to commit herself to an ongoing treatment program. Further, one expert stated that the appellant needed access to a counselor at all times to further her treatment and rehabilitation. There is insufficient evidence that the appellant has committed herself to such a program. In addition, although the appellant had been hospitalized in 1988 and 1989, those were emergency interventions, not long-term treatment. The evidence therefore does not indicate that the appellant is committed to the kind of treatment plan that the medical experts indicated was necessary.

Given the overall pattern of the appellant's behavior and the severity of her problems, the appellant has failed to articulate a reasonable accommodation that would preclude a recurrence of violent behavior. Additionally, the appellant's misconduct was so egregious that she was disqualified from her position. See Thompson v. Department of Justice, 51 MSPR 43, 49 (1991). For these reasons, the appellant has failed to demonstrate that she is a "qualified handicapped person." Thus, she has failed to substantiate her allegation of handicap discrimination.

Lawson v. VA, NY-0752-10-0185-I-1 (NP 4/10/2014), examined a disability defense to an adverse action based on an allegedly threatening statement:

On review, the appellant particularly emphasizes her allegations of disability discrimination and notes the medical evidence that was in the record below. Even considering the appellant's allegations of disability discrimination on review, she has failed to identify the existence of a genuine dispute of material fact warranting further adjudication on this issue. White v. U.S. Postal Service, 117 M.S.P.R. 244, ¶ 22 (2012); Redd v. U.S. Postal Service, 101 M.S.P.R. 182, ¶¶ 5, 13-14 (2006). In particular, the appellant alleges that the agency should have accommodated her condition through means other than removal, such as by reassigning her or placing her on paid administrative leave. Although an agency must accommodate employees who are otherwise qualified for their jobs, employees who have committed misconduct are not considered "otherwise qualified." See, e.g., Burton v. U.S. Postal Service, 112 M.S.P.R. 115, ¶ 17 (2009). The appellant had a full and fair opportunity to contest the merits of the charges below. Because the agency proved that the appellant committed the misconduct in charges 1 and 4, the appellant was not "otherwise qualified" and the agency had no obligation under the Americans with Disabilities Act or the Rehabilitation Act to accommodate her asserted conditions in the manner she suggests. See id.; see also Fitzgerald v. Department of Defense, 85 M.S.P.R. 463, ¶ 4 (2000); Laniewicz v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 83 M.S.P.R. 477, ¶ 8 (1999). Further, the law does not immunize a disabled employee from being disciplined for misconduct in the workplace, provided that the agency would impose the same discipline on an employee without a disability. Fitzgerald, 85 M.S.P.R. 463, ¶ 4; Laniewicz, 83 M.S.P.R. 477, ¶ 5. The appellant has not alleged that the agency would not have imposed the same discipline on another employee without a disability who committed similar misconduct. A risk of suicide appeared to preclude consideration of an appellant as a qualified disabled person in DeDonato v. USPS, 25 MSPR 286, 293 (1984), although the Board's analysis of the point was unclear:

Without much analysis, the Board justified removal of a deputy marshal, required to carry a firearm, who experienced a paranoid delusional episode off duty, leading to an agency determination that the appellant could not be entrusted with a weapon. Dept. of Army, 63 MSPR 242, 245-46 (1994), determined that an agency discriminated against an alcoholism counselor with a bipolar disorder when the difficulties caused by that illness, affecting the appellant's behavior, could be controlled through lithium medication that the appellant agreed to take and did use for a substantial period of time:

...What is at issue here thus is not the prognosis for the appellant's medical condition but whether he will continue a course of treatment that will keep that condition under control.

As the administrative judge noted, then, the result in this appeal depends not upon an evaluation of medical evidence or the appellant's past behavior but upon an assessment of the risks involved on the agency's part if it is required to return him to duty as a Counselor. In certain cases, the Board has considered the risks involved in returning an appellant to work. It has found that a "well-grounded" concern existed that an appellant suffering from delusional paranoid disorder would commit violent acts in the future; that appellant, however, carried a gun as part of his duties as a Deputy United States Marshal and clearly suffered from a more serious disorder than the one at issue here, which has not manifested itself in violent conduct or threats of such conduct. See Lassiter, 60 MSPR at 142-46. Moreover, the record in Lassiter did not suggest that the appellant's condition could be completely controlled by medication. See 60 MSPR at 142-47. Other cases in which the Board has emphasized the risks involved in returning an appellant to duty similarly have involved appellants with very serious mental and/or emotional illnesses who have engaged in violent behavior; in those cases, similarly, no showing was made that these conditions could be controlled so that the appellants could perform the duties of their positions without endangering themselves or others. See Flanagan v. U.S. Postal Service, 56 MSPR 134, 135-38 (1992) (paranoid schizophrenic who had engaged in violent behavior); Mazzarella v. U.S. Postal Service, 56 MSPR 47, 51-55 (1992) (appellant with "explosive personality disorder" who had committed "extreme," "out-of-control," hyper-aggressive acts). Finally, we note that the Board has viewed with some caution an agency's generalized protestations of risk in this area, especially where an appellant's medical condition is under control and his job performance is satisfactory. See Anderson v. Department of Transportation, 59 MSPR 585, 590-92 (1993); Stanley v. Department of Justice, 48 MSPR 1, 7-8 (1991).

Moreover, even if the medical evidence in this case is considered, the administrative judge's finding is supported by the record. As the dissent emphasizes below, medical opinion testimony was submitted that suggests that an individual who (like the appellant) has chosen not to take lithium regularly in the past is likely to do so again. Such testimony also revealed, however, that the possibility of compliance with lithium treatment is greater if the individual is forced to confront a significant change in his life, such as the possibility of the loss of his job and/or family, in the event of noncompliance. The administrative judge correctly noted that the agency's removal action certainly presents the appellant with the possibility of such a change and underscores the importance of compliance with lithium treatment.

The record suggests that the appellant has acknowledged the importance of such compliance, because his most recent treating physician (Dr. Cezayirli) has filed a statement asserting that the appellant was well, stable, and taking lithium regularly during the eight months that the appellant was under his care. We recognize that the Board has not always accorded the greatest weight to the most recent medical diagnoses, see Lassiter, 60 MSPR at 145, but Dr. Cezayirli's report was based upon treatment of the appellant for a substantial amount of time, including a period predating the appellant's removal. It is therefore entitled to be accorded significant probative weight. See Bahm, 38 MSPR at 632 (extent and duration of treatment by a physician is relevant in evaluating that physician's medical opinion). On balance, then, we conclude from the available medical evidence that the appellant will continue lithium treatment if returned to duty.

As the administrative judge noted, "[c]ertainly there is a risk which the agency must take if the appellant is returned to duty." We do not gainsay that risk. At the same time, however, the record before us as discussed above does not convince us that the risk here should be allowed to foreclose the appellant's employment as a Counselor or deny him the protections against handicap discrimination guaranteed under law to federal employees. For that reason, we affirm the initial decision and reverse the appellant's removal.

The appellant proved that he is handicapped by his mental condition, manic depression. As noted above, the mere existence of a handicap is insufficient to establish a prima facie case that an appellant is a qualified handicapped employee; he must also articulate a reasonable accommodation that the agency could make to enable him to perform successfully. See Crew, 58 MSPR at 602-03. After an earlier incident when the appellant threatened his supervisors, the agency attempted to accommodate the appellant's handicap. After the later incident, which is the basis for this removal action, the appellant articulated the additional accommodation that the agency assure that he regularly took the medication which kept his manic depression, and the associated threatening behavior, under control.

The record shows that the accommodation that was provided to the appellant did not result in his being able to perform the essential functions of his position without endangering the health and safety of others. After he was afforded the accommodation, the appellant again threatened the safety of his coworkers. Further, the Board finds that the agency showed that the additional accommodation articulated by the appellant would be an undue hardship. Having agency officials supervise the appellant's medication schedule is highly impractical to effect. Moreover, it is inappropriate, because it causes the agency to assume the appellant's responsibility and interferes in the appellant's relationship with his physician. In short, the agency proved that the appellant is not a qualified handicapped individual because, with reasonable accommodation, he could not perform the duties of his position without endangering the safety of others, and the additional accommodation requested by the appellant after the agency proposed his removal imposed an undue hardship on the agency. The Board finds therefore that the appellant did not prove handicap discrimination.

A history of some violent or antisocial behavior caused by a mental disability will not require accommodation by the agency through continued employment of an employee whose disability could be controlled by medication, but who will not adhere to a treatment plan requiring the employee to take the medication on a regular basis. See Franklin v. USPS, 687 F. Supp. 1214 (S.D. Ohio 1988).

Because a disabling medical condition, aggravated by routine job stress, meant that the agency could not utilize the appellant as a food inspector or grader working with and around machinery and knives, the employee was not a qualified disabled employee whose accommodation was required. Bonde v. USDA, 9 MSPR 545, 547 (1982). Dept. of Navy, 55 MSPR 209, 213 (1992) (visual problems of an air conditioning mechanic made accommodation impossible when the job called for the operation of a crane and forklift; the operation of that equipment with the appellant's limitations would endanger the safety of the appellant and other employees).

If an employee has a disabling condition under control and his job performance is satisfactory, the agency must be careful before attempting to remove the employee only on the basis that there is some risk that the condition may reoccur. As to an individual whose condition rendered him somewhat more susceptible to stroke and who occupied the law enforcement position of correctional officer, Stanley v. DOJ, 48 MSPR 1, 8 (1991):

[E]ven if we assume arguendo that the appellant's risk of serious injury was greater than other employees, the agency had previously accommodated other physically handicapped employees by limiting their exposure to stress and risk while still permitting them to perform as Correctional Officers, and the agency has not shown that the appellant could not be similarly accommodated....

The Board does not accept generalized protestations of risk by agencies whose missions and organic statutes, although requiring a high regard for public safety, also require accommodation for disabilities. In Anderson v. Dept. of Transp., 59 MSPR 585, 590-92 (1993), the agency argued that an air traffic control specialist position was so sensitive that individuals employed in that position who are afflicted by alcoholism should not be provided Rehabilitation Act protections. Although the employee ultimately did not prevail in his affirmative defense because he received reasonable accommodation, an element of risk was subsumed by an agency-specific accommodative requirement:

In its petition for review, the agency contends that the appellant was not a qualified handicapped person under 29 CFR 1613.702(f). It argues that, because of the critical nature of air traffic control functions, "safety-related" employees performing those functions should not be afforded the same protections under 29 CFR 1613.702 as individuals not so employed. The agency further argues that "the inherent safety concerns and risks associated with substance abuse must emphatically override any obligation the agency has to allow a demonstration of successful rehabilitation."

In support of its contention, the agency cites Borsari v. FAA, 699 F.2d 106, 110-11 (2d Cir. 1983), cert. denied, 464 U.S. 833 (1983). There, the court found that removal of an air traffic controller would promote the efficiency of the service, where the controller had been found guilty of the sale and possession of illegal drugs. However, in that case, there was no allegation that the appellant was handicapped, and thus there was no determination as to whether the agency had a duty to accommodate the appellant.

The agency also cites Kulling v. Dept. of Transp., 24 MSPR 56, 59 (1984), in which the Board found that, due to the agency's overriding concern for public safety, the agency was not required to accommodate an air traffic controller who abused drugs; and it cites Cavallaro v. Dept. of Transp., 20 MSPR 701, 706 (1984), in which the Board found that accommodation of an employee's alcoholism was not required where the condition caused the employee to engage in misconduct that threatened the agency's personnel and property. We agree that these decisions offer some support for the agency's position. We note, however, that, since the above-cited cases were decided, Congress has enacted section 614(c)(2) of the Omnibus Transportation Employee Testing Act, Pub. L. 102-143 (1991), which amends Title VI of the Federal Aviation Act of 1958, 49 App. USC 1421 et seq., and which provides, with regard to the agency, that:

The Administrator shall establish and maintain a rehabilitation program which at a minimum provides for the identification and opportunity for treatment of those employees...whose duties include responsibility for safety-sensitive functions...who are in need of assistance in resolving problems with the use of alcohol or controlled substances (emphasis supplied).

The plain language of this legislation evidences a specific legislative concern that an "opportunity for treatment" be afforded to employees of the agency who require assistance in dealing with alcoholism or substance abuse, even where those employees are responsible for safety-related functions. In our view, the agency's position on petition for review is simply incompatible with this legislative enactment.

Further, the agency's own EAP guidance manual, adopted subsequent to the issuance of the decisions relied upon by the agency, is also incompatible with the agency's position. Although that manual provides that a safety-related employee suffering from alcoholism will be removed from his normal safety-related duties and assigned to nonsafety-related duties during the initial phase of rehabilitation, it also provides that he may resume his safety-related duties during a later phase, when the EAP Manager and the flight surgeon have determined that he is alcohol-free. The EAP manual emphasizes that "[N]either the employee nor the agency is best served by having the employee unnecessarily delayed from returning to safety/sensitive-related duties."

Thus, contrary to the agency's contentions, the fact that the appellant is employed as an air traffic controller does not preclude a finding that he is a qualified handicapped person under 29 CFR 1613.702(f), and does not relieve the agency of its obligation to provide reasonable accommodation, including a reasonable opportunity to demonstrate rehabilitation. Therefore, under all the circumstances of this case, the administrative judge correctly found that the appellant was a qualified handicapped person under 29 CFR 1613.702.
