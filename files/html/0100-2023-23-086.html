broida mspb Chapter 16: Settlement, Remedies, and Enforcement Chapter 16: Settlement, Remedies, and Enforcement, Subchapter 02: Appellate Remedies, Section B: Back Pay and Related Remedies, Subsection 10: Interest--Back Pay Act, Part a: Interest--Postal Service Employee
a. Interest--Postal Service Employee

It appears that Postal Service employees who prevail in cases involving a finding of discrimination obtain interest on back pay, as do preference eligibles who prevail in any Board case resulting in back pay, but the law is unclear as to the right of Postal Service nonpreference eligibles who prevail in Board litigation to obtain interest on back pay awards if there is no finding of discrimination.

Following Loeffler v. Frank, 108 S. Ct. 1965 (1988), Dancer v. USPS, 38 MSPR 224, 227-28 (1988), determined that prejudgment interest may be awarded against the Postal Service to the same extent that interest is recoverable from a private party as a normal incident of a lawsuit under Title VII. See Gay v. USPS, 41 MSPR 476, 482 (1989) (interest followed a determination of illegal discrimination); Hooks v. USPS, 40 MSPR 400, 402 (1989) ("The Supreme Court has held that interest may be awarded against the United States Postal Service to the same extent that interest is recoverable from a private party as a normal incident of suit. Loeffler v. Frank, 108 S. Ct. 1965, 1970 (1988). Subsequent to the Supreme Court's ruling in Loeffler, the Board held that interest on back pay is appropriately awardable to an appellant who prevails in a Title VII appeal. Dancer v. U. S. Postal Service, 38 M.S.P.R. 224 (1988). However, the Board has also held that an appellant is not entitled to interest on a back pay award in a non-Title VII case involving the U. S. Postal Service because the U. S. Postal Service is not subject to the Back Pay Act. Norvell v. U. S. Postal Service, 38 M.S.P.R. 563, 568 n.4 (1988)."). The Board's ruling was not dependent on amendments to the Back Pay Act; the amendments were deemed inapplicable to the Postal Service. Norvell v. USPS, 38 MSPR 563, 568 n.4 (1988) (noting that in cases not arising under Title VII, authority to award interest to Postal Service employees depends on entitlements in Postal Service regulations). Rates were to be those set by the National Labor Relations Board. The NLRB uses a rate known as the underpayment rate, i.e., the rate paid by the taxpayer on additional taxes, computed as the short-term federal rate plus 3%. The short-term federal rate is determined quarterly, the rate for any calendar quarter being the rate determined by the Secretary of the Treasury on the first month of the previous calendar quarter. Postal regulations governing interest awards on back pay for cases not arising under Title VII call for the application of the Federal Judgment Rate established at 28 USC 1961. Thrasher v. USPS, 40 MSPR 582, 584 (1989); see Dancer, supra.

Despite a contrary implication in Rolle v. USPS, 44 MSPR 323, 325 (1990), that 1988 revisions to § 436.62 of the ELM would provide interest on back pay to nonpreference eligibles of the Postal Service without a finding of discrimination, James v. USPS, 60 MSPR 504, 507-08 (1994), concerning a nonpreference eligible whose appeal did not involve a finding of discrimination, determined that the ELM did not create interest entitlement:

We turn now to the question of whether the appellant is entitled to receive interest. The U.S. Supreme Court has held that interest on back pay may be awarded against the Postal Service to the same extent that interest is recoverable from a private party as a normal incident of suit.[12] Subsequent to the Supreme Court's ruling in Loeffler, the Board held that interest on back pay is appropriately awardable to an appellant who prevails against the Postal Service in a Title VII appeal.[13]

[12] See Loeffler v. Frank, 486 U.S. 549, 108 S. Ct. 1965, 100 L. Ed.2d 549 (1988).

[13] See Dancer v. US Postal Service, 38 MSPR 224, 226-27 (1988).

The appellant in this case, however, did not allege discrimination as a basis for her appeal. The Board has determined that an appellant is not entitled to interest on a back-pay award in a non-Title VII case involving the Postal Service because the Postal Service is not subject to the Back Pay Act. Nevertheless, the Board has also concluded that in a non-Title VII case involving the Postal Service "any support for an award of interest must derive from the U.S. Postal Service regulations."

According to the Postal Service's Employee and Labor Relations Manual (ELM), "[i]nterest is paid on back pay only when it is expressly awarded" in a Board decision. Here, the administrative judge's decision specifically ordered the agency to pay the appellant "the appropriate amount of back pay, with interest, and benefits as required by Postal Service regulations" (emphasis supplied). Under Postal Service regulations, however, the agency would not be required to pay interest to the appellant. ELM section 436.61 states that:

This section establishes procedures for paying interest when the Postal Service is obligated by law to pay it. This section is not intended to create any obligation to pay interest that the Postal Service is not otherwise obligated to pay.

Consequently, because Postal Service regulations do not require it to pay interest in this type of case, we find that the agency is not obligated to pay interest to the appellant on the back-pay award.

Evans v. USPS, 110 MSPR 58, 2008 MSPB 210 (2008), supra, awarding interest to a nonpreference eligible employee did not mention James, supra, suggesting that the ELM has changed, or that the Board's position has changed without analysis of James.

In 1993 the Board decided that when preference eligible employees prevail against the Postal Service, the Back Pay Act governs back pay matters, including interest entitlement. Prevailing preference eligibles receive interest, and the interest is calculated in accordance with the Back Pay Act, not the National Labor Relations Act. Green v. USPS, 57 MSPR 560, 565 (1993) (citing Andress v. USPS, 56 MSPR 501, 508 (1993), overruling Frazier v. USPS, 26 MSPR 584, 585 (1985)); see Davis v. USPS, 64 MSPR 652, 661 (1994) (reaffirming Andress on an OPM reconsideration petition). Under the Back Pay Act, interest is calculated daily (rather than annually under the USPS ELM) and is due through the date that the appellant receives his check. Cavanagh v. USPS, 65 MSPR 232, 239 (1994); see Corsiglia v. USPS, 72 MSPR 65, 70 (1996) (interest on a back pay award was to be paid at the overpayment rate in the Internal Revenue Code, not at the federal judgment rate, in the settlement of the case of a preference eligible employee); Johnson v. USPS, 71 MSPR 303, 308 (1996) (Postal Service employees are entitled to interest calculated in accordance with the Back Pay Act, not agency regulations: "[t]he agency has [incorrectly] paid the appellant interest at the Federal Judgment rate, which is significantly lower than the overpayment rate set forth in the Internal Revenue Code, 26 USC 6621(a)(1).").

As to the calculus, or explanation, for interest for Postal Service nonpreference eligibles, Evans v. USPS, 110 MSPR 58, 63-64 ¶¶ 11-13, 2008 MSPB 210 (2008), determined:

According to the ELM, the rate of interest on back pay for a nonpreference eligible employee of the U.S. Postal Service, such as the appellant, is calculated according to the Federal Judgment rate, as referenced in 28 U.S.C. § 1961. ELM, § 436.73. Section 1961 provides that the interest due on a money judgment shall be calculated from the date of the entry of the judgment at a rate equal to the weekly average 1-year constant maturity Treasury yield, as published by the Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System, for the calendar week preceding the date of judgment and that the interest shall be compounded annually. 28 U.S.C. § 1961(a) and (b).

Here, the Board issued an Order dated February 23, 2007, ordering the agency to cancel the appellant's removal and substitute a demotion to the next lower-graded nonsupervisory position with the least reduction in pay or grade and to pay the appellant the correct amount of back pay, with interest. Evans, 105 M.S.P.R. at 62. The agency's obligation to place the appellant in the proper position and to pay him back pay was established as of the date of the Board's February 23, 2007 Order. Thus, we find that February 23, 2007, is the relevant date for purposes of selecting the interest rate because that is the date of the Board's final decision on the merits of the appeal. Cf. Marathon Oil Company v. United States, 374 F.3d 1123, 1128 (Fed. Cir. 2004) (court found that its decision, which was the last action in a breach of contract suit and which did not remand any substantive disputes on liability, was final judgment for purposes of 28 U.S.C. § 1961(c)(2) that requires the government to pay post-judgment interest on final judgments against the United States rendered in the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit).

The applicable interest rate for the week ending February 16, 2007, one week before the Board's Order on the merits of the appeal, was 5.07%. As the statute refers to "a rate," only a single rate should be applied for the duration of the interest accrual period. Kaiser Aluminum & Chemical Corp. v. Bonjorno, 494 U.S. 827, 838-39 (1990). Accordingly, the rate of 5.07% is the correct rate to be applied for the entire accrual period. Because the agency used a lower rate to compute the interest owed the appellant, it must recompute the amount of interest owed and pay the appellant the difference between the amount of recalculated interest and the amount already paid, $132.88.

See Driscoll v. USPS, 113 MSPR 565, 568-69 ¶¶ 9-12, 2010 MSPB 82 (2010) (explaining application of the ELM and Federal Judgment Rate to back pay for nonpreference eligible employees of the Postal Service and noting that under Evans v. USPS, 110 MSPR 58 (2008), the date seven days prior to the date of the initial decision is the appropriate date for determining the rate of interest on back pay); cf. Walls v. USPS, PH-0752-11-0287-X-1 (NP 8/12/2014) ("Here, the administrative judge issued an initial decision on August 1, 2011. On 7 days prior to August 1, 2011, the rate was .21 percent. Accordingly, we find that the agency correctly used the interest rate set forth in its ELM to calculate the interest on the appellant's back pay.").

Under Toth v. USPS, 85 MSPR 404, 407 ¶ 8 (2000), a preference eligible's entitlement to interest on a back pay recovery under a settlement is defeated if the agreement reveals an intent not to pay interest. Toth was denied interest when the agreement specified that the appellant would receive back pay in accordance with § 436.62 of the ELM (providing that interest is paid on back pay only when it is expressly awarded in a settlement agreement or decision). See Rivas v. USPS, 72 MSPR 383, 392 (1996) (interest on a settlement with a nonpreference eligible employee would be calculated under § 436.63 of the ELM based on the Federal Judgment Rate); Mathis v. USPS, 72 MSPR 180, 185 (1996) (when a settlement agreement called for interest to be paid to appellant, a nonpreference eligible employee, but the agreement did not specify the method of calculation, agency regulations controlled the calculus).
