2. Information Concerning Managers, Supervisors

FLRA determined to be appropriate a request, arising from a union's representation under the negotiated procedure, for all documents concerning disciplinary action taken by the Germany Region, consisting of some 27 civilian personnel offices, against unit employees during the prior three years for making false statements. The agency was incorrect in furnishing only the proposal and decision letters rather than all of the material in the files. But the agency could withhold documentation concerning managers and supervisors: they would perform different duties and the agency would be governed by different considerations in deciding the degree of appropriate discipline for them. DODDS and Overseas Educ. Ass'n, N. Germany Area Council, 19 FLRA 790, 792 (1985). The OEA case was reviewed in court, N. Germany Area Council, Overseas Educ. Ass'n v. FLRA, 805 F.2d 1044, 1047-49 (D.C. Cir. 1986), and remanded for further consideration:

The Authority's ruling seems to suggest that the standards of expected behavior for unit employees and managerial employees might differ. But the Authority offers no guidance for determining why, in this case, such standards differ. In support of its holding, the Authority does not cite any FLRA cases which hold that information on falsification by supervisors is irrelevant to the disciplining of unit employees for the same offense.

[I]t is for an arbitrator or deciding authority to take this factor into account when assessing what penalty, if any, to impose on the grievant.... It is generally accepted in arbitration practice that enforcement of rules and assessment of discipline be exercised in a consistent manner unless a reasonable basis exists for the variation. Elkouri & Elkouri, How Arbitration Works, 684-85 (4th ed. 1985).

...

...Arbitrators regularly consider such evidence as relevant to determining whether a unit employee has been disciplined for just cause. See, e.g., Kast Metals Corp., 70 LA 278, 283-84 (Roberts 1978); Peerless Laundry Co., 51 LA 331, 335 (Eaton 1968); Potlatch Forests, Inc., 51 LA 685, 688 (Williams 1968); Lukens Steel Co., 42 LA 849, 850 (Crawford 1964); Great Atlantic & Pacific Tea Co., 39 LA 823, 825 (Turkus 1962).

...

[C]ases before the...[MSPB] suggest that agencies must establish consistent reasons for disciplining a particular employee more severely than other employees charged with the same offense....

[A]s the cases before the MSPB suggest, the Authority has not explained adequately why supervisors might be held to a different standard of trust than are unit employees. If anything, supervisors might be held to an even higher standard of conduct because of the greater fiduciary nature of their duties.

On remand, the FLRA reversed its earlier position, DODDS and N. Germany Area Council, Overseas Educ. Ass'n, 28 FLRA 202, 205 (1987):

The Union sought the information concerning the discipline of management officials and supervisors for making false statements to establish whether the unit employee was being treated differently for the same or similar misconduct. We find that the information was necessary for the Union to effectively develop and present its arguments in the disciplinary action proceeding. Access to the information sought was particularly necessary in this case because...there was evidence of a number of relevant situations in which management officials were alleged to have made false statements. We therefore conclude that the information requested by the Union was necessary within the meaning of section 7114(b)(4)....

Dept. of Transp., FAA, New England Region and NAATS, 38 FLRA 1623, 1632-33 (1991), followed DODDS, concluding that the agency committed a ULP when it refused to provide information to the union concerning penalties meted out to (and disciplinary records involving) supervisors who were allegedly involved in incidents of travel voucher fraud similar to incidents leading to the discipline of unit employees. It was immaterial that the information concerned an issue raised for the first time in preparation for arbitration. The union agreed to accept the documents in sanitized form; the balance in favor of disclosure tipped against whatever privacy interests were involved in the records requested. Refer to Dept. of Treasury, IRS and NTEU, 40 FLRA 303, 310-11 (1991), implicitly rejecting the notion that the type of information available to the union is limited by what the MSPB might deem appropriate for disclosure through discovery in an appeal before that agency. Decisions discussed in earlier sections that treat requests for information about supervisors include DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, FCI Forrest City and AFGE Local 0922, 57 FLRA 808 (2002), enforcement granted in part remanded for further consideration, FLRA v. DOJ, 395 F.3d 845 (8th Cir. 2005), on remand, DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, FCI Forrest City and AFGE Local 922, 62 FLRA 308, 308-11 (2007); Dept. of Labor, Wash., DC and Nat'l Council of Field Labor Locals, AFGE, 39 FLRA 531, 538, 543 (1991); and Dept. of Commerce, Bureau of Census and AFGE Local 2782, 24 FLRA 630, 632 (1986).
