h. Agency Deliberative Process Privilege

Some agencies treat discovery requests as FOIA requests and claim exemption from discovery of materials exempt from FOIA disclosure under 5 USC 552(b)(5), making FOIA disclosure provisions inapplicable to "inter-agency or intra-agency memorandums or letters which would not be available by law to a party other than an agency in litigation with the agency."

If internal communications were not subject to discovery and hearing procedures, there would be little else to a Board hearing than a recitation by the deciding official of the facts of record and the penalty she selected. See Barber v. FDIC, 37 MSPR 620, 626 (1988) (concurring opinion of Vice Chairman Johnson on denial of agency PFR: "[T]he disciplinary proceeding before the Board members was not similar to a judicial proceeding where the reasons for their decisionmaking were beyond scrutiny. Issues concerning the reasonableness of the penalty and harmful ex parte communications with the deciding official are, in fact, subject to review by the MSPB and the courts."). Vogel v. DOJ, 9 MSPR 382, 386-87 (1982), considered, but did not decide, whether "deliberative process" privilege blocks production of information concerning an appealed personnel decision:

The Board's regulations at 5 CFR §§ 1201.71-1201.73 provide that the presiding official will oversee and direct the discovery process. Moreover, 5 CFR § 1201.72(b) states that "[a]ny person may be examined regarding any nonprivileged matter which is relevant to the issue under appeal...." The "deliberative process" privilege asserted by the Department is one of the traditional evidentiary privileges available to the government in civil litigation. It is designed to protect "the consultive functions of government by maintaining the confidentiality of advisory opinions, recommendations and deliberations, which comprise the process by which governmental decisions and processes are formulated."[3] The Board has not previously decided whether such a privilege with respect to agency predecisional deliberations concerning personnel actions should be recognized in its proceedings in view of the Board's duty to ensure that personnel actions are neither taken nor recommended for prohibited reasons. This fundamental question has not been addressed by the parties and need not be decided here. Assuming that the privilege is not applicable in Board proceedings, the presiding official's ruling requires reversal only if our examination of the withheld documents reveals that appellant could have been prejudiced by the ruling. Conversely, assuming that we should recognize the privilege for personnel deliberations, the qualified nature of the privilege requires us to inspect the documents to determine whether the presiding official correctly ruled that appellant has not made a sufficient showing of need to overcome the privilege.

[3] Jordan v. U.S. Department of Justice, 591 F.2d 753, 772 (D.C. Cir. 1978).

Our examination of the documents sought by appellant revealed no factual material which was not already part of the record. As the presiding official found, the documents discuss questions of law and policy and make recommendations concerning personnel actions under consideration in Mr. Vogel's case. There is nothing in these advisory memoranda which could in any way support appellant's contention that he was not reassigned for the reason asserted by the Department, i.e., to remedy the effects of his undisputed misconduct on his working relationships in Arizona. There is no suggestion that the decision was motivated by any prohibited purpose, such as a desire to deprive appellant of appeal rights by inducing his resignation. Accordingly, we find that the appellant was not prejudiced by the ruling even if erroneous.[4]

[4] Evidentiary privileges should not be lightly granted since they are "impediments to ascertaining [the]truth." In re Dinnan, 661 F.2d 426, 427 (5th Cir. 1981). Privileges "contravene the fundamental principle that 'the public...has a right to every man's evidence' [citations omitted]." Trammel v. United States, 445 U.S. 40, 48, 100 S. Ct. 906, 911, 63 L. Ed.2d 186 (1980). It could be argued that the purpose of the privilege--to foster the candid exchange of ideas in governmental decisionmaking--is not furthered by applying the privilege to internal agency personnel matters, since such matters are incidental to the public purpose of the governmental agency and are not essentially different from private sector personnel matters except insofar as Congress has regulated public personnel administration by law (e.g., by the Civil Service Reform Act). But cf. McClelland v. Andrus, 606 F.2d 1278, 1287 (D.C. Cir. 1979). In any case, it is clear that even under the privilege, production of the documents should have been ordered if in camera inspection had shown that they contained pertinent factual material or had any relevant bearing in support of appellant's claims. See Mead Data Cent, Inc. v. United States, 566 F.2d 242, 256 (D.C. Cir. 1977); Jepsen v. Fla. Bd. of Regents, 610 F.2d 1379, 1384 (5th Cir. 1980). Here, our examination of the documents establishes, on the contrary, that they have no such relevance.

The Board further explained its approach, Johnson v. Dept. of Treasury, 8 MSPR 170, 174-76, 7 MSPB 649 (1981):

This Board has held that discovery requests should not be evaluated in terms of compliance with 5 CFR 752.404(b) which gives an employee the right to review material "relied upon" to support a proposed adverse action. Bize, supra, 264. This Board held in Bize that there is no provision in the Board's regulations which allows for the exclusion of evidence requested in discovery because the agency did not rely on it. The Board has defined discovery as "the process whereby a party may obtain information...for the purpose of assisting...in planning and developing his/her case." 5 CFR 1201.72. Evidence which assists in planning a case may or may not be admissible, but as in FRCP 26(b), it is discoverable.

Since one of the main functions of the Board is the adjudication of cases within its jurisdiction, 5 USC 1205, the fairness of such adjudications can only be enhanced by disclosure of the facts in a case. Therefore, uncertainty as to the relevancy of requested evidence should be resolved in the favor of the movant, absent any undue delay of hardship caused by such request. Bize, id., 266.

As we said in Bize, id., 266:

The evidence denied appellant in this case was central to the case. The report detailed an investigation conducted into the charge which were subsequently levied against appellant, and selected portions of the report were relied on by the agency. It is reasonable to infer that the report contains summaries of witness interview which were not disclosed to appellant. Without imputing any bad faith to the agency, it is reasonable to conclude that even if they were not exculpatory in nature, such summaries could lead to exculpatory evidence, or other witnesses. Considering that the agency had resources to conduct interviews nationwide, access to the report would be helpful to the appellant, if for no other reason than to assist him in deciding how to commit his resources. Notably, production of the report would have placed no burden on the agency nor would it have delayed the proceedings.

In addition, the Board has reviewed appellant's request for material and finds certain other evidence denied appellant as being discoverable. The memorandum prepared by the hearing officer James Franke had to have been considered by the deciding official or appellant's oral reply would have been meaningless. 5 USC 7513(b)(2). The memorandum prepared for file by the deciding official may perhaps list reasons why a less severe penalty was not considered. This would clearly be relevant in light of our decision in Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 MSPB 313 (1981). Finally, the internal audit report, like the investigation report, may possibly lead to exculpatory evidence, or other witnesses. Therefore, we conclude that the presiding official erroneously denied appellant's request for production of the evidence.

Invocation of deliberative process privilege was commented on in Tatsis v. DHUD, CB-7121-16-0003-V-1 (NP 6/16/2016), at footnotes 8 and 9:

...Unlike the FOIA privilege, the common law deliberative process privilege can be asserted only by the head of the agency, or an official to whom such authority has been delegated by the head of the agency, and must be asserted in the form of a declaration or affidavit. See Marriott International Resorts, L.P. v. United States, 437 F.3d 1302, 1308 (Fed. Cir. 2006) (holding that assertion of the common law deliberative process privilege can be delegated); see also CACI Field Services, Inc. v. United States, 12 Cl. Ct. 680, 686 n.7 (Cl. Ct. 1987) (distinguishing the procedural requirements for invoking the FOIA and common law deliberative process privileges).

5 U.S.C. § 7114(b)(4) provides that the agency is required to furnish data "to the extent not prohibited by law." However, FOIA exemptions, such as the FOIA deliberative process privilege, are exemptions from mandatory disclosure in response to FOIA requests only; they do not prohibit agency disclosure of data or information. See Chrysler Corporation v. Brown, 441 U.S. 281, 292 (1979) (finding that the FOIA exemptions are not a mandatory bar to an agency's disclosure of information). Accordingly, FOIA does not prohibit the agency from providing the emails in response to the union's information request pursuant to 5 U.S.C. § 7114(b)(4).

The Federal Labor Relations Authority distinguished the agency deliberative process privilege from disclosure of personnel information to union officials making a routine statutory information request under 5 USC 7114, mentioned in Tatsis, supra, Dept. of Treasury, IRS and NTEU, 40 FLRA 1070, 1080-82 (1991):

Section 7114(b)(4) of the Statute limits an agency's duty to furnish data "to the extent not prohibited by law[.] " In order for the "prohibited by law" provision of section 7114(b)(4) to apply, "there must be something somewhere in the law that forbids that data's disclosure." NLRB Union, Local 6, 842 F.2d at 486.

FOIA Exemption 5 provides that the FOIA does not apply to matters that are:

[I]nter-agency or intra-agency memorandums or letters which would not be available by law to a party other than an agency in litigation with the agency[.]

Courts have interpreted Exemption 5 as incorporating the governmental deliberative process privilege, which protects advice, recommendations, and opinions that are part of the deliberative, consultative, decision-making process of government. NLRB v. Sears, Roebuck and Company, 421 U.S. 132, 150-154 (1975); Environmental Protection Agency v. Mink, 410 U.S. 73, 85-91 (1973).

We conclude that neither FOIA Exemption 5 nor the privilege it incorporates prohibits the disclosure of the requested documents. The Authority has previously determined that the FOIA does not prohibit release of data: rather, it permits agencies to invoke certain exceptions to withhold data falling within those exceptions. Dept. of Army Headquarters, XVIII Airborne Corps and Fort Bragg, 26 FLRA 407, 412-13 (1987). Thus, neither FOIA Exemption 5 nor the governmental deliberative process privilege it incorporates prohibits by law the disclosure of the documents in this case under section 7114(b)(4) of the Statute.

Even assuming that FOIA Exemption 5 and the governmental deliberative process privilege are applicable to section 7114(b)(4) of the Statute, we conclude that statutory provision and the privilege it incorporates do not forbid the release of documents in the circumstances of this case. Thus, in our view, FOIA Exemption 5 does not forbid the disclosure of data, as in this case, that was part of a deliberative process in an agency personnel decision. Rather, based on a review of court decisions, we conclude that FOIA Exemption 5 is intended to apply only to data that was part of the deliberative process in the formulation of agency policy. In this regard, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit stated in Coastal States Gas Corp. that FOIA Exemption 5 and the governmental deliberative process privilege protect against premature disclosure of proposed "policies before they have been finally formulated[.] " 617 F.2d at 866. Further, the Supreme Court in Sears stated, with respect to FOIA Exemption 5, that "frank discussion of legal or policy matters in writing might be inhibited if the discussion were made public, and that the decisions and policies formulated would be the poorer as a result." 421 U.S. at 150 (citation omitted).... The clear tenor of these cases is that the privilege protects documents prepared in the formulation of policy on behalf of an agency and not the type of personnel action at issue herein.

...

[T]he documents at issue in this case concern only one personnel action regarding one employee that has no reasonably foreseeable effect on the overall policy direction of the Agency. Therefore, we find that AFGE and Ryan do not support the conclusion that FOIA Exemption 5 and the governmental deliberative process privilege protect from disclosure documents written to assist an agency in making individual personnel decisions. Accordingly, we conclude that the documents sought in this case are not prohibited by law from disclosure.
