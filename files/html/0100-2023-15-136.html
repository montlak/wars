6. Cumulative Insubordination

If the same order is issued at successive intervals, whether by the same or higher authority, an agency cannot increase the severity of the penalty if it can reasonably anticipate that the order will not be obeyed. The successive incidents merge into one for the purpose of determining the maximum appropriate penalty. Ibarra v. Dept. of Air Force, 11 MSPR 110, 112 (1982) ("Twice within a few days of each other in May 1980, appellant was ordered in writing by his military supervisors to report for weapons qualification. He refused to report, even though he had been informed that his weapons qualification had expired."). Three charges of disrespectful conduct on one day should be treated as one incident, rather than as continuing insolent conduct warranting removal. Hemelt v. VA, 21 MSPR 74, 77 (1984). If an agency takes one set of facts and from that charges insubordination and disrespectful conduct, the charges may be merged. Ruffin v. Dept. of Army, 35 MSPR 499, 503 (1987). Charges should not be separately stated that describe very closely related incidents of AWOL and failure to follow procedures governing requests for leave. Meads v. VA, 36 MSPR 574, 582 (1988); see Eichner v. USPS, 83 MSPR 202, 211 ¶ 19 (1999) (insubordinate conduct: removal was mitigated to a three-week suspension; it involved one continuing incident, rather than a pattern or practice of the failure to follow instructions; the appellant's attempt to help another employee resulted in a delay of five-to-eight minutes before compliance with a directive to leave agency premises); Smith v. Dept. of Air Force, 48 MSPR 594, 600 (1991) (charges of insubordination and insolence: "Further, the offenses occurred on the same day and arose out of one continuing situation[.] "); cf. Oates v. Dept. of Labor, CB-7121-12-0022-V-1 (NP 5/2/2013) (n.9: "The appellant argues that the agency 'piled on' the AWOL specifications in an effort to have him removed. Although the Board would frown upon an agency sandbagging one of its own employees in such a way, we find that that is not what happened here. The record shows that the appellant's supervisor warned him at least once during this time period that he was accruing significant amounts of AWOL. Yet the appellant continued to accrue more AWOL, thus piling the incidents on himself. Moreover, we find that the specifications refer to discrete acts of misconduct and are not duplicative in any way. Cf. Southers v. Veterans Administration, 813 F.2d 1223, 1225-26 (Fed. Cir. 1987)."); Wolak v. Dept. of Army, 53 MSPR 251, 258 (1992) (discussing merger of charges outside the context of insubordination); Southers v. VA, 813 F.2d 1223 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (the agency was not to "pile on" as separate charges of misrepresentation that the same answer was given to the same repeated question repeated). But cf. Mingledough v. VA, 88 MSPR 452, 459 ¶ 15 (2001) (mitigation analysis: the appellant disobeyed an order ten times in comparison to a case involving a refusal to comply with two lawful orders); Higgins v. DHHS, 12 MSPR 167, 170 (1982) (agency could impose a suspension for a period of AWOL, hoping that the action would bring the employee back to work, and then impose removal for the AWOL that followed when the employee did not return). Stephens v. Dept. of Air Force, 34 MSPR 649, 651-52 (1987), reassessed the analysis of continuing insubordination. Appellant was removed for refusal to comply with proper orders to do certain work on Friday, August 8, 1986, and Monday, August 11, 1986:

In Ibarra, the appellant contended that the agency failed to impose progressive discipline, as required by its own regulations. The Board held that the two incidents of disobedience to an order, which occurred within a few days of each order, merged into one offense for the purpose of determining the maximum appropriate penalty under the agency's table of penalties. The Board stated that: "An agency, by continuing to issue the same order at various intervals...cannot increase the severity of the penalty in a case such as this, where the agency may reasonably anticipate that the order will not be obeyed."

In Hemelt, 21 MSPR at 77, the Board held that although three charges of improper conduct were sustained, all charges occurred on the same day and, in essence, involved one incident. Hemelt had no prior disciplinary record, and the Board found that a 60-day suspension was the maximum reasonable penalty because there was no evidence of a continuing, insolent attitude warranting removal.

In the instant case, it is true that the appellant was charged with...two instances of a failure to obey the same order. The administrative judge's discussion of the appropriateness of the penalty, unlike Hemelt however, did not rely on the fact that there were two sustained charges to impose a more severe penalty. Rather, the administrative judge found that the appellant was given several opportunities to comply with the same order to do certain work and he continued to refuse to do it. After he refused to do the work on a Friday, he was given time over a weekend to reconsider whether he would do the work assigned to him; the two refusals were not in close proximity and we do not find a basis, as in Ibarra, to conclude that the agency could have anticipated with a reasonable degree of certainty that the second order would not be obeyed. Thus, we find that it was proper for the administrative judge to consider that the appellant was given another opportunity to comply.

This case is also different from Southers v. Veterans Administration, 813 F.2d 1223 (Fed. Cir. 1987). In Southers, the court found that an agency's nineteen charges of false testimony were duplicative because they involved answers to the same question, slightly rephrased, and the agency had reason to believe that the appellant would answer the questions in the same manner. The court found that the Board committed harmful error when it sustained nineteen charges of false testimony and that only one charge should have been sustained. Here, however, the agency had no reason to expect continued insubordination; and here, even if only one charge is sustained, the penalty is appropriate.

Unlike Ibarra and Hemelt, the appellant in this case had a prior disciplinary record. Assuming arguendo that the administrative judge should have sustained only one charge of a failure to comply with an order, because of the appellant's past disciplinary record, the penalty of removal was within the bounds of reasonableness for even one instance of failure to obey a proper order, particularly when the appellant's reprimand and 5-day suspension and lack of rehabilitation potential are considered. See Shaw v. U.S. Government Printing Office, 26 MSPR 664 (1985) (removal for refusal to accept assignment to a detail was within the bounds of reasonableness); Bonds v. Department of Army, 20 MSPR 349 (1984) (one incident of insubordination, where appellant had a past disciplinary record, warranted removal).

Stated concisely, incidents not in close proximity may form the basis for distinct charges of insubordination. Washington v. VA, 49 MSPR 632, 638-39 (1991) (incidents were distinct that were separated by about two weeks; the appellant performed his assigned duties during the interval between the orders that he disobeyed), rev'd on other grounds, Washington v. VA (Fed. Cir. 1992 NP No. 91-3592); see Phillips v. GSA, 38 MSPR 206, 211 (1988) (incidents occurring for a period of at least two months), rev'd on other grounds, Phillips v. GSA, 878 F.2d 370, 373 (Fed. Cir. 1989). [Refer to Chapter 7, "Multiplicity and Merger of Charges."]
