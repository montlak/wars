o. OWCP Determination

Cantrell v. United States, 356 F.2d 915, 915-16 (4th Cir. 1966), aff'g Cantrell v. United States, 240 F. Supp. 851 (W.D.S.C. 1965), commented on the relation between workers' compensation and disability retirement determinations:

Appellant contends that because his original leg injury was employment-related, only the Compensation Board could determine his physical condition, and since the Board found only a partial disability of his leg, the Civil Service Commission could not for purposes of retirement find him "disabled."

However, the Compensation Act and the Retirement Act cover very different situations. The former provides compensation in lieu of damages for injuries sustained in the course of the work, and the Board is primarily concerned with the nature and extent of such injuries and damages. On the other hand, a disability subjecting an employee to retirement need not be work-related. The Civil Service Commission's inquiry is primarily directed not to the nature of the injury itself, but to the employee's ability to perform his duties. See 5 USC § 2251(g). The injury is considered incidentally, together with circumstances, in determining working capacity. The Board and the Commission exercise their respective jurisdictions independently and each makes its own factual determinations.

Dameron v. OPM, 40 MSPR 497, 500 (1989), added that "determinations concerning the appellant's entitlement to workers' compensation benefits are not binding on either OPM or the Board in deciding whether he proved his entitlement to a disability retirement under 5 USC 8337(a)." See Daniel v. OPM, 43 MSPR 599, 603 (1990) (OWCP approval was not determinative as to a disability retirement application: OWCP binds neither OPM nor the Board); see Christopherson v. OPM, 119 MSPR 635, 640-41 ¶¶ 9-13, 2013 MSPB 58 (2013) (denying disability benefits for lack of sufficient supportive evidence despite approval of disability benefits from SSA and compensation by OWCP). But cf. McClain v. USPS, 50 MSPR 362, 367 (1991) (in appeal involving challenges to enforced leave and a failure to accommodate a physical disability through a light duty assignment: "In interpreting the provision of the collective bargaining agreement at issue, the Board has accepted as conclusive OWCP's finding that a medical condition is job-related. See McPherson v. USPS, 48 MSPR 624, 626 n.1 (1991).").

Kimble v. OPM, 102 MSPR 604, 612 ¶ 16, 2006 MSPB 216 (2006), noted that OWCP determinations can be probative of a disability annuity claim:

Finally, although OWCP decisions are not binding on the Board, they may constitute persuasive evidence of disability and the Board must consider OWCP decisions in determining an employee's entitlement to disability retirement benefits. Suter v. Office of Personnel Management, 88 M.S.P.R. 80, ¶¶ 9, 12 (2001); Brewer v. Office of Personnel Management, 75 M.S.P.R. 163, 168-69, aff'd, 124 F.3d 227 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (Table). Here, the appellant submitted evidence showing that she received OWCP benefits from July 31, 1987, through January 27, 1992, when she began working as an Office Assistant for the Army Corps of Engineers. The appellant also submitted a December 1, 1998 OWCP decision accepting her occupational disease claim for a condition of aggravation of major depression beginning on September 25, 1997, when she was separated from her position with the Army Corps of Engineers. The OWCP decision awarded payment of appropriate medical expenses relating to the appellant's depression and compensation for lost wages as a result of disability. In awarding the appellant benefits, the OWCP decision constitutes relevant evidence showing the existence of a mental disorder sufficient to preclude the performance of duties. See Surma v. Office of Personnel Management, 90 M.S.P.R. 98, ¶ 14 (2001). Thus, we believe that the OWCP decisions constitute persuasive evidence that the appellant is entitled to disability retirement benefits because the decisions are not outweighed by inconsistent or inadequate medical evidence. See Suter, 88 M.S.P.R. 80, ¶ 12.

A determination of a state workers compensation board was given no weight by MSPB in Dameron v. OPM, 40 MSPR 497, 499-500 (1989). See Outlaw v. OPM, 49 MSPR 189, 194 (1991) (OWCP determinations may be considered in the disability annuity case, although it does not bind the Board); cf. Furst v. OPM, 83 MSPR 439 (1999) (considering an agency-directed fitness for duty exam of a USPS employee).
