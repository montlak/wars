1. De Novo Hearing

When a hearing is convened, it is de novo. The Board does not conduct an appellate review of an administrative record, unless the hearing is waived. See, e.g., McDowell v. DOD, 76 MSPR 281, 283 (1997) (de novo review was conducted of the agency decision not to extend the time for filing an application for a law enforcement retirement credit; the standard of review was not whether the agency action was arbitrary and capricious); Daley v. OPM and DHHS, 11 MSPR 601, 604 (1982) (recognizing the de novo nature of a disability retirement appeal, whether the appeal was initiated by the employee or the agency); Bize v. Dept. of Treasury, 3 MSPR 155, 163 (1980) ("While a presiding official may exclude evidence from a hearing because it is repetitious...there is no provision which allows for the exclusion of evidence because the agency did not rely on it.").

Gagliardo v. Dept. of Transp., DC-0752-15-0117-I-1 (NP 1/13/2023), noted of the de novo nature of Board review in an adverse action:

Contrary to the appellant's assertion, the Board need not consider the evidence an agency had before it when it proposed or effected an action because a Board appeal is a de novo review of the agency's evidence, and the Board will decide whether that evidence supports the charge. See Barrett v. Department of the Interior, 54 M.S.P.R. 356, 365 (1992); see also Jackson v. Veterans Administration, 768 F.2d 1325, 1329 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (requiring a de novo determination of the facts in a Board appeal). Thus, to have its action sustained, the agency must present preponderant evidence before the Board. See 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)(1)(B); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(b)(1)(ii). The appellant has not, therefore, shown that the agency needed to show that the proposing and deciding officials had preponderant evidence in support of the action when they decided to take their respective actions. In any event, the thoroughness or lack of thoroughness of an agency investigation of alleged misconduct is not a proper basis for not sustaining an agency's charge. Uske v. U.S. Postal Service, 60 M.S.P.R. 544, 550 (1994), aff'd, 56 F.3d 1375 (Fed. Cir. 1995).

Similarly, from Leopold v. USPS, SF-0752-17-0084-I-1, n.2 (NP 1/26/2023):

The appellant also argues on review that the administrative judge violated his due process rights by considering two situations involving undelivered or delayed mail in sustaining the negligent performance charge. The appellant contends that the administrative judge was precluded from considering these situations because they were not mentioned in the proposal notice or the decision letter, they were not raised by the deciding official during the appellant's oral reply to the proposal notice, and he was not charged with either incident. The Board has rejected the notion that its scope of review is limited to consideration of the administrative record established before the agency. See Morgan v. U.S. Postal Service, 48 M.S.P.R. 607, 611 (1991). Under 5 U.S.C. § 7701(a) and (b), the Board must conduct a hearing if requested by an appellant and consider de novo all the relevant evidence presented by both parties, whether offered at a hearing or transmitted as a part of the agency's administrative record. Id. During the hearing, the appellant testified that he was aware of the incidents of undelivered mail during the relevant timeframe, and the administrative judge properly considered this testimony in sustaining the charge.

Miller v. VA, 839 Fed. Appx. 458 (Fed. Cir. 2020 NP), explained that:

There is no legal requirement that all the evidence an agency presents in a Board appeal must have been presented to the agency at the time of the agency action that is under review. While the Board generally may not uphold an adverse agency action on a ground different from the charge levied by the agency as the basis for the action, see King v. Nazelrod, 43 F.3d 663, 667 (Fed. Cir. 1994), the evidence that can be presented to the Board in support of that charge is not limited to the evidence recited in the agency's notices of proposed and final action, see Licausi v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 350 F.3d 1359, 1364 (Fed. Cir. 2003). Among other reasons for introducing additional evidence in Board proceedings, it is frequently the case--as it was here--that parties raise new issues to the Board, such as affirmative defenses, and that those new issues give rise to newly relevant evidence.[2]

[2] Ms. Miller contends that "the Board is limited to reviewing the grounds invoked by the agency and may not 'substitute what it considers to be better basis and evidence (undisput[ed] new rebuttal evidence in this case) for removal than what was identified by the agency and Notice.'" Reply Br. 8 (quoting O'Keefe v. U.S. Postal Serv., 318 F.3d 1310, 1315 (Fed. Cir. 2002)). That contention is based on a misquotation of O'Keefe. The words "and evidence" and the ensuing parenthetical do not appear in the quoted passage from O'Keefe. Hence, that case does not stand for the proposition that an agency's presentation of evidence in Board proceedings is limited to evidence previously set forth in a notice of proposed removal. Instead, O'Keefe stands for the quite different proposition that the Board may not affirm a removal action on the basis of charges not set out in the notice of proposed removal.

Because of the de novo nature of proceedings before the Board, the agency is not limited to the evidence at the time of the proposal or to any additional evidence obtained by the agency prior to actual removal. "Nothing in the law or in our regulations restricts an agency to reliance upon its documentary, administrative record, or withholds from that agency a full and fair opportunity to cross-examine or otherwise rebut an appellant's evidence presented for the first time at the Board's hearing." Foster v. DHHS, 18 MSPR 339, 342 (1983). What is determinative is whether the agency proves its case before the AJ. Review is not limited to the administrative record compiled by the agency. Blackshear v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1986 NP No. 85-2229); see Aviles-Wynkoop v. DOD, 825 Fed. Appx. 834 n.1 (Fed. Cir. 2020 NP) ("Ms. Aviles-Wynkoop suggests that it was improper for the Board to rely on those affidavits because they presented evidence that was not included in the notice of proposed termination. The affidavits, however, were submitted before the evidentiary deadline establish by the administrative judge. And Ms. Aviles-Wynkoop has not identified any requirement that an agency attach to a notice of proposed termination all evidence of the misconduct described in the notice.").

The de novo nature of the hearing was emphasized, Sterrett v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-10-0403-I-1 (NP 7/19/2011):

...The administrative judge committed no error in permitting the agency to submit additional evidence on appeal because the Board's review of an adverse action is a de novo proceeding, in which the agency bears the burden of proof by a preponderance of the evidence. See Jackson v. Veterans Administration, 768 F.2d 1325, 1329 (Fed. Cir. 1985). As such, all relevant evidence submitted up until the record closes is admissible. See Brook v. Corrado, 999 F.2d 523, 528 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (the evidence need not have been before the agency at the time of the removal). While the Board must review the agency's decision on the grounds invoked by the agency, see Gonzalez v. Department of Homeland Security, 114 M.S.P.R. 318, ¶ 7 (2010), that does not mean the Board is restricted to the agency's record in reaching a decision, see Jackson, 768 F.2d at 1329 n.4.

See Knight v. USDA, SF-0752-15-0309-I-1 (NP 4/27/2016) ("He further argues that C.B.'s statements in support of these allegations postdated the agency's suspension decision. As an initial matter, we find it immaterial that C.B.'s statement postdates the agency's suspension decision. It is well settled that the Board's review is not limited to the administrative record before the agency; rather, the Board is mandated by statute to consider de novo all the relevant evidence presented by both parties. Zeiss v. Veterans Administration, 8 M.S.P.R. 15, 17-18 (1981)."). [Refer to Callahan v. Dept. of Navy, 748 F.2d 1556 (Fed. Cir. 1984), followed, Sanders v. USPS, 801 F.2d 1328 (Fed. Cir. 1986), found later in this chapter, "Adjudication After Waiver, Failure to Attend Hearing."]

In a nonprecedential decision the Board may have suggested limitations on use by an agency of information tending to prove a charge when that information was collected after proposal and decision letters were issued, Eagleheart v. USPS, SF-0752-06-0167-B-2 (NP 3/28/2011):

...The Board adjudicates the charge as it is described in the agency's proposal and decision notices. Rackers v. Department of Justice, 79 M.S.P.R. 262, 276 (1998), aff'd, 194 F.3d 1336 (Fed. Cir. 1999) (Table). Accordingly, the administrative judge erred in considering evidence collected after the appellant's removal in sustaining the specification. This error did not affect the correctness of her conclusion, however, because it is undisputed that the agency was unaware of this evidence and did not consider it in determining to remove the appellant. See Stuhlmacher v. U.S. Postal Service, 89 M.S.P.R. 272, ¶ 14 (2001). Moreover, the error does not provide a basis for disturbing the administrative judge's credibility determinations, which we find are supported by the record below.

Cf. Bidnick v. DOJ, AT-0752-14-0060-I-1 (NP 4/15/2015) ("While the Board will consider an appellant's evidence and arguments regardless of whether he raised them before the agency, see Morgan v. U.S. Postal Service, 48 M.S.P.R. 607, 610-11 (1991) (rejecting the notion that the Board's scope of review is limited to consideration of the administrative record established before the agency); Stewart v. Office of Personnel Management, 8 M.S.P.R. 289, 293 (1981) (concluding that the Board is both authorized and mandated to consider de novo all available relevant evidence), the Board may find them less credible when he raises them for the first time before the Board, see, e.g., Reynolds v. Department of Justice, 63 M.S.P.R. 189, 195 (1994)."). [Refer to Chapter 1, "De Novo Review of Agency Actions," and to Chapter 4, "De Novo Review."]

Although the requirement of a de novo hearing is uniform as to matters within the Board's jurisdiction, the burdens of proof vary as they are applied to evidence developed during the hearing and evidence that is otherwise part of the Board record. The appeal may be assessed through a preponderant evidence analysis of factual disputes in an adverse action (Chapter 6 of the Guide); a substantial evidence standard may apply to a performance-based action (Chapter 9 of the Guide) or to some cases involving VA employees; a clear and convincing evidentiary standard may apply to a whistleblower reprisal case (Chapter 13 of the Guide).
