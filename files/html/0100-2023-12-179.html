broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section G: Timeliness of PFR, Subsection 09: Justification for Late Filing, Part i: Misinterpretation, Ignorance of MSPB Regulation, Instruction
i. Misinterpretation, Ignorance of MSPB Regulation, Instruction

An appellant's confusion and lack of sophistication may be taken into account when determining whether good cause for late filing exists. The appellant must show that his confusion is related to a specific ambiguity in either the instructions he received or in a Board procedure relating to a PFR. Abney v. OPM, 89 MSPR 305, 309 ¶ 6 (2001); Noble v. USPS, 73 MSPR 59, 63 (1997); see Stuart v. OPM, 91 MSPR 206, 209 ¶ 6 (2002) ("The fact that the appellant is legally unskilled does not relieve him of the responsibility for timely filing his petition for review."); Gareis v. Dept. of Interior, 90 MSPR 107, 112 ¶ 13 (2001) (delayed PFR: "An appellant's lack of sophistication in Board matters and a general inability to understand instructions and procedures does not establish good cause to waive the filing deadline.").

Forst v. OPM, 97 MSPR 142, 144-45 ¶¶ 7-8 (2004), placed emphasis on the "specific ambiguity" factor as to an appellant who stated he was confused concerning the filing date for a PFR because of the language in the initial decision about the 60-day period for going to the court of appeals after an initial decision becomes final:

An appellant's confusion and lack of sophistication, which contribute to a late filing, may be taken into account when determining whether good cause for a late filing exists. Noble v. U.S. Postal Service, 73 M.S.P.R. 59, 63 (1997) (citing Walls v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 29 F.3d 1578, 1583 (Fed. Cir. 1994)). An appellant must show, however, that such confusion is related to a specific ambiguity in either the instructions he received or in a Board procedure. Id. Here, although the appellant asserts that he mistakenly believed that he had sixty days to file an appeal with the Board, he has not identified a specific ambiguity in the initial decision warranting his mistaken belief. The initial decision, in fact, provided the appellant with the exact date on which it would become final unless he filed a petition for review with the Board.

We therefore find that the appellant has not established by preponderant evidence that he acted with due diligence or reasonable prudence under the circumstances of this case, or that there were circumstances beyond his control that affected his ability to comply with the time limit. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(a)(2)(ii) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b)(2)(i)(B)]; Alonzo, 4 M.S.P.R. at 184; see also Evans v. Office of Personnel Management, 85 M.S.P.R. 36 ¶ 9 (1999) (a mistaken filing with the Federal Circuit prior to the deadline for filing a petition for review with the Board generally does not excuse a later untimely filing with the Board); Shelton v. Department of the Air Force, 20 M.S.P.R. 120, 121 (1984) (an employee's explanation that he misunderstood the filing deadline and believed he could file his petition for review within thirty days after the initial decision became final did not establish good cause for the delay in filing a petition for review, considering the clear notice in the initial decision and the absence of any circumstances beyond the employee's control). Factors in this case supporting a finding of good cause for the appellant's filing delay are the length of the delay and the appellant's pro se status at the time of filing. These factors standing alone, however, are insufficient to establish good cause for the appellant's delay in filing a petition for review. See Simon v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 65 M.S.P.R. 176, 181 (1994), review dismissed, 52 F.3d 343 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table).

Counsel has an obligation to read and follow the instructions in an initial decision pertaining to PFR filing requirements. In Colon v. USPS, 71 MSPR 514, 517 (1996), counsel's staff called the Board's regional office for instructions about where to file a PFR. The information from the regional office was then misinterpreted by counsel's staff to mean that the PFR should be filed with the clerk of the Federal Circuit. When the mistake was discovered, the PFR was refiled with the Board a couple of weeks later. The Board declined to excuse the belated PFR. Counsel should have read the filing instructions. Even assuming reasonable reliance upon somewhat ambiguous information from the Board's regional office, counsel waited too long to correct the filing error following its discovery. Chairman Erdreich filed a dissenting opinion arguing that a timely, but misdirected PFR to the Federal Circuit should have been excused in the same manner that the Board excuses a PFR similarly misfiled with OPM, OSC, or the employing agency. See Dabner v. EPA, CH-0752-18-0572-I-1 (NP 5/1/2023) ("[A] representative's miscalculation of the fi ling deadline generally does not constitute good cause for a filing delay. Day v. Department of Housing and Urban Development, 50 M.S.P.R. 680, 682 n.2 (1991), aff'd, 975 F.2d 870 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table).").Andre v. Dept. of Army, 91 MSPR 342, 345-46 ¶ 9 (2002), declined to forgive a late PFR based on the appellant's explanation that the agency's attorney could waive a filing deadline established in the initial decision. The appellant must follow the rules as well. See Chavez v. OPM, SF-0831-12-0698-I-1 (NP 11/5/2013) (declining to excuse a late PFR when the appellant believed the time for seeking Federal Circuit review was the time for filing a PFR with the Board), reissued, Chavez v. OPM, SF-0831-12-0698-R-1 (NP 12/19/2013); Maldonado v. OPM, 52 MSPR 104, 106 (1991) (appellant's erroneous reading of a straightforward initial decision does not excuse an untimely PFR), aff'd, Maldonado v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1993 NP No. 92-3212) (lateness was not excused because the appellant misread the Board's instructions to mean that he could file his PFR on a date that would cut off his right to judicial review in the Federal Circuit; "the initial decision...clearly informed the appellant that the initial decision would become final on a date certain, here, July 31, 1991, unless a petition for review was filed by that date or the Board reopened the case on its own motion"); McClellan v. DOJ, 33 MSPR 678, 679-80 (1987) (insufficient to confuse the time for filing with the appellate court and the Board); cf. Gowdy v. DOJ, 104 MSPR 592, 595 ¶ 7, 2007 MSPB 33 (2007) ("Even if the appellant had been proceeding pro se at that time, his failure to file his petition for review in accordance with these unambiguous instructions would constitute a failure to exercise due diligence or ordinary prudence.").

Misinterpretation of Board regulations that are clearly stated in the initial decision governing the filing of a PFR does not justify filing the PFR several weeks late. Grigsby v. Dept. of Army, 1 MSPR 757, 758, 2 MSPB 165 (1980); see Scott v. SSA, 110 MSPR 92, 94-95 ¶ 9, 2008 MSPB 219 (2008) ("The appellant's assertion that she telephoned the Board's Northeastern Regional Office and notified the AJ and a paralegal specialist that she intended to seek review of the ID does not establish good cause for the filing delay, nor does her claim that she was unaware that she could ask for such an extension to file her PFR."); Guenther v. USPS, 68 MSPR 667, 669-70 (1995) (untimely PFR was not excused because the appellant learned months later that he could file a PFR from an appeal dismissed by the AJ as untimely; directions and time limits for filing a PFR were stated in the initial decision); Caballero v. Dept. of Army, 59 MSPR 298, 301-02 (1993) (immaterial that agency officials did not inform the appellant of the PFR process; the initial decision provided filing instructions; the appellant could have contacted the Board for further information); Mata v. OPM, 53 MSPR 552, 554 (1992) (Philippines retirement case: "While the appellant asserts that she was not informed of the criteria to show good cause and that she was ignorant of her rights under the retirement law, she does not allege that she contacted or attempted to contact the Board for further information"; lack of prejudice to OPM is not considered until the appellant shows good cause for a belated filing); Rudzwick v. USPS, 49 MSPR 104 (1991) (delay was not excused because the appellant was unsure whether one or another portion of the initial decision was subject to a PFR); Franklin v. DHHS, 48 MSPR 133, 135 (1991) (failure to follow "straightforward directions" in a notice requiring the refiling of a PFR); Jones v. USPS, 43 MSPR 404, 405 (1990) (late petition was not excused because the appellant thought he was seeking regulatory review under 5 CFR 550.804 rather than a review of his adverse action decision); Zelmer v. USPS, 41 MSPR 64, 66 (1989) (initial decision properly advised the appellant of the use of a PFR to challenge a settlement agreement); Saber v. Dept. of Transp., 21 MSPR 227, 228 (1984) (PFR of addendum decision: "error in interpreting Board instructions does not constitute good cause for an untimely filing"); Hempill v. DHHS, 20 MSPR 112, 114 (1984) (delayed PFR was not excused because the appellant's representative did not understand the decision letter); Trujillo v. Dept. of Energy, 2 MSPR 336, 336-37, 2 MSPB 499 (1980) (delay was not excused because appellant's representative misinterpreted a clear deadline).

Inability to count days is not an excuse for most belated filings. See Wallace v. OPM, 56 MSPR 162, 164 (1992) (failure to count weekends as component of time available to file PFR), aff'd, Wallace v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1993 NP No. 93-3151) ("When faced with a time limit in which to file with the board, ordinary prudence would compel one to seek competent authority for the method under which the time would be computed, such as would be found in the board's regulations, and not proceed on assumption or information from an unauthorized source."); Harrison v. VA, 96 MSPR 571, 573 ¶ 6 (2004) ("her miscalculation of the number of days she had in which to file a PFR does not constitute good cause for the filing delay"); McWilliams v. USPS, 51 MSPR 72, 75 (1991) (confusion or miscalculation of the number of days allowed to file a PFR does not constitute good cause for a waiver of the regulatory time limit); Day v. DHUD, 50 MSPR 680, 682 n.2 (1991) (miscalculation of the filing date does not constitute good cause for an untimely filing); Chavez v. OPM, 50 MSPR 356, 357 (1991) (delay was not excused because the envelope containing the initial decision did not have a dated postmark; initial decision contained information explaining when a PFR should be filed); Andrews v. OPM, 46 MSPR 511, 513 (1990) (no excuse that the appellant thought he had 30 days to file from the time that the initial decision became final; "failure to follow the straightforward directions in the initial decision constitutes a failure to exercise due diligence"); Kortz v. OPM, 45 MSPR 496, 498 (1990) (belief that time was calculated in workdays rather than calendar days was insufficient to excuse a late PFR); Gaff v. Dept. of Transp., 45 MSPR 387, 390 n.2 (1990) (miscalculation of the time allowed for a reply to a PFR was not excused); Shelton v. Dept. of Air Force, 20 MSPR 120, 121 (1984) (filing time was not waived when the appellant misconstrued instructions to permit the filing of a PFR within 30 days from the final date of the initial decision); cf. Walls v. MSPB, 29 F.3d 1578 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (distinguishing Kortz and allowing a late appeal because, unlike Kortz, Walls was not given a specific deadline for his appeal and instead miscalculated the appeal period when he interpreted ambiguous language concerning the filing date); Thompson v. DOJ, 51 MSPR 43, 46 (1991) (untimely reply was not excused by the appellant's belief that, based on experience with court cases, the agency PFR was served on the date he received it rather than the postmark date). But see Urban v. VA, CH-0752-10-0261-I-1 (NP 3/7/2011) (delay of a day in filing an appeal was excused, in part, because of incorrect calculation of the time for filing: "The appellant's explanation that she believed Federal holidays were excluded from the calculation of the timeframe for filing, while inaccurate, is not unreasonable.").

It is not just the appellant's misinterpretation, or lack of knowledge, of Board regulations that proves insufficient to support waiver of a late PFR. A late PFR is not excused because the appellant relied on his representative's improper advice concerning its filing date. See El-Amin v. USPS, 62 MSPR 313, 314 (1994) ("The appellant's acceptance of the advice of his attorney, even if that advice is erroneous, does not provide a basis for waiving the filing deadline."); Stibbe v. GPO, 57 MSPR 5, 6 (1993) ("the fact that the appellant may have received bad advice from his representatives does not establish good cause as to his untimely petition"). But a one-month delay in filing a PFR was excused for an appellant who attempted to contact the Board through a union representative and received confusing advice from union representatives concerning filing requirements. Nathaniel v. USPS, 10 MSPR 318, 319 n.1 (1982).

Neither reluctance to file a PFR nor lack of familiarity with the Board's administrative practice constitutes good cause for the waiver of the Board's timeliness requirements. Rodgers v. USPS, 57 MSPR 172, 174 (1993) (appellant thought it was "hopeless to appeal"); see Thomas v. Dept. of Interior, 92 MSPR 165, 168 ¶ 7 (2002) ("A lack of experience with the Board's appeal process...is not a valid excuse for allowing a clearly-stated time limit to elapse."); Tamayo v. OPM, 88 MSPR 685, 687-88 ¶ 7 (2001) (the appellant's assertions about lacking the faith and courage to proceed did not establish good cause); McMullen v. Dept. of Treasury, 83 MSPR 448, 450 ¶ 4 (1999) (inexperience with legal matters does not warrant the waiver of a PFR filing deadline); Keatting v. Dept. of Navy, 77 MSPR 642, 645 (1998) ("Good cause is not shown where the stated basis for the untimely filing is that the appellant is unfamiliar with the Board's procedures."); Bergman v. OPM, 25 MSPR 261, 262 (1984) (belated PFR was not excused because the appellant did not know he could file a petition, if anyone would petition on his own behalf, or if the Board would reopen the case on its own).

In Caldwell v. Dept. of Interior, 58 MSPR 54, 57 (1993), the Board refused an untimely PFR:

The appellant's claims of confusion and a lack of sophistication in legal matters also fail to establish good cause for his delay in filing. A general inability to understand instructions and procedures does not provide a basis for a waiver of the time limits for filing a petition for review.... Moreover, the appellant did not contact the Board for either an explanation of the initial decision or an extension of time in which to file the petition....

Burgess v. USPS, 78 MSPR 484, 487 (1998), reprised the theme:

The appellant's sworn statement alleging her confusion and lack of sophistication in legal matters is insufficient to show good cause for the 14-day delay in filing her petition for review. A general inability to understand instructions and procedures does not provide a basis for a waiver of the time limit for filing a petition for review. See Holloway v. Office of Personnel Management, 54 MSPR 507, 509 (1992); Suclan v. Office of Personnel Management, 52 MSPR 348, 350 (1992); see also Allen v. U.S. Postal Service, 44 MSPR 369, 372 (1990), aff'd, 918 F.2d 187 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (Table). Further, we note that the appellant did not contact the Board for either an explanation of the initial decision or an extension of time in which to file the petition. See Holloway, 54 MSPR at 510; Allen, 44 MSPR at 372. Such failure weighs against a finding of due diligence or ordinary prudence. See Dougherty v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 52 MSPR 311, 314 n.2 (1992)....

See Aquino v. Dept. of Navy, SF-315H-17-0254-I-1 (NP 7/6/2022) ("Although the appellant claims he was not informed of his right to file a petition for review 'until recently,' he is an e-filer and acknowledges receiving the initial decision. When an initial decision clearly informs an appellant of when to file a petition for review, his failure to read and follow these instructions does not show the due diligence necessary to constitute good cause for a waiver of the filing deadline."); Finger v. Dept. of Interior, AT-3330-11-0522-I-1 (NP 4/2/2012) ("Turning to the appellant's claim that he was confused by the filing instructions in the initial decision and thought that he could not file the petition before the initial decision became final on August 9, 2011, it appears that the appellant mistakenly believed that the instructions in the initial decision for filing a petition for judicial review with the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit also applied to filing a petition for review with the Board. The instructions in the initial decision regarding where and when to file with the Board or the Federal Circuit were clear on their face and self-explanatory. If the appellant had read and followed them, the untimely filing of the petition for review could have been avoided. It is clear from the appellant's motion, however, that he did not read the initial decision's filing instructions thoroughly. The appellant's error in reading the initial decision does not establish good cause for the untimely filing. See Colon v. U.S. Postal Service, 71 M.S.P.R. 514, 517 (1996) (a party's hastiness in reading a Board notice containing filing instructions does not evidence the due diligence necessary to excuse an untimely filing), review dismissed, 132 F.3d 52 (Fed. Cir. 1998) (Table)."); Acierto v. OPM, 83 MSPR 179, 181 ¶ 4 (1999) (a delayed PFR: "Unfamiliarity with Board procedures...is insufficient to establish good cause for an untimely filing."); Smith v. OPM, 45 MSPR 508, 511 (1990) (appellant's explanation that "she did not know what to do" was insufficient to excuse an untimely PFR); Wenzel v. Dept. of Interior, 33 MSPR 344, 350 (1987) (counsel's unfamiliarity with Board proceedings or involvement with other legal matters may not serve as sufficient cause for the waiver of timeliness requirements).

Confusion over whether to submit a PFR or a request for an extension to file a PFR is no excuse for lateness. Donahue v. OPM, 23 MSPR 46, 48 (1984). Lack of a copy of Board regulations is not an excuse either, as long as the initial decision notifies the appellant of his review rights and filing requirements. Desai v. Dept. of Army, 31 MSPR 295, 296 (1986). If the initial decision does not specify a PFR deadline, the error will not excuse a late PFR if the appellant does not state that omission as his reason for late filing and if the appellant was furnished with a copy of the Board's regulations when he was issued the adverse action decision by the agency. See Upshaw v. DOD, 56 MSPR 94, 97-98 (1992). But in Hamner v. DHUD, 93 MSPR 84, 87-88 ¶¶ 8-9 (2002), the Board excused two months of filing delay when the initial decision did not provide a filing deadline, the appellant was involved in reserve training, and the appellant was pro se. Upshaw was distinguished as involving a delay of seven months with no reason offered for the delay other than the failure of the initial decision to include a filing deadline.

Unsuccessful attempts to obtain congressional or other outside assistance necessary to follow Board regulations may not excuse delay beyond the regulatory time limits. See Rogers v. DOD, 75 MSPR 696, 699 (1997) (appellant's pursuit of appeal rights through other federal agencies does not excuse a belated PFR); Ford v. OPM, 74 MSPR 513, 515 (1997) (involvement of congressman was not sufficient to excuse delay); Schoenherr v. VA, 73 MSPR 99, 102 (1997) (efforts to seek assistance from congressional offices and from federal agencies other than the MSPB did not excuse a belated PFR); Smith v. Dept. of Army, 68 MSPR 607, 610 (1995) (information from an agency EEO office concerning the time for filing a PFR did not excuse a delay); Vaughn v. USPS, 53 MSPR 418, 420 (1992) ("The appellant's delay while he allegedly sought assistance from various agencies and individuals, and the lack of union assistance, also fail to justify his untimely submission, particularly in light of the clearly stated instructions in the initial decision."); Wilsey v. OPM, 52 MSPR 42, 43 (1991) (belated appointment with a congressional office and apparently incorrect advice from a legal aid office were insufficient to excuse a one-day delay in curing a deficiency when the deadline was "clearly stated"); Necessary v. Dept. of Interior, 49 MSPR 356, 358 (1991) (contact with a congressional office).
