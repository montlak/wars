4. Refuting Participation

To rebut a prima facie case of strike participation, the appellant must either establish that he had no knowledge of the existence of the strike or demonstrate that his absence was due to circumstances other than intentional participation in the strike. Baracco v. Dept. of Transp., 15 MSPR 112, 115 (1983) (relying on Schapansky, 13 MSPR 589, 592-93 (1982), supra); see Cummings v. FAA, 17 MSPR 79, 81 (1983) (appellant was on a camping trip and unaware of the existence of the strike). But see Roper v. FAA, 17 MSPR 89, 90 (1983) (appellant could not claim he was unaware of a strike because he purposefully avoided his obligation to stay informed of developments). Once an agency presented evidence of strike participation, it was proper for the Board to draw an adverse inference from the refusal of the employee to testify or otherwise offer rebuttal evidence: "Silence," is "often evidence of the most persuasive character," Adams v. Dept. of Transp., 735 F.2d 488, 492 (Fed. Cir. 1984):

The judicial process is not entirely divorced from common sense and the everyday experiences of humankind. Petitioners had every reason and incentive to strenuously deny the charge of strike participation if they were not in truth strikers. But fear of prosecution for perjury, one may devoutly hope, has not entirely disappeared. The law and experience teach that a striker can be expected to refrain from falsely testifying under oath that his absence was due to non-strike reasons.

If an employee was on leave before the strike and the agency acted to cancel the leave, strike participation was not demonstrated merely by absence during the strike; the absence was compatible with conduct other than striking, e.g., attending to functions planned for the leave period that the employee felt were more important than returning to work. Although the employee could properly be charged AWOL for leave properly canceled, the severity of the conduct was not the same as that of striking; accordingly, for a penalty based upon a presumption of striking arising from AWOL during the strike, the court remanded the case to the Board for reconsideration of whether the Board could find that strike participation had in fact occurred and for a determination of what penalty might be appropriate for a period of AWOL divorced from strike participation. Letenyei v. Dept. of Transp., 735 F.2d 528 (Fed. Cir. 1984), following remand, Letenyei v. Dept. of Transp., FAA, 767 F.2d 898 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (affirming dismissal); see McPartland v. FAA, 14 MSPR 506, 508 (1983) (sustaining the authority of the agency to cancel leave and remove an employee who participated in a strike when he was supposed to be on duty). For discussion of the leave or duty status of PATCO union negotiators during the strike, refer to Fitzgerald v. Dept. of Transp., 22 MSPR 343, 346-49 (1984).
