c. Foreign Mail Problem

The Board may recognize and excuse mailing delays that seem endemic, as it did for an appellant who became involved in both a late appeal and a late PFR resulting from substantial mailing delays between the United States and the Philippines. Rosales v. OPM, 41 MSPR 590, 592 (1989); see Floresca v. OPM, 69 MSPR 93, 96 (1995) (excusing an eight-month delay in following up on a PFR filed (and lost) in international mail); Steele v. USPS, 44 MSPR 327, 329-30 (1990) (examining various mailings to see if there was a pattern of delay in mail arriving from the Virgin Islands); Sincero v. OPM, 41 MSPR 239, 241-42 (1989) (recognizing mailing delays to the Philippines); Lacorte v. OPM, 37 MSPR 538, 540 (1988) (Philippines mail delays). Mailing delays involving residents of Panama were acknowledged in Bocanegra v. OPM, 52 MSPR 114, 117 (1992) (considering an appeal from an OPM reconsideration decision). See Rahman v. OPM, 63 MSPR 219, 221 (1994) (appellant demonstrated mailing delays between the United States and Bangladesh).

The appellant from a country known to experience mailing delays must still respond to Board orders directing that cause be shown for a late PFR. Junio v. OPM, 40 MSPR 269, 270 (1989). The Board declined to accept an excuse of mailing delays from the Philippines when the delay was about two years and the appellant asserted that delays were common of "months or even a year." Carsolin v. OPM, 43 MSPR 300, 302 (1990); see Caguiat v. OPM, 58 MSPR 410, 412 (1993) (two- to three-year delay: "Even assuming the Philippines mail system contributed to the appellant's delay in filing his petition, there is no evidence to suggest it was responsible for a delay of this magnitude."); Dinoso v. OPM, 53 MSPR 561, 563 (1992) ("Although we recognize that mail between the United States and the Philippines is often delayed, the appellant's vague statement does not specifically allege mail delays or indicate when she received the Clerk's December 9, 1991 notice."); Granfil v. OPM, 47 MSPR 136, 139 (1991) (declining to excuse a six-month delay in filing a PFR attributed by the appellant first to illness and then to the Philippine mails: "He does not specify the length of time the mail was delayed."); Rullan v. OPM, 40 MSPR 300, 303 (1989) (Philippines: a delay of about three years was not excused; the appellant did not submit a sworn statement concerning the belated receipt of the initial decision; once he received the initial decision, he was required to exercise due diligence); cf. Alaguena v. OPM, 48 MSPR 98, 100 (1991) (Philippines: mailing delays did not excuse a failure to ensure the mailing of a corrected PFR within time permitted after the appellant acknowledged he had received the clerk's order).

Suratos v. OPM, 56 MSPR 201, 203 (1993), declined to excuse a 25-day delay in an overseas case:

In Rosales v. OPM, 41 MSPR 590, 592 (1989), we recognized that there are frequent mail service delays between the continental United States and the Philippines. Here, however, the appellant has not shown good cause for his untimely filing because he has not presented particular evidence beyond a general claim of mail delay sufficient to establish that his mail was affected by these delays and that these delays were beyond his control. In this connection, the appellant has not alleged that he received OPM's reconsideration decision after the date that the decision became final, or that the date that he mailed the appeal was early enough to be timely but for mail delay. Consequently, a general claim of mail delay is not sufficient to show good cause for the untimely filing of his appeal....

See Din v. OPM, 46 MSPR 489, 492 (1990) (slow delivery of mail to Pakistan did not excuse delay in PFR mailed about a month after the initial decision was received). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Philippines Retirement Appeal."]
