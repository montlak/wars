broida flra Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices, Subchapter 03: Union Violations, Section D: Duty of Fair Representation, Subsection 02: Scope, Part f: Benefit Programs
f. Benefit Programs

Many federal sector unions offer and administer or sponsor benefit programs, particularly those providing insurance. Antilles Consol. Educ. Ass'n and Bender, 36 FLRA 776, 789-92 (1990), considered the DFR in the context of service fees charged union members and nonmembers who subscribe to the benefit programs administered through unions. The union and the agency had an agreement requiring unit employees to be offered dental/optical benefits plans. Employee participants would contribute a specific portion of the premium and the agency contributed the balance. The union then decided to charge each nonmember participant in the plan a yearly administrative charge of $60 in addition to the required premiums. Union members' administrative costs were covered by their union memberships. The ALJ determined that the actual administrative costs for each nonmember came to about $30 per year. The union was ordered to make whole employees who were charged more than that amount. FLRA analyzed the case as one involving the DFR and began its discussion by reviewing the history of the NTEU decision, allowing a union to distinguish between members and nonmembers when it provided counsel to members involved in MSPB litigation. FLRA also reviewed the Ft. Bragg decision, permitting a union to assess and publicize a fee requirement for nonmembers who wished to be included in a lawsuit concerning the employment status of school teachers. FLRA proceeded with its analysis of the particulars of the insurance administration scheme:

We will first determine whether the Union's activities in relation to the insurance plans were undertaken in the Union's capacity as exclusive representative of unit employees. For the following reasons, we conclude that these activities were undertaken in that capacity. We will then address the Union's specific arguments that the duty of fair representation did not attach in this case.

It is clear that the particular dental/optical plans involved here were arrived at through collective bargaining.... In our view, "negotiations" over "proposals" which culminate in "agreements" consistent with statutory requirements are clear indicia of collective bargaining....

It is clear also that, apart from the content of the plans, the Union sought and was granted at the bargaining table the authority to administer the insurance plans....

...As the content of, and the Union's authority to administer, the plans were determined by the parties through collective bargaining, and as the Union's authority and obligation to engage in collective bargaining derive solely from its status as exclusive representative, we conclude that the Union's activities in relation to the plans were undertaken in the Union's capacity as exclusive representative.... Accordingly, the Union was obligated, under section 7114(a)(1) of the Statute, to administer the plans "without discrimination and without regard to labor organization membership."

...

As all unit employees were entitled to the benefits provided, pursuant to the Union's authority and obligation to engage in collective bargaining, solely in Article 38 of the parties' agreement, this case is unlike either NTEU or Ft. Bragg. In NTEU, all employees were guaranteed, by statute, the right to appeal certain personnel actions to the MSPB and to be represented in those appeals by counsel of their choice. Similarly, in Ft. Bragg, the union's right to represent members in the lawsuit was not dependent on its collective bargaining relationship with the agency and the union did not seek to impose a service fee for a benefit to which non-members were entitled solely under a collective bargaining agreement. Here, by contrast, the Union clearly obtained its authority over administration of the dental/optical plans through collective bargaining, those plans are provided only in the collective bargaining agreement, and the plans apply, by their terms, to all unit employees.

Likening the administration of the insurance plans to the administration of hiring halls by unions, treated by the Supreme Court in Breininger v. Sheet Metal Workers Local 6, 110 S. Ct. 424 (1989), FLRA continued in Antilles, 36 FLRA at 795-800:

We recognize that the administration of insurance benefits differs from the administration of a hiring hall. Nevertheless, we find the Court's analysis compelling. In particular, like the union in Breininger, the Union here assumed the role of administering the insurance benefits. That is, whether or not the Agency was required to provide these benefits under the Comparability Law, the Union sought and was granted the right to administer the benefits. The Union sought and was granted this right pursuant to its status as exclusive representative of bargaining unit employees. The Union's right to administer the plans was obtained through collective bargaining and is contained in, and enforceable through, the parties' collective bargaining agreement.

...

Although we have concluded that the Union is obligated to fairly represent all unit members in administering the...benefits...the question remains whether the Union violated its duty here by requiring non-members to pay a $60 yearly administrative fee to participate.... For the following reasons, we conclude that, by imposing this fee, the Union violated its duty of fair representation....

...

Section 7114(a)(1) of the Statute requires a union to represent the interests of all unit employees "without discrimination and without regard to labor organization membership."...

It is uncontroverted that Union members were not charged an administrative fee.... The fees in this case were assessed based exclusively on the member status of unit employees....

Here, union members were eligible to participate in the insurance plans for the cost of their premiums alone. They were not required to pay any additional monies to participate in the plans.

...

...By requiring the payment of the administrative fee, the Union discriminated against non-members on the basis of their member status and, thereby, violated sections 7114(a)(1) and 7116(b)(8) of the Statute.

In addition, we conclude that the Union's imposition of the administrative fee on non-members violated section 7116(b)(1) of the Statute. Section 7116(b)(1) prohibits a labor union from interfering with, restraining, or coercing employees in the exercise of employees' rights under the Statute.... We note, in this regard, that in determining whether section 7116(b)(1) has been violated, objective, rather than subjective, standards are used. See American Federation of Government Employees Local 1931, AFL-CIO, Naval Weapons Station Concord, Concord, California, 34 FLRA 480, 487 (1990).

Applying an objective standard, we conclude that the Union's imposition of an administrative fee for non-members to participate in the insurance plans could reasonably tend to have a coercive effect on the exercise of unit employees of their protected rights under the Statute.... In our view, imposition of the fee could lead unit employees reasonably to conclude that they would receive better, or preferential, treatment if they joined the union than if they did not. As such, imposition of the fee interfered with...the exercise of their rights to join or assist, or refrain from joining or assisting, the Union.

...

Based on these conclusions, we find it unnecessary to address the General Counsel's contentions that the Union violated the Statute by failing to segregate the funds it received for administering the insurance plans from its other monies. In addition, we do not address the extent to which the reasonableness of a fee charged to both member and non-member participants would be subject to review under the Statute.

In Antilles, the union was required to refund all fees collected from nonmember unit employees.

Collateral and incidental negotiated benefits should also be administered with the DFR in mind. In Dept. of Army, Watervliet Arsenal and NFFE Local 2109, 39 FLRA 318, 336 (1991) (ALJ Decision), the union breached that duty by negotiating an agreement that the employer permit union members participating in an asbestos testing program to participate in the testing on administrative leave, while nonmembers of the unit were required to use their off-duty time if they desired to participate in the program.
