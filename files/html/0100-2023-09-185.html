3. Appeal Filed Through Representative

5 CFR 1201.31 (2022), establishes the process for designation of a representative:

Representatives.

(a) Procedure. A party to an appeal may be represented in any matter related to the appeal. Parties may designate a representative, revoke such a designation, and change such a designation in a signed submission, submitted as a pleading.

The right to file an appeal is personal to the employee, a designated representative, or a party properly substituted under 5 CFR 1201.35 (2022). There are no regulatory requirements concerning the form of a designation of representative. Nonetheless, a written designation must be made and, under 5 CFR 1201.24(a)(9) (2022), the appeal must be signed by the appellant or by the appellant's representative. Under 5 CFR 1201.31(a) (2022), a party must designate a representative by a signed pleading, or through electronic filing, governed by 5 CFR 1201.14 (2022). For appeals filed by representatives through the e-filing system, the designation is uploaded as part of the appeal intake through a PDF file that is a scan of the written and signed designation. If the appeal is signed by a representative, and the designation of representative is signed by the appellant, the Board is assured that the appeal is being submitted with the authorization of the appellant. The regional office may not continue the processing of a case with an undesignated representative making submissions for the appellant; in that situation, the region must deal directly with the appellant.

If an undesignated representative files an appeal on time, and the appellant submits a signed appeal confirming that he is personally appealing to the Board, the Board may waive the requirement that an appeal be filed by either the appellant or his duly designated representative. See Goode v. Dept. of Navy, 93 MSPR 122, 124-25 (2002) (IRA appeal). If the appeal is filed by an undesignated representative who fails repeatedly to respond to a request for a proper designation, the appeal may be dismissed for failure to prosecute. See Morris v. OPM, 37 MSPR 401, 405 (1988) (reinstating an appeal because the record showed good faith efforts to comply with requirements concerning the designation of the representative). If an undesignated representative files an appeal on behalf of an appellant who may be mentally incompetent, the AJ should ascertain whether a guardian is appointed and whether the guardian appointed a representative. If there is no guardian, the AJ should make diligent efforts to obtain pro bono representation for the appellant. If counsel cannot be obtained, the AJ should not enter an adverse order against the appellant but should dismiss the appeal without prejudice to reinstitute "under circumstances conducive to fair adjudication." Smith v. OPM, 61 MSPR 512, 514-15 (1994). The failure to properly designate a representative at the regional level is not cured by proper designation of a representative at the PFR level. Cf. Desrochers v. USPS, 27 MSPR 137, 138-39 (1985) (dismissing an enforcement petition that was unaccompanied by a proper designation of the representative who filed the petition).

Representatives may prepare appeals when their clients are not in the office to sign them. The designation of a representative should be supplied with the appeal. If the Board doubts the authenticity of an appellant's signature on a designation form or power of attorney, the Board examines available evidence in an effort to resolve the genuineness of the appellant's signature. See Vanderpool v. USPS, 56 MSPR 116, 118 (1992) (Board analyzed handwriting without the use of expert testimony). To ensure that the Board is well satisfied that the appellant fully agrees with an appeal being filed and that the representative is duly appointed to file the appeal, the Board's designation and signature requirements are readily met through the use of one form that is tacked on to the end of the appeal. Or take a blank piece of paper and type on it: "I designate [representative's name] to be my representative and I subscribe to the contents of the foregoing appeal." Have the appellant sign and date it before he leaves the office and submit the designation form with the appeal signed by the representative. The Board will be happy; the client is protected. More graphically, a simple yet sufficient format for a designation is:

I subscribe to the contents of the foregoing appeal and designate __________ as my representative.

[date] [signed]___________ [Appellant]

Lawyers occasionally get the idea that they should be able to represent a client without a designation because no designation is needed for court work or other legal representation. Counsel should understand that the Board will not recognize an appeal filed by an undesignated representative for the good reason that not all representatives are lawyers with disciplinary rules governing their conduct and malpractice insurance covering their errors. The Board wants to ensure that a representative is truly that and not a friendly interloper. If, for some reason, the designation of representative cannot be filed at the time the appeal is due, file the appeal on time (with the appellant's signature, if possible), explain matters to the Board, and submit the designation as soon as possible.

A note for e-filers: the representative is usually designated when the appeal is filed, and the e-filed appeal will attach a pdf of the designation with a block for entry of the representative's email and consent to receive filings by email. If the appeal is underway and a representative later joins in for the appellant, the e-filing system does not allow for a representative or appellant to immediately sign the representative up for service of pleadings and orders. Until the e-filing system is modified to allow for post-appeal additions of representatives to the service list, send an email to the assigned AJ or the AJ's law clerk with a copy of the designation and request that the region add the representative as an e-filer on the service list.
