M. ANNUITY CALCULATION ISSUES

If the appellant disputes the annuity calculations of OPM, the issue must be presented to OPM for a decision before the Board considers the matter, irrespective of the genesis of the dispute, for example, an enforcement proceeding, Mercado v. OPM, 115 MSPR 65, 67-68 ¶¶ 5-7, 2010 MSPB 189 (2010):

Here, OPM has provided a computer printout showing that it has computed the amount of the appellant's retroactive annuity payment and paid the appellant that amount. ...To the extent there is a question as to the correct monthly annuity benefit, the appellant, as discussed infra at ¶ 7, should present the issue to OPM in the first instance.

Indeed, OPM asserts that the administrative judge's earlier finding of noncompliance was erroneous as the Board's final order merely directed OPM to approve the appellant's application for disability retirement benefits. Citing to Parkin v. Office of Personnel Management, 103 M.S.P.R. 468 (2006), contends that its payment of interim benefits shows that it was in compliance at the time the Recommendation was issued.

The appellant does not contest the fact of payment, but rather, in a submission filed prior to OPM's submission of the payment printout, argues that OPM incorrectly calculated his "high 3" average salary and attaches some pay statements to support his claim. Consistent with the rationale of Parkin, the Board will not consider the calculation issue in the first instance. Parkin concerned a petition for enforcement of a Board decision dismissing an appeal as moot following OPM's rescission of its denial of an appellant's application for disability retirement during the processing of the appellant's appeal challenging that denial. Parkin, 103 M.S.P.R. 468, ¶¶I 2-3. While the Board in Parkin held that a Board decision dismissing an appeal as moot is not an enforceable decision, it stated further that, even if there were an enforceable decision on the merits, the Board would lack jurisdiction to consider the appellant's challenge to OPM's calculations because the appellant had not raised the issue to OPM in the first instance and OPM had not issued a final decision on the calculation issue. Parkin, 103 M.S.P.R. 468, ¶ 9. As the appellant here has not raised a challenge to OPM's calculation of his annuity with OPM in the first instance, we will not consider his challenge now.

OPM has a duty to explain its calculations, Harlston v. OPM, 109 MSPR 148, 151 ¶ 4, 2008 MSPB 132 (2008):

...OPM has not provided its calculations that led to its conclusion that the two annuities would be the same. An appellant has a right to the information relied upon by OPM to calculate its annuity determinations and, as OPM has sole access to that information, the Board expects OPM to provide it. Kozer v. Office of Personnel Management, 52 M.S.P.R. 93, 95-96 (1991). Where the Board has found that evidence in the record established that an appellant's CSRS retirement annuity was equal to the disability retirement annuity he could receive, the Board has dismissed the appeal as moot. Kozer, 45 M.S.P.R. at 507. In contrast, the Board has vacated an initial decision finding mootness where the record was absent of any evidence comparing the two methods of computing the appellant's retirement and disability retirement annuities. Dudar, 85 M.S.P.R. 579, ¶ 8. Here, as in Dudar, the record lacks any evidence from OPM to support a finding that the two annuities would be equal. Therefore, the AJ's finding that the two annuities would be equal and his conclusion that the appeal is moot are unsupported by the record, do not meet the requirements of 5 C.F.R. § 1201.111(b), and must be vacated. See id.; Dillingham v. Department of Justice, 73 M.S.P.R. 538, 541-43 (1997); Kissel v. U.S. Postal Service, 42 M.S.P.R. 154, 159-60 (1989); Addison v. Department of the Treasury, 30 M.S.P.R. 615, 619 (1986), aff'd, 864 F.2d 148 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (Table).
