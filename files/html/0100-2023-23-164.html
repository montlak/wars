g. Demotion

If through mitigation of removal to demotion the issue is whether there was compliance with the demotion order, the agency need only establish that in good faith it placed the appellant in a position having substantially the same duties and responsibilities as the position he would have occupied had the agency initially demoted him to that grade and position instead of removing him. This does not preclude a geographical reassignment. Butler v. USDA (Fed. Cir. 1986 NP No. 86-784). Back pay is calculated at the pay grade for the demoted position from the effective date of the removal. Glover v. USPS, 23 MSPR 306, 308-09 (1984). [Refer to Chapter 1, "Disciplinary Reassignment; Unified Penalties; Reassignment Leading to Reduction in Pay," and refer to Chapter 7, "Ineffectiveness as Supervisor; Demotion."]

A summary of the decisions concerning determination of positions for employees reinstated subject to a demotion was provided by Gutkowski v. USPS, 101 MSPR 399, 405-06 ¶ 15, 2006 MSPB 68 (2006), aff'd, Gutkowski v. USPS, 505 F.3d 1324, 1327-28 (Fed. Cir. 2007), post:

While the agency has no continuing obligation to reassign an appellant to a higher-graded position following reinstatement, consistent with Davis, the agency does have a continuing obligation to provide notice of and priority consideration for vacant higher-graded positions for which the employee is qualified and which the agency elects to fill in those instances where the position on reinstatement is less than the highest-graded position to which he would be entitled under the Board's final order.[3] Cuda v. U.S. Postal Service, 80 M.S.P.R. 195, 202 (1998); Stabile v. Defense Commissary Agency, 76 M.S.P.R. 658, 662 (1997). Thus, the agency here must provide the appellant notice of and priority consideration for all vacant positions which it elects to fill and for which the appellant is qualified or could become qualified, until such time as he is placed in the next lower-graded non-supervisory position below the level of his former Level 16 position.

[3] The obligation to provide notice assumes that the agency had no positions available, during the relevant time period, at the highest grade to which the appellant was entitled under the Board's Order. Of course, if the agency has failed to reinstate the appellant to the highest-graded position under the terms of the Board's order, where such position was available between the date of the first Board decision ordering the demotion and the time of reinstatement, the agency will be ordered to retroactively reassign the appellant to such position.

On appeal, the Federal Circuit affirmed in Gutkowski v. USPS, 505 F.3d 1324, 1328 (Fed. Cir. 2007), suggesting but not directly deciding that an MSPB remedial order could not require an agency to place an appellant in violation of a collective bargaining agreement so as to adversely affect other employees and expose the agency to contract grievances. For further description of treatment of demotions directed through Board orders, refer to Fischer v. Dept. of Treasury, 97 MSPR 546, 549-53 ¶¶ 6-16 (2004), post.

If the demotion is to a specified type of job, e.g., to the position of a teacher, the Board compares position descriptions of the employee's assigned position and the position desired by the employee to determine whether the agency complied with the penalty upheld by the Board. In Novinsky v. DOD, 37 MSPR 272, 275-76 (1988), the Board ordered demotion to the position of teacher. Appellant's enforcement petition sought the position of education specialist. The Board compared descriptions of the positions, found them to be substantially different, and determined that the agency properly placed the appellant into a teacher's position. As to collateral effects of the demotion, involving geographical location and bargaining unit status, the Board found nothing amiss in the agency's actions:

...Appellant's request for a separate maintenance allowance for his dependents is essentially unrelated to the action appealed and to our compliance orders and, therefore, the agency's denial of such request cannot constitute noncompliance. As for his request for an assignment in Spain, we note that appellant properly was reemployed in the same geographic area, Naples, Italy, to which he was assigned prior to his removal. The fact that appellant may occupy a position in a Naples-area bargaining unit, the members of which he once supervised, does not constitute a conflict of interest as summarily alleged by appellant. Bargaining unit membership or lack thereof is not personal to a particular employee but rather is incidental to whether the particular position encumbered by the employee is located within the certified bargaining unit....

Gomez v. USDA, 68 MSPR 202, 205-06 (1995), described the agency's obligations when a penalty is mitigated to demotion from a nonsupervisory or nonmanagerial position:

The burden is on the agency to submit proof of compliance with the Board's order. McDonough v. US Postal Service, 60 MSPR 122, 125 (1993). In determining whether an agency has complied with a Board order, the Board must determine whether an agency has placed the employee as nearly as possible in the status quo ante. See Holtgrewe v. F.D.I.C., 57 MSPR 307, 309 (1993). This principle applies not only where an action has been ordered cancelled, but also where, as here, the penalty has been mitigated. Id.

As previously noted, the Board's mitigation order directs the agency to place the appellant in "the next lowest grade" from the appellant's former Smokejumper, GS-6, position. Accordingly, there is no merit to the appellant's contention that he should have been demoted merely in step, and not in grade. Similarly, we find that the language of the Board's order did not obligate the agency to search beyond the appellant's former work unit for a suitable position. Cf. Hurst v. Department of the Navy, 61 MSPR 277, 281-82 (1994) (agency was not required to seek placement of disabled appellant beyond a single appointing authority); Novinsky v. Department of Defense, 37 MSPR 272, 276 (1988) (in complying with the Board's demotion order, the agency properly reinstated the employee to a position in the same location to which he was assigned prior to his removal).

As the agency conceded, the "next lowest graded position" would be a GS-5 position. The agency also implicitly acknowledged that there were vacant GS-5 positions at the Missoula location at the time of the Board's order, but asserted that the appellant was not qualified for any of them. The agency further stated that the offered position of Laborer, WG-2, was in the appellant's former work unit, and had the same number of guaranteed full-time pay periods as the appellant's former GS-6 position. In a later submission, the agency noted that the salary rate for the WG-2 position is only 9 cents less than that of a GS-5, Step 1 position, and that therefore the two positions are "equivalent."

The Board's order presupposes that the appellant must be qualified for any position to which he is demoted. Hey v. US Postal Service, 57 MSPR 443, 450 (1993). However, where an issue of compliance involves reassignment to a lower-graded position for which the appellant is qualified, the agency must submit documentation that it evaluated all available vacancies in terms of the appellant's abilities and experience. McDonough, 60 MSPR at 126. The agency here submitted no such documentation, but merely asserted that the appellant was not qualified for any but the WG-2 position. Such conclusory statements fail to satisfy the agency's burden of showing compliance with the Board's order.

On remand, the agency must identify all GS-5 positions which were vacant at the Missoula location as of June 7, 1994. For every such position for which the agency finds the appellant unqualified, it must submit explanation and documentation in support of its determination. If the appellant was qualified for a vacant GS-5 position that is not presently vacant, the agency must place the appellant in any currently vacant GS-5 position, for which he qualifies, at the Missoula site. If there is no such position available at this time, the agency must place the appellant in the highest available position for which he is qualified until such time as a GS-5 or equivalent position becomes available which the agency elects to fill. See Hawkins v. US Postal Service, 56 MSPR 633, 640-41 (1993). The agency must provide the appellant with timely notice of any such position vacancy which the agency elects to fill. Id.

If the administrative judge determines that the appellant should have been placed in a GS-5 position, effective June 7, 1994, the agency must make the appropriate adjustments to its back pay award and submit supporting documentation. Id.

Assessing how properly to demote an employee, the agency considers the qualifications required for a position. See Hey v. USPS, 57 MSPR 443, 450 (1993) ("[t]he agency fully supported its assertion that the appellant was not qualified for the Transportation Requirements Analyst position based on its point-by-point evaluation of his qualifications for the duties of the position[.]") (distinguishing Michael v. USPS, 41 MSPR 4, 5-6 (1989) (agency was in noncompliance with a mitigation decision when the agency speculatively asserted that the appellant was insufficiently trustworthy for the positions identified by the appellant during compliance proceedings)). If a remedial order is cast in general terms, e.g., if the demotion is to a position with the minimum grade reduction possible, the order presupposes that the employee is qualified for the job claimed. The agency rebuts the employee's claimed qualifications for a higher-level job by a thorough analysis of the required qualifications and the employee's credentials. Hey v. USPS, 57 MSPR 443, 450 (1993); see Fischer v. Dept. of Treasury, 97 MSPR 546 (2004), post.

McDonough v. USPS, 60 MSPR 122, 127 (1993), added points concerning demotions to the next highest position within the appellant's qualifications. First, during compliance proceedings the agency must submit documentation that it evaluated all available vacancies in terms of the appellant's abilities and experience. Second, if the agency protests that a given position was unavailable because the appellant was not qualified or could not become qualified without undue interruption of the agency's mission, the agency must explain how much training the appellant needed to qualify for the position and whether that training would cause an undue interruption in its mission. McDonough cited Jackson v. VA, 31 MSPR 135, 137 n.2 (1986), concluding that a training period of up to 90 days was not an undue disruption. Vitanza v. USPS, 94 MSPR 385, 389 ¶ 8 (2003), summarized the agency's compliance obligations when a supervisor is demoted to a nonsupervisory position and reiterated that an agency need not create a position for the purpose of accommodating a disciplinary demotion.

Lucas, 64 M.S.P.R. at 179.

To show that it placed the appellant in the proper position, the agency must submit documentation showing that it evaluated all available vacancies in terms of the appellant's abilities and experience. See Davis v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 M.S.P.R. 417, 422 (1995). The evaluation period for purposes of placement begins with the date of the initial decision, if that is the first Board decision mitigating the agency's removal action, and extends through the date of the employee's reinstatement. A body of decisional law concerns mitigation of adverse actions against supervisors and managers to demotions to nonsupervisory and nonmanagerial positions. "Generally, when the Board mitigated the penalty imposed on a supervisor to a demotion, it ordered placement in a nonsupervisory position with the least reduction in grade and pay." See Altieri v. DLA, 50 MSPR 82, 85 (1991). In 2004 the Board altered the law, overruling some decisions, when it decided Fischer v. Dept. of Treasury, 97 MSPR 546 (2004), 549-53 ¶¶ 6-16 (2004), permitting agencies to set, with deferential Board review, the level of demotions that agencies may impose. Fischer was removed from the position of GS-11 Senior Customs Inspector based on several charges of misconduct. The AJ mitigated the removal to demotion to a nonsupervisory position with the least reduction in grade and a 90-day suspension. Additional litigation resulted in an order setting the appellant's pay at a GS-9, step 4, rate. The agency petitioned the Board for reconsideration, and on reconsideration the Board revised its policy concerning agency discretion in setting pay levels in cases involving demotions imposed through Board mitigation orders. The Board decided that agencies should be given discretion, as in other matters involving choice of penalties, when setting levels of demotions:

Resolution of the pay-setting dispute before us must take into account three related principles. First, selection of the penalty for employee misconduct is "committed primarily and largely to the discretion of the agency" except where the agency's determination "appears totally unwarranted." Hagmeyer v. Department of the Treasury, 757 F.2d 1281, 1284 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Second, the Board will review an agency-imposed penalty only to determine if the agency considered all the relevant factors and exercised management discretion within the "tolerable limits of reasonableness." Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 M.S.P.R. 280, 306 (1981). Third, when the Board sustains fewer than all of the agency's charges, the Board may mitigate the penalty to the "maximum" reasonable penalty if it finds the original penalty "too severe," so long as the agency has not indicated that it desires that a lesser penalty be imposed on fewer charges. Lachance v. Devall, 178 F.3d 1246, 1260 (Fed. Cir. 1999). The Board has not always followed these fundamental principles when considering a petition for enforcement of a Board order that mitigates a penalty to a demotion. Today we correct matters.

The Board's first decision on the appellant's petition for enforcement, 90 M.S.P.R. 73, relied on Stabile v. Department of Defense, 85 M.S.P.R. 253 (2000), for its holding that the appellant's demotion to a lower-graded position had to be accomplished with the least reduction in pay. In Stabile, the Board acknowledged that there were inconsistent decisions on this subject. Id. ¶ 6. For example, in Kopec v. Department of the Army, 63 M.S.P.R. 576, 579 (1994), and Slaughter v. Department of Agriculture, 56 M.S.P.R. 349, 352 (1993), the Board held that, when complying with an order mitigating a removal to a demotion, an agency has the "discretion" to set the appellant's pay at Step 1 of the grade to which he had been demoted in the absence of any indication that this was improper under regulation or other authority. (Although Kopec and Slaughter involved compliance with an initial decision on an interim basis pending the outcome of an agency petition for review, for purposes of setting pay there is no difference between an agency's interim and final compliance obligations. Fletcher v. Department of Agriculture, 63 M.S.P.R. 165, 169 (1994).)

By contrast, in Davis v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 M.S.P.R. 417, 422 (1995), overruled on other gds., Stabile, 76 M.S.P.R. at 662, the Board held that, to comply with an order mitigating a removal to a demotion, an agency "may not simply impose a demotion that it believes is within the bounds of reasonableness," but instead must ensure that the demotion results in the "least reduction in grade and pay." As support for this approach, the Board cited Lucas v. Department of Defense, 64 M.S.P.R. 172, 178-79 & n.5 (1994), overruled by Stabile v. Defense Commissary Agency, 76 M.S.P.R. 658 (1997), which rejected the agency's argument that the magnitude of the reduction in grade and pay should be judged by a reasonableness standard; according to Lucas, the more-deferential reasonableness standard applies when the Board reviews a demotion that was originally effected by the agency, but it does not apply when pay is set pursuant to a Board order mitigating a penalty to a demotion.

In Stabile, 85 M.S.P.R. 253 ¶ 7, the Board overruled Kopec and Slaughter, under which the agency had been given discretion to set the pay in the lower-graded position as low as Step 1. Relying on Lucas, the Board held that, when it mitigates a penalty to a demotion "without further limitation as to pay level within a grade," the agency must set the appellant's pay at the step of the lower-graded position that results in the least reduction in pay consistent with the agency's pay-setting regulations regarding demotions for cause. Id. ¶¶ 7, 9. This rule, however, takes away agency discretion and contradicts Hagmeyer and Douglas. It also verges on independent setting of the penalty at the Board level, a notion that the court expressly rejected in Lachance v. Devall, 178 F.3d at 1259.

We find no support in law, policy, or logic for the distinction drawn in Lucas between the standard for reviewing a penalty originally imposed by the agency and the standard for reviewing a pay-setting determination made pursuant to a Board-ordered demotion. The ultimate criterion for evaluating the penalty in either situation is whether the penalty exceeds the tolerable limits of reasonableness. Douglas, 5 M.S.P.R. at 306. In mitigating an agency-imposed penalty to a demotion, the Board is concluding that the penalty originally selected by the agency is unreasonable. It does not necessarily follow, however, that, in implementing the Board-ordered demotion, anything more than the smallest-possible reduction in pay would also be unreasonable.

Again, where, as here, the Board sustains fewer than all the charges, it may mitigate to the "maximum" reasonable penalty if it finds that the penalty originally imposed by the agency was too severe. Devall, 178 F.3d at 1260. But, the Board has no business imposing a one-size-fits-all rule that, unless the agency's pay-setting regulations require a different result, a Board-ordered demotion must always result in the smallest-possible reduction in pay. Cf. Douglas, 5 M.S.P.R. at 303 (penalty selection should be based on "specific, individualized considerations, rather than...generalizations unrelated to the individual situation") (citations omitted). Ordering a demotion with the smallest-possible reduction in pay when not all the charges are sustained would be consistent with binding precedent in Devall only if such a penalty would be the "maximum" reasonable one; if a greater reduction in pay does not exceed the maximum reasonable penalty, then the Board should not modify it.

In light of the above, we hold that an agency has the discretion to choose the step of the grade to which an employee is demoted pursuant to a Board order. The Board will modify the agency's pay-setting determination only if it amounts to a penalty that exceeds the tolerable limits of reasonableness. The agency's pay-setting regulations regarding demotions for cause, if any, are but one factor to consider in this reasonableness inquiry. See Douglas, 5 M.S.P.R. at 305. To the extent that Stabile, 85 M.S.P.R. 253, Davis, 67 M.S.P.R. 417, Lucas, 64 M.S.P.R. 172, and any other decisions hold otherwise, they are hereby OVERRULED. Likewise, the Board's prior decisions in this compliance matter, 93 M.S.P.R. 505; 90 M.S.P.R. 73, are VACATED.

Applying the above-stated principles to the present case, we find the agency in compliance with the final Board order on the merits. The appellant, a law-enforcement officer stationed at the United States-Mexico border, allowed his girlfriend, a Mexican national, to enter the United States with six passengers in her automobile without passing through the designated point of entry for inspection, and later left them unattended in a cargo area. He also entered Mexico without declaring his firearm. Additionally, while in uniform and on duty, the appellant left his post to accompany his girlfriend to a meeting with the Immigration & Naturalization Service (INS) Port Director to complain about the INS's having impounded her car. The appellant also used an INS computer without authorization to investigate the impoundment on his girlfriend's behalf. See 69 M.S.P.R. at 616-19.

Despite the appellant's approximately 11 years of satisfactory, discipline-free service with the agency at the time of his removal, his misconduct "severely diminished the agency's trust in him by his use of his position to interfere with the operational decisions of another agency [the INS] as well as his own agency. [He] intentionally violated agency policy on more than one occasion." (undisturbed factual findings of the administrative judge). The appellant's actions negatively affected the "cooperative" relationship required between the agency and the INS. Heightening the seriousness of the appellant's misconduct is his status as a law enforcement officer. See Crawford v. Department of Justice, 45 M.S.P.R. 234, 237 (1990) (a law enforcement officer is held to a higher standard of conduct than other employees). What the appellant did was serious, and it warrants a severe penalty.[2] Cf. Jackson v. U.S. Postal Service, 79 M.S.P.R. 46, 50 (1998) (the Board has the discretion to modify a final merits decision on a petition for enforcement).[3]

[2] We note that the characterization of the appellant's misconduct in the final Board decision on the merits as "relatively minor and technical in nature" is inaccurate and inconsistent with the Board's decision to apply the severe sanction of a demotion and a 90-day suspension, incongruously implying that the appellant's actions were, indeed, serious. Fisher, 69 M.S.P.R. at 618.

[3] The record supports the agency's assertion in its motion that, contrary to the final Board decision on the merits, the appellant was not a supervisor, although he may have had some informal supervisory duties. In any event, if as the agency contends the appellant should not be treated as a supervisor for penalty purposes, the effect would be that an aggravating factor is absent. Cisneros v. Department of Defense, 83 M.S.P.R. 390, 398 (1999), aff'd, 243 F.3d 562 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (Table).

The appellant's rate of basic pay when he was removed was $35,865. His rate of basic pay upon his reinstatement effective in early 1995 at the GS-9, Step 1 level was $28,345. This was a 20% reduction in pay. This reduction does not seem excessive or beyond the tolerable limits of reasonableness, even considered alongside the 90-day suspension that the Board also ordered, given the misconduct described above, and given that it would appear that, after just 3 years of satisfactory performance, the appellant was earning almost what he was earning at the time of his removal. (Our analysis is based on the 1998 General Schedule and assumes that the appellant timely received within-grade increases. 5 U.S.C. § 5335(a)(1). If he did not earn within-grade increases after he was reinstated, that would have been because of poor performance, not the Board's mitigation order.) Nothing in the agency's pay-setting regulations regarding demotions for cause suggests that setting the pay as low as Step 1 of the lower-graded position is inappropriate.

Our prior decision holding that the agency should have set the appellant's pay at Step 4 amounted to fine-tuning and an inappropriate incursion into a matter committed to agency discretion. We do not find that setting the appellant's pay at GS-9, Step 1 upon reinstatement exceeded the maximum reasonable penalty for his misconduct, and the agency has not indicated that it would impose a lesser penalty for fewer than all of the charges. See Devall, 178 F.3d at 1260. Accordingly, we find the agency in compliance with the Board's final order on the merits.

Fischer overruled earlier decisions. Among the decisions overruled was the lead pay-setting case involving penalties mitigated to demotions, Stabile v. Defense Commissary Agency, 76 MSPR 658 (1997), itself overruling earlier law. Mr. Stabile was removed on March 11, 1995, from his position of New York commissary grocery manager, but the Board mitigated the removal to a suspension and a demotion to the next lower-graded nonmanagerial position. The initial decision became final on October 24, 1995. The appellant filed a petition for enforcement asserting that the agency's decision to place him into a WG-5 Store Worker position in Virginia did not restore him to the next lower-graded position.

Stabile, supra, 76 MSPR 658, 662-65 & n.2, involved a complex analysis that appeared to involve the following steps: (1) "the agency must submit documentation showing that it evaluated all available vacancies for which the employee was qualified, or could become qualified without undue interruption to the agency's mission"; (2) "the evaluation period for purposes of position placement begins with the date of the initial decision, if that is the first Board decision mitigating the agency's removal action, and extends through the date of the employee's reinstatement"; the appellant does not have an indefinite entitlement to reassignment to a higher-graded position until he is placed in the position with the least reduction in pay under the Board's final decision; (3) the agency has "a continuing obligation to provide notice of vacant higher-graded positions for which the employee is qualified and which the agency elects to fill in those instances where the position on reinstatement is less than the highest-graded position to which he would be entitled under the Board's final order"; (4) the agency must provide the appellant priority consideration for those positions, and priority consideration "requires that the agency consider the appellant before any other candidate for a position and do so in good faith"; (5) "only if the administrative judge determines that the appellant was entitled to a higher-graded position that was not available between the date of the initial decision (or the first Board decision ordering a demotion) and the date of reinstatement [does] the agency have a continuing obligation to notify the appellant of vacancies for higher-graded positions and provide him priority consideration for those positions"; (6) "the employee [is to] be placed as nearly as possible in his previous situation, including his former facility, within the terms of the Board's order," although reassignment to a position outside the appellant's former facility is possible if the agency demonstrates that "overriding circumstances precluded assignment to a position at the employee's former facility."

To demonstrate compliance under Stabile, the agency was required to perform the following steps: (1) submit a list of all vacant positions at the former facility "for which the appellant qualified, or could become qualified without undue interruption within the pay level of the next lowest nonmanagerial position and the position assigned to the appellant"; (2) "for those positions for which the agency found the appellant unqualified, it must submit a detailed explanation as to why the appellant was unqualified"; (3) as to the agency's claim that the wage grade positions at the former facility "were unsuitable because they were hourly wage positions and not the equivalent of a salaried position, the agency must submit supporting documentation, including a salary chart."

As to relief, the Board added the following requirements in Stabile. If the AJ determines that the appellant should have been placed in a higher-level position, the agency must: (1) retroactively place the appellant in such higher level position; (2) make the appropriate adjustments to its back pay award; and (3) submit supporting documentation"; and "if a higher-graded position than the one to which the employee was reinstated was available between the time of removal and the date of the Board's final decision, and the employee was qualified for the positions, back pay must be computed at the higher level." In its full glory, Stabile stated, id. at 662-65:

It is the agency's burden to establish compliance with a final Board order. Gomez v. Dept. of Agriculture, 68 MSPR 202, 205 (1995). Where, as here, the final Board order mitigates the removal action to a demotion to the next lower-graded nonmanagerial position, the agency may not simply impose a demotion that it believes is within the bounds of reasonableness; it must place the employee in a nonmanagerial position with the least reduction in grade and pay. Davis v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 MSPR 417, 422 (1995). To show that it placed the employee in the proper position, the agency must submit documentation showing that it evaluated all available vacancies for which the employee was qualified, or could become qualified without undue interruption to the agency's mission. Id. The evaluation period for purposes of position placement begins with the date of the initial decision, if that is the first Board decision mitigating the agency's removal action, and extends through the date of the employee's reinstatement. See McDonough v. U.S. Postal Service, 60 MSPR 122, 126 (1993).[4] To the extent that Davis v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 MSPR at 424, and Lucas v. Department of Defense, 64 MSPR 172, 179-80 (1994), suggest an entitlement to vacancies occurring after this period, they are overruled. Accordingly, we reject the appellant's argument that he has an indefinite entitlement to reassignment to a higher-graded position until such time as he is placed in the position with the least reduction in pay under the Board's final decision.

[4] The period of vacancy consideration for the purposes of entitlement to immediate placement is distinct from the period of vacancy consideration for purposes of back pay. The back pay consideration period extends from the date of the agency's action through the date of the final Board decision. Vaughn v. U.S. Postal Service, 75 MSPR 25, 30 (1997). Thus, if a higher-graded position than the one to which the employee was reinstated was available between the time removal and the date of the Board's final decision and the employee was qualified for the positions back pay must be computed at the higher level. Id.

While the agency has no continuing obligation to reassign an appellant to a higher-graded position following reinstatement, consistent with Davis, the agency does have a continuing obligation to provide notice of vacant higher-graded positions for which the employee is qualified and which the agency elects to fill in those instances where the position on reinstatement is less than the highest-graded position to which he would be entitled under the Board's final order.[5] Thus, the agency here must continue to provide the appellant notice of all vacant positions which it elects to fill and for which the appellant is qualified or could become qualified, until such time as he is placed in the next lower-graded nonmanagerial position below the level of his former GS-9 position.

[5] The obligation to provide notice assumes that the agency had no positions available, during the relevant time period, at the highest grade to which the appellant was entitled under the Board's Order. Of course, if the agency has failed to reinstate the appellant to the highest-graded position under the terms of the Board's order, where such position was available between the date of the first Board decision ordering the demotion and the time of reinstatement, the agency will be ordered to retroactively reassign the appellant to such position.

Further, to ensure that the notice is meaningful and adequately protects the appellant's rights, we hold that the agency must provide the appellant priority consideration for such positions. Cf. Jackson v. Veterans Administration, 31 MSPR 135, 136 (1986) (where there is an absence of vacancies at the equivalent of the grade next lower to appellant's WS-2 the appellant's rights are adequately protected by affording him an opportunity for placement in the first non-supervisory vacancy, at the equivalent of WS-1 which the agency elects to fill and for which the appellant is qualified or could become qualified without undue interruption). Priority consideration, as described in Smith v. Dep. of the Interior, MSPB Docket No. PH-0351-96-0126-X-1, slip op. 7 (Aug. 25, 1997), and Jackson v. Department of the Army, 69 MSPR 268, 271-72 (1996), requires that the agency consider the appellant before any other candidate for a position and do so in good faith.

Additionally, the concept of status quo ante requires that the employee be placed as nearly as possible in his previous situation, including his former facility, within the terms of the Board's order. Thus, the appellant will be entitled to be paid at a rate which is the least reduction, calculated from his retained rate, in pay available within the grade to which he is assigned. This is so even if the actual assigned grade is lower than the one grade demotion ordered, so long as the assigned grade otherwise complies with Stabile and with the Board's previous order.

In a ruling subsequently reversed on appeal on a procedural point, the Board determined that the agency's implementation of a Board mitigation order directing demotion of a supervisor to "the next highest nonsupervisory craft position" may take into account the agency's obligations under a collective bargaining agreement precluding the agency from reassigning supervisory employees to the bargaining unit as full-time employees, Harvey v. USPS, 85 MSPR 43, 45-47 ¶¶ 3-8 (1999):

The agency reinstated the appellant to the position of Part-Time Flexible Distribution/Window Clerk, PS-05. The appellant filed a petition for enforcement in which she alleged that she should have been appointed to a full-time PS-06 position as the next highest nonsupervisory craft position for which she was qualified. In response, the agency asserted that reassigning the appellant to a full-time bargaining unit position would violate the 1994-98 collective bargaining agreement (CBA) between the agency and the American Postal Workers Union and a 1990 national arbitration decision by Professor Carlton J. Snow (Snow Award). The agency contended that the Snow Award prohibited it from reassigning supervisory employees to the bargaining unit as full-time employees because doing so would deny bargaining unit employees with more seniority the opportunity to bid on the position and could deny part-time flexible employees the opportunity to be converted to full-time status. The administrative judge accepted the agency's argument that the arbitration decision prevented the appellant from being assigned to a full-time position....

...

It is the agency's burden to establish compliance with a final Board order. Stabile v. Defense Commissary Agency, 76 MSPR 658, 662 (1997). Where, as here, the final Board order mitigates the removal action to a demotion to the next highest nonsupervisory craft position for which the appellant is qualified, the agency may not simply a demotion that it believes is within the bounds of reasonableness; it must place the employee in a nonsupervisory craft position with the least reduction in grade and pay. Davis v. United States Postal Service, 67 MSPR 417, 422 (1995), overruled in part on other grounds, Stabile, 76 MSPR at 662....

Here, the evaluation period spanned from August 21, 1998, when the initial decision was issued, to September 21, 1998, when the appellant was reinstated. According to the agency, the following full-time vacancies were available during that period: Distribution/Window Clerk-General Expediter; Mail Processor Distribution Clerk; Flat Sorter Machine Operator; and Parcel Post Distributor, Machine. The agency states that all of these positions were at PS Level 5. The agency contends that the appellant could not be placed into any of these full-time positions without impairing the seniority rights of other clerk craft employees.

The Board treats the provisions of a collective bargaining agreement as having the weight of regulations and requires agencies to comply with them. Smith v. United States Postal Service, 55 MSPR 348, 356 (1992); Hicks v. United States Postal Service, 52 MSPR 561, 564 (1992). Under Article 37.2.D.6 of the CBA, a Postal Service employee begins a new period of seniority when reinstated into the clerk craft. According to the declarations submitted by the agency, the appellant could not be assigned to any of the full-time vacancies without impairing the rights of the more senior full-time regular employees bidding on those vacancies. We find that the agency has provided sufficient evidence that it has evaluated all available vacancies for which the appellant is qualified, or could become qualified without undue interruption to the agency's mission.

Over the dissent of Judge Schall, who opined that the Snow Award made good sense, since it avoided violation of the contractual seniority rights of third parties, the Federal Circuit reversed the Board in Harvey because the Postal Service had not raised the matter of the Snow Award by filing an appeal from or objecting to the Board's decision directing mitigation. It was too late for the employer to raise the problem of the arbitration award in response to compliance proceedings. "The mitigation Order of the MSPB was a final action, was not appealed, and must be complied with." Harvey v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 00-3191).

Of demotions ordered by the Board in lieu of removal and agency interests in geographically reassigning the appellant, Holtgrewe v. FDIC, 57 MSPR 307, 310-13 (1993), suggested that the agency may need to go beyond arguments of "work imbalances" to justify the reassignment. Chairman Levinson offered a concurring opinion that would have vested greater deference in the agency reassignment decision. Under the majority opinion:

In Jackson v. Veterans Administration, 31 MSPR 135, 136 (1986), a case where the agency was ordered "to cancel appellant's removal [and] to substitute in its place a demotion to a non-supervisory position at the next lowest grade," the Board held that "the agency should not be burdened by having to create a job for which no need exists for the purpose of accommodating a disciplinary demotion." The Board reiterated its ordered relief and added, alternatively:

In the event an equivalent position does not presently exist for which appellant is qualified or could become qualified through education and training without undue interruption of the agency's mission, the agency is ORDERED to place appellant in the highest available non-supervisory position for which he is presently qualified until such time as an equivalent position becomes vacant and which the agency elects to fill.

Id. at 137. The Board further stated in Jackson that "[t]he particular position to which appellant is to be assigned shall be at the considered discretion of the agency to the extent consistent with this Order." Id.

It is well settled that, where an agency is ordered to restore an employee to the position from which he was removed, it must provide the Board with a strong overriding interest for its failure to do so. See Mann, 29 MSPR at 274. In the event of a removal mitigated to a demotion to another position, however, Jackson indicates that more deference is given to the agency's discretion to determine the needs of its mission when complying with the Board's order. Nevertheless, the concept of status quo ante still requires that the employee be restored as nearly as possible to his previous situation, within the terms of the Board's order. See Kerr, 726 F.2d at 733. For this reason, the Board in Jackson, 31 MSPR at 137, while recognizing that an agency should not be burdened with creating a position for which no need exists, also protected the appellant's rights by ordering his placement into the highest available nonsupervisory position for which he qualified until such time as a position called for by the Board's order became available. In addition, in Mann, 29 MSPR at 275, the Board examined the legitimacy of the agency's reasons for reassigning the appellant rather than reassigning her replacement.

Upon balancing the competing interests of the agency and the appellant in this case in light of the reasoning in Jackson and Mann, we note, initially, that the agency has not stated at what point the Nebraska office became overstaffed. Its claim that there were no vacancies for bank examiners of any grade level is not persuasive, since the appellant occupied the position of a grade 9 bank examiner until he was removed and would have been entitled to return to that position in Nebraska had he received all the relief he sought in his appeal. Moreover, the appellant performed successfully as a grade 7 employee, and it is not apparent why he would need a "clean slate" in order to do so again.

We note the argument in the concurring opinion, that the Board's demotion orders should not be construed to give an appellant a superior claim to any job unless the person holding it can be considered his "replacement." As a practical matter, however, distinguishing "replacement" employees from other employees could be difficult or impossible. For example, if an agency fills an appellant's former position at the same time that it fills a position identical to it, the incumbent of either position could be considered a "replacement." In addition, if the agency hires an entry level employee after removing an appellant from a higher level position, and if it later promotes another employee to the appellant's grade level, either employee could be considered a "replacement." Moreover, the fairness of a test that would, in effect, treat "replacement" employees differently than others is doubtful, particularly inasmuch as an employee may not even be aware, when he is hired, promoted, or reassigned, that he is considered a "replacement" employee.

Additionally, we note that an agency can virtually always move work into an office that is overstaffed. Although such an action could result in an inefficient or otherwise undesirable arrangement, so that requiring an agency to take the action could therefore be deemed unreasonable, the agency's authority to move the work would, nevertheless, seem to preclude a finding that the agency "could not have [returned the appellant to his former location] without either creating an unnecessary job or transferring another employee who could not be considered a replacement."

In this case, the agency has proffered a lack of grade 7 vacancies, and its desire to have the appellant return to work with a "clean slate," as the reasons for its decision to solve its asserted overstaffing problems by reassigning the appellant rather than another employee. Implicit in the initial decision mitigating the agency's action, however, is the finding that the appellant was a previously-satisfactory employee who was promoted to duties that were beyond his current capabilities, but who should have been allowed to return to duty at his previous level where he had exhibited satisfactory performance. We find that the agency has not met its burden of proving that overriding circumstances require the appellant's reassignment in order to bring about this result. See Mann, 29 MSPR at 274-75.
