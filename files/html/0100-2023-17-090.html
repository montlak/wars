d. Method of Filing; Service

FLRA accepts exceptions through its electronic filing system. Exceptions may be filed through other means permitted by its regulations, noted earlier. Before 1998, exceptions could be filed only in person or by certified mail. When certified mail was required, the exceptions would be accepted if served by regular mail if the party served received them and could show no harm by that method of service. DOD, DCAA, Cent. Region and AFGE Local 3529, 35 FLRA 316, 320 (1990); see AFGE Local 2094 and VAMC, New York, New York, 51 FLRA 1612, 1613 n.2 (1996) ("the Agency does not claim, and it is not apparent, that the Agency was prejudiced by the regular mail service...we take no action with respect to the deficiency"); see NFFE Local 561 and Dept. of Army, Corps of Eng'rs, Mobile, 52 FLRA 207, 209 (1996) (service of exceptions by regular mail was harmless error); DOD, DLA and AFGE Local 2144, 39 FLRA 269, 272 (1991) (harmless errors included regular mail, failure to include copies of listed enclosures with copy of exceptions mailed to agency, and failure to include proof of service). There is an advantage to certified mail: if the envelope lacks a postmark, instead of applying a rule that dates the mailing five days before FLRA's receipt of the envelope, FLRA gives the party filing by certified mail an opportunity to submit a postmarked certified mail receipt to show that the exceptions were timely filed. Dept. of Navy, Naval Air Station, Corpus Christi and NFFE Local 797, 35 FLRA 1161, 1162 n.* (1990). The receipt should be stamped by the Postal Service (or by the person served, i.e., FLRA). A statement by a union representative concerning delivery of exceptions to the Post Office does not constitute proof of service pursuant to § 2429.27(b) of the regulations. Dept. of Army, Hq., XVIII Airborne Corps, Ft. Bragg and AFGE Local 1770, 37 FLRA 877, 878 (1990); cf. Dept. of Army, Corpus Christi Army Depot and AFGE Local 2142, 71 FLRA 304, 305 (2019) (Member DuBester concurring) (exceptions sent by certified mail, returned to sender for no apparent reason [followed by e-filing of duplicate exceptions], was timely when mail bore timely postmark); cf. Bureau of Prisons, FCC Miami and AFGE Local 3690, 73 FLRA 154 (2022) (delivery by USPS to wrong address allowed reconsideration of procedural dismissal of arbitration exceptions). During the Pandemic starting in early 2020, FLRA is not accepting in-person filings. The FLRA website should be checked to determine when in-person filings will again be accepted.

Facsimile transmission to FLRA is not an authorized or sufficient means of filing exceptions. Pueblo Depot Activity and NAGE Local R14-5, 50 FLRA 310, 310 n.* (1995); see AFGE Local 3615 and SSA ODAR, 65 FLRA 647, 648 (2011) (rejecting fax-filed agency opposition to exceptions; no waiver of time limit for filing was granted when agency did not establish that its delay was for reasons other than its inadvertence, accident, or mistake); Nat'l Gallery of Art and AFGE Local 1831, 55 FLRA 480, 480 (1999) (rejecting union's fax-filed opposition to agency exception). But it appears that if service is made by fax, the service will be considered effective without a showing by the other party of prejudice through not receiving a copy of the exceptions through the mail or by personal delivery. See Tidewater Va. FEMTC and Dept. of Navy, Norfolk Naval Shipyard, 53 FLRA 1149, 1154 (1998). But see Dept. of Navy, Marine Corps Depot Barstow and AFGE Local 1482, 66 FLRA 708 (2012) (dismissing exceptions faxed to union after agency apparently failed to remedy the service deficiency to FLRA's satisfaction).

Speaking of service of exceptions on the opposing party--not filing of exceptions with FLRA--as to email service, NTEU and DHS, C&BP, 69 FLRA 614, 616 (2016):

The Authority's Regulations state that documents may be served on other parties by email, "but only when the receiving party has agreed to be served by email."[17] When an excepting party fails to properly serve a copy of its exceptions on the opposing party, the Authority considers whether the failure harmed the opposing party.[18]

Here, the Agency indicated on its statement of service that it served its exceptions on the Union via both email and first class mail.

In its opposition to the Agency's exceptions, the Union attached a motion to dismiss the Agency's exceptions. In the motion, the Union asserts that the Agency served the Union with a copy of the exceptions solely by email, and that the Union did not consent to service by email. The Union also attached email messages between the parties in which it appears that the Agency's representative asked the Union's representative whether, in addition to an electronic copy, the Union would like a hard copy of the Agency's exceptions via first class mail. Finally, the Union argues that the Authority should dismiss the Agency's exceptions for failure to properly serve the Union with a copy of the exceptions.

However, the Union does not allege that it suffered any harm as a result of the Agency's failure to properly serve its exceptions, and there is no dispute that the Union timely filed its opposition, which closely tracked the Agency's exceptions. As such, even assuming that the Union did not consent to email service, the Union still timely filed its opposition and therefore suffered no harm.[21]

Service via interoffice mail does not comport with FLRA's service requirements. SSA, OHA and AFGE Local 3615, 55 FLRA 349, 351 (1999) (counting time for response to exceptions from date union received them, not date agency deposited them in interoffice mail); see SSA and AFGE Local 1923, 57 FLRA 530, 533 (2001) (time for filing union's opposition to agency's exceptions counted from time union was properly served rather than from date the agency placed copy of the exceptions under door of union's office).

FLRA assesses problems with service of exceptions based on their effect. If the service deficiency is quickly cured and the other party is able to timely file a response, no harm is done and the exceptions will be considered, SSA, ODAR and AFGE Local 3506, 67 FLRA 597, 599-600 (2014) (Member Pizzella dissenting):

A party filing timely exceptions with the Authority must serve a copy of its exceptions on the opposing party. When an excepting party fails to serve a copy of its exceptions on the opposing party, and the opposing party moves to dismiss based on that failure, the Authority considers whether the failure harmed the opposing party.[41] Here, the Union argues that the Agency's failure to serve the Union with a copy of its exceptions resulted in the Union not having "adequate time...to respond to the exceptions." However, two days after the Agency's failed attempt at service, the Agency successfully served a copy of its exceptions on the Union, and the Union was able to file its opposition four days before it was due. This indicates that the Union was not harmed by the Agency's initial failure to serve the Union.[43] Accordingly, we deny the Union's motion.
