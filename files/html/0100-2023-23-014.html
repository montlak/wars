g. Appellant's Understanding of Agreement

The Board requires the AJ to make an effort to ensure that the appellant understands the settlement she signs. Determining what the appellant does or does not understand can be a tricky business. Many agreements include a recitation that the parties have read, consulted with their attorneys, and fully understand the agreement that they sign. The requisite understanding of the settlement may be inferred from the circumstances and from a failure of the appellant to affirmatively disclaim an understanding of the agreement.

In Kowalczyk v. Dept. of Army, 44 MSPR 616, 623 (1990), the attorney represented to the AJ that the appellant agreed to a written settlement contract. The AJ acted on the copy of the settlement that she was sent. In fact, the appellant had not signed the agreement and later said he did not agree to it, but the Board determined the AJ acted properly based on the representations of appellant's counsel by accepting the agreement and dismissing the appeal. The Board added that a determination of whether the appellant understood the agreement can be determined from record evidence and from the appellant's failure to assert he did not understand the settlement:

...The Board has held that it is error for an administrative judge to dismiss an appeal without first documenting in the record whether the parties reached a settlement agreement, whether they understood the agreement's terms, and whether they had agreed that the settlement agreement was to be enforceable by the Board....

Prior to entering the settlement agreement into the record in this appeal, the administrative judge determined that the parties had reached a settlement agreement. She failed, however, to determine whether they understood the agreement's terms, and whether they had agreed that the settlement agreement was to be enforceable by the Board. See Mahoney, 37 MSPR at 149.

Neither the appellant nor his attorney has asserted that the appellant did not understand the terms of the settlement agreement. It is apparent from the record, although the appellant may not like the terms of the settlement agreement, he understood them. Additionally, having been represented, any alleged failure of the appellant to understand the settlement agreement is insufficient to set the agreement aside.... Accordingly, the administrative judge's failure to determine whether the appellant understood the terms of the agreement before entering the settlement agreement into the record did not prejudice the appellant's substantive rights.

Agreements will often have a provision stating that the agreement is not effective until it contains all signatures. Kowalczk does not say whether the settlement was structured to require all signatures (including the appellant's, since the agreement had a signature line for the appellant). The decision is not a good one, relying as it does on an attorney's representation that the agreement was acceptable when the settlement lacked the appellant's signature. An attorney may be presumed to have the authority to settle an appeal, as noted in Kowalczk, but the AJ should have stepped in and refused to accept that settlement without the appellant's signature until the AJ received clarification directly from the appellant as to whether he did or did not accept the settlement. [Refer later in this chapter to "Error or Lack of Authority of Appellant's Counsel or Union Representative--Agreement Sustained."]

Although the AJ is supposed to ensure that the appellant understands a settlement, the Board will not set aside a settlement agreement if the adjudicatory error is not shown (or alleged) to have prejudiced the parties' rights. See Enright v. Dept. of Army, 51 MSPR 18 (1991) (Table) (although the initial decision did not contain a specific finding that the parties understood their agreement and agreed to its terms, a memorandum to the file from the AJ indicated that the appellant understood and had agreed to the terms of the agreement); Mahboob v. Dept. of Navy, 44 MSPR 509, 512 (1990) (settlement was effective even though the AJ did not specifically document in the tape-recorded prehearing settlement conference or elsewhere in the record whether the parties understood the terms of the agreement and whether they agreed that the agreement was to be enforceable by the Board), rev'd on other grounds, Mahboob v. Dept. of Navy, 928 F.2d 1126 (Fed. Cir. 1991).

The circumstances will be examined in a challenge to an initial decision dismissing an appeal as settled. If the AJ does not specifically document the understanding of the parties to the agreement, the Board may rely on the facts that the parties were represented, the agreement was reached after a period of negotiations, the appellant did not contend that he failed to understand the agreement, and the agreement signed by the appellant contained a provision stating that he accepted and agreed to the terms of the agreement. See Adams v. Dept. of Navy, 53 MSPR 379, 382 (1992) (neither the appellant nor the agency challenged entering a recorded agreement into the record; the appellant did not allege, and the record did not indicate, that the appellant did not understand the settlement terms); Clark v. Dept. of Treasury, 48 MSPR 330, 333 (1991) (appellant acknowledged that he understood the agreement when he signed it, and appellant did not later contend that he did not understand the agreement; settlement was reached five days after negotiations began and five days after the agency representative signed it); Cranfield v. TVA, 44 MSPR 384, 389 (1990) (neither the appellant nor the agency alleged that they did not fully understand the terms of the agreement); Niday v. Dept. of Army, 42 MSPR 673, 676 (1990) (although the AJ did not document that the parties understood the terms of the agreement, the parties were represented, the settlement was reached at least two weeks after negotiations began, appellant did not contend that he did not understand the terms of the agreement, but only disagreed with the agency's interpretation of one of the provisions).
