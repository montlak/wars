3. Preponderance of Evidence

"Under 5 USC 7701(c)(1), the agency always has the burden of proving its case." Hill v. Dept. of Air Force, 3 MSPR 432, 433, 3 MSPB 506 (1980). The burden of proof in an adverse action is by a preponderance of the evidence. The Board's regulatory definition of a preponderance of evidence is stated at 5 CFR 1201.4(q) (2022): "The degree of relevant evidence that a reasonable person, considering the record as a whole, would accept as sufficient to find that a contested fact is more likely to be true than untrue." [formerly 5 CFR 1201.56(c)(2) (2014)] [Refer to Chapter 9, "Necessary Proof; Substantial Evidence," for the role of the substantial evidence test in performance cases.]

The Federal Circuit explained the concept of preponderant evidence, Dillard v. OPM, 792 Fed. Appx. 927, 930 (Fed. Cir. 2019 NP):

...A preponderance of the evidence is defined as "[t]he degree of relevant evidence that a reasonable person, considering the record as a whole, would accept as sufficient to find that a contested fact is more likely to be true than untrue." 5 C.F.R. § 1201.4(q); see Ortiz v. Principi, 274 F.3d 1361, 1365 (Fed. Cir. 2001) (quoting In re Winship, 397 U.S. 358, 371-72 (1970) ("The burden of proving something by a 'preponderance of the evidence' requires the trier of fact to believe that the existence of a fact is more probable than its nonexistence before [he or she] may find in favor of the party who has the burden to persuade the [judge] of the fact's existence.")).

Every element of the agency's case must be established by preponderant evidence. McDonough v. USPS, 4 MSPR 382, 384, 4 MSPB 441 (1980); see Nazelrod v. DOJ, 50 MSPR 456, 462 (1991) (quoting a regulatory definition of preponderant evidence and determining the testimony of the witness to be so equivocal that, without corroboration, it did not sustain the theft charge); Kennedy v. Dept. of Army, 22 MSPR 190, 193 (1984) (the agency was required to prove by preponderant evidence charges of false statements with the intent to harm or destroy the reputation of colleagues, supervisors, and the agency).

The agency's burden is to prove its case. That the appellant offers nothing other than allegations in response to the agency's evidence does not serve to prove the agency's case if the agency's evidence is insufficient to sustain its charges. See Berube v. GSA, 30 MSPR 581, 590 (1986) ("the agency's evidence on this charge was diffuse, internally inconsistent and weak"); Santiago v. Dept. of Air Force, 3 MSPR 457, 459 (1980) ("The assertion that the agency's evidence, whatever its quality, must be found to be the preponderance since the appellant introduced only his assertions is without merit.").

It is not the AJ's job to tell the agency precisely what evidence it needs to support its charges, Danko v. DOD, 5 MSPR 426, 428-29, 5 MSPB 435 (1981):

The agency's failure to support its action in this case apparently stems, at least in part, from a misconception of its role and the role of the Board in the appeals process. For example, in the petition for review the agency argued that its evidence against appellant was sufficient since he merely denied some of the alleged deficiencies and did not provide evidence refuting the charges; that the presiding official could have requested any additional documents necessary for deciding the case; that it could have provided the presiding official with technical assistance to interpret the reviewer's notes if it had known that it was necessary; and that it assumed the presiding official would have known that it did not introduce copies of appellant's work product because it contained confidential information prohibited from release under 18 USC § 1905. All of these arguments underscore the agency's misunderstanding of what 5 USC § 7701(c)(1) means when it states that the agency must support its decision by a preponderance of the evidence. That section does not mean that the Board will advise an agency of the evidence that it should produce to support its case. It means that the agency has the burden of providing "relevant evidence which a reasonable mind, considering the record as a whole, might accept as sufficient to support a conclusion that the matter asserted is more likely to be true than not true." 5 CFR § 1201.56(c)(2) [now 5 CFR 1201.4(q)].

In this case the agency had the responsibility of producing enough evidence to substantiate appellant's alleged deficiencies; if it could not produce certain evidence, it had the burden of showing it was privileged. The agency also had the responsibility of providing whatever expert testimony that might be necessary to explain its documents....

The preponderance of evidence standard is not the same as a determination of whether the agency action is "arbitrary" or "unreasonable." In re Van Sciver, 1 MSPR 99, 99, 1 MSPB 94 (1979); cf. Remy v. VA, 8 MSPR 141, 145 & n.2, 7 MSPB 624 (1981) ("To require an appellant to prove that he or she did not commit the alleged offense by a preponderance of the evidence is legal error"; the issue is not whether the agency reasonably concluded that the appellant committed the misconduct). The preponderance of evidence standard places a greater burden of proof on the agency than does the substantial evidence standard. Flood v. Dept. of Navy, 19 MSPR 163, 164 n.* (1984). The burden does not shift as to elements of the agency's case; the burden extends to the rebuttal of appellant's allegations countering the agency's charges. See Rudnick v. GSA, 11 MSPR 247, 250 (1982). [Refer to the employee's burden of proving affirmative defenses later in this chapter, "Affirmative Defenses."]

The Board offered an explanation of the meaning of the term preponderant evidence in Johnson v. Dept. of Air Force, 13 MSPR 236, 238 (1982): "[P]reponderance of the evidence exists when such evidence has, when considered and compared with that opposed to it, more convincing force and produces in the mind of the trier of fact a belief that such evidence is more likely true than not true." Wordy, but better. Black's Law Dictionary supplies a slightly more concise description of preponderance: "Greater weight of evidence, or evidence which is more credible and convincing to the mind."

The Supreme Court defines the burden of showing something by a preponderance of the evidence by stating that the burden "simply requires the trier of fact to believe that the existence of a fact is more probable than its nonexistence before he may find in favor of the party who has the burden to persuade the judge of the fact's existence." Concrete Pipe & Products of California, Inc. v. Construction Laborers Pension Trust, 113 S. Ct. 2264, 2279 (1993). The definition is a balancing test--an analysis designed to see who has produced the stronger case through evidence on key disputed facts. The Board borrowed from the Supreme Court in Jaramillo v. Dept. of Army, 81 MSPR 469, 473 ¶ 7 (1999), providing another explanation of the preponderant evidence test:

The statute requires that the agency prove its charges by a preponderance of the evidence. 5 USC § 7701(c)(1)(B). "The burden of showing something by a preponderance of evidence...simply requires the trier of fact to believe that the existence of a fact is more probable than its nonexistence before [he] may find in favor of the party who has the burden to persuade the [judge] of the fact's existence." Metropolitan Stevedore Co. v. Rambo, 521 U.S. 121, 137 n.9, 117 S. Ct. 1953, 1963 n.9, 138 L. Ed.2d 327 (1997) (citation omitted). Thus, "the preponderance standard goes to how convincing the evidence in favor of a fact must be in comparison with the evidence against before that fact may be found," and "when the evidence is evenly balanced, the [party with the burden of persuasion] must lose." Id. (citations omitted).

See Myers v. USDA, 88 MSPR 565, 573 ¶ 23 (2001) (reiterating Supreme Court's Rambo analysis).

Evidence is factual information, but to meet the preponderance test, the evidence offered should be relevant (it has something to do with the charges) and material (it adds something to the ability to analyze the charges). The evidence should also be comprehensible. You do not win a case if no one knows what you are talking about. If the evidence offered by the agency is so inconsistent and confusing that it is impossible to tell if the charges are correct, the agency case cannot be sustained. See Vasquez v. Dept. of Army, 27 MSPR 312, 315 (1985) (charged misrepresentation of timecards). [Refer to Chapter 4, "Circumstantial and Secondary Evidence."] Yet the evidence offered by the agency need not be overwhelming to be preponderant. As to an appellant's challenge to the "scanty" evidence offered in support of an adverse action, the Board found in favor of the agency in Duncan v. Dept. of Educ., 15 MSPR 31, 33 n.2 (1983):

Appellant argues in this connection that there was no requirement upon him to introduce evidence to refute the "scanty" evidence presented by the agency to substantiate its specifications in order for the presiding official to determine that the evidence failed to meet the preponderant evidence standard. However, as noted by the agency in its response to the petition for review, appellant did not deny the specifications underlying the charges in testifying at the hearing, offered no testimony, and introduced no evidence with respect to the specifications he now contests. The presiding official, therefore, properly determined that the unrebutted evidence presented by the agency was sufficient to be deemed preponderant evidence.

The Board occasionally uses the preponderance standard when it speaks of ultimate proof of whether an action promotes service efficiency. Although the use of the term is normally reserved for a shorthand description of the process of weighing evidence on a disputed fact, Epperson v. Dept. of Treasury, 11 MSPR 569, 574 (1982):

...In determining whether an agency has sustained its burden of proof, the question is not how many of the original specifications are sustained by the presiding official, but rather whether the reasons and specifications sustained are sufficient to support the agency action. Neither the statute nor the regulations incorporates a mathematical formula or requires a numerical test to determine the preponderance of evidence. Therefore, we agree with the presiding official that the agency supported its demotion action by a preponderance of the evidence, even though only 14 of its specifications were sustained.
