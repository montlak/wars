8. Effect of Military Service; Deposit for Military Service

Military service may affect civil service retirement benefits. Under Libertiny v. OPM, DA-0841-16-0520-I-1 (NP 2/21/2023):

Generally, an annuitant who performed military service after December 31, 1956, must meet the following requirements before his separation from civilian service to receive credit for his military service in his FERS annuity: (1) waive his military retired pay; and (2) make a deposit for each period of military service performed after December 31, 1956. 5 U.S.C. §§ 8411(c), 8422(e); see Barth v. Office of Personnel Management, 116 M.S.P.R. 123, ¶ 11 (2011); 5 C.F.R. § 842.306. However, there is a limited exception to the waiver requirement if an annuitant's retired pay is "based on a service-connected disability" that was "incurred in combat with an enemy of the United States," or "caused by an instrumentality of war and incurred in line of duty during a period of war[.]" 5 U.S.C. § 8411(c)(2)(A) (emphasis added); see Barth, 116 M.S.P.R. 123, ¶¶ 9, 11.

Sparks v. OPM, AT-0831-16-0677-I-1 (NP 6/15/2022), explained:

An annuitant who retires after September 7, 1982, is entitled to receive credit for active military service performed after 1956 under both CSRS and the Social Security system if he deposits an amount equal to 7% of his total post-1956 military pay, plus interest, with the Civil Service Retirement and Disability Fund. Hooten v. Office of Personnel Management, 114 M.S.P.R. 205, ¶ 6 (2010); see 5 U.S.C. § 8334(j). If the annuitant fails to make such a deposit, OPM must recalculate the annuity payments when he first becomes eligible for Social Security benefits to exclude credit for the post-1956 military service. 5 U.S.C. § 8332(j); Hooten, 114 M.S.P.R. 205, ¶ 6. Those employees who retire on or after October 1, 1983, must make such a deposit before their separation from service upon which entitlement to an annuity is based. Hooten, 114 M.S.P.R. 205, ¶ 6; 5 C.F.R. § 831.2104. However, the Board will order OPM to permit a post-separation deposit if there was administrative error by the individual's employing agency or OPM and the failure to make the deposit prior to retirement was the product of that administrative error. Hooten, 114 M.S.P.R. 205, ¶ 6; 5 C.F.R. § 831.2107(a)(1).

See Chupko v. Some of the complexities of the deposit for military service were elaborated on in Clark v. OPM, PH-0831-16-0234-I-1 (NP 2/3/2023):

Subsection 8332(j)(1) provides that military service is not included in calculating the service credit on which a CSRS annuity is based if the individual is entitled, or would on proper application be entitled, to monthly OASI benefits under 42 U.S.C. § 402. However, subsection 8332(j)(2)(A) provides that subsection 8332(j)(1) does not apply to any period of military service to which the individual has made a deposit with interest under section 8334(j). Such a deposit must be made before the date of the individual's retirement or before the date OPM takes final action on his retirement application. Collins v. Office of Personnel Management, 45 F.3d 1569, 1571 (Fed. Cir. 1995); 5 C.F.R. §§ 831.2104(b), 831.2107(b). Here, as noted above, OPM recalculated the appellant's annuity to eliminate credit for his post-1956 military service after he became eligible for OASI benefits in 2015 because he did not make the post-1956 military service deposit. In his Board appeal, he does not challenge OPM's finding that he did not make a military service deposit but argues that section 307 of Pub. L. No. 97-253 exempts him from the limitations of 5 U.S.C. § 8332(j) and the deposit provisions of section 8334(j) and entitles him to a smaller reduction of his monthly annuity. An appellant bears the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, that he is entitled to the benefits he seeks under the CSRS. See Cheeseman v. Office of Personnel Management, 791 F.2d 138, 140-41 (Fed. Cir. 1986).

Section 307 of Pub. L. No. 97-253 is codified as amended in a statutory note titled "Recomputation at Age 62 of Credit for Military Service of Current Annuitants." 5 U.S.C. § 8332 note. Section 307(a) reads as follows:

The provisions of section 8332(j)...relating to credit for military service, shall not apply with respect to any individual who is entitled to an annuity under subchapter III of chapter 83 of title 5, United States Code, on or before the date of enactment of this Act [Sept. 8, 1982] or who is entitled to an annuity based on a separation from service occurring on or before such date of enactment.

The administrative judge found that section 307 did not apply to the appellant because he was not "entitled" to an annuity on or before the date of its enactment and because the laws that are applicable to his annuity rights are those that were in effect at the time of his July 1, 2009 retirement. The appellant argues on review that section 307(a) applies to him because he was "entitled" to a CSRS annuity as of September 8, 1982, seemingly by virtue of the fact that he began his Federal employment in 1981.

As the title of the statutory note indicates, section 307 applies to individuals who were "current annuitants," i.e., entitled to a CSRS annuity, as of September 8, 1982. 5 U.S.C. § 8332 note (Recomputation at Age 62 of Credit for Military Service of Current Annuitants); see Jackson v. Office of Personnel Management, No. 97-3331, 1997 WL 758780, at *2 (Fed. Cir. Dec. 10, 1997). Under CSRS, an "annuitant" is defined as a former employee "who, on the basis of his service, meets all requirements of this subchapter for title to annuity and files claim therefor." 5 U.S.C. § 8331(9). To be eligible for a CSRS annuity, an individual must have at least 5 years of creditable service and must serve at least 1 of the last 2 years of Federal service in a position covered by the Civil Service Retirement Act of 1954. 5 U.S.C. § 8333(a)-(b). Here, the appellant, who became a Federal employee in 1981, was not a "current annuitant," or even eligible to apply for a CSRS annuity, on or by September 8, 1982. Accordingly, we agree with the administrative judge's determination that section 307(a) does not apply to the appellant. The appellant's assertion on review that he was "entitled" to an annuity on or before September 8, 1982, because he was already a Federal employee is unpersuasive and provides no basis to disturb this finding.

The appellant also argues that, even if section 307(a) does not apply to him, his annuity should be reduced pursuant to the alternative method described in section 307(b). We disagree. Section 307(b) established an alternate method of determining any reduction in a CSRS annuity due to an annuitant's eligibility for OASI. 5 U.S.C. § 8332 note; Kerr v. Office of Personnel Management, 29 M.S.P.R. 284, 286 (1985), aff'd, 802 F.2d 469 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (Table). However, it only applies to individuals described in section 307(a), i.e., annuitants who retired on or before September 8, 1982. 5 U.S.C. § 8332 note; Burdick v. Office of Personnel Management, 49 M.S.P.R. 216, 218 (1991); Hartness v. Office of Personnel Management, 30 M.S.P.R. 360, 362 (1986). Here, because the appellant retired after September 8, 1982, he is not eligible for the alternate method of reducing his annuity described in section 307(b). See Burdick, 49 M.S.P.R. at 218.

In light of the foregoing, we agree with the administrative judge's determination that OPM correctly reduced the appellant's annuity upon his becoming eligible for OASI benefits in 2015 because he did not timely make a military service deposit. We further find no merit to the appellant's contention that he is entitled to an alternate reduction amount because he indisputably retired after September 8, 1982. We have considered the appellant's remaining arguments regarding his belief that he is covered by section 307 of Pub. L. No. 97-253, but find that they are contrary to the clear and controlling language of the statute....

Military service cannot fulfill basic retirement eligibility requirements, Tirado v. Dept. of Treasury, 757 F.2d 263, 264-65 (Fed. Cir. 1985):

Petitioner's major claim is that he did have the five years service necessary for disability retirement because, though he admittedly did not have five years of civilian service, the total of his civilian and military service exceeded five years. He directly challenges the holding of the full Board that military service cannot be counted in measuring an employee's initial eligibility for disability retirement. His argument is that the word "service," in the legislation relating to federal civil service retirement (Chapter 83 of Title 5 of the United States Code, Subchapter III), is generally defined (5 U.S.C. Sec. 8331(12)) as "employment creditable under section 8332," and in turn Sec. 8332 ("Creditable service") expressly allows credit for military service (Sec. 8332(c) and (d)). It follows, Tirado says, that the five years service required in Sec. 8337(a) for eligibility for disability retirement includes military as well as civilian service.

The difficulty with this position is that Sec. 8337(a) very specifically requires five years of "civilian service" for eligibility for disability retirement. The same is true of the eligibility provision for a non-disability retirement based on time-in-service. 5 U.S.C. Sec. 8333(a). Similarly, deferred retirement is available at age 62 to an employee who becomes ineligible for civil service retirement "after completing 5 years of civilian service." 5 U.S.C. Sec. 8338(a). These coordinated and express provisions demonstrate that Congress wanted to restrict eligibility for civil service retirement--both disability and longevity retirement--to persons who have had at least five years of civilian federal employment. That is the minimum eligibility requirement.

Once that threshold standard of eligibility has been met, military service can be counted (in defined circumstances) in computing the amount of the proper retirement annuity to which the applicant is entitled. That is the special function of the statutory provisions, supra, on which petitioner relies--Secs. 8331(12) (defining "service" in terms of Sec. 8332) and 8332(c) and (d) (crediting military service in appropriate circumstances). Those sections have nothing to do with the minimum eligibility requirement of five years of civilian service. Indeed, it would be most extraordinary if Congress, in adopting those particular provisions, silently and impliedly meant to delete or override "civilian" from the specific eligibility provisions (which have long been in the law). The opposite is true--Congress obviously wanted only those individuals with a minimum of federal civilian service to be entitled to a federal civil service annuity.

The necessity for a basic minimum of civilian service, without augmentation or credit for military service, was restated by Reid v. OPM, 708 Fed. Appx. 677, 678-79 (Fed. Cir. 2017 NP):

Carmen P. Reid ("Reid") appeals from a final decision of the Merit Systems Protection Board ("Board"), holding that her military service was not creditable towards the satisfaction of the general eligibility requirement for a retirement annuity under the Federal Employees' Retirement System ("FERS"). Reid v. Office of Personnel Mgmt., No. SF-0842-17-0039-I-1 (M.S.P.B. Jan. 10, 2017). Because the Board did not err in reaching its decision, we affirm.

Title 5, section 8410 governs the general eligibility requirements for FERS. That section states: "Notwithstanding any other provision of this chapter, an employee or Member must complete at least 5 years of civilian service creditable under 5 U.S.C. § 8411 in order to be eligible for an annuity under this subchapter." Section 8411, titled "Creditable service," includes both certain civilian and certain military service.

Reid argues that the five year eligibility requirement may be met by summing her approximately four years and seven months of civilian service pursuant to 5 U.S.C. § 8411(b) and one year and two months of military service according to 5 U.S.C. § 8411(c)(1)(B). The Government argues that 5 U.S.C. § 8410 explicitly provides a baseline requirement of five years of exclusively "civilian service creditable under 5 U.S.C. § 8411" (emphasis added), and that the military service may only be creditable to determine the amount of the award once the baseline requirements are met.

We agree with the Government. The plain language of § 8410 requires five years of civilian service. To allow the non-civilian, i.e. military, service to count towards the eligibility criteria in § 8410 would render superfluous the word "civilian." "It is a cardinal principle of statutory construction that a statute ought, upon the whole, to be so construed that, if it can be prevented, no clause, sentence, or word shall be superfluous, void, or insignificant." TRW Inc. v. Andrews, 534 U.S. 19, 31 (2001) (internal quotation marks omitted). The latter part of § 8410, "creditable under 5 U.S.C. § 8411," does not expand the meaning of "at least 5 years of civilian service," but narrows the types of civilian service that may give rise to eligibility.

Our holding is also governed by Brown v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 872 F.2d 401 (Fed. Cir. 1989). There, interpreting 5 U.S.C. § 8442(b)(1) (setting forth the eligibility criteria for FERS benefits to widows and widowers), we held that the phrase "after completing at least 18 months of civilian service creditable under section 8411" did not allow using military service to establish eligibility. This was because the latter half of that phrase ("creditable under 5 U.S.C. § 8411") "does not change the basic requirement of section 8442 that creditable service must be civilian service to establish eligibility," 872 F.2d at 402, and the military service of the type contained in Section 8411 could only be used to compute the amount of an annuity. Id.

Reid argues that the basis for our decision in Brown was "not relevant law" because we cited a case dealing with Civilian Service Retirement Systems ("CSRS") and not FERS. See id. (citing as cf. Tirado v. Dep't of the Treasury, 757 F.2d 263, 264-65 (Fed. Cir. 1985)). We disagree. The basis for our decision in Brown was the text of the statute in § 8442, which is in relevant part identical to the text at issue here.

Reid cites as authority Cieslinski v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 610 F. App'x 979 (Fed. Cir. 2015) (nonprecedential) ("By statute, a federal employee 'must complete at least 5 years of civilian service,' to include military service if the employee has made the required deposit...in order to be qualified to receive a FERS annuity payment."). Cieslinski could not meet the eligibility requirements even if military experience could be included because he failed to make the payments to FERS necessary to claim such entitlement. 610 F. App'x at 981. See Smith v. Orr, 855 F.2d 1544, 1550 (Fed. Cir. 1988) ("[A] general expression in an opinion, which expression is not essential to the disposition of the case, does not control a judgment in a subsequent proceeding."). Second, Cieslinski is a non-precedential opinion, and thus has no binding force. Fed. Circ. R. 32.1(d). We also agree with the Board that the confusing language cited by Reid from the Office of Personnel Management's CSRS and FERS Handbook for Personnel and Payroll Offices cannot overrule the clear language of the statute.

The analysis in Reid, supra, was affirmed in a precedential circuit decision, Montelongo v. OPM, 939 F.3d 1351, 1352-54 (Fed. Cir. 2019), determining that time spent as a cadet in a military academy could not serve as basic FERS qualifying service:

The Office of Personnel Management (OPM) ruled that the cadet time did not qualify and therefore denied him the annuity. The Merit Systems Protection Board rejected Mr. Montelongo's challenge to OPM's ruling. We affirm.

Mr. Montelongo was a student cadet at West Point from July 1973 to June or July 1977--roughly four years. He then served in the United States Army from June 8, 1977, until December 31, 1996, when he retired. He did not argue for counting his post-Academy career in the military; the only issue was whether his cadet time should be counted in meeting § 8410's five-year threshold. It was, as it still is, undisputed that Mr. Montelongo's cadet time was "military service" that was creditable service under 5 U.S.C. § 8411(c)(1). But the administrative judge assigned to the matter concluded that being "creditable service" under § 8411 does not make the cadet time into the "civilian service" for which § 8410 sets a five-year minimum for annuity qualification. To be eligible for a FERS annuity, an employee "must complete at least 5 years of civilian service creditable under section 8411...." 5 U.S.C. § 8410. Under the plain language of that provision, for service to count in meeting the five-year minimum, it must be both "civilian service" and "creditable under section 8411." It is not enough for the applicant's service to satisfy the second half of this dual requirement, i.e., that it be creditable under § 8411--which means that it is "service" used for certain FERS purposes such as calculating the amount of an annuity to which the applicant is otherwise entitled. See id., § 8401(26) (defining "service" as "service which is creditable under section 8411"); id., § 8415(a) ("the annuity of an employee retiring under this subchapter is 1 percent of that individual's average pay multiplied by such individual's total service"). The service that qualifies under § 8410 also must satisfy the first half of that section's dual requirement: it must be "civilian service."

We agree with the Board that Mr. Montelongo's cadet service was not "civilian service." The statute, in its definitional section, expressly specifies that West Point cadet time is "military service." Id., § 8401(31). That definition is key to resolution of the legal issue because the relevant FERS statutory provisions make clear that, at least as relevant here, "military service" and "civilian service" are distinct categories.

Thus, § 8411 includes both military and civilian service as creditable "service," but it recognizes the distinction: it provides "credit for," among other things, certain periods of "military service," § 8411(c)(1), while separately allowing credit for and referring to certain "civilian service," § 8411(b)(3), (i)(1). The definitional section of the statute, § 8401, also separately refers to "military service," § 8401(31), and to "civilian service," § 8401(11)(C), (12)(A), 31, 37(A), 38(A). Indeed, the specific subsection that defines "military service" to include cadet time itself refers to "civilian service" as something distinct. § 8401(31). In this statutory context, which contains no other provision that overrides the separation of the two categories for present purposes, the qualification of cadet time as "military service" means that it is not "civilian service."

In Brown v. Office of Personnel Management, 872 F.2d 401, 402 (Fed. Cir. 1989), we insisted on the military-civilian distinction in a very similar context involving the FERS program. The question there was whether certain military service creditable under 5 U.S.C. § 8411 counts for meeting a "civilian service" eligibility requirement for FERS benefits to widows stated in § 8442. We held that it does not. We explained: "That section 8442(b)(1) says 'civilian service creditable under section 8411' and section 8411(c)(1)(A) allows credit for military service prior to 1957 does not change the basic requirement of section 8842 that creditable service must be civilian service to establish eligibility." Brown, 872 F.2d at 402. That reasoning carries over to the § 8410 issue here.

It also is consistent with precedent under the counter-part of the FERS provision at issue here for the Civil Service Retirement System (CSRS) that predated the 1986 enactment of the FERS regime. Specifically, 5 U.S.C. § 8333(a), since its enactment in 1948, has used language similar to § 8410, requiring five years of "civilian service" for eligibility for an annuity. Both before and after the enactment of the FERS provisions, we have held that only civilian service counts under the CSRS eligibility provision, so that, while military service may count in calculating the amount of an annuity if eligibility is established, the five-year minimum for eligibility may not be met by combining civilian and military service. See, e.g., Villanueva v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 980 F.2d 1431, 1432-33 (Fed. Cir. 1992); Tirado v. Dep't of Treasury, 757 F.2d 263, 264 (Fed. Cir. 1985). That precedent reinforces our reliance on the "civilian service"/"military service" distinction in interpreting § 8410.

In our non-precedential decision in Reid v. Office of Personnel Management, 708 Fed. Appx. 677 (Fed. Cir. 2017), we drew the same conclusion about § 8410 that we draw here. We explained:

The plain language of § 8410 requires five years of civilian service. To allow the non-civilian, i.e. military, service to count towards the eligibility criteria in § 8410 would render superfluous the word "civilian."... The latter part of § 8410, "creditable under 5 U.S.C. § 8411," does not expand the meaning of "at least 5 years of civilian service," but narrows the types of civilian service that may give rise to eligibility.

Id. at 678-79. We find that reasoning to be sound and now adopt the Reid reasoning and conclusion.

If an annuitant has not made such a deposit, OPM must recompute the annuity when the annuitant becomes eligible for social security benefits by excluding credit for the post-1956 military service. OPM has promulgated regulations implementing the above provisions. Under 5 C.F.R. § 831.2104, and for purposes of this appeal, there are two categories of individuals who may make a deposit for service performed before the separation on which title to a civil service annuity is based: (a) An employee currently occupying a position subject to subchapter III of chapter 83 of title 5, United States Code, including a person who was eligible to make a deposit under section 831.2104(a) but who failed to make the deposit before separation due to administrative error; and (b) a former employee who was separated with title to an annuity or who retired from a position subject to subchapter III of chapter 83 of title 5, United States Code, after September 8, 1982, and before October 1, 1983. The rest of 5 C.F.R. part 831, subpart U, sets forth a regulatory scheme that describes the procedures used by individuals under these two categories. An individual described in section 831.2104(a) "shall file an application for deposit with the appropriate office of the employing agency," while an individual described in section 831.2104(b) may, at the time of filing an application for retirement or death benefits, file an application for deposit or complete a deposit with OPM. 5 C.F.R. § 831.2105(a)-(b). McDevitt v. OPM, 118 MSPR 204, 206-08 ¶¶ 6-11, 2012 MSPB 72 (2012), summarizes the ins and outs of the military deposit cases and the impact of administrative errors:

An annuitant who retires after September 7, 1982, is entitled to receive credit for active duty military service performed after 1956 under both CSRS and the Social Security system if he deposits an amount equal to 7% of his total post-1956 military pay with the Civil Service Retirement and Disability Fund. Hooten v. Office of Personnel Management, 114 M.S.P.R. 205, ¶ 6 (2010); see 5 U.S.C. § 8334(j). If the annuitant fails to make such a deposit, OPM must recalculate the annuity payments when he first becomes eligible for Social Security benefits to exclude credit for the post-1956 service. 5 U.S.C. § 8332(j); Hooten, 114 M.S.P.R. 205, ¶ 6. Employees who retire on or after October 1, 1983, must make such deposit before their separation from service upon which title to an annuity is based. Hooten, 114 M.S.P.R. 205, ¶ 6; 5 C.F.R. § 831.2104. The Board will order OPM to permit a post-separation deposit, however, if there was administrative error by the individual's employing agency or OPM and the failure to make the deposit prior to retirement was the product of the administrative error. King v. Office of Personnel Management, 97 M.S.P.R. 307, VII 4, 15 (2004), aff'd sub nom. Grant v. Office of Personnel Management, 126 F. App'x 945 (Fed. Cir. 2005); 5 C.F.R. § 831.2107(a)(1).

The Board may find administrative error and waive the deposit deadline when: (1) the employee can show that he relied on misinformation in electing not to make the deposit; (2) an application package contains obvious errors or internal inconsistencies in which OPM or the employing agency has an obligation to investigate and resolve the problem before processing the application; or (3) an employee elected to make the deposit and the paperwork is in order, but neither the employing agency nor OPM followed through to ensure the deposit was made. King, 97 M.S.P.R. 307, ¶ 12 n.2.

As to the first scenario, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit has held that, "when an employee, at the time of election, asks for information regarding the amount of the military deposit or the consequences of failing to make a deposit, the government commits administrative error under 5 U.S.C. § 831.2107(a)(1) if its response either misrepresents the dollar amount in question, or is so indirect, inaccurate, or incomplete as to confuse the employee as to the amount of the deposit or the effect of any failure to make the deposit on the annuity recalculation." McCreary v. Office of Personnel Management, 459 F.3d 1344, 1349 (Fed. Cir. 2006). In so holding, the court affirmed the Board's longstanding policy that military veterans are entitled to expect that the government will provide them with accurate answers to questions concerning their deposit requirements to enable them to make informed decisions on matters that may significantly affect their annuities. Id.

On review, the appellant reiterates his argument made below that his inability to complete the military deposit while still an employee was the fault of his employing agency. He further asserts that his current employment status has no bearing on the appeal, and that even though he can currently make the military deposit, he has still been harmed by the administrative error that prevented him from making the deposit during his previous appointment because he might be required to pay additional interest on the amount of the deposit until the date the deposit is actually made. If, however, the appellant was prevented from making a deposit before separation from the IRS due to administrative error, and if he suffered any pecuniary loss because of this error, then he should be permitted to make a belated deposit. We are unable to determine from the record, however, whether the appellant incurred any loss, in the form of additional interest, even if there was administrative error by the IRS. Section 842.307(a)(4), 5 C.F.R., states that "[i]nterest is charged to the date of deposit." Further, the CSRS and FERS Handbook for Personnel and Payroll Offices (Handbook) (formerly Federal Personnel Manual Supplement, 830-1), indicates that, generally, there is no provision in law for the waiver of interest charged on military deposit accounts. See Chapter 23, Section 23A3.1-5K (made applicable to FERS employees by Chapter 23, Section 23B3.1-2D). The Handbook also states, however, that under certain circumstances, if an agency error caused an employee to be liable for additional interest, the agency may pay interest on behalf of its employee. Chapter 23, Section 23A3.1-5K. There is no indication in other appeals, in which the Board has found administrative error and ordered OPM to allow an appellant to make a belated deposit for military service, whether interest on the deposit was assessed up to the date of actual payment, or through some earlier date. Cf., e.g., Julian v. Office of Personnel Management, 70 M.S.P.R. 364, 369 (1996); Nunez v. Office of Personnel Management, 69 M.S.P.R. 326, 332-34 (1996).

If the appellant is currently eligible to make the military deposit and if the administrative judge finds that interest must be assessed until the date of the deposit, and further finds that neither the Board nor OPM has the authority to set an earlier date through which interest will run in the event of administrative error, then the administrative judge must dismiss the appellant's appeal as moot. OPM acknowledges in its petition for reconsideration that 5 U.S.C. §§ 8411(c)(1)(B) and 8411(d) are "seemingly incongruent," but urges that, under established rules of statutory construction, both provisions should be given effect. See United States v. Borden Co., 308 U.S. 188, 198 (1939). Specifically, OPM argues that there is a general rule against interpreting a statutory provision in derogation of another provision and courts have a "duty to construe statutes harmoniously" whenever possible. N. Singer, Sutherland Statutes and Statutory Construction § 53.1 (6th ed. 2000). Where statutes are incongruent, "[t]he correct rule of interpretation is, that if diverse statutes relate to the same thing, they ought all to be taken into consi[d]eration in construing any one of them, and it is an established rule of law, that all acts in pari materia are to be taken together, as if they were one law." United States v. Freeman, 44 U.S. 556, 564 (1845). OPM argues that the two provisions in question in this case can both be given effect when they are considered along with other statutory provisions that the Board failed to consider.

Among the provisions that OPM asserts that the Board failed to consider are parts of the Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act of 1994 (USERRA). Pub. L. No 103-353, 108 Stat. 3149 (1994). Specifically, 38 U.S.C. §§ 4316(b)(3) and (4) provide that a person deemed to be on furlough or leave of absence while serving in the uniformed services shall not be entitled to any benefits to which he would not otherwise be entitled if he had remained continuously employed, and that he may be required to pay the employee cost, if any, of any funded benefit continued to the extent other employees on furlough or leave of absence are so required. Section 4318 of Title 38, which addresses pension benefits, provides in subsection (a) that, upon reemployment, individuals who were on LWOP for military service are considered not to have incurred a break in service for purposes of accruing benefits and for determining nonforfeitability of pension benefits. Subsection (b)(2) provides, however, that a person so reemployed shall be entitled to accrued benefits that are contingent on the making of, or derived from, employee contributions only to the extent the person makes payment to the plan with respect to such contributions, but that no such payment may exceed the amount the person would have been permitted or required to contribute had he remained continuously employed throughout the period of leave of absence. And, as pertains to the matter at issue in this case, another statute, 5 U.S.C. § 8422(e)(1)(B), provides that, where military service interrupts creditable civilian service and reemployment occurs on or after August 1, 1990, the deposit payable may not exceed the amount that would have been deducted and withheld from basic pay during civilian service if the employee had not performed the period of military service.

Reading all of these statutory provisions together, we agree with OPM that where, as here, military service interrupts civilian service, a deposit not exceeding the amount that would have been deducted and withheld from his basic pay had he remained in civilian service during the period in question is required. Chevron U.S.A., Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 467 U.S. 837, 843 (1984) (where Congress has not directly addressed the precise question at issue, a reviewing forum should not simply impose its own construction of a statute, particularly where the administration of a congressionally created program necessarily requires an administrative agency to formulate policy and make rules to fill implicit and explicit gaps). Further, we note the Board's statement in the Whittacre decision regarding the lack of a specific OPM regulation mentioning a deposit requirement to obtain credit for periods of service while in a military LWOP status. Whittacre, 118 M.S.P.R. 33, ¶ 8. A regulation does provide that any period of time for which service credit under chapter 84 of title 5, U.S. Code, is specifically allowed by a provision of law is creditable subject to any applicable deposit requirements. 5 C.F.R. § 842.303(c). As we have found, 5 U.S.C. § 8411(d) provides that creditable service shall be allowed for leaves of absence without pay granted an employee while performing military service, § 8411(c)(1)(B) provides that military service performed after December 31, 1956, and before an employee's separation is creditable if a deposit is made, and § 8422(e)(1)(B) provides for the way in which the amount of deposit is calculated where military service interrupts creditable civilian service and where reemployment occurs on or after August 1, 1990. We therefore find that 5 C.F.R. § 842.303(c) implements these statutes to require the payment of a deposit under these circumstances. The appellant's argument that the fact that the 6-month limit generally applicable to service credit for leaves of absence unrelated to military service does not apply to an individual who is performing military service, 5 C.F.R. § 842.304(a)(4), does not cause us to reach a contrary conclusion.

The reasoning in Whittacre, supra, was confirmed in Faris v. Dept. of Air Force, ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2022 NP), determining that the requirement for a military deposit did not contravene USERRA:

Mr. Faris argues that the FERS statutory scheme, by requiring him to pay a deposit to receive FERS credit for periods of military service while he was on LWOP from his civilian job, denies him the USERRA-protected benefit of receiving FERS credit without paying a deposit. Mr. Faris' contentions are defeated by the clear language of the applicable statutory provisions.

The FERS statute provides that "an employee or Member shall be allowed credit for...each period of military service performed after December 31, 1956...if a deposit (including interest, if any) is made with respect to such period in accordance with section 8422(e)." 5 U.S.C. § 8411(c)(1)(B) (emphasis added). Plainly, § 8411(c)(1)(B) requires that an employee seeking credit for a period of military service must make a deposit in order to have such a credit allowed. This unambiguous statutory language compels us to conclude that Mr. Faris is not entitled to credit without paying the deposit. See Consumer Prods. Safety Comm'n v. GTE Sylvania, Inc., 447 U.S. 102, 108 (1980) ("[T]he starting point for interpreting a statute is the language of the statute itself. Absent a clearly expressed legislative intention to the contrary, that language must ordinarily be regarded as conclusive.").

In attempting to evade this straightforward analysis, Mr. Faris points to § 8411(d), which provides that "[c]redit under this chapter shall be allowed for leaves of absence without pay granted an employee while performing military service...." Mr. Faris argues he should be able to "claim rights to benefits" under this provision. However, reading the statute as a whole, as we must, see, e.g., Corley v. United States, 556 U.S. 303, 314 (2009) ("A statute should be construed so that effect is given to all its provisions...."), § 8411(d) merely clarifies that an employee on LWOP status to perform military service is eligible to receive FERS credit for his service. It does not absolve him of the obligation to comply with the conditions for obtaining such a credit, including making the deposit required by § 8411(c)(1)(B).

USERRA does not support a different conclusion. To the contrary, the statute explicitly contemplates that "a person who is absent from a position of employment by reason of service in the uniformed services...may be required to pay the employee cost, if any, of any funded benefit continued...." 38 U.S.C. § 4316(b)(1) & (4) (emphasis added). Another provision of USERRA, § 4316(b)(6), further provides that "[t]he entitlement of a person to a right or benefit under an employee pension benefit plan is provided for under section 4318"; and § 4318 explains that "[a] person reemployed under this chapter shall be entitled to accrued benefits...that are contingent on the making of...employee contributions...only to the extent the person makes payment to the plan with respect to such contributions...." 38 U.S.C. § 4318(b)(2) (emphasis added). Hence, USERRA is entirely consistent with the military deposit requirement.

As Mr. Faris points out, and the USAF does not dispute, a civilian employee in LWOP status who is not serving in the military can receive FERS credit for up to six months in any calendar year without making a deposit. See Bain v. Off. of Pers. Mgmt., 978 F.2d 1227, 1230 (Fed. Cir. 1992) ("Taken as a whole, the statutory scheme[ ] of FERS...allow[s] federal employees up to six months per year of retirement credit for leaves of absence...."). This does not make for a USERRA violation, however. As the USAF correctly responds, USERRA expressly provides that a person serving in the military while on LWOP status from their civil-service job may "be required to pay the employee cost, if any, of any funded benefit continued pursuant to paragraph (1) to the extent other employees on furlough or leave of absence are so required." 38 U.S.C. § 4316(b)(1) & (4) (emphasis added). For his USERRA claim, the pertinent comparison is between Mr. Faris' LWOP and employees "having similar seniority, status, and pay" who are on similar "furlough or leave of absence...." 38 U.S.C. § 4316(b)(1)(B); see also Tully v. Dep't of Just., 481 F.3d 1367, 1369 (Fed. Cir. 2007) (holding that "leave of absence" that "triggers a right to equivalent treatment" is one that is "comparable to the leave provided to the service member for military service," and rejecting petitioner's argument that he was entitled to "the best benefits available to any employee for any leave of absence"). Mr. Faris has not shown how such a comparison disfavors him. Moreover, as the USAF states, "employees on LWOP for reasons besides military service cannot receive more than six months of service credit in a calendar year, whereas employees on LWOP for military service do not face this limitation--a distinction favoring members of the military."

Our conclusions are consistent with Whittacre v. Office of Personnel Management, 120 M.S.P.R. 114 (2013), on which the MSPB relied in declining to adopt Mr. Faris' interpretation of the relevant statutes. In Whittacre, the MSPB considered the same statutes we have discussed here and held that when "military service interrupts civilian service, a [FERS] deposit not exceeding the amount that would have been deducted and withheld from his basic pay had he remained in civilian service during the period in question is required." 120 M.S.P.R. at 120.

Because the statutes required Mr. Faris to pay a deposit if he wished to receive FERS credit for his military service time, he was not entitled to receive FERS credit without making a deposit, and therefore Mr. Faris was not denied an employment benefit to which he was entitled. Accordingly, his USERRA claim fails, and the MSPB did not err in denying his request for corrective action.

Faris also determined that the appellant was not entitled to pay a deposit for FERS credit for service while he was on LWOP to participate in inactive duty National Guard training, id. at ____:

Title 5, § 8411(c)(1) allows for the accrual of FERS credit for military service. "Military service" is expressly defined as "active service." 5 U.S.C. § 8401(31) ("[T]he term 'military service' means honorable active service...."). Therefore, Mr. Faris' inactive duty training is not eligible for FERS credit.

There were appeals involving deposits that individuals sought to make for post-1956 military service that were blocked because they did not make timely elections. The appellants received negative reconsideration decisions from OPM and they appealed to the Board. The Board issued a lot of decisions stating that the deposits could be belatedly made because one or another of the forms used in the retirement process did not adequately explain the requirements of the deposit process and the deficiencies in the forms were equated to administrative error excusing the delay in making the deposits. Then, in King v. OPM, 97 MSPR 307, 311-18 ¶¶ 4-18 (2004), the Board revisited the issue and rewrote the law to avoid errors in retirement forms as administrative error:

The history of post-1956 military service deposit cases

In 1982, Congress provided employees who had post-1956 military service a chance to avoid a reduction in their CSRS benefits when they reached age 62 and began drawing SSA benefits if they had previously made a deposit to the CSRS fund for their post-1956 military service equal to seven percent of their total post-1956 military pay. 5 U.S.C. §§ 8332(j) and 8334(j). OPM's initial implementing regulations did not allow an annuitant to make a deposit for his post-1956 military service after he left federal employment. 47 Fed. Reg. 43,634 (1982) (interim regulations); 48 Fed. Reg. 38,781 (1983) (final regulations). Later, OPM amended its regulations to allow for postseparation deposits when the annuitant's failure to make the deposit before leaving federal employment was due to administrative error. 49 Fed. Reg. 20,631 (1984) (interim regulations); 57 Fed. Reg. 32,153 (1992) (final regulations). OPM has never defined the meaning of the term due to administrative error. It thus has been up to the Board to define what is meant by that term.

Several retirement forms relate to an employee's allegation that he failed to make a deposit for post-1956 military service due to administrative error, and the Board has considered those forms in adjudicating appeals that raise such a claim. Prior to the abolishment of the Federal Personnel Manual (FPM) at the end of 1993, FPM Letter 831-83 required agencies to provide employees with a certification form stating: "I have read the information about the effect of not making a deposit for my post-1956 military service and I do NOT want to make the deposit. I understand that I can't change my decision after I retire." For example, in Cox v. Office of Personnel Management, 69 M.S.P.R. 320, 323-24 (1996), Nunez v. Office of Personnel Management, 69 M.S.P.R. 326, 333 (1996), and Mopps v. Office of Personnel Management, 69 M.S.P.R. 314, 319 (1996), the Board found that the failure to provide this certification constituted administrative error, regardless of any other notice the employee may have received concerning the deposit. See also Perley v. Office of Personnel Management, 88 M.S.P.R. 183, ¶¶ 6-7 (2001); Priano v. Office of Personnel Management, 83 M.S.P.R. 35, ¶ 9 (1999); Jacob v. Office of Personnel Management, 69 M.S.P.R. 340, 344 (1996). The Board held in Drury v. Office of Personnel Management, 79 M.S.P.R. 493 (1998), that this requirement applies only to retirements that took place on or before December 31, 1993. Id. ¶¶ 7-9.

Early versions of Standard Form (SF)-2801, the retirement application itself, provided no information about the deposit option. Thus, the Board held that OPM's processing of the early versions of the SF-2801 contained inadequate information about the election requirement and constituted administrative error. Owens v. Office of Personnel Management, 76 M.S.P.R. 543, 548 (1997).

OPM revised the SF-2801 in 1990 to include more information concerning the deposit for post-1956 military service. On the first page of the 1990 version, the SF-2801 requires prospective annuitants to indicate whether they have performed military service, and, if they have, to complete Schedule A. Schedule A contains the following question and explanation:

If any of your military service occurred on or after January 1, 1957, have you paid a deposit to your agency for this service? (You must pay this deposit to your agency before separation. You cannot pay OPM after you retire.) See Section B of the instructions for the effect on your annuity if the deposition is not paid.

Section B of the instructions to the 1990 version of SF-2801 explains that prospective annuitants may pay a deposit to cover their post-1956 military service. The instructions state, in bold-face type: "The military service deposit must be paid to your agency while you are still employed." The instructions explain that if a deposit is not made to the CSRS fund prior to retirement and if the employee is eligible for SSA benefits, the appellant's CSRS annuity will be recomputed at age 62 to eliminate credit for the post-1956 military service. In Drury, the Board found that the 1990 version of SF-2801 provides employees with sufficient notice of the opportunity to make a deposit and of the consequences for not making the deposit. Drury, 79 M.S.P.R. 493, ¶ 13.

The revised OPM Form SF-2801 also contains Schedule D, entitled Agency Checklist. This portion of the retirement application, completed by the employing agency, is a means for it to ensure that the application is complete before it sends the application to OPM for processing. Section 8 of the Agency Checklist contains 18 questions (a through r) identifying documents attached or actions taken in connection with the application. The agency may answer each question by placing an "X" in the appropriate box for either "Yes" or "Not Applicable." Question 8.i. asks: "If post-1956 military service is involved and applicant has not made application to make a military service deposit, [is] OPM Form 1515 [attached?] " Question 8.j. asks: "If post 1956 military service deposit is not made, was applicant counseled about the effects of not paying the deposit?" The Agency Checklist does not require any signature, review or verification from or by the employee.

OPM's CSRS and FERS [Federal Employees' Retirement System] Handbook (OPM Handbook) provides that the Agency Checklist is "REQUIRED in all cases." Handbook, § 40B1.1-1. The Board held in Cox that an employing agency's failure to adhere to the mandatory requirements of a prior version of the Checklist constitutes administrative error justifying waiver of the deadline for making a deposit for post-1956 military service. Cox, 69 M.S.P.R. at 323.

OPM Form 1515, "Service Credit Payments for Post-1956 Military Service," is a part of OPM's retirement application package and explains to the applicant that you must either make a deposit for the military service or have your annuity benefits reduced at age 62, repeating this information in its instructions. Form 1515 directs employees to SSA if they have questions about whether they might be eligible for SSA benefits, and to their employing agency if they have questions about making the deposit. The form also requires employees to choose between the following two elections: "I want to make (or complete) this deposit. I will make the necessary payment to my employing agency, or, I do not want to make (or complete) this deposit." The OPM Handbook states that Form 1515 is REQUIRED when a retiring employee had military service after 1956 and has not paid a military service deposit. Handbook, § 40B1.1-1. Form 1515 has been revised several times. The August 15, 1995 version contains the above-noted language. The current version, which appears to have come into use in April 1998, reads as follows: "I do not want to make (or complete) this deposit. I understand this decision is irrevocable." [Bold in original.]

The Board's case law may not have been clear regarding the effect of omitting Form 1515 from the application package. Early cases such as Cox and Mopps held that Form 1515 is required. Cox, 69 M.S.P.R. at 322-23; Mopps, 69 M.S.P.R. at 318. However, Cox and Mopps also involved the failure to provide the required FPM certification, which alone would have been enough to warrant a belated opportunity to make a deposit under Nunez, 69 M.S.P.R. at 332-33, aside from any issue regarding any inaccuracies in or absence of the Agency Checklist.

In a more recent case, Davis v. Office of Personnel Management, 90 M.S.P.R. 436, ¶¶ 9-11 (2001), the Board found that the absence of Form 1515, coupled with the agency's certification on the Agency Checklist that Form 1515 was inapplicable, constituted administrative error warranting a belated opportunity to make a deposit. The Board made this holding even though the appellant was apprised of the deposit election by the current version of SF-2801. Davis, 90 M.S.P.R. 436, ¶ 3. The Board reached a similar conclusion in Sutcliff v. Office of Personnel Management, 86 M.S.P.R. 101, ¶ 10 (2000), although that case involved the element of misinformation in addition to the omission of Form 1515. On the other hand, the Board held in Clarke v. Office of Personnel Management, 73 M.S.P.R. 435 (1997), that Form 1515 was insufficient to adequately apprise the appellant of the consequences of failing to make a deposit for his post-1956 military service. Id. at 439.

The above history of the case law on what constitutes "administrative error" and what forms are adequate to apprise an appellant of the opportunity to make a deposit for post-1956 military service prompted the Board to request an advisory opinion from the Director of OPM pursuant to 5 U.S.C. § 1204(e)(1)(A) in King and in Grant. The Board solicited OPM's and the parties' views about the meaning of the phrase "due to administrative error." In requesting the advisory opinion, the Board pointed out that its decision in Drury held that the 1990 version of the SF-2801 is by itself sufficient to advise an individual of the military deposit requirement and the consequences of failing to make a deposit before separation. The Board also indicated that other decisions, such as Davis and Fromme, found that the omission of Form 1515 or a Certification pursuant to FPM Letter 831-83 constitutes administrative error. The Board pointed out that some cases, for example Johnson, stated that administrative error automatically entitles the appellant to make a post-separation deposit, whereas other decisions like Owens concluded that the appellant must show his failure to make the deposit before separation was "due to" this error. The Board asked OPM to explain why it requires Form 1515 in addition to Form SF-2801, but does not find that the omission of Form 1515 is administrative error warranting the opportunity to make a post-separation deposit.

OPM submitted an advisory opinion, which we have carefully considered. Although afforded the opportunity to respond to OPM's advisory opinion, the appellants in King and in Grant did not do so. Upon consideration of OPM's advisory opinion and a review of the Board's case law, we reach the following conclusions.

The Board will order OPM to allow an individual to make a post-separation deposit for post-1956 military service only if he shows that OPM or the employing agency committed administrative error in the processing of his application and that his failure to make the deposit prior to his separation was "due to," that is, caused by, that administrative error.

OPM's regulation permits a postseparation deposit only when the failure to make the deposit before separation was "due to" an administrative error. 5 C.F.R. § 831.2104(a). Apart from its plain language, the history of this regulatory provision indicates that the administrative error exception should not be given an expansive scope. Public Law No. 97-253, enacted in 1982, created the opportunity for individuals to receive credit for post-1956 military service under both CSRS and SSA by making a deposit for such service. As described above, OPM soon thereafter issued regulations to implement the deposit law. The initial regulations did not provide for post-separation deposits. Later, OPM amended its regulations to allow for post-separation deposits under very limited circumstances, that is, when failure to make the deposit before separation was due to administrative error. In announcing this change, OPM stated as follows:

It was and remains OPM's intent to require employees to pay the deposit prior to separation, but it has come to our attention that certain individuals would be unfairly treated if there were no exception to this rule. In some cases, employees have been mistakenly advised by their employing agency that there would be an opportunity to pay [the] military deposit after retirement. In other cases, employees have not been able to collect the information they need to complete the application for military deposit prior to retirement. Also, during a transitional period following enactment of Pub. L. [No.] 97-253, military service deposits could be made by employees separating prior to October 1, 1983, directly to OPM and this was perhaps a source of confusion. While the law and regulations, if strictly applied, would prohibit these individuals from making a deposit after separation, such strict enforcement would work a hardship on many blameless persons.

49 Fed. Reg. at 20,631. OPM was thus trying to eliminate unfair[ ] treat[ment] of blameless persons who were prevented from making a deposit before separation. Clearly OPM did not intend that anyone who could identify an irregularity in the processing of his retirement application would be allowed to make a post-separation deposit, but rather that the individual prove that his failure to make the deposit before separation was due to administrative error a very limited concept. Therefore, cases such as Davis, Perley, Fromme, Johnson, Priano, and Horton v. Office of Personnel Management, 84 M.S.P.R. 53 (1999), which permitted a postseparation deposit upon a showing of administrative error, without requiring that the failure to make the deposit prior to retirement was "caused by" or "due to" administrative error, will no longer be followed.

There is no administrative error when the employee is provided with and completes the 1990 version of OPM Form 2801 electing to either make or not make a deposit for his post-1956 military service.

As explained above, Schedule D, the Agency Checklist, is completed solely by the employing agency. It is intended as a means for helping the employing agency to complete a retirement application before it sends the application to OPM for processing. The agency, not the appellant, answers the questions on the Checklist by placing an "X" in the appropriate box for either "Yes" or "Not Applicable." The appellant does not sign or affirm to anything on the Checklist. Thus, we find that, while inaccuracies on the Checklist may be agency mistakes, they cannot amount to administrative error since the appellant has the opportunity through the other forms in his part of the application to decide and affirm whether or not he wants to make a deposit for his post-1956 military service. Thus, to the extent prior Board decisions such as Cox, 69 M.S.P.R. at 323, may have suggested that errors on the Agency Checklist constitute administrative error warranting a belated opportunity to make a deposit for post-1956 military service, those decisions will no longer be followed.

As we have described it, Form 1515 explains that an individual will receive CSRS credit for post-1956 military service after becoming eligible for SSA benefits only if he makes a deposit for that service before separation from federal service, and that failure to pay the deposit will result in a reduction in his CSRS annuity when he becomes eligible for SSA benefits. The form requires the individual to complete and sign an Employee Election in which he is given the choice between making a deposit and not making a deposit. The employing agency's failure to provide the appellant with this form was considered administrative error because the form states, in the Instructions to Employing Office, that [t]his form must be completed when an employee retires and agency records show that the employee has not made or completed a deposit for post-1956 military service. See Owens, 76 M.S.P.R. at 549 (an employing agency commits administrative error when it does not provide Form 1515 to a retiring employee who performed post-1956 military service). Moreover, OPM's Handbook requires an employing agency to have a retiring employee complete Form 1515 when the employee has post-1956 military service and the deposit has not already been completed. OPM Handbook, §§ 1A1-1.1(A), 23A3.1-1(C)(2). Nonetheless, OPM's processing of a retirement application without Form 1515 will not in every case necessarily lead to administrative error which would allow the annuitant to make a deposit for his post-1956 military service after he has separated from federal service. If an employee is provided with full and complete information in the 1990 version of the retirement application, Form 2801, the failure to provide him with Form 1515, which is neither required by statute nor regulation, may not, depending on the circumstances, constitute administrative error which would allow him to make a post-separation deposit for his post-1956 military service which he expressly declined to make in his retirement application.

In enacting the military deposit law, Congress stated that CSRS credit for post-1956 military service would automatically be lost when an annuitant became eligible for SSA benefits at age 62 unless the annuitant had previously made a deposit for that military service. We acknowledge this feature of the law, in combination with the OPM regulation requiring that the deposit be made before separation from federal service, may lead to unfortunate outcomes in some cases. However, it is not our place to rewrite the statute or OPM's regulations.

King was summarized by Elie v. OPM, DA-0831-13-0058-I-1 (NP 7/28/2014):

An employee who retires after September 7, 1982, is entitled to receive credit for active military service performed after 1956 under both the CSRS and the Social Security system if he deposits an amount equal to seven percent of his total post-1956 military pay, plus interest, with the Civil Service Retirement and Disability Fund. Lamb v. Office of Personnel Management, 112 M.S.P.R. 335, ¶ 9 (2009); see 5 U.S.C. § 8334(j). If the employee fails to make such a deposit, OPM must recalculate the annuity payments when he first becomes eligible for Social Security benefits to exclude credit for the post-1956 service. 5 U.S.C. § 8332(j); Lamb, 112 M.S.P.R. 335, ¶ 9. Those who retire on or after October 1, 1983, must make such a deposit before their separation from service upon which entitlement to an annuity is based. 5 C.F.R. § 831.2104. The Board will order OPM to permit a post-separation deposit, however, if there was administrative error by the individual's employing agency or OPM and the failure to make the deposit prior to retirement was the product of that administrative error. King v. Office of Personnel Management, 97 M.S.P.R. 307, ¶¶ 4, 15 (2004), aff'd sub nom. Grant v. Office of Personnel Management, 126 F. App'x 945 (Fed. Cir. 2005); 5 C.F.R. § 831.2107(a)(1).

The Board has found that there is no administrative error when, as here, an employee is provided with and completes the 1990 version of SF-2801 electing not to make a deposit for his post-1956 military service. King, 97 M.S.P.R. 307, ¶¶ 16-17. SF-2801 informs the employee that, if any of his military service occurred on or after January 1, 1957, he must pay a deposit for this service to his employing agency before separation to continue to receive CSRS credit for that service after he becomes eligible for Social Security benefits. Id. SF-2801 informs the employee further that he cannot pay OPM for post-1956 military service after he retires. Id. Section B of the instructions explains that if the employee does not pay the military service deposit while still employed, and he is eligible for Social Security benefits at age 62, OPM will recompute his annuity at that time to eliminate credit for the post-1956 military service. Id., ¶ 7.

The result of the many Board appeals is that if the employing agency does not adequately process an employee's attempt to make a military deposit before his separation, MSPB may require OPM to set a time limit for the deposit under 5 CFR 831.2107(a)(1). See Pinkston v. OPM, 57 MSPR 347, 350 (1993). That result occurred in Cox v. OPM, 69 MSPR 320, 322-24 (1996), with the Board directing OPM to set a time limit to complete a deposit for post-1956 military service when the agency committed what was described as administrative error by not using a "Employing Office Checklist" required by OPM to ensure that employees were given the forms to make the deposit and to obtain from the employees a certification stating their intent as to the deposit (Holtermann v. OPM, 57 MSPR 380 (1993), distinguished as involving application of a deposit-related regulation, 5 CFR 831.2104(b), that did not allow for waiver of the time limit for former employees, when for current employees § 831.2104(a) permitted a belated deposited based on agency administrative error). See Lancaster v. OPM, 112 MSPR 76, 80-81 ¶¶ 12-15, 2009 MSPB 140 (2009) (vague responses from the agency concerning deposit information, resulting in unexpected reductions in annuities, resulted in a finding of administrative error and another opportunity for the appellant to make a military service deposit); Fromme v. OPM, 86 MSPR 186, 191 ¶ 11 (2000) (agency did not explain to the appellant what would happen if he did become eligible for social security without making the deposit); Julian v. OPM, 70 MSPR 364 (1996) (summarizing the military deposit cases); Jacob v. OPM, 69 MSPR 340 (1996) (analysis similar to Cox); Nunez v. OPM, 69 MSPR 326 (1996) (analysis similar to Cox and Jacob); cf. Sutcliff v. OPM, 86 MSPR 101 (2000) (materially incorrect information concerning the deposit for military service constituted administrative error); Riddick v. OPM, 85 MSPR 472 (2000) (materially incorrect information from the agency retirement counselor of the amount of the deposit led the Board to require OPM to set a time for the necessary deposit). But see Holtermann v. OPM, 57 MSPR 380, 384-95 (1993) (describing OPM "grace period" for military deposits; distinguishing Pinkston because there was no administrative error).

Material misrepresentation by an agency concerning a deposit for military service constituted administrative error, Thomas v. OPM, 107 MSPR 334, 341-45 ¶ 14-19, 2007 MSPB 285 (2007) (Member Sapin dissenting):

A civil service annuitant who retires after September 7, 1982, is entitled to credit for post-1956 active duty military service under both the CSRS and the Social Security system only if he deposits with the Civil Service Retirement and Disability Fund an amount equal to 7 percent of his total post-1956 military pay. 5 U.S.C. §§ 8332(j)(1), 8334(j)(1)(A) (1996 & 2006 Supp.); McCrary, 459 F.3d at 1347; Clements v. Office of Personnel Management, 93 M.S.P.R. 115, ¶ 6 (2002). An employee who retires after October 1, 1983, must make this deposit before his separation from service. Clements, 93 M.S.P.R. 115, ¶ 6; 5 C.F.R. § 831.2104. If an annuitant does not make this deposit, OPM must recompute the annuity when he becomes eligible for Social Security benefits by excluding credit for the post-1956 military service. 5 U.S.C. § 8332(j)(1); McCrary, 459 F.3d at 1347; Clements, 93 M.S.P.R. 115, ¶ 6. Under OPM's regulation, an annuitant may make a post-separation deposit if he can show that OPM or the employing agency committed an administrative error and that his failure to make the deposit prior to his separation was "due to [the] administrative error." 5 C.F.R. § 831.2104(a); King v. Office of Personnel Management, 97 M.S.P.R. 307, ¶ 15 (2004), aff'd sub nom. Grant v. Office of Personnel Management, 126 F. App'x 945 (Fed. Cir. 2005); Owens v. Office of Personnel Management, 76 M.S.P.R. 543, 548 (1997), abrogated in part on other grounds by King, 97 M.S.P.R. 307, ¶ 17. Because OPM has never defined the meaning of the term "due to administrative error," it has been up to the Board to define what is meant by that term. King, 97 M.S.P.R. 307, ¶ 4.

The Board has found that the plain language and regulatory history of 5 C.F.R. § 831.2104(a) indicate that the administrative error exception should not be given expansive scope. 97 M.S.P.R. 307, ¶ 15. OPM amended its regulations to allow for post-separation deposits under very limited circumstances where the failure to make the deposit was due to administrative error. Id. In amending its regulations to allow for post-separation deposits, OPM noted that certain individuals would be unfairly treated if there were no exception to the rule. Id. (quoting Notice of Interim Military Service Deposit Regulations, 49 Fed. Reg. 20,631 (May 16, 1984)). OPM cited as examples employees who were mistakenly advised by their employing agency that they could make the deposit after retirement and employees who were not able to collect the information they needed to complete the application prior to retirement. Id. (quoting 49 Fed. Reg. 20,631). The Board has found as follows:

OPM was thus trying to eliminate "unfair[ ] treat[ment] " of "blameless" persons who were prevented from making a deposit before separation. Where an annuitant receives full and fair notice of the requirement to make a deposit, the government is not required to inform an annuitant about the dollar consequences of electing not to make the deposit. Collins v. Office of Personnel Management, 45 F.3d 1569, 1573-74 (Fed. Cir. 1995). But the Board has consistently found administrative error where, in response to an employee's inquiry, the employing agency provides material misinformation concerning the deposit and the consequences of not making the deposit prior to separation. McCrary, 459 F.3d at 1348; Gilliam v. Office of Personnel Management, 91 M.S.P.R. 352, ¶ 14 (2002). The Federal Circuit recently examined the government's obligations when responding to a direct inquiry regarding the military deposit in McCrary, 459 F.3d at 1348. The court held as follows:

[W]hen an employee, at the time of an election, asks for information regarding the amount of the military deposit or the consequences of failing to make the deposit, the government commits administrative error...if its response either misrepresents the dollar amounts in question or is so indirect, inaccurate, or incomplete as to confuse or mislead the employee as to the amount of the deposit or the effect of any failure to make the deposit on the annuity recalculation. The government will best avoid administrative error by giving the individual a direct answer and providing the exact amount needed for the deposit as well as at least a reasonably accurate estimate of the annuity adjustment likely to result from a decision not to make a deposit. These numbers should be readily available to the government and normally will impose no unreasonable burden upon it.

Id. at 1349.

We find that the appellant has failed to show that his employing agency is responsible for his professed belief that his Social Security benefits, rather than his CSRS annuity, would be reduced at age 62 if he did not make the deposit. Cf. McCrary, 459 F.3d at 1349 (agency retirement counselor affirmatively misled the appellant in response to a direct inquiry about the dollar consequences of the deposit requirement). The appellant and his wife provided competing explanations as to how he came to believe that his Social Security benefit would be reduced if he did not pay the deposit. According to the appellant and his wife, he was so informed by an unknown individual in civilian personnel in 1988, by other people at McChord AFB, by someone named Barbara in McChord AFB's civilian personnel office at the time of his retirement, and by Ms. Bara. In addition, he testified that he "presumed" it would come out of his Social Security check. Id. This vague and conflicting testimony is not sufficient to support a finding of administrative error, particularly where the SF-2801 and OPM Form 1515 correctly informed the appellant of the consequences of his failure to make a deposit prior to his separation. Cf. McCrary, 459 F.3d at 1348-49. The explanatory paragraph on Schedule A of the form itself referred to "the effect on your annuity if the deposit is not paid." The explanatory paragraph on OPM Form 1515 informed the appellant that he must either make the deposit "or have [his] annuity benefits reduced at age 62."

The appellant's employing agency nonetheless did commit administrative error when Ms. Bara, in response to the appellant's direct inquiry, failed to inform the appellant of the amount of his deposit. See McCrary, 459 F.3d at 1349 (when an employee asks for information regarding the amount of the military deposit, "[t]he government will best avoid administrative error by giving the individual a direct answer and providing the exact amount needed for the deposit"). Specifically, the appellant testified that he asked Ms. Bara the amount he needed to pay back and that Ms. Bara did not provide him with any figures, did not know how to calculate the deposit amount, and referred him to the "military side." This administrative error does not justify a waiver of the deadline for making the deposit, however, because the appellant did not show that his failure to make the deposit was "due to" the lack of specific information about the amount of the deposit. See Owens, 76 M.S.P.R. at 548 (appellant was not entitled to waiver of deposit deadline where he did not establish that his failure to make the deposit was "due to" the employing agency's administrative error). The appellant testified that his military pay was less than $1,200 per year, and he conceded that he knew the deposit would have been a small amount. He also had page 2 of OPM Form 1515, which explicitly informed him that the deposit was 7 percent of his military basic pay. He further testified that he figured he could work more hours after he retired and make up the difference in his Social Security benefit. Had the appellant read the relevant portions of the SF-2801 and OPM Form 1515, he would have been alerted to the fact that his annuity would be affected by his failure to make the deposit. Thus, the appellant's own testimony shows that, although he knew that the deposit was only a small amount, he decided not to make the deposit based upon his mistaken belief that he could make up the reduction in his Social Security benefit. The appellant's "mistake" was caused by his failure to read the forms he executed rather than any error by OPM or his employing agency. Under these facts, we cannot find that the appellant's failure to make the deposit was "due to" administrative error. See Owens, 76 M.S.P.R. at 548.

She, therefore, found that the appellant was entitled to a deferred annuity and reversed OPM's reconsideration decision. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.111.

[4] Section 8332(c)(1) provides in pertinent part:

(A) [T]he service of an individual who first becomes an employee or Member before October 1, 1982, shall include credit for each period of military service performed before the date of the separation on which the entitlement to an annuity under this subchapter is based....

...

The Civil Service Retirement Act of May 29, 1930, as amended, was amended by the enactment of Public Law No. 80-426, which took effect on April 1, 1948. The new amendment changed the existing provision that permitted entitlement to an annuity based upon a combination of five years of civilian and military service by requiring eligibility to be based solely on five years of civilian service. See Civil Service Retirement Act--Amendment, Pub. L. No. 80-426, § 5, 62 Stat. 48 (1948).

In her initial decision, the administrative judge relied upon Thornton v. Office of Personnel Management, 24 M.S.P.R. 372 (1984), where the Board found that military service could be combined with civilian service to meet the eligibility requirement. However, in Thornton, the appellant's separation from the service occurred before the effective date of Public Law 80-426 on April 1, 1948, see id. at 374, while in the present case, the appellant was separated from the service on December 20, 1948, after the law was changed.

Under 5 U.S.C. § 8332(g),[6] the appellant is entitled to have his military service considered as civilian service if he left his civilian position during wartime or a declared national emergency and did not apply for and receive a lump-sum credit for his retirement contributions. The record indicates that the appellant left his civilian position in June 1943, during World War II, to enter military service and returned to his civilian position at the Watervliet Arsenal immediately upon being discharged from the military. The record further shows that his civilian position was covered under the civil service retirement system, and that the appellant did not apply for and receive a lump-sum credit for his retirement contributions. Therefore, we find that the appellant was entitled under 5 U.S.C. § 8332(g) to have his military service considered as civilian service, and thus, he has satisfied the five years minimum civilian service requirement under 5 U.S.C. § 8338(a).

[6] Section 8332(g) provides in pertinent part:

An employee who during the period of a war, or of a national emergency as proclaimed by the President or declared by Congress, leaves his position to enter the military service is deemed, for the purpose of this subchapter, as not separated from his civilian position because of that military service, unless he applies for and receives a lump-sum credit under this subchapter.

The appellant was employed with the Department of Army from May 1966, through October 1966. During this time, he was covered by the Civil Service Retirement System (CSRS). The appellant then served as an enlisted member of the United States Army from October 1966, through October 1968 and from September 1972, through March 1983. Finally, the appellant began working for the Department of Commerce in February 1984, where he remained until January 2003. While employed with the Department of Commerce, the appellant was covered by the Federal Employee's Retirement System (FERS), and he made the necessary deposit for his military service to be counted towards that FERS annuity computation. See 5 C.F.R. § 842.306(a)(2) (indicating that post-1956 military service is creditable subject to the employee making a qualifying deposit).

The appellant retired at age 55. Following retirement, OPM notified the appellant that he would receive an annuity supplement as part of his FERS retirement benefit to bridge the gap until he became eligible for Social Security benefits. At some point, the appellant requested an audit of his FERS annuity supplement calculation. OPM responded with an explanation, including the fact that the appellant's military service was not counted towards his annuity supplement.

The appellant submitted a request that OPM include his military service in the calculation of his annuity supplement, but OPM denied that request in February 2011. The appellant requested reconsideration, but in February 2013, OPM affirmed its initial decision.

Under 5 U.S.C. § 8421, a retiree who is entitled to an annuity may be eligible for an annuity supplement prior to age 62. In part, the calculation of that annuity supplement is based on an annuitant's total years of service. 5 U.S.C. § 8421(b)(3)(A). However, the relevant statutory provision specifies that "the term 'service' does not include military service." 5 U.S.C. § 8421(b)(4)(C). OPM's regulations implementing FERS similarly provide that the annuity supplement calculation includes "total creditable service under FERS, excluding military service not performed during an absence of leave without pay from civilian service." 5 C.F.R. § 842.504(a)(1). "Military service" includes honorable active service in the armed forces. 5 U.S.C. § 8401(31). The appellant's military service qualifies under this definition.

The relevant facts of this case are indistinguishable from those in James v. Office of Personnel Management, 83 M.S.P.R. 239 (1999). Mr. James performed honorable active service in the armed forces before working as a civilian under the FERS system. Id., ¶ 5. He appealed to the Board, arguing that, because he made a deposit for his military service, which was considered part of his creditable service in calculating his basic annuity, that military service should also be counted towards his annuity supplement. Id., ¶ 2. The Board rejected Mr. James' argument, finding that an annuity supplement is based in part on the annuitant's "total years of service," but that the term "service" excludes military service. Id., ¶ 6; see 5 U.S.C. § 8421(b)(C)(4). The Board noted that the relevant provisions of the law provide no exception for military service for which a deposit has been paid and that the Board is bound by the language of the statute as it is written. James, 83 M.S.P.R. 239, ¶ 6.

In his initial appeal, and on petition for review, the appellant asserts that his military service should be counted in the calculation of his annuity supplement. However, in making this argument, the appellant has failed to recognize the distinction between the statutory and regulatory provisions for a basic annuity and those for an annuity supplement. Compare 5 U.S.C. § 8411(c)(1)(B), and 5 C.F.R. § 842.306(a)(2) (providing that, for the purposes of a basic annuity, creditable service includes post-1956 military service if an appropriate deposit is made), with 5 U.S.C. § 8421(b)(4)(C), and 5 C.F.R. § 842.504(a)(1) (providing that, for the purposes of an annuity supplement, military service is excluded from the calculation unless it was performed during an absence of leave without pay from civilian service).

Although the record suggests that the appellant submitted a deposit for his post-1956 military service, that deposit does not affect the calculation of his annuity supplement. The law requires that his military service be excluded from that calculation....

Service credit under CSRS for military service is not allowed if the employee is receiving a military retirement based on the same service, according to Jeffrey v. OPM, 28 MSPR 81, 84-85 (1985), involving time spent by the appellant as a midshipman at the Naval Academy:

Judicial interpretations of the relevant pre-1956 statutory language have consistently concluded that Congress only sought to bar double retirement credit for periods of military service. The courts found that Congress only sought to bar civil service retirement credit for periods of military service actually credited toward military retirement. The change in the applicable statutory language made by Congress in 1956 and its legislative history is insufficient to impute to Congress an intent to change the pre-existing judicial interpretations under which OPM admits appellant would be entitled to retirement service credit. Indeed, as appellant has pointed out, what legislative history of the 1956 change does exist indicates that Congress did not intend to change the then existing law with regard to the crediting of military service for retirement purposes. See 1956 U.S. Code Cong. and Ad. News, 3825, 3728.

We, therefore, conclude that the presiding official correctly determined that appellant is entitled to service credit for the period in question. As the courts have stated, Congress intended to prevent dual retirement credit for time spent in the military service and therefore precluded, except under circumstances not relevant here, an individual from receiving retirement service credit for civil service retirement purposes if he or she was receiving a military retirement based upon the same service. Bailey v. U.S., 511 F.2d 540 (Ct. Cl. 1975); Hobson v. U.S., 151 Ct. Cl. 201 (1960); Bond vs. U.S., 135 F. Supp. 433 (Ct. Cl. 1955). There is no indication that Congress sought to preclude civilian retirement service credit for periods of military service which are not the basis of an individual's military retirement annuity. Since 10 U.S.C. § 971 specifically excludes service at a military academy from inclusion in calculating military retirement, it is clear that appellant would not receive double credit for his service as a midshipman.

If an individual is receiving military retirement pay, it appears that service credit is not allowed for the underlying military service unless the military retirement was awarded for a disability incurred in combat "or as a result of an instrumentality of war." In an overpayment case, Dickson v. OPM, 37 MSPR 465, 467 (1988), the Board stated:

In applying for immediate retirement, appellant had indicated on his retirement application that his military-retirement pay was not for "a disability incurred in combat or caused by an instrumentality of war."Id.
