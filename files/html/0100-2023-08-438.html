4. General Characteristics of Tests Rather Than Individual Examination Results

Dow v. OPM, 68 MSPR 285, 288-90 (1995), presented a challenge to the ALJ examination process. At issue were the specific composition of an OPM-established panel created to review and assess the qualifications of ALJ candidates and the procedures established by OPM in deciding the appellant's examination rating appeal. The Board determined that its authority under 5 CFR 300.104(a) does not extend to employment decisions directed toward an individual applicant; nor does that authority extend to procedural aspects of the selection process:

Under 5 CFR 300.104(a), the Board has jurisdiction over the appeal of a "candidate who believes that an employment practice which was applied to him or her by the Office of Personnel Management violates a basic requirement in 5 CFR 300.103." Section 300.101 provides that, "the term 'employment practices' includes the development and use of examinations, qualification standards, tests, and other measurement instruments." While the term "employment practices" has a broad and inclusive meaning, we find nothing that would encompass the particular results of the application of an employment practice or qualification standard to an individual. See Vesser, 29 F.3d at 603; Dowd, 713 F.2d at 723-24. See also Maule v. OPM, 40 MSPR 388, 393 (1989), aff'd, 892 F.2d 1050 (Fed. Cir. 1989) (Table) (procedural decisions are not employment practices; rejection of application as untimely is not employment practice).

We find persuasive OPM's contention that "employment practices" appealable to the Board must encompass matters that are amenable to a general application to a group. We believe that this conclusion is supported by subpart A of 5 CFR part 300 which does not give applicants appeal rights to the Board from examination ratings or the rejection of their applications; it provides for filing such appeals to OPM. See 5 CFR 300.104(b). Before defining employment practices, section 300.101 states: "The purpose of this subpart is to establish principles to govern...the employment practices of the Federal Government...that affect the recruitment, measurement, ranking, and selection of individuals...." Section 300.102 indicates that the subpart is directed to implement the policy that competitive employment practices conform to certain principles, including nondiscrimination, while section 300.103 sets forth the basic requirements, including that the job analysis be based on factors important in evaluating candidates and cover "a single position or group of positions, or an occupation or group of occupations, having common characteristics." Thus, we find that the object of this subpart in defining "employment practices" is to encompass "merit" considerations that are generally applied in making Federal hiring decisions. See Maule, 40 MSPR at 393, citing Dowd, 713 F.2d at 723.

Accordingly, an applicant may appeal a claim that an examination or other merit consideration is generally applied in a discriminatory fashion or is inherently biased against a certain group of individuals, including him or her. Cf. Curtin v. OPM, 846 F.2d 1373, 1377-78 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (court considered claims that portion of ALJ examination was biased in favor of applicants with more varied job experience and that certain applicants were self-limited by their previous positions). Moreover, an applicant may appeal a valid test or other "merit consideration" claiming that it was wrongly applied to him.[*] See Dowd, 713 F.2d at 724. See also Banks v. Dept. of Agric., 59 MSPR 157, 160 (1993) (agency misapplication of a valid OPM requirement also constitutes an employment practice appealable to the Board), aff'd, 26 F.3d 140 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table).

[*]  Accordingly, we disagree with the appellant's contention that the judge has set a per se rule that the Board may never consider an otherwise valid employment practice which is misused or misapplied to a candidate. However, we agree with the administrative judge's finding that an appellant may not indirectly appeal his rating simply by alleging procedural irregularities in the examination process.

The claim dismissed by the administrative judge here does not involve such a claim, however, but is limited to an allegation of bias by a member of the panel towards the appellant. We find that claim insufficient to fall within our jurisdiction. See Banks, 59 MSPR at 160 (bare allegation that agency did not consider candidate's education and experience did not show that he was subjected to an employment practice; non-selection and irregularities in selection process were not appealable matters); Maule, 40 MSPR at 393-94 (OPM's rejection of application as untimely under its regulations concerned a procedural exercise of OPM's administrative discretion and did not involve the "substantive 'development and use of examinations, qualification standards, tests and other measurement instruments'"); cf. Beam v. OPM, 66 MSPR 469, 481 (1995) (to establish jurisdiction over claim that OPM employed unannounced qualification standard based on partisan political affiliation when it re-ranked candidates, candidate would have to show that OPM in fact applied such criterion).

We further agree with the administrative judge's finding that OPM's appeal and review process does not constitute an "employment practice" under section 300.104(a). The appellant argues that a review of the description of the appeal process in the agency announcement of the ALJ examination clearly shows that it is an "integral part[ ] of the examination process." He further claims that the appeal process was applied to him in a manner violating a basic requirement because it denied him due process inasmuch as OPM relied on ex parte evidence in deciding his rating appeal. In its response, OPM argues that its appeal process is, at most, "a review of the individual examination, not the application and use of a general employment practice itself."

Based on our review of the ALJ announcement, we find that the appeal process does not fall within the definition for an "employment practice" as defined by section 300.101 ("includes the development and use of examinations, qualification standards, tests and other measurement instruments"). While describing the appeal process in the announcement for the ALJ examination implements the policy set forth in section 300.102 that competitive employment practices "insure to the candidate opportunity for appeal or administrative review, as appropriate," we find nothing in that process itself that constitutes a "merit consideration" appealable under section 300.104(a); mere procedural decisions are not employment practices. See Maule, 40 MSPR at 393. The appellant's claim that he was denied due process involves procedural matters that are not subject to Board review; the Board's jurisdiction is limited to whether employment practices satisfy the "basic requirements" set forth in section 300.104, which we discussed above. 5 CFR 300.104(a). See Dowd, 713 F.2d at 722; Banks, 59 MSPR at 160. Accordingly, we conclude that the administrative judge correctly dismissed this claim for lack of jurisdiction.
