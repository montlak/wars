a. Modification of Settlement Provision

The general rule, stated in Forston v. Dept. of Navy, 60 MSPR 154, 159 (1993), is that "the Board lacks the authority to unilaterally modify a material term in a settlement agreement." See Rodriguez v. DOJ, 84 MSPR 685, 689 ¶ 7 (2000) (a party was entitled to no more than what was provided by the terms of the agreement); cf. Hamilton v. VA, 92 MSPR 467 ¶ 5 (2002) (Board may not unilaterally modify the terms of settlement agreement); Weslowski v. Dept. of Army, 80 MSPR 585, 589 ¶ 7 (1999) (same as Rodriguez). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Modification, Amendment of Agreement."]

Capps v. USDA, 97 MSPR 381, 388 ¶ 20 (2004), explained the general limitations on enforcement proceedings seeking modification of settlements:

The Board notes that, as relief, the appellant has requested enforcement of certain provisions and modification or removal of other provisions of the settlement agreement. However, when a party to a settlement agreement materially breaches the agreement, the non-breaching party has the option of either enforcing the terms of the agreement or rescinding the settlement agreement in its entirety and going forward with the original appeal. See Pierson v. Department of the Army, 86 M.S.P.R. 203 ¶ 5 (2000); McClain v. U.S. Postal Service, 40 M.S.P.R. 66, 70 (1989). Partial enforcement, modification, and removal of particular terms are not available remedies.

It rarely happens, but the Board may modify the term of an agreement to provide the benefit of the bargain to the injured party. Affirming the its authority to modify the terms of an agreement and fashion a remedy to meet the purpose of the contract, Byron v. Dept. of Navy, 42 MSPR 665, 672 (1989), involving an allegation that an agency breached a reinstatement agreement by causing the appellant to lose the security clearance necessary to hold the position he acquired through reinstatement, explained:

In the exercise of its broad enforcement authority, the Board may modify the terms of the order or agreement for which enforcement is sought where necessary to fashion a remedy for the harm to a party resulting from the other party's noncompliance. See, e.g., Petrecz v. Office of Personnel Management, 33 MSPR 21 (1987) (where for almost two years the agency failed to comply with an order to place the appellant's name on an eligibility list for vacancies in certain positions, the agency was appropriately ordered to give him priority consideration for the same positions for 30 months); Pearson v. Department of the Army, 38 MSPR 255 (1988) (where the agency breached its agreement to retain appellant and assist him to apply for other positions for a period of 240 days, the Board extended the period during which the agency was ordered to comply for another 240 days). In this case, the Board is without authority to order the agency to reinstate appellant's security clearance or to order it to retain him in his position without it. Department of the Navy v. Egan, 484 U.S. 518 (1988). Thus the Board must fashion a remedy for the agency's breach of the settlement agreement if it is found to have caused the appellant to lose his security clearance. In that event, we find that compliance with the agreement will require the agency to place the appellant in another position as equivalent as possible to the firefighter position to which the agency agreed to restore him. If appellant establishes on remand that the agency's violation resulted in his loss of clearance, the administrative judge shall order the agency to cancel the proposal to remove the appellant from his position and to reassign him to a nonsensitive position of the same grade.

An agency's noncompliance made it impossible for the object of the settlement to be fulfilled: the appellant's timely filing of a disability retirement application. The Board directed the agency to fulfill the intent of the settlement agreement by adjusting the date of the employee's resignation to permit the timely filing of the retirement application. Johnson v. FEMA, 41 MSPR 561, 566 (1989); see Martin v. DOJ, 90 MSPR 690, 696 ¶ 8 (2002) (similarly extending the time for the completion of actions under a settlement to avoid the effect of agency noncompliance).

In Lary v. USPS, 472 F.3d 1363 (Fed. Cir. 2006) (reh'g den., Lary v. USPS, 493 F.3d 1355 (Fed. Cir. 2007), on remand, Lary v. USPS, 106 MSPR 597, 2007 MSPB 220 (2007), dismissed as moot, Lary v. USPS, 108 MSPR 145, 2007 MSPB 291 (2007)), the agency failed to provide documentation required under a settlement to permit the appellant to retire. The court essentially rewrote or reformed the settlement agreement to require the agency to redo the separation of the employee in a manner that permitted him to apply for disability retirement with the supporting material that the agency should have provided the first time around. The effect of this approach was to create a somewhat fictitious employment situation that permitted the parties, through settlement, to establish the basis for a retirement benefit that might not otherwise have been available to the employee, an approach not always favored by OPM.

Following the lead of the Federal Circuit in Lary, the Board modified a settlement to achieve a result that was close to what the parties intended in Sanchez v. DHS, 110 MSPR 573, 579 ¶ 12, 2009 MSPB 31 (2009), when the settlement agreement involved reinstatement and a period of LWOP until the appellant would qualify for an early retirement from a law enforcement job and then be separated on the date he could retire when he reached the age of 50. The agency was required to earlier terminate the appellant because of the effect of a statute, 5 USC 7371(b) requiring that a law enforcement officer convicted of a felony be removed from employment as a law enforcement officer. The agency breached the agreement, although it maintained it had no choice but to do so. To remedy the problem, the Board directed the agency to place the employee into a non-law enforcement position to qualify for an early retirement. Flores v. USPS, 115 MSPR 189, 194, 198 ¶¶ 10, 18, 2010 MSPB 215 (2010), noted that although the Board lacked authority to add terms to a settlement or materially alter its terms, there was some flexibility as to the remedy ordered in the form of specific compliance with a settlement provision:

A settlement agreement is a contract, and, as such, it will be enforced in accordance with contract law. Allen v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 112 M.S.P.R. 659, ¶ 7 (2009); see Lutz, 485 F.3d at 1381. In construing the terms of a settlement agreement, the words of the agreement are of paramount importance. Greco v. Department of the Army, 852 F.2d 558, 560 (Fed. Cir. 1988); Galatis v. U.S. Postal Service, 109 M.S.P.R. 651, ¶ 10 (2008). The Board has no authority to unilaterally modify the terms of the parties' settlement agreement, Galatis, 109 M.S.P.R. 651, ¶ 10, or to read a nonexistent term into an agreement that is unambiguous, Hamilton v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 92 M.S.P.R. 467, ¶ 6 (2002)....

...

On remand, the administrative judge shall require the parties to submit further evidence and argument on the issues of whether compliance with the assignment provisions of the settlement agreement would necessarily violate the CBA, or any other law, rule, or regulation, and whether the Board may order specific performance in this matter. The administrative judge will then determine whether specific performance is an available remedy for the appellant under the facts of this case, keeping in mind that a Board order of specific performance need not mirror the performance contemplated by the settlement agreement; rather, it should be drawn so as best to effectuate the purposes for which the contract was made and upon such terms as justice requires. See Sanchez, 110 M.S.P.R. 573, ¶ 8 & n.4 (citing Lary, 493 F.3d at 1357; Lary, 472 F.3d at 1369).
