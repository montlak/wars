4. Receipt of Disability Annuity

Orias v. Dept. of Navy, 11 MSPR 350, 351-52 (1982), determined that if an agency considers separation of an employee due to a medical condition, the late FPM Ch. 339-1-3b required that the employer consider leave if the disability would respond to treatment, reassignment to a position with less rigid requirements, or disability retirement. Orias, reviewed by EEOC, Orias v. Dept. of Navy, EEOC 03820090 (11/8/83), favored the use of reassignment. As to the last option, disability retirement, the Board determined that if an employee had a disability that could be reasonably accommodated by the agency, the employee should not be retired. Appleman v. OPM, 11 MSPR 305, 308 (1982). [Refer to Chapter 14, "Disability Retirement."]

An appellant is not procedurally barred via judicial estoppel from claiming disability discrimination when she receives disability retirement benefits. The Board will consider the effect, if any, of the appellant's disability retirement award upon her disability discrimination claim. Nanette v. Dept. of Treasury, 92 MSPR 127, 132 n.2 (2002) (citing Lamberson v. VA, 80 MSPR 648 ¶ 31 (1999)). The relation between OPM's grant of a disability retirement application and an appellant's assertion that he is a qualified disabled employee was considered in Twine v. DHHS, 36 MSPR 388, 392-93 (1988), involving the removal of an animal caretaker for physical inability to perform the job. The Board determined that the appellant's limitations precluded reassignment. As to the effect of receipt of a disability annuity after his separation:

In this case, there is no plausible reason to believe that the appellant's handicap can be accommodated. The appellant suffers from chronic low back pain and therefore cannot bend, stoop, or lift. Further, the appellant had been found medically unfit to perform the duties of his position. In addition, the Office of Personnel Management granted the appellant's application for disability retirement from his position.

In order to qualify for a disability retirement annuity, the appellant was required to establish that he was unable, because of disease or injury, to render useful and efficient service in his position, and that he was not qualified for reassignment to a vacant position which is in the agency at the same grade or level and in which the employee could render useful and efficient service. See 5 USC 8337(a). Under the circumstances of this case, a finding by the Office of Personnel Management that the appellant met the criteria for a disability retirement annuity diminishes the appellant's claim that he can be reasonably accommodated. Therefore, we find that the administrative judge correctly determined that the appellant is not a qualified handicapped employee.

A similar claim was resolved in a similar manner in West v. USPS, 44 MSPR 551, 559 (1990). As to an employee whose agency-initiated disability retirement application was granted and who appealed the retirement, the Board considered, but rejected, evidence that the agency could have attempted to follow a psychiatrist's advice concerning treatment of an employee who was suffering from a paranoid personality disorder. The medical treatment was not widely endorsed; it was not certain to succeed; there was no evidence that the agency was made aware of the treatment until the time of the hearing; and the treatment could have caused the agency to disregard its own internal procedures for handling grievances and complaints that the employee might have filed. Brunda v. Dept. of Navy and OPM, 28 MSPR 148, 151-152 (1985).

Lorenz v. USPS, 84 MSPR 670, 673-676 ¶¶ 5-12 (2000), determined that if the employee's condition could have been accommodated so that he could have continued working, then his retirement could be involuntary. The agency's failure to accommodate, standing alone, did not make the employee's decision to retire involuntary. The employee can show that his disability retirement was a constructive removal by proving that he indicated to the agency that he wished to continue working but that he had medical limitations requiring a modification of his working conditions or duties--that is, accommodation--and that there was a reasonable accommodation available that the agency unjustifiably failed to offer him. An employee will not prevail in his appeal if he applies for disability retirement without requesting altered duties or working conditions, or without informing the agency that he would like to continue working if possible, but who later claims in litigation that his disability retirement constituted a constructive removal because the agency failed to accommodate his condition. In evaluating a claim of a retirement forced by lack of accommodation for a disabled employee, the Board considers the agency's efforts following notice by the employee to the agency that accommodation is sought.

Under Lorenz, if accommodation was impossible, the employee's disability retirement was not a constructive removal. Other theories could not lead to a different conclusion, for in claims of coercion, duress, and intolerable working conditions, the issue is whether the employee had a choice between retiring or continuing to work but was forced to choose retirement by improper acts of the agency. An employee who is unable to work because of a medical condition that cannot be accommodated does not have a choice.

Under Nordhoff v. Dept. of Navy, 64 MSPR 478, 486 (1994), if the Board concludes that the appellant's retirement was the result of disability discrimination, it orders the agency to cancel the appellant's separation by disability retirement. Cancelation results in a cessation of annuity payments. The disability retirement benefits the appellant received would be considered by the OPM as an overpayment subject to waiver when the individual is without fault and when recovery would be against equity and good conscience. 5 USC 8346(b); 5 CFR 831.1401-.1407 (2022). Back pay for any or all of the period following the appellant's separation would be governed by the Back Pay Act, 5 USC 5596, and 5 CFR 550.805(c)(1) (2022), providing that no award of back pay can be made for "any period during which an employee was not ready, willing, and able to perform his or her duties because of an incapacitating illness or injury." If the Board finds that the agency committed disability discrimination by failing to accommodate a disabled employee, the appellant must have been ready, willing, and able to work either in his former position, with modifications, or in another position he could have held through reassignment. If the appellant could not perform any work following the time of his retirement, or at the time of his appeal, he would not be entitled to back pay for that period, a factor that the appellant would need to take into account in determining whether to appeal his separation from federal service.

On a related issue, the relation between a disability discrimination claim and receipt of Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) benefits, defined to preclude any kind of substantial gainful work, the Supreme Court observed in Cleveland v. Policy Management Systems Corp., 119 S. Ct. 1597, 1600 (1999), that the SSDI recipient is not barred from asserting a disability discrimination claim, but must meet some preliminary requirements:

We believe that, in context, these two seemingly divergent statutory contentions are often consistent, each with the other. Thus pursuit, and receipt, of SSDI benefits does not automatically estop the recipient from pursuing an ADA claim. Nor does the law erect a strong presumption against the recipient's success under the ADA. Nonetheless, an ADA plaintiff cannot simply ignore her SSDI contention that she was too disabled to work. To survive a defendant's motion for summary judgment, she must explain why that SSDI contention is consistent with her ADA claim that she could "perform the essential functions" of her previous job, at least with "reasonable accommodation."
