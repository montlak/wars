5. Conduct of Questioning

AJs are vested with latitude to conduct the hearing, to arrange the order of examination and cross-examination of witnesses, and to determine the subject matter that may be inquired into during various parts of the hearing. In Franks v. Dept. of Army, 14 MSPR 387, 389 (1983), the AJ split questioning into two parts: one concerning jurisdiction, the other concerning the merits of a demotion. The agency representative, not an attorney, apparently did not fully understand the battle plan and did not develop evidence on the merits. On an agency PFR, the Board determined that the hearing was fairly conducted. The agency's failure to develop evidence was not attributable to the AJ. Without a showing that inaccurate or incorrect evidence resulted, there was no harmful error demonstrated by an appellant's contention that the AJ failed to conduct a fair hearing because he permitted witnesses to signal each other and permitted interruption of counsel during cross-examination. The appellant did not object to the conduct at the hearing, and the appellant failed to establish that the conduct occurred or that the AJ was aware of it. Preny v. IRS, 24 MSPR 669, 671 (1984); see Yoshida v. Dept. of Navy, 14 MSPR 300 (1983) (directions by the AJ to a witness to answer questions or rulings by the AJ on objections did not reflect interruptions so excessive as to decrease the effectiveness of cross-examination by the appellant's representative). AJs may ask questions at hearings. See Kent v. SSA, DE-0752-16-0391-I-1 (NP 10/27/2022) ("And, although the appellant asserts that the administrative judge did not ask him questions at the hearing, an administrative judge has the option of asking a witness questions, but he is not required to do so.").

A minor variant of the sequestration rule is a practice, honored upon counsel's request by most AJs, of asking a witness to stand out in the hall during argument over an objection to a material portion of the testimony of that witness. The objection may be that the testimony is irrelevant. The party offering the witness may be asked to make an offer of proof. If the offer is made while the witness is listening to the offer, the witness has just been told what her testimony should be. To avoid the problem, counsel making the objection and suggesting the need for an offer of proof should also suggest the desirability of exclusion of the witness during discussion of the objection and offer of proof. The Board obliquely deflected an assertion of witness coaching, explaining that counsel and technical representatives have some freedom in discussions with witnesses during hearings, Low v. Dept. of Energy, DA-0752-14-0086-I-1 (NP 9/24/2014):

The appellant objects to this testimony, alleging that it was prompted by a person apparently acting as an agency technical advisor during the course of the hearing. The Board has long recognized the appropriateness of the presence of a technical advisor during the course of a hearing. See Day v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 50 M.S.P.R. 680, 682-83 (1991), aff'd, 975 F.2d 870 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table). Moreover, the Board has found no error in a technical advisor consulting with agency counsel and witnesses during the hearing. See Jeffers v. Veterans Administration, 40 M.S.P.R. 567, 571-72, aff'd, 892 F.2d 1050 (Fed. Cir. 1989) (Table). Even assuming that the administrative judge erred in failing to disallow such consultation, the appellant has not shown how her rights were prejudiced thereby. The appellant has not identified any evidence that she was precluded from presenting due to the alleged actions of the technical advisor. Therefore, she has shown no basis upon which the Board should disallow the testimony of the witness from being considered. See Karapinka v. Department of Energy, 6 M.S.P.R. 124, 127 (1981) (the administrative judge's procedural error is of no legal consequence unless it is shown that it has adversely affected a party's substantive rights).

An AJ properly limited repetitious questions, Dinkins v. USPS, 5 MSPR 165, 167, 5 MSPB 222 (1981): "We find no procedural error in the presiding official's limiting appellant from repeatedly asking a question of a witness that had been answered to the best ability of the witness. The presiding official has an obligation to avoid delay and disruption in the conduct and disposition of proceedings."

The Board reversed and remanded an appeal when the AJ made a substantial number of erroneous evidentiary rulings concerning the examination of witnesses and the introduction and exclusion of evidence. The AJ allowed the agency representative to ask leading questions, allowed critical agency witnesses to testify from previously prepared statements without any indication that they could not recollect events, and chastised appellant's counsel for raising objections that were properly made. The AJ severely curtailed appellant's substantive right to a fair and impartial hearing. Lockner v. Dept. of Transp., 14 MSPR 352, 354 (1983). But errors concerning admission of evidence do not generally invalidate a hearing. Even if the Board improperly admits evidence of a privileged conversation, the Board will be sustained if other evidence provides substantial support for its decision. Ortiz v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 87-3158).
