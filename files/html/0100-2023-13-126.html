a. Action Following Termination of Criminal Proceeding

The critical point for agency action in terminating the indefinite suspension and deciding what next to do is at the point of the disposition of the criminal charges by the court. The entry by the appellant into a plea bargain with a prosecutor does not terminate the indefinite suspension. The indictment is not effectively resolved until the date the sentencing court enters its order concerning the criminal charges. See Campbell v. DLA, 31 MSPR 691, 694 (1986). If an indefinitely suspended employee appeals and loses but is subsequently acquitted, the Board's final order implicitly requires reinstatement of the employee upon acquittal, although the agency may attempt to remove the employee if it can prove the evidence of misconduct. Drake v. VA, 26 MSPR 34, 36 n.1 (1985). Because the agency may still prove the misconduct by a preponderance of evidence, the employee is not entitled to automatic reinstatement following acquittal or dismissal of the indictment. See Wiemers v. DOJ, 29 MSPR 9, 10 n.* (1985), aff'd, Wiemers v. MSPB, 792 F.2d 1113 (Fed. Cir. 1986); cf. Payne v. USPS, 69 MSPR 503, 508 (1996) (when the employee is removed because of a criminal conviction and that conviction is later reversed, the employee receives full back pay following a successful petition to reopen his appeal).

Although an indefinite suspension is not void because of dismissal of criminal proceedings following, for example, a hung jury and a city attorney's decision not to further prosecute, the agency must then move expeditiously to terminate the suspension after the outcome of the criminal proceeding. The agency's failure to do so provides the basis for an enforcement petition (assuming a preexisting appeal). West v. DOJ, 26 MSPR 576, 578 (1985); cf. Hofmann v. USDA, 31 MSPR 399, 401 (1986) (appeal filed to contest an indefinite suspension was dismissed with prejudice following withdrawal of the appeal; a petition for enforcement was subsequently filed to challenge an ongoing indefinite suspension treated as a new appeal; the time limit for an appeal was waived). [Refer to earlier discussion of Campbell in "Indefinite Suspension."]

Although most appeals involving an indefinite suspension concern the continuation of the suspension or back pay entitlement following termination of criminal proceedings, some collateral problems may arise once the suspension is terminated. In Newbold v. Dept. of Treasury, 58 MSPR 532, 536-37 (1993), the appellant asked the Board to direct the agency to vacate the suspension following his acquittal; additionally, the appellant protested his placement on administrative leave pending a security clearance investigation following termination of the suspension. The Board declined to involve itself in the security clearance problem, and it determined that the agency was not required to vacate an otherwise proper suspension:

The appellant also requests that the Board order the agency to "cancel and vacate" the indefinite suspension as of January 20, 1993. In support of his argument, he cites Hofmann v. Department of Agriculture, 42 MSPR 453 (1989). We note, however, that although the Board in Hofmann cancelled the appellant's indefinite suspension when his indictment was dismissed, the Board did not order the agency to vacate it. See Hofmann, 42 MSPR at 458-59. The Board has held that, in cases where an indefinite suspension based on an indictment was proper when effected, the indefinite suspension will remain proper as long as that indictment remains outstanding. See Campbell v. Defense Logistics Agency, 31 MSPR 691, 694 (1986), aff'd, 833 F.2d 1024 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (Table). Further, in Shaffer v. Defense Logistics Agency, 35 MSPR 664, 667-68 (1987), aff'd, 862 F.2d 321 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (Table), the Board followed the holding of the Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit that an employee is not entitled to back pay for any period of an indefinite suspension based on an indictment, while the indictment remains viable and regardless of the outcome of the criminal charges, if the indictment was proper when effected. See Wiemers, 792 F.2d at 1116. Therefore, we see no basis for vacating the agency's action or awarding the appellant back pay for any time that the indictment was outstanding.

The appellant also argues that the agency has improperly placed him in an administrative leave status pending its security clearance investigation, and that its failure to return him to active duty constitutes reprisal. Nonetheless, since the agency has returned the appellant to a pay status, if not active duty, it has terminated the suspension, and has complied with the first initial decision in this appeal. See 5 USC 7511(a)(2), 7501(2) (suspension means "the placing of an employee...in a temporary status without...pay"). In any event, the Board lacks the authority to review the merits of an agency's decision regarding an employee's security clearance, and has taken a limited view of status quo ante remedies where security clearances are involved. See Hill v. Department of the Air Force, 49 MSPR 271, 275 (1991); Jones v. Department of the Navy, 48 MSPR 680, 689-90 (1991), aff'd, 978 F.2d 1223 (Fed. Cir. 1992). In this case, it is undisputed that the appellant's position requires a security clearance, and given both the nature of his position and the relevance of the circumstances surrounding the criminal charges to it, we conclude that his administrative leave status does not constitute noncompliance. Furthermore, although the Board will generally resolve a timely allegation of reprisal in a compliance proceeding, we are precluded from reviewing discrimination or reprisal allegations that are intertwined with an agency's denial of a security clearance. See Pangarova v. Department of the Army, 42 MSPR 319, 324 (1989). Therefore, we have no authority to inquire further into the agency's decision to place the appellant on administrative leave, and the administrative judge's failure to address his reprisal allegation regarding the agency's decision does not provide a basis for reversal of the initial decision. Panter v. Department of the Air Force, 22 MSPR 281, 282 (1984).
