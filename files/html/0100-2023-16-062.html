2. Opportunity Period Requirements

5 USC 4302(c)(6) [formerly 4302(b)(6)] requires each performance appraisal system to provide for action against an unacceptable performer after the employee has an opportunity to demonstrate acceptable performance. Agencies are to assist employees whose performance is unacceptable. Agency compliance with the statutory requirement is an element of proof to be met by the agency under the substantial evidence standard in all actions under 5 USC 4303. The requirement is a condition precedent to an unacceptable performance action; the absence of an opportunity to demonstrate acceptable performance is not merely a procedural error, Sandland v. GSA, 23 MSPR 583, 588-90 (1984):

[A]n employee has a substantive right to an improvement period prior to the institution of a performance based action. In contrast, an employee's performance during the requisite notice period need not be considered in reaching the ultimate conclusion of whether an action is appropriate. In determining Congressional intent with regard to Section 4302(b)(6), it is also significant that the House version of S. 2640 originally included an opportunity to improve under Section 4303 and stated that the opportunity to improve was to be during the notice period. S. 2640, 95th Cong., 2d Sess. 42 (1978), reprinted in Legislative History of the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978, Vol. I at 1166 (1979). The version of S. 2640 as passed in the Senate, and adopted in its present form, recognized the special role of an opportunity to demonstrate acceptable performance as a right which must be afforded prior to the proposal of any action and which is separate from the procedural guarantees of Section 4303.

In support of its contention that the presiding official's harmful error analysis was inappropriate, the agency conceded that the requirement of a reasonable opportunity to improve is not a procedural matter. At 5 CFR Section 432.204, OPM identifies the statutory procedural requirements to which an employee is entitled. Title 5 USC Section 4302(b)(6) is not among the identified provisions. As indicated above, however, OPM recognizes in its regulations at 5 CFR Section 432.201 that 5 USC Section 4302(b)(6) is a principal statutory requirement. The fact that an agency must comply with 5 USC Section 4302(b)(6) prior to proposing a reduction in grade or removal for unacceptable performance supports the finding that the requirement is not a procedure to be followed in taking the action; rather it is a substantive condition precedent. See 5 CFR Section 432.203(b).

The fundamental substantive importance of Section 4302(b)(6) is further testified to by Merit Systems Principle Six which states that "[e]mployees should be retained on the adequacy of their performance, inadequate performance should be corrected, and employees should be separated who cannot or will not improve their performance to meet required standards." 5 USC Section 2301(b)(6). The legislative history of Merit Systems Principle Six indicates that:

Employees are to be retained on the basis of their performance and those who cannot or do not meet the required standards should be separated. Training is to be provided to employees to improve organizational and individual performance.

S. Rep. No. 969, 95th Cong., 2d Sess. 19 (1978), U.S. Code Cong. & Admin. News 1978, 2741.

Employees whose performance is inadequate should be encouraged to improve, and employees who can not and will not improve their performance to meet established standards should be separated from the Federal service.

H.R. Rep. No. 1403, 95th Cong., 2d Sess. 16 (1978).

Congress thus intended that agencies provide employees whose performance is unacceptable with encouragement and afford them training and assistance in order to achieve improved performance.

Based on the statute, the implementing regulations and the legislative history, we find that an employee's right to a reasonable opportunity to improve is a substantive right; indeed, it is one of the most important rights, benefitting both the employee and the agency, in the entire Chapter 43 appraisal scenario.

Under 5 USC Section 7701(c)(1)(A), in Chapter 43 actions the burden is on the agency to support its "decision" by substantial evidence. The agency's decision necessarily embraces the proper invocation of the substantive "principal statutory requirement" identified by OPM at 5 CFR Section 432.101...as well as the specific application of that regulation to the individual employee. The agency's evidentiary burden, therefore, includes proof that the safeguard provided by 5 USC Section 4302(b)(6), the only substantive right identified by OPM as a principal statutory requirement, and a necessary prerequisite for all actions brought under Chapter 43, was properly afforded.

The agency may establish a prima facie case by documentary or testimonial evidence to the effect that appellant was offered a reasonable opportunity to improve. Absent a challenge from the appellant, such a minimal initial showing would suffice to meet the agency's burden. In the face of a non-frivolous challenge, the burden rests with the agency to show full compliance with the requirement of Section 4302(b)(6) by substantial evidence.
