2. Witness Exclusion

The Board tacitly encourages abbreviated hearings. The easiest way to shorten a hearing is to exclude witnesses. The Board tends to affirm witness exclusion when an AJ's rulings are challenged on appeal. The Board may affirm the ruling by stating that the excluded evidence was immaterial and, therefore, its exclusion was harmless. Alternatively, the Board may say that the proffer of the evidence was insufficient. Both of these methods were employed in Smith v. Dept. of Air Force, 34 MSPR 373, 376 (1987), affirming dismissal of an employee for AWOL and threatening his supervisor. The appellant sought to call a witness who would have said that the deciding official also made a threat to a subordinate that he would "blow his head off" if he "got into his refrigerator again to get a cold drink." But the evidence was not properly proffered because the appellant did not suggest whether the witness could establish that the statement was perceived as a threat by the subordinate or whether the statement could otherwise be perceived as a threat under the legal tests defining threats that have been established by the Federal Circuit. The evidence was immaterial because it would not have affected the outcome of the appeal: the appellant had a prior disciplinary record and was charged with AWOL and making multiple threats against his supervisor. See Brakefield v. Dept. of Commerce, SF-0752-09-0903-I-3 (NP 10/21/2011) ("While the appellant did file a timely objection to the administrative judge's ruling on witnesses, her objection below did not name the witnesses in question, nor explain why their testimony would be relevant or why the administrative judge erred in denying them."); Tyler v. Dept. of Army, DC-0752-10-0401-I-1 (NP 1/5/2011) ("'[t]o obtain reversal of an initial decision on the ground that the [administrative judge] abused his discretion in excluding evidence, the petitioning party must show on review that relevant evidence, which could have affected the outcome, was disallowed.' Jezouit v. Office of Personnel Management, 97 M.S.P.R. 48, ¶ 12 (2004), aff'd, 121 F. App'x 865 (Fed. Cir. 2005). Even if the administrative judge abuses her discretion in excluding an appellant's witness, the appellant will only prevail where the exclusion prejudiced his substantive rights. See Lamb v. U.S. Postal Service, 46 M.S.P.R. 470, 477 (1990); Christensen v. Department of Health and Human Services, 11 M.S.P.R. 209, 211 (1982) (reversible error not shown where the petition for review does not state how the absence of witnesses impaired the proceedings or the appellant's rights)."); Butler v. Dept. of Air Force, 73 MSPR 313, 317 (1997) ("[T]he appellant has not summarized the proffered testimonies of these witnesses and has not shown that their testimonies would be relevant to the issues raised in the appeal and would be nonrepetitive."); Brakefield v. Dept. of Commerce, SF-0752-09-0903-I-3 (NP 10/21/2011) ("While the appellant did file a timely objection to the administrative judge's ruling on witnesses, her objection below did not name the witnesses in question, nor explain why their testimony would be relevant or why the administrative judge erred in denying them.").

As to a witness who is requested in a prehearing submission and disapproved, if the proffer was originally made in general terms and the witness was not approved, nothing prevents another, more detailed proffer, coupled with a request for reconsideration before or during the hearing. See Lee v. EPA, 115 MSPR 533, 543-44 ¶ 15, 2010 MSPB 240 (2010) (objections to an AJ's ruling excluding witnesses was accompanied by an offer of proof as to their testimony). An AJ may disapprove a witness as to whom no interview was conducted or whose testimony's relevance is not apparent. See Jordan v. Dept. of Interior, DC-1221-14-0224-W-1 (NP 4/7/2016) ("We find no abuse of discretion in the administrative judge's denial of the appellant's witness based on his failure to discuss her anticipated testimony in advance. Without talking to the witness, the appellant could not confirm the accuracy of his proffers. See Franco v. U.S. Postal Service, 27 M.S.P.R. 322, 325 (1985) (finding that an administrative judge has wide discretion to exclude witnesses where it has not been shown that their testimony would be relevant, material, and nonrepetitious).").

Witnesses may be precluded as a sanction for violation of a Board regulation or order. Brunner v. DHS, 116 MSPR 679, 2011 MSPB 79 (NP 8/31/2011), affirmed the AJ's preclusion of an important agency witness when the agency failed to list the witness in initial disclosures then required by prior Board regulations:

Here, the administrative judge denied the agency's request for Kreuziger's testimony because the agency did not identify Kreuziger in its initial disclosures. We find no abuse of discretion in the imposition of this sanction because the agency's omission of the polygraph examiner from its initial disclosure reflected, at best, a lack of due diligence. Specifically, given the agency's reliance on the polygraph to support its action against the appellant, its oversight in identifying the examiner in its initial disclosure could, and apparently did, mislead appellant's counsel into believing that the agency was not going to rely on him as a witness. We also reject the notion that the agency complied with its disclosure obligation because the examiner's name appeared in the report of investigation which it submitted to the appellant as part of the agency file.
