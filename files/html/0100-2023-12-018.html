C. CREDIBILITY FINDINGS

The outcome of many appeals depends on resolution of factual disputes. When issues of fact are resolved on the basis of credibility determinations, the Board expects its AJs to analyze evidence on more than guesswork. The Board requires explicit credibility findings. The Board may decline to defer to an AJ's credibility findings that are abbreviated, based on improper considerations, or unsupported by the record. See Redschlag v. Dept. of Army, 89 MSPR 589, 602 ¶ 13 (2001). Well-reasoned credibility determinations are particularly important as to issues, e.g., whistleblowing reprisal, that require the Board to determine the intent and motivation of agency officials. Schmittling v. Dept. of Army, 92 MSPR 572, 585 ¶ 31 (2002). Unless there are unusual circumstances, credibility determinations involving conflicting testimony on material issues should be based on the AJ's personal observation of the witnesses' demeanor while testifying at the hearing. Callery v. DOJ, 35 MSPR 233, 235 (1987). Finizie v. VA, ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2021 NP), noted:

The AJ also noted that Kocher's demeanor during her own testimony and during Simon's testimony hurt her credibility. For example, the AJ observed that Kocher was "antagonistic," aggressively "chomping on gum," and "glowering" at Simon.

...

With respect to Petitioners' argument that it was "inappropriate, unprofessional, and perhaps defamatory" for the AJ to take into account Kocher's demeanor in making its credibility determinations, we disagree. It is appropriate for an AJ to take into account demeanor when assessing credibility. See, e.g., Purifoy v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, 838 F.3d 1367, 1373 (Fed. Cir. 2016). Indeed, we have explained that credibility determinations based upon demeanor observations are entitled to special deference. See id.

Passage of time does not appear to diminish Board reliance on the AJ's credibility determinations, Daquino v. VA, DE-1221-12-0487-W-2, n.14 (NP 2/27/2023) (Member Leavitt dissenting):

The appellant argues on review that the administrative judge's ability to make credibility determinations was impaired due to the passage of more than 2 years between the hearing dates and the issuance of the initial decision. We see no reason to discount the administrative judge's credibility determinations on that basis alone. The administrative judge may have made his credibility determinations during or shortly after the hearing. Even if he did not do so, the administrative judge is still in a better position to make those determinations than the Board members, who did not have the opportunity to observe the witnesses....

Although an AJ assesses credibility through observation of witnesses during the hearing, the AJ should not rely on her observation of the appellant's attitude and behavior at the hearing as circumstantial evidence that she engaged in the charged misconduct. Cheng v. USDA, 84 MSPR 144, 146 ¶ 4 (1999).

Evaluation of the testimony of medical experts is not ordinarily to be based on the credibility of the physicians. The evidence is analyzed and weighed to determine if the opinion was based on a medical examination and if the opinion provided a reasoned explanation for its findings as distinct from conclusory assertions. The AJ should also consider the qualifications of the expert rendering the opinion and the extent and duration of the expert's familiarity with the treatment of the appellant. See Lassiter v. DOJ, 60 MSPR 138, 142 (1993). But see Stevens v. Dept. of Air Force, 81 Fed. Appx. 342 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP) (referring to the Board's evaluation of physician's report in terms of the review of a credibility finding); Paetow v. VA, 118 MSPR 462, 2012 MSPB 98 (2012) (treating conflicting medical evaluations and a credibility analysis).
