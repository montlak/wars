10. Split Samples; Private Testing

A negotiable proposal in NFFE Local 15 and Dept. of Army, Army Armament, Munitions and Chem. Command, Rock Island, 30 FLRA 1046, 1067-69 (1988), permitted employees to retain portions of samples to obtain independent lab analysis. The proposal also required management to reimburse the employee for the costs incurred in independent testing if the independent testing refuted the employer's test results. The proposal did not violate law or regulations; instead it provided additional procedures as a check on the accuracy of the agency's results. The proposal did not conflict with management's means for accomplishing its work. FLRA's conclusion in NFFE Local 15, was reaffirmed on remand (requested by FLRA in the D.C. Circuit) in 33 FLRA 436, 451-52 (1988); see NFFE Local 2058 and Army Aberdeen Proving Ground, 31 FLRA 241, 249-52 (1988); NFFE Local 178 and Army Aberdeen Proving Ground, 31 FLRA 226, 235-37 (1988); NFFE Local 1437 and Army Armament Research, Dev. and Eng'r Ctr., Dover, 31 FLRA 101, 109, 111-13 (1988). The remand decision in NFFE Local 15 and Dept. of Army, Army Armament, Munitions and Chem. Command, Rock Island, 33 FLRA 436, 451-53 (1988), determined that a proposal was negotiable under the final Guidelines that employees retain a portion of their own urine samples. But under those final drug testing guidelines, issued April 11, 1988, initial and confirmatory screenings must occur at the same location. For that reason, proposals relating to procedural protections during field testing--requiring that "[u]pon a positive urinalysis test, each sample will be divided with half being sent to a remote laboratory"--became moot. NFFE Local 1437 and Army Armament, Research, Dev. and Eng'g Ctr., 33 FLRA 493, 496 (1988), on remand from 31 FLRA 101; see NFFE Local 178 and Army Aberdeen Proving Ground, 33 FLRA 521, 528 (1988), and NFFE Local 2058 and Army Aberdeen Proving Ground, 33 FLRA 702, 704 (1988) (same proposal moot on remand and reconsideration).

In a result that was not sustained on judicial review, FLRA confirmed negotiability of a procedure under the final Guidelines that a sample be divided so that the employee could retain his or her portion and be given instructions for proper storage. The employee's portion would be tested in the event of a confirmed positive test result on the agency's sample. NFFE Local 2058 and Army Aberdeen Proving Ground, 33 FLRA 702, 708 (1988) (noting that procedure did not reference field testing procedure). The D.C. Circuit reviewed the split sample proposal in Dept. of Army, Aberdeen Proving Ground v. FLRA, 890 F.2d 467, 471-73 (D.C. Cir. 1989). The court considered whether the proposal was inconsistent with the Executive Order, considered a government-wide regulation, and if not inconsistent, whether the proposal violated management rights. In dispute was whether the review conducted by a medical review officer ("MRO") would be final, or whether the employee could submit to a supervisor results of analysis of the portion of the sample retained by the employee. The court first determined that under DHHS guidelines concerning the drug-testing program, the interpretation of the medical officer was to be conclusive. Allowing a nonmedical personnel supervisor to refute the result reached by the MRO was considered by the court to be inconsistent with the DHHS Guidelines. Allowing the employee to provide supplementary evidence, e.g., new or split samples, to facilitate the supervisor's personnel decision, undercut the MRO's authority in a manner inconsistent with government-wide regulations. The proposal concerning split samples was nonnegotiable. Contrary conclusions by FLRA in NFFE, Local 15 and Dept. of Army, Army Armament, Munitions and Chem. Command, Rock Island, 30 FLRA 1046 (1988), and NFFE Local 2058 and Army Aberdeen Proving Ground, 31 FLRA 241 (1988), on initial remand from the court, 33 FLRA 436 (1988), and 33 FLRA 702 (1988), were set aside. See AFGE, Dept. of Educ. Council of AFGE Locals and Dept. of Educ., 38 FLRA 1068, 1092, 1099-103 (1990), enforcement denied as to other proposals, Dept. of Interior, Minerals Mgmt. Serv. v. FLRA, 969 F.2d 1158, 1160-61 (D.C. Cir. 1992) (exploring, but not deciding, various aspects of Proposal 5, concerning retesting); NFFE Local 15 and Army Armament, Munitions and Chem. Command, Rock Island, 35 FLRA 936, 938-39 (1990) (Proposal 9); NFFE Local 2058 and Army Aberdeen Proving Ground, Installation Support Activity, 35 FLRA 926, 928 (1990) (remand decision dismissing the negotiability petition following a court decision).

FLRA added in AFGE Local 446 and Dept. of Interior, Nat'l Park Serv., Blue Ridge Parkway, 43 FLRA 836, 873-74 (1991), involving a provision (Proposal 4J) that required a grant of administrative leave for employees to pursue private testing:

Having determined that the proposal would allow for the grant of administrative leave for the purpose of allowing employees to take a private drug test, we further find that we are unable to determine the negotiability of the proposal.... Before explaining that outcome, however, it is necessary to address certain contentions made by the Agency and our precedent involving similar proposals.

The Agency asserts that private testing is not contemplated by either the Executive Order or the Guidelines and, consequently, that the granting of administrative leave is not a condition of employment over which the Agency has an obligation to bargain. The Agency's premise concerning private testing cannot be sustained. There is no absolute prohibition on private testing in the cited authorities. Rather, the availability of split sample or private sample testing is contemplated by the Guidelines. In the comments to the Guidelines, HHS specifically rejected only the presentation "to the Medical Review Officer a split sample or private sample that does not fully comply with these Guidelines." 53 Fed. Reg. at 11971. Therefore, the use of a private sample, under certain circumstances, does not conflict with the Guidelines, as long as the sample otherwise comports with the requirements of the Guidelines. See National Federation of Federal Employees, Local 1384 and U.S. Department of the Air Force, 3245th Air Base Group, Hanscom Air Force Base, Massachusetts, 41 FLRA 195 (1991). In such circumstances, a grant of leave might be negotiable.

In this case, an examination of the circumstances under which the private sample test results can be used leads to our finding that we are unable to determine the proposal's negotiability. As we stated in connection with Proposal 4I, which involved testing of a split sample, information pertaining to who will receive test results and whether those results will be used by someone other than the MRO to assess the medical significance of results is indispensable to determining whether the proposal is negotiable. For example, in National Park Service, 41 FLRA at 1183-86, we found a proposal that would have authorized up to 4 hours of official time for the purpose of allowing employees to take a private test was outside the duty to bargain because, as explained by the union, the purpose of the private test was to provide information to a supervisor to be used in refuting a positive test result. As such a purpose was inconsistent with the Guidelines, the proposal was deemed to be outside the duty to bargain. In comparison, in Bureau of Reclamation, 39 FLRA at 1512-14, we found negotiable a proposal authorizing up to 4 hours of administrative leave to take a drug test, because the record established that the drug test referred to was the drug test required by the agency.

The Union has failed to provide the information necessary to make a negotiability determination and the record is devoid of any evidence on which such a ruling can be made. Consequently, we will dismiss the petition for review as to this proposal.
