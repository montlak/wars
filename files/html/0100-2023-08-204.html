3. Position Change

OPM defines reassignment, 5 CFR 210.102 (2022):

(12) Reassignment means a change of an employee, while serving continuously within the same agency, from one position to another without promotion or demotion.

The assignment and reassignment of an employee is within management's discretion to allocate work and, unless there is a reduction in grade or pay, a reassignment of positions or duties is not an action appealable to the Board. See Clark v. MSPB, 361 F.3d 647, 650 (Fed. Cir. 2004) (noting the prior holding of Federal Circuit in a nonprecedential case that no appeal lies from a conversion from a nonappropriated to an appropriated fund position if there is no loss in pay); Thomas v. MSPB, 98 Fed. Appx. 848, 850 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP) ("Lateral reassignment is not an adverse action under the board's statutory jurisdiction if it does not result in a loss of grade or pay."); Fargo v. SSA, 84 Fed. Appx. 73, 74-76 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP) (reassignment is not within the Board's jurisdiction despite alleged violations of merit system principles; the court rejected a balancing approach that would provide equitable jurisdiction over reassignments that are challenged as abusive; the court also rejected the argument that jurisdiction should attach because the reassignment might entail loss of future promotional opportunities, citing McEnery v. MSPB, 963 F.2d 1512 (Fed. Cir. 1992)); Roche v. USPS, 80 F.3d 468, 470-71 (1996) ("Reassignment of a preference eligible postal employee to a position at the same pay and grade is not appealable to the Board. Wilson v. Merit Sys. Protection Bd., 807 F.2d 1577, 1579 (Fed. Cir. 1986)."); Dreher v. USPS, 711 F.2d 907, 909 (9th Cir. 1983) (reassignment/shift change: "Although the statute does not expressly state that the § 7512 list is exhaustive, similar language has been consistently construed to preclude relief for actions not so specified.... Section 7512 appears to us to represent a legislative determination that certain actions are serious enough to require procedural safeguards, but that applying those safeguards to every personnel decision would unduly hamper agency administration.... We see no reason to interpret 'adverse action' more broadly than § 7512 already does."); Melton v. SSA, PH-3443-21-0369-I-1 (NP 10/14/2022) ("The Board lacks jurisdiction over reassignments that do not constitute a reduction in grade or pay, even if a reassignment reduces the employee's status, duties, or responsibilities. Artmann v. Department of the Interior, 926 F.2d 1120, 1122 (Fed. Cir. 1991). A reduction in pay must be ascertainable at the time of the personnel action without reference to anticipated future developments. McEnery v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 963 F.2d 1512, 1514-15 (Fed. Cir. 1992)."); McGaugh v. DOJ, DA-3443-11-0672-I-1 (NP 8/27/2012) ("Moreover, even assuming arguendo that the appellant has been reassigned, the Board has similarly held that a reassignment action that does not result in a reduction in pay or grade, even if the appellant's duties were diminished, is not appealable to the Board. Loggins, 112 M.S.P.R. 471, ¶ 10."); Weinstein v. OPM, 50 MSPR 92, 94-95 (1991) (because the Board lacked jurisdiction over a reassignment that did not involve change either in grade or pay, the AJ lacked authority to order the agency to amend records pertaining to a reassignment); Bruning v. VA, 32 MSPR 213, 216 (1987) (the Board lacks jurisdiction of an employee's allegation of an improperly motivated reassignment). But see Mims v. DOD, 71 MSPR 74, 79 (1996) ("An involuntary reassignment that also constitutes a reduction in grade is appealable as an adverse action."); Sarver v. Dept. of Treasury, 20 MSPR 499, 501 (1984) (assignment and reassignment of an employee's duties is within management's discretion to allocate work; however, the Board has jurisdiction to determine whether the agency has circumvented an order to reinstate an employee); Charles v. Dept. of Navy, 3 MSPR 113, 114 (1980) ("Under 5 U.S.C. 7512, a reassignment not involving a reduction in grade or pay is not an appealable action. Nor is there any regulation making such reassignment appealable.").

IRA actions aside, the Board's adverse action appellate jurisdiction does not include an agency's assignment of work. See Clarke v. OPM, 57 MSPR 30, 35 (1993); cf. Talley v. Dept. of Army, 50 MSPR 261, 263 (1991) ("The Board lacks jurisdiction to review an employee's voluntary reassignment or resignation taken to avoid the consequences of an impending RIF."); Burkwist v. Dept. of Transp., 27 MSPR 419, 420 (1985) (base pay comparison determines whether a reassignment has resulted in a reduction in pay); cf. 5 USC 7106 (defining management rights under the labor relations provisions of the CSRA to include assignment of employees); Neitzel v. Dept. of Army, DC-3443-14-0897-I-1 (NP 7/2/2015) (Board lacked jurisdiction over a change of a duty station argued to be in violation of agency regulations). [Refer to this chapter, "Pay and Benefits; Classification; Debt; Leave" and to Chapter 10, "Reassignment During or Following RIF."]

As to reassignment, the Federal Circuit explained, Sumner v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 99-3053):

...A "removal" within the meaning of the Board's jurisdictional statutes means removal from the federal service, not a reassignment from one job to another. Mr. Sumner argues that his reassignment must be regarded as an adverse agency action because it was adverse to his interests in various ways. It may well be true that the action was adverse to Mr. Sumner's interests, but that is not sufficient to give the Board jurisdiction over the action. The Board's limited jurisdiction does not extend to appeals from a reassignment--even an unwelcome reassignment--if it does not result in a loss of grade or pay for the employee. See Artman v. Department of the Interior, 926 F.2d 1120, 1122-23 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (the Board lacks jurisdiction over reassignments that do not constitute a reduction in grade or pay, "even though the reassignment reduces the employee's status, duties or responsibilities")....

With the exception of whistleblowing reprisal cases, or perhaps a USERRA claim, a reassignment is not a matter made appealable to the Board because it results from a prohibited personnel practice. The appropriate remedy may be for the appellant to seek the assistance of the Special Counsel, who has the authority to file a corrective action complaint with the Board. In Chleapas v. DHEW, 1 MSPR 479, 481, 1 MSPB 464 (1980), a decision that preceded the Whistleblower Protection Act provisions establishing an individual right of action, an employee complained of a detail that resulted from reprisal for whistleblowing activities; the Board could not review the case. The employee's remedy was through a complaint to OSC. Another employee who was dissatisfied with a detail should have used a grievance procedure to challenge the detail rather than challenging it through an MSPB appeal from a removal for AWOL after he failed to report for the detail. Bowen v. DOJ, 38 MSPR 332, 334-35 (1988). An allegation of retaliation through reassignment for the filing of a classification appeal, a prohibited personnel practice, was found not to be within the Board's appellate jurisdiction unless joined with an action falling within the Board's appellate jurisdiction. Anderson v. NASA, 5 MSPR 476, 478, 5 MSPB 477 (1981). Conversely, the Board lacks jurisdiction over the failure of an agency to select an appellant for a reassignment. Tankesley v. TVA, 54 MSPR 147, 151 (1992).

On the performance side of the law, regulations governing unacceptable performance actions need not be followed when the reassignment is for other management reasons. Jahn v. USDA (Fed. Cir. 1991 NP No. 90-3498); cf. Bishop v. Dept. of Commerce, 62 MSPR 138, 141 (1994) (reassignment was not appealable in conjunction with the adverse termination of a probationary supervisory appointment). [Refer to Chapter 9 concerning performance-based actions.]

If a reassignment does not furnish a basis for appellate jurisdiction under an adverse action or whistleblowing reprisal theory, it will be hard to find jurisdiction under another theory. If no displacement is involved in a reassignment, the action is not a reduction in force. Harpster v. Dept. of Army, 39 MSPR 43, 45 (1988); Lopez v. DHUD, 5 MSPR 52, 53, 5 MSPB 128 (1981) ("A reassignment from one organizational unit to another without a change in grade, pay or competitive level is not an [appealable] adverse action under 5 CFR 1201.3." The agency was not required to follow RIF procedures then in effect, there being no release from a competitive level, demotion, or displacement.). [Refer to Chapter 10 concerning RIFs.] Nor does the reassignment become a constructive removal because, having failed to accept the reassignment, the appellant is removed from the designated new job by adverse action for AWOL. Gabriel v. USDA, 38 MSPR 300, 302 n.2 (1988). [Refer to Chapter 8, "Refusal to Accept Reassignment."]

When a reassignment is coupled with a downgrading, there is an appealable action, at least as to the downgrading. If the employer asserts that the downgrading action was voluntary, and the employee makes a nonfrivolous allegation that it was not, the Board must provide a hearing and the AJ must address all material issues of fact and law presented on the record. Ragland v. IRS, 1 MSPR 758, 759, 2 MSPB 167 (1980); see McAlexander v. DOD, 105 MSPR 384, 2007 MSPB 103 (2007) (in a decision that was not too clear on jurisdictional analysis, the Board considered a request for review of an arbitration award involving a reassignment from one pay system to another with retained pay; the case was apparently treated as a reduction in grade and analyzed as a constructive adverse action); cf. Broadway v. Block, 694 F.2d 979, 982 (5th Cir. 1982) ("Reassignments with grade and pay retention are not included as adverse actions subject to review by the MSPB, and on appeal by the federal judiciary. The CSRA does not ignore reassignments, however. 5 U.S.C. Sec. 2302 prohibits the federal government as an employer from taking 'personnel action' which constitutes a 'prohibited personnel practice.' Personnel action under this provision includes appointments, promotions, transfers, reassignments and other expressly enumerated actions. Id. § 2302(a)(2)(A). The Special Counsel of the MSPB is authorized and required to investigate any allegation of prohibited personnel practices, and may request the MSPB to consider and order corrective action on the matter. 5 U.S.C. § 1206(a)-(c). He is also required to investigate any allegation concerning 'activities prohibited by any civil service law, rule, or regulation....' Id. § 1206(e)(1)(D). However, there are no statutory provisions for judicial oversight of the Special Counsel's efforts...."). [Refer to Chapter 6, "Constructive Adverse Action"; refer later in this chapter to "Disciplinary Reassignment; Unified Penalties; Reassignment Leading to Reduction in Pay."] A reassignment may violate a settlement, or it may violate a Board restoration order following a successful adverse action appeal. The reassignment may be actionable under an IRA appeal for whistleblower reprisal. [Refer to Chapter 16, "Compelling Reason for Reassignment; Detail; Different Duties" and "Agreement to Reassign; Work Schedule Change; Light Duty Position; Duration of Agency's Commitment."]

If a reassignment is not appealable, perforce neither is a detail--even if the detail is to a lower-graded position--as long as the appellant's pay is not adversely affected. See Scorcia v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 95-3059) (a detail without a reduction in grade or pay is not an appealable adverse action within the Board's jurisdiction); Nortz v. VA, 47 MSPR 526, 530 (1991) ("A detail, even to a lower-graded position that does not result in a reduction in pay, is not an action that is appealable to the Board."); Carty v. Dept. of Air Force, 31 MSPR 24, 25 n.* (1986) ("The Board does not have jurisdiction to review the reassignment of an employee which does not involve reduction in pay or grade.... The detail of an employee to another position is not within the Board's appellate jurisdiction because it does not involve a reduction in pay or grade."). A detail does not fall within the Board's jurisdiction when the appeal includes a charge of prohibited discrimination. See Snow v. Dept. of Air Force, 39 MSPR 582, 584 (1989) (despite an EEO allegation, there was no jurisdiction over a detail from a GS-12 to GS-7 position as a result of the pendency of a conflict of interest investigation; because the official position had not changed, there was no reduction in either grade or pay); cf. Treese v. USPS, 77 MSPR 187, 193 (1998) ("A detail by its very nature is temporary and involves no formal appointment; the employee continues to be the incumbent of the position from which he was detailed."); Dixon v. USPS, 64 MSPR 445, 450 (1994) (a detail is a temporary position change; the employee continues to be the incumbent of the original position); White v. Dept. of Air Force, 63 MSPR 90, 93 n.3 (1994) (a detail involving a reduction in grade but without a reduction in pay is not directly appealable to the Board; it may fall under IRA jurisdiction).

One special case permits the Board to establish ordinary appellate jurisdiction over what otherwise appears to be a reassignment. Russell v. Dept. of Navy, 6 MSPR 698, 711, 6 MSPB 585 (1981), explained that jurisdiction exists as to a reassignment that amounts to a constructive downgrading--a situation that occurs when an appellant's job is upgraded because of a classification error or the issuance of a new classification standard, but without a change in duties, soon after the appellant is reassigned out of that job. If the duties are changed after the reassignment and the job is then upgraded based on those changes, the reassignment does not constitute an adverse action. See Pfefferkorn v. DOJ, 57 MSPR 658, 662-63 (1993). [Refer to Chapter 6, "Constructive Demotion Through Reassignment; Russell."]

As to transfers, OPM regulations definite the term, 5 CFR 210.102 (2022):

(18) Transfer means a change of an employee, without a break in service of 1 full workday, from a position in one agency to a position in another agency.

The failure to secure a transfer from one agency to another is considered tantamount to nonselection for a position. The Board has no jurisdiction over the denial of a transfer, and jurisdiction will not likely be conferred based on an argument that the failure of the gaining agency to transfer was for reasons involving the employee's suitability for a position. See Brodt v. MSPB, 11 F.3d 1060, 1061 (Fed. Cir. 1993) ("It follows that the refusal of the Postal Service to transfer Mr. Brodt to another position at the same pay and grade is not an action appealable to the Board."); Gaff v. Dept. of Transp., 45 MSPR 387, 392 (1990) (Board lacked jurisdiction over an appeal challenging the failure of agency to accept the appellants as transferees to that agency). But see Colburn v. DOJ, 80 MSPR 257, 259 ¶ 6 (1998) (recognizing the right to appeal an involuntary transfer irrespective of a change in pay rate; a transfer is tantamount to removal); Yaksich v. Dept. of Air Force, 71 MSPR 355, 364-65 (1996) (Board treated as a constructive adverse action case an appeal by an individual claiming that discriminatory conditions forced her to accept separation from one agency and transfer to another).
