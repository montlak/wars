a. What Constitutes Evidence

McNair v. DOJ, 6 MSPR 583, 584-85, 6 MSPB 491 (1981), described the type of evidence meeting the substantial evidence test in a WIGI appeal. The agency submissions consisted of the WIGI denial, a redetermination decision, and the appellant's most recent performance appraisal. The two decisions were no more than the allegations supporting the action, not evidence of deficiencies. The appraisal was not evidence in support of the negative ALOC determination, as the appraisal showed the appellant met the requirements of her position as to quality and quantity of work. The agency failed in its burden of proof and was directed to grant the step increase. See Dillon v. Dept. of Army, 13 MSPR 273, 274 (1982) (allegations contained in a negative ALOC determination are not evidence in support of those allegations). In other cases the reconsideration or redetermination decision is likewise not evidence; it only comprises the charges supporting the action. McNair, supra. The ALOC determination may, however, constitute substantial evidence if it incorporates by reference materials substantiating work deficiencies. Stankis v. EPA, 11 MSPR 586, 589-90 (1982), on reconsideration, 20 MSPR 250, 253 (1984) (applying a preponderance standard as a result of a court remand and setting aside the agency's WIGI denial). [Refer to Chapter 4, "Evidentiary Value of Charge," and earlier in this chapter to "Charges, Work Papers," particularly to the DePauw decision.]

An appellant's admission that he failed to perform at an ALOC is substantial evidence supporting the denial of a WIGI. McCloskey v. Dept. of Treasury, 6 MSPR 680, 682, 6 MSPB 570 (1981). Testimony of a supervisor and copies of appellant's work product constitute substantial evidence. See Dowd v. DHHS, 21 MSPR 374, 377 (1984). Work product is evidence. See Whitehead v. Dept. of Navy, 20 MSPR 8, 11 (1984) (WIGI denial was sustained as to an EEO technician whose work product contained grammatical errors and did not include necessary information; the appellant could not process EEO complaints without detailed guidance).

If an appellant is made aware of agency evidence supporting the agency case for a negative reconsideration decision, the AJ errs if he refuses to consider the evidence because it was not specifically referenced by the negative determination letter. Nordell v. DHHS, 15 MSPR 276, 283 (1983) (the appellant was fully aware of the evidence and had a right to respond to it).
