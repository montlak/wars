4. Alternative Form of Annuity

Alternative annuities were suspended, and then precluded after 1990, as described by Haywood v. OPM, 65 MSPR 603, 607, 606-11 (1994), with the exception of a continuing option for an alternative annuity when its denial "would be against equity and good conscience, due to a life-threatening affliction or other critical medical condition affecting such individual":

Prior to December 1, 1990, retiring employees could elect an "alternative annuity" consisting of a "lump-sum credit" plus an appropriately reduced lifetime annuity. See 5 U.S.C. § 8343a; 5 C.F.R. §§ 831.2204-.2205. "Lump-sum credit" is defined as "the unrefunded amount" consisting of--

(A) retirement deductions made from the basic pay of an employee or Member;

(B) amounts deposited by an employee or Member covering earlier service, including any amounts deposited under section 8334(j) of this title; and

(C interest on the deductions and deposits at 4 percent a year to December 31, 1947, and 3 percent a year thereafter compounded annually to December 31, 1956, or, in the case of an employee or Member separated or transferred to a position in which he does not continue subject to this subchapter before he has completed 5 years of civilian service, to the date of the separation or transfer;

but does not include interest--

(i) if the service covered thereby aggregates 1 year or less; or

(ii) for the fractional part of a month in the total service[.]

5 U.S.C. § 8331(8); see 5 C.F.R. § 831.2202.

By the Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act (OBRA) of 1990, Congress suspended alternative annuities for annuities commencing after December 2, 1990, and before October 1, 1995, but provided several exceptions to this suspension. See 5 U.S.C. § 8343a(f)(2) (amended by OBRA of 1993, Pub. L. No. 103-66, § 11002(a), (d), 107 Stat. 409, which changed this temporary suspension to a permanent ban); 5 C.F.R. § 831.2203(h). The exception involved in this appeal was specified at 5 U.S.C. § 8343a(f)(2)(B) (1993) (amended by OBRA of 1993, Pub. L. No. 103-66, § 11002(a), (d), 107 Stat. 409), and, provided that an alternative annuity shall be allowed if disallowing such an annuity "would be against equity and good conscience, due to a life-threatening affliction or other critical medical condition affecting such individual." [2] (Emphasis added.)

[2] Effective October 1, 1994, this is the sole exception for the now-permanent ban on alternative annuities. See OBRA of 1993, Pub. L. No. 103-66, § 11002(a), (d), 107 Stat. 409.

Pursuant to a specific statutory grant of authority, 5 U.S.C. § 8343a(a), OPM has promulgated regulations implementing the statutory suspension of alternative annuities. See 5 C.F.R. 031.2203(h). With respect to the statutory phrase "life-threatening affliction or other critical medical condition," OPM incorporated by reference its preexisting regulatory definition--at 5 C.F.R. § 831.2207(c)(2)-(3)--of the same statutory phrase contained in the OBRA of 1987. See OBRA of 1987 Pub. L. No. 100-203, § 6001, 101 Stat. 1330-275; 5 C.F.R. § 831.2203(h)(1)(i). Under 5 C.F.R. § 831.2207(c)(2)-(3), the statutory phrase "life-threatening affliction or other critical medical condition" is defined as "a medical condition so severe as to reasonably limit an individual's probable life expectancy to less than one year."

The administrative judge found that this regulatory definition was erroneous because it did not take into account all portions of the statutory language, specifically, that it took into account only the "life-threatening affliction" portion of the statutory language and not the "against equity and good conscience" or the "other critical medical condition" portion. We agree in part with the administrative judge's findings in this regard.

Contrary to the administrative judge's apparent determination, we find that the phrase "against equity and good conscience," as used in 5 U.S.C. § 8343a(f)(2)(B), does not constitute a separate standard for determining whether an individual qualifies for the statutory exception. Section 8343a(f)(2)(B) provides that an alternative annuity shall be allowed if disallowing such an annuity would be "against equity and good conscience, due to a life-threatening affliction or other critical medical condition affecting such individual." (Emphasis added.) It is apparent that the phrase "against equity and good conscience" merely describes the justification for excepting individuals suffering from a "life-threatening affliction or other critical medical condition," and does not constitute an independent basis for excepting individuals based on other types of reasons, such as financial status.

We agree with the administrative judge, however, that the regulatory definition does not adequately take into account the "other critical medical condition" portion of the statutory language. We find that the plain meaning of the statutory phrase "critical medical condition" is not limited to a condition likely to be fatal within one year, as defined by OPM regulation, but encompasses other types of serious medical conditions including, e.g., a condition likely to be fatal within several years. See Webster's Ninth New Collegiate Dictionary (1990) (defining "critical" as, inter alia, "relating to or being the stage of a disease at which an abrupt change for better or worse may be expected" or "being or relating to an illness or condition involving danger of death"); Webster's Encyclopedic Unabridged Dictionary of the English Language (1989) (defining "critical" as, inter alia, "dangerous"). To ignore the "other critical medical condition" portion of the statutory language would be contrary to the rule of statutory construction that all provisions of a statute be given meaning and that no part be rendered superfluous or insignificant. See Bonggat v. Department of the Navy, 59 M.S.P.R. 175, 179 (1993); Caddell v. Department of Justice, 52 M.S.P.R. 529, 532 (1992). Hence, we find that OPM's definition of this statutory phrase was too narrowly drawn.

OPM argues on petition for review that, because it has been charged with the administration of the statutory provision in question, the Board should give great deference to its implementing regulations. See, e.g., Newman v. Love, 962 F.2d 1008, 1012 (Fed. Cir. 1992). The Board will not follow the agency's interpretation, however, when there are compelling reasons to conclude that it is in conflict with the plain language of the governing statute. See id. As discussed above, and that the implementing regulation is inconsistent with the governing statute because it fails to adequately take into count all portions of the statutory language, and unreasonably restricts the plain meaning of the statutory terms. We therefore find that OPM's interpretation is not entitled to our deference.

OPM also argues on petition for review that Congress presumptively approved of and adopted OPM's preexisting (since 1988) regulatory interpretation of the statutory phrase "against equity and good conscience, due to a life-threatening affliction or other critical medical condition," used in the OBRA of 1987, when it re-used this same phrase in the OBRA of 1990. The pertinent statutory provision in the OBRA of 1987 required a one-year partial (40 percent) deferral of the "lump-sum credit," which was previously payable in full (100 percent) upon retirement, but allowed an exception to this partial deferral in situations where such a deferral "would be against equity and good conscience: due to a life-threatening affliction or other critical medical condition affecting such individual." OBRA of 1987, Pub. L. No. 100-203, § 6001, 101 Stat. 1330-275, amended by Pub. L. No. 101-103, § 6, 103 Stat. 672 (1989), amended by Pub. L. No. 101-508, § 7001(a)(2)(C)(i) 104 Stat. 1388-328 (1990); see 5 U.S.C.A. § 8343a Historical and Statutory Notes (West Supp. 1993): 5 C.F.R. § 831.2207 (promulgated in 1988); see also 5 C.F.R. § 831.2208 (promulgated in 1991). Because it is manifest that this one-year deferral of the lump-sum credit would be "against equity and good conscience" as to an individual suffering from a medical condition likely to be fatal within one year, the regulatory interpretation of the statutory phrase would appear to be appropriate within the context of the OBRA of 1987.

In the context of the pertinent provisions in the OBRA of 1990, however, the regulatory interpretation is problematic, as discussed above. The legislative history of the OBRA of 1990 sheds little light on the meaning of this statutory phrase, but we see nothing therein to indicate that the entitlement to an alternative annuity was intended to be narrowly and arbitrarily restricted to individuals suffering from conditions likely to be fatal within one year. Rather, the statutory language reasonably should be interpreted as referring to a medical condition that is likely to significantly shorten the individual's life (even if not necessarily likely to be fatal within one year) because, as to individuals suffering from such a medical condition, the effect of the ban on alternative annuities is that their probable premature death would preclude them from fully recovering even the moneys they actually paid into the Civil Service Retirement Fund. See generally 5 U.S.C. § 8331(8); 5 C.F.R. § 831.2205.

It is true that when Congress adopts a new law incorporating sections of a prior law without change, Congress is presumed to have been aware of the administrative or judicial interpretation of the incorporated sections and to have adopted that interpretation. See Lindahl v. Office of Personnel Management, 470 U.S. 768, 782 & n.15 (1985); Steele v. Office of Personnel Management, 57 M.S.P.R. 458, 462 (1993). For the reasons discussed above, however, we find that this presumption is not tenable under the circumstances because OPM's regulatory interpretation of the governing statute is too narrowly drawn and is inconsistent with the plain meaning of the statutory language.

The administrative judge found, and we agree, that the appellant did not establish that she had "a medical condition so severe as to reasonably limit [her] probable life expectancy to less than one year," as required by 5 C.F.R. § 831.2207(c)(2)-(3). Because we find that this regulation, as applied to this case, constitutes an erroneous and unreasonable interpretation of the governing statute, we will look solely to the statutory language ("life-threatening affliction or other critical medical condition") to determine whether the appellant qualifies for the exception to the ban on alternative annuities. See Buckley v. Office of Personnel Management, 46 M.S.P.R. 172, 176 (1990), aff'd, 932 F.2d 980 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (Table). The administrative judge found that the appellant had a "critical medical condition" on the basis of her end-stage renal disease and chronic renal failure. The administrative judge provided no reasons for this finding, however.

For an annuitant to establish her entitlement to an alternative annuity, she must submit to OPM evidence of a life-threatening affliction or other critical medical condition, certified by a physician, on or before the date she elects to receive an alternative annuity. See 5 C.F.R. § 831.2207(c)(3)(iii). If the proffered medical condition is not one that constitutes prima facie evidence of a life-threatening affliction or other critical medical condition, OPM reviews the physician's certification to determine whether the cited condition is life-threatening or critical. See 5 C.F.R. § 831.2207(c)(3)(iv).

The record shows that the medical evidence considered by OPM consisted of summaries of doctors' visits and test results. The evidence does not include any medical opinion as to the seriousness of the appellant's medical condition or conditions, and does not otherwise show that the appellant suffered from a life-threatening or other critical medical condition.

...

Based on the current record, we find that the appellant has not established that she suffered from a "life-threatening affliction or other critical medical condition" at the time of her retirement. However, we find that she should be afforded another opportunity to prove her claim for an alternative annuity, for the following reasons.

As discussed above, the statutory phrase "critical medical condition" is not limited to conditions that are likely to be fatal within one year, as defined by OPM's regulations, but includes conditions that are likely to be fatal within several years. Since this test is less stringent than the one formerly applied by OPM, it may be that, on remand, the appellant could prove her entitlement to an alternative annuity.

Further, OPM failed to adequately apprise the appellant of what evidence was needed to establish her claim. In its initial decision, OPM simply stated that the appellant was not eligible for an alternative annuity because her employing agency did not certify that she met the necessary medical requirements. In its reconsideration decision, OPM simply stated that the appellant was not eligible for an alternative annuity based on its review of the medical evidence.

Individuals requesting alternative annuities are usually not represented by an attorney. In addition, the applicants tend to be ill, even if their medical condition is ultimately found not to be life-threatening or critical. Moreover, such proceedings sold not be adversarial in nature. As the agency charged with the responsibility of administering civil service retirement law, OPM has an affirmative duty to fully and accurately inform claimants of their rights and obligations. See. e.g, Wilber v. Office of Personnel Management, 47 M.S.P.R. 444, 447 (1991); Beasley v. Office of Personnel Management, 43 M.S.P.R. 70, 74 (1989). Applicants for alternative annuities should not have to guess what medical evidence is required to establish their claims. We therefore find that OPM has an obligation to provide more than a conclusory statement that the applicant did not meet her burden of proof, and should provide at least as much guidance as it does in the disability retirement cases where the applicant is specifically apprised of the areas in which the medical evidence is deficient.

See Wutke v. OPM, 67 MSPR 523, 526-30 (1995) (remanding to determine whether the appellant's medical problems met the statutory criterion of "other critical medical condition" and whether there was a basis for waiver of the time limit for electing an alternative annuity under the statute or regulation, a determination that would in part depend on whether a notice of election rights was required).

The alternate annuity with lump sum payment was for employees who were separated from the government involuntarily, other than for cause on charges of misconduct or delinquency, a situation described in an appeal involving a group of employees affected by an agency restructuring in Allen v. OPM, 77 MSPR 212, 216-22 (1998):

The appellants claim the right to elect to receive an alternative form of annuity under former 5 U.S.C. § 8343a(f)(2)(A). We find, for the reasons below, that the right claimed by the appellants is dependent upon a determination in the first instance of whether they were "separated from the service involuntarily" under 5 U.S.C. § 8336(d)(1). Thus, we will examine in this Opinion the circumstances relevant to proof of an involuntary separation for retirement purposes under 5 U.S.C. ch. 83. In this regard, the Board has long held that the determination of an involuntary separation for retirement purposes under 5 U.S.C. ch. 83 is totally separate and distinct from a determination of involuntary retirement or resignation for Board jurisdictional purposes under 5 U.S.C. ch. 75. Sanderson v. Office of Personnel Management, 72 M.S.P.R. 311, 315 (1996), aff'd, No. 97-3106 (Fed. Cir. Oct. 10, 1997) (Table); Silvestri v. Department of the Army, 60 M.S.P.R. 594, 603 (1993); Lematta v. Department of Health & Human Services, 22 M.S.P.R. 18, 20 & n.2 (1984); see also Anderson v. Office of Personnel Management, 11 M.S.P.R. 60, 63-64 (1982). We will more fully address those separate distinctions and set forth the applicable legal analysis in these retirement appeals.

Prior to December 1, 1990, retiring employees could elect to receive an alternative form of annuity consisting of a "lump-sum credit" plus an appropriately reduced lifetime annuity. 5 U.S.C. § 8343a; see Haywood v. Office of Personnel Management, 65 M.S.P.R. 603, 606 (1994). By the Omnibus Budget and Reconciliation Act (OBRA) of 1990, Pub. L. No. 101-508, 104 Stat. 1388, Congress suspended alternative forms of annuities commencing after December 2, 1990, and before October 1, 1995, but provided several exceptions to this suspension. Haywood, 65 M.S.P.R. at 607. The exception involved in these appeals was specified at 5 U.S.C. § 8343a(f)(2)(A) (1992), which was in effect at the time of the appellants' separations and thus is applicable in these appeals. See, e.g., Sanderson, 72 M.S.P.R. at 319. Section 8343a(f)(2)(A) provided that, excluding certain categories of employees not applicable in these appeals, an alternative form of annuity shall be allowed for "any individual...who is separated from Government service involuntarily (other than for cause on charges of misconduct or delinquency)...."[3] (Emphasis supplied.)

[3] The legislative history of the OBRA of 1990 sheds no light on the meaning of this statutory phrase. See H.R. Rep. No. 881, 101st Cong., 2d Sess. 174-76, 186 (1990); H.R. Conf. Rep. No. 964, 101st Cong., 2d Sess. 975-76 (1990); see also Haywood, 65 M.S.P.R. at 609. No Senate Report was submitted with the legislation. The OBRA of 1993, Pub. L. No. 103-66, § 11002, 107 Stat. 409, deleted subsection (f) of section 8343a, effective October 1, 1994. See Haywood, 65 M.S.P.R. at 607.

Pursuant to a specific grant of statutory authority, 5 U.S.C. § 8343a(a), OPM has promulgated regulations implementing the statutory suspension of alternative forms of annuities. See Haywood, 65 M.S.P.R. at 607. With respect to the statutory phrase "separated from Government service involuntarily (other than for cause on charges of misconduct or delinquency)," OPM's regulation provides that "[a]n individual whose annuity commences after December 1, 1990, and before October 1, 1994, may elect an alternative form of annuity only if that individual is...separated involuntarily other than for cause on charges of misconduct or delinquency." 5 C.F.R. § 831.2203(h)(1)(i)(B). In promulgating the interim version of this regulation, effective November 5, 1990, OPM provided the following supplementary information:

The second exception is for nondisability retirees who are separated from Government service involuntarily, other than for cause on charges of misconduct or delinquency. These individuals remain entitled to elect an [alternative form of annuity] during the suspension period.

***

[I]ndividuals who retire under an "early out" authority under 5 U.S.C. 8336(d)(2) or 5 U.S.C. 8414(b)(1)(B), are not considered to be involuntarily separated, since the law specifically states that such persons are "separated from the service voluntarily."

56 Fed. Reg. 6549 (1991). OPM's interpretation of the statutes it administers is entitled to considerable weight, especially where, as we find in these appeals for the reasons set forth in this Opinion, there are no compelling reasons to conclude that such an interpretation is erroneous or unreasonable. Andrada, 74 M.S.P.R. at 233.

The "early out" statute referenced by OPM, 5 U.S.C. § 8336(d), provides, in relevant part: An employee who--

(1) is separated from the service involuntarily, except by removal for cause on charges of misconduct or delinquency; or

(2) while serving in a geographic area designated by [OPM], is separated from the service voluntarily during a period in which [OPM] determines that--

(A) the agency in which the employee is serving is undergoing a major reorganization, a major reduction in force, or a major transfer of function; and

(B) a significant percentage of the employees serving in such agency will be separated or subject to an immediate reduction in basic pay...;

after completing 25 years of service or after becoming 50 years of age and completing 20 years of service is entitled to an annuity.

(Emphasis supplied.) While the "early out" statute permits, under particular circumstances, employees to retire early and receive an immediate annuity, without regard to a determination of voluntariness under 5 U.S.C. § 8336(d), the right of the appellants to elect to receive an alternative form of annuity under former 5 U.S.C. § 8343a(f)(2)(A) is subject to a determination by OPM that they were "separated from the service involuntarily" under 5 U.S.C. § 8336(d)(1) rather than "voluntarily" under section 8336(d)(2). See Edgerton v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 768 F.2d 1314, 1317 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (a claim of entitlement to an involuntary retirement under 5 U.S.C. § 8336(d)(1) must be made directly to OPM); Szczeblowski v. Office of Personnel Management, 57 M.S.P.R. 578, 581 (1993) (Title 5 charges OPM with administering the CSRS), aff'd, 19 F.3d 39 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table). In these appeals, because the appellants retired under the "early out" authority of 5 U.S.C. § 8336(d)(2), OPM found that their separations were voluntary and they are ineligible to elect to receive an alternative form of annuity under the former 5 U.S.C. § 8343a(f)(2)(A).

In reviewing OPM retirement decisions determining whether a separation is involuntary for purposes of a discontinued service retirement annuity under section 8336(d)(1), the Board has previously noted former Federal Personnel Manual (FPM) Supp. 831-1, subch. S11-2(a) (Sept. 21, 1981). This FPM provision was in effect at the time of the appellants' separations and thus is applicable in these appeals. See, e.g., Hardy v. Department of the Army, 67 M.S.P.R. 292, 296 n. (1995). FPM Supp. 831-1, subch. S11-2(a) provided, in relevant part, as follows:

a. Definition of term. The term involuntary separation means any separation against the will and without the consent of the employee, other than separation for cause on charges of misconduct or delinquency. Examples are: reduction in force; abolishment of position; lack of funds; expiration of incumbent's term of office; liquidation of an office or of an entire agency; inefficiency (unless due to the employee's misconduct); transfer of function outside the commuting area; disability (provided the separation action is initiated by the agency); separation during probation because of failure to qualify; or separation of an indefinite or temporary employee under OPM's instructions for displacement....Note, however, that whether a separation is involuntary depends upon all the facts in a particular case; it is the true substance of the action which governs rather than the methods followed or the terminology used....

(Emphasis supplied.) The U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit and the Board have applied this administrative definition of "involuntary separation" under 5 U.S.C. § 8336(d)(1) in determining whether a separation is voluntary or not for discontinued service retirement purposes. Yarbrough v. Office of Personnel Management, 770 F.2d 1056, 1058-59 (Fed. Cir. 1985); Sanderson, 72 M.S.P.R. at 315; Gammill v. Office of Personnel Management, 29 M.S.P.R. 484, 487-88 (1985). An individual asserting that his separation was "involuntary" under section 8336(d)(1) must prove the existence of the above agency actions and circumstances set forth in the FPM. See Sanderson, 72 M.S.P.R. at 315; Gammill, 29 M.S.P.R. at 488-89; see also Silvestri, 60 M.S.P.R. at 600-03.

With regard to these appeals, the Board found subsequent to the appellants' retirements that the Postal Service's 1992-93 reorganization constituted a reduction in force (RIF) as to those preference-eligibles who were released from their competitive level by demotion as a result of the reorganization. Robinson v. U.S. Postal Service, 63 M.S.P.R. 307, 311-23 (1994); Brown v. U.S. Postal Service, 58 M.S.P.R. 345, 347-48 (1993); see Krizman v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 77 F.3d 434, 438 (Fed. Cir. 1996). To prove that a separation was "involuntary" under the above FPM provision as caused by, inter alia, a RIF, an appellant in these appeals must be a preference-eligible and show that he or she had:

[D]efinite knowledge from a specific written notice that he or she faces involuntary separation from his or her position or from Federal service as of some specific date because of coming organizational changes before his or her resignation may be accepted as involuntary and qualifying for discontinued-service retirement. The notice may be given in advance of official management action effecting the changes. In certain circumstances, a general notice directed to all employees facing involuntary separation may be acceptable if prior approval of the notice is obtained from OPM.

FPM Supp. 831-1, subch. S11-2(b).

The appellants contend that their restructuring-motivated decisions to accept early-out retirements under 5 U.S.C. § 8336(d)(2) were made under "duress, coercion, and misrepresentation" by the Postal Service and thus their separations were involuntary. Evidence and argument that a presumably voluntary retirement was the product of agency coercion, misrepresentation, or duress is relevant to proof that the retirement was actually an adverse action (removal) under 5 U.S.C. ch. 75 that is directly appealable to the Board. Taylor v. U.S. Postal Service, 73 M.S.P.R. 67, 71, aff'd, 121 F.3d 727 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (Table); Gingrich v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 M.S.P.R. 583, 585 (1995); see 5 U.S.C. §§ 7512(1), 7513(d); Schultz v. U.S. Navy, 810 F.2d 1133, 1136-37 (Fed. Cir. 1987); Scharf v. Department of the Air Force, 710 F.2d 1572, 1574-75 (Fed. Cir. 1983).

As we have previously stated, however, the determination of an involuntary separation for retirement purposes under 5 U.S.C. ch. 83 is totally separate and distinct from a determination of involuntary retirement for Board jurisdictional purposes under 5 U.S.C. ch. 75. The right to a discontinued service retirement based on an "involuntary" separation under 5 U.S.C. § 8336(d)(1) requires proof by an appellant that his separation is the result of an agency action or circumstances under FPM Supp. 831-1, subch. S11-2. See Yarbrough, 770 F.2d at 1058-59; Sanderson, 72 M.S.P.R. at 315; Gammill, 29 M.S.P.R. at 488-89; see also Silvestri, 60 M.S.P.R. at 600-03. In contrast to the requirements of a 5 U.S.C. ch. 75 jurisdictional determination, a showing of wrongful actions by the employing agency, such as misinformation, coercion, or duress, is insufficient to establish that an employee's separation is the result of an agency action or circumstances under FPM Supp. 831-1, subch. S11-2. See Sanderson, 72 M.S.P.R. at 316; Gammill, 29 M.S.P.R. at 488-89; Lematta, 20 M.S.P.R. at 20 n.2; see also Richmond, 496 U.S. at 416, 434, and Holder v. Office of Personnel Management, 47 F.3d 412, 414 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (the government cannot be estopped from denying benefits not otherwise permitted by law even if the claimant was denied monetary benefits because of his reliance on the mistaken advice of a government official). Therefore, we hold that the appellants may not present individual claims and evidence that their discontinued service retirements under 5 U.S.C. § 8336(d)(2) were involuntary on the basis of misinformation, coercion, or duress in the context of these Chapter 83 retirement appeals against OPM and the Postal Service in which they seek to elect to receive an alternative form of annuity. See, e.g., Sanderson, 72 M.S.P.R. at 315-16.

CONCLUSION

Upon consideration of our analysis and findings above, we conclude that the appellants may not present individual claims that their retirements under 5 U.S.C. § 8336(d)(2) were involuntary on the basis of misinformation, coercion, or duress in the context of these Chapter 83 retirement appeals against OPM in which they seek to elect to receive an alternative form of annuity. Proof of misinformation, coercion, or duress, even if it were sufficient to establish Board jurisdiction over a Chapter 75 appeal of an involuntary resignation, would not be sufficient to prove that an action by the Postal Service and circumstances set forth in FPM Supp. 831-1, subch. S11-2 have occurred that establish entitlement to a discontinued service retirement based on an involuntary separation under 5 U.S.C. § 8336(d)(1) and the right to elect to receive an alternative form of annuity under former 5 U.S.C. § 8343a(f)(2)(A).

Whether the appellant characterizes his claim as one for lost investment income or for recovery of the amount by which his annuity was supposedly "discounted," in essence he seeks interest on the first installment of the lump-sum credit for the period between his retirement and the time payment was made. Cf. Library of Congress v. Shaw, 478 U.S. 310, 321-22 (1986) (a party's request for compensation stemming from his belated receipt of payment on a debt is a claim for interest, regardless of how the party designates the claim). We have reopened this appeal for the limited purpose of addressing the claim for interest.

The United States is immune from a claim for interest on a sum due and owing to a private party in the absence of an express statutory provision indicating that Congress intended for interest to accrue. Library of Congress v. Shaw, 478 U.S. at 317. See also Fidelity Construction Co. v. United States 700 F.2d 1379, 1383: (Fed. Cir.), cert. denied, 464 U.S. 826 (1983) (Congressional intent to permit the recovery of interest by successful claimants against the United States "cannot be implied")

In Kesselman v. Office of Personnel Management, 47 M.S.P.R. 293 (1991) we held, under an earlier version of the statute authorizing the payment of a lump-sum credit under an alternative annuity, that interest did not accrue on the first installment of the lump-sum credit between the time of the annuitant's retirement and the time that the first installment was paid. Although the pertinent statute has been amended, the provision for interest remains the same. See P.L. No. 101-239, section 4005(b), 103 Stat. 2135, as amended by P.L. No. 101-508, section 7001(a)(2) (reprinted at 5 U.S.C.A. § 8343a note (West Supp. 1992)). The statute provides that interest is payable on the second of two installments of the lump-sum credit, but it is silent with respect to the running of interest on the first installment. Since Congress has not explicitly authorized the payment of interest on the first installment of the lump-sum credit to those electing alternative annuities, the appellant cannot recover it.

Further, the Board does not have the authority to grant equitable relief for the appellant's temporary inability to invest the first installment of his lump-sum credit. See Office of Personnel Management v. Richmond, 496 U.S. 414 (1990) (equitable considerations cannot serve as the basis for a payment from the federal Treasury when such payment is not otherwise permitted by law). We are also without authority to adjudicate the appellant's claim that the purported reduction in his annuity's value amounted to a taking of his property without just compensation in violation of the Fifth Amendment to the United States Constitution. See Reid v. United States, 715 F.2d 1148, 1153 (7th Cir. 1983) (federal district courts have exclusive jurisdiction over claims alleging a taking of property without just compensation where the amount sought is less than $10,000).
