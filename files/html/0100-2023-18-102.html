d. Advisor

Large agencies have hordes of personnel specialists and lawyers waiting to provide advice. Too often they wait, and they wait, for their managers to seek their counsel when it would do some good. Yet a manager may actually seek out the personnel experts before action is taken. This does not pose a problem to the Federal Circuit, a court that stated it takes "well-nigh irrefragable proof" to overcome the presumption that government officials act in good faith in deciding whether to remove an employee. Gonzales v. DLA, 772 F.2d 887 (Fed. Cir. 1985). In Gonzales, decided before Stone, the court explained that there is no prohibition against a decisionmaker calling for advice from those who are not adversaries of the offending employee, a situation described as no more than an example of permissible institutional decisionmaking. The court observed that "[i]n the real world of government decision-making, administrators cannot be expected to perform their duties in a complete vacuum without consultations or search for advice." See Big Crow v. DHHS, DE-0752-15-0368-I-1 (NP 3/10/2016) ("At the hearing, the deciding official testified that HR staff advised her on whether she could accept the appellant's suggestion that the agency allow him to exhaust his leave until his planned retirement date as opposed to removing him. It thus appears that the deciding official merely received procedural guidance and personnel advice from the HR staff as opposed to any ex parte information. We find that there was nothing improper in this and there was nothing to prevent her from receiving such advice and assistance. See Fickie v. Department of the Army, 86 M.S.P.R. 525, ¶ 30 (2000) (finding no due process violation or harmful procedural error where the documents the appellant presented generally indicated that the deciding official received advice regarding the legal propriety of the proposed action and procedural matters).").

In Willingham v. Dept. of Navy, 809 Fed. Appx. 872, 876 (Fed. Cir. 2020 NP), the allegation was that an appellant in an indefinite suspension appeal was denied due process through interaction of the deciding official and agency counsel. A claim of attorney-client privilege blocked the appellant from obtaining information about their exchanges when the appeal was before the Board. In court, rather than considering the seemingly more limited issue of privilege, the court essentially said the contacts could have made no difference, that is, they were not material, since the deciding official had no choice but to suspend the employee following suspension of his clearance:

Willingham also contends that his constitutional right to due process was violated by the deciding official's invocation of attorney-client privilege concerning communications between him and the legal department of MSC. But the deciding official's decision to suspend Willingham indefinitely was based entirely on the suspension of Willingham's security clearance, an unreviewable determination and one which was not entrusted to the deciding official in any event. Because Willingham could not perform his duties without a security clearance, and the clearance had been suspended, the identified ex parte communications do not create "a procedural defect so substantial and so likely to cause prejudice that it undermines the due process guarantee and entitles the claimant to an entirely new administrative proceeding," Stone v. FDIC, 179 F.3d 1368, 1376-77. A different deciding official, free of any ex parte communication, would have little choice but to reach the same conclusion: that Willingham's position required a security clearance, and he did not hold one. See Hornseth, 916 F.3d at 1375-76.

Before Stone, no error resulted from the VA Central Office recommendation for the removal of a field employee when, without the recommendation, a suspension might have been imposed. Communications between VACO and the deciding official were not ex parte statements made by an adversary, as was the case in Sullivan v. Dept. of Navy, 720 F.2d 1266 (Fed. Cir. 1983), and Ryder v. United States, 585 F.2d 482 (Ct. Cl. 1978). The appellant had an opportunity to respond to the VACO recommendation--considered "neutral" to avoid decisional bias. Jackson v. VA, 22 MSPR 350, 352-53 (1984). But see Boddie v. Dept. of Navy, 827 F.2d 1578 (Fed. Cir. 1987), post [discussed later in this chapter, "Changes in Proposing, Deciding Official"]. Nor was a contact between a deciding official and a technical advisor at agency headquarters a prohibited ex parte contact. The advisor was not an adversary; he had no interest in the outcome of the case. Smith v. DLA, 25 MSPR 626, 629 (1985); see Reuter v. Dept. of Commerce, ___F.4th___ (Fed. Cir. 2023) (reviewing Federal Circuit authority concerning ex parte communications including and postdating Sullivan and Stone and determining that emails from HR officials to the deciding official concerning case status were cumulative, confirmatory, and not due process violations). Communications between the proposing official, the agency investigator, agency counsel, and the deciding official were not improper. Bilger v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 87-3454); see Keels v. Dept. of Navy, 9 MSPR 19, 20 (1981) (communication between an Employee Assistance Program Coordinator and a proposing official was not improper). But see Johnson v. Dept. of Air Force, 50 F.4th 110 (Fed. Cir. 2022) (improper for deciding official to rely on information from family members who were medical professionals when that information affected the deciding official's evaluation of the weight or credibility of evidence offered by the appellant to rebut a charge of failure to pass a random drug test).
