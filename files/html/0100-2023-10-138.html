6. Duty Status of Federal Employee Witness; Pay, Travel, Per Diem

The fair import of 5 CFR 1201.33 (2022) is that federal employees who are witnesses will be in duty (paid) status during the hearing. The agency must pay travel and per diem expenses, as appropriate, to its employees appearing as witnesses. Richardson v. USPS, 52 MSPR 96, 99 n.5 (1991). Witness fee entitlement was also addressed in the appeal of In re Maisto, 28 MSPR 436, 439-441 (1985). The agency argued that it was only required to pay its employee for the time spent at the hearing testifying, not for the time spent traveling to and from the hearing and waiting to testify. The Board disagreed, likening the appearance of a witness under Board regulations to the status of a witness whose testimony is compelled by subpoena. For a witness under subpoena, compensation is required under 5 USC 1204(b)(3) and 28 USC 1821(b) for the time used in going to and returning from the place of attendance both at the beginning and end of the attendance and at any time during that attendance. Despite the agency's (Postal Service) argument that the Board lacked jurisdiction over the appeal and therefore lacked jurisdiction to compel payment of witness fees, the Board has jurisdiction to determine jurisdiction, meaning it may hold a hearing to determine jurisdiction and the merits, and that it may require adherence to its procedural regulations throughout the hearing process:

We interpret our regulation as other federal agencies have interpreted it, to require that agency employees who testify at Board hearings be properly compensated for the time necessarily occupied in going to and from the place of attendance. Where, as here, an employee of a federal agency, including an employee of the Postal Service, is a witness at a Board hearing during his non-duty hours, he is entitled to overtime pay he would have received had he rendered service in his regular position with the agency on that day.... Cf. FPM Ch. 630, subch. 10-3.

Holliman v. USPS, 82 MSPR 355, 363 ¶¶ 16-18 (1999), determined that a postal employee's entitlement to expenses for attendance at a Board hearing as a party, as opposed to attendance as a witness, may depend on agency regulations:

The appellant was employed by the agency during the pendency of his appeal and claims the right to reimbursement of his per diem and travel costs in attending the Board's hearing. The agency denies the appellant's right to these expenses on the ground that the Board's regulations only require agencies to reimburse the expenses of employee witnesses in its proceedings, not the expenses of employee appellants. We reject the agency's argument.

Under 5 CFR § 1201.33(a), federal agencies are required to make employees available as witnesses in Board proceedings when the judge orders them to do so. The regulation provides that, when they are appearing at a Board hearing, "Federal employee witnesses will be in official duty status (i.e., entitled to pay and benefits including travel and per diem, where appropriate)." The Board has construed this regulation as applying to currently employed appellants who appear as witnesses in their appeals. White v. Social Security Administration, 76 MSPR 447, 467 n.12 (1997), aff'd, 152 F.3d 948 (Fed. Cir. 1998) (Table). In addition, the Comptroller General has ruled that an employee appellant is in official duty status when attending the hearing in his appeal and is therefore entitled to travel expenses and per diem allowance under the Travel Expenses Act, 5 USC §§ 5702 & 5704. See 31 Comp. Gen. 346 (1952); Gracie Mittelsted, No. B212292, 1984 WL 46740, *1 (C.G.). See also Bureau of Alcohol Tobacco and Firearms v. FLRA, 464 U.S. 89, 106-07, 104 S. Ct. 439, 78 L. Ed.2d 195 (1983). However, as noted above, the Act does not apply to the appellant because he is not employed by a covered agency. See 5 USC § 5701.[7]

[7] Similarly, 5 CFR § 551.424(a) provides that an employee's official time includes time spent adjusting a grievance or other appeal with the employing agency, but this regulation promulgated by the Office of Personnel Management under the FLSA does not apply to employees of the Postal Service like the appellant. See 5 CFR § 551.102.

Thus, the appellant's right to expenses for attendance unrelated to his testifying depends on agency regulations. He has failed to cite an agency regulation entitling him to the expenses that he incurred in attending the hearing as a party. We therefore find that the appellant has shown a right to reimbursement of only the travel and per them expenses to which he is entitled under 5 CFR § 1201.33(a) on the basis of his appearance as a witness.

In re Douglas, 32 MSPR 389, 391 (1987), resolved that a witness should be paid at overtime rates for time spent traveling to and from a hearing when he was not scheduled to work. The witness, a federal employee, was also entitled to commuting and parking expenses. The employee was called as a witness but did not testify because the case was settled. The claim for fees was determined through enforcement proceedings.

Witness fees are governed by 5 CFR 1201.37 (2022), and official time for federal witnesses is governed by 5 CFR 1201.33 (2022). Only a few cases involve complaints by witnesses who were not properly paid for their time. Disputes over entitlement to payment should now be processed under 5 CFR 1201.33 (2022). Before issuance of the regulation in 1997, the accepted procedure from the standpoint of the witness appeared to be an enforcement petition filed with the regional office. The petition was then processed as a "witness fee appeal," a procedure alluded to but not fully described by Board Order 1559.13 (6/30/86). Sapp v. USPS, 73 MSPR 189, 196-97 (1997), described the preregulation procedures for obtaining payment for witness. [Refer to Chapter 2, "Witness Fees; Travel Allowances."]

For a nonfederal witness who would otherwise have to pay his or her way to a hearing, agencies or appellants probably should prepay their expenses if the witness requests prepayment and the agency desires attendance of the witness. Although the agency may believe it can require the attendance of the witness through a subpoena with a promise to pay expenses and witness fee after the hearing, the witness may be unwilling to go to considerable personal expense to appear at the hearing.
