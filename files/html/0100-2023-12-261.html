d. Enforcement of Award Reviewed by MSPB

If the Board denies review of an arbitration award, the Board lacks the authority to enforce compliance with the award. Enforcement authority is available however, if the Board substantially modifies or reverses the award, Cloutterbuck v. Dept. of Labor, 88 MSPR 1, 2-3 ¶¶ 2-5 (2001):

The agency removed the appellant from his GS-11 Longshore Claims Examiner position based on a charge of falsifying official records. The appellant filed a grievance, which went to arbitration. The arbitrator sustained the charge but mitigated the penalty to a time-served suspension, i.e., he ordered the agency to reinstate the appellant to his position but awarded no back pay. Because the appellant raised claims of discrimination based on race, disability, and age, he had a right to request that the Board review the arbitrator's decision. The Board denied his request for review, finding that the appellant did not establish that the arbitrator erred as a matter of law in interpreting civil service law, rule, or regulation. Notwithstanding that disposition, the Board ordered the agency to cancel the removal action and substitute the suspension ordered by the arbitrator. It also ordered the agency to pay back pay, interest on back pay, and other benefits to the extent required by the arbitrator's decision. Clautterbuck v. Department of Labor, 85 MSPR 684 (2000).

The appellant has filed a petition for enforcement of the order included in the Board decision denying his request for review of the arbitrator's decision, asserting that the agency did not provide him with back pay for the period from the date of the arbitrator's decision to the date of his reinstatement, did not credit him with sick or annual leave for that period, and did not continue his benefits during the period of the suspension. The agency has timely responded.

Under 5 U.S.C. §§ 7121(d) and 7702, an employee may request that the Board review an arbitrator's decision regarding a matter appealable to the Board in which the employee raises allegations of prohibited personnel practices as defined by 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(1). See also 5 CFR § 1201.154(d). Furthermore, the Board has the authority to "order any Federal agency or employee to comply with any order or decision issued by the Board under [5 U.S.C. § 1204(a)(1)] and enforce compliance with any such order." 5 U.S.C. § 1204(a)(2).

In Hunter v. Department of the Air Force, 83 MSPR 7 ¶ 10 (1999), the Board found that its authority to order compliance does not extend to decisions of arbitrators. When the Board, in adjudicating a request for review of an arbitration award, substantially modifies or reverses the arbitration decision, it orders compliance by parties only, and not the arbitrator, and provides for enforcement of such compliance. See Robinson v. Department of Health & Human Services, 30 MSPR 389, 399, recons. denied, 31 MSPR 479 (1986). Here however, just as in Hunter, the Board's final decision denied the request for review. Because of that disposition, there is no Board order with which the agency is directed to comply and the Board lacks enforcement authority. Thus, our previous decision in MSPB Docket No. CB-7121-99-0060-V-1 incorrectly included language ordering the agency to comply with the arbitrator's award and informing the appellant of his right to seek compliance before the Board of that arbitrator's award. Also, the previous decision should not have included language informing the appellant of his right to request attorney fees. Because the appellant was not the prevailing party before the Board, we lack authority to award attorney fees on behalf of the appellant and his union. See 5 U.S.C. § 7701(g); 5 CFR § 1201.202(a)(2); Hunter, 83 MSPR 7 ¶¶ 8-9.

Cloutterbach was followed in Newsome v. DOJ, CH-0752-10-0878-I-1 (NP 5/19/2011):

...The Board has jurisdiction to review an arbitrator's decision only when the appellant has alleged discrimination under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(1) in connection with the underlying action. See 5 U.S.C. § 7121(d). This appellant has made no such assertion. Therefore the Board lacks jurisdiction over the arbitration decision. When the Board lacks jurisdiction to review an arbitration award, then it also lacks the authority to enforce compliance with the arbitration decision because there is no Board order to enforce. See Cloutterbuck v. Department of Labor, 88 M.S.P.R. 1, ¶ 5 (2001); see also Hunter v. Department of the Air Force, 83 M.S.P.R. 7, ¶ 10 (1999).
