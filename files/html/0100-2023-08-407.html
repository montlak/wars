1. Falsification of Employment Document

A frequent basis for a negative suitability action is falsification of an employment application, security clearance questionnaire, or a related employment document completed during the application process. To establish unsuitability based on a charge of falsification, OPM must prove by preponderant evidence that the information was incorrect and that the appellant knowingly provided incorrect information with the intent of defrauding. Baker v. OPM, CH-0731-17-0181-I-1 (NP 3/30/2023) (citing Hawes v. OPM, 122 MSPR 341(2015); 5 C.F.R. §§ 731.101(a), 732.202(a), 731.501(b).) OPM, and later MSPB, may be required to consider not only the state of mind of applicants but the clear meaning of questions that applicants are supposed to answer on pain of subsequent dismissal for a false statement. See Grayton v. OPM, 411 Fed. Appx. 328 (Fed. Cir. 2011 NP) (adverse suitability determination sustained based on false answers to preemployment questions asking for past arrest, charge or conviction of an offense); Jones v. OPM (Fed. Cir. 1994 NP No. 94-3158) (falsification of SF-86 "Questionnaire for Sensitive Positions"); Bermudez v. OPM (Fed. Cir. 1994 NP No. 94-3146) (falsification of an employment application was disqualifying); Reed v. OPM, 74 MSPR 616, 622 (1997) ("[T]he Board has consistently found that appellants' intentional misrepresentations in application documents raise serious doubts as to the appellants' honesty and fitness for employment and are sufficient to support negative suitability determinations."); Swift v. OPM, 48 MSPR 441, 445-46 (1991) (false statements in SF-171 concerning credentials: "Neither the regulations, nor the Board's case law, require that an applicant actually obtain a position as a result of a false application for these sanctions to apply"; "the administrative judge correctly found that the appellant's performance in her position was immaterial in reviewing a penalty imposed for pre-employment misconduct"); Morderosian v. OPM, 42 MSPR 371, 375 (1989) (a statement in the SF-171 that the appellant had not quit his job after being notified that he would be fired, when in fact he retired from a prior federal agency that proposed his removal); Forsha v. OPM, 33 MSPR 304, 307-08 (1987) (falsification of employment application and violation of a prior debarment order); Katchmeric v. OPM, 33 MSPR 118, 122 (1987) (falsification of an employment application is grounds for finding the applicant unsuitable; because falsification rendered the appellant unsuitable for federal employment, it was immaterial that AJ did not address the other basis for a suitability disqualification); cf. Weirich v. OPM, AT-0731-15-0629-I-1 (NP 3/18/2016) ("As the administrative judge noted, the appellant was terminated from H.A. Sack just 1 month before he first submitted an inaccurate OF-306 in May 2014, and his termination was still recent when he again attested to inaccurate information in both June and July 2014. We therefore find it implausible that the appellant inadvertently failed to review the accuracy of the forms he was submitting, on multiple occasions, particularly given that he had so recently experienced the significant event of termination."). Patton v. Dept. of Treasury, 94 MSPR 562, 567-70 ¶¶ 12-21 (2003), sustained a negative agency suitability determination based on false statements during the application process:

A suitability BI is directed towards whether the "character or conduct" of a candidate or current employee is such that employing or continuing to employ him would adversely affect the efficiency of the service. 5 C.F.R. §§ 731.101, 731.201; Edwards v. Department of Justice, 87 M.S.P.R. 518 ¶ 5 (2001). Among the many factors that might be relied upon in rendering a negative suitability determination are falsification, deception, or fraud in the examination process, and "[m]isconduct or negligence in prior employment which would have a bearing on efficient service in the position in question." 5 C.F.R. § 731.202(b). An updated BI is similarly focused. In conducting an updated BI, the agency is entitled to examine not just the events that have occurred in the applicant's life since the completion of the last BI but also any inconsistencies between prior and current responses and any new information that comes to light during the updated BI.

In its petition, the agency also asserts that the administrative judge erred in reversing its suitability determination. In an appeal of a negative suitability determination, the agency must demonstrate by preponderant evidence a pattern of conduct that is incompatible with successful performance in the position sought or that would interfere with or prevent the employing agency's effectively performing its duties or responsibilities. See, e.g., Woodward v. Office of Personnel Management, 74 M.S.P.R. 389, 400 (1997). Intentional false statements or deception or fraud in examination or appointment may form a basis for finding an individual unsuitable; the specific intent to make such a false or fraudulent statement may be inferred when the alleged misrepresentation was made with reckless disregard for the truth, and proof of such intent may be based on circumstantial evidence. See Devitto v. Office of Personnel Management, 61 M.S.P.R. 297, 302 (1994). To establish unsuitability based on falsification, the agency must show that the information was incorrect and that the applicant knowingly supplied incorrect information with the intention of defrauding the agency. See, e.g., Naekel v. Department of Transportation, 782 F.2d 975, 977 (Fed. Cir. 1986).

The agency attempted to charge the appellant with both falsification and personal misconduct. Under the personal misconduct charge, however, the agency merely repeated the same specifications of the falsification charge. Further, the agency described the personal misconduct allegedly committed by the appellant only in terms of falsification, inability to be truthful, lack of candor, and failure to provide complete and accurate information. We find, therefore, that the charge of personal misconduct merges into the charge of falsification. See Mann v. Department Of Health & Human Services, 78 M.S.P.R. 1, 7 (1998) (charges merge when they are based on the same misconduct, and when proof of one charge constitutes proof of both charges). Accordingly, the personal misconduct charge cannot serve as a separate basis for a negative suitability determination. See Langham v. U.S. Postal Service, 92 M.S.P.R. 268 ¶¶ 13-15 (2002). Thus, at issue here is whether the agency proved the charge of falsification.

As noted above, the agency charged the appellant with four specifications of falsification. With respect to the first, the evidence shows that the appellant gave a false statement about her prior undetected criminal conduct during two interviews. Specifically, although the appellant answered "no" after being asked whether she had "engaged in any criminal conduct which was never detected by authorities," the evidence shows that she admitted to the APD that she had engaged in criminal conduct not disclosed during either investigation, i.e., that she had committed thefts both when a teenager and again in her twenties. The administrative judge found that there was no evidence of criminal activity in the years between that admitted conduct and the appellant's BI interviews. This fact notwithstanding, the evidence of the appellant's untruthfulness--which occurred during the appellant's recent background investigations--is not in dispute. See generally Leibowitz v. Department of Justice, 88 M.S.P.R. 635 ¶ 10 (2001) (in an appeal of an agency negative suitability determination, it is appropriate to rely on the appellant's admissions during the BI regarding facts with which the appellant is knowledgeable).

The evidence shows, however, that the appellant gave a false statement during the initial BI when she denied being aware that any relative had been involved in smuggling or other criminal activity. As the administrative judge indicated, this specification is not supported by evidence of the 1999 arrest and subsequent guilty plea of the appellant's brother relating to smuggling marijuana from Mexico, since the arrest occurred after the agency completed the first BI. The appellant has admitted, however, that she and her brother were arrested in 1978. Clearly, based on that incident, the appellant was aware that her brother was "suspected to be involved in" some kind of illegal activity, and her denial that she was aware that any relative was suspected of such activity accordingly constituted falsification. See Leibowitz, 88 M.S.P.R. 635 ¶ 10.

Finally, as the administrative judge found, the evidence shows that the appellant falsely answered "no" during the first BI to the question of whether she had ever been dismissed, been forced to resign, or left by mutual agreement from employment. The appellant informed the investigator during the updated BI that she had been terminated in 1980 from Delta Products. See Leibowitz, 88 M.S.P.R. 635 ¶ 10. Further, we concur in the administrative judge's rejection of the appellant's claim that she had been confused about the period of time covered by this question and did not believe that it was intended to elicit a response about dismissals more than 7 years before the date of the BI.

Proof of one or more, but not all, of the specifications supporting a charge is sufficient to support the charge. Blake v. Department of Justice, 81 M.S.P.R. 394 ¶ 26 (1999). Thus, the agency's proof of three of the four specifications of the charge of falsification is sufficient to support that charge.

Further, we find that the agency did not err in determining that the appellant's falsification was such that employing her would adversely affect the efficiency of the service. Although the appellant's admitted thefts and her dismissal from employment were not recent, her false denials regarding those matters were recent. The appellant's falsification offense is enough by itself to uphold the negative suitability determination because it directly implicates her honesty and fitness for employment. See Pappas v. Office of Personnel Management, 76 M.S.P.R. 152, 161 (1997).

Additionally, Customs Inspectors, as law enforcement officers, are held to a higher standard of conduct than other federal employees. See Beck v. Department of Justice, 67 M.S.P.R. 219, 224 (1995). Truth and veracity in the performance of a Customs Inspector's duties and responsibilities, as in the performance of the duties of any law enforcement officer, are critical to fulfilling the requirements of the position and to accomplishing the agency's mission. Moreover, the agency's Code of Conduct requires its employees "to maintain the highest standards of integrity in all that [they] do." The agency is not required to apply lesser standards in adjudicating the suitability of its applicants than it applies to its employees. In sum, the appellant made false statements during the application process for the Customs Inspector position that establish that she is unsuitable for employment with the agency.
