d. Attorney Fees Claim

In a result that would be overruled by a Federal Circuit nonprecedential opinion, the Board apparently determined that it did not have jurisdiction over an arbitrator's award concerning counsel fees if the Board did not also have before it the merits of an award that was otherwise appealable. In Bell v. DOD, 43 MSPR 63, 64-65 (1989) (rev'd, Bell v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1990 NP No. 90-3132), post), involving a discrimination claim, the appellant's denied step increase was restored by an arbitrator, but the arbitrator denied counsel fees, a decision that led to a request for review of the arbitration award. The Board declined jurisdiction over the dispute:

The Board is empowered to exercise its authorities, including its authority to award attorney fees under 5 USC § 7701(g)(1), only in those cases over which it has jurisdiction.... The appellant has the burden of proving by preponderant evidence that the Board has jurisdiction over the appeal. See 5 CFR 1201.56(a)(2)(i) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b)(2)(i)(A)].

Under 5 USC § 7121(d), the Board will review an arbitrator's decision where three criteria are met: (1) The subject matter of the grievance is one over which the Board has jurisdiction; (2) the grievant alleges discrimination under 5 USC § 2302(b)(1); and (3) a final decision has been issued by the arbitrator....

Here, the appellant's attorney seeks review not of the arbitrator's decision on the underlying personnel action, which may have included a reviewable allegation of racial discrimination, under section 7121(d), but only of the arbitrator's determination declining to award attorney fees. Because review under section 7121(d) is not sought, and the merits of the arbitrator's decision are not before the Board, the Board has no subject matter jurisdiction over the appeal. In the absence of such jurisdiction, the Board has no authority to award attorney fees.... See Robinson v. DHHS, 32 MSPR 683, 686 (1987), in which the Board found that it was authorized to award attorney fees to an appellant who had won her appeal to the Board under section 7121(d) when the Board reversed the arbitrator's ruling on the merits of the agency's action against her. In that decision, the Board noted that time reasonably spent outside the Board proceeding is compensable only where that time is "in furtherance of the same goal as the Board proceeding."...Unlike Robinson, in the instant case there was no Board proceeding on the personnel action that was brought before the arbitrator.

The Federal Circuit reversed the Board, Bell v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1990 NP No. 90-3132):

...Bell's claim for attorney fees, which clearly relates to his WGI claim, is integral to his discrimination action. It is not a separate claim that might not be appealable by itself. Thus, Bell does not lose his right to appeal to the Board from the denial of attorney fees because the arbitrator granted the WGI claim Bell contended the agency wrongly withheld. In enacting 5 USC 7701(g)(1) and (g)(2) (1988), which permit the awarding of attorney fees in personnel cases, Congress intended that federal employees not have to bring a separate action for reimbursement. See S. Rep. No. 969, 95th Cong., 2d Sess. 60-61, reprinted in 1978 USCCAN 2723, 2782-83. Accordingly, we conclude the Board has jurisdiction to address the arbitrator's decision regarding attorney fees.

But lacking any claim of discrimination, the Board lacks the authority to review an arbitrator's denial of counsel fees, AFGE Local 1458 v. DOJ, 89 MSPR 120, 122 ¶¶ 4-5 (2001):

Under 5 U.S.C. §§ 7121(d) and 7702, an employee may request that the Board review an arbitrator's decision where the subject matter of the grievance is one over which the Board has jurisdiction, the employee raises a discrimination claim covered by 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(1) in connection with the underlying action, and a final decision has been issued. See Sweeney v. Department of the Army, 69 M.S.P.R. 392, 393 (1996). Here, the subject matter of the grievance, a 30-day suspension, is one over which the Board has jurisdiction, 5 U.S.C. § 7512(2), and the arbitrator has issued a final decision. However, the appellant has raised no claim of discrimination in this proceeding. In the absence of such a claim, the Board lacks jurisdiction over her request for review of the arbitration decision, see McClain v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 58 M.S.P.R. 93, 95 (1993), and it must be dismissed.

Here, the appellant, in fact, prevailed on the merits of her claim before the arbitrator, and seeks from the Board only a finding that she is entitled to attorney fees based on that proceeding. Based on our jurisdictional finding as set forth above, however, the appellant has not prevailed before the Board. Because she is not a prevailing party before the Board, the Board lacks authority to award fees on behalf of the appellant and her union. See Cloutterbuck v. Department of Labor, 88 M.S.P.R. 1 ¶ 5 (2001); Hunter v. Department of the Air Force, 83 M.S.P.R. 7 ¶ 9 (1999).

See Russell v. VA, 108 MSPR 690, 693-94 ¶ 7, 2008 MSPB 111 (2008) (without an assertion of discrimination, there is no basis for the Board to review an arbitrator's award in an adverse action case that denies counsel fees). But cf. Hollingsworth v. Dept. of Commerce, 115 MSPR 636, 639-40 ¶ 9, 2011 MSPB 26 (2011) (establishing a procedural framework for resolving a claim for compensatory damages that was presented to but not resolved by an arbitrator, the Board noted it would consider a fee request if the appellant prevailed in her request for arbitration review) (following remand, Hollingsworth v. Dept. of Commerce, 117 MSPR 327, 2012 MSPB 12 (2012) (awarding pecuniary and nonpecuniary damages)).

If the Board does not otherwise have jurisdiction over an arbitration award, it lacks the authority to compel an arbitrator to rule on a fee petition that is before him or her in a federal sector case, Hunter v. Dept. of Air Force, 83 MSPR 7, 11 ¶¶ 10-11 (1999):

[T]he Board's authority to order compliance and to enforce compliance with its orders is limited to Federal agencies and employees. 5 USC § 1204(a)(2); 5 CFR § 1201.181(a); Melendez v. Puerto Rico National Guard, 70 MSPR 252, 253-54 (1996), review dismissed, 152 F.3d 943, 1998 WL 122145 (Fed. Cir. 1998) (Table).

In the present case, as previously noted, the Board's March 6, 1998 decision was a final decision. See Hunter, 77 MSPR at 596. Because, however, the Board denied the appellant's request for review of the arbitration decision, there was no Board order with which the agency was directed to comply. See id. at 596-98. Thus, there is now no compliance order to enforce against the agency. See 5 USC § 1204(a)(2); 5 CFR § 1201.181(a); cf. Belldina v. Department of Justice, 50 MSPR 497, 502-03 (1991), and Robinson v. Department of Health and Human Services, 30 MSPR 389, 399 (when the Board, in adjudicating a request for review of an arbitration decision, substantially modifies or reverses the arbitration decision, it orders compliance by the parties only, and not the arbitrator, and provides for enforcement of such compliance), recons. denied, 31 MSPR 479 (1986). Because the Board's enforcement authority is limited to Federal agencies and employees, the Board lacks enforcement authority over the arbitrator's award of attorney fees....
