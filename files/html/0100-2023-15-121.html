5. Fear for Personal Safety

An employee who remains away from the work site because of reasonable fear of violence should he cross a picket line, but who does not join or aid the strike, cannot be removed for participation in an unauthorized work stoppage. Jones v. TVA, 10 MSPR 313, 317 (1982). The Board elaborated on this standard, concluding that the failure to report to work should be the result of a threat or other intimidating conduct directed toward the appellant, sufficient to instill in him a reasonable fear of physical danger to himself or others that a person of ordinary firmness would not be expected to resist. Johnson v. FAA, 13 MSPR 652, 660 (1982). The appellant need not challenge direct, continuous, physical threats; he need not provide corroborative evidence of consummated threats against other nonstrikers. Gustin v. FAA, 23 MSPR 199, 204 (1984).

Johnson concluded that the appellant must prove coercion by preponderant evidence. The Board rejected a standard proposed by the agency that would require proof of duress of such a nature as to constitute an affirmative defense to a criminal charge. Also rejected was a less restrictive standard: allegations of menacing tones or language, fear of financial ruin or jeopardy to career advancement, exuberant moral suasion, or any vexation, annoyance or pressure resulting in a "state of mental perturbation," reluctance to act, or a choice of inherently unpleasant situations, do not render involuntary participation in a work stoppage. Statements overheard and generalized threats directed at or related to all potential nonstrikers were insufficient to rebut an agency prima facie case of striking (statements overheard about scabs in the coal fields of West Virginia with bullet holes in them, a statement overheard on a CB radio that petitioner was on the way to the control center and that the speaker would "take care of it"). The test for determining the existence of duress is an objective one based on an assessment of the actions of a reasonable person under like circumstances. The appellant need not test the threat, nor need it be demonstrated that the threats were consummated as to others, although this would be good evidence in support of an appellant's concerns. The seriousness of threats against Johnson were discounted, considered unpersuasive. He did not report them to his supervisor. Seventy of the 120 controllers at his workplace did work, despite the tense atmosphere at the facility.

Johnson was reviewed in Johnson v. Dept. of Transp., 735 F.2d 510, 514 (Fed. Cir. 1984). Considering that the AJ utilized a subjective standard of duress and that the Board rejected that standard in favor of an objective standard of duress, the court stated:

Although the Board framed its standard in terms of how a person of "ordinary firmness" would have reacted to the threats and pressures to which Johnson was subjected, this was a restatement of the traditional "reasonable man" standard that the law frequently applies. That is an objective standard that looks to the behavior of the normal, reasonable person and not to subjective reactions of the particular actor that may result from a variety of circumstances....

Drawing an analogy to a coercion defense in a strike case and an affirmative allegation of duress by an individual seeking to set aside a resignation, the court continued in Johnson, id. at 515:

...In both situations the critical inquiry is whether the employee's action was voluntary or involuntary....

Considering all the circumstances, we cannot say that the Board abused its discretion or committed legal error in adopting the standard it did for evaluating a coercion or duress defense to a charge of striking against the government....

...

...We accept the Board's determination that although the acts that caused Johnson's fear may have made him "emotionally perturbed and reluctant to act as he did," it was not the kind of fear that a "reasonable person could not have been expected to resist."

Martel v. Dept. of Transp., 735 F.2d 504, 509 (Fed. Cir. 1984), offered additional comments concerning duress:

We have held in Johnson v. Dept. of Transp.,...issued today, that the critical inquiry in determining whether an employee's action was coerced is whether that action was voluntary or involuntary, using the objective "reasonable person" test. In using this test, the court must look to the behavior of the normal, reasonable person as opposed to the subjective behavior of a particular individual in particularized circumstances....

Martel contends that it is error to require that the type of coercion directed to the individual consist, in the case of an illegal strike, exclusively of a reasonable fear of physical danger to that person or others. We have not so limited our discussion of coercion in Johnson and do not do so here. To the extent that the board has so limited its definition, we hold that it erred. However..."striking against the government is a grave offense."...(citing Schapansky)...Given this serious situation, objective evidence of the involuntary nature of an employee's strike participation...where such evidence consists of threats of nonphysical harm, must be especially strong. Certainly, Martel's vague and speculative worries of future job harassment, leading to possible job suspension or removal, fall far short of the type of objective evidence which would suffice. A person of ordinary firmness would reasonably be expected to resist such fears, rather than join an illegal strike for which the law provides criminal penalties.

Further commentary on coercion and the Johnson and Martel analyses is provided by Ferrando v. Dept. of Transp., 771 F.2d 489 (Fed. Cir. 1985), and Schultz v. Dept. of Transp., 773 F.2d 296 (Fed. Cir. 1985).
