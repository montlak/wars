2. Individual Regarded or Perceived as Disabled

Southerland v. DOD, 117 MSPR 56, 65-66 ¶¶ 26-27, 2011 MSPB 92 (2011) [replacing the decision at 2011 MSPB 78 (2011)] (overruled on other grounds, Southerland v. DOD, 119 MSPR 566, 2013 MSPB 46 (2013)), explained the concept of an individual who is regarded as having a covered impairment:

The ADAAA defines a disability, in relevant part, as "a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities"[3] or "[b]eing regarded as having such an impairment." 42 U.S.C. § 12102(1)(A), (C); see 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(g)(1)(i), (iii). With respect to the "regarded as having such an impairment" provision, both the ADAAA and the amended regulations explain that "[t]his means that the individual has been subjected to an action prohibited by the ADA as amended because of an actual or perceived impairment that is not both 'transitory and minor.'" [4] 42 U.S.C. § 12102(3); 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(g)(1)(iii); see id., § 1630.2(l)(2).

[3] The amended regulations provide that "[a]n impairment need not prevent, or significantly or severely restrict, the individual from performing a major life activity in order to be considered substantially limiting. Nonetheless, not every impairment will constitute a disability within the meaning of this section." 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(j)(1)(ii). They explain that "the term 'substantially limits' shall be interpreted and applied to require a degree of functional limitation that is lower than the standard for 'substantially limits' applied prior to the ADAAA." Id., § 1630.2(j)(1)(iv).

[4] The Appendix to Part 1630 clarifies that the "transitory and minor" exception to coverage under the "regarded as" prong is a defense to a claim of discrimination but that "this limitation on coverage should be construed narrowly." Appendix to Part 1630, 76 Fed. Reg. at 17,015; see 29 C.F.R. § 1630.15(f).

The amended regulations further explain that

[w] here an individual is not challenging a covered entity's failure to make reasonable accommodations and does not require a reasonable accommodation, it is generally unnecessary to proceed under the "actual disability" or "record of" prongs, which require a showing of an impairment that substantially limits a major life activity or a record of such an impairment. In these cases, the evaluation of coverage can be made solely under the "regarded as" prong of the definition of disability, which does not require a showing of an impairment that substantially limits a major life activity or a record of such an impairment. An individual may choose, however, to proceed under the "actual disability" and/or "record of" prong regardless of whether the individual is challenging a covered entity's failure to make reasonable accommodations or requires a reasonable accommodation.

29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(g)(3) (emphasis added); see id., § 1630.2(j)(2) ("Whether an individual's impairment 'substantially limits' a major life activity is not relevant to coverage under paragraph (g)(1)(iii) (the 'regarded as' prong) of this section.").[5]

The "regarded as" category was expanded on in Camacho v. Dept. of Army, SF-0752-10-0967-I-4 (NP 8/25/2014), concluding it did not apply to a person who is disabled:

...The "regarded as" category covers individuals who are not disabled but who are regarded as disabled by others who have an effect on the individual's employment. See Keown v. Crowell, EEOC Appeal No. 01943171, 1995 WL 517042 at *6 (Aug. 24, 1995). The "regarded as" category applies to those who "do not in fact have the condition which they are perceived as having." Groshans v. Department of the Navy, 67 M.S.P.R. 629, 639 (1995). Here, the appellant consistently argued below that she is disabled, and the administrative judge found that she is disabled. The agency has not challenged that finding by filing its own petition for review, and therefore it is adopted as a finding of the Board. Because the appellant has been found to be a disabled individual, she cannot also claim that she is not disabled but that the agency discriminated against her because it regarded her as disabled. Therefore, to the extent that the administrative judge committed adjudicatory error in failing to consider this claim, any such error did not prejudice the appellant's substantive rights. Panter v. Department of the Air Force, 22 M.S.P.R. 281, 282 (1984).

Analysis of an assertion that the appellant was discriminated against because he was incorrectly regarded as disabled was provided by Caronia v. DOJ, 78 MSPR 201, 209-12 (1998), involving allegedly threatening remarks and applying a mixed-motive analysis:

Nonetheless, the evidence of record shows that the agency erroneously "regarded" the appellant as disabled since it relied on this impermissible motive, its perception that he had a condition that substantially limited the major life activity of working, when it proposed and effected his removal.[2] Even though the investigation into the appellant's statements showed that they were not as they had been characterized to the appellant's subordinate, the agency proposed to discipline the appellant for his remarks. Additionally, the deciding official, who was aware of the appellant's treatment for depressive illness, and that the appellant's treating professionals stated that the appellant was not homicidal, and that the appellant acted appropriately under pressure in the past, testified that he remained "gravely concerned...about what [the appellant] might do under future stressful circumstances, or even any other circumstances[.] " He stated that the statements that the appellant made to his coworkers should have been made only in therapy with his counselor. Additionally, he testified that he believed the appellant's misconduct was in explaining his psychological condition to others, and the effect that telling that he had been in therapy might have on the workplace. The deciding official minimized the appellant's prior outstanding performance evaluations and recommendation from program review for excellent work as a security officer. He also gave no consideration to the fact that the appellant acted appropriately after he had thoughts of harming his superior, by seeking treatment immediately. Additionally, he gave no consideration to the fact that the appellant's depression was controlled and that he had been released to return to work by medical professionals, who expected his performance after his return to work to be at the same level as his past performance. The deciding official testified that he viewed the appellant as not to be trusted around firearms "where the means and opportunity for homicides are immediately present. He also testified that he considered the appellant's potential for rehabilitation "to be nil. He thus treated the appellant as foreclosed generally from performing the duties of a security officer. See Forrisi, 794 F.2d at 935.

[2] The "regarded as" category covers individuals who are not disabled but who, because of attitudes or for any other reason, are regarded as disabled by others who have an effect on the individual securing, retaining, or advancing in employment. Washington v. Brown, EEOC Appeal No. 01953462, slip op. at 3-4 (Nov. 30, 1995); see also Keown v. Crowell, EEOC Appeal No. 01943171, slip op. at 5-6 (Aug. 24, 1995); Yates v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 M.S.P.R. 172, 179 (1996) (no accommodation was required where the appellant was merely regarded as disabled); Groshans v. Department of the Navy, 67 M.S.P.R. 629, 639 (1995) (the "regarded as" category applies to individuals who "do not in fact have the condition which they are perceived as having").

The remarks, referenced above, indicating what entered into the decision-making process regarding the appellant, show that the removal decision was made in reliance on the impermissible motive of the appellant's perceived disability. The evidence of discrimination therefore is tied directly to the alleged discriminatory animus, the perception of the appellant as disabled by his depression and treatment, and constitutes direct evidence of disability discrimination. See Fields v. New York State Office of Mental Retardation and Developmental Disabilities, 115 F.3d 116, 122 (2d Cir. 1997); Ostrowski v. Atlantic Mutual Insurance Cos., 968 F.2d 171, 181 (2d Cir. 1992). Where an appellant establishes direct evidence of discrimination on the basis of disability, to prevail, that is, to show that discrimination on the basis of disability was a motivating factor, he must also show that the agency's admittedly discriminatory action was prohibited, i.e., that he is a qualified disabled person against whom the agency may not discriminate. See Monette v. Electronic Data Systems Corp., 90 F.3d 1173, 1184 (6th Cir. 1996); Clark v. U.S. Postal Service, 74 M.S.P.R. 552, 561 (1997).

A "qualified" disabled person is a disabled person who can perform the essential functions of his position, with or without reasonable accommodation, without endangering the health and/or safety of himself and/or others. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.203(a). The appellant showed that he was a qualified disabled person protected by the discrimination laws because, as discussed above with respect to the charges, the evidence showed that he was capable of performing his job duties without accommodation. Thus, in this case, the appellant established by direct evidence that his perceived disability was an impermissible "motivating factor" in the agency's removal action. The record shows, however, that another factor also motivated the agency's decision, specifically the sustained charge of bringing an unauthorized firearm onto the grounds of a correctional facility. Thus, the appellant's evidence of discrimination based on disability merely establishes that the agency had mixed-motives, both permissible and impermissible, for its decision. See Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins, 490 U.S. 228, 246 (1989).

Under the Rehabilitation Act, the remedies available to an employee who proves discrimination because of his disability are defined by Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964. See 29 U.S.C. § 794a. Under Title VII, a federal employee is entitled to some relief if he proves that his disability was a "motivating factor" in the decision made, "even though other factors also motivated" the agency's decision. See 42 U.S.C. §§ 2000e-2(m), 2000e-16; Jones v. Department of the Army, 68 M.S.P.R. 398, 406 (1995). In other words, a federal employee is entitled to some relief for discrimination on the basis of disability if he shows that the agency had "mixed-motives" for its action.[3]

[3] Section 2000e-2(m) was added to Title VII, effective November 21, 1991. See Civil Rights Act of 1991, Pub. L. No. 102-166, §§ 107(a), 402(a), 1991 U.S.C.C.A.N. (105 Stat.) 1075, 1099. Section 2000e-2(m) amended Title VII to replace the "mixed-motive" framework for analyzing claims of discrimination based on direct evidence set forth in Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins, 490 U.S. 228, 246 (1989), that is, that, even if an employee proves that membership in a protected category was a substantial factor in the decision to discipline, he is not entitled to any remedy if the employer proves that it had a legitimate motive for the action, that standing alone, would have induced it to make the same decision. See Johnson v. Defense Logistics Agency, 61 M.S.P.R. 601, 605 (1994). Section 2000e-2(m) modified the rule of Price Waterhouse to hold the employer "liable once the plaintiff shows that a protected characteristic played a motivating part in the employment decision." See George v. U.S. Postal Service, 74 M.S.P.R. 71, 79-80 (1997); Beam v. Office of Personnel Management, 66 M.S.P.R. 469, 475 n.1 (1995).

The agency may limit the extent of the remedy available to the employee, however, if it proves that it would have made the same decision "in the absence of the impermissible motivating factor," i.e., discrimination on the basis of disability. 42 U.S.C. § 2000e-5(g)(2)(B). In that event, the appellant is not entitled to the remedies of reinstatement, back pay, or damages. Id.; Buchanan v. City of San Antonio, 85 F.3d 196, 200 (5th Cir. 1996); Pedigo v. P.A.M. Transport, Inc., 60 F.3d 1300 (8th Cir. 1995). See also Jones v. Department of the Army, 75 M.S.P.R. 115, 121-24 (1997) (applying the mixed-motive analysis in the context where a federal employee proved race discrimination was a motivating factor in the removal action for misconduct).

We must therefore decide whether the agency met its burden to prove by preponderant evidence that it would have taken the same action absent the impermissible motive. See 42 U.S.C. § 2000e-5(g)(2)(B); 1 Barbara Lindemann and Paul Grossmen, Employment Discrimination Law 41 (3d ed. 1996). In other words, the Board must decide whether the agency met its burden of persuasion to prove that it would have taken the same action based on the sustained charge, which we find was not tainted by the impermissible motive.

See Favreau v. Dept. of Army, SF-0752-11-0273-I-1 n.6 (NP 2/21/2014) ("The Board has held that '[a]n employee who is merely regarded as disabled by the agency does not need accommodation to perform the essential functions of [his] position because [he] "does not actually have...a substantially limiting impairment."' Carter v. Department of Justice, 88 M.S.P.R. 641, ¶ 24 (2001)); see 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(o)(4) (explaining that a covered entity is not required to provide a reasonable accommodation to an individual who meets the definition of disability solely under the 'regarded as' prong).").

Burgess v. Dept. of Interior, 95 MSPR 134, 141 ¶ 20 (2003), determined that:

...Individuals may also qualify as disabled under the "regarded as" definition when an employer mistakenly believes that a person has an impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, or an employer mistakenly believes that an actual, nonlimiting impairment substantially limits one ore more major life activities. [29 C.F.R. 1630.2(i)] Justice, 89 M.S.P.R. 379 ¶ 24. In both of these cases, the employer must entertain misperceptions about the individual. The employer must believe either that the employee has a substantially limiting impairment that the employee, in fact, lacks, or that the employee has a substantially limiting impairment when the impairment is not so limiting. Id.

For accommodation to be required for an individual perceived or regarded as being disabled, the evidence must demonstrate that, by reason of the employer's perception the agency viewed the appellant as foreclosed from performing jobs in his geographical area open to the average person having comparable training, skills, and abilities in the appellant's geographical area, Carter v. DOJ, 88 MSPR 641, 652-54 ¶¶ 23-27 (2001):

With respect to the "regarded as" definition of disability, the AJ, in finding that the agency regarded the appellant as having a substantial impairment, relied entirely on the fact that the "removal action was based on a conclusion that the appellant's back problem rendered him unfit to work as a Supervisory Border Patrol Agent." However, the fact that an employee was terminated as a result of his medical condition is not sufficient alone to establish that the employer regarded him as being substantially impaired in the life activity of working. See Moore, 221 F.3d at 952-55; Thompson v. Holy Family Hospital, 121 F.3d 537, 541 (9th Cir. 1997); Wooten v. Farmland Foods, 58 F.3d 382, 386 (8th Cir. 1995).

In addition, the record does not support a finding that the agency regarded the appellant as disabled and that it discriminated against him based on his disability by denying his request for reasonable accommodation. Individuals may qualify as disabled under the "regarded as" definition when: (1) An employer mistakenly believes that a person has an impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities; or (2) an employer mistakenly believes that an actual, nonlimiting impairment substantially limits one or more major life activities. Sutton, 119 S. Ct. at 2149-50. "In both cases, it is necessary that [the employer] entertain misperceptions about the individual--it must believe either that one has a substantially limiting impairment that one does not have or that one has a substantially limiting impairment when, in fact, the impairment is not so limiting." Id. at 2150. Thus, "[A]n employee who is merely regarded as disabled by the agency does not need accommodation to perform the essential functions of [his] position because [he] 'does not actually have...a substantially limiting impairment."' McFadden v. Department of Defense, 85 MSPR 18 ¶ 17 (1999). Therefore, once the AJ found that the appellant did not have a substantially limiting impairment and that the agency perceived him as substantially impaired, she should not have proceeded to address the reasonable accommodation issue and find that the agency discriminated against the appellant based on his perceived disability by denying his request for reasonable accommodation. Id.

The evidence, here, shows that the agency at most merely regarded the appellant's chronic back problem as foreclosing him from performing the duties of an LEO eligible for LEO retirement coverage under the Federal Employees' Retirement System, see 5 U.S.C. §§ 8407(17), 8412(d), 8422(a)(2)(B), or the Civil Service Retirement System, see 5 U.S.C. ¶¶ 8331(20), 8336(c). The appellant has not presented sufficient evidence showing that an alleged physical inability to perform the duties of a covered LEO position significantly restricts his ability to perform a class of jobs in his geographical area as compared to the average person having his comparable training, skills, and abilities. Thus, he has not established that the agency perceived him as substantially limited in the life activity of working. See 29 CFR § 1630.2(j)(3)(i); cf. Murphy, 119 S. Ct. at 2138-39 (a mechanic did not prove that he was regarded as substantially limited in the major life activity of working by reason of his dismissal for inability to obtain Department of Transportation certification where he did not present any evidence that he was unable to perform any mechanic job that did not require such certification, he had performed mechanic jobs that did not require such certification for over 22 years and, in fact, had secured another job as a mechanic shortly after leaving his employer); Shipley v. City of University City, 195 F.3d 1020, 1023 (8th Cir. 1999) (firefighter was not regarded as substantially limited in the major life activity of working where he did not submit evidence that he was regarded as unable to perform other jobs besides that of firefighter). To the extent that prior Board decisions suggest that an appellant can establish that the agency regarded him as substantially limited in the major life activity of working without showing that the agency viewed him as foreclosed from performing jobs in his geographical area open to the average person having his comparable training, skills, and abilities in his geographical area,[5] they are hereby overruled.

[5] See, e.g., Caronia v. Department of Justice, 78 MSPR 201, 209-10 (1998) (Supervisory Security Officer was regarded as substantially limited in working because the agency erroneously regarded him as unable to be trusted around firearms as a result of his depression and, thus, as foreclosed generally from performing the duties of a Security Officer); Sargent v. Department of the Air Force, 55 MSPR 387, 395 (1992) (Firefighter was regarded as substantially limited in working because the agency erroneously regarded his medical conditions as creating a risk of serious injury to him and preventing him from safely performing all of the duties of a Firefighter); Stanley v. Department of Justice, 48 MSPR 1, 7 (1991) (Correctional Officer was regarded as substantially limited in working because his work background consisted virtually exclusively of jobs in the field of law enforcement and the agency's reasons for regarding him as foreclosed from employment as a Correctional Officer would "make it difficult, if not impossible, for the appellant to secure employment elsewhere consistent with his job expectations and training").

The appellant reasserts on petition for review his argument that he was disabled because he had a record of an impairment that substantially limited his major life activity of working. An employee satisfies this definition of disability if he "has a history of, or has been misclassified as having, a mental or physical impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities." 29 CFR § 1630.2(k). The intent of the "record of" method of proving disability is "to ensure that people are not discriminated against because of a history of a disability...[F]or example,...former cancer patients...based on their prior medical history...[and] individuals misclassified as learning disabled...." 29 CFR part 1630, app., Interpretive Guidance on Title I of the Americans with Disabilities Act.

To support his argument that he was disabled based on a record of substantially limiting condition, the appellant merely relies on the fact that he was removed for his physical inability to perform the duties of his position and the documentary evidence in the record addressing his removal. We find that such evidence and argument are insufficient to show that a record relied on by the agency indicated that he was substantially impaired in the life activity of working.

Harris v. Dept. of Air Force, 100 MSPR 452, 455-58 ¶¶ 5-9 (2005), concluded that an agency did not discriminate against an individual by reason of a perception of a disability if the agency removed that individual for physical inability to perform a position that the employee contended he could perform:

Where, as here, the record is complete and a hearing has been held, the Board will examine whether an appellant has proven his discrimination claim by preponderant evidence. See Justice v. Department of the Navy, 89 M.S.P.R. 379, ¶ 22 (2001). An appellant who raises a claim of disability discrimination must first establish that he is a disabled person entitled to protection of the disability discrimination laws. Id., ¶¶ 21-22. A disabled person is one who has a "physical or mental impairment" that "substantially limits" one or more "major life activities," who has a record of such an impairment, or who is regarded as having such an impairment. 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(g); Fraser v. Department of Agriculture, 95 M.S.P.R. 72, ¶ 11 (2003). These terms must be interpreted strictly to create "a demanding standard for qualifying as disabled." Burgess v. Department of the Interior, 95 M.S.P.R. 134, ¶¶ 23-24 (2003) (quoting Toyota Motor Mfg., Kentucky, Inc. v. Williams, 534 U.S. 184, 197 (2002)). The phrase "substantially limits" precludes impairments that interfere with major life activities in only a minor way. Id., ¶ 23. The term "major life activities" refers to "'activities that are of central importance to daily life.'" Id. (quoting Toyota, 534 U.S. at 197). The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission's regulations define "major life activities" as "functions such as caring for oneself, performing manual tasks, walking, seeing, hearing, speaking, breathing, learning, and working." 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(i); Fraser, 95 M.S.P.R. 72, ¶ 12. A medical diagnosis of an impairment, by itself, is not sufficient to prove disability; an appellant must offer evidence "that the extent of the limitation caused by the impairment is substantial, in terms of the employee's own experience." Burgess, 95 M.S.P.R. 134, ¶¶ 23-25.

The appellant did not argue below that he actually had, or had a record of having, a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more of his major life activities. Rather, the appellant claimed that the agency mistakenly believed that he had such an impairment, because the agency removed him based on its erroneous perception that the impairments identified in his July 2002 fitness for duty examinations rendered him physically incapable of performing the duties of his position. An appellant may qualify as disabled under the "regarded as" definition when: (1) an employer mistakenly believes that the appellant's actual, nonlimiting impairment substantially limits one or more major life activities; (2) the appellant's actual, nonlimiting impairment substantially limits a major life activity only as a result of the employer's attitude toward the impairment; or (3) an employer mistakenly treats the appellant as having an impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities even though the appellant has no impairment. 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(l); Fraser, 95 M.S.P.R. 72, ¶ 11.

According to the physicians who conducted the appellant's fitness for duty examinations in July 2002 that were the basis for his removal, the appellant had the following physical limitations, primarily attributable to weakness and instability in his right knee: walking continuously for four hours; repeated bending for two hours; negotiating slippery or uneven walking surfaces; working on ladders or scaffolding; and climbing up to 50 feet. Dr. Petrofsky and Dr. Ellison concluded that because of these limitations, the appellant was physically incapable of safely performing the duties of his position, which required, inter alia, conducting on-site inspections of various utilities facilities under his supervision, walking on slippery and uneven surfaces around a water reservoir, climbing ladders and scaffolding, and bending and stooping in confined spaces and around machinery. The physicians agreed, however, that the appellant's limitations were not necessarily permanent, and the appellant could meet the physical requirements of his position if he "implements use of a knee brace, loses weight, increases his endurance, and engages in regular strengthening exercises." The record shows that the appellant's physical condition did improve, as he was found to be physically capable of performing the duties of his position following another fitness for duty examination in February 2003.

We find that the impairments identified in the July 2002 fitness for duty examinations are not substantial limitations of a major life activity and that the agency did not perceive them to be substantial limitations of a major life activity. While the identified impairments arguably impact the major life activity of "walking," we find that the appellant's impairments did not "substantially limit" his walking or any other major life activity. The appellant did not argue, let alone establish, that his impairments were severe or significantly restricted him in the performance of any major life activity compared to the average person in the general population. See 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(j)(1)(ii), (j)(2)(i). Further, the medical evidence established that the impact of his physical impairments was not permanent. See 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(j)(2)(iii); Kinsey v. Department of the Navy, 59 M.S.P.R. 226, 231 & n.4 (1993) ("A person is not considered an individual with handicap(s) if he suffers from a transitory illness or injury, no matter how severe, that has no permanent effect on his health."); see also Toyota, 534 U.S. at 198 (to be substantially limiting in performing manual tasks, an impairment's impact must be "permanent or long term").

The appellant's claim that he was regarded as disabled is based solely on the agency's determination that he was physically incapable of safely performing the duties of his particular position, and such a determination is insufficient to support a claim of disability discrimination. [S] ee Justice, 89 M.S.P.R. 379, ¶ 27; Carter v. Department of Justice, 88 M.S.P.R. 641, ¶ 23 (2001). The agency's awareness of the existence of an impairment alone is not a sufficient basis to support a finding of perceived discrimination. Bullock v. Department of the Air Force, 88 M.S.P.R. 531, ¶ 12 (2001), review dismissed, 32 Fed. Appx. 538 (Fed. Cir. 2002). The fact that an employee was terminated as a result of his medical condition is not sufficient alone to establish that the employer regarded him as being substantially impaired in the major life activity of working. Carter, 88 M.S.P.R. 641, ¶ 23.

See Fraser v. USDA, 95 MSPR 72, 77 ¶ 13 (2003) ("An agency's perception that an employee is unable to perform his duties while working with a particular coworker or at a particular location does not establish that the agency regarded the employee as substantially limited in the major life activity of working."); Justice v. Dept. of Navy, 89 MSPR 379, 392 ¶ 25 (2001) (restating Carter analysis); Vyas v. Dept. of Army, 83 MSPR 452, 455 ¶ 8 (1999) ("It is not enough to show that the agency regarded the appellant as foreclosed from a particular job; the agency must regard the impairment as substantially limiting employment generally.").

For an individual to be regarded as disabled, the disability perceived by the employer must constitute a disability under law, meaning that the disability must foreclose a line of work rather than acceptable performance in a given job, Pugh v. USPS, 81 MSPR 313, 322 (1999):

To prove that the agency "regarded" him as disabled, the appellant must establish that the agency viewed him as having an impairment that constituted a significant barrier to employment, in that it foreclosed generally the type of employment involved. Yates, 70 MSPR at 178. It is not enough to show that the agency regarded the appellant as foreclosed from a particular job, or from a particular place; the agency must regard the impairment as substantially limiting employment generally. See id.; Groshans v. Department of the Navy, 67 MSPR 629, 636 (1995).

Aldrup v. Caldera, 274 F.3d 282, 287 (5th Cir. 2001), involving a mixed case appeal originating with the Department of Army, noted the significance of the agency's misperception of an impairment and its effect in a perceived disability case:

An individual is "regarded as" disabled when a covered entity mistakenly believes that: (1) a person has a physical impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities; or (2) an actual, non-limiting impairment substantially limits one or more major life activities. In both situations, the covered entity must entertain some misperception regarding the individual--either that he has a substantially limiting impairment that he does not have or the impairment is not so limiting as believed....

Lacking the requisite misperception of the appellant as a disabled individual, the appellant is not likely to make out a claim that he was erroneously regarded as a disabled employee, Wentz v. USPS, 91 MSPR 176, 181-83 ¶¶ 9-12 (2002):

An individual may also show that he was the victim of disability discrimination if he was regarded as disabled. An individual may qualify as disabled under the "regarded as" definition when: (1) An employer mistakenly believes that a person has an impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities; or (2) an employer mistakenly believes that an actual, nonlimiting impairment substantially limits one or more major life activities. Justice v. Department of the Navy, 89 M.S.P.R. 379 ¶ 24 (2001); Carter v. Department of Justice, 88 M.S.P.R. 641 ¶ 24 (2001) (citing Sutton v. United Air Lines, Inc., 527 U.S. 471, 489, 119 S. Ct. 2139, 144 L. Ed.2d 450 (1999)). In both cases, it is necessary that the employer entertain misperceptions about the individual--either that the individual has a substantially limiting impairment that he does not have or that he has a substantially limiting impairment when, in fact, the impairment is not so limiting. Carter v. Department of Justice, 88 M.S.P.R. 641 at ¶ 24; (quoting Sutton, 527 U.S. at 489, 119 S. Ct. 2139).

...According to the appellant, the agency removed him because the proposing and deciding officials perceived him "as an employee who could not regularly work the overtime required by the chronically understaffed office." The appellant observes that, as a result of his medical condition, he frequently took leave, and in the ten months before the vehicle accident, letter carriers in his office were working ten hour days, six days a week.

The record does not support the appellant's allegation that he was regarded as disabled. There is no evidence that the agency officials believed that the appellant was unable to perform the duties of his position and he admitted in his testimony that he frequently worked overtime prior to his removal. Furthermore, while the appellant does not specifically identify any major life activity that his medical condition affects, his arguments focus on work-related problems (absences from work and an inability to work overtime), and thus it appears that his argument is that his medical condition interferes with the major life activity of work.

To establish that an individual was regarded as disabled from performing the major life activity of work, it must be shown that the employer believed that the individual was unable to perform a broad range of jobs compared to the average person having comparable training, skills, and abilities. Justice, 89 M.S.P.R. 379 ¶ 25; Bullock v. Department of the Air Force, 88 M.S.P.R. 531 ¶ 13 (2001). There is no indication here that the agency regarded the appellant as unable to perform the duties of his Postal Service position, let alone that he was unable to perform a broad range of jobs as compared to the average person having comparable training, skills, and abilities.

Accordingly, the appellant has failed to show that the agency regarded him as disabled.

Use of the language "reasonable accommodation" in communications with an employee does not demonstrate that the agency regarded an employee as disabled if the agency neither regarded the employee as disabled nor treated her as disabled, Groshans v. Dept. of Navy, 67 MSPR 629, 638-39 (1995):

The appellant asserts in her petition for review that the agency should be "estopped" from denying that she is a disabled employee because it "treated her situation" as one involving a disability that required reasonable accommodation. The appellant asserts that the agency referred to accommodating her on the job as necessary, that on April 18, 1994, the agency held a meeting with the appellant to discuss "reasonable accommodation," that the agency Employee Relations Specialist issued a memorandum discussing proposed accommodation of the appellant's condition in terms of "reasonable accommodation," and that the proposed removal notice referred to the agency's being "unable to accommodate" the appellant's condition. Again, even assuming that the agency considered its efforts to deal with the appellant's condition as an accommodation to her anaphylaxis, such fact does not establish that the agency regarded her as disabled.

The EEOC regulations supply the following definition of "regarded" as disabled:

Is regarded as having such an impairment means has a physical or mental impairment that does not substantially limit major life activities but is treated by an employer as constituting such a limitation; has a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits major life activities only as a result of the attitude of such employer towards such impairment; or has none of the impairments defined in paragraph (a)(2) of this section but is treated by an employer as having such an impairment.

See 29 CFR 1614.203(a)(5).

The reference to those who are regarded as having an impairment is intended to apply to those individuals who "do not in fact have the condition which they are perceived as having." See S. Rep. No. 1297, 93d Cong., 2d Sess. (1974), reprinted in 1974 USCCAN 6373, 6389-90. In such an inquiry, "the focus is on the employer's state of mind." See McKinley v. Dept. of the Army, EEOC Petition No. 01933326, slip op. at 8, 1994 WL 745021 at * 5 (Sept. 8, 1994) ("[a]n employer regards an employee as substantially limited in working if it thinks the individual has an impairment that significantly restricts him or her from currently performing a class of jobs or [a] broad range of jobs in various classes."). See also Chandler v. City of Dallas, 2 F.3d 1385, 1391-93 (5th Cir. 1993) (the city's belief that employees were unable to perform driving tasks with an adequate safety margin did not establish per se that the city regarded such employees as "handicapped" under the Rehabilitation Act), cert. denied, __U.S.__, 114 S. Ct. 1386, 128 L. Ed.2d 61 (1994); Byrne, 979 F.2d at 567 (the courts have uniformly held "that an employer does not necessarily regard an employee as handicapped simply by finding the employee to be incapable of satisfying the singular demands of a particular job" (citing Forrisi v. Bowen, 794 F.2d 931, 934 (4th Cir. 1986)).

As in the court cases cited above, the record here is clear that the agency did not doubt the appellant's ability to perform as a Contract Specialist and, in fact, she was always given satisfactory or above-satisfactory performance ratings; instead, it doubted her ability to function in the physical environment of CM-3 and offered her the opportunity to work in a temporary location so that she would be safe. While there is no dispute that it sometimes employed the terminology of reasonable accommodation in its dealings with the appellant on this matter, there is no showing that the agency regarded the appellant as disabled or treated her as such. It merely responded to her self-identified respiratory impairment. Cf. Carr v. Dept. of Defense, 61 MSPR 172, 179-80 (1994) (seeking additional medical information and statement that "[s]uch information is also necessary for management to determine how best to reasonably accommodate qualified handicapped employees" merely shows that agency was determining the appellant's status and what action it might take next).

In Sutton v. United Airlines, Inc., 119 S. Ct. 2139, 2149-50 (1999), the Supreme Court decided that individuals who claim to be regarded as being disabled must demonstrate an error by the employer in assessing the disability of an individual or the job requirements of a position:

...Under subsection (C), individuals who are "regarded as" having a disability are disabled within the meaning of the ADA. See § 12102(2)(C). Subsection (C) provides that having a disability includes "being regarded as having," § 12102(2)(C), "a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more of the major life activities of such individual," § 12102(2)(A). There are two apparent ways in which individuals may fall within this statutory definition: (1) a covered entity mistakenly believes that a person has a physical impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, or (2) a covered entity mistakenly believes that an actual, nonlimiting impairment substantially limits one or more major life activities. In both cases, it is necessary that a covered entity entertain misperceptions about the individual--it must believe either that one has a substantially limiting impairment that one does not have or that one has a substantially limiting impairment when, in fact, the impairment is not so limiting....

As to the second classification of individuals regarded as disabled, that is, an employer's erroneous judgment that a nonlimiting impairment limits the ability to work, Sutton determined that the employee must prove that the limitation perceived by the employer prevents the employee from working in a class of positions rather than a particular job before the employer's decisions rise to the level of disability discrimination. In Sutton, the plaintiffs, applicants for global commercial airline pilot positions, were turned down by United Airlines because they did not meet uncorrected visual acuity requirements. The applicants complained that myopia constituted a disability and that the employer discriminated against them because their vision with correction equaled the employer's requirement for uncorrected vision. Disqualification for myopia did not rise to the level of covered discrimination because it did not foreclose a class of employment opportunity. For the same reason, the impairment would not support a finding that the employer erroneously regarded the applicants as disabled. Under Sutton, id. at 2150-51:

There is no dispute that petitioners are physically impaired. Petitioners do not make the obvious argument that they are regarded due to their impairments as substantially limited in the major life activity of seeing. They contend only that respondent mistakenly believes their physical impairments substantially limit them in the major life activity of working. To support this claim, petitioners allege that respondent has a vision requirement, which is allegedly based on myth and stereotype. Further, this requirement substantially limits their ability to engage in the major life activity of working by precluding them from obtaining the job of global airline pilot, which they argue is a "class of employment." In reply, respondent argues that the position of global airline pilot is not a class of jobs and therefore petitioners have not stated a claim that they are regarded as substantially limited in the major life activity of working.

Standing alone, the allegation that respondent has a vision requirement in place does not establish a claim that respondent regards petitioners as substantially limited in the major life activity of working.... By its terms, the ADA allows employers to prefer some physical attributes over others and, to establish physical criteria. An employer runs afoul of the ADA when it makes an employment decision based on a physical or mental impairment, real or imagined, that is regarded as substantially limiting a major life activity. Accordingly, an employer is free to decide that physical characteristics or medical conditions that do not rise to the level of an impairment--such as one's height, build, or singing voice--are preferable to others, just as it is free to decide that some limiting, but not substantially limiting, impairments make individuals less than ideally suited for a job.

Considering the allegations of the amended complaint in tandem, petitioners have not stated a claim that respondent regards their impairment as substantially limiting their ability to work. The ADA does not define 'substantially limits," but "substantially" suggests "considerable" or "specified to a large degree."... The EEOC has codified regulations interpreting the term "substantially limits" in this manner, defining the term to mean "[u]nable to perform" or is significantly restricted." See 29 CFR §§ 1630.2(j)(1)(i), (ii) (1998).

When the major life activity under consideration is that of working, the statutory phrase "substantially limits" requires, at a minimum, that plaintiffs allege they are unable to work in a broad class of jobs. Reflecting this requirement, the EEOC uses a specialized definition of the term "substantially limits" when referring to the major life activity of working:

"significantly restricted in the ability to perform either a class of jobs or a broad range of jobs in various classes as compared to the average person having comparable training, skills and abilities. The inability to perform a single, particular job does not constitute a substantial limitation in the major life activity of working." § 1630.2(j)(3)(i).

The EEOC further identifies several factors that courts should consider when determining whether an individual is substantially limited in the major life activity of working, including the geographical area to which the individual has reasonable access, and "the number and types of jobs utilizing similar training, knowledge, skills or abilities, within the geographical area, from which the individual is also disqualified." §§ 1630.2(j)(3)(H)(A), (B). To be substantially limited in the major life activity of working, then, one must be precluded from more than one type of job, a specialized job, or a particular job of choice. If jobs utilizing an individual's skills (but perhaps not his or her unique talents) are available, one is not precluded from a substantial class of jobs. Similarly, if a host of different types of jobs are available, one is not precluded from a broad range of jobs.

The perception by an agency manager of a lack of emotional stability of an appellant does not establish a disability if there is no evidence that supervisors or managers viewed the appellant as being foreclosed generally from her line of work. See Stenmark v. Dept. of Transp., 59 MSPR 462, 470 (1993); Schulte v. Dept. of Air Force, 50 MSPR 126, 129 (1991) (an employee's inability to meet the demands of particular job because of an impairment does not necessarily mean the employee is disabled; the impairment must foreclose generally the type of employment involved). [Refer to Chapter 6, "Adverse Action Related to Disability."]

That the employer incorrectly perceives the employee as disabled, and unjustifiably fires him, does not mean that the employer discriminated on the basis of disability unless the employee's health problems generally foreclosed employment. Bullock v. Dept. of Air Force, 88 MSPR 531, 534-39 ¶¶ 7-19 (2001), explained the relation of disability discrimination and foreclosure of a broad class of employment:

An agency may remove an employee from the federal service if he is unable, because of a medical condition, to perform the duties of his position. See Ellshoff v. Department of the Interior, 76 MSPR 54, 68-69 (1997). Here, the administrative judge found that the agency failed to meet its burden of showing that the appellant was medically unfit for duty. After a thorough review of the evidence and argument raised regarding this issue, including the extensive hearing testimony, we find that the agency's petition for review on this issue fails to show error in the initial decisions. Additionally, the administrative judge did not sustain the separate charge of excessive absenteeism, and the agency's petition for review does not challenge that determination. Accordingly, we affirm this aspect of that decision.

In finding that the agency discriminated against the appellant based upon a perceived disability, the administrative judge reasoned that the agency removed the appellant because it believed that he suffered from a psychological condition that precluded him from performing not only his former Supervisory High Voltage Electrician position, but high voltage electrical work generally. She further found that the appellant was performing his duties satisfactorily until he was reassigned, and, therefore, he was a qualified disabled person. Finally, she concluded that, since the agency removed the appellant because it regarded him as unfit for duty but did not articulate a legitimate nondiscriminatory reason for its action, the removal was based on a perceived disability.

On review, the agency disagrees with these findings and argues that under the Supreme Court's decisions in Sutton v. United Airlines, Inc., 527 U.S. 471, 119 S. Ct. 2139, 144 L. Ed.2d 450 (1999), and Murphy v. United Parcel Service, Inc., 527 U.S. 516, 119 S. Ct. 2133, 144 L. Ed.2d 484 (1999), the appellant was not regarded or perceived as disabled because he was not excluded from a broad range of jobs. The appellant responds that the agency's reliance on Sutton and Murphy is misplaced because the agency has precluded him from electrician jobs. We agree with the agency that the administrative judge's focus was too narrow.

After correctly stating the elements of a prima facie case of disability discrimination under Yates v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 MSPR 172, 178-79 (1996), the administrative judge found that:

the agency viewed the appellant [ ] as having a condition which precluded him from working. Furthermore, the record reflects that the agency viewed the appellant as unable to perform not only his high voltage electrician job but any other high voltage electrician job as well. I, therefore, find...that the agency regarded him as being disabled in that it thought he had an impairment that foreclosed generally the type of employment involved, i.e., high voltage electrical work.

The administrative judge then found that the appellant was a qualified disabled employee and that he was removed based on a perceived disability. This analysis is defective.

First, the analysis fails to determine whether the agency believed the appellant had a substantially limiting impairment. The administrative judge held that the appellant did not suffer a "delusional disorder" as the agency's medical witness found, and we find no reason to disturb that conclusion. However, the record is clear (and the parties agree) that the appellant suffered some type of mental disorder, generally described as an "adjustment disorder." There is no indication in the record that the appellant's disorder, by whatever name, is of such permanence or scope as to rise to a disability under the statute.

Thus, the question was not, as the administrative judge believed, whether the agency believed merely that the appellant had a "condition which precluded him from working." That analysis skipped the crucial inquiry concerning whether the agency mistakenly believed that an actual, nonlimiting impairment substantially limited one or more major life activities. Sutton, 119 S. Ct. at 2150. The agency's awareness of the existence of an impairment alone is not a sufficient basis to support a discrimination finding. See, e.g., Hrobowski v. Henderson, Appeal No. 01973951 (EEOC Aug. 31, 2000) (agency official's affidavit that he was "aware of her disability," by itself, did not mean that the agency regarded the complainant as having an impairment that substantially limited a major life activity); see also Sutton, 119 S. Ct. at 2150[3]; Moore v. J.B. Hunt Transport, Inc., 221 F.3d 944, 954 (7th Cir. 2000).

[3] Although Sutton and Murphy interpreted the Americans with Disabilities Act, that Act's standards have been incorporated by reference into the Rehabilitation Act, which applies to federal entities. 29 U.S.C. § 791(g); McFadden v. Department of Defense, 85 MSPR 18, T 17 (1999).

"Major life activity" refers to functions such as caring for oneself, performing manual tasks, walking, seeing, hearing, speaking, learning, and working. 29 CFR § 1630.2(i); see also Sutton, 119 S. Ct. at 2145. For the major life activity of working, which is the focus of this appeal,[4] "substantially limits" means that an individual is "significantly restricted in the ability to perform either a class of jobs or a broad range of jobs in various classes as compared to the average person having comparable training, skills and abilities."[5] 29 CFR § 1630.2(j)(3); see Vyas v. Department of the Army, 83 MSPR 452 ¶ 10 (1999).

[4] According to the Interpretive Guidance on Title I of the Americans with Disabilities Act issued by the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, an employer or an investigator should examine whether an impairment limits an individual's ability to work only after determining that the impairment does not limit other major life activities, such as performing manual tasks, seeing, hearing, breathing, walking, or caring for one's self. See 29 CFR Part 1630 App. (explaining 29 CFR § 1630.2(j)); see also 29 CFR § 1614.203(a)(3) (giving examples of "major life activities").

[5] More generally, the term "substantially limits" means, among other things, that an individual is "[u]nable to perform a major life activity that the average person in the general population can perform"; or that the individual is "[s]ignificantly restricted as to the condition, manner, or duration under which the individual can perform a particular major life activity as compared to the condition, manner, or duration under which the average person in the general population can perform the same major life activity." 29 CFR § 1630.2(j); see also Sutton, 119 S. Ct. at 2145; Vyas v. Department of the Army, 83 MSPR 452 ¶ 10 (1999).

The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission's regulations enumerate several general factors to consider in determining whether an individual is substantially limited in a major life activity. See 29 CFR § 1630.2(j)(2).[6] In Sutton, the Supreme Court summarized the principles articulated in these regulations as follows:

To be substantially limited in the major life activity of working, then, one must be precluded from more than one type of job, a specialized job, or a particular job of choice. If jobs utilizing an individual's skills (but perhaps not his or her unique talents) are available, one is not precluded from a substantial class of jobs. Similarly, if a host of different types of jobs are available, one is not precluded from a broad range of jobs.

Sutton, 119 S. Ct. at 2150; see also Murphy, 119 S. Ct. at 2138-39 (the employee was not substantially limited where he was at most precluded from performing a small subset of mechanic positions).[7]

[6] Title 29 Code of Federal Regulations, section 1630.2(j)(2), states that factors to be considered are: 1) the nature and severity of the impairment; 2) the duration or expected duration of the impairment; and 3) the permanent or long term impact or the expected permanent or long term impact of or resulting from the impairment. In addition to the factors outlined above, the following factors are relevant in determining whether an individual is substantially limited in the major life activity of working: 1) the geographical area to which the individual has access; 2) the job from which the individual has been disqualified because of an impairment; 3) the number and types of jobs utilizing similar training, knowledge, skills or abilities within the geographical area from which the individual is disqualified because of the impairment; and/or 4) the job from which the individual has been disqualified because of an impairment and the number and types of other jobs not utilizing similar training, knowledge, skills, or abilities within the geographic area from which the individual is also disqualified because of the impairment. 29 CFR § 1630.2(j)(3)(ii). The regulations specifically state that to be regarded as substantially limited in the major life activity of working, one must be regarded as precluded from more than a particular job. 29 CFR § 1630.2(j)(3)(i).

[7] Although not binding upon the Board, we may look to other jurisdictions for guidance. Brown v. Department of Health & Human Services 50 MSPR 523, 529 (1991). Since the issuance of Sutton and Murphy in June 1999, several circuit courts of appeal have similarly addressed the meaning of the "substantially limited" provision. See, e.g., Moore, 221 F.3d at 953-54 (an individual trained as a bus driver was not substantially limited in the major life activity of working by medical condition restricting his exposure to cold and damp environments; employment not generally limited and he was also actually employed as a charter bus driver); Sorensen v. University of Utah Hospital, 194 F.3d 1084, 1087 (10th Cir. 1999) (a nurse was not substantially limited in the major life activity of working where her employer precluded her from working as a flight nurse because of a medical condition, but allowed her to work as a nurse in other areas of the hospital); Shipley v. City of University City, 195 F.3d 1020, 1022 (8th Cir. 1999) (a former firefighter seeking reinstatement was not regarded as disabled because he was able to perform other jobs, e.g., as a car wash attendant, salesman, dry cleaner, and dish washer repairman). The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission has also issued numerous decisions since the Supreme Court issued its decisions in Sutton and Murphy, applying the principle that to be substantially limited in a major life activity an individual must be precluded from more than one job. See, e.g., Buckler v. Mahle, Appeal No. 03990123 (EEOC Sept. 8, 2000) (an individual was not substantially limited with regard to working where, although unable to perform as a communications specialist, he had successfully performed as an editor and project manager for the same agency); Flynn Banigan v. Reno, Appeal No. 01973401 (EEOC Aug. 3, 2000) (an applicant for a Border Patrol Agent position was not substantially limited by the agency's vision requirements because she had a variety of skills and there were various positions that utilized those skills).

In the instant case, as discussed above, the appellant was first reassigned from his High Voltage Electrician position to an Engineering Assistant position and then, after he was absent for a long period of time, the agency ordered him to undergo a psychological fitness for duty exam. Thereafter, the agency proposed the appellant's removal based on his medical unfitness for duty and excessive absences. In its proposal notice, the agency outlined the findings of the fitness for duty exam that the appellant's psychological condition prevented him from displaying mental and emotional stability, particularly when called upon to interact with his chain of command. The agency also cited the finding in the report that the appellant's condition prevented him from consistently working with others in a professional, courteous, and cooperative manner. Based on this, the proposing official stated that the appellant was incapable of performing the duties of his position. The proposing official also stated that the appellant's inability to handle stress directly and adversely affected his ability to work safely with high voltage electricity. The deciding official agreed with these views and stated that the appellant's susceptibility to stress and his conduct on the job while under stress caused the official enormous concern.

Concerning the appellant's alleged stress-related inability to get along with his supervisor and others in the chain of command, the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission has held that an individual was not substantially limited in the major life activity of working where situational depression prevented her from working at one particular location under one particular supervisor. Miller v. Danzig, Appeal No. 03AO0066 (EEOC May 4, 2000); see also McAlindin v. County of San Diego, 192 F.3d 1226, 1235 (9th Cir. 1999) (mere trouble getting along with coworkers was insufficient to show a substantial limitation of a major life activity), amended on denial of rehearing, 201 F.3d 1211 (9th Cir.), cert. denied, 530 U.S. 1243, 120 S. Ct. 2689, 147 L. Ed.2d 961 (2000). These holdings are consistent with the EEOC Enforcement Guidance on the Americans with Disabilities Act and Psychiatric Disabilities at 5 (1997).[8]

[8] Because the Enforcement Guidance was issued prior to the Supreme Court's decisions in Sutton and Murphy, we carefully view any, material from it. Nevertheless, we find the Guidance to be instructive here and generally consistent with those decisions. See Sorensen v. University of Utah Hospital, 194 F.3d 1084, 1088 (10th Cir. 1999).

Thus, to the extent that the appellant's condition limited his ability to work with particular coworkers or in a particular chain of command, the condition did not substantially limit him from the major life activity of working. This is consistent with the principle that to be substantially limited in the major life activity of working one must be precluded from a broad range of positions, not just certain positions which interact with certain employers or supervisors.

Moreover, nothing in this record suggests that the agency viewed the appellant as precluded from a broad range of jobs, either inside or outside of the agency. Even if the agency believed that the appellant was unable to perform his Supervisory High Voltage Electrician position, it demonstrated that it did not believe that he was unable to perform similar positions by placing him in the Engineering Assistant position.[9] Beyond that, however, there is no evidence that the agency believed that the appellant was incapable of performing jobs consistent with his skills and abilities in the greater Montgomery, Alabama area, where the agency is located and the appellant resides. Given the administrative judge's finding that the appellant satisfactorily performed the duties of his original assigned position, and in light of the fact that the agency reassigned him to less strenuous but related duties in another position, the only reasonable conclusion is that the appellant's condition did not substantially limit his ability to work at all.

Thus, we conclude that the appellant was not perceived by the agency as disabled. The agency did not believe that the appellant was precluded from a broad range of jobs in his geographic area. Accordingly, the administrative judge incorrectly found that the agency had discriminated against the appellant based on his perceived disability. Those findings in the initial decision are hereby reversed.

[9] Based on our review of the cases, regulations, and guidance set forth above, we find that the Engineering Assistant position was sufficiently similar to the appellant's Supervisory High Voltage Electrician position to show that the appellant was not precluded from a broad range of jobs. Thus, the agency's offer of the position supports its claim that it did not perceive him to be disabled.

The Board's analysis of discrimination based on an unfounded perception of an individual as disabled was elaborated on in Stanley v. DOJ, 48 MSPR 1, 6-8 (1991), involving a correctional officer who, being removed for physical inability to perform his work, successfully established a disability discrimination case by showing that he could do the work and that the agency (incorrectly) perceived him as having a physical disability that foreclosed him from a type of employment--law enforcement:

An agency need not accommodate an employee...if the employee does not qualify as a "handicapped person" under 29 CFR § 1613.702. See Lehman v. Department of the Army, 30 MSPR 72, 74 (1986). The question of who is a "handicapped person" is best suited to a "case-by-case determination." See Forrisi v. Bowen, 794 F.2d 931, 933 (4th Cir. 1986) (citing E.E. Black, Ltd. v. Marshall, 497 F. Supp. 1088, 1100 (D. Hawaii 1980)). The inquiry is, of necessity, an individualized one--whether the particular impairment "substantially limits" the employee's ability to work or otherwise constitutes "a significant barrier to employment." See 29 CFR § 1613.702(a) and (c); Forrisi, 794 F.2d at 933. "Relevant to the inquiry are 'the number and type of jobs from which the impaired individual is disqualified, the geographical area to which the individual has reasonable access, and the individual's job expectations and training.'" Forrisi, 794 F.2d at 933 (citing Jasany v. United States Postal Service, 755 F.2d 1244, 1249 (6th Cir. 1985)). Further, an employee's inability to meet the demands of a particular job because of an actual or perceived impairment does not necessarily mean that the employee is handicapped. The impairment must foreclose generally the type of employment involved. See Anderson v. Department of the Navy, 45 MSPR 136, 141 (1990) (citing Forrisi, 794 F.2d at 934-35). See also Price v. Administrative Office of the U.S. Courts, Pet. No. 03900045, slip op. at 4-5 (EEOC May 4, 1990) (an impairment must significantly decrease an individual's ability to obtain satisfactory employment in order to be considered a "handicapping condition").

We find that the appellant meets the definition of a "handicapped person" under 29 CFR § 1613.702(a)(3) and (c). First, the parties agree that the appellant suffers from various permanent but treatable medical conditions, including a "hypercoagulable state secondary to circulating lupus anticoagulant" and hypertension. Second, although there is no evidence that any of the appellant's medical conditions affected his work performance at the time of the agency's action, the agency nevertheless removed the appellant based on its perception that his medical conditions created a risk of serious injury to the appellant and prevented him from safely performing all of the duties of his Correctional Officer position. See Geiger v. U.S. Postal Service, Pet. No. 01901409, slip op. at 6 (EEOC May 23, 1990) (the appellant met the definition of a "handicapped employee" under 29 CFR § 1613.702 because the agency regarded him as being handicapped, having erroneously concluded that his knee injury impaired his ability to work). In sum, since the agency disqualified the appellant from employment as a Correctional Officer based on its erroneous perception that his medical condition prevented him from performing jobs in that line of work, we find that the appellant has met the test as enunciated in Forrisi, 794 F.2d at 934-35. In this regard, the record shows that the appellant's work background consisted virtually exclusively of jobs in the field of law enforcement. Thus, the agency's reasons for finding that the medical impairment that it erroneously attributed to the appellant precluded his employment as a Correctional Officer would also make it difficult, if not impossible, for the appellant to secure other employment elsewhere consistent with his job expectations and training.

In addition, we find the appellant has shown that he is a "qualified handicapped person" who is entitled to the protection of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, as amended, 29 USC §§ 701 et seq. because he is a handicapped person who, with or without reasonable accommodation, can perform the essential functions of his position without endangering the health and safety of himself or others. 29 CFR § 1613.702(f). Indeed, the appellant's performance had been satisfactory up until the time of his stroke, and the record strongly suggests that the appellant would have remained capable of satisfactorily performing his full range of duties as a Correctional Officer, had the agency permitted him to remain in that position. In this regard, the appellant specifically stated in prehearing conferences and in "credible testimony" at the hearing that he desired to return to work as a Correctional Officer and that he believed that he would be able to perform all of the duties of that position. See also Jackson v. Veterans Administration, 768 F.2d 1325, 1331 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (the Board must necessarily give special deference to the administrative judge's findings regarding credibility where those findings are based on the demeanor of witnesses). Moreover, even if we assume arguendo that the appellant's risk of serious injury was greater than that of other employees, the agency had previously accommodated other physically handicapped employees by limiting their exposure to stress and risk while still permitting them to perform as Correctional Officers, and the agency has not shown that the appellant could not be similarly accommodated. Accordingly, because the agency failed to establish that it could not reasonably and safely accommodate the appellant's perceived handicap, we agree with the administrative judge and find that the agency's adverse action constituted an act of discrimination against the appellant.

If an employee is actually disabled, it appears that the "regarded as disabled" classification is inapplicable, and the appeal is analyzed on the basis of the actual disability, McFadden v. DOD, 85 MSPR 18, 23-24 ¶¶ 17-18 (1999):

First, we note that the administrative judge found that the appellant was a disabled individual because the agency regarded her as having an impairment. An employee who is merely regarded as disabled by the agency does not need accommodation to perform the essential functions of her position because she "does not actually have...a substantially limiting impairment." Adams v. Reno, EEOC No. 03940156, slip op. at 8 (Feb. 2, 1995) (citing Mansell v. Donley, EEOC No. 01891189, slip op. at n.18 (June II, 1993)); accord Yates v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 MSPR 172, 179 (1996); see also Sutton v. United Air Lines, Inc., 119 S. Ct. 2139, 2150 (1999) (under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), an employee is "regarded as" disabled when the employer "entertain[s] misperceptions about the individual--it must believe either that one has a substantially limiting impairment that one does not have or that one has a substantially limiting impairment when, in fact, the impairment is not so limiting"). Although Sutton arose under the ADA, the ADA's standards have been incorporated by reference into the Rehabilitation Act. 29 USC § 791(g).

Under the Rehabilitation Act and the ADA, an individual is considered "disabled" if she is "regarded as" having a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits a major life activity. 29 USC § 706(8)(B)(iii); 42 USC § 12102(2)(C). Under the Rehabilitation Act, "is regarded as having such an impairment" means--

has a physical or mental impairment that does not substantially limit major life activities but is treated by an employer as constituting such a limitation; has a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits major life activities only as a result of the attitude of an employer toward such impairment; or has none of the impairments as defined in (2) of this section but is treated by an employer as having such an impairment.

29 CFR § 1614.203(a)(5). The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission's definition of "is regarded as having such an impairment" for ADA purposes is virtually identical, with no substantive differences. See 29 CFR § 1630.2(i).

In its petition for review, the agency challenges the administrative judge's finding that it regarded the appellant as disabled. We agree with the agency's argument. The testimony discussed above shows that the appellant has a physical impairment that does substantially limit her major life activities and that the agency correctly perceived that she was impaired. Because the appellant was actually impaired, we find that she was not "regarded as" having a physical impairment by the agency under 29 CFR § 1614.203(a)(5).

If the appellant asserts that she notified her agency that she had a substantially limiting impairment necessitating accommodation, she cannot be "regarded" as disabled. See Jones v. Dept. of Treasury, 93 MSPR 494, 502 ¶ 18 (2003). Cf. Alford v. DOD, 118 MSPR 556, 562 n.6, 2012 MSPB 110 (2012) ("While an employee who is disabled solely under the "regarded as" prong is not entitled to reasonable accommodation, he may pursue a claim of disability discrimination on a disparate treatment theory, as the appellant did here. See 29 C.F.R. §§ 1630.2(g)(3), 1630.9(e).").
