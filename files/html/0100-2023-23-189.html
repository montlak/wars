c. Agreement to Withdraw EEOC Case or Other Litigation

Baig v. Dept. of Navy, 66 MSPR 269, 276-77 (1995), implied that if a settlement provides for withdrawal of an appeal before another tribunal, e.g., the EEOC, the agency may have to approach the EEOC to secure dismissal of the claim. The Board appeared reluctant to direct the appellant to withdraw his claim in that tribunal:

With regard to the agency's cross-petition for enforcement, we find that the appellant materially breached paragraphs one and seven of the settlement agreement when he instituted an appeal with the EEOC because he raised the same allegations in his EEOC appeal that he had raised in his Board appeal that the settlement agreement resolved. We are not empowered, however, to grant the agency's request for relief to order the appellant to withdraw his EEOC appeal.

We agree with the administrative judge that the EEOC rather than the Board is authorized to determine whether the July 29, 1989, agreement defeats EEOC's jurisdiction over the appellant's EEOC appeal. 29 CFR § 1613.401 (1993). We have held, however, that where there has been a material breach of the agreement, the nonbreaching party has the option of enforcing the terms of the agreement or rescinding the agreement and going forward with the original claim. See Betterly v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 47 MSPR 63, 66-67 (1991). In this case, the agency has not requested rescission as an alternative remedy. Therefore, if the agency wishes this remedy, additional proceedings must be conducted before a remedy is ordered. See Murphy v. US Postal Service, 54 MSPR 202, 206 (1992).[3]...

Without mentioning Baig, Wisdom v. DOD, 78 MSPR 652, 655-57 (1998), concluded that the Board has the authority to direct the appellant to take steps, including the withdrawal of a lawsuit, necessary to effectuate the appellant's commitments in a settlement. Not mentioned was how the Board would go about ensuring compliance with its directives to an employee or former employee:

Section 1204(a)(2) provides that the Board "shall...order any Federal agency or employee to comply with any order or decision issued by the Board under paragraph (1) of this subsection...." Although this provision does not by its terms apply to former employees, we find that this result is implicit in the statutory language where the underlying appeal is within the Board's jurisdiction. The U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit has held, in analogous circumstances, that the term "employee" in the civil service laws must be read to include former employees. In Bronger v. Office of Personnel Management, 740 F.2d 1552, 1555-56 (Fed. Cir. 1984), rev'd on other grounds, 769 F.2d 756 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (en banc), cert. denied, 474 U.S. 1101, 106 S. Ct. 882, 88 L. Ed.2d 918 (1986), the court rejected OPM's argument that former employees who were challenging OPM's decisions concerning their retirement annuities had no right to judicial review under 5 USC § 7703(a). The court acknowledged that sections 7701(a) and 7703(a) of title 5 do not explicitly grant former employees a right to appeal to the Board or the court, respectively. Rather, the statutory language provides appeal rights to an "employee or applicant for employment...." 5 USC § 7701(a) and 7703(a). The court nevertheless held that denying appeal rights to former employees would lead to absurd results. A critical factor in the court's analysis was its observation that by the time many individuals file their appeals, they have already been removed from the Federal service and are therefore former employees. Bronger, 740 F.2d at 1556. The court declined to find that the petitioners in Bronger, who were already retired from the Federal service when they filed their Board and judicial appeals, had entered a "'nonemployee' twilight zone" which deprived them of any appeal rights. We conclude that here, as in Bronger, interpreting the term "employee" in section 1204(a) to exclude former employees from the Board's enforcement authority would lead to absurd results where, as here, the underlying appeal was within the Board's jurisdiction.

Consistent with this analysis, both the Board and the Federal Circuit have routinely enforced settlement agreements against or in favor of individuals who were employees when they signed the agreement but who then resigned or retired, as required by the agreement. E.g., King v. Department of the Navy, 130 F.3d 1031 (Fed. Cir. 1997); Johnson v. Federal Emergency Management Agency, 41 MSPR 561 (1989). To the extent that the Board's decision in Stipp, 64 MSPR at 128 n.3, implied that the Board lacks the authority to direct appellants to comply with settlement agreements solely because they have become former employees, that part of the decision is hereby overruled.

Our interpretation of section 1204 as providing the Board with enforcement authority over the appellant also effectuates the parties' intent, as reflected in the clear language of the settlement agreement. In paragraph 6, the agreement explicitly provided that "[i]t is the intent of the parties to enter this Settlement into the record for enforcement by the Merit Systems Protection Board." After the agreement was signed, it was entered into the record of the underlying appeal. There is nothing in the agreement that suggests that the parties intended the illogical result that the agreement's prohibitions against the appellant calling the agency's hotline and filing law suits were intended to be binding and enforceable only for the one-week period between the time the agreement was signed and the appellant remained an employee. Interpretations of settlement agreements that create absurd results are disfavored. See Abernethy v. Office of Personnel Management, 62 MSPR 170, 173 (1994). We therefore conclude that the appellant's change in status from an employee to a former employee, as required by the settlement agreement, does not preclude the Board from ordering the appellant to comply with his other obligations under the agreement.

In her Recommendation the administrative judge found that the evidence established that the appellant breached the settlement agreement by making calls to the agency's hotline and by filing a lawsuit against agency officials that was related to his previous employment with the agency. The appellant was provided with an opportunity to file a brief disagreeing with the Recommendation but has not done so. The evidence cited by the administrative judge supports her findings, and we find no basis to disturb them. Accordingly, we conclude that it is appropriate to direct the appellant to comply with the settlement agreement.

ORDER

The appellant is hereby ORDERED to (1) refrain from calling any DLA hotline; and (2) submit to the Clerk of the Board within 25 days of this order evidence that he has withdrawn the lawsuit he filed in the United States District Court for the Eastern District of Texas, Docket No. 5: 97CV50.
