13. OSC Case

Settlement of a Special Counsel disciplinary case through the agreed imposition of a penalty is reviewed to determine if it both accommodates the need to penalize those who violate merit system principles and conserves time, effort, and expense. Special Counsel v. Ponce, 29 MSPR 385, 386 (1985); see Special Counsel v. Parker, 29 MSPR 659, 661 (1986) (approving a settlement after an ALJ reviewed the basis for the settlement prior to recommending approval). Settlements reached between the Special Counsel and the employing agencies may be accepted by the Board on the condition that advance notice of future personnel actions be given to the Board and to the Special Counsel to permit the renewal of petitions for stay of a personnel action. See In re Goldschmidt, 1 MSPR 118, 119, 1 MSPB 114 (1979). The Board favors settlement of Hatch Act cases, approving these settlements if they are freely entered, lawful, and serve the interests of justice and judicial economy. See Special Counsel v. Martellotta, 56 MSPR 488, 489 (1993) (approved settlement calling for 45-day suspension based on then-current provision of the Hatch Act allowing reduction of removal to suspension of at least 30 days upon unanimous vote of Board that the violation does not warrant removal); Special Counsel v. Giles, 56 MSPR 465, 467 (1993) (relying on Special Counsel v. Dracup, 44 MSPR 107, 109 (1990)).

A conditional settlement can be used when the Special Counsel recommends the settlement and the parties want to ensure that their rights are preserved if the recommendation is not accepted. Special Counsel v. Verrot, 18 MSPR 714, 714 (1983), approved a settlement of a prohibited personnel practice case that was submitted on condition that "if the penalty recommended by the Special Counsel were considered inappropriate for whatever reason, by the judge or the Board, the parties be granted a hearing on the appropriateness of the recommended discipline." [Refer to Chapter 13, "Settlement."]

For an OSC settlement to be enforceable it, like other settlements, should be part of an appeal within the Board's jurisdiction. An individual who initiates an IRA (whistleblowing) case first through the Special Counsel may settle the matter with the agency before or after the case proceeds to the Board by way of appeal. If the settlement is reached before the appeal is filed, or if the settlement is reached after the appeal is filed but the settlement is not then filed with and accepted by the Board, the appellant may be unable to secure enforcement of the agreement. This was explained, indirectly, in Szetela v. Dept. of Air Force, 55 MSPR 3, 4-7 (1992):

On April 27, 1990, during his trial period, the agency terminated the appellant for unsatisfactory conduct which did not warrant continued Federal employment. The agency specifically noted that, because of the appellant's "frequent invalid complaints [he had] personally created an atmosphere of animosity among [his] coworkers, resulting in very low morale." Subsequently, the appellant, alleging reprisal for his whistleblowing activities, filed a complaint with the Office of Special Counsel.

On January 31, 1992, after the Office of Special Counsel closed his case before it, the appellant filed an appeal with the Board's Boston Regional Office, alleging that his removal was in reprisal for his "whistleblowing activit[ie]s [sic] related to voicing concerns regarding safety at the workplace." The agency responded and moved that the appellant's appeal be dismissed because the parties had entered into a settlement agreement in which it agreed to cancel the termination action and to place the appellant in leave-without-pay (LWOP) status, effective April 27, 1990, through May 31, 1991. The agency attached the standard form (SF-50) effecting the cancellation and a copy of the settlement agreement, signed by the parties on October 29, 1991. In paragraph one of the settlement, the appellant agreed to the following:

[T]o withdraw any and all complaints he now has pending with the Office of Special Counsel, with prejudice, and further agrees not to institute any lawsuit, complaint, grievance or appeal against the [agency], or any employee or member of same, based upon any facts contained in any complaint he now has pending with the Office of Special Counsel.

...

In her initial decision, the administrative judge dismissed the appeal on the basis of the agency's complete rescission of the termination action, finding that this divested the Board of jurisdiction and that the appellant's IRA appeal therefore was moot.

In his petition for review, the appellant asserts that the administrative judge erred in finding that he had been returned to his former position when, in fact, the agency merely agreed to place him in LWOP status. The appellant also has submitted copies of forms apparently used to determine his eligibility for state (Massachusetts) unemployment compensation and copies of three SF-50 forms showing that the agency cancelled the termination and placed the appellant in LWOP status, effective through May 12, 1991.

Insofar as the appellant may be asserting that the agency has not complied with the settlement agreement because it did not return him to the status quo ante, we note that the settlement itself provides only that the appellant be placed in LWOP status. Furthermore, the Board lacks jurisdiction to enforce the terms of a settlement agreement which was not reached during the course of an appeal to the Board and entered into the record for purposes of enforcement. See Wobschall v. Department of Air Force, 43 MSPR 521, 523 (1990), aff'd, 918 F.2d 187 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (Table).

To the extent that the appellant contends that the agency did not completely rescind its action because it did not return him to his former position, we agree. Although the administrative judge found that the agency asserted that the appellant had been returned to his former position, we can find no evidence to support this finding. Although the agency submitted evidence that it cancelled the termination and returned the appellant to the agency's rolls, it further submitted a signed settlement agreement providing that the appellant would be placed on LWOP status from the effective date of the termination, April 27, 1990, until May 13, 1991. Accordingly, we find that the agency did not completely rescind the termination and that the administrative judge erred in dismissing the appeal for lack of jurisdiction on that basis....

We find, however, that this does not constitute prejudicial error because the appellant agreed to withdraw his complaint, waived his appeal rights based on his whistleblowing claim, and has not challenged the settlement agreement in any way.... In this connection, we note that the administrative judge's show cause order specifically noted that the appeal might be dismissed because the parties entered into a settlement agreement which provided for the withdrawal of the appellant's Special Counsel complaint and the cancellation of the removal.... Since the appellant has not challenged the applicability of the agreement's terms providing for the withdrawal of the Special Counsel complaint and the waiver of his appeal rights, we conclude that the Board lacks jurisdiction over his appeal. See, e.g., Ferby v. United States Postal Service, 26 MSPR 451, 457 (1985) (employees who executed "last-chance agreements" voluntarily waived their appeal rights to the Board, thus surrendering their right to challenge agency actions during the period specified in the terms of the settlement agreement); cf. Danelishen v. US Postal Service, 43 MSPR 376, 380 (1990) (where the appellant challenges the action effected through a settlement as involuntary, the Board will examine the record to determine if the agreement was coerced).
