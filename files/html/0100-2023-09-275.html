C. ENTRY OF DISCOVERY MATERIAL INTO RECORD

Information developed during discovery does not enter the record unless someone puts it there. The Board offers some suggestions involving entry of discovery materials into the hearing record in the Hearings Procedures Guide (superseded by the Judges' Handbook but still used by the Board for training and orientation), at 28-31:

I. INTRODUCTION OF DISCOVERED MATERIALS

...

The Board now requires the parties to submit all proposed exhibits with their pre-hearing submissions. The date for filing pre-hearing submissions is set by the AJ and should be prior to the pre-hearing conference. Submitted exhibits should include any fruits of discovery that the parties wish to have included in the record.

Failure to submit exhibits in accordance with a Board order is grounds for rejection.

Occasionally, one or both parties will forget to make a formal motion for the acceptance of exhibits into the record. The AJ should remind the parties of the necessity of a motion for acceptance of the exhibits. Such a reminder can be made at the end of the parties' presentations.

1. Use of Discovered Material

Board discovery procedures are designed to enable a party to obtain relevant information needed for the preparation of a party's case. 5 CFR § 1201.71.

Discovery procedures can be used when an important witness will not be available at the time of the hearing or when costs to the appellant to provide a witness would be prohibitive.

A deposition or interrogatory can be accepted into the record, subject to objection by the opposing party, in lieu of live testimony.

2. Introduction of Discovered Materials Into Evidence

The appeal record should not contain fruits of discovery except as part of the proposed exhibits. That is, the parties should submit responses to discovery only to the requesting party. If such submissions are received by the AJ, they should be returned to the submitting party.

Discovered materials are submitted into evidence as hearing exhibits received by the AJ as part of the prehearing submission. During the course of the hearing, the parties must move for submission of the exhibits--including fruits of discovery--into the appeal record.

Discovery materials may be used for impeachment purposes, even though it was not included in the party's prehearing list of exhibits.

a. Using Depositions

(1) Admitting Depositions Into Evidence

Depositions can be used to: (a) provide testimony from a witness who is not available at the time and/or place of the hearing; and/or (b) impeach the testimony of witnesses whose testimony at the hearing differs from that given at the deposition.

The parties may move for the inclusion of a deposition, or portion thereof, into the record. Such a motion would be expected where the deposition is taken in lieu of live testimony.

(2) Permitting Depositions to Be Used As Impeachment

Depositions can be used to impeach a witness by showing that prior inconsistent statements were made.

(3) Permitting Parties to Introduce Parts of Depositions

Parts of a deposition may be relevant and material while other parts may reflect that the witness has no knowledge of the pertinent matter. Thus, inclusion of only a portion of the deposition may be appropriate. Of course, the opposing party may submit another portion of the deposition or the remainder, subject to the required showing of relevance and materiality.

b. Using Interrogatories and Requests for Admissions

Interrogatories and requests for admissions are submitted the same as other exhibits, and should be accepted or rejected by the AJ and appropriately marked.

If a party submits answers to interrogatories as exhibits, the opposing party may object because of a lack of opportunity to cross-examine the witness.

In the event of such an objection, it should be noted that the objecting party had the opportunity to subpoena, depose or submit interrogatories to the witness.

c. Binding Effect of Discovery Responses

(1) Interrogatories

Because interrogatories are made under oath, evidence contained therein should be weighed accordingly. Any deviation between testimony and answers to interrogatories may be grounds for impeachment as a "prior inconsistent statement."

(2) Requests for Admissions

Responses to "requests for admissions" are not normally made under oath. However, the answerer may be subject to impeachment because of a prior inconsistent statement.

In addition, parties should be bound by admissions that limit the scope of the hearing. For example, a party indicating that no procedural error occurred would be precluded from raising such an allegation unless evidence of such error became available only after the answer was given.

However, the answerer may be subject to impeachment because of a prior inconsistent statement.

3. Effect of Procedural Error at Deposition on the Subsequent Use of Deposition

a. Improper Notice

Sanctions may be imposed for failure to provide an ordered deposition. However, where notice of the deposition was improperly given, imposition of sanctions would be inappropriate. The parties should be given the opportunity to reschedule the deposition if time allows.

b. Failure to Establish Expert Qualifications

Parties may choose to depose an expert witness. Unless the parties agree that the witness is an expert, the proponent of the witness is required to present sufficient evidence to show that the witness is an expert.

If the proponent of the witness fails to show such qualification and the witness is not called at the hearing, the offered deposition should not be weighed as if given by an expert.

c. Failure to Object when Deposition Taken

Because an AJ is not present during a deposition, the parties are expected to state their objections for the record and then continue with the deposition. Therefore, failure to object at the time of the deposition may preclude the party from objecting to the use of that portion of the deposition.

However, if the witness is testifying at the hearing, an objection may be raised to the same matter, i.e., an irrelevant question, and the AJ must issue a ruling.

Rescheduling a deposition is not necessary when a deponent will be available at the hearing. Jones v. Dept. of Justice, 21 MSPR 339 (1984).

d. Improper Completion and Return of Deposition.

The AJ must attempt to learn whether the improper completion and/or return of the deposition was intentional. If so, sanctions with the concurrence of the AJ may be appropriate. If improper completion and return were not intentional, the parties should be given time to properly complete the process.

On the mechanics of introducing an affidavit or a deposition at the hearing, the Judges' Handbook adds, in Chapter 10, section 14.a.:

(2) Affidavits and Depositions. Affidavits and depositions may be accepted into evidence at a hearing, despite objections, upon a reasonable showing by the offering party of the unavailability of the affiant to testify. There is generally no need to play recorded or videotaped depositions at the hearing unless one of the parties wishes the AJ to rule on an objection made at the deposition.

Discovery material may be entered into the record with or without a hearing. If there is a hearing, the discovery material would ordinarily be placed into the record as exhibits to the prehearing submission, offered into evidence at the hearing if the documents are not admitted during the prehearing conference. If no hearing is held, the materials will likely come into the record as part of the parties' final evidentiary submissions prior to the close of the record. The Board will not sustain an objection to discovery material entered into the record by one party if the other party has a fair opportunity to respond to the information, Crawford v. Dept. of Treasury, 56 MSPR 224, 232-33 (1993):

The agency contends that, in light of the appellant's withdrawal of his request for a hearing, the administrative judge erred by admitting into the record the depositions of its two primary witnesses, the proposing and deciding officials, because, under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, depositions are part of discovery and are normally used only where a witness is unavailable or in exceptional circumstances. The agency, however, specifically alleges prejudice only in regard to the admission of the deciding official's deposition into the record. It argues that it was prejudiced because the administrative judge based his finding that the agency failed to prove the specification that the appellant repeatedly failed to file his income tax returns, and that mitigation was warranted, on the deciding official's statement in her deposition that she viewed the appellant's alleged repeated failure to file his income tax returns as the primary concern. The agency also argues that it was prejudiced by the deciding official's testimony in her deposition that she was not familiar with Douglas v. VA, 5 MSPB 313, 5 MSPR 280, 306 (1981), because the fact that she might not have been familiar with the name of the case does not mean that she did not consider all of the relevant factors. The agency contends that, because the appellant withdrew his request for a hearing, it was deprived of the opportunity to examine the deciding official on the witness stand and to further explore these matters. The agency asserts that it objected to the admission of the depositions below, and the record supports this assertion.

An appellant has the right to withdraw a request for a Board hearing in his appeal. See Kirkpatrick v. Dept. of Interior, 49 MSPR 316, 318 (1991). After the appellant withdrew his request for a hearing in this case, the administrative judge afforded the parties 10 days from the date that the appellant withdrew his hearing request to submit additional evidence, i.e., 10 days before the March 23, 1992 close of the record. The administrative judge also allowed the parties an additional 2 days to provide rebuttal argument. The agency does not assert that the time permitted was inadequate to submit clarifying evidence and argument regarding the depositions. Thus, the agency has not shown that the administrative judge abused his discretion in accepting the depositions into the record. As the agency essentially concedes, strict adherence to the Federal Rules of Evidence and of Civil Procedure is not mandatory in administrative proceedings. Gearan v. DHHS, 42 MSPR 313, 317-18 (1989); Shrider v. USPS, 36 MSPR 650, 655 (1988).

If deposition transcripts are not placed into the record during the regional adjudication of the case, the Board is not likely to accept them at the PFR stage unless it is shown that the transcripts were unavailable before the close of the record below. See LaPre v. DOJ, 62 MSPR 329, 334 n.2 (1994).

Under exceptional circumstances, the Board considers information, first presented with a PFR, that was developed during discovery but not earlier entered into the record, an example being Staats v. USPS, 68 MSPR 65, 68-69 n.* (1995), involving the timeliness of an appeal:

The responses to interrogatories as well as other material produced in discovery were not submitted as part of the record below, in accordance with the preferred practice of the Board's administrative judges. Nevertheless, we have considered the statements produced in discovery that the parties have submitted on review. The statements have been verified by the party making them, and they are material to the timeliness question that is at issue on review. In addition, because the administrative judge decided this case without a hearing, the parties did not have the opportunity to file prehearing submissions, including proposed hearing exhibits such as excerpts of discovery materials. Furthermore, the agency was in possession of the statements that the appellant refers to on review, since they were made in response to the agency's own interrogatories.
