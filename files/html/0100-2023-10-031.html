a. Request; Form of Sworn Statement

Under 5 CFR 1201.51(c) (2022), the written motion for a continuance must be supported by an affidavit or sworn statement stating the reasons constituting the good cause required for the continuance. The regulation references the need for either an affidavit or a sworn statement. They carry the same weight, although affidavits require notarization, while sworn statements do not. Under 28 USC 1746, a sworn statement is sufficient if it meets the simple statutory requirements:

Wherever, under any law...or...rule, regulation, order, or requirement made pursuant to law, any matter is required or permitted to be supported, evidenced, established, or proved by the sworn declaration, verification, certificate, statement, oath, or affidavit, in writing of the person making the same (other than a deposition, or an oath of office, or an oath required to be taken before a specified official other than a notary public), such matter may, with like force and effect, be supported, evidenced, established, or proved by the unsworn declaration, certificate, verification, or statement, in writing of such person which is subscribed by him, as true under penalty of perjury, and dated, in substantially the following form:

(1) If executed without the United States: "I declare (or certify, verify, or state) under penalty of perjury under the laws of the United States of America that the foregoing is true and correct. Executed on (date). (Signature)"

(2) If executed within the United States, its territories, possessions, or commonwealths: "I declare (or certify, verify, or state) under penalty of perjury that the foregoing is true and correct. Executed on (date). (Signature)."

The Judges' Handbook notes at Chapter 7, section 8: "Any time an AJ requires an affidavit or sworn statement from a party, he or she should refer the party to Appendix IV to part 1201 of the Board's regulations for a sample declaration under 28 U.S.C. § 1746...."

The Board does not favor telephonic requests for continuances on the eve of the hearing. If one is made, it will be necessary to demonstrate that the request was communicated to the AJ, not just to anyone in the regional office, and that good cause exists. Marshall v. USPS, 2 MSPR 571, 573-74, 3 MSPB 91 (1980). The caution required in making a telephonic request is illustrated by Goodjoin v. GSA, 29 MSPR 54, 56 (1985):

In his petition for review, appellant contends that he asked the presiding official to grant him a continuance during a telephone conversation on March 1, 1985, because he did not have legal representation. Appellant, however, has not substantiated this contention with any evidence. Although the presiding official noted in the initial decision that he had spoken to appellant concerning certain procedural matters, he did not mention any request for a continuance; rather he stated that appellant was aware of the proper date of the hearing. Further, even if appellant requested a continuance during the conversation, he failed to comply with the Board's...Order requiring a written request supported by an affidavit showing good cause for granting the continuance.

A comment was added concerning exigent continuance requests in Biberstine v. DODDS, 37 MSPR 248, 254 n.4 (1988):

We note that the administrative judge waived the Board's regulatory requirement of filing a written motion for continuance supported by affidavit because of the short proximity of the hearing date and the fact that the parties were overseas. While this is justifiable under the circumstances, the preferable procedure is to convene a telephonic conference call with the representatives of both parties to address the merits of such a motion; otherwise, it is impossible to determine whether the granting of such a motion would cause an undue burden, be harassing, or cause unwarranted delay.

A motion for a continuance is a procedural request. Under 5 CFR 1201.55(a) (2022), "A party filing a motion for extension of time, a motion for postponement of a hearing, or any other procedural motion must first contact the other party to determine whether there is any objection to the motion, and must state in the motion whether the other party has an objection."
