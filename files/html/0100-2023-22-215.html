b. Repayment of Fee by Counsel to Client

An attorney would not ordinarily file with the Board a statement guaranteeing he will repay fees. Disputes over fees and their repayment are the province of disciplinary boards, not the MSPB. This view was adopted in Jensen v. Dept. of Transp., 858 F.2d 721, 724 (Fed. Cir. 1988), involving the Board's refusal to award counsel all the fees due him because the Board felt that counsel had demonstrated an unwillingness to repay some of those fees to his client, who counsel argued had waived any right to repayment of the fees. The court admonished:

Although we are not bound by the case law precedent of the board, we are in complete agreement with the holding in Blessin [v. Dept. of Navy, 26 MSPR 615 (1985)] that the attorney fee award must be paid to counsel rather than to the parties who pay their fees. In the past, the board has required the attorney to agree on the record to reimburse his client for any fees already received. While such an agreement by the attorney may be enforceable, an attorney's refusal to make such an agreement is no ground to reduce the attorney fee award payable to the attorney. We find no reimbursement requirement within the statutory language of the CSRA. The board should look no further than to determine whether an attorney-client relationship exists. Once the board makes this determination, the board must make the attorney fee award payable to the attorney.

In a dispute between an appellant and an attorney from whom the appellant wanted to recover some fees through Board process, the reviewing court noted that the Board is not the appropriate forum for disputes over fees between lawyers and their clients. Long v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1991 NP No. 91-3029).

On occasion the Board states that counsel is required to reimburse an appellant for attorney fees to prevent loss to the appellant and to avoid double payment. See Mudrich v. USDA, 92 MSPR 413, 423 ¶ 21 (2002). The Board observed in Rhoades v. USPS, 42 MSPR 138, 139 (1989), that it lacks jurisdiction over an appellant's allegation that his counsel has not reimbursed him for fees recovered by counsel through a Board fee petition. The Board now cautions that, as to fee arrangements between client and counsel, disputes over fees and reimbursement to clients are the province of disciplinary boards, not of the MSPB. Mercer v. DHHS, 42 MSPR 115, 119 n.7 (1989) (citing Jensen v. Dept. of Transp., 858 F.2d 721, 724 (Fed. Cir. 1988)); cf. Christofili v. Dept. of Army, 81 MSPR 384, 392 ¶ 20 (1999) (declining to consider alleged infractions of state disciplinary rules during a discovery dispute between counsel). Harkening to earlier law, however, and over the dissent of Chairman Levinson, the Board admonished in Bivens v. Dept. of Navy, 52 MSPR 602, 606 (1992), that "[b]ecause under the fee agreements the appellant's attorney is apparently to receive a percentage of back pay awarded to the appellant, and this, coupled with the payment by the appellant of the other established fees, could be in excess of the maximum reasonable fee, the administrative judge shall, on remand, obtain information regarding the total fees to be received by the appellant's attorney."

Without regard to the Board's ability to enforce repayment arrangements between attorney and client, Powell v. Dept. of Treasury, 8 MSPR 322, 330-31 n.6 (1981), stated that to avoid double benefits to counsel, an attorney who secures a fee award must repay the client for any fees already received. Attorneys employed by unions or public interest organizations must repay their respective organizations for compensation already received and expenses accrued that would normally be included in an attorney fee. In private practice, counsel should reimburse their clients for attorney fees paid to prevent loss to the clients and to avoid double benefits to counsel. Blessin v. Dept. of Navy, 26 MSPR 615, 617 (1985) (because counsel fees should be paid to counsel, an attorney should not request the Board to direct an agency to pay fees directly to the client); see Holland v. Dept. of Air Force, DA-0752-11-0696-A-1 (NP 2/25/2015) ("The appellant objects to the order that the fees awarded be paid to the attorney, arguing that she has paid him in full. It is well established that fee awards accrue to counsel. However, an attorney who secures a fee award must repay the client for any fees already received to prevent loss to the appellant and to avoid double benefits to counsel. Mudrich v. Department of Agriculture, 92 M.S.P.R. 413, ¶ 21 (2002); O'Donnell v. Department of the Interior, 2 M.S.P.R. 445, 454 n.10 (1980), overruled on other grounds by Koch v. Department of Commerce, 19 M.S.P.R. 219 (1984)."). Thomson v. Dept. of Navy, 33 MSPR 106, 113-14 (1987), observing that counsel had moved from a law firm to what appeared to be his own private practice, admonished that the attorney should reimburse his former firm for fees that were properly theirs for the time invested while the firm represented the appellant. Should a counsel fee petition be filed on behalf of an estate of an attorney, the AJ should determine the interest of the estate in the award. See Lizut v. Dept. of Navy, 42 MSPR 3, 5 n.2 (1989).
