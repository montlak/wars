f. Basis for Awarding MSPB Time

Olsen v. Dept. of Commerce, Census Bureau, 735 F.2d 558 (Fed. Cir. 1984), considered whether EAJA covered time spent in Board proceedings as well as in court. EAJA has a provision that covers fee awards by an administrative agency, involving adversary adjudications. The same standards apply to administrative cases and to judicial proceedings. 5 USC 504(a)(1). Section 504(b)(1)(C) defines adversary adjudication in terms of whether the position of the United States is represented by counsel or otherwise. 5 USC 554(a)(2) excludes from the definition of adjudication the selection or tenure of an employee other than an administrative law judge. Section 28 USC 2412(d)(3) permits a judicial award of fees to the extent provided by 5 USC 504 for time spent at the administrative level of the adversarial adjudication. The remaining issue was whether 5 USC 504 permitted an award of fees under EAJA for time spent in administrative litigation. Section 504 did not permit the award for the administrative proceedings, because cases involving tenure of employees were excluded from the statutory coverage. However, the court concluded that the Back Pay Act, 5 USC 5596(b)(1)(A)(ii), allowed a court to award fees for services rendered in administrative appeals. Declining to remand the case to the Board for a fee determination, the government conceded the amount of, if not the entitlement to, the fee. The Back Pay Act entitlement standard of the "interest of justice" was met if the position of the government was not substantially justified. In a similar result, EAJA did not support a fee award for time spent before the Board by a successful appellant in a WIGI denial appeal. Application of the Act required an adversary proceeding defined by 5 USC 554 to exclude the selection or tenure of an employee. The exclusion encompassed the step increase appeal. Zeizel v. Pierce, 784 F.2d 405 (D.C. Cir. 1986) (appeal initiated before the Reform Act).

Under Olsen, supra, EAJA cannot be used by the Board because it is dependent upon provisions of the APA that define entitlement as to actions not involving employee tenure. See Covington v. DHHS, 818 F.2d 838 (Fed. Cir. 1987); cf. Jones v. Dept. of Army, 590 Fed. Appx. 975, 977 (Fed. Cir. 2014 NP) ("'[i]t is well-settled that the law does not permit...an attorney fees award under the EAJA for legal services rendered before the Board.' Massa v. Dep't of Defense, 833 F.2d 991, 992 (Fed. Cir. 1987)."). The Board reached the same result. One decision determined that the Board cannot award fees under the Equal Access to Justice Act. Arthur v. Dept. of Army, 26 MSPR 341, 342 (1985) (still directing the AJ to consider fees for compliance efforts under 5 USC 7701(g)(1)). Another explained that if there is a fee-shifting statute, e.g., the Reform Act, 5 USC 7701(g), the EAJA (5 USC 504) is inapplicable to Board proceedings. SSA v. Goodman, 28 MSPR 120, 126 (1985); cf. Gavette v. OPM, 808 F.2d 1456, 1459-62, 1464-65 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (EAJA supplements Back Pay Act fee provisions). EAJA was no basis for fees in a retirement appeal. The appeal did not meet the requirements of 5 USC 504 because the proceeding was not an adversary adjudication and because the administrative decision on a retirement application was not agency action. Miles v. OPM, 23 MSPR 415, 416 (1984). 28 USC 2412(b), providing EAJA coverage to civil actions in the federal courts, does not supply MSPB with authority to award fees for time spent in its proceedings. See St. Clergy v. OPM, 21 MSPR 620, 621 (1984). The exception under the EAJA for awards of fees by the Board is in cases involving appeals by ALJs, whose tenure is governed by the APA. See NLRB v. Boyce, 51 MSPR 295, 300 (1991), supra.
