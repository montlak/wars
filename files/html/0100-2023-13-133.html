b. Violation of Criminal Statute

The agency's burden of proof is not affected when the action serving as a basis for the charge is also a violation of a criminal statute. Perron v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 382, 385 (1983) (citing Chisolm v. DLA, 656 F.2d 42, 48 n.11 (3d Cir. 1981)); see Berman v. Dept. of Interior, 447 Fed. Appx. 186, 192 n.2 (Fed. Cir. 2011 NP) ("Contrary to Berman's assertion, the Board may adjudicate whether or not a Government employee has engaged in criminal conduct. See, e.g., King v. Nazelrod, 43 F.3d 663 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (analyzing the elements of a charge of "theft"); Wiemers v. Merit Sys. Prot. Bd., 792 F.2d 1113 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (upholding removal despite reversal of conviction when removal was based on underlying conduct)."); Mendez v. Macy, 292 F. Supp. 802, 804 (S.D.N.Y. 1968) (considering whether there was probably cause to conduct a search; if Fourth Amendment rights were violated, the court could set the action aside as contrary to law or contrary to a constitutional right, power, privilege, or immunity); cf. Alsbury v. USPS, 392 F. Supp. 71, 75 (C.D. Cal. 1975) ("[T]he agency...cannot be constrained to blindly follow the results of a criminal trial which has a stricter standard of proof and far different consequences for the employee."), aff'd, Alsbury v. USPS, 530 F.2d 852, 855 (9th Cir. 1976); Camero v. United States, 345 F.2d 798 (Ct. Cl. 1965) (on judicial review, the reviewing court looks in the administrative record for substantial evidence of criminal misconduct); Abbott v. Dept. of Transp., 17 MSPR 591, 593 (1983) (a preponderance standard applied in an appeal by an employee terminated for engaging in a strike against the government, a violation of a criminal statute).

Contentions that government employees faced with removal are entitled to all the protections of a criminal trial are rejected. Novotny v. Dept. of Transp., 735 F.2d 521 (Fed. Cir. 1984). The preponderant evidence standard governs fraud cases, not the "clear and convincing" standard of proof used prior to the Reform Act in the Court of Claims. Schwender v. Dept. of Labor, 19 MSPR 522, 523-24 (1984); see Walker v. USPS, 10 MSPR 341, 342-43 (1982) (noting that the "preponderance of evidence" rather than "clear and convincing" evidentiary standard applies to administrative cases involving allegations of fraudulent conduct; distinguishing Wise v. United States, 603 F.2d 182 (Ct. Cl. 1979), and Urbina v. United States, 180 Ct. Cl. 194 (1982)). In an appeal involving charges of assault on a fellow employee, a court rejected the argument that the standard of proof before the Board should have been "clear and convincing" evidence. The statute only required a preponderance of the evidence. Duran v. MSPB, 707 F.2d 1174 (10th Cir. 1983); see James v. OPM, 9 MSPR 17, 18 (1981) (preponderance standard governs suitability appeals). The preponderance of evidence test does not exclude other evidentiary tests and presumptions established by the Board. The Board holds unto itself the inherent authority to establish evidentiary standards for the application of pertinent federal law. Bearden v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 217, 218 n.1 (1983). [Refer to Chapter 8 for elements of proof for various charges and offenses; refer to Chapter 13, "Agency's Clear and Convincing Evidence; Carr Factors" for the evidentiary test in whistleblower reprisal cases.]

Irrespective of the quantum of evidence required to sustain the charge, an agency charging an individual with a criminal offense (as opposed to charging conduct that could be a criminal offense) must prove all the elements of the offense, not just the occurrence of some misconduct. In Knuckles v. USPS, 1 MSPR 358, 359, 1 MSPB 346 (1980), the appellant was charged with the offense of converting postal funds to his own use in violation of 18 USC 641. At the hearing, however, the agency presented only evidence of violation of accounting procedures. The Board determined that to prove the charge, the agency was required to show that there was an unlawful taking with intent to wrongfully deprive the Postal Service of the funds. The difference between the proof and the charges could have been significant, in the Board's opinion, for the appellant was forced to defend against a charge that the Postal Service did not intend to prove. The Board remanded the case to the AJ to determine whether the mislabeling of the charge substantially prejudiced the appellant's rights and constituted harmful error.
