c. Alcoholism or Drug Addiction; Inability to Control Condition

Rivera v. OPM, 96 MSPR 665, 669-70 ¶¶ 8-11 (2004), emphasized the importance of good faith efforts toward completion of a treatment program and, presumably, later relapse, as a condition to a disability annuity based on alcohol or drug addiction:

Under 5 U.S.C. § 8337(a), an employee shall be considered to be disabled only if, among other things, the employee is found by OPM to be unable, "because of disease or injury," to render useful and efficient service in the employee's position. Thus, when an employee "is unable to render useful and efficient service because that employee fails or refuses to follow or accept normal treatment, it is wholly proper to say that the employee's disability flows, not from the disease or injury itself (as the statute requires), but from the employee's voluntary failure or refusal to take the available corrective or ameliorative action." Baker v. Office of Personnel Management, 782 F.2d 993, 994 (Fed. Cir. 1986). Congress "evidently wanted the 'disease or injury' to be the fount of the findings of disability, not the applicant's unreasonable refusal to accept proffered and reasonable medical or therapeutic help." Id. Thus, the Board has found that an applicant for disability retirement does not meet the requirements of 5 U.S.C. § 8337(a) if she refuses to continue with recommended psychological therapy, Barbagallo v. Office of Personnel Management, 83 M.S.P.R. 557, ¶ 12 (1999), or if she refuses to take a prescribed antidepressant, Wilkey-Marzin v. Office of Personnel Management, 82 M.S.P.R. 200, ¶ 15 (1999).

The evidence in this case shows that the appellant did not complete an alcohol and drug treatment program. For example, in May 1997, after three months in an outpatient program, a therapist wrote that the appellant "has not engaged in treatment. His attendance is both sporadic and inconsistent. The therapist also wrote that the appellant has not attended self-help groups and "has continued to use drugs at least 2x/month." Although the appellant completed a one-day "Detox" program at a hospital in July 1997, after an eleven-day admission in October 1997, the appellant's case manager wrote that the appellant "always had [an] excuse as to why he could not get...treatment plan assignments, so he only completed one of his assignments." On March 27, 1998, and May 6, 1998, the appellant tested positive for both cocaine and marijuana when he was attending another treatment program. In addition, on September 30, 1998, the appellant was discharged from a treatment program for "failure to comply with the program (multiple unexcused absences)." Testimony at the hearing indicated that the appellant was discharged in August 2000 from a Veterans Administration treatment program for failure to attend. The appellant testified at the hearing that he was still using alcohol and narcotics.

Under Winslett v. Office of Personnel Management, 30 M.S.P.R. 293, 296-97 (1986), an applicant for disability retirement benefits who claims addiction to alcohol as a disabling condition must show that he is unable to maintain control of his alcoholism with a treatment program. Here, the appellant failed to even complete an alcohol and drug treatment program. The appellant also has not shown that his refusal to accept facially reasonable treatment was not voluntary. Cf. Frontan v. Office of Personnel Management, 90 M.S.P.R. 427, ¶ 12 (2001) (the appellant's initial reluctance to take prescribed medication was not a voluntary decision, but was a symptom of her psychiatric illness). If the appellant had completed a treatment program and nevertheless continued to use drugs and alcohol, he could have established that the addiction was not under his control. Thus, we find that his failure to complete a treatment program emphasizes his failure to prove that any addiction could not be controlled and bars him from entitlement to disability benefits. See Wilkey-Marzin, 82 M.S.P.R. 200, ¶ 15.

Finally, we reject the AJ's finding that the situation presented in this case falls within the Mullins-Howard exception to the general rule that medical evidence must provide a reasoned explanation of how certain aspects of a particular condition render the employee unable to perform specific work requirements. First, there is no argument that the appellant has demonstrated that he would otherwise be entitled to disability benefits based upon the general rule that requires evidence showing that he was unable to perform the specific duties of his job. Second, there is no precedent for finding that an appellant who failed to complete drug and alcohol treatment and failed to end his dependency is entitled to disability retirement benefits. The facts of this case are insufficient for the Board to establish new precedent in this area of disability retirement benefits. In any event, as set forth above, there is sufficient evidence in the record to support a finding that the appellant is not entitled to disability retirement benefits, the appellant has not come forward with evidence to rebut OPM's assertion that he is not entitled to benefits, and the appellant has not met his burden of persuasion to establish entitlement to disability retirement.
