B. TERMINATION BY ADVERSE ACTION OF APPOINTMENT SECURED BY FRAUD; OPM SUITABILITY action

Our analysis advances to the individual who gets onto the federal payroll through subterfuge or deceit, most likely through false statements on an employment application. Falsification discovered after hiring, even years after hiring, may lead to action against the employee. Other problems concerning the legitimacy of the appointment involve errors relating to how the individual was selected or how the agency viewed the employee's qualifications for the job. The usual approach to a falsified employment action is termination by adverse action of a tenured employee. See Aguzie in Hopper v. OPM, 118 MSPR 608, 609-15 ¶¶ 1-13, 2012 MSPB 122 (2012) (aff'd, sub nom. Archuleta v. Hopper, 773 F.3d 1289 (Fed. Cir. 2014), 2014 opinion replaced by Archuleta v. Hopper, 786 F.3d 1340 (Fed. Cir. 2015). [Refer to Chapter 1, "Relation of OPM-Directed Suitability Removal and Adverse Action Appeal Rights, Mitigation Analysis; Aguzie."] This action may be taken by the employing agency, 5 CFR 731.301-404 (Agency Suitability Action Procedures), or it may be directed by the Office of Personnel Management. 5 CFR 731.301-.304 (2022) ("OPM Suitability Action Procedures"). The employee has reply rights before action is taken. If OPM directs removal of the employee, it notifies the agency of the action and informs the employee of his MSPB appeal rights, described at 5 CFR 731.501 (2022). [Refer to Chapter 8 for agency-initiated adverse actions based on charges of falsification of documents under "Employment Application; Security Questionnaire; Lack of Candor"; refer to Chapter 1 for suitability appeals under "Suitability Determinations by Agencies Operating Under Authority Delegated by OPM."]

The procedure just described encompasses OPM action taken against individuals for reasons stated in 5 CFR 731.202(b) (2022):

(1) Misconduct or negligence in employment;

(2) Criminal or dishonest conduct;

(3) Material, intentional false statement, or deception or fraud in examination or appointment;

(4) Refusal to furnish testimony as required by § 5.4 of this chapter;

(5) Alcohol abuse, without evidence of substantial rehabilitation, of a nature and duration that suggests that the applicant or appointee would be prevented from performing the duties of the position in question, or would constitute a direct threat to the property or safety of the applicant or appointee or others;

(6) Illegal use of narcotics, drugs, or other controlled substances without evidence of substantial rehabilitation;

(7) Knowing and willful engagement in acts or activities designed to overthrow the U.S. Government by force; and

(8) Any statutory or regulatory bar which prevents the lawful employment of the person involved in the position in question.

Through the end of 2000, once OPM instructed the agency to remove the appointee and an appeal was taken, the agency was to suspend the appointee effective the day following the date that the removal was to have been effected pending adjudication of the appeal, unless the agency desired to retain the appointee in an active duty status for that period. Regulations issued at the end of 2000, suspended for a period of time during the regulation review activities of the incoming Administration and now effective, require that the employee be separated and then appeal the separation, as is the case with adverse actions in all cases other than those of administrative law judges. See 5 CFR 731.304, .501 (2001).

Through 1992 (when the regulatory provisions were dropped), there was another procedure, described at 5 CFR Part 754 and entitled "suitability disqualification action," that permitted OPM to take, or instruct an agency to take, "a final suitability disqualification action to remove, or to take other disciplinary action against an applicant or eligible for appointment in, or an appointee or employee in, the competitive service." The grounds for the action were not specifically defined; by implication one could say that they would encompass grounds or time limitations other than those at 5 CFR 731.202. If action was taken against the employee under Part 754 and the employee appealed, 5 CFR 754.105(b) gave a competitive service employee the right to be retained in a pay status pending completion of the MSPB appeal. This included the periods encompassed by both the appeal and the PFR. Shelton v. OPM, 28 MSPR 389, 391 (1985); see Kissner v. OPM, 28 MSPR 392, 394-95 (1985) ("the appeal process is not completed until the Board has issued a final decision...appellant in this case has an entitlement to remain in an active duty status until the Board issues a final decision in his appeal"). The appeal only arose if the OPM procedure was followed. OPM was not required to direct a termination even if it found the employee unsuitable. If OPM's role in a termination was unclear, the appellant had to show that OPM ordered the termination if the Board was to secure jurisdiction over a termination otherwise outside its purview, e.g., the termination of a probationary employee. See McChesney v. DOJ, 55 MSPR 512, 515 (1992). Under law predating the Reform Act, if the Civil Service Commission refused to approve the selection of an individual for a position, the employing agency had not taken an appealable adverse action against her. See Vukonich v. Civil Serv. Comm'n, 589 F.2d 494 (10th Cir. 1978).

OPM has an additional role in policing appointments within the competitive service. Special Counsel v. Malone, 84 MSPR 342, 359-60 ¶¶ 26-27 (1999), explained as to the authority of OPM, and the authority of OSC, when an agency appears to disregard a Board order barring employment of an individual involved in an OSC prosecution:

A mechanism exists to ensure that an agency does not rehire an employee immediately after effectuating a Board removal order. The Special Counsel may petition the Board for enforcement of a removal order if the Special Counsel believes that the agency has not complied with that order. 5 U.S.C. § 1204(a)(2); 5 CFR § 1201.182(b). The Special Counsel may also file a complaint seeking disciplinary action against any employee who "knowingly and willfully refused or failed to comply with an order of the Merit Systems Protection Board." 5 U.S.C. § 1215(a)(1)(C).

Further, appointment to the federal service generally requires compliance with rigorous rules and procedures, including a suitability determination based in part on the applicant's past employment history and the reasons for separation from prior employment. See 5 CFR §§ 731.202 (suitability determinations), 731.601 (reemployment eligibility following removal for cause or resignation when faced with charges); McFalls v. Office of Personnel Management, 72 MSPR 252, 256 (1996); Katchmeric v. Office of Personnel Management, 33 MSPR 118, 119 (1987) (employee's resignation after her removal was proposed was considered in determining her suitability for federal employment). Thus, even when an employee is removed for egregious misconduct, neither the Board nor employing agencies impose a debarment,[12] see 5 CFR part 752, and the removed individual potentially could apply for the same or another position the day after he has been removed.[13] This does not mean that the individual will be rehired, however, because the reasons for his prior separation from federal employment generally will be considered before he is hired. Indeed, OPM may order the debarment of an applicant or appointee based on misconduct in prior employment that would impair the efficiency of the service. 5 CFR §§ 731.202(b)(1) and 731.304(a). Thus, a prior removal for cause or a resignation pending removal for cause will generally have the effect of debarring the removed individual from federal service for at least a number of years. Cf. 5 CFR § 731.601 (reemployment eligibility).

[12] See, e.g., Mack v. Department of the Navy, 28 MSPR 288 (1985) (removal for being charged with murder); McCray v. Department of the Army, I MSPR 460 (1980) (removal for fatally shooting another person following a traffic incident).

[13] See Wathen v. United States, 208 Ct. Cl. 342, 359-61 (1975) (an Internal Revenue Service employee sought unsuccessfully to be reinstated to his former position after he was removed for killing his fiancée).

OPM has authority to correct appointments that have been made through a regulatory error. Avalos v. DHUD, 963 F.3d 1360, 1364 n.2 (Fed. Cir. 2020), described the process or regularization of an appointment:

"Case law has described regularizing an appointment as correcting the illegal component of the appointment." Endres v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, 107 M.S.P.R. 455, 463 (2007) ("[B]ecause the agency has not shown that [petitioner's] appointment was regularized by either a variation or by correcting the illegal component of the appointment, [petitioner's] appointment...is not valid."), enforcement dismissed, 108 M.S.P.R. 606 (2008); see also Avalos v. Dep't of Hous. & Urban Dev., No. DE-0752-18-0004-I-1, at 6 (M.S.P.B. July 19, 2018) ("Various witnesses testified that, to 'regularize' an appointment means to find a legal appointment authority, or to release or remove the employee."); Regularize, BLACK'S LAW DICTIONARY (11th ed. 2019) ("To make (a situation that has existed for some time) legal or official.").
