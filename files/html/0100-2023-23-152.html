b. Performance Appraisal Dispute

Although the duty to restore an employee to the status quo does not protect the employee from poor performance following restoration, unless deficiencies occur, the agency must give the employee the same ratings on restoration that she would have received had there been no removal. The reasoning was provided in an enforcement proceeding arising from an adverse action appeal, Normoyle v. Dept. of Air Force, 63 MSPR 391, 394-96 (1994):

The last performance appraisal the appellant received before the agency removed her covered the period from July 1991 through June 1992. The first one she received after the agency restored her to her position covered the period from July 1992 through June 1993. Both appraisals were issued under a system that provided for a rating from one to nine on each performance element, as well as one overall performance rating for the employee. As the agency has indicated, the appellant received the same "fully successful" overall performance rating on the last performance appraisal she received before her removal, that she received on the appraisal issued shortly after she was restored to her position. Her post-restoration ratings on the nine performance elements, however, were all significantly lower than the element ratings she received before her removal. Before her removal, she received a rating of seven, signifying "above fully successful" performance, on each performance element. After her restoration, she received a rating of only five, signifying "fully successful" performance, on each element.

As the appellant has pointed out, an employee with a relatively unfavorable performance appraisal is at a disadvantage in competing for promotions and other assignments. This is true even when only the individual element ratings have been lowered, and the overall performance rating remains the same, since prospective employers consider element ratings as well as overall ratings when deciding whether to select an applicant. An employee who has been given improperly lowered performance element ratings on her restoration therefore has not been restored to the status quo ante. See Zamora v. Department of Defense, 58 MSPR 544, 547 (1993) (because a performance appraisal that is lowered because of alleged misconduct is punitive in nature, it is a penalty within the meaning of a settlement agreement resolving a removal based on the alleged misconduct). The agency in this case argues, however, that, although the performance ratings it issued in July 1993 for the appellant and others covered the 1-year period preceding that month, those ratings did not go into effect until the following September, about 4 months after the appellant was restored. It argues further, therefore, that the appellant was restored with the same performance ratings that she received in July 1992, before she was removed, and that she accordingly was restored to the status quo ante.

We do not find these arguments persuasive. The fact that performance ratings or other actions will have only a delayed effect does not preclude a finding that they are inconsistent with an order to restore an employee. The amount of leave credited to an employee on his restoration, for example, may not affect the employee for some time after the restoration, since the employee may not have any immediate need for the disputed hours of leave. The Board will, however, review the amount of leave credited in determining whether an employee has been properly restored. See, e.g., Gay v. United States Postal Service, 49 MSPR 219, 231-32 (1991). In addition, the period covered by the 1993 appraisal includes and consists largely of the time--nearly 7 months--during which the appellant was absent because of her removal. Clearly, the manner in which the appellant's performance was evaluated during this period is relevant in determining whether the appellant was restored to the status quo ante--i.e., in determining whether the appellant was restored so as to place her in the same position in which she would have been had she not been removed.

We note further that the administrative judge, in finding that the appellant had failed to show noncompliance with respect to this matter, stated that she could "find no law, rule, or regulation...requiring the agency to give the appellant a rating higher than 'fully successful' during the time she was not at work because of her wrongful removal." Because the agency had given the appellant a "fully successful" rating, the administrative judge found that the agency had complied with the initial decision. We know of no basis, however, for holding that an employee, regardless of the quality of her performance, is entitled to no more than "fully successful" performance ratings on her restoration. If this were true, only employees who received performance ratings no higher than "fully successful" before they were removed would be entitled to restoration to the status quo ante after their removals were overturned. Because all employees whose removals are ordered cancelled by a Board decision are entitled to be restored, as nearly as possible, to the status quo ante, we hold that, to be in compliance with such an order, the agency must give the employee the same ratings on restoration that she would have received in the absence of the removal. See Zamora, 58 MSPR at 546-47 (a settlement agreement substituting a suspension for a removal required the agency to give the appellant the same rating he would have received in the absence of the misconduct giving rise to the removal).

The duty to restore an employee to the status quo ante does not, of course, protect an employee from the consequences of performance deficiencies that may occur after the employee's restoration, or from the consequences of any deficiencies that, although they occurred before the removal, were unrelated to that action. In this case, however, there is no indication that the agency's issuance of relatively low performance ratings in 1993 resulted from any performance deficiencies occurring after the appellant's return to duty or from any other deficiencies unrelated to the removal. In fact, the 1993 appraisal itself indicates that it is a "presumptive writing," and that the appellant "was not observed a sufficient time to assign an official rating of record." In light of these statements, along with the agency's failure to present any evidence that the appellant's lowered ratings were based on legitimate considerations, we find that the agency has failed to comply with the order to restore the appellant to the status quo ante. Accordingly, we REVERSE the part of the compliance initial decision that concerns the appellant's 1993 performance appraisal and find that the appellant is entitled to a performance appraisal including the same element ratings included in her 1992 appraisal.
