b. Legal Error by OPM

The "knew or should have known" entitlement standard is applied if OPM misconstrues the intent of law or regulations defining retirement entitlements. In Davis v. OPM, 64 MSPR 6, 11-12 (1994), OPM incorrectly determined that a disability retirement could only be awarded if a disability was incurred while in a currently held CSRS position. OPM's misinterpretation of the retirement statute entitled the appellant to counsel fees:

With respect to the second of these considerations, we note that the Board's reversal of OPM's reconsideration decision was not based on any evidence that the appellant had failed to present to OPM. Instead, as we have indicated above, it was based on a conclusion that OPM's interpretation of applicable and statutory provisions was incorrect.

...

With respect to the fifth Allen category, the Board has held that an agency "knew or should have known that it would not prevail on the merits" of a retirement reconsideration decision if it "lacked a reasonable and supportable explanation for its position." Kent, 33 MSPR at 367. The agency argued below that a fee award was not warranted under this category because it "believed that it's [sic] decision to deny the appellant's application...was correct based on it's [sic] interpretation of the statues [sic] and regulation." We note, however, that this Allen category applies not only to cases in which an agency knew it could not prevail on the merits, but also to cases in which it should have known it could not prevail.

The administrative judge seems to have concluded that the agency could not have been expected to have known it would not prevail because the Board's decision in this case was inconsistent both with OPM's regulations and with previous Board decisions. We do not agree. While the administrative judge cited a regulatory provision and decisions indicating that a disability retirement applicant is required to show that he is unable to perform the duties of his current position...this requirement was not an issue in the appellant's appeal, either while the appeal was pending before the administrative judge or while it was pending before the Board. Instead, the issue was whether a showing that the appellant was disabled before he was assigned to the position in question, but while he was employed under the CSRS, precluded a finding that the appellant was entitled to a disability annuity. Finally, the fact that the Board had not addressed this issue before it decided the appellant's appeal does not preclude a finding that the agency should have known that it would not prevail. As we have noted above and in our decision on the merits of the appeal, OPM cited no support for its position regarding the issue, and none was apparent to us. We therefore find that the agency failed to provide a reasonable and supportable explanation for its disallowance of the appellant's application, and that the appellant accordingly is entitled to an award of attorney fees under the fifth Allen category. See Kent, 33 MSPR at 367.[2]

[2] OPM also argued below that an attorney fee award was not warranted because the administrative judge affirmed its reconsideration decision. We know of no basis, however, for finding that an administrative judge's erroneously [sic] ruling in favor of an agency precludes either a finding that a reconsideration decision was clearly without merit or a finding that the agency should have known that it would not prevail on the merits.

See Gammill v. OPM, 35 MSPR 279, 282-83 (1987) (fees denied because OPM reasonably interpreted its entitlement regulations concerning the effect of a mobility agreement upon discontinued service annuities, and because OPM was not aware that the appellant specifically challenged a settlement agreement he made with his employer that defined the conditions of his resignation and application for the annuity).
