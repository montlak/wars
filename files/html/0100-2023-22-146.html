a. Early Treatment by MSPB of Contingent Adjustments

Early in the Board's history, before it had much experience with counsel fee adjudications, the Board decided that it would allow "contingent" compensation for a limited class of cases. A multiplier of what would otherwise be a reasonable hourly rate would be allowed to compensate for certain risks borne by counsel. Kling v. DOJ, 2 MSPR 464, 473, 2 MSPB 620 (1980) (relying on Johnson v. Georgia Highway Express, Inc., 488 F.2d 714 (5th Cir. 1974)), listed the following as appropriate factors for enhancement: quality of professional performance, unusual time constraints, an unusually unpopular cause, or a contingency factor when the attorney has agreed to be paid only if successful. Gibson v. DOJ, 12 MSPR 436, 444, 11 MSPB 78 (1982), added an enhancement to recognize and reward achievements of a particularly resourceful attorney who secures a substantial benefit for his clients with a minimum of time invested. Examples of early application of Board law to counsels' petitions to adjust statutory fees for contingent risks include the following: Bivens v. Dept. of Navy, 52 MSPR 602, 605 (1992); Brown v. DHHS, 42 MSPR 291, 295 (1989); Helt v. VA, 34 MSPR 165, 169 (1987); Ferebee v. Dept. of Navy, 33 MSPR 447, 449 (1987); Brooks v. Dept. of Transp., 33 MSPR 399, 400 n.2, 402 (1987) (lodestar computed by the product of time and hourly rate was appropriate to compute fees recovered under contingency arrangement); Sullivan v. Dept. of Labor, 26 MSPR 629, 631 (1985); Gibson v. DOJ, 12 MSPR 436, 442, 11 MSPB 78, 83 (1982); Vandiver v. GAO, 7 MSPR 589, 590-91, 7 MSPB 429 (1981). Few decisions permitted a contingency adjustment. See, e.g., Andress v. Dept. of Transp., 23 MSPR 260, 264 (1984) (allowing contingency enhancement).

Before the Supreme Court became enmeshed in the complexities of contingency adjustments under federal fee-shifting statutes, the Federal Circuit chided the Board for failing to readily permit contingency adjustments for risk of loss. Crumbaker v. MSPB, 781 F.2d 191 (Fed. Cir. 1986), on remand, Crumbaker v. Dept. of Labor, 40 MSPR 71, 73 (1989). Then the Supreme Court became involved in the contingency adjustment business through litigation involving fee-shifting provisions of the Clean Air Act. The Supreme Court issued two decisions in Delaware Valley. The first decision, Pennsylvania v. Delaware Valley Citizens' Council for Clean Air, 478 U.S. 546, 106 S. Ct. 3088 (1986), set the stage for the Court's resolution of the contingency compensation in its second decision, reported at 483 U.S. 711, 107 S. Ct. 3078 (1987). In the second decision, Pennsylvania v. Delaware Valley Citizens' Council for Clean Air, 483 U.S. 711, 107 S. Ct. 3078 (1987) (Delaware Valley II), the Court issued a split opinion: the majority favored a rule that would limit contingency enhancement of statutory fee awards to rare cases, and then only to perhaps a maximum of 30% enhancement; the dissenters wanted a far more flexible role for contingency enhancements. The controlling opinion of the Court in Delaware Valley II, however, was the concurring opinion of Justice O'Connor, who set two basic requirements for a contingency enhancement: without an adjustment for risk, the prevailing party would have faced substantial difficulties in finding counsel in the local or other relevant market; and enhancement for contingency must reflect the difference in market treatment of contingent fee cases as a class, rather than the riskiness of any particular case.

Justice O'Connor's opinion was the subject of considerable commentary. See, e.g., Fadhl, 859 F.2d at 650, supra; Blum, 888 F.2d at 981, supra; Alberti, 896 F.2d at 935, supra. Remarks contained in the plurality opinion about rare or exceptional cases were not considered to be the controlling opinion of the court. See Wells v. Bowen, 855 F.2d 37, 44 (2d Cir. 1988) (noting that Justice O'Connor's stated conditions for enhancement were broader than those allowed by the plurality and narrower than those allowed by the dissenters); McKenzie v. Kennickell, 684 F. Supp. 1097, 1099 n.7 (D.D.C. 1988) (interpreting Justice O'Connor's opinion); see also McKenzie v. Kennickell, 875 F.2d 330 (D.C. Cir. 1989); Thompson v. Kennickell, 710 F. Supp. 1, 5, 8 (D.D.C. 1989); Blum v. Witco Chem. Corp., 702 F. Supp. 493, 499 (D.N.J. 1988); Palmer v. Shultz, 679 F. Supp. 68 (D.D.C. 1988). The debate raged on concerning the amount of contingency adjustment allowable pursuant to Delaware Valley. See Thompson v. Kennickell, 710 F. Supp. 1 (D.D.C. 1989); Palmer v. Shultz, 679 F. Supp. 68 (D.D.C. 1988); Black Grievance Comm. v. Philadelphia Elec. Co., 690 F. Supp. 1393, 1401 (E.D. Pa. 1988); Green v. Dept. of Navy, 43 MSPR 34, 37 (1989). More debate occurred on compensation of lawyers who undertook litigation on the basis of partially contingent fee arrangements. See Palmer v. Shultz, 679 F. Supp. 68 (D.D.C. 1988); Chalmers v. City of Los Angeles, 676 F. Supp. 1515, 1525 (C.D. Cal. 1987); Lizut v. Dept. of Army, 51 MSPR 549, 555 (1991).

The Delaware Valley cases caused more confusion than infusion of fees to civil service lawyers. The Board, having fought the notion of contingency enhancements, doubtless was well pleased when the Supreme Court ruled in City of Burlington v. Dague, 112 S. Ct. 2638 (1992), that fee-shifting statutes providing for a reasonable fee award to a party prevailing against the federal government do not permit enhancement of a fee award to reflect the fact that a party's attorneys were retained on a contingent fee basis. Consistent with Dague, the Board now determines that no contingency enhancement may be awarded under 5 USC 7701(g)(1) or (2). Contrary decisions were overruled. See Bivens v. Dept. of Navy, 55 MSPR 662, 663 n.1 (1992); Pecotte v. Dept. of Air Force, 55 MSPR 165, 168 (1992) ("[t]he Board may not award contingency enhancements under the authority of 5 U.S.C. § 7701(g)(2). It follows further that the prohibition against contingency enhancements extends to the Board's authority to award 'reasonable attorney fees' under the authority of 5 U.S.C. § 7701(g)(1), as well. We can discern no reason to find that Dague's prohibition against contingency enhancements should be limited to section 7701(g)(2) and not extend to section 7701(g)(1)."); Clark v. Dept. of Army, 55 MSPR 76, 79 (1992) (no contingency enhancement may be awarded under 5 USC 7701(g)(1) nor (2)).

For more information on the Board's treatment of contingency cases before the demise of contingency adjustments for statutory fee awards, refer to: Jones v. Dept. of Navy, 51 MSPR 542, 547-48 (1991) (discussing what constituted the relevant market of lawyers and how fees were measured in that market); Wilkins v. Dept. of Navy, 51 MSPR 290, 294 n.3 (1991) (locus of the relevant market); Green v. Dept. of Navy, 43 MSPR 34, 38 (1989) (quantum of proof needed to justify the enhancement and the problem of reconciling a particular enhancement in one location with a higher rate sought in another location); cf. Rothschild v. DHUD, 54 MSPR 238, 243-44 (1992) (burden of proof was on the counsel seeking the contingency enhancement); Peterson v. USPS, 53 MSPR 663, 666 (1992) (detailed evidence is required to justify an enhancement permitted under the Delaware Valley decision); Lizut v. Dept. of Army, 51 MSPR 549, 554-55 (1991) (setting original criteria for enhancement of fees; Brown v. DHHS, 50 MSPR 523, 529-32 (1991) (each adjustment was to be based on the facts of an individual case); Kelly v. Dept. of Navy, 43 MSPR 430, 437 (1990) (role of a retainer agreement relative to a contingency adjustment); Hutchison v. DHHS, 42 MSPR 302, 311 n.6 (1989) (impact of contingency enhancement on collateral aspects of a case, e.g., compliance).
