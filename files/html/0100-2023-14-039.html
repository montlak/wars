2. Articulation of Relevant Factors

Douglas identifies a dozen or more mitigating factors of a general nature. Each case presents its specific mitigating circumstances. An issue may arise concerning the degree of attention any of these factors has or should have received. The Board desires, but does not really require of agencies, articulation of relevant factors and explication of the balancing process. In theory, if the agency does not articulate the relevant factors, the Board determines the relevant factors and decides whether the agency penalty was within tolerable limits of discretion. The Board's approach was summarized in Gleason v. Dept. of Army, 38 MSPR 547, 550 (1988): "An agency need not demonstrate that it considered all mitigating factors in determining the penalty, and the administrative judge need not contemplate mitigating factors not identified by the appellant as significant." See Watson v. Dept. of Transp., 49 MSPR 509, 518 (1991) ("It is not a deciding official's responsibility to provide a list of circumstances that might have caused him to adopt a lesser penalty. Rather, the deciding official is only required to consider all of the relevant mitigating factors that actually exist in a given case and exercise management discretion within tolerable limits of reasonableness.").

Despite decisions affirming agency penalty decisions made on the basis of cursory agency reviews of Douglas factors, a decision may surface that pierces the veil of deference when an agency makes no effort to demonstrate principled application of Douglas factors, as in Brown v. USPS, 64 MSPR 425, 432 (1994), still sustaining removal:

The appellant correctly asserts that the administrative judge failed to consider mitigating factors, such as the appellant's 23 years of Federal service with no prior disciplinary record, in assessing the reasonableness of the penalty. We note that the agency's deciding official may have failed to consider relevant mitigating factors. Although the removal decision notice states, in part, that "I have given full consideration to your written answer to [the notice of proposed removal] and all other evidence of record," the decision notice does not specify whether the deciding official considered any mitigating factors, and the record does not contain a copy of the appellant's September 27, 1993 response to the proposed removal. Moreover, the deciding official failed to indicate during his testimony that he considered relevant mitigating factors.

In Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 MSPB 313, 5 MSPR 280, 306 (1981), the Board stated that it will review an agency-imposed penalty only to determine if the agency considered all the relevant factors and exercised management discretion within tolerable limits of reasonableness. In Douglas, the Board set forth a list of generally recognized relevant factors. See id. 5 MSPR at 305-06. Here, the record contains no evidence that the agency considered factors favoring mitigation, as required by Douglas. An agency's determination of an appropriate penalty is not entitled to deference when the deciding official does not consider any of the relevant mitigating circumstances.
