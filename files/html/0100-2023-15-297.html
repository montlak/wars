3. Responsibility for Work Error

It is insufficient to show that something occurred on the job that was incorrect or improper. There must be a demonstration of personal fault. That a report was typed incorrectly does not show that the appellant was negligent if she says that the handwriting of the supervisor was difficult to decipher. Davies v. USDA, 5 MSPR 251, 255, 5 MSPB 291 (1981); see Bolling, supra. If an individual is charged with work errors, responsibility may be immediately apparent because of the individual's control over the work; however, if there are other factors that may have interceded, e.g., malfunctions of equipment, the AJ will need to ascertain what caused the errors. See Lehnerd v. OPM, 55 MSPR 170, 172-73 (1992). If the appellant is charged with violating an agency regulation and the defense is that the employee was unaware of the regulation, the Board considers if the appellant should have known of the restriction. The Board may examine the duties of the position and past warnings to the employee. See, e.g., Brown v. Dept. of Air Force, 67 MSPR 500, 506 (1995).

If an employee acts on the advice of personnel on whom it is reasonable to rely, e.g., personnel specialists or lawyers, resulting violation of law cannot, unless there are other factors, be considered willful. Mauro v. Dept. of Navy, 35 MSPR 86, 94 (1987) (citing Foster v. Dept. of Labor, 31 MSPR 74, 77 (1986)). An employee will occasionally be told to perform work in a particular way and then, if higher-level management finds that the directions and the resultant work product are wrong, the employee takes the rap. This was the situation of a HUD employee directed by management to arrive at a predetermined, although not necessarily justified, result on some mortgage insurance application forms. If the employee is directed to reach a particular result, he cannot be disciplined for doing the task in the manner directed. Government employees may not refuse an instruction to do certain work because they challenge its propriety. Failure to perform as directed is undertaken at the risk of being deemed insubordinate. It would be grossly inequitable to suspend an employee for responding to a supervisor's directive, regardless of the employee's perception of the propriety of the order. Rose v. DHUD, 26 MSPR 356, 360 (1985).
