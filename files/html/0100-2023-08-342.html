b. Foreign Service

Under Isabella v. Dept. of State, 102 MSPR 259, 262-64 ¶¶ 8-13, 2006 MSPB 157 (2006), VEOA applies to certain Foreign Service appointments:

[B]elow and in its opposition to the petition for review, the agency questioned whether the VEOA applies to Foreign Service Act appointments. The Board has not decided this issue previously.

The Foreign Service Act of 1980 provides that the Secretary of State may appoint members of the Foreign Service "in accordance with this chapter and such regulations as the Secretary may prescribe." 22 U.S.C. § 3943. Further, the "Secretary [of State] shall prescribe, as appropriate, written, oral, physical, foreign language, and other examinations for appointment to the [Foreign] Service." 22 U.S.C. § 3941(b).

While the Foreign Service is to be administered "to the extent practicable in conformity with general policies and regulations of the Government" (22 U.S.C. § 3925), the Foreign Service has its own appointing authority (22 U.S.C. §§ 3941-3952), compensation system (22 U.S.C. §§ 3961-3974), forum for appealing personnel actions (22 U.S.C. §§ 4131-4140), and retirement system (22 U.S.C. §§ 4041-4069c-1), separate from those set forth in Title 5 of the United States Code for the majority of the civil service. Nevertheless, this does not mean that the Title 5 and Title 22 personnel systems are completely separate and independent. The United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit (Federal Circuit) has held that, based on the general framework of Title 5, "it is assumed that, absent other overriding provisions of law, [T]itle 5 applies to executive agencies." James v. Von Zemenszky, 284 F.3d 1310, 1320 (Fed. Cir.), reh'g denied, 310 F.3d 1364 (Fed. Cir. 2002). In that case, the Federal Circuit found that Title 5 reduction-in-force provisions applied to Veterans Health Administration (VHA) health-care professionals, despite the fact that Title 38 of the United States Code contains provisions separate from Title 5 relating to the appointment of VHA health care professionals, collective bargaining and personnel administration, pay for health care personnel, and disciplinary and grievance procedures. Id. at 1319-20. As part of the Department of State, the Foreign Service is also in the executive branch. See 5 U.S.C. § 101.

Furthermore, 22 U.S.C. § 4823 provides that "Special agent positions shall be filled in accordance with the provisions of the Foreign Service Act of 1980 (22 U.S.C. § 3901 et seq.) and Title 5." That statute further states that the Secretary of State shall prescribe the qualifications for Foreign Service Diplomatic Security Special Agent positions, and may include maximum entry age restrictions. Id. Accordingly, we find that Title 5, including the VEOA, applies to the appointment process for Foreign Service Diplomatic Security Special Agent positions, such as the one at issue here.[1]

[1] With respect to veterans' preference, we also note that the Foreign Service Act provides that "[t]he fact that an applicant for appointment as a Foreign Service officer candidate is a veteran or disabled veteran shall be considered an affirmative factor in making such appointments." 22 U.S.C. § 3941(c) (emphasis supplied). The VEOA may apply to Foreign Service officer appointments because 22 U.S.C. § 3941(c) is a statute related to veterans' preference. See 5 U.S.C. § 3330a(a)(1); see also 5 U.S.C. § 2302(e)(1)(D) (citing section 301(c) of the Foreign Service Act of 1980, which has been codified at 22 U.S.C. § 3941(c), as a "veterans' preference requirement"). However, even if this is considered a statute related to veterans' preference, "Foreign Service officer" is a term of art that does not apply to the position in question. See 22 U.S.C. §§ 3903, 3942, 3943. Thus, we need not determine at this time whether the Board has jurisdiction over VEOA appeals filed by Foreign Service officer candidates.

The provision for waiver of age requirements for preference eligibles specifies that it applies to the competitive civil service. 5 U.S.C. § 3312(a)(1). All civilian positions in the executive branch are competitive civil service positions unless (1) they are specifically excepted from the competitive civil service by statute, (2) they are positions to which appointments are made by nomination for confirmation by the Senate, or (3) they are positions in the Senior Executive Service. 5 U.S.C. § 2102(a); 5 C.F.R. § 212.101(a). The Special Agent position is not in the Senior Executive Service and appointment to the position is not made by nomination for confirmation by the Senate.

It is not clear from the record whether the Special Agent position at issue has been specifically excepted from the competitive service. Because the application process for the Special Agent position included an examination, it is possible that the position is in the competitive service. See 5 U.S.C. § 3304. In that case, 5 U.S.C. § 3312(a)(1) would apply to the selection process for the position. In any event, 5 U.S.C. § 3320 provides that:

[t]he nominating or appointing authority shall select for appointment to each vacancy in the excepted service in the executive branch and in the government of the District of Columbia from the qualified applicants in the same manner and under the same conditions required for the competitive service by sections 3308-3318 of this title. This section does not apply to an appointment required by Congress to be confirmed by, or made with the advice and consent of, the Senate.

Therefore, even if this Special Agent position is in the excepted service, the provisions of 5 U.S.C. § 3312 would apply to the selection process for this position in the same manner and under the same conditions as the selection process for a position in the competitive service. Accordingly, we find that 5 U.S.C. § 3312(a)(1) applies to the selection process for the Special Agent position at issue in this appeal either directly, if the position is in the competitive service, or indirectly, pursuant to 5 U.S.C. § 3320, if the position is in the excepted service.[2] We further find that the appellant has made a nonfrivolous allegation that the agency violated his rights under a statute related to veterans' preference and has established jurisdiction over his appeal. Because the record does not indicate whether the age requirement for this position is essential to the performance of its duties, we REMAND the appeal so that the record may be developed on this issue.

[2] If the Special Agent position is in the excepted service, the instant appeal is distinguishable from Patterson v. Department of the Interior, 424 F.3d 1151 (Fed. Cir. 2005), cert. denied, 126 S. Ct. 1797 (2006), in which the Federal Circuit found that the provisions of 5 U.S.C. § 3320 could not be applied literally to an excepted service attorney position for which the agency did not conduct an examination. Unlike the statutes related to competitive examinations enumerated in 5 U.S.C. § 3320 (5 U.S.C. §§ 3308-3311) and discussed in Patterson, which cannot not be applied to excepted service positions for which no examinations are given, 5 U.S.C. § 3312(a)(1) can be applied to age requirements for both excepted and competitive service positions.

Asatov v. AID, PH-3330-12-0145-I-1 (NP 1/2/2013) (aff'd, Asatov v. AID, 542 Fed. Appx. 937 (Fed. Cir. 2013 NP), post), determined sufficient to satisfy veterans preference requirements the agency's treatment of the appellant's application for a Foreign Service Officer Program Economist position based on "affirmative consideration" requirements for veterans under the agency's statutory appointment authority:

Under VEOA, a preference eligible who alleges that an agency has violated his rights under any statute or regulation relating to veterans' preference, and who has exhausted his rights under that section before the Department of Labor, may file an appeal with the Board. 5 U.S.C. § 3330a(a)(1), (d); Ruffin v. Department of the Treasury, 89 M.S.P.R. 396, ¶ 11 (2001). To be entitled to relief under VEOA, the appellant must prove by preponderant evidence that the agency's selection violated one or more of his statutory or regulatory veterans' preference rights. Dale v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 102 M.S.P.R. 646, ¶ 10 (2006).

The Foreign Service Act provides a separate statutory hiring authority for Foreign Service employees, and much of title 5 does not apply to them. 22 U.S.C. §§ 3941-3952. By statute, the agency head has the authority to make appointments (with exceptions not relevant here) and to promulgate regulations governing appointments. 22 U.S.C. § 3943. The agency is required by statute to consider veteran or disabled veteran status as "an affirmative factor" in making appointments. 22 U.S.C. § 3941(c). Section 3941(c) is a statute related to veterans' preference. Isabella v. Department of State, 102 M.S.P.R. 259, ¶ 11 n.1 (2006).

The appellant, a preference eligible, applied for the position of Foreign Service Officer Program Economist. The agency's policy was to award all preference-eligible applicants five additional points without regard to any disability, and, in accordance with its policy, the agency awarded the appellant five points during the application review phase. As a result, the appellant's application was forwarded for further consideration, whereupon it was determined that he did not possess the minimum educational requirements for the position. Because VEOA prohibits an agency from denying a preference eligible the opportunity to compete but does not provide that veterans will be considered for positions for which they are not qualified, the administrative judge correctly found that the appellant is not entitled to corrective action under VEOA. See Dale, 102 M.S.P.R. 646, ¶ 13.

The appellant asserts that the administrative judge's decision is inconsistent with the Board's decision in Isabella. We disagree. In Isabella, the Board held that title 5, including VEOA, applies to the appointment process for Foreign Service Diplomatic Security Special Agent Positions. The Board expressly stated, however, that, although VEOA may apply to Foreign Service officer appointments because 5 U.S.C. § 3941(c) is a statute related to veterans' preference, it need not determine at that time whether it had jurisdiction over VEOA appeals filed by Foreign Service Officer candidates. 102 M.S.P.R. 259, ¶ 11 n.1. VEOA ensures that a preference eligible employee, such as the appellant, has the opportunity to apply for such vacancies. 5 U.S.C. § 3304(f)(1); Abell v. Department of the Navy, 343 F.3d 1378, 1383 (Fed. Cir. 2003). The appellant received every consideration he was entitled to receive under VEOA as an applicant for a Foreign Service Officer position, and, therefore, it was not necessary for the administrative judge to address whether title 5 applies to the appellant as a Foreign Service Officer candidate.

The appellant also argues that the administrative judge failed to address his contention that 5 C.F.R. part 302 applies in this case. The administrative judge did not address the application of 5 C.F.R. part 302 but instead found that "the appellant would not have a right of appeal under 5 C.F.R. part 300 because the position in question was in the excepted service; 5 C.F.R. part 300 applies to the competitive service." The appellant correctly argues that 5 C.F.R. part 302 applies to appointments in the excepted service. Jarrard v. Social Security Administration, 115 M.S.P.R. 397, ¶ 10 (2010). Even assuming that 5 C.F.R. § 302.201(a) applies here, that regulation requires an agency to grant five additional points to preference eligibles under 5 U.S.C. § 2108(3)(A). It is undisputed that the agency complied with the veterans' preference provision set forth in 5 C.F.R. § 302.201(a) when it raised the appellant's initial system rating score from 94 to 99. Therefore, although the administrative judge did not address the appellant's argument concerning 5 C.F.R. part 302, her failure to do so was not prejudicial to the appellant's substantive rights and provides no basis for reversal of the initial decision. Panter v. Department of the Air Force, 22 M.S.P.R. 281, 282 (1984).

Affirming the Board, the Federal Circuit stated, Asatov v. AID, 542 Fed. Appx. 937, 939-40 (Fed. Cir. 2013 NP):

...Nonetheless, Mr. Asatov can establish a VEOA claim if he demonstrates that AID did not follow applicable veterans' preference statutes or regulations when it determined that he was not one of the best qualified candidates for the Foreign Service Officer position....It is undisputed that 22 U.S.C. § 3941(c), which provides that veteran status "shall be considered an affirmative factor" in making appointments to the Foreign Service, is applicable here. We agree with the Board that AID complied with this provision by adding five points to Mr. Asatov's examination score.

We also agree with the Board that Mr. Asatov "received every consideration he was entitled to receive under VEOA." As the AJ noted, the Foreign Service has its own appointment authority (22 U.S.C. §§ 3941-3952), compensation system (22 U.S.C. § 3961-3974), forum for appealing personnel actions (22 U.S.C. § 4131-4140), and retirement system (22 U.S.C. § 4041-4069c-1), separate from those set forth in Title 5 for the majority of the civil service. Although the Foreign Service is to be administered "to the extent practicable in conformity with general policies and regulations of the Government," 22 U.S.C. § 3925, we have held that particular sections of Title 5 may not apply to executive agencies where "overriding provisions of law" exist. See James v. Von Zemensky, 284 F.3d 1310, 1320 (Fed. Cir.), reh'g denied, 301 F.3d 1364 (Fed. Cir. 2002). As relevant to Mr. Asatov's case, the Foreign Service Act directly addresses veterans' preference and requires only that veterans' preference be "an affirmative factor" under the appointment procedures applicable to the Foreign Service. 22 U.S.C. § 3391(c). Therefore, the Board is correct that AID was not required to comply with any other provisions of Title 5 relating to veterans' preference, or with regulations in 5 C.F.R. part 302. See 5 C.F.R. 302.101(a) (providing that veteran preference regulations in part 302 apply to excepted positions "subject to a statutory requirement to follow the veteran provisions of title 5").
