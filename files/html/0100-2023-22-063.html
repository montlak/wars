10. Settlement

If a prevailing party claims fees after a case is settled, the AJ may be required to determine whether the agency knew or should have known, on the basis of the evidence available to it, that the Board would not sustain the action taken against the appellant. If there are multiple charges and one would suffice to support the action taken, the Board will deny fees on the basis of the one sustainable charge, if the agency demonstrates that it had credible, probative evidence to establish the charge and had a reasonable likelihood of prevailing on the merits of that charge. See DiGiulio v. Dept. of Treasury, 66 MSPR 659, 666 (1995). Under Moore v. Dept. of Army, 59 MSPR 261, 266 (1993), the same analysis defeats a claim based on the "clearly without merit" standard, involving an examination of the degree of fault on the employee's part and the existence of any reasonable basis for the agency's action. See Lokos v. EEOC, 44 MSPR 115, 120-21 (1990) (denying fees: when the Board considers whether fees should be awarded after a case is settled and it makes a determination of whether the agency knew or should have known that it would not prevail on the merits, the Board may consider whether the charges, if sustained, would have supported the penalty that was imposed), rev'd, Lokos v. EEOC, 928 F.2d 412 (Fed. Cir. 1991 NP) (circuit court determined that the Board took so long to adjudicate the fee dispute--more than three years at the PFR level--that fees were to be paid as a matter of equity: "In order to compensate claimants who have been deprived of benefits because of unreasonable delays in administrative proceedings which have violated claimants' statutory rights, the courts have fashioned equitable remedies, which may include payment of money damages. In so doing, the courts have undertaken to provide remedies which will balance efforts to afford claimants their rights with an appreciation of an agency's administrative problems. See White v. Mathews, 559 F.2d 852, 860 (2d Cir. 1977), cert. denied, 435 U.S. 908 (1978); Sharpe v. Harris, 621 F.2d 530, 531 (2d Cir. 1980); Lewis v. Heckler, 605 F. Supp. 9, 10-12 (E.D. Ky.1984). Under the circumstances of this case, we have determined that the appropriate equitable remedy to be provided in this case is to adopt the decision of the administrative judge as the basis for the court's decision and judgment."); Miller v. DOJ, 28 MSPR 669, 672 (1985) (following settlement of a fee petition based on the "knew or should have known" criterion: "[O]ur review of the record supports the presiding official's conclusion that had an initial decision on the merits been issued, there was a reasonable likelihood that the presiding official would have found removal for AWOL to be warranted.").

White v. USPS, 62 MSPR 600, 602 (1994), denied fees after the parties settled by reducing a penalty:

...An agency's penalty selection is part of the merits of the case, and an award of attorney fees is warranted where the agency knew or should have known that its choice of penalty would not be sustained. See Lambert v. Department of the Air Force, 34 MSPR 501, 505-07 (1987).

Here, the appellant contends, as he did below, that the agency knew or should have known that it could not prevail in its selection of the penalty of removal for a single act of sexual misconduct in light of the Board's case law, the appellant's length of satisfactory service (over nineteen years), and his lack of a prior disciplinary record. However, as the administrative judge correctly found, there is no per se rule that a single instance of sexual misconduct will not support the penalty of removal. Further, none of the cases cited by the appellant in support of his position presented the aggravating factors that the administrative judge noted here, such as: (1) The appellant's supervisory status, and the fact that he engaged in the sexual misconduct with a lower-ranking employee; (2) his lack of remorse; (3) his failure to acknowledge the misconduct; and (4) the disruption caused by the misconduct.

White v. USPS, 61 MSPR 194, 200 (1994), awarded fees based on violation of a settlement agreement:

The appellant also requests an award of attorney fees incurred in responding to the agency's exceptions to the administrative judge's recommendation. We find that an award of attorney fees is in the interest of justice. The agency knew or should have known that the district court order which did not adjudicate the merits of the settlement agreement could not excuse the agency's failure to comply with the agreement. The agency was aware that the arbitrator stated in his decision that he lacked jurisdiction to address the conflict between the settlement of the appellant's statutory claim and the collective bargaining agreement. There is no evidence that the agency ever sought resolution of this conflict in a proper forum. Under the circumstances, and considering the agency's repeated failure to adhere to its agreements or to take appropriate steps to seek a legal resolution of the conflict between the appellant and the union, we find that an award of attorney fees is warranted. The amount of the award will be determined by the administrative judge on the basis of a request from the appellant with supporting documentation and the agency's response.
