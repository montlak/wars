4. Dismissal Without Prejudice to Refile ("DWOP")

As a general rule, withdrawal of an appeal is an act of finality, resulting in an initial decision dismissing the appeal. But there is an exception. With approval of the AJ, an appeal may be dismissed without prejudice to its refiling. The initial decision dismissing the case without prejudice (DWOP) will state the conditions governing its refiling. Once timely refiled, the appeal proceeds, with a slightly modified docket number, and it will eventually be closed out with another initial decision. The DWOP is similar to a continuance, but it removes the case from the AJ's docket (advantageous for the AJ, whose performance standards set processing times for appeals) and it provides additional time for the parties to conduct discovery or settlement negotiations or for other reasons that may or may not directly relate to the litigation.

It was a long time coming, but in 2012 the Board issued a regulation on dismissals without prejudice to the refiling of appeals, codifying past practice, 5 CFR 1201.29 (2022):

(a) In general. Dismissal without prejudice is a procedural option that allows for the dismissal and subsequent refiling of an appeal.

(b) Procedure. Dismissal without prejudice may be granted on the judge's own motion or upon request by either party. The decision whether to dismiss an appeal without prejudice is committed to the sound discretion of the judge, and may be granted when the interests of fairness, due process, and administrative efficiency outweigh any prejudice to either party.

(c) Refiling. Except in certain USERRA appeals under Part 1208 involving the use of military leave, a decision dismissing an appeal without prejudice will include a date certain by which the appeal must be refiled. The judge will determine whether the appeal must be refiled by the appellant or whether it will be automatically refiled by the judge as of a date certain. When a dismissal without prejudice is issued over the objection of the appellant, the appeal will be automatically refiled as of a date certain.

(d) Waiver. When a dismissed appeal must be refiled by the appellant, requests for waiver of a late filing based upon good cause will be liberally construed.

The regulation made two changes, or clarifications, in past practice. First, it is not just the appellant's motion. Second, if the appellant misses the refiling deadline, the waiver doctrine is to be when their original appeals were timely filed and there is some basis for an untimely refiling. The discussion that follows concerns law developed without a specific regulation on the DWOP process--the common law of DWOPs.

The Board described the DWOP in Ryan v. DHS, 112 MSPR 43, 45 ¶ 4, 2009 MSPB 136 (2009) (an arbitration review proceeding):

In general, a dismissal without prejudice is a procedural option available to the Board's administrative judges when the circumstances of a case warrant such an action. See Jones v. Department of Health & Human Services, 52 M.S.P.R. 669, 672 (1992). Further, the Board has held that an administrative judge may dismiss an appeal without prejudice where such a dismissal is in the interest of fairness, due process, and administrative efficiency. See e.g., Gidwani v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 74 M.S.P.R. 509 (1997); Bowens v. Office of Personnel Management, 59 M.S.P.R. 390, 392 (1993). In particular, the Board's administrative judges have dismissed cases without prejudice pending decisions and actions that are not before the Board and over which the Board has no control. Those Board decisions generally involve dismissals pending completion of criminal proceedings and, as in this case, retirement matters. See Wilson v. When reviewing decisions of arbitrators or administrative judges, the Board has not typically dismissed cases without prejudice; however, we find that such a dismissal is appropriate here because it is in the interest of fairness, due process, and administrative efficiency for the parties to resolve this matter as set forth in their settlement agreement.

Under longstanding Board practice and under its regulation, with prior Board approval, an appeal may be withdrawn without prejudice to refiling of the appeal. "A dismissal without prejudice is a dismissal in which the appellant may refile the same claim, without loss of any rights. Black's Law Dictionary 1596 (7th ed. 1999)." Johnson v. Dept. of Treasury, CH-300A-10-0760-I-1 (NP 5/12/2011). Before the 2012 regulation, a dismissal without prejudice to refile was ordinarily initiated by a motion from the appellant. When dismissal without prejudice to refile an appeal was not defined by Board regulations, the terminology was (still is) significant--to be distinguished from a dismissal with prejudice to refiling. Cf. Gorny v. Dept. of Interior, DE-0752-08-0233-X-1 (NP 4/1/2011) ("Accordingly, we find that dismissal of the petition for enforcement with prejudice to refiling (i.e., the parties normally may not refile this appeal) is appropriate under these circumstances, and we accept the settlement agreement into the record for enforcement purposes."); cf. Graves v. Dept. of Air Force, SF-3330-10-0696-I-1 (NP 3/7/2011) ("There are no Board regulations specifically governing the dismissal of an appeal without prejudice. Rather, dismissal of an appeal without prejudice is a procedural option that is committed to the sound discretion of the administrative judge.").

Federal Circuit decisions do not extensively discuss the DWOP process, although the Board's use of DWOPs are noted in some of its decisions in this subsection. See Hahn v. EPA, 445 Fed. Appx. 350, 353 (Fed. Cir. 2011 NP) ("In this case, the administrative judge decided to rule on Ms. Hahn's claims against the EPA rather than awaiting a decision by OPM, and there is no reason to overturn that decision. See Gingery v. Dep't of the Treasury, 111 M.S.P.R. 134, 138 (2009) ('An [administrative judge] has wide discretion to control the proceedings before her and the dismissal without prejudice to refiling is a procedural option committed to her sound discretion.'); Gidwani v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, 74 M.S.P.R. 509, 511 (1997) ('It is well settled that dismissal of an appeal without prejudice is a procedural option that is committed to the sound discretion of the administrative judge.').").

The DWOP is popular with AJs, for it allows them to close out a case and gain credit in their performance standards for a timely case disposition. A continuance occurs while the case is actively docketed and the resultant adjudication delay may cause the appeal to exceed the AJ's performance standard on timely case processing. The problem with the dismissal without prejudice is that, unlike with a continuance, the case ordinarily must actually be refiled by the appellant within the time set by the dismissal order. See Santaro v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2001 NP No. 01-3189) (rejecting the argument that dismissal without prejudice equates to a suspension of administrative proceedings). If the case is not timely refiled, the Board must deal with collateral litigation over timeliness, and the appellant risks never being heard because an appeal once timely filed was not timely refiled. It is always the appellant who bears the risk of an untimely refiling. That risk is not inherent in a continuance. [Refer to Chapter 3, "Case Suspension" and "Continuance Procedure."] To avoid the problem of an untimely refiling, the Board should adopt a uniform practice of automatically refiling a case that is dismissed without prejudice for a specific period of time. AJs will ordinarily use an automatic refiling procedure if it is requested in the DWOP motion, or they may decide to do so without a request. If a DWOP is issued at the instance of the AJ or the agency, it should have an automatic refiling provision so the appellant cannot be penalized by inattention to a stated mandatory refiling deadline.

Appeals decided before the Board's regulatory revisions that included DWOP procedures, 5 CFR 1201.29 (2011) noted that "[d]ismissals without prejudice are not governed by specific Board regulations; rather, they are committed to the administrative judge's sound discretion." Ayers v. NASA, 80 MSPR 550, 552-53 ¶ 5 (1999). "The Board affords its administrative judges much discretion in dismissing appeals." Baker v. USPS, 86 MSPR 349, 353 ¶ 10 (2000); see Neubeiser v. FERC, 47 MSPR 125, 127-28 (1991) (noting the practice with approval). "[T]o show a basis for reversal for an abuse of discretion, the appellant 'must prove that the error caused substantial harm or prejudice to his rights which could have affected the outcome of the case.'" McNair v. EEOC, AT-1221-10-0888-W-1 (NP 6/1/2011) (citing Curtin v. Office of Personnel Management, 846 F.2d 1373, 1379 (Fed. Cir. 1988)). There are no limitations on the reasons justifying dismissals without prejudice. Hart v. USDA, 81 MSPR 329, 333 ¶ 7 (1999), noted that "AJs commonly dismiss appeals without prejudice to refiling when the hearing is set for a date after the expiration of the 120-day period from the date the appeal was filed." The procedure was used when an appellant explained that he needed a continuance because he could not locate local counsel. Rosenberg v. CFTC, 13 MSPR 240, 241 (1982); see Hall v. Dept. of Labor, DC-0752-16-0124-I-1 (NP 9/14/2016) (DWOP to give the appellant time to secure counsel); cf. Lumpkins v. VA, AT-0752-13-0297-I-1 (NP 5/1/2014) (DWOP when the AJ was concerned about the appellant's competency to pursue the Board appeal); Sadler v. OPM, 93 MSPR 48, 49 ¶ 5 (2002) (it was appropriate for the AJ to use a DWOP to allow an appellant time to find representation); Desmond v. VA, 90 MSPR 301, 302-03 ¶¶ 4-5 (2001) (dismissal without prejudice was appropriate when the AJ decided that appellant should explore possible representation). Inability to secure counsel is but one reason for a DWOP.

The appellant appears to argue that there was no reason to dismiss the appeal without prejudice for purposes of allowing her deposition because discovery is already closed pursuant to a November 17, 2009 hearing order, and that "reopening" discovery violates her due process rights. Again, we disagree. The order stated that discovery would close on January 20, 2010, "[u]nless otherwise specified." The agency served its notice of deposition on the appellant well within the deadline for initiating discovery, and the administrative judges' subsequent orders specified that discovery would remain open after that date. It was well within the administrative judges' discretion to keep discovery open beyond the original deadline to prevent the agency from being prejudiced by the appellant's inability to attend a deposition. See McCray v. Department of Defense, 68 M.S.P.R. 186, 196 (1995) (under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.41(b)(4), an administrative judge has broad discretion to rule on discovery matters, and absent an abuse of discretion, the Board will not find reversible error in such rulings); cf. White v. Government Printing Office, 108 M.S.P.R. 355, ¶¶ 8-9 (2008) (the administrative judge abused her discretion in denying the appellant's motion to compel on the basis that discovery had closed when the agency's delay in responding to the appellant's discovery requests pushed the contested discovery issues beyond the discovery deadline).

The appellant also argues that the dismissal without prejudice violated her due process rights because she has not been afforded a jurisdictional hearing. We find that the appellant's due process rights have not been violated in this appeal because the initial decision did not abrogate any right that the appellant may have to a jurisdictional hearing; it merely postponed the date of such a hearing in an attempt to resolve important evidentiary issues.

The appellant further argues that the administrative judge based her decision to dismiss the appeal without prejudice on the "bias[ed]" and "corrupt" rulings of the administrative judge previously assigned to this appeal. Although the initial decision referred to some of the previous administrative judge's orders, we find that the initial decision was not based on those orders. Rather, the initial decision was based on the current administrative judge's findings that the agency has a right to depose the appellant and question her at a hearing and that the appellant is unable to submit to such examination at this time. Therefore, we will not consider the propriety of any of the previous administrative judge's orders at this time.

The appellant appears to argue that she should not have to submit to a deposition because the agency has not articulated a reason for its alleged failure to accommodate her. Without deciding whether this matter would excuse the appellant from submitting to a deposition, we find that her argument is factually incorrect. The agency submitted a lengthy and detailed statement of its version of events, as well as voluminous documentary evidence in support of its position, all within the timeframe for doing so.

The appellant also argues that the dismissal without prejudice constitutes disability discrimination, and that the administrative judge is holding her appeal hostage until she is able to submit to a deposition. She further argues that the administrative judge should not have imposed a 90-day refiling deadline. We find that the administrative judge's decision does not constitute disability discrimination, and that her alleged holding the appeal "hostage" is actually a reasonable attempt to accommodate both the appellant's psychological condition and the agency's right to meaningful discovery in this appeal. See Thomas v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 63 M.S.P.R. 649, 663-64 (1994) (the appellant was entitled to depose agency employees because the depositions were reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence under 5 C.F.R. § 1201.72(a)). We further find that the administrative judge did not abuse her discretion in setting a 90-day refiling deadline for the appeal. The Board disfavors dismissals without prejudice that do not contain a specific refiling date, especially where it is unclear when the matter underlying the dismissal will be resolved. See Schulte v. Department of the Air Force, 100 M.S.P.R. 141, ¶ 6 (2005). Given the Congressional mandate that Board cases be expeditiously adjudicated, a case may not go on indefinitely. Selig, 102 M.S.P.R. 189, ¶ 6. In this case, all of the medical evidence shows that the appellant can be expected to recover from her condition sufficiently to submit to a deposition and testify at a hearing at some point in the future. In light of this information, it was reasonable for the administrative judge to postpone adjudication for up to 90 days and revisit the hearing and deposition issues at that time. Contrary to the appellant's assertion, the refiling deadline does not presuppose a "miraculous healing," or for that matter, any recovery by the appellant at all. It does however, account for the fact that the appellant may recover, as all of her doctors expect her to, thus avoiding the need for the administrative judge to make a difficult decision about what to do when the agency's rights are potentially prejudiced by the appellant's inability to testify or be deposed.

AJs are to ensure that when cases are dismissed without prejudice, appellants know what they must do and when they must do it to reactivate their appeals. The initial decision dismissing an appeal without prejudice to its refiling should set a refiling deadline or provide for automatic refiling on a date certain if the appeal is not earlier refiled. The standards that AJs should follow in the DWOP initial decision are summarized in Nelson v. USPS, 113 MSPR 644, 647-49 ¶¶ 8-10, 2010 MSPB 96 (2010) (aff'd, Nelson v. MSPB, 414 Fed. Appx. 292 (Fed. Cir. 2011 NP)), involving a DWOP pending adjudication of concurrent criminal proceedings:

The Board has repeatedly held that its dismissal without prejudice practice should not become a trap to deny an appellant the opportunity to have his case decided on the merits. See Jaramillo v. Department of the Air Force, 106 M.S.P.R. 244, ¶ 6 (2007); Hodges v. Office of Personnel Management, 101 M.S.P.R. 212, ¶ 11 (2006); Jackson v. Office of Personnel Management, 89 M.S.P.R. 302, ¶ 10 (2001); Brown v. Office of Personnel Management, 86 M.S.P.R. 417, ¶ 8 (2000). Accordingly, it has identified specific standards applicable to determining whether good cause exists for excusing an untimely refiled appeal of a matter previously dismissed without prejudice pending the resolution of criminal proceedings. See Gaddy v. Department of the Navy, 100 M.S.P.R. 485, ¶ 13 (2005). These include the following: the appellant's pro se status; the timeliness of the initial appeal; the appellant's demonstrated intent throughout the proceedings to refile the appeal; the length of the delay in refiling; confusion surrounding and arbitrariness of the refiling deadline; the number of prior dismissals without prejudice; the agency's failure to object to the dismissal without prejudice; and the lack of prejudice to the agency in allowing the refiled appeal. Id. Further, in Jaramillo, 106 M.S.P.R. 244, the Board noted its policy to stay its proceedings when criminal proceedings involving the same matter are pending. Id., ¶ 7. Although the administrative judge did not apply the appropriate analysis to the refiled appeal in this case, the error did not affect the outcome, and, therefore, is not grounds for overturning the initial decision.

Although it is true that (1) this pro se appellant did timely file his initial appeal, (2) his appeal was previously dismissed without prejudice only once and at the agency's request; and (3) there is no apparent prejudice to the agency in allowing the refiled appeal, the record does not otherwise support finding that the appellant established good cause to excuse his untimely refiling. Specifically, the initial decision dismissing the appeal without prejudice clearly identified the date for refiling the appeal and warned the appellant that failure to refile by that date would "result in a finding that the appellant has waived his right to pursue the issues raised by the appeal, absent a showing of good cause for any filing delay." Cf. Hodges, 101 M.S.P.R. 212, ¶ 13 (finding the refiling deadline unreasonable because it did not set a "date certain" for when the appellant must refile her appeal). Further, the deadline was not arbitrary, but based on the agency's estimate of when the criminal proceedings would be concluded. Conversely, as previously noted, the appellant failed to provide any evidence justifying any confusion over the deadline, and in particular, supporting his belief that he had either 6 months from the Board's final decision or 60 days from the end of his criminal case to refile his appeal. Moreover, even if he believed that refiling before his criminal case was completed would have been futile, he nonetheless failed to explain why, given the date certain for refiling, he neglected to contact the regional office to request an extension. In addition, the appellant's 4-month delay was hardly minimal. See Gulley v. Department of the Treasury, 101 M.S.P.R. 48, ¶ 9 (2006). Even considering the delay in the light most favorable to the appellant, he still waited approximately 1 1/2 months after the July 31, 2009 conclusion of his criminal case to refile his appeal.

Thus, considering this case under the specific standards applicable to a refiled appeal of a matter previously dismissed without prejudice pending the resolution of criminal proceedings, we find that the appellant has failed to show that good cause exists for excusing his untimely refiling. Accordingly, we decline to waive the refiling deadline in this appeal.

Dismissal without prejudice is a procedural option that is committed to the sound discretion of the AJ. Ballard v. VA, 73 MSPR 598, 599-602 (1997); see Polen v. DOD, 72 MSPR 1, 5 (1996) (AJ has broad discretion to dismiss without prejudice and to set a reasonable interim deadline for refiling); Deaton v. USDA, 71 MSPR 544, 546 (1996) (noting that AJs have broad discretion to dismiss appeals without prejudice); Lewis v. Dept. of Air Force, 69 MSPR 40, 43 (1995) (sound discretion of the AJ, rather than a Board regulation, governed the procedural option of dismissal without prejudice).

The Board suggested some limitations on the use of DWOPs, discouraging their use in an adverse action appeal pending disposition of related issues and claims in a federal district court Title VII (EEO) suit, Wilson v. VA, 102 MSPR 70, 73-75 ¶¶ 9-13, 2006 MSPB 127 (2006):

In Milner v. Department of Justice, 87 M.S.P.R. 660, ¶ 13 (2001), the Board reiterated its previous holding that the dismissal of a case without prejudice to its refiling was an option left to the sound discretion of the administrative judge. It held further, however, that this discretion was to be exercised in a manner consistent with the policies stated in that decision. Id. Those policies include that of deciding appeals within 120 days after their filing dates, to the extent practicable, and of acting in a manner consistent with the purpose of this goal, i.e., consistent with the purpose of benefiting appellants by preventing delays that adversely affect them.[1] See id. ¶ 10. The Board noted further that the 120-day goal was to be applied in a manner "consistent with the interests of fairness and other priorities of the Board." Id.

Under the circumstances of this case, we find that the administrative judge erred in dismissing the appellant's petition for appeal pursuant to French. In French, a pro se appellant had raised the issue of his mental incapacity in connection with his application for disability retirement. The U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit vacated the Board's first decision on the appellant's appeal, remanded the case to the Board, and directed the Board to formulate procedures for the processing of a case of an apparently nonfrivolous claim of past incompetence by one presently incompetent and not represented by counsel or a conservator. See French v. Office of Personnel Management, 810 F.2d 1118, 1120 (Fed. Cir. 1987).

In establishing these procedures, the Board noted that they were to be used in disability retirement cases of the kind present in French. It directed the Regional Office to make diligent efforts to obtain appropriate representation for the appellant. The Board further stated that if counsel could not be obtained, the Regional Office should not enter an adverse order against the appellant, but if necessary, should dismiss the case without prejudice to reinstitution under circumstances conducive to fair adjudication. Id. at 498-99. The Board has subsequently applied the French procedures to cases involving appellants who claimed mental disability and applied for waivers of repayment after they had received annuity overpayments. See Dennison v. Office of Personnel Management, 42 MSPR 358 (1989); Eddy v. Office of Personnel Management, 38 MSPR 442 (1988).

In contrast, this case does not involve an appellant who bears the burden of proving his entitlement to annuity benefits. Rather, here the agency must show by a preponderance of the evidence that the appellant committed the misconduct alleged, and that his removal is reasonable and promotes the efficiency of the service. See 5 USC §§ 7513(a) and 7701(c)(1)(B); Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 MSPB 313, 5 MSPR 280, 306 (1981). Moreover, the appellant did not claim that he suffers from a mental disability, and the record does not contain any medical evidence to support such a finding. Finally, the record does not show that the appellant asked the Board for assistance in obtaining a representative after the administrative judge properly informed him of the possibility of obtaining pro bono representation.

In French, 810 F.2d at 1119-20, the court did not indicate that the procedures it directed the Board to formulate should be extended beyond retirement appeals in which appellants claim that they are mentally incompetent and present medical evidence to support such claims. Because these factors are absent here, we find no reason to apply the French standards to the present case.

There does not appear to be any limit on the duration of a DWOP, except that it should not be less than 35 days (the time necessary for the initial decision dismissing the case to become final and subject to PFR). See Washington v. USPS, 78 MSPR 650, 651 ¶ 4 (1998).

A DWOP is not granted of right. If an AJ declines to grant the dismissal (or declines to repeatedly DWOP a case), the appellant is obliged to pursue his appeal or suffer a dismissal (with prejudice) for lack of prosecution, Green v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1998 NP No. 97-3407):

The board did not abuse its discretion in denying Green's motion to dismiss his appeal without prejudice after it had already granted two similar motions that had delayed the appeal's adjudication for almost two years. We do not fault the board's conclusion that "further delay is not in the interest of justice and is highly prejudicial to the agency." The board also did not abuse its discretion in exercising its authority under 5 CFR § 1201.43(b) (1998) to dismiss the appeal for failure to prosecute in the wake of Green's failure to participate in prehearing conferences and to respond to telephone calls by the administrative judge and the agency representative.

The DWOP is usually initiated by motion with service of the motion on the opposing party, see Mughal v. OPM, 54 MSPR 554, 555 (1992), although an AJ could enter a DWOP as a result of discussions in a status conference. If an appellant seeks a DWOP, the AJ should ensure the agency is provided an opportunity to respond to the request. Rodarte v. OPM, 29 MSPR 224, 225 (1985) (remanding to the regional office for the AJ's consideration of briefs by both parties on a dismissal motion), followed, Soto v. VA, 88 MSPR 626, 628 ¶ 4 (2001) (AJ erred in entering DWOP when he did not ensure that the agency had notice and opportunity to respond to the appellant's request; the error was harmless based on the appellant's intervening refiling). Any representative seeking, or consenting to, a DWOP should be properly designated as representative. An AJ erred by agreeing to DWOP an appeal at the request of an undesignated representative when the appellant was not a party to the conference call discussing the dismissal and the appellant earlier indicated he would represent himself. Visconti v. EPA, 78 MSPR 17, 20-21 (1998) (also suggesting that the AJ should not have attempted to determine the appellant's competency in an appeal not involving the potential right to an annuity).

Under current Board regulations and earlier decisions, discussed below, an AJ may DWOP a case without the appellant's request. Under earlier practice, assuming the dismissal was not the AJ's idea, it was ordinarily the appellant, not the agency, who had the option of seeking a DWOP. Draper v. Dept. of Army, 51 MSPR 518, 520 (1991) (agency should seek a continuance based on a date certain for proceeding with the appeal), relying on Thomas v. VA, 51 MSPR 218, 221 (1991); see Ballard v. VA, 73 MSPR 598, 599-602 (1997) (discussed in the section below on agency-initiated dismissals). In Allison v. USPS, 81 MSPR 335, 339 ¶ 9 (1999), the Board permitted its AJ to dismiss an appeal as withdrawn, with prejudice, after the AJ warned the appellant that the agency might oppose his grievance-arbitration and that the AJ would not dismiss the appeal without prejudice, as requested by the appellant in his request to withdraw the appeal. The Allison result appears to be ill-considered. If there were doubts concerning the arbitrability of the grievance, the AJ could have permitted the appellant to resolve those issues during a DWOP. Alternatively, the AJ could have denied the DWOP, insisted that the appellant proceed to hearing, and either sanctioned the appellant by dismissal or adjudicated the appeal on the record if the appellant did not cooperate in prehearing and hearing procedures.

Agnew v. USPS, PH-0752-04-0598-I-6 (NP 8/23/2013), suggested at footnote 2 that if an appeal is DWOP'd based on medical conditions, refiling should not be preconditioned on production of medical evidence:

We note our disagreement with the administrative judge's previous requirement that upon refiling, the appellant would have to present medical documentation establishing his inability to participate in a hearing since December of 2008 and his concomitant ability to now participate in a hearing. We believe this to be contrary to the Board's practice of dismissing an appeal without prejudice due to medical incapacity subject to automatic refiling, at which point "[i]f the appellant is still medically unable to proceed, medical documentation satisfactorily justifying the continued delay must be submitted to the administrative judge." Ayers v. National Aeronautics & Space Administration, 80 M.S.P.R. 550, ¶ 9 (1999) (citing Zell v. Department of the Army, 57 M.S.P.R. 86, 88 n.2 (1993)).

Once the DWOP'd appeal is refiled, the appellant is expected to proceed with expedition. An AJ was not faulted for denying a continuance because of illness of counsel when the appellant refiled his appeal knowing of his counsel's health problems. Appellant was represented by a second attorney. See Ray v. USPS, 22 MSPR 228, 230 (1984). Not all DWOP motions are granted. In Key v. GSA, 60 MSPR 66, 69 (1993), the unrepresented appellant did not initiate discovery within the 25 days from the date of the acknowledgment order allowed for the initiation of discovery. The appellant subsequently retained counsel, who objected to the AJ's refusal to waive the rule governing the initiation of discovery. Counsel then moved for withdrawal of the appeal without prejudice to permit the parties to pursue discovery. The Board affirmed the AJ's refusal to grant the motion:

In Sheehan, the Board affirmed the administrative judge's decision to grant the appellant's motion to dismiss his appeal without prejudice to refiling so as to allow him time for discovery even though the appellant first requested discovery at a prehearing conference after the discovery period had passed. Id. The administrative judge found good cause for the untimeliness of the discovery request in that he had discontinued processing the case based on the parties' representations to him that they were settling the matter, and the information sought through discovery was directly related to the charges against the appellant and the reasonableness of the penalty imposed. Id. at 205-06. Here, settlement is not an issue and the then-unrepresented appellant simply failed to initiate discovery during the allotted time, of which he was notified. Thus, the appellant has failed to establish that the administrative judge abused his discretion in denying the appellant's untimely requests.

Milner v. DOJ, 87 MSPR 660, 665-67 ¶¶ 10-15 (2001), seemed to suggest a departure from the frequent use of the DWOP in lieu of a continuance, although the Board may have limited its approach to cases arising under USERRA. Significantly, in Milner, the Board made reactivation of the appeal automatic upon the expiration of a dismissal without prejudice in a USERRA case. More significantly, the Board (again) disavowed strict adherence to a 120-day adjudication period for the USERRA appeal:

The Board's policy of deciding appeals within 120 days is based on the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978, Pub. L. No. 95-454, 92 Stat. 1111 (CSRA), which reflects Congressional intent that appeals be decided expeditiously. For example, 5 U.S.C. § 7701(i)(4) provides that it "shall be the duty of the Board, an administrative law judge, or employee designated by the Board to hear any proceeding under this section to expedite to the extent practicable that proceeding." See also 5 U.S.C. § 7702(a)(1) (requiring the Board to decide within 120 days appeals in which discrimination prohibited by section 2302(b)(1) is alleged). Section 7701(i)(1) provides, however, that when the Board announces dates by which it expects to complete action on appeals, these dates are to be "consistent with the interests of fairness and other priorities of the Board." In Thomas v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 51 MSPR 218, 220 (1991), the Board explained that the policy underlying this CSRA statutory requirement that the Board expeditiously process appeals was to benefit appellants by preventing delays that adversely affected appellants who might be unemployed while their appeals were pending. A similar expeditious processing policy applies to individual right of action appeals. See 5 U.S.C. § 1221(f) (the Board must issue a final order or decision "as soon as practicable" after the proceeding is initiated).

In contrast, there is nothing in the statutory language of USERRA which directs the Board to expeditiously process USERRA complaints. See 38 U.S.C. chapter 43. The 120-day time limit for adjudication that the Board has applied to appeals governed by section 7701 does not apply in USERRA cases. See Bodus v. Department of the Air Force, 82 MSPR 508 ¶¶ 12-17 (1999) (cases that are within the Board's jurisdiction solely because of USERRA are not governed by the CSRA provisions at 5 U.S.C. §§ 7701-02). In fact, an amendment to USERRA that Congress enacted in 1998 led the Board to abandon the regulatory filing deadline it had originally set for USERRA cases. That amendment provided that the "Board shall adjudicate any complaint brought before the Board pursuant to subsection (a)(2)(A) or (b), without regard as to whether the complaint accrued before, on, or after October 13, 1994." 38 U.S.C. § 4324(c)(1) (emphasis added). In the wake of that amendment, the Board stated in the supplementary information accompanying its interim regulations governing USERRA appeals that "it would be inconsistent with the Congressional intent in enacting USERRA and its predecessor laws for the Board to establish a filing time limit by regulation. Therefore, there is no time limit for filing a USERRA appeal." 65 Fed. Reg. 5410, 5411 (2000).

Nothing in any of these statutory provisions supports a dismissal of cases without prejudice to refiling solely to avoid exceeding the Board's 120-day adjudicatory standard. Dismissing cases without prejudice solely to avoid the 120-day adjudicatory standard places an unnecessary burden on the appellants and results in an inaccurate picture of how long it actually takes the Board to adjudicate an appeal. Even with regard to a mixed-case appeal, where the Board is required under 5 U.S.C. § 7702, to decide the appealable action and the discrimination claim within 120 days, a dismissal of a case without prejudice to refiling does not effectuate that statutory purpose, because such a dismissal merely postpones an adjudication on the merits.

Accordingly, we hold that henceforth it is the Board's policy that: (1) A case will not be dismissed without prejudice to refiling simply to avoid exceeding the 120-day adjudication standard; (2) the dismissal of a case without prejudice to refiling will be granted or ordered only at the request of one or both of the parties or in order to avoid a lengthy or indefinite continuance; (3) the dismissal of a case without prejudice to refiling is a procedural option that is left to the sound discretion of the AJ, see Ballard v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 73 MSPR 598, 600 (1997), but this discretion must be exercised in a manner consistent with the policies set forth in this Opinion and Order*; and (4) to effectuate the USERRA statutory scheme, which contains no time limit for filing USERRA appeals, a USERRA case that has been dismissed without prejudice to refiling will be considered automatically refiled by the date set forth in the dismissal order, unless there is evidence that the appellant has abandoned the case.
