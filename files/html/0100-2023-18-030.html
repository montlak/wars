2. Agency Regulation; Collective Bargaining Contract

As to permissible delay in an agency decision following an oral or written reply to an adverse action proposal--5 USC 7513(b)(4) calls for a written decision at the earliest practicable date. Delays are analyzed under the harmful error rule, meaning that the employee must show the result likely would have been different had there been no delay. See Epstein v. DHHS, 6 MSPR 235, 237, 6 MSPB 203 (1981). If an agency has an internal regulation requiring that adverse action proposals be issued within a certain period of time following discovery of the underlying misconduct, an agency delay of several weeks is not harmful error without a showing of prejudice to the appellant. Walton v. GSA, 7 MSPR 178, 181, 7 MSPB 94 (1981). In Kumferman v. Dept. of Navy, 785 F.2d 286 (Fed. Cir. 1986), the employee argued that harmful error required reversal because an agency regulation stated the corrective influence of a suspension is greatly diminished if it follows the offense by six months or a year. The court observed that the case was a removal and that the agency deferred resolution of the case pending the outcome of an investigation into the charges against the employee. The court declined to overturn the removal: the employee was not prejudiced by the delay and no harmful procedural error was created by the passage of time. In one appeal, the Board did not become involved in a harmful error analysis and allowed its AJ to bar consideration of charges more than three years old when the AJ interpreted a collective bargaining contract, the equivalent of an agency regulation, to require that result. See Recio v. Dept. of Army, 8 MSPR 567, 572 (1981). [Refer to additional discussion of the Sanders case in Chapter 7, "Staleness of Charge."]

Stevens v. Dept. of Air Force, 395 Fed. Appx. 679, 681-82 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP), declined to directly enforce a provision of a negotiated agreement that the court viewed as permissive rather than mandatory as to timing of a disciplinary action:

First, we reject Stevens' procedural error claim. Section 5.01 of the MLA between the agency and IAW states:

[T]he Employer will strive to effect disciplinary action within either 45 days of the offense, the Employer's awareness of the offense, or the completion of an investigation of the matter by other than the supervisor, whichever occurs later. If, for reasons of significantly changed circumstances, further delay in taking the action is anticipated, a notice from the Employer to the employee advising that disciplinary action is being considered, the general basis of the action, and that the employee will be informed when a decision has been made satisfies the requirements of this section.

We see no error in the Board's determination that this provision articulated a goal, rather than a mandatory time limit. We agree that, because no language in the agreement "specif[ies] a consequence for noncompliance," it is "best read as a housekeeping requirement that is not judicially enforceable." See Timken U.S. Corp. v. United States, 421 F.3d 1350, 1357 (Fed. Cir. 2005) (internal quotation omitted). There is, moreover, no suggestion that the error, even if it existed, was harmful. The Board defines harmful error as one that "is likely to have caused the agency to reach a conclusion different from the one reached." 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(c)(3) [now 5 CFR 1201.4(r)]. Here there has been no such showing. Courts are "reluctant to conclude that every failure of an agency to observe a procedural requirement voids subsequent agency action." Timken, 421 F.3d at 1357. Therefore, the Board correctly rejected Stevens' harmful procedural error claim.

Garcia v. DHS, DA-0752-15-0297-I-1 (NP 8/24/2016), said of a contract article requiring adverse action at the earliest practicable date:

Harmful error under 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)(2)(A) cannot be presumed; an agency error is harmful only when the record shows that the procedural error was likely to have caused the agency to reach a conclusion different from the one it would have reached in the absence or cure of the error. Stephen, 47 M.S.P.R. at 681, 685. The appellant has not claimed that the agency's alleged violation of the collective bargaining agreement likely caused the agency to reach a result different than it would have reached had it issued the notice of proposed removal earlier. The appellant's only claim of harm is the personal suffering he endured while being under investigation for 11 months. However, he has not explained how the personal harm he suffered would have affected the agency's decision to remove him.
