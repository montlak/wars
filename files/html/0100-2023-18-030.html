III. INJUNCTION

FLRA is empowered to petition the court of appeals for temporary relief and enforcement of its orders. 5 USC 7123(b). AFGE Local 2953 v. FLRA, 730 F.2d 1534, 1538 (D.C. Cir. 1984). Injunction authority was confirmed during a strike by air traffic controllers. See, e.g., United States v. PATCO, 653 F.2d 1134 (7th Cir. 1981). The jurisdiction of courts to review injunction requests by labor organizations was discussed in Columbia Power Trades Council v. Dept. of Energy, 671 F.2d 325, 327-29 (9th Cir. 1982), rejecting the principle that district courts had jurisdiction to enjoin, at the behest of an aggrieved union, conduct involving a dispute (concerning the obligation to bargain about wages of prevailing rate employees) that could be characterized as an unfair labor practice:

The only provision made for judicial jurisdiction appears in 5 U.S.C. § 7123. An aggrieved party may appeal the Authority's action to a court of appeals, the Authority may petition a court of appeals for enforcement of its orders, and the Authority may seek an injunction in a district court after it has issued a complaint. At no point does the Act entitle a party to petition a district court for relief. Given the broad purpose of the Act to meet the special requirements of government, the leadership role of the Authority, and the limited role of the judiciary in this statutory scheme, it is manifestly the expressed desire of Congress to create an exclusive statutory scheme.... [T]he Authority possesses exclusive jurisdiction over federal labor relations matters.

...

The Union's claim that this case does not involve any unfair labor practice and is therefore subject to the jurisdiction of the district court is an argument which we cannot accept. It would frustrate the Congressional scheme...if exclusive jurisdiction could be thwarted by a party's characterization of the lawsuit.... "It is the conduct being regulated, not the formal description of governing legal standards, that is the proper focus of concern."...

NTEU v. King, 961 F.2d 240, 244-45 (D.C. Cir. 1992), left open the question of the availability of injunctive relief to unions seeking to protect First Amendment rights to distribute literature. The lawsuit was placed on a "suspension" status while FLRA was given a few months to sort out a related ULP case. Employees may not seek equitable relief in district courts to avoid pay losses if they have not exhausted contractual remedies, i.e., grievance procedures. However, employees may litigate constitutional claims without exhaustion of contractual remedies. See, e.g., Hubbard v. EPA, 982 F.2d 531, 532 (D.C. Cir. 1992); Andrade v. Lauer, 729 F.2d 1475, 1490-93 (D.C. Cir. 1984). "Issues of noncompliance with an Authority remedial order that has been enforced in a court of appeals are properly dealt with in contempt proceedings before the court." AFGE Local 987 and Bradley, 53 FLRA 364, 369 (1997). Refer to FLRA's injunction policy, discussed in Part 2 of the General Counsel's ULP Manual, available on the FLRA website, www.flra.gov.
