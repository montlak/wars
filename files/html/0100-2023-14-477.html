broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 15: Pay, Premiums, and Allowances, Section O: Incentive Pay; Awards, Subsection 01: Initial Determination of Substantive Nonnegotiability

1. Initial Determination of Substantive Nonnegotiability

FLRA initially decided that standards for payment of incentive pay were not negotiable in NTEU and IRS, 14 FLRA 463, 469 (1984) (Proposal 5), vacated and remanded, NTEU v. FLRA, 793 F.2d 371, 372 (D.C. Cir. 1986), post. The proposal stated:

Incentive money is paid at the rate of $.09 per one-tenth of an efficiency point over 100 percent. For example, performance at 125% efficiency equals $22.50 in incentive pay. The money will be distributed on a pay period basis with the regular salary check. If the employee works overtime he/she will be paid $.04 more per one-tenth of a point otherwise payable. If employees of more than one grade work the same F/P [function and program] task then the $.09 will be increased by $.02 for each grade above the minimum grade assigned the F/P task.

FLRA determined, over the dissent of Mr. Haughton, 14 FLRA at 470:

[A]n integral aspect of management's exercise of its rights to assign work and direct employees is to establish a system of rewards and sanctions for employee performance, including the provision of incentives to encourage and reward superior performance. The nature of the incentive (e.g., monetary or nonmonetary), the amount of a monetary incentive, and the circumstances under which an incentive may be awarded are essential components of management's judgment. That is, they directly relate to the potential success of the incentive in motivating the performance of particular job tasks and, hence, to some extent determine the priorities for accomplishing the agency's work.

...

...The rate of the incentive and the circumstances under which it will be provided has direct bearing on the successful use of performance-based incentives by management in order to accomplish the work of the agency in a manner consistent with the exigencies of effective government.... [T]hese matters are an essential aspect of the job requirements which operate to encourage and reward successful performance, within the meaning of the management rights to direct employees and assign work. Consequently, these proposals are outside the duty to bargain.

Operation of an incentive pay system was considered by FLRA to be an integral aspect of management's exercise of its right to direct employees and to assign work. Johnson VAMC, Clarksburg and AFGE Local 2384, 15 FLRA 347, 351 (1984). That result applied to incentive pay in its various permutations. A proposal was nonnegotiable that required that objective standards be announced in advance, before awards could be given to employees, and that any employee meeting the standard be given an award. AFGE Local 32 and OPM, 19 FLRA 743, 744 (1985). A proposal was nonnegotiable that an outstanding appraisal result in a quality step increase (QSI) and that an excellent appraisal make an employee eligible for a QSI. It interfered with management's right to direct work by setting the level of rewards for successful performance. AFGE Local 3269 and DHHS, FDA, Region V, 15 FLRA 906, 906-07 (1984); see AFGE Local 32 and OPM, 19 FLRA 743, 744 (1985) (Proposal 2 nonnegotiable: "any employee meeting the announced standard for any award will be given the award; no awards will be given unless objective standards are announced in advance," relying on NTEU and IRS, 14 FLRA 463 (1984)). A proposal requiring a monetary award to an employee receiving an outstanding appraisal was nonnegotiable. AFGE Local 1940 and USDA, Plum Island Disease Ctr., 16 FLRA 816, 822-24 (1984) (Proposal 4). Negotiation over the substance of incentive pay remained permissively negotiable. The Employee Incentive Awards Program, established by 5 USC 4501, et seq., provided the discretion to agencies to permit the negotiation of formulae for the determination of the value of awards in proportion to the monetary value of the suggestion. NTEU Chap. 207 and FDIC, 14 FLRA 598, 601-04 (1984) (Proposal 2).

Although the incentive pay scheme was nonnegotiable, if incentive pay was established by management, procedural aspects of its implementation were negotiable. There was nothing objectionable concerning a proposal that once management assigned employees to positions subject to the incentive pay system, specific job tasks would, to the maximum extent feasible, be distributed so that each employee would have a fair and equitable opportunity to earn incentive pay. NTEU and IRS, 14 FLRA 463, 463-65 (1984). Proposals were negotiable mandating notices informing employees that particular job tasks were not those subject to incentive pay and requiring that employees be given a list of job elements supporting incentive pay. NTEU and IRS, 14 FLRA 463, 467 (1984). Proposals were nonnegotiable that had the effect of equalizing pay, and forcing distribution of awards. A proposal was nonnegotiable on the basis of a union position statement that the intent of the proposal was to mandate that, if management rewarded an employee for performance accomplishments, all other employees with higher levels of performance would be rewarded with a higher degree of recognition. The proposal established both levels of performance to be rewarded and the monetary amounts to be awarded. The proposal violated the then-extant FPM Chapter 451 Subchapter 4-2a(8), precluding automatic cash awards solely on the basis of performance appraisals. OPM regulations then precluded mandatory performance awards of the nature urged by the union. NAGE Local R4-17 and VAMC, Hampton, 16 FLRA 992, 993-94 (1984).
