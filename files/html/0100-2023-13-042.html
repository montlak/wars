i. Ineffective Resignation

A resignation may lack effect if the agency does not act upon it but allows the appellant to return to work and then proceeds to remove her. The appellant's appeal of the removal may then be viewed as a change of mind as to her earlier resignation. See Frank v. EEOC, 90 MSPR 458, 462 ¶ 9 & n.5 (2001). The Board looks to all the circumstances, including communication of a resignation to a supervisor or manager, to determine the intent of the appellant, Balagot v. Dept. of Defense, 102 MSPR 96, 98-102 ¶¶ 2-14, 2006 MSPB 131 (2006):

The appellant was employed as a Sales Store Checker for the Defense Commissary Agency. On May 28, 2005, the agency denied the appellant's request for leave to attend her granddaughter's christening the next day. The agency alleged that, when the Assistant Customer Service Supervisor informed the appellant that her leave request for May 29, 2005, had been denied, she told him that she was going to take that day off anyway. The agency did not allege that she told this supervisor that she intended to resign. However, the appellant apparently told a co-worker that she was going to quit and did not report to work on May 29. The agency claimed that, after the appellant told the co-worker she was going to quit, she turned over her cash till to the co-worker and left work more than four hours early on May 28, 2005. The agency also claimed that, when the appellant returned on May 31, 2005, the appellant spoke to her supervisor about her absence from work, her supervisor informed her that the time she had missed would be recorded as absent without leave (AWOL), and the appellant then told her supervisor that she was resigning. The appellant left the commissary, and the agency processed her resignation form without her signature with an effective date of May 31, 2005.

The appellant filed an appeal, alleging that she did not intend to resign when she told the co-worker that she quit, but that the agency told her it had processed her resignation when she attempted to return to work following her absence....

...

An employee is free to resign at any time, to set the effective date of his resignation, and to have his reasons for resigning entered in his official records. 5 C.F.R. § 715.202(a).[2] An agency is entitled to rely on an employee's expression of a present intent to resign only when that expression is definite and unequivocal. Hammond v. Department of the Navy, 50 M.S.P.R. 174, 181, review denied, 954 F.2d 732 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (Table). In determining whether an employee has effectively resigned, the Board considers the totality of the circumstances. Robinson v. U.S. Postal Service, 50 M.S.P.R. 433 (1991) (the Board considered the circumstances surrounding the appellant's oral resignation in determining that he had an intent to resign effective immediately); Heinze v. Department of the Interior, 47 M.S.P.R. 375 (1991), aff'd, 949 F.2d 403 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (Table); see also Heining v. General Services Administration, 68 M.S.P.R. 513, 519-20 (1995) (the Board and the courts have determined whether a resignation or retirement is voluntary based on whether the totality of the circumstances supported the conclusion that the employee was effectively deprived of free choice in the matter). In addition, the Board has determined that a decision to retire has no legal effect if it is not communicated to a responsible official. See Jostsons v. U.S. Postal Service, 58 M.S.P.R. 74 (1993), aff'd, 26 F.3d 139 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table).

[2] To the extent that the appellant claims her resignation was ineffective because she did not sign a letter of resignation and did not sign or date the agency's resignation form, she has failed to make a nonfrivolous allegation that her resignation was involuntary. An employee can make a valid oral resignation where the employee's words and actions express a clear intent to resign and a written resignation is unnecessary under such circumstances. Sanderson v. Office of Personnel Management, 72 M.S.P.R. 311, 316 (1996) (citing Robinson v. U.S. Postal Service, 50 M.S.P.R. 433, 438-39 (1991)), aff'd, 129 F.3d 134 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (Table), cert. denied, 522 U.S. 1115 (1998).

Still, resignations are presumed voluntary. Matthews v. U.S. Postal Service, 93 M.S.P.R. 109, ¶ 9 (2002). The Board has no jurisdiction over presumed voluntary actions unless and until they are found to be involuntary. Cruz v. Department of the Navy, 934 F.2d 1240 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (en banc). An appellant is entitled to a hearing on the issue of Board jurisdiction over an appeal of an allegedly involuntary resignation only if he makes a nonfrivolous allegation casting doubt on the presumption of voluntariness. Burgess v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 758 F.2d 641, 643 (Fed. Cir. 1985). A nonfrivolous allegation of Board jurisdiction is an allegation of fact which, if proven, could establish a prima facie case that the Board has jurisdiction over the matter in issue. Dumas v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 789 F.2d 892, 894 (Fed. Cir. 1986). In determining whether the appellant has made a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction entitling him to a hearing, the administrative judge may consider the agency's documentary submissions; however, to the extent that the agency's evidence constitutes mere factual contradiction of the appellant's otherwise adequate prima facie showing of jurisdiction, the administrative judge may not weigh evidence and resolve conflicting assertions of the parties and the agency's evidence may not be dispositive. Ferdon v. U.S. Postal Service, 60 M.S.P.R. 325, 329 (1994).

The appellant claimed that she did not intend to resign on May 28, 2005, when, after learning that her leave request had been denied, she told a co-worker that she was quitting and did not return the following day. The Board has found that employees have effectively resigned under somewhat similar facts in Robinson and Heinze. In Robinson, the Board found that the appellant's statements and actions signified an intent to resign effective immediately where, after learning that his request for the weekend off had been denied, the appellant stated that he was quitting, left the premises before the end of his scheduled workday, and did not return to work for his scheduled tours of duty the following two days. 50 M.S.P.R. at 438. In Heinze, the Board also found that the appellant evidenced an intent to resign effective immediately where he stated that he was resigning "right now," turned in his keys, collected his belongings, and left before the end of his work shift. 47 M.S.P.R. at 378.

In both Robinson and Heinze, however, the appellants told their supervisors that they were quitting. Here, by contrast, the appellant apparently told a co-worker that she was quitting, but neither party has alleged that on May 28, 2005, the appellant told a responsible agency official that she was resigning.

This distinction is significant. In Jostsons, the Board found the appellant's retirement effective where she had communicated her decision to retire to a responsible agency official by mailing her retirement application to the agency's personnel department. 58 M.S.P.R. at 79. While Jostsons involved a retirement and not a resignation, the two actions are essentially the same in that they both involve the employee's decision to terminate employment. The only practical difference between the two actions is that retirees are entitled to an annuity after leaving employment. In Jostsons, the Board applied the same principles it had applied to the resignation in Robinson to determine whether the agency was obligated to allow the appellant to withdraw her retirement application. See also Aldredge v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 70 M.S.P.R. 141, 146 (1996) (citing Jostsons and applying the principles of Robinson to appellant's efforts to rescind his decision to retire). Accordingly, we find that the appellant's allegation that she expressed her intent to resign only to a co-worker, and not to her supervisor or some other responsible agency official, is a circumstance that, if proven, would support the appellant's argument that her actions on May 28, 2005, did not constitute an effective resignation.

Consequently, we find that the appellant has made a nonfrivolous allegation that neither her actions on May 28, 2005, nor the fact that she failed to report for work on May 29, 2005, after she allegedly told her supervisor that she did not intend to report on that day despite the fact that he denied her request for leave, constituted a definite and unequivocal expression of a present intent to resign. Therefore, unless the appellant failed to nonfrivolously allege that she did not effectively resign on May 31, 2005, the appellant's allegations are sufficient to entitle her to a jurisdictional hearing to determine whether, in fact, the appellant effectively resigned on May 28, 2005. See Dumas, 789 F.2d at 894.

With respect to what occurred on May 31, 2005, the agency alleged that the appellant went to the commissary on May 31, spoke to her supervisor about her absence, was notified by the supervisor that the time she had missed would be considered AWOL, and then told her supervisor she was resigning. The agency's account of May 31 is completely at odds with that of the appellant, however. According to the appellant, she attempted to return to work on May 31, but was told that she was not on the schedule and that the agency had processed her resignation. If proven, the appellant's allegations would establish a prima facie case that her May 31, 2005 "resignation" was involuntary, thereby entitling the appellant to a jurisdictional hearing.

The appellant raised these allegations in the attachments to her initial appeal form, but the initial decision failed to acknowledge or address these allegations. Further, in finding that the appellant failed to raise a nonfrivolous allegation of fact entitling her to a jurisdictional hearing, the administrative judge relied on the evidence that the agency submitted in its response to the appeal. Because such evidence constituted mere factual contradiction of the appellant's otherwise adequate prima facie showing of jurisdiction, the administrative judge improperly relied on the agency's evidence to dismiss this appeal for lack of jurisdiction without providing the appellant a jurisdictional hearing. See Ferdon, 60 M.S.P.R. at 329.
