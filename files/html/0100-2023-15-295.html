b. Assigned Standard

There is a significant limitation on Chapter 75 procedures applied to performance problems: an agency may not circumvent Chapter 43 by charging that an employee should have performed better than the standards communicated to him under Chapter 43. In a decision discussed in Chapter 9, Lovshin v. Dept. of Navy, 767 F.2d 826, 842 (Fed. Cir. 1985), the court said of performance-based actions pursued as adverse actions under Chapter 75 rather than performance cases under Chapter 43:

...They function well as alternative procedures. The significant change affecting Chapter 75 cases is not found in Chapter 43, but rather in Chapters 23 and 77, under which violations of merit principles are made a defense. It would, for example, be inconsistent with the merit principles set out in 2301(b)(6) and (7) for an agency to bring an action "for cause" under Chapter 75 based solely on performance that is governed by and meets the critical elements set forth for the employee's position. An agency may not circumvent Chapter 43 by charging that an employee should have performed better than the standards communicated to him in accordance with Chapter 43.

See Fairall v. VA, 33 MSPR 33, 40 (1987) ("The only limitation that the court identified on an agency's use of Chapter 75 for performance-based actions was a prohibition on an agency's use of Chapter 75 to circumvent Chapter 43 by charging that an employee should have performed better than the standards...required him to perform," citing Lovshin v. Dept. of Navy, 767 F.2d 826, 842 (Fed. Cir. 1985)), aff'd, Fairall v. VA, 844 F.2d 775 (Fed. Cir. 1987) ("procedural baggage of Chapter 43 is inappropriate in this Chapter 75 action") [discussed at length in Chapter 9] ; cf. Lovato v. Dept. of Air Force, 48 MSPR 198, 201 (1991) (as to removal for being physically disqualified for a position: "Absent evidence of an attempt by the agency to circumvent Chapter 43 by charging that the appellant should have performed better than the elements communicated to him under Chapter 43 required him to perform, we find no error in the agency's decision to take the action under Chapter 75.").

A pre-Lovshin decision determined that the Board may compare ad hoc standards with those in a performance appraisal system to determine reasonableness, but if the ad hoc standards are slightly higher, they are not unreasonable, particularly if the appellant did not meet the appraisal system standards. Huffman v. DHHS, 20 MSPR 345, 347-48 (1984). The Board refused to rely on ad hoc, arbitrary time frames to measure acceptable performance in Young v. Dept. of Navy, 7 MSPR 73, 80, 7 MSPB 7 (1981), declining to sustain removal for a single instance of proven unacceptable performance charged under Chapter 75. Nonetheless, ad hoc standards were used as to a manager who should have been aware of production goals, even though the manager testified he was not given formal notice of those goals. Shustyk v. USPS, 32 MSPR 611, 617 (1987).

Bowling v. Dept. of Army, 40 MSPR 348, 351-52 (1989) (following remand, Bowling v. Dept. of Army, 47 MSPR 379, 383 (1991) [discussed later in this chapter, "Measurement of Work Error"] ), suggested the limits of ad hoc standards:

We recognize that it was within the agency's discretion to take the action against the appellant under 5 USC Chapter 75, governing adverse actions, rather than under 5 USC Chapter 43, governing performance-based actions.... In doing so, however, the agency may not circumvent Chapter 43 procedures by charging that the appellant should have performed better than the standards communicated to her.... Chapter 75 may be used to remove an employee for performance-based reasons only if the agency meets all requirements for establishing "such cause as will promote the efficiency of the service."... If it removes an employee under Chapter 75 based solely on performance that is governed by and meets the critical elements set forth for her position, the agency does not meet this standard. See Lovshin v. Department of the Navy, 767 F.2d at 842; Giltner v. Department of Air the Force, 39 MSPR 253, 259, 260 (1988).[2]

[2] We are aware of the agency's position that it removed the appellant for violating guidelines governing the practice of nursing, not for failing to meet her performance standard. The substance of the agency's charge, however, that the appellant committed a specified number of errors in recording hearing test data, is clearly covered by a performance standard of her position. The agency did not specify additional factors surrounding the charge that would make consideration of the performance standard improper or irrelevant.

Graham v. Dept. of Air Force, 46 MSPR 227, 235-36 (1990), involving removal for failure to maintain medical credentials, summarized the relation between performance standards developed for Chapter 43 cases and removals undertaken pursuant to Chapter 75 for poor performance:

[T]he Board has found that although the establishment of valid performance standards is an important substantive right of an employee under Chapter 43, there is no comparable provision requiring the establishment of valid performance standards for the long-term measurement of performance under Chapter 75.... The only limit that the court [in Lovshin] identified on an agency's use of Chapter 75 for performance-based actions is a prohibition on its use to circumvent Chapter 43 by charging that an employee should have performed better than the standards communicated to him in accordance with Chapter 43 requirements....

We recognize that in Wellman [10 MSPR 591 (1982)] ...the Board has found that in a Chapter 75 performance-based adverse action, an agency must prove the standard against which the employee's performance was measured.... These cases, however, are not inconsistent with our finding in the present appeal. In these cases, the Board simply required that the agency's imposed standard be reasonable and provide for accurate measurement of the employee's performance.... [U]nlike performance appraisal systems...measurement of performance in actions brought under Chapter 75 will frequently be ad hoc in nature....

Thus, the Board did not require that a specific standard be established and identified in advance for the employee; rather, it simply required that when the agency took an action, it prove that its measurement of the employee's performance was both accurate and reasonable. In the present case, the evidence does not show that the appellant had a specific set of standards governing the particular performance deficiencies with which he was charged.... [T]he agency had contended that the standard involved was that of "basic medical care." The appellant has not argued...that the agency's standard of basic medical care was unreasonable or did not provide an accurate measurement by which to judge his performance. Therefore, his assertion that the agency action must be reversed solely on the basis that it did not identify a particular standard must fail.

Following Graham, the Board recapitulated in Moore v. Dept. of Army, 59 MSPR 261, 265 (1993) (reviewing a counsel fee petition):

[A]n agency may bring an action for unacceptable performance under the procedures of 5 USC chapter 75, and specific intent is not a necessary element of an unacceptable performance charge.... The only limit on an agency's use of chapter 75 for performance-based actions is a prohibition on its use to circumvent chapter 43 by charging that the appellant should have performed better than required under the standards communicated to her in accordance with chapter 43 requirements.... Case law does not require that a specific standard of performance be established and identified in advance for the appellant in an action brought under chapter 75; rather, it simply requires that, when an agency takes an action for unacceptable performance under chapter 75, it prove that its measurement of the appellant's performance was both accurate and reasonable....

The linkage between performance standards and a Chapter 75 performance-based action can be weak. Shorey v. Dept. of Army, 77 MSPR 239, 244-45 (1998), emphasized notice of performance deficiencies rather than exact requirements of performance standards:

Our reviewing court, the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, has allowed the continued use of chapter 75 to effect actions that are entirely or partially performance-based. See Lovshin v. Department of the Navy, 767 F.2d 826, 843 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (en banc) (an agency may rely on either chapter 75 or chapter 43 to take a performance-based action). The agency's SF-50 states that the action taken was a removal under chapter 75. In addition, the administrative judge found that the agency removed the appellant pursuant to the provisions of chapter 75...and neither party, upon petition for review, has challenged her finding. The Board has held that an agency may not process an action under chapter 43 and then change the theory of its case to chapter 75 after hearing, by which point it has determined that it has not complied with all chapter 43 requirements. See Ortiz v. U.S. Marine Corps, 37 MSPR 359, 363 (1988). However, that is not what occurred here. We find that the action in this matter was taken, and was legitimately taken, under chapter 75.

We further find that the administrative judge has erred in applying chapter 43 standards to a chapter 75 case. It is well established that a specific standard of performance need not be established and identified in advance for the appellant in a performance action brought under chapter 75; rather, when an agency takes such an action under that chapter, it must simply prove that its measurement of the appellant's performance was both accurate and reasonable. See Moore v. Department of the Army, 59 MSPR 261, 265 (1993), appeal dismissed, 16 F.3d 422 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table).

An agency may not, though, circumvent chapter 43 by charging that an employee should have performed better than the standards communicated to her in accordance with chapter 43. See Lovshin, 767 F.2d at 842. The record here reflects no indication of surprise or circumvention; nor does the appellant make any such claim. The administrative judge, herself has usefully summarized the parties' stipulations, which set forth the specific requirements conveyed to the appellant, and the agency's numerous discussions of those requirements with her, including memoranda of deficient performance, followed by a 90-day performance improvement plan (PIP), with a 60-day extension. Based on the record evidence, the administrative judge's own findings, and the state of chapter 75 case law regarding performance-based actions, we find that the agency proved that its measurement of the appellant's performance was both accurate and reasonable. See Moore, 59 MSPR at 265....
