a. Factually Close Case

The 1984 Yorkshire decision seemed to make plain that an appellant who prevailed against agency charges was substantially innocent of the charged misconduct. At issue was the end result: a win for the employee under the Board's requirement that an agency prove its facts by preponderant evidence. Attempts were made to dilute a result-oriented analysis of the "substantial innocence" fee category by considering whether the evidence before the Board was close. Agency advocates wanted the Board to consider the evidentiary result as well as the determinants of that result.

In Thomson v. MSPB, 772 F.2d 879, 881 (Fed. Cir. 1985), the appellant was removed for threatening his supervisors and sleeping on duty. On appeal the Board concluded, on the basis of conflicting evidence, that the employee slept on his job but did not threaten his supervisor. The removal was reversed and a penalty of five days' suspension was imposed as the maximum sanction for a first offense of sleeping on duty. A counsel fee petition was denied by MSPB as not warranted in the interest of justice. The court reversed and remanded for reconsideration:

[T]he presiding official was wrong in laying down per se rules "that cases decided primarily on the basis of credibility do not warrant fee awards," "and that the interests of justice are not served by a fee award in factually close cases." There is no warrant in the Civil Service Reform Act or its legislative history or this court's decisions for such dogmatic per se pronouncements.... As for "factually close" cases, we do not say that the closeness of the evidence cannot ever be considered, as one factor, in determining whether the employee was substantially innocent of the charges. We hold, however, that all "factually close" cases do not automatically fall into the class in which fees are not available in the interest of justice.

On remand, the Board criticized the AJ for recharacterizing in the counsel fee proceedings the factual findings and conclusions drawn in the initial decision on the merits of the case. The Board determined that reduction of the removal to a five-day suspension warranted fees: the "outcome by the appellant represents a significant degree of success." The case was remanded to the region for determination of the reasonableness of the fees requested, Thomson v. Dept. of Navy, 33 MSPR 106, 112 (1987):

The administrative judge did not explicitly address whether the extent of the appellant's success was sufficient to entitle him to an attorney fee award. She did state that the appellant was "not substantially innocent of all the charges," but this conclusion stemmed from her finding that the agency was not negligent in taking the action, not as a separate basis for finding that an attorney fee award was not warranted. Although the administrative judge's findings on the less serious specifications of threats and threatening behavior were based on a resolution of factually close circumstances, "all 'factually close' cases do not automatically fall into the class in which fees are not available in the interest of justice."... Further, if the administrative judge ruled implicitly that an appellant must prevail on all charges to obtain entitlement to a fee award, such ruling is inconsistent with the court's determination that "the employee must prevail on substantially all the charges to be found 'substantially innocent.'" Yorkshire, 746 F.2d at 1458.

The Federal Circuit appeared to conclude that a factually close case would not bar fee recovery under the standard of substantial innocence. See Boese v. Dept. of Air Force, 784 F.2d 388, 391 (Fed. Cir. 1986) ("It is true that the presiding official thought the case 'close,' and for that reason his decision may perhaps not show that the defalcation charges were 'clearly without merit' or 'wholly unfounded' (emphasis added), but the decision does show, at the least, that Boese was 'substantially innocent'...of those charges."). A year after Boese, however, Massa v. DOD, 833 F.2d 991, 993 (Fed. Cir. 1987), determined that closeness of the evidence is a factor to be considered under the substantial innocence test:

In short, the substantially innocent standard means that, based on the record established before the Board, an employee is essentially without fault for the charges alleged, and was needlessly subjected to attorney fees in order to vindicate himself. Allen, 2 MSPB at 593 n.35, 2 MSPR at 434 n.35; see also Boese v. Department of Air Force, 784 F.2d 388, 391 n.4 (Fed. Cir. 1986); Yorkshire, 746 F.2d at 1457. As this court has stated, Congress did not intend the substantially innocent standard to punish agency misconduct, but "to minimize the burden an unsubstantiated accusation places upon innocent employees." Yorkshire, 746 F.2d at 1457. Thus, regardless of whether an employee prevails on all or only some of the contested charges, the closeness of the evidence is a factor to be considered in determining whether the employee is substantially innocent. See Thomson v. Merit Sys. Protection Bd., 772 F.2d 879, 881 (Fed. Cir. 1985).

In this case, the record shows that the agency lacked sufficient evidence to establish that Massa accepted gratuities from a government contractor. Massa, 815 F.2d at 72-73. Massa, however, did not win by default. He consistently asserted from the outset of the agency's action that he attended the racetrack at his father's invitation with the understanding that his father would pay a portion of the expenses, and without knowingly accepting anything from the contractor's agent, Mr. Potoker. Without an evidentiary basis to refute Massa's explanation, the agency nonetheless removed him. Despite early and continuing notice of Massa's position, the agency was unable to marshal enough evidence to overcome it. Massa was nothing less than an "innocent victim" of the agency's improper conduct. See Wise, 780 F.2d at 1000. Having considered the record as a whole, we conclude that Massa was essentially without fault for the charges alleged and needlessly subjected to attorney fees. Thus, he was "substantially innocent," and is entitled to an award of fees in the interest of justice.

A year later, the Board again held that an applicant for fees must go beyond an agency's failure to prove its charges by preponderant evidence to succeed. The Board properly considered the closeness of the evidence. Cayaban v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 87-3260).

The Board seemingly abandoned the disqualifying effect of the factually close case under the substantial innocence test, albeit with some prodding from the court, in Jackson v. Dept. of Air Force, 32 MSPR 525, 529 (1987) ("Here, although the administrative judge held that the 'weight of the evidence' was 'approximately equal,' he concluded that the agency failed to prove by preponderant evidence that appellant suffered from a condition which disqualified him from the duties of his position. Under the circumstances of this case, we find that appellant was substantially innocent of the charge against him and that 'substantial innocence' is an adequate ground for allowance of fees in the interest of justice."). Yet the decisions of the Federal Circuit concerning the relationship between factually close cases and the substantial innocence test for fee entitlement are unclear.
