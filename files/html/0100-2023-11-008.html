a. Offer of Proof

If an objection to a question at a hearing is sustained by the AJ and the testimony of the witness concerning that matter is not admitted, the party asking the question may make an offer of proof. See Judges' Handbook, Chapter 10, section 14.a. [misnumbering appears on the MSPB website]. The offer of proof need not be particularly elaborate. Edmond v. TVA, 23 MSPR 489, 492 (1984), explained:

We also conclude that the appellant made a sufficient offer of proof to meet the requirements of 5 CFR § 1201.62(b). The agency argues that because the appellant's counsel submitted no affidavits, statements, or evidentiary summaries that might be required by Fed. R. Evid. 103(a)(2), his statement on the record concerning the nature of the testimony was insufficient. Although they often provide helpful guidance, the federal rules of evidence are not binding on presiding officials or parties to a proceeding before the Board. Appellant's counsel described the proffered testimony sufficiently to determine the propriety of its exclusion. 5 CFR § 1201.62(b).

The offer of proof is a statement either of what a witness would say if he were permitted to answer or of what is expected to be proved by the answer to the question. The offer of proof can be an oral statement from the representative of the party making the offer; it may be a written statement of what the witness would say if testifying; or it may be accomplished, if allowed by the AJ, through question and answer on the record with the prospective witness. Whatever form it takes, the offer must be made or the exclusion of the evidence will not be reviewed in a PFR. Make sure, through an offer of proof, that the record is protected when an AJ decides to exclude evidence. Request reconsideration of the exclusion ruling after the offer is made. If an offer of proof is made during the hearing, while a witness is on the stand whose testimony is sought, it is good practice to ask the AJ to excuse that witness from the hearing room while the offer is made and discussed, so that the witness is not essentially instructed what to say if his testimony is allowed.

As to documents offered into evidence but rejected by the AJ, the offer of proof is generally the document itself, coupled with a brief explanation of why it is significant to the case. Confirm with the AJ that she has established, as part of the case file, a rejected exhibits file. In this way, a higher-level adjudicator will be able to see what was refused by the AJ and to make a determination on the propriety of the ruling excluding the document from evidence. The AJ is required by 5 CFR 1201.61 (2022) to describe any evidence and testimony that has been offered in the hearing and excluded, and the AJ is to ensure that the description is made a part of the record.
