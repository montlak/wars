1. Refusal or Failure to Perform Work

The Board has not issued an all-encompassing definition of insubordination. Because unjustified refusal to follow directives and deliberate disrespect for authority take so many forms, the definition of insubordination develops around what justifications are unacceptable. Few proffered justifications for direct refusals to work are accepted; in those few cases, the order was unlawful or the employee did not truly refuse to do the work; instead, the employee was unable to perform the work or the directives were unclear. One decision reversed removal for insubordination of an employee who tried but failed to comply with a supervisor's impossible workload requirements. See Yetman v. Dept. of Army, 36 MSPR 425, 427 (1988). A charge of negligence, also characterized as an assertion that the employee refused to do her job, was not sustained when the appellant replied to a request to take dictation that she had to do time and attendance reports and a travel voucher. The practice was for the appellant to tell her supervisor when she was working on a job that had to be completed. The appellant did not refuse to take dictation, but only informed the supervisor that she was working on other tasks at the time. Davies v. USDA, 5 MSPR 251, 253, 5 MSPB 291 (1981). But the Board will sustain discipline when it is demonstrated that an employee deliberately failed to carry out work orders. Meads v. VA, 36 MSPR 574, 584 (1988); see Mitchell v. USPS, 7 MSPR 141, 142 (1981) (mail carrier was removed for failing to meet the requirements of his position when he refused to obey supervisory instructions concerning the distribution of mail: "If the employee is continually insubordinate, it does not promote the efficiency of the service to return him to duty in a two-man post office with the supervisor whose instructions he refuses to follow."). The decisions of the Board and the courts suggest concern about impromptu one-person "strikes." Lumped into the category of failure to follow orders is the charge that an employee slowed down work, such as the machinery he operates. Rewald v. USPS, 34 MSPR 13, 15 (1987).

The rule in federal and private sectors is: obey first, grieve later. See Wyche v. USPS, 208 Fed. Appx. 858, 861 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP) ("An employee does not have an unfettered right to disregard an order merely because there is substantial reason to believe that the order is not proper. Meads v. Veterans Admin., 36 M.S.P.R. 574, 578-79 (1988); see also Ingram v. Dep't of Justice, 44 M.S.P.R. 578, 582 (1990), aff'd, 925 F.2d 1479 (Fed. Cir. 1991). The employee must first comply with the order and then register his complaint or grievance, except in limited circumstances where obedience would place the employee in a clearly dangerous situation. Id."); DeBusk v. United States, 132 Ct. Cl. 790, 797 (1955) ("If the conduct of his superiors and coworkers was improper, as plaintiff alleges, the remedy lies in the established procedures. Certainly it could not be corrected by internal conflicts in the operation of a large organization."); Malone v. VA, CH-0752-12-0171-I-1 (NP 3/4/2014) ("The Board has held that an employee does not have the unfettered right to disregard an order merely because there is substantial reason to believe that the order is not proper; he must first comply with the order and then register his complaint or grievance, except in certain limited circumstances where obedience would place the employee in a clearly dangerous situation, or when complying with the order would cause him irreparable harm. Cooke v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 M.S.P.R. 401, 407-08 (1995), aff'd, 73 F.3d 380 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table)."); Frickey v. Dept. of Army, 61 MSPR 475, 480 (1994) (there was a duty to follow orders, if a clearly dangerous situation did not exist, and lodge a complaint later); Lewis v. Bureau of Engraving and Printing, 29 MSPR 447, 453 (1985) ("[E]ven if the appellant believed that he did not have to obey his supervisor's order, he was nonetheless bound to obey the order at the time it was given and to challenge it later.").

Personal inconvenience does not justify insubordination. As to a refusal to travel, arguably justified by family circumstances, in SSA v. Boham, 38 MSPR 540, 546 (1988) (the charge was refusal to accept assignments requiring travel, not insubordination, but the reasoning applies to insubordination charges):

The respondent...does not have a right to be exempted from assignments which required travel since there is no inherent right for employees to be excused from inconvenient work assignments. Employees may seek and management may, in its discretion, grant such exceptions. For this Board to judge the reasonableness of such agency actions, as respondent urges, on hindsight would unduly limit an agency's right to assign work by unnecessarily impeding its ability to insure the performance of its mission.

The Board considers the broader implications of an incident of insubordination than may appear in the conduct specifically charged against the employee. One appeal resulted in reversal of an AJ's decision to mitigate a removal stemming from insubordination. The incident charged was but one part of a more serious refusal to accept program direction, a refusal that was a "direct challenge to management over who was to be ultimately responsible for the planning and direction." Bassett v. Dept. of Navy, 34 MSPR 66, 69 (1987). An appellant was directed to account for and return some documents to the agency. Although he was no longer in possession of the documents, he was insubordinate because he offered no response to the agency's directive. Clark v. EEOC, 42 MSPR 467, 475 (1989). A refusal to answer questions or cooperate in an assignment is a failure to carry out assigned work. See Shaw v. Dept. of Air Force, 80 MSPR 98, 113 (1998). The refusal or purposeful failure to take agency-sponsored training warranted removal for insubordination. Hayes v. USDA, 9 MSPR 618, 622-23 (1982) (the appellant failed to bring psychological or other problems to his supervisors' attention until four months after he was first directed to complete training).

Misrepresentation reflects disrespect toward authority and constitutes insubordination "since agency employees are expected to respect authority and follow the orders of supervisory officials." Phillips v. GSA, 38 MSPR 206, 213 (1988), rev'd, Phillips v. GSA, 878 F.2d 370, 373-74 (Fed. Cir. 1989). The Federal Circuit's Phillips decision reinjected the element of intentional refusal to obey an order into the insubordination equation as to an employee disciplined for failing to pay a delinquent government-sponsored Diners Club card billing:

Insubordination by an employee is a willful and intentional refusal [emphasis supplied] to obey an authorized order of a superior officer which the officer is entitled to have obeyed....

...

[T]he petitioner never at any time refused to obey the agency's order to produce documentary proof of the payment of the Diners Club bill, but at all times tried to obey the order and in fact finally did so, although a few days late.

Although the Board apparently accepts the concept in Phillips that insubordination must be established by a willful and intentional refusal to obey, Forgett v. Dept. of Army, 45 MSPR 198, 203 (1990), the employee may be disciplined for an impermissible delay in executing her supervisor's orders. This is a diluted version of insubordination that may support a penalty as great as or less than the penalty supported by insubordination, depending on the circumstances. See Ford v. Dept. of Navy, 43 MSPR 495, 500-01 (1990) (appellant never specifically refused to obey, and eventually complied; appellant lacked intent necessary to sustain insubordination; appellant engaged in impermissible delay in executing her supervisor's orders); Bellamy v. Dept. of Navy, 13 MSPR 526, 528 (1982) (appellant failed to follow through on a directive to obtain a passport when he failed to obtain the necessary supporting documentation or to explain why he could not obtain the material).
