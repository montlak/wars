10. Retroactivity of 1989 Whistleblower Amendments; Continuing Violation

The WPA provisions became effective as to actions initiated after July 9, 1989. Actions that were earlier proposed, threatened, or taken do not have the protection of the WPA. Eidmann v. MSPB, 976 F.2d 1400 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (applying the rule to provisions of the WPA governing Special Counsel prosecutions for prohibited personnel practices); Knollenberg v. MSPB, 953 F.2d 623 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (also providing some explanation of the nature of an IRA action); Wuchinich v. Dept. of Labor, 53 MSPR 220, 223-24 (1992) (determining the effective date of the WPA relative to a series of actions involved in the nonselection for a promotion). The Board further elaborated on the definition of what constitutes "pending" status of a performance-based action in Augustine v. DOJ, 50 MSPR 648, 650-51 (1991); Herring v. Dept. of Treasury, 44 MSPR 673, 675 (1990) ("In Marshall v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 44 M.S.P.R. 28, 31-33 (1990), we found that the Act's savings clause, as interpreted by the Board's regulations set forth at 54 Fed. Reg. 53,500, 53,522 (1989) (to be codified at 5 C.F.R. § 1201.191(b)), does not give employees the right to appeal to the Board an action that was pending upon the effective date of the Act. In Marshall, at 32-33, we further found that an action is 'pending' as of the date an employee receives notice of the proposed agency action."); Marshall v. VA, 44 MSPR 28, 31 (1990) (quoting and interpreting the savings clause of the WPA and applying the Board's interpretative regulation at 5 CFR 1201.191(b)); see Burns v. Dept. of Air Force, 45 MSPR 293, 296 (1990) (interpreting § 7(b) of Pub. L. 101-12; 5 CFR 1201.191(b)(2)).

In 1994 the WPA was amended to redefine burdens of proof and remedies and to add provisions concerning covered personnel actions. Deciding the amendments were substantive because they increased the scope of governmental liability, the Board declined to retroactively apply the 1994 amendments in Caddell v. DOJ, 66 MSPR 347 (1995), aff'd, Caddell v. DOJ, 96 F.3d 1367 (Fed. Cir. 1996). See Davis v. DOD (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 95-3700) ("No provision of the WPA is to be applied by the Board in such a way as to affect any pending administrative proceeding that began before the effective date of the Act. WPA, Pub. L. No. 101-12, § 7(b), 103 Stat. 16, 34 (1989). The term 'proceeding' encompasses existing agency proceedings, including personnel actions that were proposed, threatened, or taken before July 9, 1989. Butz v. Dept. of Navy, 45 MSPR 539, 540 (1990)."), followed in Davis v. DOD (Fed. Cir. 996 NP No. 95-3700); Briley v. Nat'l Archives and Records Admin., 71 MSPR 211, 223 (1996) (a 1994 WPA amendment adding the coverage of the statute to a decision ordering a psychiatric fitness examination was not retroactive because it enlarged the category of actions encompassed in the definition of covered personnel action).

Nonselection for a promotion after the effective date of the WPA was within the statutory coverage, even if the vacancy announcement was posted before the statute's effective date. Slake v. Dept. of Treasury, 53 MSPR 207, 214 n.6 (1992) (failure to secure a career ladder promotion after the effective date would be covered even though the petitioner might have been eligible for promotion earlier); see Di Pompo v. VA, 62 MSPR 44, 48-49 (1994) ("[t]he fact that the alleged whistleblowing activities of the appellant's father took place prior to the July 9, 1989 effective date of the WPA does not deprive the Board of IRA jurisdiction because the alleged reprisal against the appellant by the agency took place after the effective date of the WPA."); Wuchinich v. Dept. of Labor, 53 MSPR 220, 223 (1992) (following Slake and noting that "there is no indication that any other action directly leading to the appellant's non-selection arose prior to the effective date of the Act").

In extensive discussion of continuing violations, Gergick v. GSA, 49 MSPR 384, 389 (1991), determined that an IRA action is timely even if it is part of a continuing practice of reprisal that began before the effective date of the statute. See Owen v. Dept. of Air Force, 63 MSPR 621, 630 (1994); cf. Pulcini v. SSA, 83 MSPR 685, 693 ¶ 15 (1999) ("[A] continuing practice of reprisal violates the WPA."). A broad interpretation of continuing violations was rejected in Bosley v. MSPB, 162 F.3d 665, 667 (Fed. Cir. 1998):

Mr. Bosley argues that he has suffered a "continuing violation" of the WPA from 1988 to the present because the denial of a quality step increase in 1988 has had continuing effects on each of his paychecks since then. A "continuing violation" has been held to overcome statutory time bars when it would have been unreasonable to expect the plaintiff to sue at an early stage in a continuing course of conduct, such as when particular conduct would constitute, or be recognized as, a violation only in light of later events. See Galloway v. General Motors Service Parts Operations, 78 F.3d 1164, 1166-67 (7th Cir. 1996). That was plainly not the case here. The denial of a pay increase to Mr. Bosley had monetary effects that continued into the future, just as any refusal to promote an employee or hire an applicant would have. Such consequences of an alleged violation, however, do not constitute separate violations or render the original violation continuing. See Delaware State College v. Ricks, 449 U.S. 250, 259, 101 S. Ct. 498, 66 L. Ed.2d 431 (1980); Brown Park Estates-Fairfield Dev. Co. v. United States, 127 F.3d 1449, 1456 (Fed. Cir. 1997); Dasgupta v. University of Wis. Bd. of Regents, 121 F.3d 1138, 1140 (7th Cir. 1997); Lane v. United States, 208 Ct. Cl. 955, 956 (1975). Mr. Bosley's "continuing violation" theory with respect to the denial of his quality step increase is therefore inapplicable on the facts of this case.
