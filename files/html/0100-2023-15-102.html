5. Transaction With Another; Gift; Bribe; Honorarium; Loan

"Solicitation of a bribe is a very serious offense for a government procurement officer, which goes to the heart of the procurement process." Swindle v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 95-3779). Acceptance of gratuities from a private contractor with intent to influence warranted removal in Creedon v. DLA (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-1288). Involvement in bribery equates to removal. Moserowitz v. FAA (Fed. Cir. 1986 NP No. 86-1013); cf. United States v. Sun-Diamond Growers of California, 119 S. Ct. 1402 (1999) (construing necessary elements of the "illegal gratuity statute," 18 USC 201(c)(1)(A)).

Discharge is justified if, in violation of agency rules, an employee accepts food, gifts, or entertainment from a firm whose products the employee is supposed to review. Bruch v. DHHS (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-1019); see Lewis v. DOJ, 34 Fed. Appx. 774, 776 (Fed. Cir. 2002 NP) (demotion to a nonsupervisory job of an employee who solicited tickets for a sporting event from an individual subject to agency regulatory processes); Aliota v. VA, 65 MSPR 644, 653 (1994) (following remand from 60 MSPR 491 (1994)) (discipline included suspension and a geographical reassignment, following the receipt of a consultation fee by the appellant from a pharmaceutical firm that contracted with the VA to conduct a clinical study); Gonzalez v. Dept. of Air Force, 51 MSPR 646, 650 (1991) (charge of attempting to use a public office for personal gain arising from communication by a contract price analyst to a contractor appearing to solicit future employment; an agency regulation stating that agency personnel "may have financial interests or engage in financial transactions to the same extent as private citizens not employed by the Government" only permitted transactions consistent with another regulation prohibiting actions that resulted in or had the appearance of using public office for private gain); Baker v. DHHS, 41 MSPR 363, 367-68 (1989), aff'd, Baker v. DHHS, 912 F.2d 1448 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (acceptance of a meal from a contractor and divulging confidential information to the contractor); Burkett v. GSA, 27 MSPR 119, 122-23 (1985) (sustaining removal of a building manager on a charge of using the office for the purpose of obtaining private gain; use of the government contracting process to purchase materials to repair personal residence).

Heffron v. United States, 405 F.2d 1307, 1313 (Ct. Cl. 1969), sustained dismissal of a contract administrator who accepted bottles of liquor from a contractor:

...The Congress appropriates funds to provide what it deems adequate salaries, frequently adjusted, for those who execute its laws, and on the other hand, the effort is made to restrict the citizenry to expressing its good will towards them in tokens other than money and articles of value. It may well be anticipated, however, that the smallest leak in the dike will swiftly widen, and the old river of gratuities will again flow in the old way. Human nature will reassert itself. It may not be unreasonable, therefore, to believe that what is required is a combination of emphatic warnings and drastic penalties. If at times, as here, this results in tragically wrecking an honorable career for an infraction apparently not of the gravest, this is part of the price that must be paid to maintain the respect and the self-respect of our Government. It is not the result of arbitrary whim or personal vindictiveness. If we judges think we know of some better way to handle this problem, we are not at liberty to impose it in the adjudication of cases.

Fontes v. Dept. of Transp., 51 MSPR 655 (1991), concerned acceptance by appellant of a discounted room rate from the operator of a hotel who also controlled a security firm being inspected by appellant's team. OPM regulations aside, acceptance of a gratuity from someone whose business operations are subject to inspection by the agency could undermine the agency's interest in assuring that its employees refrain from actions that might result in, or create the appearance of, losing complete independence and impartiality in inspection activities. Fontes mitigated a removal to a demotion to a nonsupervisory position based on the agency's overstatement of the notoriety of appellant's offense as well as the agency's exaggeration of the amount of the gratuity accepted by the appellant. Wells v. DOD, 53 MSPR 637, 644-45 (1992), discounted the severity of attendance by the appellant at a class valued at $60 and paid for by an agency contractor. The gratuity was not of significant value; the appellant did not repeat his act; and no evidence showed that the appellant acted maliciously or for gain or that the contractor received any special consideration for providing the gratuity to the appellant. Faitel v. VA, 26 MSPR 465, 470 (1985), imposed a 14-day suspension for acceptance of very small personal loans from an outside contractor doing business with the agency. The appellant, an engineering technician, was not then directly involved in administration of the corporation's contract with the agency. Pfaehler v. DHHS, 20 MSPR 159, 161 (1984), involved the removal, reduced to a suspension, of an employee charged with using her position to further the private interest of a grant applicant in violation of agency standards of conduct. The appellant's conduct apparently stemmed from her interest in holistic medicine; no monetary gain appeared to be involved. Upon appellant's display of contrition, her lack of training, and her potential for rehabilitation, the removal penalty was mitigated.

Acceptance of something of value must be a knowing acceptance. If the gratuity, e.g., a meal, is thought to have been provided by someone from whom it can be legitimately accepted and it turns out to have been paid for by someone else, the charge of acceptance of gratuities will not be sustained. Massa v. DOD, 815 F.2d 69 (Fed. Cir. 1987). If the contractor provides a benefit to an employee engaged in an otherwise arms-length transaction with the contractor, and if the benefit is demanded by the employee with some justification on the employee's part, the agency does not establish impropriety by a wholly subjective belief on the part of the contractor that the benefit was necessary to prevent future disagreements concerning agency business. See Paniagua v. GSA, 23 MSPR 229, 232 (1984).

The 1989 Ethics Reform Act precluded receipt by employees of any honorarium, a term in turn defined to include any compensation paid for an appearance, speech, or article. Tested against the First Amendment, the prohibition fell since, in most cases, the content of the petitioners' intended communications had nothing to do with their jobs and did not even arguably have an adverse impact upon service efficiency. The Supreme Court decided that a wholesale disincentive to employees' expression imposed a significant burden on the public's right to read and hear what the employees would otherwise have said. Determining that the government failed to demonstrate that the recited harms were real, not merely conjectural, and that the regulation would in fact alleviate those harms in a direct and material way, the Court concluded that the speculative benefits of the legislation advanced by the government did not justify the burden on employees' freedom to engage in expressive activities. The law violated the First Amendment as to employees at or below the level of GS-16. United States v. NTEU, 115 S. Ct. 1003 (1995).
