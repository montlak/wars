g. Erroneous Ruling

The Board considers an allegation of bias a serious matter challenging the integrity of the AJ and going to the core of the judicial function. These charges are not taken lightly and should not be lightly presented. An erroneous ruling by an AJ, standing alone, is not evidence of bias. Weaver v. Dept. of Navy, 2 MSPR 129, 134, 2 MSPB 297 (1980). A "judge's rulings alone, even if they are shown to be incorrect...are not sufficient to establish bias or incompetence mandating disqualification." Womack v. USPS, 67 MSPR 58, 60 (1995). That the AJ accepted one party's version of the facts over that of the other is not evidence of bias. Hutchcraft v. Dept. of Transp., 60 MSPR 299, 303 (1994) ("Indeed, resolving such evidentiary conflicts is a fundamental function of the adjudicatory process"); cf. Stroncheck v. Dept. of Navy, AT-0752-21-0347-I-1 (NP 5/9/2022) ("It is well settled that an administrative judge's case-related rulings, including those concerning credibility, even if erroneous, are insufficient to establish bias. King v. Department of the Army, 84 M.S.P.R. 235, ¶ 6 (1999); Myers v. Department of Agriculture, 81 M.S.P.R. 496, ¶ 29 (1999)."). Nor, under Hutchcraft, is it inappropriate for an AJ to make a ruling as strong as possible: "The fact that an adjudicator gives two reasons for rejecting an argument, rather than one, is evidence of prudence, not bias." An appellant's contentions concerning the alleged bias of the AJ, representing no more than a disagreement with the AJ's analysis of the facts or an assertion that he incorrectly ruled on certain objections to the introduction of evidence, are insufficient to demonstrate bias. Adams v. Dept. of Transp., 15 MSPR 72, 78 (1983). The AJ is not biased because she did not consider the testimony of a witness when the appellant did not make a prehearing submission proposing any witnesses. Beeler-Smith v. OPM, 112 MSPR 479, 481 ¶ 4, 2009 MSPB 203 (2009). Repeated attempts by the AJ to prevent the appellant's representative from introducing irrelevant evidence are not evidence of bias. Azevedo v. Dept. of Air Force, 35 MSPR 47, 50 (1987).
