11. Library of Congress; GAO; Congressional Employees

Library of Congress employees work in the legislative branch; they are not in the competitive service. They may not appeal removals to the MSPB. Gardner v. Library of Congress, 774 F.2d 1081 (Fed. Cir. 1985); see Nieuwdorp v. Library of Congress, 872 F.2d 1000, 1002 (Fed. Cir. 1989) ("For this court to entertain Nieuwdorp's petition for review from an arbitrator's decision, we would have to have review jurisdiction over a final decision made under the Library's personnel regulations. This court has previously held that the Library's personnel regulations do not give us jurisdiction to review the personnel decisions of the Head Librarian. See Carter v. Library of Congress, 261 Fed. Appx. 283 (Fed. Cir. 2008) (the court lacks jurisdiction to review arbitrator's award arising from an adverse action against a Library employee when the court could only review a case that could have been appealed to the Board and the Board had no jurisdiction over the adverse action); Gardner v. Library of Congress, 774 F.2d 1081 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Therefore, under section 7121(f) we are without jurisdiction to entertain Nieuwdorp's petition as well."); Barger v. Mumford, 265 F.2d 380 (D.C. Cir. 1959) ("The Librarian contends that such exemption applies to all employees of the Library, including appellant. Although we have found no court decision expressly deciding that the Library of Congress 'in or under' the legislative branch of the Government, we do find that status officially recognized in several ways.").

For a discussion of the procedures governing EEO complaints by Senate employees, and judicial review of those complaints, refer to Riggin v. Office of Senate Fair Employment Practices, 61 F.3d 1563 (Fed. Cir. 1995), and Johnson v. Office of Senate Fair Employment Practices, 35 F.3d 1566 (Fed. Cir. 1994). Chen v. GAO, 821 F.2d 732 (D.C. Cir. 1987), reviewed the system used by GAO to address employee discharge cases. See Willis v. GAO (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 00-6001) (reviewing a decision of GAO Personnel Appeals Board sustaining the dismissal of an employee; the MSPB CALJ served as the GAO's hearing officer). Schmelzer v. Office of Compliance, 155 F.3d 1364 (Fed. Cir. 1998), makes brief mention of RIF protections provided by the 1995 Congressional Accountability Act for legislative employees. See Wooten v. Office of Compliance (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 00-6002) (applying usual judicial review standards to arguments based on the substantiality of evidence before the Office of Compliance hearing officer); Hill v. GAO (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 98-6006) (appeal from RIF of GAO employee). Office of the Architect of the Capitol v. Office of Compliance, 361 F.3d 633 (Fed. Cir. 2004), provides insight into the EEO process applied to a disability discrimination claim. The structure of the Congressional Accountability Act and limitations under the Speech and Debate Clause of the Constitution upon suits against members of Congress accused of discriminatory personnel practices were explored in Fields v. Office of Eddie Bernice Johnson, 459 F.3d 1 (D.C. Cir. 2006). Although the evidence did not demonstrate reprisal, Duncan v. Office of Compliance, 541 F.3d 1377, 1379 (Fed. Cir. 2008), determined that the anti-reprisal section of the Congressional Accountability Act, 2 USC 1317(a), covered a complaint of opposition to "any practice made unlawful" under the Occupational Safety and Health Act, 29 USC 651, et seq. Morris v. Office of Compliance, 608 F.3d 1344 (Fed. Cir. 2010), concluded that the Federal Circuit lacked jurisdiction to review a decision of the Board of Directors of the OCC when it denied exceptions to an arbitrator's award involving a termination action. For unfair labor practice analysis applied to legislative employees, refer to U.S. Capitol Police v. Office of Compliance, 878 F.3d 1355 (Fed. Cir. 2018). Some components of the labor relations system for congressional employees are described in U.S. Capitol Police v. Office of Compliance, 908 F.3d 748 (Fed. Cir. 2018), and U.S. Capitol Police v. Office of Compliance, 913 F.3d 1361 (Fed. Cir. 2019). For employees of the legislative branch covered by the Congressional Accountability Act and with appeals within the jurisdiction of the Office of Compliance, the MSPB entered an interagency agreement of February 2009 with the OCC providing for the Board to assign administrative judges for mediation and hearings.
