6. Format of Petition for Review

The style and substance of the petition are outlined in the regulation, 5 CFR 2424.22(b) (2022), earlier excerpted. FLRA offers a preprinted form for a negotiability appeal on its Internet website, www.flra.gov, and current regulations suggest that the form "or a substantially similar format" is to be used to present the petition for review, and e-filing is available. The regulatory requirements are not absolute, and deviations from FLRA procedures are allowed to ensure adjudication of negotiability disputes. If the union fails to provide the text of a disputed proposal in its petition for review, that omission may readily be remedied by a statement of position of either the union or the agency. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, IRS, 21 FLRA 667, 667-68 (1986); see AFGE Council of Prison Locals, Local 171 and FCI El Reno, 52 FLRA 1484, 1488-89 (1997) (only statutory requirement is negotiability appeal be instituted before 15th day after date the agency alleges nonnegotiability; FLRA regulations govern other aspects of process; corrections to conform to procedural requirements established by FLRA may be made within time specified by FLRA).

Despite FLRA's occasional ruling that a union may remedy technical deficiencies in negotiability petitions, that opportunity is not always provided to the union. If the petition fails to include necessary material, e.g., text of regulations sought to be negotiated, the petition will be dismissed. IBEW Local 1245 and Dept. of Interior, Bureau of Reclamation, 25 FLRA 201, 204 (1987). There must be a proposal in dispute. Identifying a potential area of collective bargaining does not frame a proposal within FLRA's ability to determine a bargaining obligation. See IFPTE Local 777 and Dept. of Army, Corps of Eng'rs, Chicago, Ill., 49 FLRA 1517, 1518 (1994). FLRA may be unsure whether the union is really requesting a negotiability determination, e.g., when the union's communication with FLRA seems more in the nature of a protest against the agency's bargaining techniques than a negotiability appeal. FLRA may then dismiss the petition without prejudice to the union's right to refile if no time limit would otherwise be violated, e.g., when the union's petition appears to be from an unrequested declaration of nonnegotiability. See IFPTE Local 89 and Dept. of Interior, Bureau of Reclamation, 45 FLRA 230, 230-31 (1992). Negotiability issues must be timely raised and not delayed pending resolution of another issue. A union should not object on procedural grounds to the agency's rejection of a contract and expect that FLRA will later permit the union to file a response concerning the substantive negotiability of the proposals contained in the agreement. AFGE Local 1815 and Army Aviation Ctr., Ft. Rucker, 28 FLRA 1172, 1173 (1987).

The better practice is for the petition for review to be complete. A union petition for review in a negotiability case must contain an explicit statement of the meaning of the proposal by the exclusive representative. Int'l Ass'n of Firefighters Local F-109 and Dept. of Army, Hq., Carlisle Barracks, 8 FLRA 35, 36 n.* (1982). If the language of union proposals and the explanation in the record provided by the parties does not reveal the intended purpose and effect of the proposals, the negotiability appeal will be dismissed as insufficiently specific. NTEU Chap. 66 and IRS, Kansas City Serv. Ctr., 6 FLRA 49, 50 (1981). The explanation need not be elaborate. Approved as sufficient in IFPTE and Dept. of Interior, Bureau of Reclamation, Denver, 43 FLRA 998, 999 (1992), was a statement that a proposal regarding the selection of employees to serve on an advisory committee was submitted "in connection with impact and implementation bargaining concerning the ASC [Administrative Service Center] Fitness Center." If a union's statement of position regarding the meaning of a provision is not sufficiently precise, it may be amended. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Bureau of Gov't Fin. Operations, 21 FLRA 652, 652 (1986).

FLRA provided an example of how a complete petition for review could have saved the day, AFGE Local 3760 and DHHS, SSA, 33 FLRA 498, 501 (1988):

Where a union files a petition which meets the requirements of the regulations and an agency fails to file a statement of position, the Authority will determine the negotiability of the proposals in question on that record.... In this case, the failure on the part of the Agency to file a statement of position did not cause dismissal of the Union's petition. The record in this case consisted of the Union's incomplete petition for review and the Union's incomplete response to the Authority's request to provide certain missing information. The record was insufficient for the Authority to make a negotiability determination.

The Union also asserts that we should grant its request for reconsideration because the Authority has provided no guidance concerning the action a union should take when an agency fails to file a statement of position. However, the Authority has consistently held that the parties bear the burden of creating a record upon which the Authority can make a negotiability determination. A party failing to meet this burden acts at its own peril.... A union filing a petition for review under part 2424 of our regulations should comply fully with the requirements set out in those regulations and submit a petition which can support its position concerning the negotiability of the proposals in dispute.

To conserve resources, FLRA may consolidate cases involving proposals that present identical issues. Nat'l Weather Serv. Employees Org. and Dept. of Commerce, NOAA, Nat'l Weather Serv., 44 FLRA 18, 19 (1992); see NFFE Local 951 and Dept. of Interior, BLM, 59 FLRA 951, 951 (2004) ("Given the similarities in the cases, and noting the parties' arguments are the same in both cases, we have consolidated them for decision.") (union petition for review granted on other grounds, NFFE Local 951 v. FLRA, 412 F.3d 119 (D.C. Cir. 2005)).
