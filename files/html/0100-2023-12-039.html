a. Conditional Cross-PFR

A cross-PFR can be used to preserve a claim or argument only if the appellant's PFR is granted. See Diefenderfer v. Dept. of Transp., 108 MSPR 651, 657-58 n.3, 2008 MSPB 105 (2008). A cross-petition was used to preserve a claim that was conditionally waived in an appeal on the basis of an outcome that was assured by an AJ when that outcome was reversed by the Board through an agency PFR. That situation arose when an EEO claim was waived based upon the AJ's assurance to the appellant (and the agency) of a particular remedy when it was clear that the AJ would set aside as procedurally deficient a period of enforced leave, Bennett v. Dept. of Transp., 105 MSPR 634, 642-43 ¶¶ 21-22, 2007 MSPB 133 (2007):

As noted above, the appellant raised in his appeal a claim of discrimination based on gender and race, as well as a claim of reprisal for his prior EEO activity. During a status conference held before the initial decision was issued, he withdrew this claim and his request for a hearing, based on his "understanding that the [administrative judge] would reverse the agency action based on the parties' joint stipulations...." In his cross petition for review, the appellant asks that he be permitted to reinstate his claim and request for a hearing if the agency's petition for review is granted based on any factual assertions in that petition. He also states that, if the Board denies the agency's petition for review, he withdraws his own petition.

We have not granted the agency's petition, and we therefore consider the appellant's cross petition withdrawn. However, the appellant withdrew his affirmative defenses and his request for a hearing on those claims under circumstances in which he had reason to believe that the Board could award him back pay. If the appellant were to prevail on his claim of discrimination, the administrative judge, the Board, or the EEOC might conclude that he is entitled to relief to which he would not otherwise be entitled. See, e.g., Gibson, 527 U.S. at 217-22 (holding that the EEOC had the authority to award compensatory damages). Under these circumstances, and in light of the appellant's expressed interest in pursuing his discrimination and reprisal claims if they have not become moot, we REMAND this appeal for a hearing on those claims. If the administrative judge finds on remand that the appellant has substantiated any of his claims of discrimination and reprisal, the administrative judge shall determine whether relief may be ordered based on this finding.
