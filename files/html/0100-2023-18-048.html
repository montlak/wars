d. Error in Date

Misstatement of the date of a criminal conviction in an adverse action notice was not harmful error if the employee was fully aware of the referenced case. Walcott v. USPS, 52 MSPR 277, 282 (1992) ("[A] mistake in stating the date of an incident of misconduct, absent a showing by the appellant that the misstatement constituted harmful procedural error, did not warrant not sustaining the charge."), accord, Redschlag v. Dept. of Army, 89 MSPR 589, 620 ¶ 55 (2001) ("Even if the agency incorrectly identified the date on which this incident occurred, the appellant did not show that the misstatement constituted harmful procedural error."); Viana v. Dept. of Treasury, 114 MSPR 659, 661-62 ¶ 5, 2010 MSPB 196 (2010) (error in the date of an incident does not alone constitute harmful procedural error); Hayes v. Dept. of Navy, 15 MSPR 378, 379 (1983) ("Notwithstanding the preciseness of the date of occurrence, we find that the evidence of record is clear regarding the charge and specification for the removal action and the documentation in support of the charge"); Palmer, supra.

In a labor strike case, an employee was given an adverse action proposal stating that he was on strike from August 13 through the date of the proposal, August 18. The employee was on approved leave through August 16. The employee argued that the notice was deficient. The court disagreed: the notice fairly advised the employee that he would have to defend the charge of striking on August 17. Proof of one day of striking was held "entirely sufficient" to warrant removal. Campbell v. Dept. of Transp., FAA, 735 F.2d 497 (Fed. Cir. 1984). Hutson v. Dept. of Interior, 67 MSPR 432, 445 (1995), determined that an error in dates in the proposal notice was insufficient to set the charge aside:

Regarding the AJ's finding that the appellant did not make these statements on July 13, 1992, as alleged by the agency, we note that the agency's removal decision letter does state that the appellant made these statements on July 13, 1992. However, the agency's removal proposal letter indicates that the statements were merely reported to Bauer on July 13, 1992, and Schiller-O'Dean's July 25, 1992 affidavit indicates that the statements were made on unspecified dates prior to July 13, 1992. Under the circumstances, we find that the dates of the alleged statements are not a material part of the misconduct charged under this specification and that the imprecise statement in the agency's removal decision letter did not prejudice the appellant's ability to refute the specification as charged.

An error in dates of AWOL in a proposal did not invalidate the action when the appellant knew of and defended against the substance of the charges, Williams v. USPS, 68 MSPR 150, 153-54 (1995):

In the notice of proposed removal, the agency charged the appellant AWOL for 1.25 hours on January 5, 1993. The Absence Analysis of the appellant's leave record shows, however, that on January 5, the appellant used 3.5 hours of sick leave and there is no AWOL indicated. The appellant's leave record reflects that he was AWOL for 1.75 hours on January 6, 1993. Id. Thus, it appears that the agency's charge referred to an incorrect date and amount of AWOL.

An employee is entitled to advance written notice stating the specific reasons for the proposed action. 5 USC 7513(b)(1). In order to satisfy this notice requirement, an agency "is required to state the specific reason for a proposed adverse action in sufficient detail to allow the employee to make an informed reply." Smith v. Department of Labor, 25 MSPR 102, 103 (1984). Where procedural error is shown, however, reversal is warranted only where such error was likely to have had a harmful effect upon the outcome regarding the charge. The burden is on the appellant to show that, based upon the record as a whole, the error was harmful. See Palmer v. United States Postal Service, 36 MSPR 263, 266 (1988).

Although the agency referred to the AWOL as occurring on January 5, the appellant was placed on notice that his leave record reflected the AWOL as occurring on January 6. His leave record was included in the agency's file. Copies of the agency's file were provided to the appellant. Additionally, the appellant affirmatively defended against a January 6 AWOL charge. His prehearing statement shows that he was aware of the nature of the AWOL incident in the charge, and defended against it. Further, he made no claim that the erroneous date and amount of the alleged AWOL adversely affected him. Additionally, we note that the appellant's shift generally began at 2300 each day, and thus spanned two dates. He was charged AWOL for the beginning of the shift that started on January 5 and ended on January 6. In view of the foregoing, we find that the erroneous date did not constitute harmful error.

Lewis v. USDA, 268 Fed. Appx. 952, 958-59 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP), ascertained no error in the agency's failure to specify precise dates of misconduct that occurred over a period of time, when the specifics of the misconduct were made known to the appellant:

Mr. Lewis contends that because of the lack of specificity in the first three specifications, he was denied a fair opportunity to respond to those charges. It was inherent in the nature of the charges in those specifications, however, that great specificity was not possible. Three of the four specifications alleged a pattern of conduct over time, rather than a particular incident. And the individual instances of inappropriate conduct, such as angry remarks or insulting words, were not the type as to which witnesses would ordinarily be expected to recall the precise date and surrounding circumstances. Nonetheless, while the agency was not able to provide the particular date and location for each incident of allegedly inappropriate conduct, the agency provided, with regard to each specification, the names of those employees who had complained about Mr. Lewis's conduct and the particular nature of his conduct. Moreover, a number of the witnesses prepared affidavits containing details regarding Mr. Lewis's inappropriate conduct, and he had copies of those affidavits prior to the hearing before the agency and before the administrative judge. Under these circumstances, we are satisfied that the agency's failure to provide additional details about the alleged misconduct did not constitute an unfair failure to provide discovery.

See Greenough v. Dept. of Army, 73 MSPR 648, 655 (1997) (date of an incident cited in the proposal was immaterial; the appellant did not question the date or indicate that any vagueness hampered his response to the notice). Nevertheless, there are limits. See Smith v. Dept. of Labor, 25 MSPR 102, 103 (1984) (when the proposal charged AWOL from March through April, the AJ correctly refused to consider AWOL from July through October).
