3. Transitional Assistance

Although agencies have an obligation as a result of a Presidential directive and internal agency policy to provide relocation or other assistance to employees whose careers are about to be displaced or terminated by RIF, violation of those generalized directives will not offer an affirmative defense to a RIF, Clark v. Dept. of Air Force (Fed. Cir. 1998 NP No. 98-3029):

Appellants also argue on appeal that the board failed to account for the agency's failure to convert them from their term positions to the newly funded permanent positions, as directed by Presidential Memorandum on Career Transition Assistance for Federal Employees (Sept. 12, 1995) ("I hereby direct the head of each executive department or agency...to the greatest extent practicable...to provide career transition assistance to the agency's surplus and displaced employees...includ[ing]...establishing policies that required the selection of a well-qualified surplus or displaced internal agency employee who applies for a vacant position in the commuting area, before selecting any other candidate from either within or outside the agency"), and by Federal Personnel Manual § 3-5(b) (Dec. 1989) ("Term employees hired from competitive registers may be noncompetitively converted to career-conditional appointments when they meet the requirements of 5 CFR 315.703 and come within reach for permanent appointment on an appropriate register during the period of their term appointment.")....

Appellants' arguments do not satisfy their burden on appeal. Neither reference to the Presidential Memorandum, nor the "to the greatest extent practicable" request found there establishes improper practices by the agency or legal error by the board sufficient to reverse the board's decision. Similarly, the Federal Personnel Manual is not applicable here because appellants term appointments did not confer competitive status. In fact, their appointment agreement states: "This appointment does not confer competitive status. You will not be eligible to compete for promotions to permanent positions under the merit promotion program or be considered for other position changes to permanent positions (reassignment/change to lower grade)."...

The Board lacks jurisdiction to remedy general shortcomings in an agency's placement programs, Carey v. MSPB, 768 F.2d 1338, 1339-40 (Fed. Cir. 1985):

Appellant argues in the abstract that "when the agency failed to implement an effective positive employment program, and failed to take any action...under this program or the Displaced Employee Program while hiring other persons into these positions, [appellant] was 'denied restoration rights.'..." No standing, however, is afforded by section 330.202 to appeal to the board from alleged deficiencies in the general operation of the programs challenged here. Carey should have brought before the board such a case or controversy as would have enabled it to evaluate a claim of a discernible injury to him, not a generalized grievance over matters of agency management.

While Carey has complained that CSA and HHS did not establish programs effective in promoting the rehiring of ex-employees of the CSA, his appeal did not with adequate particularity attribute to the alleged failure any specific injury suffered by him. In effect, Carey has asked the board to remedy an injury that he himself is unable or unwilling to detail. This, according to section 330.202, is a burden that he must carry if he is to invoke the power of the board to consider his appeal.

Carey advances only the proposition that logically he must have been injured by these omissions. The Board, however, is not empowered to take up appeals for the purpose of confirming a petitioner's belief in either logic or suspicion. This is not a basis for jurisdiction.... Here 5 CFR 330.202 authorizes an appeal on a showing of specified factual information.... That information must be explicit as to a detriment improperly imposed because of employment of another. Only thus can the inquiry of the board be reasonably focused. Further, the scope of jurisdiction...does not describe any right to appeal alleged deficiencies in the general operation of the displaced employee programs.

In short, while the objective to be sought under section 330.202 should be something akin to securing a new position for an employee, what Carey seeks instead in this appeal is the establishment of new or better personnel programs in HHS and OPM. Such an objective is not attainable through the board.
