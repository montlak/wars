broida mspb Chapter 14: Retirement and Restoration Rights Chapter 14: Retirement and Restoration Rights, Subchapter 01: Retirement, Section F: Survivor Annuities, Subsection 06: Consent of Spouse to Waiver of Survivor Annuity, Part b: Attempt to Modify Amount of Spousal Survivor Annuity
b. Attempt to Modify Amount of Spousal Survivor Annuity

Once an individual retires with an election for a survivor annuity, any change to the amount of that annuity, up or down, must be made within limited periods of time, subject to waiver, Thomas v. OPM, 72 MSPR 77, 79-81 (1996):

Based on the parties' written submissions, the administrative judge found that although the appellant's request to change her survivor annuity election was untimely because she did not make this request within the 30-day revocation period as required by 5 C.F.R. § 831.621, waiver of that regulatory deadline was required because OPM failed to provide her with adequate notice of her election rights and the corresponding deadlines....

...

On review, the appellant reiterates her contention that she timely requested to change her survivor annuity election within 18 months of her retirement as instructed by OPM. Id. The administrative judge found, however, that those instructions concern 5 C.F.R. § 831.622(b)(1), which provides an 18-month period to increase a reduction of an annuity to provide a survivor annuity for a spouse or to provide a survivor annuity where none had been elected at the time of retirement. Since the appellant had elected a reduced annuity with a partial survivor annuity for her spouse at the time of retirement, and now was attempting to decrease the annuity reduction to provide a lesser survivor annuity for her spouse, the administrative judge found that the 18-month filing period did not apply to her request. Id.; 5 C.F.R. § 831.622(b)(1).

...

As the administrative judge correctly noted, there are three potential bases for waiving a statutory or regulatory deadline: (1) The statute or regulation itself specifies circumstances for a waiver; (2) affirmative misconduct by the agency warrants equitable estoppel of the statutory or regulatory provision; or (3) the agency fails to provide notice of election rights and corresponding deadlines, if such notice is required by statute or regulation. Holtermann v. Office of Personnel Management, 57 M.S.P.R. 380, 384 (1993); Speker v. Office of Personnel Management, 45 M.S.P.R. 380, 385 (1990), aff'd, 928 F.2d 410 (Fed. Cir.) (Table), modified, Fox v. Office of Personnel Management, 50 M.S.P.R. 602, 606 n.4 (1991).

The administrative judge found that OPM was required to waive the 30-day regulatory deadline under the third basis--i.e., inadequate notice of election rights from OPM--because OPM never informed the appellant that she had 30 days from the date of her first regular monthly payment to change her annuity election as it was required to do under 5 U.S.C. § 8339(j). On petition for review, OPM contends that the administrative judge erred in finding that it failed to inform the appellant of the 30-day deadline, and that, in any event, any such failure would not require waiver of the regulatory deadline because the notification requirement of 5 U.S.C. § 8339 does not apply to the right to change an election within 30 days of receiving the initial payment under 5 C.F.R. § 831.622(a). We agree with both of OPM's contentions.

As OPM argues, the administrative judge's waiver determination is inconsistent with applicable legal precedent. In Belanger v. Office of Personnel Management, 1 F.3d 1223, 1229 (Fed. Cir. 1993), the court held that "the right to change an election within thirty days of receiving the initial payment is not a 'right of election' within the notification requirement of 5 U.S.C. § 8339." Therefore, the court held, OPM's failure to notify an annuitant of the 30-day deadline for changing an election under 5 C.F.R. § 831.622(a) did not establish a basis for waiving the 30-day regulatory deadline. Belanger, 1 F.3d at 1228-30. We find that the court's decision in Belanger is dispositive here, and that the administrative judge thus erred in applying the notification requirement of 5 U.S.C. § 8339(j) to the election rights set forth at 5 C.F.R. § 831.622(a).

We also agree with OPM that the administrative judge erred in finding that OPM did not inform the appellant that she had 30 days after the first regular monthly payment to request a change of her annuity election. As OPM asserts, the instructions on the retirement application form, which the appellant completed and signed in May 1994, state: "[C]onsider your election carefully. No change will be permitted after your annuity is granted except as explained in the instructions." In Belanger, the court found that instructions identical to the ones here constituted adequate notice of the permanency of the annuitant's elections. Belanger, 1 F.3d at 1230. We therefore reach the same conclusion here. Id.

Accordingly, we find that the administrative judge erred in finding that OPM was required to waive the 30-day regulatory deadline for changing an election under 5 C.F.R. § 831.622(a) under the third basis for waiver. We further find that the appellant has not established any other basis for waiver of the regulatory deadline. The first basis for waiver is inapplicable here because the statute and the implementing OPM regulations do not specifically provide for waiver. See generally 5 U.S.C. § 8339(j); 5 C.F.R. §§ 831.621, 831.622. Nor has the appellant shown that a waiver is warranted on the basis of affirmative misconduct by OPM. Although the appellant claims that she was misled by OPM's "erroneous" instructions concerning 5 C.F.R. § 831.622(b)(1), she has not identified any error in those instructions, as discussed above, and we find that her reliance on such instructions in filing her request to decrease her spouse's survivor annuity was not reasonable. Cf. Belanger, 1 F.3d at 1228 (annuitant failed to make a threshold showing of reasonable reliance on OPM's correspondence containing alleged misinformation where OPM's correspondence at the very least provided the annuitant with ample reason to seek verification of his understanding of the effect of his divorce decree on a survivor annuity for his former wife).

Nunes v. OPM, 111 MSPR 221, 227 ¶ 14, 2009 MSPB 73 (2009), noted:

[U]nder 5 U.S.C. § 8339(o), an annuitant who was married at the time of retirement has an eighteen-month window after his retirement during which to make an election to reduce his monthly annuity payments for the purpose of providing his spouse with a survivor annuity, or to increase his monthly annuity reduction to provide a greater survivor annuity for his spouse, and OPM has an obligation to inform annuitants of this right of election on an annual basis.

In Nunes, the Board remanded the appeal to the AJ to determine if the 18-month period was subject to waiver by reason of the appellant's failure to receive OPM's notice concerning the election opportunity from OPM.

Because only an employee or retiree can make the election for an annuity, no election to increase a survivor annuity (to full survivor benefits from an election for partial benefits) can occur after the employee or retiree dies. Pruden v. OPM, 68 MSPR 681, 683-84 (1995) (citing Diehl v. OPM, 50 MSPR 519, 522 (1991)). But, the Board has remanded a case to determine if the lack of a proper notice of a survivor annuity election option influenced an employee to select a lower survivor annuity than was his intent. Rollins v. OPM, 112 MSPR 557, 562 ¶ 12, 2009 MSPB 212 (2009) (citing Jordan v. OPM, 100 MSPR 623, 628-29 ¶ 13 (2005), following remand, Rollins v. OPM, 113 MSPR 542, 2010 MSPB 77 (2010) (granting upward adjustment in survivor annuity); see McDonald v. OPM, 115 MSPR 236, 240-01 ¶ 12, 2010 MSPB 228 (2010) (if OPM did not send out the required notice informing the annuitant of his opportunity to add a survivor annuity, on remand the AJ would have to consider whether--but for affirmative misrepresentations from the employer concerning a choice of a survivor annuity relative to eligibility for disability retirement, to be considered under the doctrine of equitable estoppel--the appellant's husband would have elected a survivor annuity for her). McDonald was overruled by Cerilli v. OPM, 119 MSPR 404, 410 ¶ 10, 2013 MSPB 31 (2013), as to proof of notice, OPM need not supply a mailing list; an OPM affidavit was sufficient as to the nature of a mailing. Cerilli relied on Schoemakers v. OPM, 180 F.3d 1377 (Fed. Cir. 1999).
