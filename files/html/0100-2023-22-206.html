b. Requirements of Addendum Initial Decision

A decision on a fee motion is usually referred to as an addendum initial decision. The minimum requirements for the addendum initial decision on fees are that, as to each of the elements for granting or denying the fee motion, the decision must identify all material issues of fact; disclose the evidentiary basis for the factual findings; clearly set out those findings; and, as necessary and possible from the written record, explain how credibility issues were resolved. Morrissey v. Dept. of Navy, 38 MSPR 508, 512 (1988).

When the Board rules on a counsel fee motion, it should be evident from its decision what documents or evidence it is relying on. Bruning v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 87-3005). An AJ who awards counsel fees must provide findings of fact showing that the award is in the interest of justice. Sabo v. USPS, 1 MSPR 547, 547, 1 MSPB 526 (1980). The AJ who awards fees for a smaller number of hours than claimed is required to identify the hourly charges he finds excessive and to explain why and by what amount they are reduced. Tri v. SEC, 33 MSPR 1, 4 (1987) (citing Fagan v. Dept. of Navy, 29 MSPR 64, 65 (1985)); see Crumbaker v. MSPB, 781 F.2d 191 (Fed. Cir. 1986) [discussed earlier in this chapter, "Reasonableness of Time Invested."] ; Kahalekulu v. Dept. of Army, 46 MSPR 555, 559 (1991) ("The administrative judge failed to identify and discuss all of the hours which he believed were compensable in the addendum initial decision on attorney fees, thus leading to the confusion in the agency's petition for review regarding the discrepancy between the hours specifically allowed and the total award"; reductions still found appropriate); cf. Powell v. Dept. of Treasury, 3 MSPR 89, 90 (1980) (order granting fees vacated and remanded for lack of analysis of statutory and regulatory requirements); Manning v. DOJ, 3 MSPR 87, 88-89 (1980) (fee award was vacated, remanded; AJ granted fees on the oral request of counsel at the close of the hearing; the award contained no mention of a specific fee or reasons for the amount of time spent by counsel).

If the AJ does not articulate the reasoning supporting a fee award, the Board may, upon a petition to review the addendum decision, remand the case to the AJ for issuance of a proper decision. Rose v. Dept. of Navy, 34 MSPR 119, 122 (1987); Leth v. GSA, 5 MSPR 508, 508, 5 MSPB 503 (1981); see Swanson v. DLA, 35 MSPR 115, 119 (1987) (failure of counsel to demonstrate support for his hourly rate). The Board itself may analyze fee entitlement if deductions by the AJ from claimed fees are unclear or insufficiently detailed. See Capehart v. USPS, 83 MSPR 385, 389 ¶ 6 (1999) (remand not necessary following a determination of entitlement when the charges were unchallenged by the agency and appeared reasonable); Buckler v. Fed. Retirement Thrift Inv. Bd., 78 MSPR 198, 200 (1998) (awarding fees for consultations when the record was clear and remand was unnecessary); McGovern v. EEOC, 42 MSPR 399, 413-14 (1989) (analyzing fee entitlement, granting some fees, denying some, and noting as to a component of the fees: "[b]ecause the appellant here did not timely submit any documentation to support an award of fees for any work whatsoever with regard to the motion for fees itself, he has not met his burden, and the administrative judge should not have done it for him"); Hayes v. Dept. of Air Force, 14 MSPR 523, 527 (1983) (failure of addendum decision to provide findings on the reasonableness of fees was not a bar to Board's evaluation "if a review of the record indicates that 'the parties have had adequate opportunity to develop their respective positions with regard to this issue'"). If the appellant supplements the record on fees at the level of a PFR from the addendum decision, and that supplementation is necessary because the AJ did not bring to the appellant's attention perceived deficiencies that the appellant could have addressed before the addendum initial decision was issued, the Board may decide the disputes over the amounts of fees if the record, supplemented on PFR, is sufficient, and avoid a remand. See, e.g., Hart v. Dept. of Transp., 115 MSPR 10, 2010 MSPB 183 (2010) (partial fees awarded by AJ, whose analysis was subject to close scrutiny and multiple revisions by the Board).
