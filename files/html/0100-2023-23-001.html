CHAPTER 16

SETTLEMENT, REMEDIES, AND ENFORCEMENT

I. SETTLEMENT

As they said at the top, "[i]n labor disputes, as in other kinds of litigation, even a bad settlement may be more advantageous in the long run than a good lawsuit." Air Line Pilots Ass'n, Int'l v. O'Neil, 111 S. Ct. 1127, 1137 (1991). When Arthur Goldberg was asked by President Kennedy how a threatened labor strike was averted, the Labor Secretary said, "the trick is to be there when it's settled." (For readers below a certain age, Goldberg was also an Associate Justice of the Supreme Court, and later Ambassador to the United Nations.) If you've reached this far in your case, you're nearing the end.

Said the Board in McCarter v. Dept. of Navy, 114 MSPR 599, 602 ¶ 6, 2010 MSPB 181 (2010):

As a matter of policy, the Board favors the settlement of actions between an agency and its employees. See Social Security Administration v. Givens, 27 M.S.P.R. 360, 362 (1985). Settlement of disputes serves to avoid unnecessary litigation and to encourage fair and speedy resolution of issues. See Roberson v. Department of the Air Force, 27 M.S.P.R. 11 (1985).

But settlement efforts are encouraged, not required, Martin v. GSA, SF-0752-13-0050-B-2 (NP 5/3/2016):

...Although the Board "strongly encourages" settlement, discussion of possible options is not required if either party concludes in good faith that no compromise of any kind is possible. The appellant has shown no support for her claim that agencies in all cases must be required to engage in settlement discussions, and we are aware of no such support.

Fully as many Board appeals are resolved through settlement as are decided through merits and jurisdictional determinations. Cases settle at the regional offices at the rate of at least 50%. Additional settlements occur through the efforts of the Office of Appeals Counsel when it receives petitions for review. The study Clean Record Settlement Agreements and the Law (MSPB Office of Policy and Evaluation 2013), reports that for the period of FY 2009-2012, 65-68% of adverse action appeals settled. Although the quality of settlements varies, Board practice would suffer considerably greater delays in adjudication if the many cases that now settle were pursued through the full course of litigation. Certain problems occur with some frequency as a result of settlements. The Board cannot enforce a settlement from a case not within its jurisdiction. Some settlements are made that are not offered to the Board for later enforcement. Appellants may believe that they have been tricked or forced into settlements and then seek to withdraw from them and reinstitute Board litigation. Some settlements are breached. The parties must then determine whether to remedy the breach or rescind the breached agreement. The decisions and discussion that follow trace the parameters of the law governing creation and vacation of settlements of MSPB appeals. Also considered in this chapter are regulations and decisions that describe the Board's enforcement authority--the power of the Board to require parties to comply with the relief directed by the Board on successful prosecution of an appeal and the power of the Board to require parties to comply with the obligations they undertake in cases they settle through an agreement accepted into the Board's record for purposes of enforcement.
