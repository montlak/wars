2. Character

"Evidence concerning character, particularly regarding a person's veracity, is a factor to consider in assessing a witness's credibility." Smith v. VA, 93 MSPR 424, 429 ¶ 18 (2003). The use of character evidence is described by the Federal Rules of Evidence:

Rule 404. Character Evidence Not Admissible To Prove Conduct; Exceptions; Other Crimes

(a) Character evidence generally

Evidence of a person's character or a trait of character is not admissible for the purpose of proving action in conformity therewith on a particular occasion, except:

(1) Character of accused--In a criminal case, evidence of a pertinent trait of character offered by an accused, or by the prosecution to rebut the same, or if evidence of a trait of character of the alleged victim of the crime is offered by an accused and admitted under Rule 404 (a)(2), evidence of the same trait of character of the accused offered by the prosecution;

(2) Character of alleged victim--In a criminal case, and subject to the limitations imposed by Rule 412, evidence of a pertinent trait of character of the alleged victim of the crime offered by an accused, or by the prosecution to rebut the same, or evidence of a character trait of peacefulness of the alleged victim offered by the prosecution in a homicide case to rebut evidence that the alleged victim was the first aggressor;

(3) Character of witness--Evidence of the character of a witness, as provided in rules 607, 608, and 609.

(b) Other crimes, wrongs, or acts

Evidence of other crimes, wrongs, or acts is not admissible to prove the character of a person in order to show action in conformity therewith. It may, however, be admissible for other purposes, such as proof of motive, opportunity, intent, preparation, plan, knowledge, identity, or absence of mistake or accident, provided that upon request by the accused, the prosecution in a criminal case shall provide reasonable notice in advance of trial, or during trial if the court excuses pretrial notice on good cause shown, of the general nature of any such evidence it intends to introduce at trial.

The Federal Rules of Evidence provide guidance on collateral evidence of reputation, opinion, and prior bad acts:

Rule 405. Methods of Proving Character

(a) Reputation or opinion

In all cases in which evidence of character or a trait of character of a person is admissible, proof may be made by testimony as to reputation or by testimony in the form of an opinion. On cross-examination, inquiry is allowable into relevant specific instances of conduct.

(b) Specific instances of conduct

In cases in which character or a trait of character of a person is an essential element of a charge, claim, or defense, proof may also be made of specific instances of that person's conduct.

Rule 608: Evidence of Character and Conduct of Witness

(a) Opinion and reputation evidence of character

The credibility of a witness may be attacked or supported by evidence in the form of opinion or reputation, but subject to these limitations: (1) the evidence may refer only to character for truthfulness or untruthfulness, and (2) evidence of truthful character is admissible only after the character of the witness for truthfulness has been attacked by opinion or reputation evidence or otherwise.

(b) Specific instances of conduct

Specific instances of conduct of a witness, for the purpose of attacking or supporting the witness' credibility, other than conviction of a crime as provided in rule 609, may not be proved by extrinsic evidence. They may, however, in the discretion of the court, if probative of truthfulness or untruthfulness, be inquired into on cross-examination of the witness (1) concerning the witness' character for truthfulness or untruthfulness, or (2) concerning the character for truthfulness or untruthfulness of another witness as to which character the witness being cross-examined has testified.

The giving of testimony, whether by an accused or by any other witness, does not operate as a waiver of the accused's privilege against self-incrimination when examined with respect to matters which relate only to credibility.

The Board states that it will accept character evidence, Hillen v. Dept. of Army, 35 MSPR 453, 459 (1987) ("Character evidence may be used for impeachment of a witness on the theory that certain characteristics render that person more prone to testify untruthfully.... This form of impeaching evidence may be established by prior misconduct or reputation...."), but not many cases explain when or how it will be received. [Refer to Chapter 5, "Required Analysis," concerning the analysis required in an initial decision.] Christensen v. USPS, 51 MSPR 681, 688 (1991), following Hillen, declared that "character evidence may be used for impeachment of a witness on the theory that certain characteristics render that person more prone to testify untruthfully, and that such impeaching evidence may be established by prior misconduct or reputation."

Fouquet v. USDA, 82 MSPR 548, 563 ¶ 40 (1999), explained the admissibility of extrinsic evidence pertaining to the truthfulness of a witness:

...Character evidence can be relevant if the proffered evidence has some direct relevancy to the charges. Under the Federal Rules of Evidence, the credibility of a witness may be impeached with evidence of a prior felony conviction of that witness. See Rule 609(a)(1), Fed. R. Evid.; Green v. Bock Laundry Machine Company, 490 U.S. 504, 505-507, 109 S. Ct. 1981, 1983, 104 L. Ed.2d 557 (1989). Moreover, extrinsic proof of prior misconduct pertaining to a witness's capacity to tell the truth may be presented to impeach the credibility of that witness. Hawkins v. Smithsonian Institution, 73 MSPR 397, 403 (1997), citing Carrick v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 MSPR 280, 283, aff'd, 69 F.3d 555, 1995 WL 628823 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table).

Character evidence is typically offered to demonstrate that the appellant is a good person and would not, therefore, commit the charged offense. Of character evidence, and its close relative, evidence of habit, subsequently discussed, Remy v. VA, 8 MSPR 141, 147-48, 7 MSPB 624 (1981), observed:

The Federal Rules of Evidence permit evidence of an accused's character to be offered by the accused as an exception to the general rule prohibiting use of character evidence to prove or disprove a particular act. Fed. R. Evid. 404. Evidence of habit is also clearly permitted. Fed. R. Evid. 406. Character and habit are closely akin. Character is a generalized description of one's disposition, or of one's disposition in respect to a general trait, such as honesty, temperance or peacefulness. "Habit" in modern usage, both lay and psychological, is more specific. It describes one's regular response to a repeated specific situation. Agreement is general that habit evidence may be highly persuasive as proof of conduct on a particular occasion. McCormick, Evidence 340-41 (2d ed. 1972)....

The testimony of witnesses that the appellant has a general reputation for honesty and integrity is circumstantial evidence in his favor. See Haebe v. DOJ, 81 MSPR 167, 176 ¶ 18 (1999) (involving evidence that was overcome by circumstantial evidence of false statements), rev'd on other grounds, Haebe v. DOJ, 288 F.3d 1288 (Fed. Cir. 2002); Rudnick v. GSA, 11 MSPR 247, 249 (1982) (character evidence is admissible to determine the appellant's habits in completing time sheets and his intent relative to the charge of falsifying overtime reports). If an appellant offers testimony as to his honesty and integrity, he places his character in evidence and subjects himself to examination and rebuttal evidence concerning his own truthfulness. Ott v. Dept. of Army, 14 MSPR 642, 644 (1983) (appellant was charged with making an unauthorized financial commitment to a volunteer instructor); see Remy v. VA, 8 MSPR 141, 148, 7 MSPB 624 (1981) (considering evidence that the appellant did not use obscene language).

Character evidence from an agency is not relevant unless the appellant puts his character into issue during the hearing or unless the evidence is directly relevant to the charges. Jackson v. VA, 21 MSPR 63, 65 (1984), a sexual harassment case, declared:

With regard to the hearing, the agency claims the presiding official improperly excluded the testimony of three agency witnesses, which was offered to show past conduct of the appellant similar to the acts with which he was charged, and to show bad character after appellant presented evidence to show his good character. The agency claims that the excluded testimony would have shown "false" testimony of the appellant, and that the presiding official, therefore, did not have before her all the relevant evidence. The appellant, in his reply to the agency petition, asserts that the statements given both in writing and in testimony were to specifically show that the reasons given by the agency in its letter of proposed removal were not true because other people were present on these occasions, and their testimony directly contradicted the testimony of agency witnesses.

Based upon review of the hearing transcript, we find that the testimony elicited from appellant's witnesses, as well as the affidavits submitted with his reply to the proposal notice, were directly limited to the reasons and instances for which he was charged. Contrary to the agency contention, appellant's character was not a matter introduced or presented by him at the hearing, although the list of appellant's witnesses' expected testimony mentioned his integrity, and his behavior toward females. Thus the presiding official appropriately excluded this testimony as not being relevant. See 5 CFR § 1201.62. Moreover, if the failure of the presiding official to allow this testimony were to constitute error, it would not be reversible error under the circumstances, since it did not specifically pertain to the five specifications of alleged sexual harassment. In our view, the agency has not made a sufficient showing of harm or prejudice in this contention. See Karapinka v. Department of Energy, 6 MSPB 114 (1981).

Character evidence can be offered by the appellant during his case, by other witnesses, or through cross-examination of agency witnesses. To be relevant, the evidence must have a bearing on the charges: character evidence would have no relevance in the typical unacceptable performance case, whereas it would be relevant in a theft case. The evidence, to be persuasive, should come from individuals in a position to know the appellant well who can provide a fairly objective opinion based on that knowledge. Placing the appellant's mother on the stand will not help much, but testimony from a minister, public official, community leader, coworkers, or neighbors may be beneficial.

Although character evidence may be admissible, it may not be persuasive. The appellant in Jack v. DOD, 29 MSPR 231 (1985), complained that the AJ disallowed his character witnesses. He was removed for attendance problems, use of government facilities for repairing personal items, and conversion of donated property for his own use. Regarding the exclusion of the character witnesses, the Board said in Jack, 29 MSPR at 232 n.* that, even assuming these witnesses would have testified that to their knowledge the appellant's character and reputation for integrity were good, "we would not find such testimony sufficient to overcome the evidence specifically related to the charges at issue."
