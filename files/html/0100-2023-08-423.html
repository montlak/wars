d. Standing; "Interested Person"

A broad view of standing to raise a regulatory challenge was articulated in Senior Executives Ass'n v. OPM, 67 MSPR 643, 646-48 (1995):

The Senior Executives Association (SEA) has petitioned the Board under 5 USC 1204(a)(4) and (f) (Supp. V 1993), to review the validity of three regulations: 5 CFR 317.901(c)(4) and (5) (1993), which were promulgated to implement 5 USC 3395(e)(1) and (3), and 5 CFR 610.408 (1993), which was promulgated to implement 5 USC 6122-26. The SEA requests that the Board declare the regulations invalid, order any agency affected by the invalidation of the regulations to cease compliance with and implementation of the regulations and order corrective action necessary to assure compliance with the order. Section 317.901(c)(4) concerns detailing career Senior Executive Service (SES) members during the moratorium on involuntary reassignments in the 120-day period following the appointment of a new agency head or a noncareer supervisor. Section 317.901(c)(5) gives agencies discretion to impose a 120-day moratorium when an individual is detailed to the position of head of an agency or noncareer supervisor. Section 610.408 bars SES members from accumulating credit hours under an alternative work schedule.

Upon the filing of a petition by an interested person, the Board may in its discretion review any provision of a rule or regulation issued by the Office of Personnel Management (OPM or agency), 5 USC 1204(f)(1)(B) (Supp. V 1993), and declare the provision invalid on its face if it would require any employee to commit a prohibited personnel practice, or invalidly implemented if it has required any employee to commit a prohibited personnel practice. 5 USC 1204(f)(2) (Supp. V 1993). The Director of OPM is entitled to participate in the review. 5 USC 1204(f)(3) (Supp. V 1993).

In response to the SEA's petition for review, OPM has raised the threshold issue of the standing of the SEA to request regulation review. Specifically, OPM contends that SEA is not an "interested person" because it is a private organization concerned with issues of career federal executives and not a statutorily recognized exclusive representative of its members. OPM also argues that while the SEA has the authority to comment on regulations in the rulemaking process, it is not an "interested person" with respect to a request for regulation review.

The term "interested person" has not been defined in the statute, 5 USC 1204(f)(1)(B) (Supp. V 1993), or in the relevant regulations, 5 CFR 1203.1-1203.22. There is no requirement that an "interested person" be a statutorily recognized exclusive representative of employees. While the Board has received requests for the review of regulations from unions, see, e.g., NTEU v. OPM, 45 MSPR 432 (1990); Nat'l Council of Field Assessment Locals v. DHHS, SSA, 31 MSPR 590 (1986), it has also received requests from individuals, see, e.g., Larson v. OPM, 29 MSPR 317 (1985); Johnson v. United States Customs Service, Dept. of the Treasury, 31 MSPR 104 (1986), several employees, see, e.g., Brooks v. OPM, 59 MSPR 207 (1993), and a committee of employees, see, e.g., Office of Legislative and Pub. Affairs Legal Defense Comm. v. Bell, 10 MSPB 686, 12 MSPR 143 (1982). In none of these cases did we find that the requester was not an "interested person" and therefore lacked standing to request review of a rule or regulation.

The only test for "interested person" found in the Board's case law is that an "interested person" must establish that the regulation in question is applicable to him or her. Joseph v. MSPB, 10 MSPB 210, 11 MSPR 242, 243 (1982). The SEA has submitted a declaration executed by its current president under penalty of perjury that it is a professional association incorporated in 1980 with 2,979 members from all cabinet-level departments and 44 administrative and independent agencies, commissions and corporations. The declaration states that the SEA represents the interests of career SES employees of the federal government. The regulations in question are applicable to career SES appointees in the federal government. The 2,979 members of SEA are SES appointees. Accordingly, we find that the SEA is an "interested person" in this case and can petition for review of 5 CFR 317.901(c)(4) and (5) and 610.408.

A union may petition for regulation review as an "interested person" under 5 USC 1204(f)(1)(B) based on its representational status. See, e.g., NTEU v. OPM, 76 MSPR 244, 247 (1997). Although the Board reviewed an OPM FPM provision on its own motion, In re Exceptions from Competitive Merit Plans, 9 MSPR 116, 117 (1981), review is ordinarily undertaken through a private party's request. The party seeking review should, however, establish that he is an "interested person," i.e., he must show how the regulation he seeks to challenge was applied to him. See Joseph v. MSPB, 11 MSPR 242, 243 (1982).
