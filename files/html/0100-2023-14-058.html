a. Deference to Higher Level Management Assessment

The Board does not specifically state who really is in the best position to determine potential for rehabilitation, but some guidance was offered in Rezza v. DOJ, 35 MSPR 40, 44(1987). Appellant, an FBI Special Agent, was removed for misappropriation of government funds. The deciding official, apparently located in Washington, felt that appellant did not possess the potential for rehabilitation. Appellant's immediate supervisor and coworkers were confident in his continued ability to perform as a Special Agent. Appellant offered evidence, apparently from a clinical psychologist, that he would not relapse into the compulsive gambling that seemed to lead to his troubles. The Board was not persuaded:

The evidence that the appellant's immediate supervisors and coworkers remained confident in the appellant's continued performance as a Special Agent does not outweigh the judgment of Mr. Glover...an official at agency headquarters [with] ...a broader view of the effect of the appellant's conduct on the agency as a whole than the appellant's supervisor and coworkers in Philadelphia.

For a similar result leading to a determination by the Board to place greater emphasis on the judgment of a second--rather than first--line supervisor, refer to Williams v. Executive Office of President, 54 MSPR 196, 200 (1992). Slaughter v. USDA, 56 MSPR 349, 353 (1993), provided a more extensive analysis of why it would place primary deference in a higher-level agency official's judgment on a penalty:

In concluding that the penalty of removal was unreasonable, the administrative judge found that the failure of the deciding official, E.T. Leonard, to discuss the case with the appellant's supervisors led to a penalty that exceeded the bounds of reasonableness. She also stated that the appellant's second-level supervisor, Thomas Barlow, had testified, after being informed of the appellant's prior disciplinary record, that he probably would take the appellant back. The agency argues that the administrative judge erred in weighing the testimony of these officials.

We agree with the agency. Although Leonard acknowledged that he did not speak to the appellant's supervisors before making the decision to remove the appellant, he testified that he reviewed the entire case before making his decision. In addition, he testified at the hearing, with knowledge of the mitigating factors, that he considered removal appropriate. Furthermore, we agree with the agency that, as a national personnel official, Leonard had a broader view than Barlow of the effect of the appellant's misconduct on the agency as a whole. Leonard dealt with conduct matters on a regular basis, unlike Barlow, and he therefore was better able to assess the ramifications of imposing a penalty less severe than removal. Our review of the evidence reveals no reason why his determination that removal was appropriate should have been discounted.[10]

[10] In Rezza v. Dept. of Justice, 35 MSPR 40, 44 (1987), the Board found that a national personnel officer's view of an incident of misconduct outweighed the views of the appellant's supervisor and coworkers. The administrative judge distinguished the present case from Rezza by finding that Leonard viewed the case in a vacuum without knowledge of all the facts. As noted above, however, Leonard reviewed the entire case before making his decision. Moreover, when he testified at the hearing that removal was appropriate, he did so with knowledge of the mitigating factors.

We note further that, in testifying that he "probably [would] not" have removed the appellant, Barlow indicated that he was referring only to the penalty he would have imposed based on the current charges, and he admitted he was not "privy to all [Leonard's] background knowledge." He also acknowledged that he lacked direct knowledge of the appellant, since he only saw him ten to twelve times a year and his assessment of the appellant's performance was based on second-hand information. We agree with the agency that Barlow's entire testimony does not offer persuasive support for mitigation of the penalty.

Batara v. Dept. of Navy, 123 MSPR 278, 282-83 ¶ 7, 2016 MSPB 15 (2016), discounted the opinion of lower level officials concerning the appellant's ability to perform despite acknowledged misconduct:

The Board has held that the penalty judgment belongs to the agency, not to an appellant's supervisor, and that, in the absence of an agency's failure to consider the relevant Douglas factors adequately, a supervisor's opinions are insufficient to overcome the agency's judgment concerning the seriousness of the misconduct and the appropriateness of the agency-imposed penalty. Gebhardt v. Department of the Air Force, 99 M.S.P.R. 49, ¶¶ 19-20 (2005); Edwards v. Department of the Army, 87 M.S.P.R. 27, ¶ 9 (2009), aff'd sub nom. Rodriguez v. Department of the Army, 25 Fed. Appx. 848 (Fed. Cir. 2011). Because the record reflects that the deciding official weighed the relevant factors in arriving at the removal penalty, the opinions of the three agency officials were insufficient to overcome the agency's judgment, which was made and explained by the deciding official. Therefore, we find that the administrative judge erred in failing to defer to the agency's penalty determination. See Saiz, 122 M.S.P.R. 521, ¶ 5; Balouris, 107 M.S.P.R. 574, ¶ 6; Batten, 101 M.S.P.R. 222, ¶ 11.

See Clark v. DHS, DC-0752-13-0661-I-1 (NP 2/21/2023) ("Specifically, she argues that the deciding official's loss of trust and confidence is irrelevant because the deciding official was not in her chain of command. The Board has held, however, that the penalty judgment belongs to the agency, not to an appellant's supervisor, and that, in the absence of an agency's failure to consider the relevant Douglas factors adequately, a supervisor's opinions are insufficient to overcome the agency's judgment concerning the appropriateness of the agency-imposed penalty. Batara v. Department of the Navy, 123 M.S.P.R. 278, ¶ 7 (2016); see Gebhardt v. Department of the Air Force, 99 M.S.P.R. 49, ¶¶ 19-21 (200[5]).").

Even management's endorsement of rehabilitative potential may not satisfy the Board, as in VA v. Chiappetta, CB-7521-16-0014-N-1 (NP 12/2/2022):

In his initial decision, the ALJ briefly acknowledged that the respondent was unlikely to commit a similar offense in the future. By contrast, the respondent's petition for review places great emphasis on his potential for rehabilitation. The respondent's rehabilitative potential is not the only factor for the Board to consider in arriving at its penalty determination. Social Security Administration v. Davis, 19 M.S.P.R. 279, 283, aff'd, 758 F.2d 661 (Fed. Cir. 1984). It is, however, an important factor, and we find that rehabilitative potential in this case is present. During agency proceedings, the respondent expressed remorse and acknowledged that he should have handled things differently. See Williams v. Government Printing Office, 7 M.S.P.R. 183, 185 (1981) (finding that the appellant's numerous apologies, among other things, augured well for his rehabilitative potential). Furthermore, the Deputy Secretary opined that the respondent would be very unlikely to repeat the same misconduct in the future. See Wentz v. U.S. Postal Service, 91 M.S.P.R. 176, ¶ 24 (2002). Nevertheless, we find that the evidence of rehabilitative potential is tempered by the respondent's continued efforts to deflect the blame to other FOH members and his apparent failure to recognize that his own participation, however tacit, fostered the conditions for the group's existence. See Betz v. General Services Administration, 55 M.S.P.R. 424, 428, 431 (1992).

See Vernon v. USPS, 87 MSPR 392, 398 ¶ 13 (2000) ("[J]udgment about trust and confidence in an employee rests not with the individual supervisor but with the agency.").
