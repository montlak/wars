4. Tacking of Prior Service: Competitive Service Probation

We move to whether service immediately before initiation of a competitive service (one year, except two years for certain DOD personnel before the end of 2022) probationary period counts toward completion of the probationary period. Consideration commences with the OPM regulation providing government-wide guidance on tacking of prior service for the competitive service probationer, 5 CFR 315.802 (2022):

Length of probationary period; crediting service.

(a) The probationary period required by § 315.801 is 1 year and may not be extended.

(b) Prior Federal civilian service (including nonappropriated fund service) counts toward completion of probation when the prior service:

(1) Is in the same agency, e.g., Department of the Army;

(2) Is in the same line of work (determined by the employee's actual duties and responsibilities); and

(3) Contains or is followed by no more than a single break in service that does not exceed 30 calendar days.

(c) Periods of absence while in a pay status count toward completion of probation. Absence in nonpay status while on the rolls (other than for compensable injury or military duty) is creditable up to a total of 22 workdays. Absence (whether on or off the rolls) due to compensable injury or military duty is creditable in full upon restoration to Federal service. Nonpay time in excess of 22 workdays extends the probationary period by an equal amount. An employee serving probation who leaves Federal service to become a volunteer with the Peace Corps or the Corporation for National and Community Service serves the remainder of the probationary period upon reinstatement provided the employee is reinstated within 90 days of termination of service as a volunteer or training for such service.

(d) The probationary period for part-time employees is computed on the basis of calendar time, in the same manner as for full-time employees. For intermittent employees, i.e., those who do not have regularly scheduled tours of duty, each day or part of a day in pay status counts as 1 day of credit toward the 260 days in a pay status required for completion of probation. (However, the probationary period cannot be completed in less than 1 year of calendar time.)

The question of whether probation was completed differs from whether an individual is an "employee" under 5 USC 7511 based on continuous (no break in service) prior employment in other than temporary positions of less than one year. Noting the distinctions in the calculus, Ney v. Dept. of Commerce, 115 MSPR 204, 209-10 ¶ 14, 2010 MSPB 219 (2010), explained:

An appellant who has not served a full year under her appointment can show that she has completed the probationary period, and so is no longer a probationer, by tacking on prior service if: (1) the prior service was rendered immediately preceding the probationary appointment; (2) it was performed in the same agency; (3) it was performed in the same line of work; and (4) it was completed with no more than one break in service of less than 30 days. Hurston, 113 M.S.P.R. 34, ¶ 9. Alternatively, an employee can show that, while she may be a probationer, she is an "employee" with Chapter 75 appeal rights because, immediately preceding the adverse action, she had completed at least 1 year of current continuous service in the competitive service without a break in federal civilian employment of a workday. Id. Given the appellant's prior federal service, the administrative judge erred in failing to provide an appropriate Burgess notice informing the appellant how she could establish that she was an employee with adverse action appeal rights to the Board. See generally Smart v. Department of Justice, 113 M.S.P.R. 393, ¶¶ 12-15 (2010); see also McCormick v. Department of the Air Force, 307 F.3d 1339, 1341-43 (Fed. Cir. 2002).

See Simons v. USDA, DC-0752-15-1179-I-2 (NP 10/21/2022) (restating the alternative approach).

Higgins v. Dept. of Army, SF-315H-14-0609-I-1 (NP 10/10/2014), explained the difference in approach:

The appellant here served only 11 weeks of his 1-year probationary period. His prior federal civilian service was with Department of the Air Force, not in the same agency from which he was terminated. Thus, his prior federal service does not count towards the completion of his probationary period. Similarly, because his last period of prior federal employment ended on February 17, 2014, he had a break in service of more than a workday between the end of his employment with Department of the Air Force and his appointment to the position in question and, therefore, he does not have 1 year of current, continuous service. Consequently, the appellant does not qualify as an "employee" under either 5 U.S.C. §§ 7511(a)(1)(A)(i) or (A)(ii).

[Refer earlier in this chapter to "Break in Service."]

For the probationer, prior service tacks for purposes of determining the completion of probation if it in the same agency, if it is in the same line of work, and if there is no more than a 30-day break in service between the two jobs. There is no exception for prior work in a temporary appointment. See Barnett v. MSPB, 341 Fed. Appx. 639, 641-42 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP) (separation of about five months between two probationary appointments prevented the second probationary period from being completed through tacking for the earlier service and it also precluded the second appointment from vesting an appeal right through more than a year of current continuous service). Under 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii), to obtain an appeal right the competitive service appellant needs to have a completed a year of current continuous service under other than a temporary appointment limited to a year or less. A person with past employment that continues into a probationary appointment may be terminated before the end of probation and may end up with an appeal right based on the total current service being more than one year. The job before probation and the job during probation need not be the same, as long as the earlier position is not a temporary appointment limited to a year or less. See Smart v. DOJ, 111 MSPR 147, 150-51 ¶ 9, 2009 MSPB 61 (2009) (competitive service; explaining that an employee may not have sufficient prior service to provide appeal rights because of a break in service, but prior service may, under several specified conditions, be tacked onto service in the current probationary period to provide a year of overall service and appeal rights).

Johnson v. MSPB, 495 Fed. Appx. 68, 70-71 (Fed. Cir. 2012 NP), restated the equation as it restated the AJ's analysis:

Although Dr. Johnson conceded that he did not fit within the meaning of "employee" in § 7511 because he was terminated during the probationary period, the AJ considered the full record and recognized that Dr. Johnson had prior federal service in the year 2006. The AJ analyzed whether the prior federal service could be counted towards the completion of the probationary period--i.e., "tacked"--to show that Dr. Johnson was not subject to the appellate restrictions of a probationary employee. The AJ concluded that Dr. Johnson failed to make any assertion that his prior service could be counted towards the probationary period because the prior service would have to be (1) rendered immediately preceding the probationary appointment; (2) performed in the same agency; (3) performed in the same line of work; and (4) completed with no more than one break in service of less than 30 days. 5 C.F.R. § 315.802(b); Hurston v. Dep't of the Army, 113 M.S.P.R. 34, ¶ 9 (2010); see also Vannoy v. Dep't of Air Force, 73 F.3d 380 (Fed. Cir. 1995).

See Loucks v. MSPB, 360 Fed. Appx. 130, 131 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP) ("But even if these positions could somehow be considered to involve 'the same line of work' as her Health Technician position for purposes of the second prong of 5 C.F.R. § 315.802(b), her previous positions would still not count towards the completion of her later probationary period because they ended more than 30 calendar days before she was hired for the Health Technician position. Because her previous employment fails to satisfy the third prong of 5 C.F.R. § 315.802(b), her probationary period remained uncompleted at the time of her termination, and the Board lacked jurisdiction over her appeal.").

Because of the difference in the statutory definition of a competitive service employee and the regulatory calculus of probation, some employees who do not have sufficient current continuous service to meet the statutory definition will have sufficient service to have completed probation under OPM's regulation on tacking for prior service. See Barnett v. MSPB, 341 Fed. Appx. 639, 641-42 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP) (applying both sets of rules: a five-month break in service prevented tacking of prior service for completion of probation and it precluded an appeal right based on more than a year of current continuous service).

Sosa v. DOD, 102 MSPR 252, 253-58 ¶¶ 2-13, 2006 MSPB 149 (2006), explored tacking of service in a temporary appointment immediately before commencement of a competitive service appointment requiring completion of probation:

On February 23, 2004, the appellant received a temporary appointment not to exceed February 22, 2005, as a competitive service WG-6914-04 Store Worker with the Defense Commissary Agency (DECA), Fort Hamilton Commissary, Brooklyn, New York. On June 27, 2004, without a break in service, he received a career-conditional appointment as a WG-7407-05 Meatcutting Worker at the same commissary subject to completion of a one-year probationary period commencing June 27, 2004. On April 13, 2005, the agency terminated his employment for reasons arising post-appointment: unauthorized absence; failure to follow leave-requesting procedures; and failure to follow instructions. The agency processed the action as a termination occurring prior to the expiration of the required one-year probationary period. The appellant filed a petition for appeal and requested a hearing.

After the administrative judge (AJ) informed the appellant that the Board may lack jurisdiction over his appeal of his termination during a probationary period, the appellant asserted that he should be deemed to have completed his one-year probationary period because he had continuously been "in the same line of work" during his 14 months of service with DECA, despite the change in his appointment from WG-4 Store Worker to WG-5 Meatcutting Worker. The AJ informed the parties of Board precedent concerning the "tacking" of prior service in the "same line of work" toward completion of a probationary period in a new position, and he ordered the parties to file the appellant's position descriptions and any other relevant documentation on that issue. (Citing Aizin v. Department of Defense, 52 M.S.P.R. 146 (1991)). Subsequently, the AJ informed the parties of his view that the Board held, sub silentio, in Williams v. Department of Defense, 96 M.S.P.R. 335 (2004), that prior service in a temporary appointment limited to one year or less could not count toward completion of a probationary period in connection with a subsequent career-conditional appointment. The AJ afforded the parties the opportunity to submit additional evidence and argument on the jurisdictional issue.

...

An "employee," as defined by 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1), has appeal rights to the Board from certain agency actions such as a removal for cause. See 5 U.S.C. §§ 7512, 7513(d), 7701. Section 7511(a)(1) provides, in pertinent part, that:

(1) "employee" means--

(A) an individual in the competitive service--

(i) who is not serving a probationary period or trial period under an initial appointment; or

(ii) who has completed 1 year of current continuous service under other than a temporary appointment limited to 1 year or less[.]

5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A). Thus, the appellant could be an "employee" with Board appeal rights if he had completed his one-year probationary period prior to his termination. See 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(i); Williams, 96 M.S.P.R. 335, ¶ 9. Further, even if the appellant had not completed his probationary period prior to his termination, he might nevertheless have Board appeal rights if he satisfied the criteria of 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii). See McCormick v. Department of the Air Force, 307 F.3d 1339, 1341-43 (Fed. Cir. 2002), pet. for reh'g en banc denied, 329 F.3d 1354 (Fed. Cir. 2003).

...

There is no dispute that the appellant was terminated only 9 1/2 months after his career-conditional appointment as a Meatcutting Worker, which was subject to completion of a one-year probationary period. The appellant asserts that his prior service as a Store Worker should count toward completion of his probation. He asserts that despite the change in his position title and grade level, he continuously performed the same duties in the Fort Hamilton Commissary Meat Department throughout his 14 months of employment.

Prior service under a temporary limited appointment may count toward completion of an individual's probationary period if it was: (1) rendered immediately prior to the career or career-conditional appointment or conversion; (2) in the same line of work; (3) in the same agency; and (4) with no more than one break in service of less than 30 days. See 5 C.F.R. § 315.802(b); Youngs v. Department of the Army, 73 M.S.P.R. 551, 557-59 (1997)[2] ; Raman v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 34 M.S.P.R. 428, 430-31 (1987), aff'd, 845 F.2d 1034 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (Table); Angell v. Department of the Navy, 4 M.S.P.R. 266, 267-68 (1980). Contrary to the AJ's conclusion, the Board did not overrule this long-standing precedent in Williams, 96 M.S.P.R. 335. See Ellefson v. Department of the Army, 98 M.S.P.R. 191, ¶ 16 (2005) (post-Williams decision acknowledging that prior service in competitive service positions can be credited toward completion of a later probationary period in a competitive service position if the criteria set forth above are met). In Williams, the Board did not address or decide whether prior service under a temporary limited appointment may count toward completion of a probationary period. See Williams, 96 M.S.P.R. 335, ¶¶ 9-10. Although Williams holds that service in a temporary appointment limited to one year or less cannot, by statute, count towards completion of the one-year current continuous service requirement of 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii), see Williams, 96 M.S.P.R. 335, ¶ 13, such service may nevertheless count toward completion of a probationary period if the criteria set forth above are satisfied, see Youngs, 73 M.S.P.R. at 557-59; Raman, 34 M.S.P.R. at 430.

[2] We recognize that Youngs, 73 M.S.P.R. 551, was overruled in part on other grounds by McCormick, 307 F.3d at 1342-43. See Johnson v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 99 M.S.P.R. 362, ¶¶ 5-7, review dismissed, 161 Fed. Appx. 945 (Fed. Cir. 2005) Ellefson v. Department of the Army, 98 M.S.P.R. 191, ¶ 9 (2005).

Thus, although the appellant's service as a Store Worker was under a temporary limited appointment, that service counts toward the completion of his probationary period if it was rendered immediately prior to his career-conditional appointment, in the same line of work, in the same agency, and with no more than one break in service of less than 30 days. See 5 C.F.R. § 315.802(b); Youngs, 73 M.S.P.R. at 557-59; Raman, 34 M.S.P.R. at 430-31; Angell, 4 M.S.P.R. at 267-68. If the appellant has completed his one-year probationary period based on proper consideration of his Store Worker service, he is an "employee" under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(i) with adverse action appeal rights to the Board. See Williams, 96 M.S.P.R. 335, ¶ 9; Youngs, 73 M.S.P.R. at 557-58.[3] Thus, we must consider whether the appellant's Store Worker service satisfies the criteria for counting toward completion of his probationary period.

[3] We note that the appellant was serving a career-conditional appointment as a Meatcutting Worker that was subject to a probationary period. Thus, our analysis is entirely consistent with Board precedent holding that the appeal rights of an individual serving in the competitive service under an appointment that does not require completion of a probationary or trial period are governed exclusively by 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii). See Johnson, 99 M.S.P.R. 362, ¶¶ 6-10.

There is no dispute that the appellant's service as a Store Worker was rendered immediately prior to his career-conditional appointment as a Meatcutting Worker, in the same agency, with no break in service. However, the parties disputed below whether the appellant had been employed "in the same line of work." In determining whether the appellant had been employed "in the same line of work," the nature and character of the duties actually performed controls over intent or job titles. See 5 C.F.R. § 315.802(b)(2) ("the same line of work" is "determined by the employee's actual duties and responsibilities"); Kaiser v. Department of the Army, 75 M.S.P.R. 440, 447 (1997) (5 C.F.R. § 315.802(b)(2) "requires a close analysis of the 'actual duties and responsibilities' of the two positions to determine whether the appellant's prior service is creditable towards his probationary period."); Aizin v. Department of Defense, 52 M.S.P.R. 146, 149-50 (1991); Raman, 34 M.S.P.R. at 431; Van Skiver v. U.S. Postal Service, 7 M.S.P.R. 18, 21 (1981).

We find that the appellant has made nonfrivolous allegations that he served in the same line of work throughout his 14 months of service with DECA, and that a hearing is warranted. See Aizin, 52 M.S.P.R. at 150. The appellant alleged that starting February 24, 2004, he was assigned to the Meat Department and immediately learned the task of processing and preparation of ground meat. The appellant claims that he performed the same duties on a daily basis throughout his tenure, such as stocking, monitoring, and cleaning the Meat Department display case, and stocking and storing meat deliveries in the meat locker. He also claims to have assisted the Meatcutters during busy periods by preparing simpler items like veal cutlets and stew.

We note that the official position descriptions indicate clear differences between the WG-4 Store Worker and WG-5 Meatcutting Worker positions. The Meatcutting Worker position is in a different occupational category at a different grade level, and entails duties that are not required of a Store Worker, such as cutting, trimming and boning meat using handtools and powered meatcutting equipment. The appellant concedes that he did not perform all of the duties of a Meatcutting Worker, but asserts that he performed all of the duties that were assigned to him. Because the appellant has made nonfrivolous allegations that the actual duties he performed remained the same under both positions, the position descriptions are not dispositive. See Kaiser, 75 M.S.P.R. at 446-47; Aizin, 52 M.S.P.R. at 150; Van Skiver, 7 M.S.P.R. at 20-21. Thus, the appellant is entitled to a hearing to determine whether he is an "employee" under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(i) because his prior service as a Store Worker was creditable toward completion of his probationary period in the Meatcutting Worker position. See Aizin, 52 M.S.P.R. at 150 (the appellant was entitled to a hearing based on his nonfrivolous allegations that he performed the same duties in two different positions and thus may have completed his probationary period).

See Smith v. SSA, SF-315H-16-0544-I-1 (NP 7/6/2022) ("for purposes of regulatory tacking onto prior service to complete a probationary period, a prior temporary appointment and a single break in service that does not exceed 30 calendar days are permissible, provided that the prior service was with the same agency and in the same line of work. Sosa v. Department of Defense, 102 M.S.P.R. 252, ¶ 9 (2006); 5 C.F.R. § 315.802(b); see McCrary v. Department of the Army, 103 M.S.P.R. 266, ¶¶ 10-12 (2006) (finding that competitive-service tacking rules apply to excepted-service appointments), called into question on other grounds by Francis v. Department of the Air Force, 120 M.S.P.R. 138, ¶¶ 17-18 (2013)."..."the appellant cannot rely on 5 C.F.R. § 315.802(b) to tack his Student Trainee appointment onto his Legal Assistant appointment. Regardless of the appellant's claim that he performed the same duties at ICE and at the agency, he cannot meet the regulatory requirement because these two appointments were with different agencies. Ellefson, 98 M.S.P.R. 191, ¶ 16.").

"While subsections 7511(a)(1)(B) and (C), which govern the excepted service, have different requirements concerning preference eligible and nonpreference eligible appointees, Martinez v. Department of Homeland Security, 118 M.S.P.R. 154, ¶ 5 (2012); Maibaum, 116 M.S.P.R. 234, ¶ 9, the provisions governing the competitive service do not differentiate between appointees on the basis of their preference eligibility, 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(i)-(ii)." Smith v. SSA, SF-315H-16-0544-I-1, n.4 (NP 7/6/2022).

Liu v. USDA, 106 MSPR 178, 184-85 ¶¶ 11-13, 2007 MSPB 159 (2007), following Sosa, also addressing the effect on completion of a probationary appointment following service in a temporary position, stated that Liu's service in an excepted service temporary appointment could qualify for tacking when the Liu was then appointed to a career-conditional competitive service position subject to a year-long probation period:

In addition, the administrative judge failed to address whether the appellant's 5 months of prior temporary service in her GS-05 position could be "tacked" on to her near 12 months of service in her career-conditional GS-06 appointment and credited towards completion of her probationary period to satisfy subsection (i) of section 7511(a)(1)(A). Prior service under a temporary limited appointment may count toward completion of a later probationary or trial period if it was: (1) rendered immediately prior to the career or career-conditional appointment; (2) in the same line of work; (3) in the same agency; and (4) completed with no more than one break in service of less than 30 days. See 5 C.F.R. § 315.802(b); Sosa v. Department of Defense, 102 M.S.P.R. 252, ¶ 9 (2006). This concept applies to the excepted as well as the competitive service. See McCrary v. Department of the Army, 103 M.S.P.R. 266, ¶¶ 10-15 (2006) (finding that the appellant, who was terminated from her excepted service Guidance Counselor position during her probationary period, but who had prior service as a Guidance Counselor in a series of consecutive term appointments in the competitive service, had completed her probationary period).

Here, it is undisputed that the appellant's temporary service as a GS-0404-05 Biological Science Laboratory Technician was rendered immediately prior to her career-conditional appointment to the GS-0404-06 position of the same title, in the same agency, i.e., in the Department of Agriculture, Agricultural Research Service Field Organization in Stoneville, Mississippi, and with no break in service. Thus, the only remaining issue is whether her temporary appointment was "in the same line of work" as her subsequent permanent appointment. In determining whether an appellant has been employed "in the same line of work," the nature and character of the duties actually performed controls over the intent or job titles. See 5 C.F.R. § 315.802(b)(2) (the "same line of work" is "determined by the employee's actual duties and responsibilities"); Sosa, 102 M.S.P.R. 252, ¶ 11; Kaiser v. Department of the Army, 75 M.S.P.R. 440, 447 (1997) (section 315.802(b)(2) of 5 C.F.R. "requires a close analysis of the 'actual duties and responsibilities' of the two positions to determine whether the appellant's prior service is creditable towards his probationary period").

The appellant alleged below that her temporary position was similar to her permanent position and that after she was hired as a permanent employee she still performed work for her former supervisor. She also stated that her temporary and permanent positions were in the "same research unit in the same agency." Given these assertions, and the fact that the appellant's temporary and permanent positions shared the same title, i.e., Biological Science Laboratory Technician, we find that she has raised non-frivolous allegations that she served "in the same line of work" throughout her 17 months of service with the agency. Thus, the appellant is entitled to a hearing to determine whether she is an "employee" under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(i) because her prior service as a GS-0404-05 Biological Science Laboratory Technician was creditable toward completion of her probationary period in the GS-0404-06 position of the same title. See Sosa, 102 M.S.P.R. 252, ¶ 13 (remanding for a hearing based on the appellant's non-frivolous allegations that he performed the same duties in two different positions and thus may have completed his probationary period).

Fitzgerald v. Dept. of Air Force, 108 MSPR 620, 624-30 ¶¶ 6-16, 2008 MSPB 102 (2008), determined that excepted service employment could add to the time spent in a competitive service probationary appointment to provide appeal rights to the individual fired during probation. The decision analyzed the nature of service as a Air National Guard Technician, but the significance of the decision was broader:

...The issues of whether prior service as an NGT can be counted toward the completion of the probationary period of an individual appointed in the competitive service, and whether such service counts as "current continuous service" under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii), appear to be matters of first impression....

The Board has jurisdiction over the removal of an "employee." 5 U.S.C. §§ 7512(1), 7513(d). The term "employee" means

(A) an individual in the competitive service--

(i) who is not serving a probationary or trial period under an initial appointment; or

(ii) who has completed 1 year of current continuous service under other than a temporary appointment limited to 1 year or less;

(B) a preference eligible in the excepted service who has completed 1 year of current continuous service in the same or similar positions--

(i) in an Executive agency; or

(ii) in the United States Postal Service or Postal Rate Commission; and

(C) an individual in the excepted service (other than a preference eligible)--

(i) who is not serving a probationary or trial period under an initial appointment pending conversion to the competitive service; or

(ii) who has completed 2 years of current continuous service in the same or similar positions in an Executive agency under other than a temporary appointment limited to 2 years or less.

5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1). The Board, interpreting and applying OPM's regulations, has held that for competitive service employees, "current continuous service" means a period of employment or service immediately preceding an adverse action without a break in federal civilian employment of a workday. See, e.g., Ellefson v. Department of the Army, 98 M.S.P.R. 191, ¶ 14 (2005); 5 C.F.R. § 752.402(b) (defining "[c]urrent continuous employment" as a period of employment or service immediately preceding an adverse action in the same or similar positions[4] without a break in federal civilian employment of a workday.)[5]

[4] The "same or similar positions" requirement set forth in the regulation does not apply to competitive service employees. Thompson v. Department of the Treasury, 100 M.S.P.R. 545, ¶ 7 n.1 (2005).

[5] In 1988, the Office of Personnel Management, interpreting 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A), issued a final rule defining the term "current continuous employment." 53 Fed. Reg. 21,619, 21,623 (1988). The term "current continuous employment" in the applicable statute, however, was changed without comment in 1990, to "current continuous service" under the Civil Service Due Process Amendments. Compare 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A) (1988) with Pub. L. No. 101-376, § 2(a), 1990 U.S.C.C.A.N. (104 Stat.) 461, and H.R. Rep. No. 101-328, at 1, 6 (1990), reprinted in 1990 U.S.C.C.A.N. 695, 700.

In Williams v. Department of Defense, 96 M.S.P.R. 335, ¶ 12 (2004), the Board noted that the question of whether service in the excepted service could count toward the 1-year current continuous service requirement of 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii), appeared to be an issue of first impression. The Board noted that, although Mr. Williams' prior service in the excepted service may or may not count toward the completion of the 1-year current continuous service requirement, it did not need to decide that issue because an intervening temporary appointment Mr. Williams had received meant that he did not have 1 year of current continuous service "under other than a temporary appointment limited to 1 year or less." Id. ¶ 12-13; cf. Beets v. Department of Homeland Security, 98 M.S.P.R. 451, ¶ 8 (2005) (finding it unnecessary to address whether prior service in the competitive service counted toward 2 years of current continuous service under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(C)(ii) because the positions in question were not "the same or similar"). Unlike Mr. Williams, the appellant's appointment to the excepted service position of WG-8852-10 Aircraft Mechanic with the District of Columbia Adjutant General at Andrews Air Force Base, Maryland, was not a temporary appointment limited to 1 year or less. Thus, the first question before us is whether prior service in the excepted service can count toward the 1-year current continuous service requirement for individuals in the competitive service.

The starting point for any case involving statutory interpretation is the language of the statute itself, which must be examined to determine Congress' intent on the question at issue. Adkins v. Office of Personnel Management, 104 M.S.P.R. 233, ¶ 12 (2006). Statutory provisions should not be read in isolation; rather, each section of a statute should be construed in connection with other sections so as to produce a harmonious whole. Styslinger v. Department of the Army, 105 M.S.P.R. 223, ¶ 17 (2007). It is a normal rule of statutory construction that identical words used in different parts of the same act are intended to have the same meaning. Butler v. Social Security Administration, 331 F.3d 1368, 1372 (Fed. Cir. 2003); Styslinger, 105 M.S.P.R. 223, ¶ 30.

Here, as reflected in the structure of 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1), Congress was aware that the executive branch of government under title 5 of the United States Code included positions that generally fell within two categories: Competitive service and excepted service. See 5 U.S.C. §§ 2102-03 (describing the competitive and excepted service). Nevertheless, 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii), as well as 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(B) and 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(C)(ii), all refer to current continuous "service" without specifying that such "service" must be in the competitive or excepted service, respectively. In fact, because the same phrase, "current continuous service," is used in sections 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii), 7511(a)(1)(B), and 7511(a)(1)(C)(ii), we apply the above presumption that Congress intended the phrase to have the same meaning in each of those sections. This is especially true given the close proximity of these provisions to each other. See Commissioner of Internal Revenue v. Lundy, 516 U.S. 235, 250 (1996) (finding that the interrelationship and close proximity of certain provisions presented a "classic case" for application of the normal rule that identical words used in different parts of the same act are intended to have the same meaning). Thus, although each of the above sections defines the term "employee" by reference to an individual or preference eligible presently "in" or occupying a position in the competitive or excepted service, we find that the phrase "current continuous service" in those sections means service, in either the competitive or excepted service, that immediately precedes an adverse action without a break in federal civilian employment of a workday. See Dade v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 101 M.S.P.R. 43, ¶¶ 9, 12 (2005) (finding that prior service as an Audiologist in the competitive service with the Department of the Army may be counted, under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(B)(i), toward the completion of 1-year of current continuous service in an Audiologist position with the Department of Veterans Affairs (DVA), even if the DVA Audiologist position was in the excepted service). This interpretation of 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(ii) is consistent with 5 C.F.R. § 752.402(b), which does not define current continuous employment as a period of service confined to either the competitive or excepted service. See McCrary v. Department of the Army, 103 M.S.P.R. 266, ¶ 8 (2006) (noting that the definition of "current continuous service" is the same in competitive service and excepted service cases).

We now turn to the question of whether the appellant's NGT service in particular, along with his service as an Aircraft Mechanic at the March Air Reserve Base, provide him with 1 year of current continuous service under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii). As the successor to the state militias of the nation's early years, the National Guard is a deeply imbedded feature of our national defense system. Singleton v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 244 F.3d 1331, 1333 (Fed. Cir. 2001). The role of the National Guard, however, does not fit neatly within the scope of either state[6] or national concerns. Id. The National Guard is an essential reserve component of the Armed Forces of the United States, available with regular forces in time of war, and on stand-by to be federalized to assist in controlling civil disorders. Id. The activity, make-up, and function of the National Guard are provided for, to a large extent, by federal law. Id. At the same time, the National Guard is available to serve state needs, and within each state, the National Guard is a state agency under state authority and control. Id.

[6] Although we refer in this paragraph, for purposes of convenience, to "state" concerns, agencies, authority, and control, we note that under 32 U.S.C. § 101(3), (6), the terms "National Guard" and "Air National Guard" mean certain qualifying parts of the organized militia of the several States and Territories, Puerto Rico, and the District of Columbia.

Mirroring this special posture of the National Guard is the unusual status of its employees. In addition to purely military personnel, the National Guard employs civilians to perform a wide range of administrative, clerical, and technical tasks. Often the responsibilities and duties of these employees, who are generally referred to as NGTs, correspond directly to those of other civilian employees, yet they arise in a distinctly military context, implicating significant military concerns. Special Counsel v. Everett, 28 M.S.P.R. 348, 350 (1985), overruled on other grounds by Ockerhausen v. State of New Jersey Department of Military & Veterans Affairs, 52 M.S.P.R. 484, 489 (1992).

We note that 5 U.S.C. § 7511(b)(5) provides that "[t]his subchapter does not apply to an employee...who is described in section 8337(h)(1), relating to technicians in the National Guard," and that section 8337(h)(1), in turn, provides that the term "technician" means an individual employed under section 709(a) of title 32 who, as a condition of the employment, is required under section 709(b) of such title to be a member of the National Guard and to hold a specified military grade. This case, however, does not involve an adverse action taken against an NGT who has appealed that action to the Board. Instead, it involves the question of how to treat NGT service in the excepted service in determining whether an individual in the competitive service has appeal rights to the Board.

A person like the appellant, who was employed under 32 U.S.C. § 709(a), must be a military technician (dual status) as defined in section 10216(a) of title 10, United States Code. 32 U.S.C. § 709(b). Section 10216(a)(1), in turn, provides that, for purposes of that section "and any other provision of law," a military technician (dual status) like the appellant "is a Federal civilian employee." In fact, under 32 U.S.C. § 709(e), an NGT "is an employee of the Department of the Army or the Department of the Air Force, as the case may be, and an employee of the United States." See Ockerhausen, 52 M.S.P.R. at 488 (even though National Guard technicians work for state organizations under the authority of a state official, they must be considered civilian employees of the Department of the Army or the Department of the Air Force to the same extent as other employees of the Department of the Army or the Department of the Air Force).[7] Such an NGT position is simply "outside the competitive service if the technician employed in that position is required under subsection (b) to be a member of the National Guard." 32 U.S.C. § 709(e). Given that military technicians like the appellant are considered, for purposes of "any other provision of law," to be federal civilian employees, and that they are also considered to be employees of the Department of the Air Force and the United States to the same extent as other employees of the Department of the Air Force, we hold that the AJ correctly concluded that the appellant is an "employee" under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii) because he completed 1 year of current continuous service under other than a temporary appointment limited to 1 year or less, i.e., he completed 1 year of employment or service immediately preceding an adverse action without a break in federal civilian employment of a workday. See Ellefson, 98 M.S.P.R. 191, ¶ 14; Ockerhausen, 52 M.S.P.R. at 488 ("[A] technician...must be deemed an employee in a covered position 'in an agency' who has the same rights as other civilian employees of those agencies, save those rights specifically denied them in the National Guard Technicians Act.").

[7] We note that, under 20 C.F.R. § 1002.306, a National Guard civilian technician is considered a State employee for purposes of the Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act of 1994, but is considered a federal employee for "most other purposes." In addition, for purposes of OPM's regulations relating to restoration to duty from uniformed service, in the case of a National Guard technician employed under 32 U.S.C. § 709, the employing agency is the adjutant general of the state in which the technician is employed. 5 C.F.R. § 353.102(2).

We recognize that, under 32 U.S.C. § 709(g), "[s]ections 2108, 3502, 7511, and 7512 of title 5 do not apply to a person employed under this section." As set forth above, subchapter II of chapter 75, relating to removals, suspensions for more than 14 days, reductions in grade or pay, or furloughs for 30 days or less, does not apply to NGTs. 5 U.S.C. § 7511(b)(5). The agency argues that it is not logical for Congress to have "exclude[d] the service while the employee is a National Guard Technician, but then require[d] the federal agency to count the time that previously 'did not apply'" when calculating a probationary period or current continuous service under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii). However, 32 U.S.C. § 709(g) and 5 U.S.C. § 7511(b)(5) do not exclude service performed while an employee is an NGT; rather, they simply hold that such NGTs are not "employees" entitled to appeal to the Board while they hold such positions. The appellant was not employed under section 709 at the time of his termination in this case.[8] We also note that the agency relies upon an initial decision issued by a Board AJ finding that time spent in an NGT excepted service position cannot be applied toward the completion of a probationary period in the competitive service. Board initial decisions are of no precedential value, however, and cannot be cited or relied upon as controlling authority. Rockwell v. Department of Commerce, 39 M.S.P.R. 217, 222 (1988).

[8] The agency is incorrect in suggesting that service in a position that is not covered by 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1) can never be combined with subsequent service in a position covered by section 7511(a)(1) for purposes of determining an individual's appeal rights. Although an individual who works for a Non-Appropriated Fund (NAF) activity has no ch. 75 rights as long as he holds such a position, see Taylor v. Department of the Navy, 1 M.S.P.R. 591 (1980), NAF service is credited toward completion of probation under a subsequent career-conditional appointment when the NAF service was in the same agency, the same line of work, and contained or was followed by no more than a single break in service not exceeding 30 days. 5 C.F.R. § 315.802(b).

Having found that the appellant meets the definition of "employee" set forth at 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii), and that the Board has jurisdiction over this appeal on that basis, we need not address the AJ's ruling that the appellant also meets the definition of "employee" set forth at 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(i).

See Williams v. DOD, 96 MSPR 335, 340 ¶ 12 (2004) (declining to decide whether employment within one agency in an excepted service position can tack for purposes of completion of a probationary period in the competitive service). But see Sinclair v. VA, PH-315H-11-0354-I-1 (NP 1/9/2012) (issue of tacking in prior employment with a different agency for a competitive service probationary appellant: dictum: "In addition, the appellant's prior service was not eligible for tacking because it was under a series of temporary appointments in the excepted service. But see Bowman v. The Board synthesized some decisions concerning tacking of temporary service to time worked in probationary appointments in Raman v. DHUD, 34 MSPR 428, 430-32 (1987), involving an appellant with a temporary excepted service employment as an attorney before his appointment as a competitive service loan specialist:

The Board has held that service in temporary, limited appointments generally cannot be counted for purposes of determining whether a competitive service employee has completed one year of current continuous service. See Compton, 31 MSPR at 404; Morris, 32 MSPR at 80; Fish v. Department of Navy, 29 MSPR 595 (1986). However, prior service in a temporary, limited appointment may be credited towards completion of a new probationary period where the service was rendered immediately preceding career or career-conditional appointment or conversion. See Haning v. U.S. Marine Corps, 31 MSPR 252 (1986); Compton, 31 MSPR at 405, n.2; Angell v. Department of Navy, 4 MSPR 340, 4 MSPR 266 (1980).

In Compton and Morris, the appellants were both serving in temporary appointments at the time of their terminations. Here, however, the appellant was serving in a career-conditional appointment when his termination occurred. His temporary limited appointment as a General Attorney immediately preceded his conversion to the career-conditional appointment. Therefore, we hold that the administrative judge erred in finding that the appellant's prior temporary service could not be counted towards completion of his probationary period under Compton and Morris.

The appellant's prior temporary service fails to meet the remaining conditions necessary to credit that service towards completion of his probationary period.

In addition to the condition that the prior service immediately precede a career or career-conditional appointment, the Federal Personnel Manual (FPM) provides that the prior service must be rendered in the same agency and the same line of work, without more than one break of less than thirty (30) days. See FPM Chapter 315, Appendix A-3(c)(l) (Apr. 27, 1982). See also Long v. Department of the Navy, [32 M.S.P.R. 438 (1987)] ; Angell, 4 M.S.P.R. at 267. Based on our review of the record, we find that while the appellant's prior service was rendered in the same agency without a break of more than thirty days, it was not in the same line of work.

"Same line of work" is defined as meaning that the two positions are so similar that they require the same qualifications and the nature of the work would place the positions in the same competitive level for reduction-in-force purposes. See FPM Chapter 315, Appendix A-3(c)(3); Haning, 31 M.S.P.R. at 254. The Board has held that the nature and character of the duties performed controls questions of intent or technical title. Yancey v. Department of the Army, [32 M.S.P.R. 606 (1987)] .

The record evidence shows that the qualifications and nature of the duties for the positions of Loan Specialist and General Attorney are different. Attorneys are required to be members of a state bar. Their duties range from conducting legal research and preparing legal documents to representing the agency in administrative or court proceedings. In contrast, a Loan Specialist's duties include analyzing and evaluating loan collateral, reviewing delinquent and defaulted loans, counselling loan applicants and developing policies to govern lending programs. In addition, the positions of Loan Specialist, GS-1165, and General Attorney, GS-905, are not in the same occupational level or classification series for purposes of reduction in force.

The appellant asserts that during his appointment as General Attorney with the agency's Philadelphia Regional Counsel Office, he was actually performing the duties of a Loan Specialist. In support of his assertion, the appellant submitted a series of performance appraisals and a performance description addendum for the position of Loan Specialist (Realty).

We find the evidence to be insufficient to establish that the appellant actually performed the duties of a Loan Specialist while serving in his temporary appointment as General Attorney. We find further that, since the two positions are not in "the same line of work," the appellant's prior service as a General Attorney cannot be counted towards completion of his probationary period and that he was a probationary employee when the agency removed him on December 12, 1986.

Other decisions restate the relation between temporary appointments and completion of probationary periods through tacking. To qualify under the former FPM as time served toward the completion of a probationary period after conversion to career-conditional status, the temporary employee must have been employed without a break in service and in the same line of work as that held after conversion. Coltin v. Dept. of Navy, 20 MSPR 16, 17 (1984); see Starling v. SBA, 21 MSPR 551, 552 (1984) (for purposes of determining completion of probation, remanding to determine, for purposes of tacking, whether temporary and permanent loan specialist positions were same or similar jobs) (citing Angell v. Dept. of Navy, 4 MSPR 266, 4 MSPB 340 (1980) [appellant had a term limited appointment of a year, one-day break in service, and a career conditional appointment in a clerk typist job: "The appellant here served almost two years when she was notified that she would be terminated. She fulfilled the criteria established by OPM which all go toward furthering the purpose of the regulation, which is that the agency have one year to determine an employee's fitness. To deny the appellant the right of appeal because of a break of one day in her service would defeat the intent of the regulations"]), following remand, 25 MSPR 392, 393 (1984) (jobs were dissimilar; permitting no tacking). But see Lewis v. DOJ, 3 MSPR 326, 328 (1980) (a clerk-typist who earlier served as student aide: "Since prior service in temporary appointments limited to terms of 1 year or less are excluded by statute [5 USC 7511(a)(1)(A)] from being credited toward completion of a probationary period, the criteria set forth at FPM 315, App. A-3 regarding the crediting of prior service do not apply to such appointments."). Lewis does not appear to have been specifically overruled, but its approach is inconsistent with the OPM regulation allowing tacking of temporary service without limitation concerning the duration of that appointment.

Earlier OPM regulations and many older MSPB cases refer to rules from the late Federal Personnel Manual (FPM) governing crediting toward completion of probation of prior federal service. Under the FPM, prior service was creditable toward completion of the probationary period when the prior service was rendered: (1) immediately preceding the career-conditional appointment; (2) in the same agency; (3) in the same line of work; and (4) with no more than one break of less than 30 days during the prior service. FPM Ch. 315, App. A, A-3(c)(1)(a)-(c). Under the FPM Appendix, the prior service counted "regardless of whether it was under competitive appointment (career-conditional, career, probational, permanent, war service indefinite, indefinite, limited, or unlimited temporary), under excepted appointment, as a status quo employee, or as a nonappropriated fund employee of a Department of Defense instrumentality." See Yancey v. Dept. of Army, 32 MSPR 606, 609 (1987) (relying on FPM); Sullivan v. USDA, 32 MSPR 194, 196 (1987) (relying on FPM). Long v. Dept. of Navy, 32 MSPR 438, 441-42 (1987), summarized the law developed under the FPM:

In the initial decision, the administrative judge, citing Rochniak v. Department of the Navy, 9 MSPB 808, 10 MSPR 603 (1982) and Oulvey v. Veterans Administration, 7 MSPB 203, 7 MSPR 312 (1981), aff'd, 690 F.2d 681 (8th Cir. 1982), correctly noted that an employee who is competitively appointed from a civil service register is required to serve a new probationary period. See Federal Personnel Manual (FPM) Chapter 315, Subchapter 8-2(a) (May 23, 1983). Although this is the general rule, there is an exception to it which the administrative judge failed to note. Thus, he incorrectly stated that, under any circumstances, prior federal service of an individual who is competitively appointed from a register may not be counted towards completion of the probationary period.

The Office of Personnel Management is required to publish in the FPM a list of the conditions under which prior service may be counted toward completion of a probationary period. 5 CFR § 315.802; FPM Chapter 315, Subchapter 8-2(f). According to the FPM, prior federal service may be counted towards completion of a new probationary period of an individual who is competitively appointed to a career or career-conditional appointment from a register or who is converted to a career or career-conditional appointment if the prior service were: (1) rendered immediately preceding the career or career-conditional appointment or conversion; (2) in the same line of work; (3) in the same agency; and (4) with no more than one break of less than 30 days during that service. See FPM Chapter 315, Appendix A-3(c)(1) (Apr. 27, 1982); Marcus v. United States, 473 F.2d 896, 200 Ct. Cl. 544 (1973); Haning v. U.S. Marine Corps, 31 MSPR 252 (1986); Angell v. Department of the Navy, 4 MSPB 340, 4 MSPR 266 (1980).

Although this exception was not noted in Rochniak or in Oulvey, those appeals were correctly decided since the appellants in those cases did not meet the conditions for counting prior federal service. To the extent that Rochniak, Oulvey, or other Board cases imply that prior federal service may never be counted toward a new probationary period when an eligible is competitively appointed from a civil service register, they are MODIFIED for the reasons stated in this Opinion and Order.

In the instant case, the appellant's prior federal service may not be counted toward completion of her probationary period since that service was not in the same agency....

An early case applied a (now defunct) FPM provision requiring that for tacking to occur service in a temporary position had to be immediately preceding the career or career-conditional appointment or conversion. When an employee would otherwise receive credit toward completion of a competitive service probationary period through creditable service in a temporary position, but her service was interrupted by a one-day break, the Board disregarded technical application of OPM regulations and considered the probationary period completed to avoid defeating the intent of the regulations governing probation. Angell v. Dept. of Navy, 4 MSPR 266, 268, 4 MSPB 340 (1980). The current OPM regulations allow up to a 30 day break in service for tacking. 5 CFR 315.802(b)(3) (2022).

The line of work requirement, discussed below, still applies to time served in an excepted service job when the appellant seeks to credit that time to the time served in a competitive service probationary appointment. Spurlock v. MSPB (Fed. Petitioner argues that the board erred in dismissing her appeal because she had completed a probationary period in her former position and was not required to complete a second probationary period, or because the agency was estopped from contending that her dismissal was effectuated during the probationary period. We cannot agree with petitioner's arguments. Petitioner was required to complete a second probationary period following her appointment to the career conditional position regardless of her thirteen months of previous employment in the excepted service. See Federal Personnel Manual, Chapter 315, Subch. 8-2. Furthermore, the thirteen months of employment in the excepted service cannot be credited toward the second probationary period, because the position in the excepted service was not in the same line of work as the position in the competitive service, as evidenced by the different occupational code for the latter, which was reflected on the Standard Form 50. To reduce (or explain) some confusion concerning crediting of prior service in early cases, we reprint below an excerpt from the (now retired) Appendix to FPM Chapter 315, "Determining Completion of a Probationary Period," of September 19, 1989 (the FPM provision differs from the current regulation, 5 CFR 315.802(b)(3) (2022), particularly as to breaks in service):

Competitive service term (temporary) appointments are treated, for purposes of probationary requirements, as are competitive service appointments without time limitation. Gamble v. Dept. of Army, 111 MSPR 529, 531 n.1, 2009 MSPB 118 (2009), explained as to an employee terminated during a competitive service trial period in a temporary appointment:

Under 5 C.F.R. § 316.304, prior federal civilian service of an individual with a term appointment is credited toward completion of the trial period in the same manner as it is in 5 C.F.R. § 315.802, meaning that such prior service must have been in the same agency and the same line of work, and it contained or was followed by no more than a single break in service that did not exceed 30 calendar days.

Periods an employee is on disability retirement before the initiation of probation do not tack for purposes of completing probation, Baggan v. Dept. of State, 109 MSPR 572, 575-77 ¶¶ 5-9, 2008 MSPB 196 (2008):

To qualify as an "employee" with appeal rights under 5 U.S.C. Chapter 75, the appellant, as a competitive service employee, must show that he either is not serving a probationary period or has completed one year of current continuous service under an appointment other than a temporary one limited to a year or less. 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A); McCormick v. Department of the Air Force, 307 F.3d 1339, 1341-44 (Fed. Cir. 2002). An appellant who has not served a full year under his appointment can show that he has completed the probationary period, and so is no longer a probationer, by tacking on prior service in the competitive service if: (1) The prior service was rendered immediately preceding the probationary appointment; (2) it was performed in the same agency; (3) it was performed in the same line of work; and (4) it was completed with no more than one break in service of less than 30 days. 5 C.F.R. § 315.802(b); Ellefson v. Department of the Army, 98 M.S.P.R. 191, ¶ 16 (2005). Alternatively, an employee can show that, while he may be a probationer, he is an "employee" with Chapter 75 appeal rights because, immediately preceding the adverse action, he had completed at least one year of current continuous service in the competitive service without a break in federal civilian employment of a workday. Ellefson, 98 M.S.P.R. 191, ¶ 14.

The appellant argues that he was not a probationer at the time of his separation, noting that prior to his appointment as a Program Analyst, he received disability retirement, effective October 11, 1996, from the competitive service position of Procurement Analyst with the Department of Transportation. He asserts that he retired from that position due to a compensable injury and points to a regulation regarding completion of probationary periods, which states in part that "[a]bsence (whether on or off the rolls) due to compensable injury...is creditable in full upon restoration to Federal service." 5 C.F.R. § 315.802(c). The context of this provision, however, shows that it was intended to enable an employee who had begun a probationary period, and who subsequently was absent from his position for a reason addressed in 5 C.F.R. § 315.802(c), to have part or all of the period of absence counted towards the probationary period already begun at the time of the absence. 5 C.F.R. § 315.802(c) begins with a statement that "[p]eriods of absence while in a pay status count toward completion of probation" (emphasis added), and it then provides that other kinds of absences in a non-pay status, including those caused by compensable injury, also are creditable. Nothing in 5 C.F.R. § 315.802(c) indicates that any of the absences described therein may be credited toward a probationary period not yet begun. Therefore, the period during which the appellant was receiving a disability retirement annuity is not creditable toward completion of the probationary period that he began on April 15, 2007.

Furthermore, the appellant's prior service as a Procurement Analyst with the Department of Transportation cannot be tacked on to fulfill his probationary period that began in April 2007 because it was not with same agency. See 5 C.F.R. § 315.802(b); Ellefson, 98 M.S.P.R. 191, ¶ 16. Therefore, for the foregoing reasons and because the appellant was separated from his Program Analyst position less than a year after his appointment, he was serving a probationary period at the time of separation and so is not an "employee" under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(i).

In the alternative, the appellant argues that as a disability retiree he remained in federal service for the duration of his retirement and so, at the time of the adverse action, he had more than a year of current continuous service without a break of a workday and, therefore, was an "employee" under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii). However, the appellant's disability retirement was not current continuous federal service because he was separated from federal service and removed from the rolls of the Department of Transportation when he retired in 1996. Cf. Campbell v. U.S. Postal Service, 88 M.S.P.R. 546, ¶¶ 7-9 (2001) (appellant completed a year of current continuous service even though she was in a non-duty, non-pay status because she was not formally separated); Tom v. Department of the Interior, 32 M.S.P.R. 126, 128-30 (1987) (seasonal employee, despite being placed in non-duty, non-pay status for 2 months during the year prior to his separation, completed a year of current continuous service because he remained on the agency's rolls). Moreover, the Board has held that 5 C.F.R. § 315.802(c), the provision permitting the crediting of absences due to compensable injuries, does not affect the computation of an employee's current continuous service. See Gadsden v. Department of State, 102 M.S.P.R. 79, ¶ 14 (2006). Therefore, the appellant did not have a year of current continuous service at the time of the adverse action and so was not an "employee" with Board appeal rights under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii).
