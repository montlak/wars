q. Night Differential

Night pay differential is provided under 5 USC 5343(f) for Prevailing Rate employees, and under 5 USC 5545(a) for General Schedule employees. OPM regulations governing the night pay differential for General Schedule employees are at 5 CFR 550.121, .122 (2022), and for Prevailing Rate employees at 5 CFR 532.505 (2022). The pay premium for night differential was discussed in AFGE Local 607 and DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, FCI Elkton, 61 FLRA 707, 709-10 (2006):

5 U.S.C. § 5545(a) authorizes a 10 percent night differential for "regularly scheduled work between the hours of 6:00 P.M. and 6:00 A.M." See also 5 C.F.R. § 550.121(a).[5] The Office of Personnel Management (OPM) is authorized to prescribe regulations necessary for the administration of premium pay and hours of duty. See 5 U.S.C. § 5548; 5 U.S.C. § 6101(c). OPM has defined the term "regularly scheduled work" in 5 U.S.C. § 5545(a) as work which has been scheduled in advance of an administrative workweek. See 5 C.F.R. § 610.102 [6]. See also United States Dep't of Justice, Immigration and Naturalization Service, 19 FLRA 823, 824 (1985) (INS). Thus, an employee is entitled to night shift differential, under 5 U.S.C. § 5545(a), when night work has been scheduled in advance of an administrative workweek. See United States Dep't of Health and Human Services, Social Security Administration, Baltimore, Md., 37 FLRA 1469, 1474 (1990) (SSA).[7] Moreover, an agency is obligated to reschedule an employee's tour of duty when it is known in advance of an administrative workweek that there will be a different work requirement (such as night work) in that workweek. See 5 C.F.R. § 610.121(b) (2). See also INS, 19 FLRA at 824-25

[5] C.F.R. § 550.121(a) provides, in pertinent part:

Except as provided by paragraph (b) of this section, night work is regularly scheduled work performed by an employee between the hours of 6 P.M. and 6 A.M. Subject to § 550.122, and except as otherwise provided in this subpart, an employee who performs night work is entitled to...a night pay differential amounting to 10 percent of his or her rate of basic pay.

[6] "Regularly scheduled work" is defined in 5 C.F.R. § 610.102 as "work that is scheduled in advance of an administrative workweek under an agency's procedures for establishing workweeks in accordance with § 610.111." Furthermore, "[a]dministrative workweek" is defined in 5 C.F.R. § 610.102 as "any period of 7 consecutive 24-hour periods designated in advance by the head of the agency under section 6101 of title 5, United States Code."

[7] Night shift differential is in addition to overtime. See 5 C.F.R. § 550.122.

An employee may be entitled to night shift differential even when night work has not been scheduled in advance. See INS, 19 FLRA at 825. In this regard, an employee is entitled to night shift differential, even when not previously scheduled, where the agency: "(i) [h]ad knowledge of the specific days and hours of the work requirement in advance of the administrative workweek, and (ii) had the opportunity to determine which employee had to be scheduled, or rescheduled, to meet the specific days and hours of that work requirement." 5 C.F.R. § 610.121(b)(3). See also INS, 19 FLRA at 825.

An award was set aside that required management to compensate employees for the loss of night duty differential when they were assigned to the day shift for training. The award abridged management's right to assign work. VAMC, Lebanon and AFGE Local 1966, 19 FLRA 392, 393 (1985); cf. AFGE Local 1441 and Dept. of Army, Corps of Eng'rs, St. Paul, 70 FLRA 161 (2016) (analyzing OPM night shift differential regulation, 5 CFR 532.505; arbitrator determined that employees were not temporarily assigned to day shift).

Intermittent, part-time immigration inspectors were not entitled to additional compensation for night overtime under 8 USC 1353(a). That provision applies only to night overtime performed by full-time employees working regular tours of duty. 49 Comp. Gen. 577 (1977). The Comptroller General ruled that part-time employees are not entitled to the extra compensation, even if they are working regularly scheduled shifts. Comp. Gen. No. B-197533 (July 1, 1980). AFGE Local 1917 and INS, 18 FLRA 33, 35 (1985). The topic of night differential pay for immigration officers was discussed in the context of governing OPM regulations and Court of Claims decisions in DOJ, INS and INS Council, AFGE Local 2805, 19 FLRA 823, 824-25 (1985). Concerning the relation of FLSA overtime payment and the payment of night shift differential, refer to Dept. of Air Force, Warner Robins ALC and AFGE Local 987, 68 FLRA 102, 104-05 (2014) ("when applying the formula found in 5 C.F.R. § 551.512, the night-shift differential is included, as required by the FLSA, when determining 'total remuneration,' for the purpose of calculating the employee's 'regular rate.' But the night shift differential is not included in the employee's 'straight time' when the FLSA overtime pay is calculated. Thus, in regard to the FLSA overtime calculation, the OPM guidance is not inconsistent with, or contrary to, the plain wording of 5 C.F.R. § 551.512. Accordingly, under the standard described above, the OPM guidance is entitled to deference in regard to its calculation of FLSA overtime.").

An award was set aside that continued the practice of paying employees a night shift differential established by contract and past practice when there was wage legislation effectively directing a different type of compensation formula that negated the continuation of the differential. United Power Trades Council and Army Corps of Eng'rs, N. Pac. Div., 21 FLRA 501, 504 (1986). An award granting night differential pay was set aside because it was not based on "regularly scheduled work" performed between the hours of 6 P.M. and 6 A.M. as required by 5 USC 5545(a) and 5 CFR Parts 550 and 610. The added work was not scheduled as part of the grievants' administrative work week; instead, it was irregular and occasional during the course of scheduled on-call duty. DHHS, SSA, Baltimore and AFGE Local 1923, 37 FLRA 1469, 1473-75 (1990); see Dept. of Transp., FAA and NATCA, 52 FLRA 649, 656-57 (1996) (setting aside award of night differential pay when employees were using flexible work schedule, meaning they were not working evening hours supporting payment of differential). FLRA set aside, as contrary to law, an award that granted the differential to General Schedule employees on annual leave who otherwise would have been regularly scheduled for night work. GSA and AFGE Council 236, 46 FLRA 494, 499 (1992). Another award was set aside that determined, for purposes of pay retention, that night differentials were a part of a prevailing rate employee's scheduled rate. The night differential was not subject to pay retention. See Dept. of Army, Corpus Christi Army Depot and AFGE Local 2142, 48 FLRA 966, 969 (1993).

Assuming a night differential calculated in accordance with 5 USC 5544, nothing in that statute or 5 CFR Part 532 (2022) (regulating wage and differential payment to prevailing rate employees) precludes the parties from utilizing pay-setting authority to negotiate basic rates of pay that augment the statutory premium. See GPO and Sheet Metal Workers Int'l Ass'n Local 100, 57 FLRA 299, 301-02 (2001), reconsideration denied, 59 FLRA 273 (2003). If an arbitrator determines that an employee was given an assignment that violates a contract, apparently FLRA will not permit the award to pay a night shift differential to the employee that he would have received had he remained at his original assignment if the award does not also return the employee to that original assignment. Bureau of Mint, Denver and Mint Council, AFGE, 17 FLRA 56, 56-57 (1985) (no violation of law or contract).
