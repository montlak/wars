2. Settlement; Vacatur of Initial Decision

If the parties settle an appeal during the pendency of a PFR, the PFR is dismissed as moot once the settlement is accepted into the record for enforcement. Perham v. OPM, 45 MSPR 551, 553 (1990). If the parties desire that the Board accept a settlement into the record for enforcement at the PFR stage, the Board ensures that its requirements are met. An example of treatment of a settlement received at the PFR stage is Jones v. USPS, DC-0353-12-0242-I-2 (NP 9/20/2013):

After the filing of the petition for review, the parties submitted a document entitled "Agreement Between Kristy M. Jones and USPS" signed and dated by the appellant on June 17, 2013, and by the agency on June 18, 2013. The document provides, among other things, for the dismissal of the appeal.

Before dismissing a matter as settled, the Board must decide whether the parties have entered into a settlement agreement, understand its terms, and intend to have the agreement entered into the record for enforcement by the Board. See Mahoney v. U.S. Postal Service, 37 M.S.P.R. 146, 149 (1988). We find here that the parties have, in fact, entered into a settlement agreement, that they understand the terms, and that they want the Board to enforce those terms.

In addition, before accepting a settlement agreement into the record for enforcement purposes, the Board must determine whether the agreement is lawful on its face, whether the parties freely entered into it, and whether the subject matter of this appeal is within the Board's jurisdiction, that is, whether a law, rule, or regulation grants the Board the authority to decide such a matter. See Stewart v. U.S. Postal Service, 73 M.S.P.R. 104, 107 (1997). We find here that the agreement is lawful on its face, that the parties freely entered into it, and that the subject matter of this appeal--an appeal from a preference-eligible employee of the U.S. Postal Service alleging the denial of restoration to duty rights after partial recovery from a compensable injury--is within the Board's jurisdiction under 5 U.S.C. § 7701(a) and 5 C.F.R. § 353.304. Accordingly, we find that dismissal of the petition for appeal "with prejudice to refiling" (i.e., the parties normally may not refile this appeal) is appropriate under these circumstances, and we accept the settlement agreement into the record for enforcement purposes.

See Roberts v. OPM, 84 MSPR 622 (1999) (Table) (accepting settlement at the PFR stage and, in accordance with the settlement, dismissing an appeal with prejudice to refiling); Huberdeau v. Dept. of Treasury, 83 MSPR 591, 592 & n.* (1999) (reopening the appeal, despite the possible untimeliness of the PFR, to vacate the initial decision based on a settlement agreement that was not accepted into the record for lack of a jurisdictional determination); McDonald v. USPS, 81 MSPR 448, 449 (1999) (dismissing a fee petition with prejudice to its refiling; accepting a counsel fee settlement for purposes of enforcement); Perales v. USPS, 71 MSPR 493, 494 (1996) (PFR in a fee dispute was settled; a counsel fee motion was dismissed with prejudice; the settlement was accepted for enforcement purposes); Van Cura v. OPM, 65 MSPR 19 (1994) (settlement of a counsel fee petition; an addendum initial decision was vacated and the fee motion dismissed, with prejudice to refiling, as settled); Thompson v. NASA, 62 MSPR 336, 337 (1994) (initial decision vacated and the appeal dismissed as settled); cf. Tarel v. Dept. of Army, AT-1221-20-0409-W-1 (NP 9/26/2022) (honoring settlement provision that initial decision be vacated); Cline v. VA, PH-0752-19-0248-I-1 (NP 9/26/2022) (accepting settlement into record; noting the agreement specified an alternative process for the appellant to pursue in the event of a breach); Bowers v. Dept. of Army, 65 MSPR 404 (1994) (Table) (vacating the initial decision and dismissing the appeal as settled, but without entering the settlement into the record for enforcement: the settlement called for the appellant to withdraw his appeal; the parties did not want the Board to enforce the terms of the agreement).

If, after an appellant files a PFR, he notifies the Board that the case settled and he wishes to withdraw his appeal, the Board may leave the record open for receipt of the settlement agreement for purposes of enforcement, but the Board will not allow the appellant to then reactivate the PFR and his appeal, even if the appellant contends that the case was really not settled, unless there are "unusual circumstances such as misinformation or new and material evidence...." Gaither v. DOJ, 44 MSPR 221, 223 (1990). If a settlement is filed with the Board during its adjudication of a PFR, and if there is an issue of timeliness of the PFR, the Board appears inclined to accept the settlement and avoid the timeliness issue. See Harrington v. Dept. of Navy, 35 MSPR 312, 313 (1987). Should the settlement be submitted after the agency has filed a PFR, and should the appellant then assert that the agreement has been breached, the Board will accept the settlement and remand the dispute to the region for adjudication as an enforcement petition. See Mills v. VA, 66 MSPR 170, 172-73 (1995).

Parties should exercise some caution as they submit settlements to the Board during the PFR stage. If the parties desire that the Board accept an agreement for purposes of enforcement, this condition should be plainly stated as a precondition to the Board's acceptance of the agreement and dismissal of the underlying appeal and the PFR. In Krupp v. USPS, 66 MSPR 446, 447 (1995), the parties may have intended that their agreement result in dismissal of the appeal at the PFR stage only if the Board accepted the agreement, but the condition was unstated in the agreement. The Board, deciding there was a jurisdictional issue, dismissed the case on the basis of the settlement but did not enter the agreement into the record for purposes of enforcement:

After the filing of the petition for review, the parties submitted a settlement agreement dated January 23, 1995. The agreement provides, inter alia, for the withdrawal of this appeal.

Before dismissing a matter as settled, the Board must determine whether the parties have entered into a settlement agreement, understand its terms, and intend to have the agreement entered into the record for enforcement by the Board. See Mahoney v. USPS, 37 MSPR 146, 149 (1988). We find here that the parties have, in fact, entered into a settlement agreement, and that they understand its terms. We further find that, although the settlement agreement is silent on the matter of whether the parties intend to have the agreement entered into the record for enforcement purposes, the agreement cannot be so entered because there has been no finding that the Board has jurisdiction over the subject matter of this appeal--the appellant's reassignment. See Shaw v. Dept. of the Navy, 39 MSPR 586, 594 (1989).

Accordingly, we find that dismissal of the appeal with prejudice to re-filing is appropriate under these circumstances.

See McCarthy v. Dept. of Army, 69 MSPR 436, 437 (1996) (dismissing a PFR following settlement with the observation that "[w]e find that the parties do not, however, intend to have the agreement entered into the record for enforcement by the Board because they acknowledge that a question remains as to whether the Board has jurisdiction over the subject of this appeal."); Porchiazzo v. USPS, 67 MSPR 314, 315 (1995) (after the parties submitted a settlement agreement at the PFR stage, the Board dismissed an appeal with prejudice to its refiling; the Board could not accept the settlement agreement because the agreement did not state that the parties wanted the Board to enforce its terms; further, there was no finding that the Board had jurisdiction over the appeal).
