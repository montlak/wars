XVIII. REDUCTIONS IN FORCE

The decision to conduct a RIF is a management right under § 7106(a)(2)(A). DOD, Dept. of Army, Hq., Ft. Sam Houston and AFGE Local 2154, 8 FLRA 623, 624 (1982). For those interested in a summary of the operation or legal structure of a RIF, we recommend reading NRC v. FLRA, 895 F.2d 152, 156-57 (4th Cir. 1990). Another summary of RIF procedures and some of the negotiable aspects of RIFs appears in Dept. of Air Force, Scott AFB and NAGE Local R7-23, 35 FLRA 844 (1990).

FLRA determined that a union could not require negotiation of a proposal that required management to incorporate into its contract existing RIF regulations, deciding that negotiation over subjects concerning the exercise of management's rights is precluded, regardless of whether those subjects are addressed in regulations. NTEU and Dept. of Energy, 19 FLRA 224, 227 (1985) (Proposal 3); see NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Fin. Mgmt. Serv., 29 FLRA 422, 427 (1987) (permitting as negotiable Provision 5, that management follow RIF regulations and resolving as nonnegotiable section that defined RIF to include reclassification due to changes in duties); AFGE Local 1770 and Dept. of Army, Ft. Bragg Dependent Sch., 28 FLRA 493, 516 (1987) (Provision 7) (allowing negotiation of general definition of RIF). The case law that follows, however, does not support a complete preclusion of negotiation in the area of RIFs: to the contrary, many proposals are negotiable. FLRA approved as negotiable a proposal that management use RIF principles when separating or downgrading an employee through no fault of the employee. The proposal allowed management to define through its own procedures and regulations if and when employees will be downgraded, and the proposal had no effect on the agency's ability, through classification procedures, to determine when employees grades would have to be reduced for classification reasons. NAGE Local R7-23 and Dept. of Air Force, Hq. 375th Air Base Group, Scott AFB, 26 FLRA 916 (1987) (Proposal 2). Nothing barred a proposal that management engage in impact bargaining when conducting a transfer of function. See NAGE Local R1-203 and Dept. of Interior, Fish and Wildlife Serv., 55 FLRA 1081, 1083-84 (1999) (Proposal 2). AFT Indian Educators Fed. and Dept. of Interior, BIA, 63 FLRA 585 (2009), approved negotiability of an article extending RIF rights to a defined group of excepted service employees, unprotected by statute or regulation, when those employees' service contracts were threatened with nonrenewal for reasons of budget or program conditions.

A broadly-stated proposal that "The Employer will make other reasonable efforts, if appropriate and possible, in order to avoid a RIF, was negotiable, NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., 55 FLRA 1174, 1175-76 (1999) (Provision 1):

We construe the Agency's claim that Provision 1 would affect its "right to conduct a RIF" as an assertion that the provision would affect management's right to layoff employees under section 7106(a)(2)(A) of the Statute. Because the Union does not dispute here, and did not dispute before the Judge, that the provision affects management's right to layoff employees, we find that the provision affects that right. See American Federation of Government Employees, Council of GSA Locals, Council 236 and General Services Administration, 55 FLRA 449, 452 (1999).

With respect to whether the provision is an appropriate arrangement, the Agency does not dispute that it is an arrangement; the Agency argues only that the provision excessively interferes with management's rights. Determining whether a proposal or provision excessively interferes with management's rights is one aspect of resolving whether it is an appropriate arrangement. See National Association of Government Employees, Local R14-87 and Kansas Army National Guard, 21 FLRA 24, 29-33 (1986) (KANG). As relevant here, the Authority determines whether a provision is appropriate, or whether it is inappropriate because it excessively interferes with the relevant management right, by weighing the "competing practical needs of employees and managers." KANG, 21 FLRA at 31-32.

Provision 1 is substantially similar to the proposals in Congressional Research Service and the provision in POPA, cited by the Judge, which the Authority found did not excessively interfere with management's right to layoff employees. The proposals in Congressional Research Service required the Agency to "exhaust other methods of cutting costs prior to conducting a RIF whenever it was 'practicable' or 'possible' for the Agency to do so." Congressional Research Service, 25 FLRA at 308. The Authority found that the proposals did not excessively interfere with management's right because the benefit to employees outweighed the burden placed on management's exercise of its rights. In this respect, the Authority found that the proposals provided the agency with discretion to determine what means to use to avoid the RIF, and that the proposals would not prevent the agency from carrying out the RIF if other means were not practical or possible. The Authority reached the same conclusion with regard to the provision in POPA, which required the agency to "avoid RIF actions whenever possible by using attrition or other means." POPA, 41 FLRA at 843.

Consistent with the Authority's decisions in Congressional Research Service and POPA, we conclude that the benefit afforded by Provision I to employees, who would otherwise lose their jobs, outweighs the burden placed on management's exercise of its rights and, thus, that Provision 1 would not excessively interfere with management's right to layoff employees. Accordingly, the provision constitutes an appropriate arrangement under section 7106(b)(3) and is not contrary to law.

Proposals that effectively preclude agencies from reorganizing, by precluding them from determining whether their functions will be performed, excessively interfere with the right of the agency to determine its administrative and functional structure. See AFGE Local 2004 and DOD, DLA, New Cumberland, 56 FLRA 660, 661-62 (2000) (Proposal 6). Proposals advanced as negotiable arrangements for employees adversely affected by RIFs should be tailored to address the likely effects upon unit employees. AFGE Local 2004, supra, 56 FLRA at 662.
