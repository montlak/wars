13. Frequency of Tests; Exemption From Testing

An agency has the right to require random drug testing as part of its internal security program. Proposals to bar random testing altogether are incompatible with the right to determine internal security practices. NAGE Local R14-9 and Army Dugway Proving Ground, 30 FLRA 1083, 1086-87 (1988); see IAM and Dept. of Army, Corpus Christi Army Depot, 59 FLRA 830, 831 (2004) (Proposal 1, requiring abolition of random drug testing for unit employees was nonnegotiable infringement of right of agency to determine its internal security practices; union did not argue that proposal constituted negotiable procedure or arrangement). If the proposal bars any unit employees from being randomly tested for drug use in the future, the proposal is nonnegotiable irrespective of whether there are currently employees in the unit who are classified as critical. NFFE Local 405 and Army Aviation Sys. Command, 33 FLRA 604, 607-10 (1988); see NAGE, FUSE, Local R1-144 and Dept. of Navy, Naval Underwater Sys. Ctr., Newport, 42 FLRA 730, 741-43 (1991) (Proposal 3, that there be one-year increment between random tests for given employees, nonnegotiable); IFPTE Local 128 and Dept. of Interior, Bureau of Reclamation, 39 FLRA 1500, 1525-27, 1529-30 (1991) (Proposal 14: nonnegotiable to propose a delay in urinalysis until after employee enters rehabilitation); (Proposal 16: nonnegotiable that employees not be subjected to testing for year following earlier, negative result); see NTEU and Dept. of Energy, 41 FLRA 1241, 1260, 1265 (1991) (Proposal 2, demanding exemption from testing for "reasonable suspicion" of employees during rehabilitation, up to 30 days, nonnegotiable). Nonnegotiable was a proposal that "[e]mployees who are placed in the follow-up testing pool who are subject to regular unannounced testing as well as testing imposed by [the] Employee Assistance Program will not be tested more than 12 times per year under both plans combined." The limitation constituted too great an infringement on management's right to control internal security to qualify as a negotiable arrangement. AFSCME Local 3097 and DOJ, Justice Mgmt. Div., 42 FLRA 412, 455, 460 (1991) (Proposal 27).

AFGE Local 738 and Dept. of Army, Ft. Leavenworth, 38 FLRA 1203, 1204, 1206 (1990) (Proposal 1), declared nonnegotiable a proposal restricting the frequency of drug testing. Graphics Communications Int'l Union, Local 98-L and DOD, DMA, Hydrographic Topographic Command, 39 FLRA 437, 438, 444 (1991), also declared nonnegotiable two proposals that limited the frequency of drug testing. The first proposal precluded the agency from subjecting an employee to any further random tests during a calendar year when the employee tests negative on a random test. The second proposal precluded the agency from subjecting an employee to further random testing during a calendar year when the employee volunteered for a drug test and the result of the voluntary test was negative. The proposals were not appropriate arrangements because "the reduced effectiveness of the drug testing program which would result from the proposals outweighs the benefit to employees of not being subject to random testing for a portion of the year." The proposals interfered excessively with the right to determine internal security practices.

Indirect limitations on the frequency of tests were considered in IFPTE Local 89 and Dept. of Interior, Bureau of Reclamation, Grand Coulee Project Office, 48 FLRA 516, 522-23 (1993), FLRA ruling nonnegotiable Proposal II.B.4, requiring the employer to ensure that employees included in the random drug testing pool "are working under accurate and up-to-date position descriptions and performance plans." The proposal interfered with internal security practices because it precluded testing of employees whose position descriptions were not up to date. The proposal was not advanced as an arrangement. [Refer later in this chapter to "Rehabilitation."]

A different result was reached in AFSCME Local 3097 and DOJ, Justice Mgmt. Div., 42 FLRA 412, 522, 525 (1991), determining negotiable Proposal 66: "Employees scheduled for random testing shall not be subject to testing if that employee has been tested under 'accident' or 'reasonable suspicion testing' and the follow-up of such testing has not been completed":

Consistent with the preference expressed in the Executive Order and the FPM Letter for counseling and rehabilitation instead of termination for a single incident of drug use, and because the proposal would prohibit random testing for only a short time, we find that the impact of Proposal 66 on the Agency's right to determine its internal security practices is minimal. Against this minimal impact on management's rights stands the significant benefit to unit employees provided by protection from termination. On balance, we find that the benefit afforded employees by Proposal 66 outweighs the interference with management's right. We conclude, therefore, that the proposal does not excessively interfere with the Agency's right to determine its internal security practices. Accordingly, Proposal 66 constitutes a negotiable appropriate arrangement within the meaning of section 7106(b)(3) of the Statute.
