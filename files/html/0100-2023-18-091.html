6. Inadequate Representation

Lack of representation, or inadequate representation, at an oral reply is not harmful error; it is the appellant's responsibility properly to present the reply. This includes the obligation either to find adequate representation or to request a continuance or postponement of the oral reply until proper representation can be obtained. McLaughlin v. Dept. of Interior, 4 MSPR 70, 72, 4 MSPB 159 (1980). Although the employee has the right to representation at an oral reply session, the agency has no obligation to provide the representative. Hobson v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-582). The Board reversed a finding of harmful error made by an AJ who concluded that the appellant was placed at a disadvantage because he was unable to use the representative of his choice during an oral reply. It was not demonstrated that it was within the range of probability for the outcome to have differed if the employee had his choice of representative. Johnson v. FAA, 24 MSPR 475, 476 (1984). There was no error when the agency refused to recognize a union representative during the processing of a RIF. Representational rights under 5 USC 7513(b)(3) do not extend to RIFs that are processed pursuant to OPM regulations that make no provision for a right of representation before an agency. Washington v. Dept. of Navy, 44 MSPR 278, 281 (1990) (at the point of the representational request, the appellant was not affected by an adverse action, i.e., her position had been abolished through the application of RIF procedures and not through the application of the adverse action procedures of Chapter 75; OPM RIF regulations do not provide a right to representation before an agency). If the agency should have only recognized a representative with a written designation, there was no harmful error if the reply was pursued with that individual, a union representative, and the appellant present at a meeting with the deciding official. The agency reasonably believed that the union official who accompanied the appellant and reviewed the file was the appellant's representative. Hairston v. DOD, 835 Fed. Appx. 571 (Fed. Cir. 2020 NP) ("Putting aside the question of whether there is a substantive right to adequate representation in this context, Hairston has never disputed--and does not now dispute--the charges against him. That fact alone supports dismissal, and no representative, no matter how talented, could dispute that fact.").
