b. Entitlement to Hearing

The probationer's right to a hearing depends on nonfrivolous allegations of Board jurisdiction, that is, allegations of denial of the procedural due process required for probationary terminations based upon preappointment conditions. Under Munson v. DOJ, 55 MSPR 246, 251 n.2 (1992) (removal for alleged false statement and omission of information during a pre-employment interview), when the appellant did not present allegations of failure to receive procedural protections, she was not entitled to a hearing on jurisdiction; the appeal was dismissed for lack of jurisdiction. Khalsa v. Dept. of Treasury, 12 MSPR 408, 410 (1982), concluded that a hearing should be provided to an individual making a nonfrivolous claim of termination for preappointment reasons--a prior arrest record and noncompliance with tax laws--if those were not the bases stated in the termination letter. The AJ was then to determine if the incorrect citation of reasons was harmful error.

Robinson v. DOJ, 48 MSPR 141, 144 (1991), affirmed the jurisdictional dismissal of an appeal by a probationer who alleged that bankruptcy proceedings that commenced prior to his appointment constituted the real but unstated reason for his dismissal. Rather than allowing the appellant a hearing, the Board evaluated the evidence at hand and then discounted Robinson's claim because it was not raised earlier with his agency when he was terminated. Additionally, the agency's documentary submissions showed that it terminated Robinson for failure to follow an order and that, since the agency hired the appellant "while knowing of his bankruptcy, it is improbable that it would have taken action to remove him on that basis almost a year later." The Board essentially weighed the allegation and the evidence to determine whether a hearing was justified.

What the Board looks for is a combination of an allegation and supporting evidence, although it states that nonfrivolous allegations alone lead to a hearing, Graham v. DOJ, 50 MSPR 285, 288 (1991):

A probationary employee bears the burden of establishing Board jurisdiction over his appeal by the preponderance of the evidence.... In Copeland v. Federal Aviation Administration, 7 MSPR 190, 191-92 (1981), the Board found that a hearing was warranted in the appeal of a probationary employee who was terminated for pre-appointment reasons where the employee articulated sufficient nonfrivolous circumstances for finding a possible violation of the procedural requirements of § 315.805. Thus, a Board jurisdictional hearing is warranted in an appeal by a probationary employee who has been terminated for alleged pre-appointment reasons only if he makes a nonfrivolous allegation that the procedures were not in accordance with § 315.805....

In Stokes v. Federal Aviation Administration, 761 F.2d 682, 685-86 (Fed. Cir. 1985), the court held that, to be entitled to a jurisdictional hearing, the employee must introduce an allegation of jurisdiction which is supported by "factual assertions indicating that the allegation is not a pro-forma pleading." Absent such factual assertions, the employee is not entitled to a hearing to establish jurisdiction over his appeal....

In this case, the appellant has not made a nonfrivolous assertion of Board jurisdiction under Stokes. The Board agrees with and adopts the administrative judge's finding that the appellant's allegation of procedural error is facially nonmeritorious. Thus, the appellant is not entitled to a jurisdictional hearing....

A hearing was denied in Lindsay v. Dept. of Treasury, SF-0752-10-0771-I-1 (NP 6/14/2011) (termination during probation for preappointment condition):

...The Board has required that there be some evidentiary support for the appellant's allegations in order to justify a jurisdictional hearing. Riojas v. U.S. Postal Service, 88 M.S.P.R. 230, ¶ 13 (2001) (citing Briscoe v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 55 F.3d 1571, 1573 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (before an appellant is entitled to a hearing, the Board may request sufficient evidence to determine if there is support for what otherwise might be bald allegations); Rhodes v. Department of Commerce, 86 M.S.P.R. 476, ¶ 15 (2000); Burrell v. Environmental Protection Agency, 81 M.S.P.R. 427, ¶ 15 (1999). Here, there is no specific factual information supporting the appellant's assertion that the agency failed to comply with the procedural requirements of 5 C.F.R. § 315.805 in terminating his appointment. Moreover, in his response to the administrative judge's July 28, 2010 order, the appellant "concede[s] that the agency did follow the procedures of providing [him] with a proposal letter and a chance to respond...." Therefore, the administrative judge correctly found that the appellant failed to nonfrivolously allege facts that could establish Board jurisdiction over this appeal on any basis.
