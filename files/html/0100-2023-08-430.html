e. Scope of Administrative Grievance Procedure

The Board invalidated a provision of the (now defunct) FPM that expanded upon an exclusion from the grievance process; the FPM provision denied grievance coverage to protests about failure to secure noncompetitive promotions when the exclusion in the regulation [formerly at 5 CFR 771.206(c)(1)(iii) (1992)] denied coverage only to nonselection for competitive promotion from a group of properly ranked and certified candidates. OPM should have gone through the processes required by the Administrative Procedure Act to amend its regulation governing grievances rather than using the FPM to accomplish that result. Joseph v. Devine, 19 MSPR 66, 69 (1984), explained the prohibited personnel practice that resulted from OPM's action:

Our inquiry, however, cannot end with the determination that the challenged rule is inconsistent with the underlying regulation. We must also decide if the challenged rule's implementation would require the commission of one of the prohibited personnel practices set forth in 5 USC 2302. In this case, the relevant subsection of the prohibited personnel practice provisions is 5 USC 2302(b)(11) [now 2302(b)(12)], which prohibits an agency from, inter alia, failing to take a personnel action, as defined in 5 USC 2302(a), if that failure is in violation of a law, rule or regulation implementing one of the merit system principles enumerated in 5 USC 2301(b).

The challenged rule, which precludes agencies from processing grievances concerning noncompetitive promotion decisions, does involve a personnel action. The decision to promote or not to promote an employee in a career ladder classification series is "a decision concerning pay" and, as such, is included within the definition, in 5 USC 2302, of personnel actions. 5 USC 2302(a)(2)(A)(ix).

In addition, the challenged rule allows that decision to be made in a fashion which violates regulations implementing a merit system principle. The regulations which the rule violates are contained in 5 CFR Part 771. Those regulations establish the mandatory coverage of agency grievance systems and provide that management decisions, not otherwise excluded by 5 CFR 771.206, shall be grievable. By improperly excluding noncompetitive promotion decisions from that coverage, the challenged rule violates those grievance system regulations.

Moreover, those regulations implement one of the merit system principles which constitute the theoretical underpinning of the entire federal personnel management system. By unlawfully limiting grievable matters, the challenged rule violates a regulatory scheme which serves to ensure the effectuation of the merit system principle that employees "should receive fair and equitable treatment in all aspects of personnel management." Therefore, the challenged rule can be invalidated under 5 USC 1205(e).
