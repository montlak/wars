broida mspb Chapter 16: Settlement, Remedies, and Enforcement Chapter 16: Settlement, Remedies, and Enforcement, Subchapter 03: Enforcement, Section A: Enforcement of MSPB Decision, Subsection 07: Compelling Reason for Reassignment; Detail; Different Duties, Part a: Replacement Employee
a. Replacement Employee

Williams v. DHHS, 32 MSPR 259, 261-62 (1987), precluded a reassignment justified, from the agency's perspective, when the appellant's position was filled prior to his return:

The only reasons provided by the agency for failing to reinstate the appellant to her former position are: 1) the appellant's former position has been filled by another employee; 2) it would be unduly disruptive to return the appellant to her former position because its current incumbent has developed extensive knowledge of the staff and organization of the agency which is valuable for instantaneous communication in food or drug crises; and 3) the regional organization would be disrupted if the administrative functions performed by the current incumbent were transferred again within such a brief period of time. We find that these concerns do not meet the standard of a strong overriding interest or a compelling reason for failing to reinstate the appellant in her former position.

The fact that the agency filled the appellant's former position does not establish a compelling reason for failing to reinstate the appellant.... Moreover, the agency has presented no evidence to substantiate its assertions that returning the appellant to her former position would unduly disrupt the agency's work. The administrative judge simply repeated these assertions, made in the agency's response to the appellant's petition for enforcement, in finding that the agency's reasons for not reinstating the appellant were valid. Statements made in such a response, however, are not evidence.... Furthermore, the agency has presented no evidence to show that the appellant, given her former occupancy of the position, is not acquainted with the staff and organization of the agency and has not handled similar situations in the past. Finally, it is to be expected that some disruption will occur whenever an incumbent of a position is changed. This alone is not sufficient to prevent the reinstatement of an employee who has been wrongfully removed from her position.

Williams was quoted with approval in Taylor v. Dept. of Treasury, 43 MSPR 221, 226 (1990), declining again to allow considerations of displacement to control an agency's compliance obligations. Grimaldi v. Dept. of Interior, 60 MSPR 49, 52-53 (1993), also eschewed displacement of another employee as a justification for reassignment. Grimaldi added that an agency could not justify reassigning an employee only because the employee is absent for a period as a result of a work-related illness or accident; to justify reassignment the agency must have stronger evidence than speculation that the employee would not return to the position. See Jones v. Dept. of Army, 73 MSPR 183, 186-87 (1997) ("The fact that another employee occupies the position to which the appellant should have been restored does not establish a compelling reason for failing to restore the appellant"; "If the agency decided not to displace the employees who had been assigned to the appellants' positions as a result of the improper demotions, it was those employees, not the appellants, who occupied 'overstrength' positions."); Griffin v. Dept. of Army, 68 MSPR 240, 243 (1995) ("[a]n agency may not refuse to reinstate a prevailing appellant to his or her position merely because it has hired a replacement employee."); Clemons v. Smithsonian Inst., 50 MSPR 74, 81 (1991) (that the agency filled the appellant's former position does not establish a compelling reason for failing to reinstate the appellant to a position on the night shift); Cavanagh v. USPS, 65 MSPR 232, 242 (1994) ("The fact that the agency filled the appellant's former position does not establish a compelling reason for failing to reinstate the appellant."; remanding to determine proper shift assignment); Hoover v. Dept. of Navy, 57 MSPR 545, 556 (1993) (a RIF appeal: "[t]he fact that an agency has filled the position to which the appellant should have been restored does not establish a compelling reason for failing to reinstate the appellant[.]").
