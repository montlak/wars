broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 19: Critical Elements and Performance Standards, Section C: Broad Criteria, Subsection 07: Factors Beyond Control of Employees, Part b: Nonnegotiable Provisions
b. Nonnegotiable Provisions

A proposal that elements include only work within employees' control was nonnegotiable, POPA and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 47 FLRA 10, 27-28 (1993) (Provision 7):

The first part of sentence 2 in Provision 7 requires that performance elements include only those aspects of work over which employees have control. Provisions which require management to adjust or change performance expectations in specified circumstances dictate the content of performance standards and, thereby, directly interfere with management's rights to direct employees and assign work. NAFEC, 36 FLRA at 845. The first part of sentence 2, by limiting the content of critical elements to those aspects of work over which employees have control, restricts the Agency's authority to determine the content of employees' critical elements. Therefore, we conclude that the first part of sentence 2 directly interferes with management's rights to direct employees and assign work under section 7106(a)(2)(A) and (B) of the Statute. See, for example, id. at 847-48 (provision requiring management to make allowances for factors beyond the control of an employee in evaluating performance held to require management to change or adjust its performance expectations).

The Union does not assert that the first part of the second sentence constitutes an appropriate arrangement under section 7106(b)(3) for employees adversely affected by the exercise of a management right. In this regard, the first part of the second sentence is distinguishable from a proposal, requiring that management make allowances for factors beyond employees' control in applying performance standards, which we recently determined was an appropriate arrangement in National Federation of Federal Employees, Local 1974 and U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs, Regional Office, Portland, Oregon, 46 FLRA 1170 (1993).... The first part of the second sentence bars inclusion of aspects of work beyond employees' control in performance standards. Even assuming, without deciding, that the first part of the second sentence is an arrangement for adversely affected employees, the Union has not explained how preclusion of performance standards which, for example, may be aimed at assessing employees' ability to cope with exceptional situations would not excessively interfere with management's rights to direct employees and assign work. We will consider whether a provision constitutes an appropriate arrangement when it is not argued in order to avoid an anomalous result. However, where, as here, the Union has not created a record that would permit us to determine whether the first part of the second sentence constitutes an appropriate arrangement, we will not examine that issue.

In NAGE Local R1-144, FUSE and Dept. of Navy, Naval Underwater Sys. Ctr., Newport, 38 FLRA 456, 467, 470-71 (1990) (Proposal 4), a proposal was nonnegotiable that "[n]o employee shall be held responsible for the work performed by other employees, supervisors, managers, contracts, etc., [over] which they have no control":

...In Food and Nutrition Service, the Authority distinguished between proposals that require management merely to consider matters outside the control of the employee in applying performance standards and those that require that employees be absolved of accountability and responsibility for their work performance in situations where that performance has been affected by matters outside the employee's control. Proposals in the former category concern the application of performance standards, do not directly interfere with management's rights, and are generally negotiable.... Proposals in the latter category directly interfere with management's rights to determine the content of performance and elements and to evaluate employees against them.... Because sentence three is not limited to identifying circumstances that management should merely consider when evaluating employees, but would prevent management from holding employees accountable for their work performance in identified circumstances, it directly interferes with management's rights to direct employees and assign work....

A provision was not negotiable that precluded employees from being penalized under performance standards due to the destruction of their work materials for reasons outside their control. Alternative sources of information were available to employees when their work product was destroyed. AFGE Local 32 and OPM, 26 FLRA 452, 455 (1987) ("[U]nder the proposal here, any alternative assignment made because of the destruction of work materials arguably could be construed as a penalty. In such circumstances, the proposal would prevent the Agency from considering an employee's substandard performance of the alternative assignment in evaluating the employee's overall performance."). Nonnegotiable were proposals that describe specific situations outside the control of the employees so as to insulate employees from performance actions or other penalties, essentially permitting arbitral review of the content of performance standards. AFGE Local 32 and OPM, 19 FLRA 93, 93-95 (1985); see AFGE Local 32 and OPM, 28 FLRA 714 (1987) (Proposal 10 nonnegotiable, limiting responsibility for employees caught up in backlog and requiring consideration for relative difficulty of work under review); see NTEU and DHHS, Office of Hearings and Appeals, 44 FLRA 293, 294, 296-99 (1992) (nonnegotiable: arrangements that time be nonchargeable that involved travel between old and new offices to perform certain assigned duties and excusing employees from adverse affects of delays in case processing related to office relocation); IAM and Dept. of Treasury, Bureau of Engraving & Printing, 33 FLRA 711, 716-18 (1988) (Proposal 2: nonnegotiable to preclude management from considering approved sick leave requests in employee's performance appraisal); NTEU Chap. 237 and USDA, Food and Nutrition Serv., 32 FLRA 62, 64-65 (1988) (nonnegotiable: portion of proposal that management not hold employees responsible for meeting levels of performance when their performance is affected by factors beyond their control; proposal precluded management from holding employees accountable when their performance is affected by factors outside their control: "because Provision 1 concerns the content of performance standards and the job requirements of employees in relation to matters beyond an employee's control, the provision does not concern an arrangement for adversely affected employees").

The union could not require negotiations on a proposal requiring management to include in a performance standard a phrase that it would take into account the failure to distribute work equitably. Measures of timeliness and quantity of employee work were within the control of management. AFGE Local 32 and OPM, 17 FLRA 326, 327 (1985). Proposals were nonnegotiable that precluded the agency from evaluating employees on the basis of the size of their backlogs. AFGE, Nat'l Council of Social Security Payment Ctr. Locals and SSA, Office of Program Serv. Ctrs., 7 FLRA 818, 821-22 (1982). Another nonnegotiable proposal required management to develop performance standards that take into consideration the relative difficulty of the work subject to review. AFGE Local 32 and OPM, 28 FLRA 714, 718-21 (1987); see POPA and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 56 FLRA 69, 73-85 (2000) (considering 11 proposals pertaining to "special examining time" and "nonexamining time" that could or could not be charged against productivity standards of employees when defined conditions were met); POPA and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 39 FLRA 783, 818, 820-21 (1991) (Proposal 15 declared nonnegotiable: provision would have given employee choice of when unfamiliar work would be evaluated or what adjustments would be made for evaluation of that work); POPA and PTO, 25 FLRA 384, 408-09 (1987) (considering proposal involving the implementation of performance standard of production goal achievement for patent examiners).

A proposal interfered with the right of management to establish performance standards mandating that time limits for certain types of work, e.g., claims development, be adjusted to take into consideration added research needed to ensure compliance with newly issued regulations. NFFE, Council of Consol. SSA Locals and DHHS, SSA, 17 FLRA 657, 658-59 (1985). Proposals were nonnegotiable that precluded the agency from considering course enrollment statistics in appraising an instructor's performance. Whether enrollment was a factor relevant to performance was immaterial to the negotiability of the proposal. Although FLRA approved proposals setting forth general, nonquantitative standards, e.g., a proposal was acceptable that required allowances for factors beyond the control of employees, FLRA had not approved proposals setting specific limitations on management's authority to identify critical elements or establish standards. NFFE and Haskell Indian Junior College, Bureau of Indian Affairs, Dept. of Interior, Lawrence, 22 FLRA 539, 541-43 (1986); see POPA and PTO, 25 FLRA 384, 391-95 (1987) (Sections 3.G and 3.J, essentially calling for validation of performance standards or units of measurement employed by those standards, were nonnegotiable; they constituted contractual limitations on establishment of performance standards).
