3. Withdrawal of Appeal

Appellants may initiate Board appeals; they may withdraw them. The decisions that follow result from the efforts by appellants to reinstate appeals earlier withdrawn. Because the withdrawal of an appeal is a matter of jurisdictional consequence, and because the Board's jurisdiction is always at issue, the existence or legitimacy of the withdrawal of the appeal may be raised at any time during a proceeding, or the Board may raise that issue on its own. See DeLoach v. Dept. of Air Force, 108 MSPR 485, 2008 MSPB 78 (2008).

Unconditional withdrawal of an appeal is an act of finality that removes the appeal from Board jurisdiction, Kravitz v. OPM, 75 MSPR 44, 46-48 (1997):

The issue now before us is whether an appellant may withdraw her appeal before the Board, regardless of the agency's agreement, acquiescence, or objection, prior to issuance of a final Board order. For the reasons discussed below, we conclude that, generally speaking, it is the appellant's right to withdraw her appeal regardless of the agency's view on the matter.

The appellant in this case filed her Board appeal under 5 USC 8347(d)(1) which provides that "an administrative action or order [of OPM] affecting the [civil service retirement] rights or interests of an individual or of the United States...may be appealed to the Merit Systems Protection Board under procedures prescribed by the Board." We are aware of no statutory provision stating that, once an individual elects to exercise her statutory right to appeal an OPM action or order to the Board, she may not subsequently elect to withdraw that appeal unilaterally; and OPM has not asserted that any such provision exists. Further, nothing in the Board's regulations provides that an appellant may not withdraw her appeal at any time prior to the issuance of the Board's final order.

In cases involving Special Counsel complaints seeking disciplinary action against Federal employees for allegedly committing prohibited personnel practices, and other agency's complaints seeking Board approval of adverse personnel actions against administrative law judges, the Board has held that, in considering a complainant's motion for a voluntary dismissal, it must consider primarily the interests of the respondents. Special Counsel v. Parra, 49 MSPR 123, 125 (1991); National Labor Relations Board v. Beddow, 47 MSPR 103, 105 (1991); Social Security Administration v. Abell, 47 MSPR 98, 101 (1991). The Board will grant such a motion unless the non-moving party would suffer a clear legal prejudice. Special Counsel v. Parra, 49 MSPR at 125; National Labor Relations Board v. Beddow, 47 MSPR at 105; Social Security Administration v. Abell, 47 MSPR at 101. Here, the agency has not identified how it would be prejudiced by the Board's dismissing this appeal pursuant to her request to withdraw it, and we can discern no possible prejudice.[3]

[3] It is difficult to foresee a circumstance in which an appellant's decision to withdraw a Board appeal with prejudice to refiling could prejudice an agency. An agency claiming such prejudice has a difficult burden.

In its decisions in Parra, Beddow, and Abell, the Board referred to Rule 41(a)(2), Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, regarding the voluntary dismissal of an action. Special Counsel v. Parra, 49 MSPR at 125; National Labor Relations Board v. Beddow, 47 MSPR at 105, Social Security Administration v. Abell, 47 MSPR at 100; Fed.R.Civ.P. 41(a)(2). That rule affords a plaintiff the right to dismiss an action "upon an order of the court and upon such terms and conditions as the court deems proper." Fed.R.Civ.P. 41(a)(2). Although Rule 41(a)(2) ordinarily applies to dismissals without prejudice, it specifically applies to dismissals with prejudice as well. Nothing in Rule 41(a)(2) requires that the opposing parties agree to the dismissal of an action for a court to act. Id.

The intent of the "terms and conditions" clause in Rule 41(a)(2) is to protect a defendant from any prejudice or inconvenience that might result from the plaintiff's voluntary dismissal, and attorney fees are commonly awarded to the defendant as a "term and condition." See, e.g., Taragan v. Eli Lilly & Co., 838 F.2d 1337, 1340 (D.C. Cir. 1988); GAF Corp. v. Transamerica Ins. Co., 665 F.2d 364, 367 (D.C. Cir. 1981); LeCompte v. Mr. Chip, Inc., 528 F.2d 601, 603 (5th Cir. 1976); see generally 9 Wright & Miller, Federal Practice and Procedure §§ 2364-2367 (1994). The Board, however, lacks the authority to award attorney fees to an agency. Lewis v. Dept. of the Army, 31 MSPR 476, 478 (1986).

Although the Board is not bound by the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, we may look to them for guidance. See Hebert v. Department of the Navy, 57 MSPR 68, 72 (1993); Special Counsel v. Parra, 49 MSPR at 125. Here, we believe that Rule 41(a)(2) supports the holding that the Board may dismiss an appeal based solely on an appellant's request, provided that the opposing party would not be prejudiced. As noted above, the agency here has not identified how it would be prejudiced by the Board's dismissing the appeal pursuant to the appellant's request to withdraw it, and we can discern no possible prejudice.

The "with prejudice" is implicitly defined in Sanders v. DHS, DE-0752-17-0238-I-1 (NP 7/25/2022): "we find that dismissing the appeal 'with prejudice to refiling' (i.e., the parties normally may not refile this appeal) is appropriate under these circumstances." The dismissal with prejudice resulting from withdrawal of an appeal has res judicata effect, McGarry v. VA, DE-1221-15-0415-W-1 (NP 9/9/2016):

The agency removed the appellant from his position as Chief of Veterans Affairs Police Services in Grand Junction, Colorado, effective July 8, 2013. He filed a timely Board appeal challenging that removal on July 26, 2013. McGarry v. Department of Veterans Affairs, MSPB Docket No. DE-0752-13-0762-I-1. That appeal was dismissed without prejudice at the appellant's request and redocketed on September 16, 2014. McGarry v. Department of Veterans Affairs, MSPB Docket No. DE-0752-13-0762-I-2. The administrative judge dismissed that appeal, stating as follows:

[T]he appellant's counsel indicated that the appellant had authorized him to withdraw this appeal as the appellant no longer wished to pursue it.... I then accepted the withdrawal as knowingly and freely made after satisfying myself that the appellant and his counsel understood that the withdrawal would result in my dismissing the appeal with prejudice to refiling.

DECISION

The appeal is DISMISSED.

This initial decision became the Board's final decision, as neither party filed a petition for review.

In the instant IRA appeal, filed in May 2015, the appellant asserted that his July 2013 removal constituted retaliation for whistleblowing disclosures he allegedly made in 2011. The agency filed a motion to dismiss the appeal arguing, among other things, that the appeal should be barred under the doctrine of res judicata. The administrative judge allowed the appellant an opportunity to respond to the motion to dismiss, and the appellant did so.

In an initial decision, the administrative judge found that the IRA appeal was barred under the doctrine of res judicata, concluding that all three of the elements were satisfied for the application of that doctrine: (1) the prior judgment was rendered by a forum with competent jurisdiction (the MSPB); (2) the prior decision was a final decision on the merits; and (3) the same cause of action (the appellant's removal) and the same parties were involved in both cases. See Peartree v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 M.S.P.R. 332, 337 (1995). The administrative judge noted that res judicata bars not only claims that a party raised, but also claims that the party could have raised in the earlier action. Peartree, 66 M.S.P.R. at 337. Here, the appellant could have raised, but did not, the affirmative defense of whistleblower retaliation in the adverse action appeal.

The appellant asserts in his petition for review that the administrative judge "did not formally designate that [the adverse action appeal] was dismissed 'with prejudice' and Appellant did not understand that the proceeding was so dismissed thereby precluding this appeal. The earlier order simply pronounces the Appeal was 'dismissed.'" This argument is disingenuous. Although the administrative judge did not repeat the phrase "with prejudice" in the final sentence of his initial decision, he expressly stated that he accepted the withdrawal only after assuring himself "that the appellant and his counsel understood that the withdrawal would result in my dismissing the appeal with prejudice to refiling." The Board has found that a voluntary dismissal with prejudice does constitute a decision on the merits, barring relitigation under the doctrine of res judicata. Brown v. Department of the Navy, 102 M.S.P.R. 377, ¶ 10 (2006); Cavanagh v. U.S. Postal Service, 44 M.S.P.R. 485, 489 (1990). Accordingly, we conclude that the administrative judge properly determined that this appeal was barred by the application of the doctrine of res judicata.

Under NLRB v. Beddow, 47 MSPR 103, 105 (1991), an ALJ furlough case, a motion for voluntary dismissal will be granted unless the respondent will suffer clear legal prejudice, but the nature of the case--a furlough complaint against ALJs--implied the ability of the agency to reinitiate the case if necessity arose. In the ordinary appeal, the appellant is master of the appeal. See Felton v. USPS, CH-0353-13-0365-I-1 (NP 9/13/2013) ("However, the Board has held that it is the appellant's right to unilaterally withdraw her appeal at any time prior to the issuance of the Board's final order regardless of the agency's view on the matter. Kravitz v. Office of Personnel Management, 75 M.S.P.R. 44, 46 (1997). Further, it is difficult to foresee a circumstance in which an appellant's withdrawal of a Board appeal with prejudice to re-filing could prejudice the agency. Id. at 47 n.3."); cf. Whetten-Hernandez v. VA, DE-3443-16-0457-I-1 (NP 4/25/2023) (appellant's withdrawal of appeal was final despite agency's contention in a PFR that the appeal should have been dismissed for lack of jurisdiction; MSPB may dismiss an appeal as withdrawn despite agency objections).

The dismissal of an appeal is a matter of adjudicatory formality. "Where the presiding official is requested to dismiss an appeal on motion and with the consent of the parties, and she determines that dismissal is appropriate, an initial decision should be issued under 5 CFR § 1201.111 containing findings of fact and conclusions, as well as the reasons or bases therefor, in support of the decision to dismiss." Curtis v. EEOC, 3 MSPR 481, 482, 4 MSPB 4 (1980). If the AJ bases a dismissal on withdrawal of an appeal communicated to the AJ through a telephone conference, it is best that the AJ to advise the parties by memorandum of the nature of the conference before dismissing the appeal. See Lopez v. Dept. of Interior, 94 MSPR 393, 395 ¶ 5 (2003) ("When an appellant indicates during a teleconference that he would like to withdraw his appeal, the administrative judge should memorialize that fact for the record."); cf. Hufenbach v. MSPB, 250 Fed. Appx. 978, 979 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP) ("She does not state that she was misinformed as to the withdrawal; to the contrary, she was told that oral withdrawal would not suffice."). Since conference summaries ordinarily provide a few days for either side to offer corrections or objections, the parties can then advise the AJ of any error in the AJ's conclusions about the parties' statements, and the parties can avoid the situation in Krisovitch v. USPS, 46 MSPR 611, 614 (1991), when the appellant's PFR brought into dispute the communications to the AJ and the Board was required to remand the case to the AJ to determine if the appellant agreed to divestiture of jurisdiction. See Appell v. USPS, 69 MSPR 363, 365 (1996) (appeal was remanded for adjudication when the appellant alleged an error in the AJ's conference summary noting that the appellant withdrew his appeal).

The AJ should ensure that the appellant's request to withdraw the appeal is unequivocal. In Walker v. USPS, 86 MSPR 1, 2-3 ¶¶ 2, 6 (2000), the AJ improperly created a constructive withdrawal of an appeal based on the appellant's failure to deny the agency's representation that the appellant desired to withdraw the appeal:

The appellant filed this appeal pro se, asserting that the agency improperly removed him from his Modified Letter Carrier position effective August 3, 1999.... In an October 28, 1999 show cause order, the AJ stated that he was "advised on October 25, 1999, by the agency representative, that the appellant had notified the agency that date that it was his intention that his appeal be withdrawn." The AJ further stated that, "[b]ecause it appears that it is the appellant's intention that this appeal be withdrawn, he is hereby notified that his appeal will be dismissed unless he is able to show that such action should not occur," and that such a showing must be in writing and must be submitted by November 5, 1999. The appellant did not respond to this order. On November 19, 1999, the AJ issued the initial decision dismissing the appeal as withdrawn.

...

The only indication in the record that the appellant withdrew his appeal is the AJ's show cause order relating the agency representative's hearsay statement that the appellant expressed "his intention that his appeal be withdrawn." See Appell v. U.S. Postal Service, 69 MSPR 363, 365 (1996); Hopkins v. Office of Personnel Management, 67 MSPR 289, 291 (1995). There is no evidence in the record that the appellant actually withdrew his appeal. See id.; see also Amante v. Department of the Army, 77 MSPR 636, 638 (1998); Phillips v. Department of the Air Force, 71 MSPR 381, 383 (1996). The pro se appellant stated on his appeal form that he has a "mental disability,"... It...appears that the appellant was confused about the appeal process. See Ramos, 82 MSPR 65 ¶ 7; Amante, 77 MSPR at 638. Under these particular circumstances, we find that the record does not show that the appellant intended to withdraw his appeal. See Ramos, 82 MSPR 65 ¶ 7.

See Frederick-Bey v. DOD, DC-0752-11-0799-I-1 (NP 6/15/2012) ("The administrative judge explicitly advised the appellant several times that his withdrawal was final and that the Board will not reinstate the appeal once it is withdrawn. Nevertheless, the appellant unequivocally withdrew his appeal and walked out of the hearing. Thus, the administrative judge did not err in dismissing the appeal as withdrawn. Lincoln v. U.S. Postal Service, 113 M.S.P.R. 486, ¶ 8 (2010). Furthermore, because the appeal was properly dismissed as withdrawn, the Board need not consider the appellant's claim that the administrative judge was biased and that he committed procedural error."), aff'd, Frederick-Bey v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2013 NP No. 2012-3165) (withdrawal of appeal was construed from the appellant's departure from the hearing after the AJ told him that his appeal would be deemed withdrawn if he left).

The Board remanded a case to the AJ after the appeal was dismissed as withdrawn by the appellant based on a settlement reached in a grievance proceeding. There was no record evidence that the appellant actually withdrew her appeal and nothing to show that the AJ made any attempt to contact the appellant to determine if she was withdrawing her appeal based on the settlement. The proper approach would have been to dismiss the appeal as settled, but the record was unclear on that process too, since the AJ did not ascertain whether the parties' agreement was reached knowingly, voluntarily, and whether the parties desired Board enforcement of the agreement. Cason v. Dept. of Army, 118 MSPR 58, 2012 MSPB 57 (2012); see Neely v. USPS, CH-0752-12-0539-I-1 (NP 7/11/2013) ("The Board's decisions recognize a distinction between the settlement and the withdrawal of an initial appeal. See Cason v. Department of the Army, 118 M.S.P.R. 58, ¶¶ 5-7 (2012). Whereas the settlement of an initial appeal requires the administrative judge to make findings concerning the Board's jurisdiction and the legality of the settlement agreement on its face, id., ¶ 7, the withdrawal of an appeal, which is an act of finality, need only be expressed in clear, unequivocal, and decisive terms, id., ¶ 5.").

The Board directed processing of an appeal withdrawn by an appellant whose English was not strong, Ramos v. OPM, 82 MSPR 65, 67 ¶¶ 6-7 (1999):

In the recorded portion of the telephonic conference, in which OPM's representative participated, the administrative judge stated as follows: "During the course of this conference today, I explained in some detail the basis for OPM's reconsideration decision to the appellant, Mr. Ramos, and he came to the conclusion that it wasn't quite as unreasonable as he had thought and he also decided that he wanted to withdraw his appeal. Isn't that correct, Mr. Ramos?" The appellant responded: "Yea." The administrative judge then stated: "And Mr. Ramos, aren't you withdrawing this appeal voluntarily?" The appellant again responded: "Yea." The administrative judge then stated that he would be issuing an initial decision dismissing the appeal.

We find that the appellant's mere acquiescence in statements made by the administrative judge does not clearly show that he intended to withdraw his petition for appeal. The record below and the appellant's petition for review indicate that he is confused because he believes that he "won" after the first initial decision remanding his appeal to OPM for adjudication on the merits was issued. Moreover, the appellant is pro se and he admittedly cannot fully comprehend English. In addition, he filed his petition for review within a week after the initial decision was issued. Under these particular circumstances, we find that the appellant did not intend to withdraw his petition for appeal. See Amante v. Department of the Army, 77 MSPR 636, 638 (1998).

An implied withdrawal of an appeal was set aside, Wooten v. OPM, 86 MSPR 113, 115-16 ¶¶ 6-7 (2000), as a result of miscommunication between the appellant and the AJ:

Here, contrary to the implication in the initial decision, the appellant did not withdraw his appeal in writing. Moreover, the tape of the status call indicates that the appellant simply agreed to statements made by the administrative judge, as follows:

Administrative Judge (AJ): During the status conference call held today on June 5 [sic], 1999, the appellant indicated that he wished to withdraw his appeal before the Board and that he would be in turn submitting documentation to the Office of Personnel Management to support his qualifications for the payment of the unpaid annuity via the provision under 5 USC § 8342(c) under the sixth provision. Is that correct, Mr. Wooten?

Appellant: Yes, your honor.

AJ: And does the agency have any objections to that?

Agency: No.

AJ: OK, so the appeal will be dismissed as withdrawn.

Appellant: Thank you, your honor.

We have previously found that an appellant's mere acquiescence in statements made by an administrative judge is not the kind of clear and unequivocal statement necessary to effect a withdrawal. See Ramos, 82 MSPR 65 ¶ 7. Further, the petition for review indicates that the appellant continues to have questions as to what was supposed to happen to his appeal. Given this confusion, and in consideration of the appellant's pro se status, we find that he did not intend to withdraw his petition for appeal with prejudice to its ultimate resolution by the Board. See id.; Amante, 77 MSPR at 638. We note that there is no indication in the record that OPM has taken any additional action on the appellant's claim in the ensuing months.

See Lojewski v. USPS, AT-0353-16-0069-I-1 (NP 10/11/2016) ("Although the appellant's counsel filed a pleading stating that the appellant anticipated withdrawing her appeal, the record does not reflect that the appellant took any affirmative action to withdraw the appeal or made any affirmative representations that she was withdrawing the appeal. See Hopkins v. Office of Personnel Management, 67 M.S.P.R. 289, 291 (1995) (finding that an administrative judge erred in dismissing an appeal as withdrawn when the only evidence in the record regarding the appellant's withdrawal of her appeal was a memorandum reflecting that the appellant's representative indicated during a teleconference that he was considering withdrawing the appeal)[.] ").

An appellant who called and left a message at a Board office stating that he wished to "cancel" an appeal, and asking that the Board call him, did not demonstrate an unequivocal desire to withdraw the case with prejudice to its refiling, Carter v. Dept. of Navy, 87 MSPR 373, 376 ¶ 6 (2000):

...The administrative judge, by interpreting the word "cancel" in the appellant's telephone message to be equivalent to "withdraw," apparently inferred that the appellant intended to forfeit forever his right to contest his removal before the Board. However, in Board parlance there is no special meaning attached to the word "cancel." Even if there were, there is no indication of what the appellant meant. There also is no indication that the appellant understood that if he withdrew his appeal he could never refile it....

The Board considers the state of the record when it determines if the appellant's withdrawal of an appeal was through clear, unequivocal, and decisive action, Spencer v. R.R. Retirement Bd., 93 MSPR 80, 83 ¶¶ 4-5 (2002):

...The relinquishment of one's right to appeal to the Board, though, must be by clear, unequivocal and decisive action. Etheridge v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 67 M.S.P.R. 53, 56 (1995).

Here, the administrative judge dismissed the appellant's appeal as withdrawn, noting that "[o]n November 30, 2001, during the processing of this appeal, the appellant contacted the Administrative Judge and withdrew her appeal[.] " However, Tab 6 of the initial appeal file is a designation of agency representative form, and Tab 5, as noted above, is the appellant's response on jurisdiction and timeliness specifically requesting that her "appeal go forward as timely filed." The appeal file does not contain any evidence of a written or oral request by the appellant to withdraw her appeal. In addition, the appellant alleges on petition for review that she never discussed the case with the administrative judge. Under these circumstances, namely, appellant's pro se status, the lack of any record of the appellant's request to withdraw her appeal, her stated wish in her November 30, 2001 filing to appeal OPM's reconsideration decision, and her contention that she did not withdraw her appeal, we find that the record does not establish that the appellant relinquished her right to appeal to the Board by clear, unequivocal or decisive action. See Etheridge, 67 M.S.P.R. at 56; cf. Tozier v. Department of the Interior, 41 M.S.P.R. 167, 168-69 (1989) (the appellant's letter to the administrative judge unequivocally stated that he wished to withdraw his appeal)....

A reviewing court considers the record of communications between the appellant and the AJ to determine if the Board correctly determined that the withdrawal of the appeal was unequivocal or conditional, i.e., without prejudice to later refiling, Conde v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 2001 NP No. 00-3421):

Conde's withdrawal may properly be the basis for a dismissal with prejudice only if the AJ informed him of the fact that the dismissal would be with prejudice. See Allison v. United States Postal Serv., 81 MSPR 335, 337 (1999) (denying petition for review and upholding dismissal with prejudice because the AJ "informed the appellant's representative that he could withdraw the appeal for any reason but that such withdrawal would be with prejudice"), aff'd 232 F.3d 908 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (Table).

...

Conde does not dispute the Board's finding that the AJ informed him that "if he withdrew his appeal, there would be no further proceedings before the Board on this matter." We hold that this unequivocal warning was sufficient to inform Conde that his withdrawal would result in a dismissal with prejudice. The fact that the AJ might not have used the term "prejudice" does not render the warning insufficient. While Conde did not have the assistance of counsel, in the absence of persuasive evidence to the contrary, the clear and plain meaning of the AJ's warning was sufficient to inform Conde of the effect of his withdrawal. Importantly, Conde has neither argued, nor submitted any evidence to indicate, that he made any statements during the October 5 conference indicating that he intended to preserve the opportunity to re-file a Board appeal on this matter.

Conde's only evidence to indicate that he did not appreciate the effect of his withdrawal is his October 31 letter to the AJ. In the letter, however, Conde does not state that he had believed that his withdrawal would result in a dismissal without prejudice.

Because withdrawal of an appeal may be conditional, the withdrawal should be unequivocal if it is to be dismissed with prejudice. The AJ cannot assume that a request for withdrawal means withdrawal with prejudice unless there is an explicit statement of intent; withdrawal may be conditioned on other events, e.g., release from incarceration or recovery from illness, so that the appeal can continue after the occurrence of the stated event. Massey v. Dept. of Army, 31 MSPR 435, 437 (1986). There is an exception to the possibility of the conditional withdrawal of an appeal: an appellant may not withdraw his appeal should the Board fail to reverse the AJ on a PFR. The Board will adjudicate the case and issue a final decision. Moton v. USPS, 25 MSPR 639, 640 (1985). [Refer later in this chapter to "Dismissal Without Prejudice to Refile ('DWOP')."]

Settlement agreements often waive appeals, Lawrence v. OPM, 108 MSPR 325, 328 ¶ 6, 2008 MSPB 56 (2008):

A waiver of appeal rights in a settlement agreement is enforceable and not against public policy if the terms of the waiver are comprehensive, freely made, and fair, and the execution of the waiver was not the result of duress or bad faith on the part of the agency. Wyatt v. U.S. Postal Service, 101 M.S.P.R. 28, ¶ 18 (2006). Such a waiver divests the Board of jurisdiction over an appeal. Id. Nevertheless, an appellant may establish that the Board has jurisdiction over an action taken pursuant to the terms of a settlement agreement wherein the appellant agreed to waive her appeal rights if the appellant can establish that the settlement agreement was invalid due to fraud, duress, coercion, or misrepresentation by the agency. Id.

In Frazier v. USPS, 18 MSPR 310, 311 (1983), the appellant sought to withdraw an appeal based on a settlement. The letter withdrawing the appeal was misplaced at the regional office and a decision was issued on the merits. The agency then withdrew the settlement because the appeal was adjudicated. The Board sorted the problem out at the PFR level by vacating the initial decision and directing the agency to abide by the settlement to avoid the manifest injustice of leaving the appellant without a remedy. Frazier noted that "because appellant requested the termination of his appeal, the initial decision should have addressed only the jurisdictional issue created by the withdrawal of the appeal, without discussion of the disputed factual issues on the merits of the removal action." There is no single process required or followed to consider the appellant's attempt to reinstate a withdrawn appeal. De Vaughn v. USPS, 96 MSPR 427, 428-29 ¶¶ 7-9 (2004), and other decisions address the problems involving withdrawal or reinstatement of the appeal through PFRs, when the appellant files a PFR. Other decisions address withdrawn appeals through the analysis applicable to a belated appeal. De Vaughn, supra, 96 MSPR at 428 n.*. See Duncan v. Under Bass v.
