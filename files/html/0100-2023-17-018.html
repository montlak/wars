d. Postal Service Non-Preference Eligible

Several postal RIFs spawned a lot of litigation. A preference eligible in the Postal Service has a right to appeal a RIF to the Board. Tomasello v. USPS, 73 MSPR 640, 642 (1997) (demotion). Under Bell v. USPS, DC-0752-14-0613-I-1 (NP 2/24/2016):

...Except as otherwise provided by law, U.S. Postal Service employees are deemed not to be employees under title 5 of the United States Code. See 5 U.S.C. § 2105(e). The provisions of title 5 relating to a preference eligible as defined in 5 U.S.C. § 2108(3) apply to an employee of the U.S. Postal Service in the same manner and under the same conditions as if the employee were subject to the competitive service. 39 U.S.C. § 1005(a)(2). Thus, a preference-eligible employee of the U.S. Postal Service who has been demoted by a RIF is entitled to appeal that action to the Board. Love v. U.S. Postal Service, 76 M.S.P.R. 490, 493 (1997); see 39 U.S.C. § 1005(a)(2); 5 U.S.C. § 3501(b); 5 C.F.R. §§ 351.202(a)(2), 351.901.

A nonpreference eligible postal employee is not covered by RIF regulations; a RIF of one of these employees is not appealable to the Board. See Love v. USPS, 76 MSPR 490, 493 (1997) ("The right of an employee of the Postal Service to appeal a RIF action to the Board is limited to preference eligibles."); Love v. USPS, 72 MSPR 571, 573 (1996) ("If the appellant is not a preference eligible, he may not appeal the matter of his RIF demotion to the Board."); Strachan v. USPS, 64 MSPR 333, 339 (1994) (same). Under Raymond v. USPS, 45 MSPR 16, 18-19 (1990):

As detailed below, we find that the Board lacks jurisdiction over RIF appeals of non-preference eligible employees in the U.S. Postal Service.

Under 39 USC 201, the Postal Service is "an independent establishment of the executive branch of the Government of the United States." Except as otherwise provided by law, Postal Service employees are deemed not to be employees under title 5. See 5 USC 2105(e).

Section 1005 of title 39 pertaining to the Postal Service provides, however, as follows:

(a)(2) The provisions of title 5 relating to a preference eligible (as that term is defined under section 2108(3) of such title) shall apply to an applicant for appointment and any officer or employee of the Postal Service in the same manner and under the same conditions as if the applicant, officer, or employee were subject to the competitive service under such title.

Thus, Postal Service employees who are preference eligibles have the same rights as competitive service employees under title 5. Employees, such as the appellant, however, who are not veterans preference eligible employees have not been afforded the rights of competitive service employees and have no rights under title 5 unless specifically granted by some other provision. We find no statutory provision granting non-preference eligible employees of the Postal Service RIF appeal rights.[2]

[2] As a non-preference eligible employee in the Postal Service, the appellant also has no right of appeal to the Board under Chapter 75 procedures. Such right is afforded only to preference eligible employees of the Postal Service, See 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(B), or to non-preference eligibles who are supervisors, management employees, or employees "engaged in personnel work in other than a purely confidential clerical capacity" and who have "completed 1 year of current continuous service in the same or similar position." See 39 USC 1005(a)(4)(A). Thus, assuming, without deciding, that her placement on LWOP was involuntary, and constituted a constructive suspension of more than 14 days, the appellant has no right of appeal to the Board because she is excluded from coverage under Chapter 75 pursuant to 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(B).

Under 5 USC 3502, OPM is authorized to prescribe regulations for the release of competing employees in a RIF which give due effect to "military preference." Under 5 USC 1302(b) and (c) OPM is authorized to prescribe and enforce regulations that implement the Congressional policy that "preference" shall be given to "preference eligibles" in "retention" in the competitive and excepted service in Executive agencies. The Board generally will afford due deference to an agency's interpretation of statutes it is charged with implementing and to its implementing regulations. See Jones v. Office of Personnel Management, 37 MSPR 163, 167 (1988).

OPM has issued regulations and guidance implementing its statutory mandate which, with respect to the Postal Service, afford retention preference and appeal rights to the Board only for preference eligible employees affected by RIFs. Postal Service employees who are preference eligible employees are covered under OPM's regulations at 5 CFR part 351. See 5 CFR 351.202(a)(1). These regulations are not applicable to non-preference eligible employees of the Postal Service, and OPM guidance on the RIF regulations in the Federal Personnel manual states that:

this chapter [chapter 351] applies to each Federal civilian employee: (1) in the Executive branch with these modifications:...(c) Preference eligible employees of the U.S. Postal Service have RIF rights equivalent to those held by competitive service employees pursuant to section 1005(a) of the Postal Reorganization Act of 1970 although Postal Service employees are not subject to title 5, United States Code. Non-preference eligibles have no RIF rights under the Act or title 5.

See par. 2-2(1)(c), FPM Letter 351-22 at 12 (September 1, 1987).

Accordingly, we find that the appellant has no right to appeal to the Board alleging that she was furloughed either under the RIF regulations or under any other statutory or regulatory provision.

See Krizman v. MSPB, 77 F.3d 434, 436 (Fed. Cir. 1996) ("Ordinarily, employees of the Postal Service do not enjoy the protections of the RIF regulations.... By statute, however, preference-eligible employees, i.e., certain veterans and relatives of veterans, see 5 USC 2108, enjoy special rights under the RIF regulations, including the right to appeal RIF actions to the Board."); cf. Grigel v. USPS, DE-0351-16-0401-I-1 (NP 7/5/2022) ("Because we lack jurisdiction over the appellant's RIF demotion and alleged involuntary retirement claims, we cannot adjudicate his age discrimination claim.").

As the Board stated in Raymond, supra, a downgrading resulting from reorganization cannot be appealed through adverse action procedures by nonpreference eligible Postal Service employees. See Bergon v. USPS, 64 MSPR 228 (1994), aff'd sub nom. Schall v. USPS, 73 F.3d 341 (Fed. Cir. 1996). Schall explained, 73 F.3d at 343-44:

Having abandoned his claim to preference eligibility, Schall now argues that career postal employees should be allowed to appeal the agency's RIF actions regardless of their status as nonpreference eligible employees. But that is an argument best addressed to Congress.

The Postal Service is "an independent establishment of the executive branch of the Government of the United States," 39 USC 201, and its employees are generally not entitled to civil service protection, 5 USC 2105(e). Therefore, Schall must point to some law, rule, or regulation that gives him a right to appeal the agency's action or the board has no jurisdiction. See 5 USC 7701(a); Van Werry v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 995 F.2d 1048, 1050 (Fed. Cir. 1993).

Specific categories of postal employees do have limited rights under Title 5. The Civil Service Reform Act of 1978, Pub. L. No. 95-454, 92 Stat. 1111, gave preference eligible postal employees the right to appeal adverse actions as provided by title 5, Subchapter II of Chapter 75. Nine years later, Congress also amended 39 USC 1005(a), and gave nonpreference eligible supervisory and managerial employees a limited right to appeal adverse actions. Postal Employee Appeal Rights Act of 1987, Pub. L. No. 100-90, 1(a), 101 Stat. 673. Even if Schall fits under this statute, however, he still has no right to appeal because a RIF action is not adverse action initiated against a particular employee due to his conduct or performance, but an action directed at the employee's position.... Congress having explicitly excluded RIFs from those adverse actions covered by Subchapter II of Chapter 75, Schall cannot appeal the agency's RIF action as if it were an adverse action.

Marcoux v. USPS, 63 MSPR 373, 378-80 (1994), added as to postal managers and supervisors:

...Before 1987, those employees were not entitled to appeal either RIF actions or adverse actions to the Board. Since that time, there has been no statutory or regulatory change that would provide those employees with a right to file RIF appeals with the Board. Legislation has been enacted, however, that generally provides them with a right to appeal to the Board actions covered by subchapter II of chapter 75. Act of Aug. 18, 1987, Pub. L. No. 100-90, 101 Stat. 673 (1987).

Nothing in the 1987 legislation indicates that Congress intended, in enacting it, to provide Board review of RIF actions affecting nonpreference-eligible employees of the Postal Service. Furthermore, nothing in the history of that legislation indicates such an intent. While congressional debate concerning the legislation included numerous references to the proposed right to appeal adverse actions or disciplinary actions, it includes no references to RIF actions. In fact, the legislation was described by its "surrogate floor manager" during Senate debate of the measure as "simply extend[ing] to nonveteran postmasters and postal supervisors the same rights with respect to adverse actions as their veteran counterparts currently enjoy under title 5 of the U.S. Code." 133 Cong. Rec. S10757 (daily ed. July 28, 1987) (statement of Sen. Simpson). Construing chapter 75 as covering actions such as that at issue here would, in effect, provide nonpreference-eligible postal employees with adverse action rights that are not provided to preference-eligible employees, i.e., the right to chapter 75 procedures in connection with RIF actions. This construction therefore would provide non-preference-eligible employees with rights Congress never intended to grant when it enacted the 1987 legislation.

Second, we note that the procedures prescribed in chapter 75--e.g., notifying the employee of the reason for the proposed action, providing him with an opportunity to respond and to submit affidavits and other evidence in support of the response, and providing him with "a written decision and the specific reasons therefor," 5 USC 7513(b)-(d) are quite different from those prescribed in the RIF regulations. In RIF actions, decisions regarding the retention, separation, or assignment of an employee generally are related to management decisions regarding the abolishment, establishment, and retention of positions in the agency, and to the qualifications and relative retention standings of affected employees, rather than to the kinds of "reasons" on which chapter 75 actions are based. While the RIF regulations were designed to accommodate considerations appropriate to RIF actions, chapter 75 procedures were not. Chapter 75 procedures therefore cannot, in our view, appropriately be applied to actions in the nature of a RIF.

Third, we note that the agency is required to apply RIF procedures when demoting preference-eligible employees pursuant to a reorganization, as well as when taking other actions specified in 5 CFR 351.201(a)(2). Nothing in the history of the legislation providing adverse action appeal rights to employees such as the appellant indicates that Congress intended to require the agency to apply one set of procedures to employees who are not preference-eligibles, while applying an entirely different set of procedures to preference-eligible employees. A holding that assignments such as that at issue here are adverse actions, however, would have that effect.
