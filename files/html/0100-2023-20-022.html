j. Communication to News Media; Congress

Assuming the protected nature of the content of the communications, disclosures to news media and to members of Congress or congressional staff members appear to be protected under Arauz v. DOJ, 89 MSPR 529, 534-35 ¶¶ 8-11 (2001) (following remand, Arauz v. DOJ, 93 MSPR 166 (2002)):

The administrative judge also referred to evidence that the appellant had met with reporters from the Los Angeles Times and other news media in January 1997, and that this meeting resulted in the publication of an article in the Times on January 16, 1997, in which the appellant was described as having made disclosures regarding citizenship fraud. He found, however, that the disclosures the appellant had made on this occasion were not protected because they had been "directed by members of Congress, for political reasons," and because construing 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8) as covering disclosures made under these circumstances "would violate the constitutional principle of separation of powers by unduly interfering with the role of the Executive Branch."

We do not need to reach the constitutional issue on which the administrative judge found the appellant's disclosures to news media unprotected. See Ashwander v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 297 U.S. 288, 346-47, 56 S. Ct. 466, 80 L. Ed. 688 (1936) (federal courts commonly avoid deciding constitutional questions when alternative grounds for decision are available). We find instead that the appellant's disclosures to Congress and to the news media were inextricably intertwined, and that the administrative judge should have considered the disclosures together. For example, the Times article reported that in the appellant's interview with the Times, she alleged that she had submitted written statements to Congress detailing that agency officials ignored possible illegal voting registration practices. We find further, for the reasons stated in connection with our discussion of the disclosures to congressional staff members, that the appellant reasonably believed that the information she disclosed to the news media evidenced a violation of law, and we therefore find that those disclosures are protected under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8)....

Because the administrative judge found that the appellant's January 1997 disclosures to news media were not protected, he did not specifically address the question of whether those disclosures were a contributing factor in the personnel actions at issue in this appeal. He did, however, find that the appellant's previous disclosures to Congress on the same subject were a contributing factor in some, but not all, of those personnel actions. This finding was based on the responsible officials' awareness--or lack of awareness of the disclosures to Congress at the time they engaged in their allegedly retaliatory activities. Specifically, he found that the Los Angeles District Director, the Deputy Director, and the Special Assistant to the Deputy Director had actual or constructive knowledge of the appellant's January 1997 disclosures. Because the officials who were aware of those disclosures gained their knowledge through the January 17 Times article mentioned above, it is apparent that the same officials also were aware of the disclosures to the news media that resulted in the publication of that article. This fact, together with the additional facts outlined below, support a finding that the appellant's disclosures to the news media were a contributing factor in the challenged personnel actions.

A protected disclosure may be found to be a contributing factor in a personnel action if the disclosure was made known not long before the personnel action occurred. See, e.g., Scott v. Department of Justice, 69 M.S.P.R. 211, 238 (1995), aff'd, 99 F.3d 1160 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table). As the administrative judge indicated in his initial decision...all the personnel actions that have been attributed to the officials with knowledge of the appellant's disclosures to news media occurred within a year after the January 16 Times article was published. This proximity in time provides a sufficient basis on which to conclude that the appellant's disclosures to news media were a contributing factor in the personnel actions for which officials aware of that article were responsible. See Smith v. Department of Agriculture, 64 MSPR 46, 65 (1994)....
