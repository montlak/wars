a. IRA Case

An IRA appeal was deemed suitable for substitution. The appeal, if successful, could result in corrective action embracing back pay and the award of consequential damages and attorney fees. Carpio v. OPM, 94 MSPR 506, 507-08 ¶ 5 (2003). An IRA appeal could not, however, be initiated by an employee's personal representative, Glover v. Dept. of Army, 94 MSPR 534, 537-38 ¶¶ 9-10 (2003):

The statutory provision that authorizes the filing of IRA appeals, i.e., 5 U.S.C. § 1221(a), defines those entitled to file such an appeal in language that is at least as specific as that authorizing appeals of separations. That is, while 5 U.S.C. § 7701(a) refers to an appeal by "[a]n employee, or applicant for employment," and while 5 U.S.C. § 7513(d) refers to "[a]n employee against whom an action is taken under this section," 5 U.S.C. § 1221(a) provides a right to file an IRA appeal to "an employee, former employee, or applicant for employment" against whom a "personnel action is taken, or proposed to be taken...." Applying the principles stated above, including the rule of expressio unius, we find that Congress clearly intended, in enacting 5 U.S.C. § 1221(a), to give the right to file an IRA appeal only to employees, former employees, and applicants for employment, and that it authorized those individuals to file such appeals only when they themselves were the subject of the action that had been taken or proposed. Like 5 U.S.C. §§ 7513 and 7701, 5 U.S.C. § 1221(a) simply does not extend appeal rights to all persons who might benefit from corrective action that could have been ordered if the appeal had been filed. See Estate of Pyc, 73 M.S.P.R. at 328 (beneficiary of employee's estate not authorized to file appeal of separation); accord, McCurry, 45 M.S.P.R. at 396.[3] Clearly, the appellant is neither an employee, a former employee, nor an applicant for employment, and she was not the subject of the detail and reassignment she seeks to challenge.

[3] The appellant argues that "the limitation of the right of appeal under § 7701 was expanded in Steele v. Office of Personnel Management, 57 M.S.P.R. 458 (1993), aff'd, 50 F.3d 21 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table), to allow a widow to lodge an appeal under § 7701 to the Merit Systems Protection Board." We note, however, that the appeal in Steele was authorized under 5 U.S.C. § 8347(d)(1), which provides that "an administrative action or order affecting the rights or interests of an individual or of the United States under [the]subchapter [governing rights under the Civil Service Retirement System] may be appealed to the Merit Systems Protection Board...." This language, consistent with its purpose and the context in which it appears, has been construed as giving a right of appeal to persons other than employees, i.e., to those whose rights or interests have been affected by an action or order concerning retirement. E.g., Bronger v. Office of Personnel Management, 740 F.2d 1552, 1555 (Fed. Cir. 1984), rev'd on other grounds on rehearing, 769 F.2d 756 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (en banc), cert. denied, 474 U.S. 1101, 106 S. Ct. 882, 88 L. Ed. 2d 918 (1986). While the appellant in Steele was the widow of an employee, rather than an employee herself, the subject of her appeal was a survivor annuity to which she alone would be entitled, and not benefits she obtained through her deceased husband's estate. Steele, 57 M.S.P.R. at 460.

Finally, as the administrative judge noted, and as the Board noted in McCurry and in Estate of Pyc, the Board's regulations provide for pursuit of an appeal by a party properly substituted for an appellant who has died or otherwise become unable to pursue the appeal himself, but they do not provide for substitution of a party before the appeal has been filed. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.35; Estate of Pyc, 73 M.S.P.R. at 328; McCurry, 45 M.S.P.R. at 396 n.
