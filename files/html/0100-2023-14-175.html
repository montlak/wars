2. Advice of Rights

A union may require negotiations on the advice to be given employees before an investigatory interview that may result in disciplinary action against them. The proposal does not infringe on internal agency security practices, nor does it interfere with management determinations to interview employees. The proposal constitutes a procedure under § 7106(b)(2) to ensure that an employee who is to be interrogated is notified of the circumstances of the questioning. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, IRS, 8 FLRA 136, 136-38 (1982) (12 union proposals); remanded for reconsideration, Dept. of Treasury v. FLRA, 707 F.2d 574, 577 (D.C. Cir. 1983); see NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, BATF, 43 FLRA 1442, 1443-46 (1992) (Proposal 1) (negotiable procedure: "at the time the inspector should reasonably believe that the information offered by the employee indicates that the conduct of the employee could reasonably result in discipline to the employee, the inspector must then advise the employee of the right to union representation").

A proposal is negotiable that before questioning an employee on a matter relating to a disciplinary action, the employee will be advised in writing of the right to be represented by the union or a personal representative approved by the union. Questioning employees under oath by management on matters relating to disciplinary actions plainly is a personnel policy and practice and a matter affecting the working conditions of employees under § 7103(a)(14). The participation of employees in the interrogations is under compulsion deriving solely from the employment relationship. Consequences may flow from the interrogations directly affecting the work situation and involvement of the employees questioned. That the proposal goes beyond § 7114(a)(2) is immaterial. That section neither precludes the proposal nor preempts broader contract protections for employees. As to the timeliness of the agency investigation efforts, even assuming some delay inevitably results from the procedure, the agency did not show that the delay prevented it from acting pursuant to its statutorily reserved rights. AFGE, Nat'l INS Council and DOJ, INS, 8 FLRA 347, 354 (1982) (Proposal 5). Negotiable was a proposal that when Office of Internal Affairs investigators investigate EEO complaints, the investigators inform employees of their purpose and that the agents provide appropriate notice of rights to employees when they move beyond an EEO investigation. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., 46 FLRA 696, 746 (1992) (Provision 23).

A series of specific proposals for investigative disclosures and advice of rights was approved for negotiation in NTEU and NRC, 47 FLRA 370, 373-74 (1993). FLRA was only presented with the defense that the proposals were nonnegotiable because they might impede interviews by the agency inspector general, a defense FLRA declined to accept:

Proposal 1

Article 3--Employee Rights

Section 3.3.2

When the person being interviewed is accompanied by a union representative, in both criminal and non[ ]criminal cases, the role of the representative includes, but is not limited to[,] the following rights:

(1) to clarify the questions;

(2) to clarify the answers;

(3) to assist the employee in providing favorable or extenuating facts;

(4) to suggest other employees who have knowledge of relevant facts; and

(5) to advise the employee.

Proposal 2

Section 3.4

[W]hen an investigation is being conducted and where the employee is a potential recipient of disciplinary action, the employee shall be advised by the investigator of the general nature of the interview, and of his/her right to be represented by the Union in accordance with Section 3.3.1 and 3.3.2 above, prior to taking any oral or written statement from that employee....

Proposal 3

Section 3.4.1

Where the subject of an investigation is being interviewed regarding possible criminal conduct and prosecution, at the beginning of the interview the employee shall be given a statement of Miranda rights. The warning shall contain the language listed in Appendix A to this Agreement. If the employee waives his/her rights, the employee shall so indicate in writing and will be given a copy for his/her records.

Proposal 4

Section 3.4.2

In an interview involving possible criminal conduct where prosecution has been declined by appropriate authority, at the beginning of the interview the employee shall be given a statement of the Kalkines warning in writing. Further, the employee will acknowledge receipt of the warning in writing and shall receive a copy for his/her records.

FLRA noted, 47 FLRA at 377:

As explained by the Union, Proposals 1-4 apply to criminal and noncriminal investigatory interviews and are intended to codify and supplement employees' rights to Union representation under section 7114(a)(2)(B) of the Statute. Under Proposals 1-4, in criminal and noncriminal investigatory interviews, where the employee subject to an examination reasonably believes that the interview may result in discipline, not only would the employee be entitled, upon request, under section 7114(a)(2)(B), to Union representation, but also to the additional protections provided by the proposals. The protections provided by the proposals would apply only in those situations in which an employee is entitled to Union representation under section 7114(a)(2)(B) of the Statute.[3] We find that the Union's explanation of the proposals is consistent with the wording of those proposals and we will adopt that interpretation for purposes of this decision.

[3] An employee's right to union representation under section 7114(a)(2)(B) of the Statute applies to all investigations, including criminal investigations, conducted by an agency. See U.S. Department of Justice, Immigration and Naturalization Service, Border Patrol, El Paso, Texas, 42 FLRA 834, 840 (1991).

See FOP Lodge 1F and VAMC Providence, R.I., 57 FLRA 373, 386 (2001) (Proposal 13(a)) (proposal was negotiable that a union representative shall be available to an employee who is subject of disciplinary action and that interview or questioning of the employee be reasonably delayed to make union representative available).

FLRA permits negotiation of proposals for agencies to provide Miranda and Kalkines [from Kalkines v. United States, 473 F.2d 1391, 1392-94 (Ct. Cl. 1973)] warnings. Those provisions were approved for negotiation in NTEU and NRC, 47 FLRA 370, 375 n.2 (1993) (reviewing Proposals 1-4) (discussed later in this chapter, "Inspector General Investigation"). FLRA explained the two types of warnings:

Under Miranda, in a criminal case, statements obtained during custodial interrogation of a defendant may not be utilized unless prior to the questioning the person is "warned that he [or she] has a right to remain silent, that any statement he [or she] does make may be used as evidence against him [or her], and that he [or she] has a right to the presence of an attorney, either retained or appointed." Miranda, 384 U.S. at 444. Under Kalkines, a Federal employee can be discharged for not answering a question if adequately informed both that the employee is subject to discharge for not answering and that the employee's replies and the "fruits" of such replies cannot be employed against the employee in a criminal case. Kalkines, 473 F.2d at 1383.

Borrowing from the MSPB some explanation of Kalkines and Garrity, we are informed by Carey v. Dept. of State, DC-0752-11-0195-I-1 (NP 11/17/2011):

...In Garrity, 385 U.S. at 497-500, the Court held that coerced statements obtained by a government employer during an investigation--statements made in response to threats of removal from employment pursuant to a New Jersey statute--cannot be used against an employee in a criminal proceeding. This appeal is not a criminal proceeding, nor is the agency seeking to introduce any allegedly coerced statements made by the appellant in the course of any investigation. In addition, the remedy at issue in Garrity--the exclusion of the employee's statements from a criminal proceeding--is not at issue here. The court in Kalkines, 473 F.2d at 1393-94, held that the government cannot discharge an employee on the basis of the employee's refusal to answer questions when the employee has not been informed that he would be granted immunity from criminal prosecution based on and as a result of the answers to such questions....

See FOP Lodge 1F and VAMC Providence, R.I., 57 FLRA 373, 387 (2001) (approving for negotiation Proposal 13(b), seeking agency agreement to rights against coerced testimony under Garrity v. New Jersey, 385 U.S. 493 (1967)); NTEU Chap. 21 and Dept. of Treasury, Bureau of Engraving & Printing, 18 FLRA 405, 406-07 (1985) (Proposal 2, reciting employee's right to silence, was nonnegotiable because it interfered with right of management to take disciplinary action against employees and to assign work, including right to have employees account for their conduct and work performance); AFGE Local 1812 and USIA, 16 FLRA 308, 308-09 (1984) (Provision 1, conferring right to remain silent on employees was nonnegotiable: it interfered with rights to take disciplinary action and direct employees); Tidewater Va. FEMTC and Navy Pub. Works Ctr., Norfolk, Va., 15 FLRA 343, 345-46 (1984) (rejecting as nonnegotiable right to silence expressed in Provision 1: "by totally immunizing employees from disciplinary action for refusing to answer questions concerning their official duties in discussions which they believe may result in disciplinary proceedings, the provision directly interferes with substantive management rights"). [Refer later in this chapter to "Criminal Investigation."]

A union could not compel negotiations on a proposal that essentially required the employer to give Kalkines warnings to witnesses or victims interviewed by investigators when the individuals interviewed would not be implicated in criminal wrongdoing. Use of the warning could discourage statements by those interviewed, a result that could reasonably be considered by the employer to interfere with agency investigations. AFGE Fed. Prison Council 33 and DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, 51 FLRA 1112, 1114-16 (1996) (Proposal 1).

A union may not negotiate for a contract provision that precludes the employer from using evidence developed without a warning to the employee of the right to union representation, Portsmouth FEMTC and Portsmouth Naval Shipyard, 34 FLRA 1150, 1157-59 (1990):

As the right to discipline employees encompasses the investigation to determine whether discipline is justified, it follows that the right also encompasses the use of the evidence obtained during that investigation....

...By limiting the evidence which can be used to support a disciplinary action, the proposal directly interferes with management's right to take disciplinary action....

A proposal which directly interferes with the right to discipline employees does not constitute a negotiable procedure....

...

...The proposed arrangement establishes a blanket prohibition on the use of evidence obtained in an interview where an employee was not afforded the contractual right to representation.... As the Union acknowledges, the proposal may "prevent management from winning its case...."

The proposal would apply to the Agency's "inadvertent as well as intentional failures to afford union representation...." The proposal would apply without regard to...the nature of the misconduct involved....

On balance, we find that the benefits to employees are outweighed by the limitations placed on the Agency's ability to discipline employees....

Portsmouth Naval Shipyard was followed in IFPTE, Local 4 and Dept. of Navy, Portsmouth Naval Shipyard, 35 FLRA 31, 35 (1990) (Provision 1).
