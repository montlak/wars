2. Veterans Readjustment Act

"The VRA authority provides for appointment of qualified, covered veterans for positions up to and including the GS-11 level or its equivalent. 38 U.S.C. § 4214(b)(1)(A)." Morris v. Dept. of Army, 494 Fed. Appx. 63, 64 (Fed. Cir. 2012 NP). 38 USC 4214 provides:

(a)(1) The United States has an obligation to assist veterans of the Armed Forces in readjusting to civilian life. The Federal Government is also continuously concerned with building an effective work force, and veterans constitute a uniquely qualified recruiting source. It is, therefore, the policy of the United States and the purpose of this section to promote the maximum of employment and job advancement opportunities within the Federal Government for qualified covered veterans (as defined in paragraph (2)(B)) who are qualified for such employment and advancement.

(2) In this section:

(A) The term "agency" has the meaning given the term "department or agency" in section 4211(5) of this title.

(B) The term "qualified covered veteran" means a veteran described in section 4212(a)(3) of this title.

(b)(1) To further the policy stated in subsection (a) of this section, veterans referred to in paragraph (2) of this subsection shall be eligible, in accordance with regulations which the Office of Personnel Management shall prescribe, for veterans recruitment appointments, and for subsequent career-conditional appointments, under the terms and conditions specified in Executive Order Numbered 11521 (March 26, 1970), except that--

(A) such an appointment may be made up to and including the level GS-11 or its equivalent;

(B) a veteran shall be eligible for such an appointment without regard to the number of years of education completed by such veteran;

(C) a veteran who is entitled to disability compensation under the laws administered by the Department of Veterans Affairs or whose discharge or release from active duty was for a disability incurred or aggravated in line of duty shall be given a preference for such an appointment over other veterans;

(D) a veteran receiving such an appointment shall--

(i) in the case of a veteran with less than 15 years of education, receive training or education; and

(ii) upon successful completion of the prescribed probationary period, acquire a competitive status; and

(E) a veteran given an appointment under the authority of this subsection whose employment under the appointment is terminated within one year after the date of such appointment shall have the same right to appeal that termination to the Merit Systems Protection Board as a career or career-conditional employee has during the first year of employment.

(2) This subsection applies to qualified covered veterans.

(3) A qualified covered veteran may receive such an appointment at any time.

(c) Each agency shall include in its affirmative action plan for the hiring, placement, and advancement of handicapped individuals in such agency as required by section 501(b) of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 (29 U.S.C. 791 (b)), a separate specification of plans (in accordance with regulations which the Office of Personnel Management shall prescribe in consultation with the Secretary, the Secretary of Labor, and the Secretary of Health and Human Services, consistent with the purposes, provisions, and priorities of such Act) to promote and carry out such affirmative action with respect to disabled veterans in order to achieve the purpose of this section.

...

(f) Notwithstanding section 4211 of this title, the terms "veteran" and "disabled veteran" as used in subsection (a) of this section shall have the meaning provided for under generally applicable civil service law and regulations.

(g) To further the policy stated in subsection (a) of this section, the Secretary may give preference to qualified covered veterans for employment in the Department as veterans' benefits counselors and veterans' claims examiners and in positions to provide the outreach services required under section 6303 of this title, to serve as veterans' representatives at certain educational institutions as provided in section 6305 of this title, or to provide readjustment counseling under section 1712A of this title.

OPM regulations define the scope of Veterans Readjustment Act appointments, 5 CFR Part 307 (2022):

§ 307.101 Purpose.

This part implements 38 U.S.C. 4214 and Executive Order 11521, which authorizes agencies to appoint qualified covered veterans to positions in the competitive service under Veterans Recruitment Appointments (VRAs) without regard to the competitive examining system.

§ 307.102 Definitions.

For purposes of this part--

Agency, as defined in 38 U.S.C. 4211(5), means any agency of the Federal Government or the District of Columbia, including any Executive agency as defined in section 105 of title 5, and the United States Postal Service and Postal Rate Commission.

Covered veterans, as defined in 38 U.S.C. 4212(a)(3), means any of the following:

(1) Disabled veterans;

(2) Veterans who served on active duty in the Armed Forces during a war or in a campaign or expedition for which a campaign badge has been authorized;

(3) Veterans who, while serving on active duty with the Armed Forces, participated in a United States military operation for which an Armed Forces Service Medal (AFSM) was awarded pursuant to Executive Order 12985 (61 FR 1209); and

(4) Recently separated veterans.

Disabled veteran, as defined in 38 U.S.C. 4211 means:

(1) A veteran who is entitled to compensation (or who, but for the receipt of military retired pay, would be entitled to compensation) under laws administered by the Secretary of Veterans Affairs; or

(2) A person who was discharged or released from active duty because of a service-connected disability.

Qualified, as defined in 38 U.S.C. 4212(a)(3) with respect to employment in a position, means having the ability to perform the essential functions of the position with or without reasonable accommodation for an individual with a disability.

Recently separated veteran, as defined in 38 U.S.C. 4211(6), means any veteran during the three-year period beginning on the date of such veteran's discharge or release from active duty.

Substantially continuous service is defined in 5 CFR 315.201(b)(3).

War means any armed conflict declared by Congress as such.

§ 307.103 Nature of VRAs.

VRAs are excepted appointments, made without competition, to positions otherwise in the competitive service. The veterans' preference procedures of part 302 of this chapter apply when there are preference eligible candidates being considered for a VRA. Qualified covered veterans who were separated under honorable conditions may be appointed to any position in the competitive service at grade levels up to and including GS-11 or equivalent, provided they meet the qualification standards for the position. To be eligible for a VRA as a covered veteran under paragraph (2) or (3) of the definition of that term in § 307.102, the veteran must be in receipt of the appropriate campaign badge, expeditionary medal, or AFSM. For purposes of a VRA, any military service is qualifying at the GS-3 level or equivalent. Upon satisfactory completion of 2 years of substantially continuous service, the incumbent's VRA must be converted to a career or career conditional appointment. An individual may receive more than one VRA appointment as long as the individual meets the definition of a covered veteran at the time of appointment.

§ 307.104 Treatment of individuals serving under VRAs.

(a) Because VRAs are made to positions otherwise in the competitive service, the incumbents, like competitive service employees, may be reassigned, promoted, demoted, or transferred in accordance with the provisions of part 335 of this chapter.

(b) A veteran with less than 15 years of education must receive training or education prescribed by the agency.

(c) Appointments are subject to investigation by OPM. A law, Executive order, or regulation that disqualifies a person for appointment in the competitive service also disqualifies a person for a VRA.

(d) The Veterans Recruitment Appointment date for a recently separated veteran must occur before the end of the 3-year eligibility period and may not be extended.

The Veterans Readjustment Act facilitates entry into the competitive service for qualified veterans, Perry v. VA, 32 MSPR 81, 85 (1987), noted:

Appellant's initial appointment was made under the Veterans Readjustment Program, pursuant to 38 USC 2014, which grants veterans of the Vietnam War era eligibility for excepted service appointments to positions otherwise in the competitive service. See 5 CFR Part 307; Smith v. Department of Navy, 4 MSPR 20, 4 MSPB 113 (1981). VRA appointments shall be converted to career or career-conditional appointments within 30 days after completion of two years of substantially continuous, satisfactory service. 5 CFR 316.705(a)(2); FPM Ch. 307, at A-7 (May 3, 1985).

OPM regulations define VRA appointees' appeal rights, 5 CFR 307.105 (2022):

Individuals serving under VRAs have the same appeal rights as excepted service employees under parts 432 and 752 of this chapter. In addition, as established in § 315.806 of this chapter, any individual serving under a VRA, whose employment under the appointment is terminated within 1 year after the date of such appointment, has the same right to appeal that termination as a career or career-conditional employee has during the first year of employment.

The VRA appointee's right of appeal on termination during the first year of the appointment on procedural grounds extends only to individuals terminated because of preappointment reasons. Ballard v. Dept. of Air Force, 27 MSPR 287, 288 n.* (1985); see Martinez-Claudio v. VA, 33 MSPR 278, 281 (1987) (summarizing appeal rights for the VRA appointee in the first year of the appointment and noting that "[s]ervice by a preference eligible in a prior temporary competitive or excepted service position may be counted toward completion of the one-year service requirement of 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(B)."); cf. Vernon v. Dept. of Treasury (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP No. 03-3016) (Board lacked jurisdiction over a terminated VRA appointee's assertions that his removal violated the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments and breached a collective bargaining agreement). An appellant's appeal rights as a VRA probationary employee who has been terminated during his probationary period are the same as those for probationary employees in the competitive service. Polite v. Dept. of Navy, 49 MSPR 653, 656 (1991). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Appeal of Probationary Termination."]

Following the first year of service, a preference eligible VRA employee has Board appeal rights. The VRA employee who achieves competitive status through conversion to a career or career-conditional appointment has statutory appeal rights identical to other employees with that status. If there is no conversion, however, the employee will not likely be able to secure MSPB jurisdiction through a claim that he should have been converted. See Anderson v. MSPB, 12 F.3d 1069 (Fed. Cir. 1993). If a VRA appointee converts to the competitive service, he acquires normal appeal rights, assuming completion of a probationary period, if required. Jakes v. VA, 793 F.2d 293 (Fed. Cir. 1986), reversed the Board's conclusion that removal was justified of a VRA appointee who had more education than was then permitted for an appointee under that program. The court apparently considered that the agency should have referred the case to OPM for its determination of whether to take action against the employee for fraud or some other misconduct on his part concerning the appointment process (none was shown); cf. Miller v. United States, 717 F.2d 109 (3d Cir. 1983) (the court characterized the separation as an adverse action, rather than as the nullification of a void appointment through "correction of administrative oversight"; the employee was on the rolls for two years). [Refer to Chapter 6, "Appointment," concerning procedures used to cure void and voidable appointments.]

Even if an agency reduces the "probationary" period for a VRA appointee, the MSPB's one-year jurisdictional adverse action appeal limit applies. See Toomey v. USPS, 71 MSPR 10, 13-14 (1996). Because, under law predating the 1984 Veterans Benefits Improvement Act, no law or regulation created a right of appeal to the Board by an employee appointed under the Veterans Readjustment Program before completion of one full year of service, the agency could not enlarge the Board's statutory jurisdiction through an erroneous notice of appeal rights. Altheimer v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-1159). [For additional cases discussing appeal rights of VRA employees, refer to this chapter: "Preference Eligible Status"; "One Year of Qualifying Service; Current Continuous Service in The Same or Similar Position"; "Break in Service"; "Tacking of Prior Service: Competitive Service Probation."]

The Board, agencies, and appellants have the same problems in VRA cases as they do in any others in calculating service that counts toward the accrual of sufficient service to establish appeal rights. [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Tacking of Prior Service: Competitive Service Probation," concerning the calculation and tacking of service for probationary employees.] A VRA appointee under 5 CFR 307.103 (2022) has an appeal right under 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(B) if he is a preference eligible with one year of continuous service in the same or a similar position. Garcia v. Dept. of Air Force, 5 MSPR 188, 190, 5 MSPB 241 (1981); see McLoughlin v. Dept. of Treasury, 78 MSPR 150, 155 (1998) (for prior service to be credited toward completion of VRA initial year of appointment, employee must show that prior service was (1) rendered immediately preceding appointment; (2) in same agency; (3) in same line of work; (4) completed with no more than one break of service of less than 30 days; AJ was obliged to advise appellant of jurisdictional requirements before dismissing appeal); cf. Hudson v. USPS, AT-0752-17-0106-I-1 (NP 1/30/2023) ("In addition, we find that the appellant may not tack her prior Federal service at the Morrisville Post Office because she had a break in service of approximately 1 month. See 5 C.F.R. § 752.402; see also Winns, 124 M.S.P.R. 113, ¶ 18 (overruling a line of Board decisions, which found that an appellant may establish 'current continuous service' for purposes of section 7511(a)(1)(B) under a 'continuing employment contract' theory, despite a break in service of a workday)."). A veterans preference eligible employee may accumulate time toward this requirement under consecutive positions acquired through a temporary appointment in the competitive service, and through a Veterans Readjustment Act appointment in the excepted service held by permanent assignment. Short v. VA, 8 MSPR 70, 72, 7 MSPB 563 (1981).

Gettings v. Dept. of Air Force, 48 MSPR 502, 506-09 (1991), explained subtleties of VRA appointments and addressed tacking of prior service in VRA and temporary appointments:

...A Veterans Readjustment Appointment is an excepted appointment, made after April 8, 1970, to a position otherwise in the competitive service, of a veteran who served during the Vietnam era. 5 CFR 307.101(c). As a preference eligible in the excepted service, a VRA appointee is required to complete one year of "current continuous service in the same or similar positions" under 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(B) before he is entitled to the adverse action rights of 5 USC 7513.[3] See Martinez-Claudio v. VA, 33 MSPR 278, 280 (1987).

[3] During the first year of a VRA appointment, an employee enjoys the same rights of appeal afforded career or career-conditional employees in the first year of employment. 38 USC 2014(b)(1)(D). The latter may appeal to the Board from a termination based on partisan political or marital status discrimination, or a termination based on a preappointment condition where the termination was not effected in accordance with certain procedural requirements. See 5 CFR 315.805-806.

In finding that the appellant did not complete one year of current, continuous service in the same or similar positions, the administrative judge first compared the similarity of the appellant's duties as a GS-5 Grounds Maintenance Contract Monitor with his duties as a GS-7 Construction Inspector. This was error. In making a determination whether an individual has the requisite service under section 7511(a)(1)(B), it is the one-year period immediately prior to separation that counts, not some earlier period. Carr v. USPS, 44 MSPR 681, 683 (1990). The period at issue in this case is therefore from December 5, 1988, until December 5, 1989, during which time the appellant served as a GS-7 Construction Inspector at Beale AFB, and a GS-9 Service Contract Monitor at McClellan AFB.

The Board has held that a temporary appointment can be combined with a later VRA appointment to meet the one-year requirement of section 7511(a)(1)(B). See Geter v. Dept. of Army, 19 MSPR 143, 145 (1984); Short v. VA, 8 MSPR 70, 72 (1981). The administrative judge found, however, that the appellant's temporary appointment to the GS-7 position at Beale AFB could not be combined with his appointment to the GS-9 position at McClellan AFB because these positions were held under separate VRA appointments. We disagree. The statute, and our decisions in Geter and Short, focus on whether the positions in question are the "same or similar" in nature, not on the legal authority under which the appointments were made.

Our conclusion that two or more VRA appointments can be combined to meet the one-year requirement is reinforced by reference to the Federal Personnel Manual (FPM), which indicates that service under the VRA Program is intended to be limited to a single two-year term, even if it entails more than one appointment and/or a promotion. The FPM gives the following guidance regarding the effect of a promotion:

The promotion, reassignment, or transfer of an employee serving under a veterans readjustment appointment does not relieve the employee or the agency of their obligations with respect to satisfactory participation and completion of the developmental plan. If necessary, the plan may be changed to meet the employee's needs in the new position.... The modified plan, however, must be one that can be completed prior to the scheduled conversion date.

FPM Ch. 307, Subch. 1-5(6) (May 3, 1985). The provision describing eligibility for conversion to career status states as follows:

An employee serving under a veterans readjustment appointment shall have his/her appointment converted to career or career-conditional appointment within 30 calendar days after he/she meets the following conditions: (a) He/she has completed without break in service of more than 30 calendar days, two years of substantially continuous service under a veterans readjustment appointment or appointments....

FPM Ch. 315, Subch. 7-7(a) (July 21, 1982).

We conclude that the appellant was a preference eligible in the excepted service who had completed one year of current continuous service. His entitlement to the adverse action procedural and appeal rights of 5 USC 7513 thus depends on whether the positions he held as a GS-7 Construction Inspector and as a GS-9 Service Contract Monitor were the "same or similar."

Positions are "similar" within the meaning of section 7511(a)(1)(B) when they "involve related or comparable work that requires the same or similar skills." Mathis v. USPS, 865 F.2d 232, 234 (Fed. Cir. 1988). According to the official job description for the GS-7 position at Beale AFB, its purpose is "to provide inspection and surveillance over alteration and new construction performed by civilian contractors and monitor service contracts." The listed purpose of the GS-9 position at McClellan AFB is "to provide technical assistance as a Quality Assurance Evaluator (QAE) performing inspection duties related to the surveillance of a variety of service type contracts in accordance with AFR 400-28 and other types of surveillance plans." Both positions thus involve the oversight of the performance of civilian contractors. The job description for the GS-9 position, however, includes a number of duties and responsibilities not included in the GS-7 job description: Writing statements of work specifications; preparing cost estimates and work orders; establishing delivery dates and the minimum standards to be met by service contractors; developing the Quality Assurance Evaluation surveillance plans for service contracts; approving or disapproving the workmanship of craftsmen performing the contracts; and certifying final payment to the contractor when the contract is complete.

Were we to base our determination solely on the official job descriptions, we would have no trouble concluding that the position of GS-7 Construction Inspector is not similar to the position of GS-9 Service Contract Monitor. The appellant testified, however, that he did not perform the duties described in the job descriptions for either the GS-7 or GS-9 positions. He stated that his actual job duties remained the same throughout his employment with the Air Force, namely, monitoring the performance of a particular grounds contractor. This testimony was unrebutted.

The Board faced a similar divergence between job descriptions and actual job performance in Van Skiver v. USPS, 6 MSPB 630, 7 MSPR 18 (1981), aff'd on reconsideration, 9 MSPB 28, 9 MSPR 380 (1982). In that case, "knowledge of a scheme" was an integral part of the duties of a distribution clerk, but not of the duties of a mail handler, according to the official job descriptions. Id. 7 MSPR at 19. The Board found "the salient feature of this case [to be] that there is no evidence in the record that appellant ever performed duties requiring knowledge of a scheme." Id. In resolving the conflict between the position descriptions and the testimony regarding Van Skiver's actual job duties, the Board quoted with approval the following statement from a Court of Claims decision:

What is important is not what the former Chairman intended, but what the nature of the work plaintiff actually performed [as a Confidential Assistant] was as compared with the work plaintiff was required to perform as a Personnel Assistant.

Id. at 21 (quoting Dargo v. United States, 176 Ct. Cl. 1193, 1202 (1966)). The Board concluded that, "since it is clear that appellant's duties did not significantly change despite his different job assignments, the positions were in the same line of work." Id. We similarly conclude that, since the actual job duties performed by the appellant were the same under the GS-7 and GS-9 appointments, these positions were the "same or similar" within the meaning of 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(B).

The limitations on appeal rights of VRA employees are for those with less than a year of current continuous service in the same or similar positions. Service for more than a year through combined dissimilar positions does not meet the requirements for a ordinary appeal rights. Perez v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1986 NP No. 86-1155); see Martinez-Claudio v. VA, 33 MSPR 278, 281 (1987) (considering the effect of service in a position preceding a VRA appointment). Under the former FPM Ch. 307, subch. 1-8(b) (May 3, 1985), and other law predating case refining the concept of current continuous service, temporary and term employment did not count toward the two-year service requirement included under the VRA authority for conversion to career or career-conditional appointment. Jones v. Dept. of Air Force (Fed. Cir. 1989 NP No. 89-3202). Under Polite v. Dept. of Navy, 49 MSPR 653, 657 (1991), if the agency mistakenly calculates the first year of a VRA appointment for an individual who is a preference eligible, and has appeal rights under the Civil Service Due Process Amendments, the Board applies its harmful error rule to determine if the employee was given minimal due process: prior notice of the charges, an explanation of the supporting evidence, and an opportunity to respond. Without minimal due process, the removal will be reversed. If these rights were provided, the removal will not be reversed for procedural error, i.e., the failure to adhere to statutory or regulatory provisions governing adverse actions against tenured employees. [Refer to Stephen v. Dept. of Air Force, 47 MSPR 672 (1991), and earlier in this chapter to "Harmful Error Analysis; Stephen."]

A nonpreference VRA appointee with an intermittent schedule had appeal rights based on more than two years of service in the same or similar positions, Jolivette v. Dept. of Navy, 100 MSPR 216, 219-22 ¶¶ 6-12 (2005):

An "employee," as defined in 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1), has appeal rights to the Board from certain agency actions such as a removal for cause. See 5 U.S.C. §§ 7512(a), 7513(d). The record shows that the agency appointed the appellant to his Firefighter position under 5 C.F.R. § 307.103 upon selection from a VRA list. The record also indicates that the appellant is a nonpreference eligible, that his appointments and positions were in the excepted service, and that his work schedule was intermittent. A VRA appointment is an excepted-service appointment to a position otherwise in the competitive service, and VRA appointees are converted to the competitive service after satisfactorily completing two years of substantially continuous service under the VRA program. 5 C.F.R. §§ 307.102(b), 307.104.

Because the appellant was a nonpreference eligible in the excepted service, the applicable statutory provision is section 7511(a)(1)(C), which covers:

(C) an individual in the excepted service (other than a preference eligible)--

(i) who is not serving a probationary or trial period under an initial appointment pending conversion to the competitive service; or

(ii) who has completed 2 years of current continuous service in the same or similar positions in an Executive agency under other than a temporary appointment limited to 2 years or less[.]

The appellant was serving "under an initial appointment pending conversion to the competitive service," so the remaining question under section 7511(a)(1)(C)(i) is whether he was "serving a probationary or trial period" under such an appointment. See Forest v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 47 F.3d 409, 412 (Fed. Cir. 1995); Pennington v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 57 M.S.P.R. 8, 10 n.2 (1993). It appears that, contrary to the AJ's finding that the appellant completed his one-year probationary period, the appellant, as a nonpreference eligible VRA appointee, was subject to a two-year probationary period. 5 C.F.R. §§ 307.102(b), 307.104. However, we need not determine the length of the appellant's probationary period or whether he completed it[2] because we find that he satisfied the alternative jurisdictional criteria under section 7511(a)(1)(C)(ii). See Van Wersch v. Department of Health & Human Services, 197 F.3d 1144, 1148-51 (Fed. Cir. 1999) (subsections (C)(i) and (C)(ii) of section 7511(a)(1) are alternative criteria, so that an individual need not satisfy both criteria to be an "employee" with Board appeal rights).

[2] "For intermittent employees [in the competitive service] ..., each day or part of a day in pay status counts as 1 day of credit toward the 260 days in a pay status required for completion of [the 1-year] probation" in the competitive service. 5 C.F.R. § 315.802(d). Consistent with section 315.802(d), the Board stated in Moran v. Department of the Treasury, 77 M.S.P.R. 532, 536 (1998), that "[a]n intermittent employee who has been in a pay status for 260 days is considered to have completed his probationary period and is, therefore, an 'employee' with appeal rights to the Board under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(i)." Accord Rison v. Department of Defense, 73 M.S.P.R. 390, 392 n.1 (1997); Gibson v. Department of the Treasury, 36 M.S.P.R. 335, 337 (1988). The AJ here relied on Moran to hold that the appellant was an "employee" with appeal rights because he was in a pay status for more than 260 days. ID at 2-3. However, section 315.802(d) and Moran pertain solely to the definitional criteria under section 7511(a)(1)(A) for individuals in the competitive service, for which a 1-year probationary period applies; they do not necessarily apply to individuals in the excepted service, like the appellant, whose probationary period is not necessarily limited to 1 year. We therefore find that the AJ's reliance on Moran is insufficient to establish that the appellant is an "employee" with Board appeal rights.

The appellant's intermittent VRA appointment was "other than a temporary appointment limited to 2 years or less,"[3] so the remaining question under section 7511(1)(a)(C)(ii) is whether he "completed 2 years of current continuous service in the same or similar positions in an Executive agency." It is undisputed that the appellant served as a Firefighter with an "intermittent" work schedule from July 29, 1997, to May 7, 2004. "Intermittent employment means employment without a regularly scheduled tour of duty," 5 C.F.R. § 340.401(b) (original emphasis), and it is undisputed that the appellant was on nonduty, nonpay status for a substantial portion of his nearly seven years of employment with the agency.

[3] We do not decide whether a non-intermittent VRA appointment is "other than a temporary appointment limited to 2 years or less."

In Gutierrez v. Department of the Treasury, 99 M.S.P.R. 141 (2005), and Pollak v. Department of the Treasury, 99 M.S.P.R. 187 (2005), the Board addressed a similar issue of how periods of nonduty, nonpay status for seasonal employment should be counted in determining whether the "current continuous service" requirement under section 7511(a)(1)(A)(i) is satisfied. The Board noted that "current continuous service" is defined, in pertinent part, as "a period of employment or service immediately preceding an adverse action...without a break in Federal civilian employment of a workday." 5 C.F.R. § 752.402(b); Pollak, 99 M.S.P.R. at 190; Gutierrez, 99 M.S.P.R. at 145. The Board held that periods of nonpay status consistent with the terms of seasonal employment counts fully toward satisfying the "current continuous service" requirement, even though they do not count fully toward satisfying the "probationary or trial period" requirement[4], since nonpay status while remaining on the rolls does not constitute a "break in service." Pollak, 99 M.S.P.R. at 189-90; Gutierrez, 99 M.S.P.R. at 145-47. Although Gutierrez and Pollak involved subsection (A), rather than subsection (C) as here, the holding in those cases interpreting "probationary or trial period" and "current continuous service" in subsection (A)(i)-(ii) applies equally to interpreting the identical statutory terms in subsection (C)(i)-(ii). See McCormick v. Department of the Air Force, 307 F.3d 1339, 1341-42 (Fed. Cir. 2002) (finding that the interpretation of the statutory term "or" in section 7511(a)(1)(C), set forth in a prior decision, was equally applicable to interpreting the same statutory term "or" in section 7511(a)(1)(A)). We therefore find that "current continuous service" under section 7511(a)(1)(C)(ii) includes periods of nonpay status consistent with the terms of intermittent (or seasonal) employment.[5]

[4] "Absence in nonpay status while on the rolls (other than for compensable injury or military duty) is creditable up to a total of 22 workdays," and "[n]onpay time in excess of 22 workdays extends the probationary period by an equal amount" in the competitive service. 5 C.F.R. § 315.802(c).

[5] In Gibson, 36 M.S.P.R. at 337, and Holt v. Department of the Treasury, 3 M.S.P.R. 8, 9 (1980), the Board held that periods of nonpay status do not count toward "current continuous service." Gibson and Holt are typical of many cases issued prior to Van Wersch, 197 F.3d 1144, and McCormick, 307 F.3d 1339, in that they commingle the "probationary or trial period" requirement and the "current continuous service" requirement, rather than separately and alternatively examining those requirements as Van Wersch and McCormick require. For the reasons discussed in the text above, we overrule Gibson and Holt to the extent that they are inconsistent with this decision.

There is, however, a significant difference between subsection (C)(ii) and (A)(ii), of section 7511(a)(1). To wit, subsection (C)(ii), unlike subsection (A)(ii), includes the additional requirement that the "current continuous service" be "in the same or similar positions in an Executive agency." All of the appellant's service at issue here, from July 29, 1997, to May 7, 2004, was in the Department of the Navy of the Department of Defense, an Executive agency. 5 U.S.C. §§ 101, 102, 105. Positions are similar within the meaning of section 7511(a)(1) when they are in "the same line of work," which has been interpreted as involving related or comparable work that requires the same or similar knowledge, skills, and abilities. See Mathis v. U.S. Postal Service, 865 F.2d 232, 234 (1988); Burrell v. U.S. Postal Service, 76 M.S.P.R. 204, 209 (1997). Although the record shows that the appellant was promoted on July 27, 2003, from a GS-4 Firefighter position to a GS-5 Firefighter position, the two positions are clearly in the same line of work, and the agency has not argued otherwise. We therefore find that the appellant is an "employee" under section 7511(a)(1)(C)(ii) with a right to appeal his termination (or removal) to the Board.

Under the terms of his VRA appointment, the appellant was required to serve a two-year probationary period after which he would be placed non-competitively into the competitive service. Although it is undisputed that the appellant would have completed this period on December 16, 1992, if he had not been indefinitely suspended from October 20, 1992, until the date of his removal, we find that his placement in a non-duty, non-pay status prevented him from completing this probationary period. In this connection, we note that the administrative judge, in effect, rejected the agency's reliance on FPM Ch. 315, subch. 7-7c, 8-4c, finding the twenty-two work day rule to be inapplicable. See also FPM Ch. 315, Appendix A-3(2) (September 19, 1989). These provisions apply, respectively, to the computation of "substantially continuous" service to qualify a veterans readjustment appointee for conversion to a competitive service position and to the computation of service to complete the probationary period in the competitive service. While neither application specifically determines the end of the trial period of an excepted service appointment made pending conversion to the competitive service, we find that the purposes of the rule are sufficiently similar that the same rule should apply for this related purpose. See 5 CFR 315.803; Hawkins v. Department of the Treasury, 52 MSPR 686, 690 (1992) (since demotion from supervisory position to lower-graded position and termination of probationer in competitive service were analogous, FPM guidance for probationary terminations was applied to demotion). We also find that, if the appellant had completed the two year trial period, he would have been converted to the competitive service, and thus he would have been an "employee" with appeal rights under 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(A).

We further find that the appellant does not have appeal rights under 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(C)(ii). This provision affords appeal rights to a nonpreference eligible in the excepted service who has completed two years of current continuous service in an executive agency under other than a temporary appointment limited to two years or less. Based on our review of the legislative history of the Civil Service Due Process Amendments, we find that Congress intended sections 7511(a)(1)(C)(i) and 7511(a)(1)(C)(ii) to be mutually exclusive. In this connection, the legislative history refers to 7511(a)(1)(C)(i) as "[t]he probationary exclusion," stating that the exclusion is intended to address specific job situations such as VRA appointments. See 136 Cong. Rec. S11,135 (daily ed. July 30, 1990) (statement of Sen. Pryor). The legislative history indicates that Congress intended that those individuals serving trial periods of employment under 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(C)(i) were not to be afforded appeal rights under 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(C)(ii). Id. Cf. Pennington v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 57 MSPR 8, 10-11 (1993) (5 USC 7511(a)(1)(C)(ii) applied to nonpreference eligible in the excepted service serving under a permanent appointment; 7511(a)(1)(C)(i) did not apply to him since he was not serving under a probationary or trial period pending conversion to the competitive service). Consequently, 7511(a)(1)(C)(ii) does not provide the appellant with appeal rights because he was serving in a trial period of employment pending conversion to the competitive service.

The appellant does have the same limited appeal rights as probationers in the competitive service who make a non-frivolous allegation that their terminations resulted from discrimination based on marital status or partisan political reasons. See Polite v. Department of the Navy, 49 MSPR 653, 656 (1991). Because the administrative judge found that the appellant was an employee with full appeal rights to the Board, she never properly informed him of what he needed to show to establish an appealable jurisdictional issue.... Accordingly, we find that remand is appropriate.
