A. INSUBORDINATION AND SIMILAR MISCONDUCT

"Agency employees are expected to respect authority and to follow the orders of supervisory officials." Parbs v. USPS, 107 MSPR 559, 571 ¶ 24, 2007 MSPB 302 (2007) (aff'd, Parbs v. USPS, 301 Fed. Appx. 923 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP)). "Insubordination is defined as 'a willful and intentional refusal to obey an authorized order of a superior, which the superior is entitled to have obeyed.'" Redfearn v. Dept. of Labor, 58 MSPR 307, 311 (1993) (quoting Phillips v. GSA, 878 F.2d 370, 373 (Fed. Cir. 1989)), followed in Bishopp v. Dept. of Air Force, 75 MSPR 33, 38 (1997); see Farrier v. VA, 60 MSPR 71, 73-74 (1993) (citing Ford v. Dept. of Navy, 43 MSPR 495, 500 (1990) (employee's refusal to obey must be intentional and willfully disobedient)); Rodriguez v. USDA, 27 MSPR 79, 83-84 (1985) (no unfettered discretion to ignore agency orders; insubordination may be a sufficient cause for removal). "Insubordination is a serious offense that disrupts the work place and interferes with and threatens the ability of the work force to perform its duties." Mazares v. Dept. of Navy, 302 F.3d 1382, 1386 (Fed. Cir. 2002) ((removal of two civilian seaman when they disobeyed order to be vaccinated against anthrax)). The time-honored rule followed by labor arbitrators is "obey first, grieve later." The Board follows suit, Noppenberger v. USPS, PH-0752-12-0550-I-1 (NP 3/7/2014):

Regarding the appellant's contention that following his supervisors' instructions on February 24, 2012, and April 2, 2012, would have violated the collective bargaining agreement and would have required him to work for free, employees do not have an unfettered right to disregard supervisory instructions. See Harris v. Department of the Air Force, 62 M.S.P.R. 524, 528, review dismissed, 39 F.3d 1195 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table). Rather, an employee must obey the agency order, even if he believes it to be improper, and protest the propriety of the order later. See id. This rule reflects the fundamental management right to expect that its decisions will be obeyed and its instructions carried out.[4] See Nagel v. Department of Health & Human Services, 707 F.2d 1384, 1387 (Fed. Cir. 1983) (permitting employees to refuse to do work because of disagreements with management violates the first law of the work place, namely that it is a place for doing work)....

[4] Recognized exceptions to the requirement that agency orders be obeyed apply in extreme or unusual circumstances. See, e.g., Gragg v. U.S. Air Force, 13 M.S.P.R. 296, 299 (1982) (order placing employee in dangerous situation); Fleckenstein v. Department of the Army, 63 M.S.P.R. 470, 474 (1994) (order to make disclosure that could cause irreparable harm). The appellant has failed to show that any such exceptions apply here.

Definition of "insubordinate defiance of authority" is provided by Bennett v. Dept. of Air Force, 84 MSPR 132, 136-37 ¶ 6 (1999):

...The Board's cases involving a charge of "insubordinate defiance of authority," construe it as the intentional failure to obey an order combined with defiant behavior. See, e.g., Smith v. Department of the Air Force, 48 MSPR 594, 596 (1991); Winner v. Department of the Air Force, 10 MSPR 177, 178 (1982). Although "insolence" has been brought as a separate charge together with a charge of "insubordinate defiance of authority," e.g., Smith, 48 MSPR at 596, insolent behavior can be properly considered as a factor in determining whether insubordinate conduct amounted to a "defiance of authority," see Ballew v. Department of the Army, 36 MSPR 400, 401-02 (1988) (upholding a charge of "defiance of authority" based on an allegation of "insolent behavior'); see also Webster's Encyclopedic Unabridged Dictionary Of The English Language 379 (1989) (listing "insolent" as synonym for "defiant"); Webster's New International Dictionary 687 (2d ed. 1957) (defining "defiant" as insolent)....

See Stanford v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-15-0444-I-1 (NP 9/15/2016) ("A charge of "insubordinate defiance of authority" also requires proof of defiant or insolent behavior. Alvarado v. Department of the Air Force, 103 M.S.P.R. 1, ¶ 23 (2006); Bennett v. Department of the Air Force, 84 M.S.P.R. 132, ¶ 6 (1999).").

"A charge of insubordination is generally not the same as a charge of insolence and rudeness." Hawkins v. Smithsonian Inst., 73 MSPR 397, 401 (1997). To prove insubordination, involving the willful and intentional refusal to obey an authorized order of a superior officer, the agency must allege and prove that the appellant's response was in response to an actual order. See Hanna v. Dept. of Labor, 80 MSPR 294, 301 ¶ 8 (1998). [Refer later in this chapter to "Discourtesy; Related Problems."] A refusal to obey an unlawful order, however, does not constitute insubordination. Schmidt v. United States, 145 Ct. Cl. 632, 636 (1969) (refusal to accept a geographical reassignment that "was issued to serve political purposes and not to promote the good of the service").

Insubordination is a frequent offense in the Board's annals. The charge encompasses a wide range of misdemeanors and felonies, sins and omissions. Agency supervisors may confuse inability to do work with refusal to do it, incompetence with impertinence. Mitigating factors abound, yet the rule is clear: workers work first, complain later. Violation of the rule results in a test of wills between employer and employee. Generally, the result is preordained. Administrative and judicial reviews reduce the emotion and poignancy of times past and leave for review bare and uncolored facts appearing on transcript or hearing audio files.

A caveat: 5 USC 2302(b)(9) makes it a prohibited personnel practice for an agency to:

take or fail to take, or threaten to take or fail to take, any personnel action against any employee or applicant for employment because of--

...

(D) refusing to obey an order that would require the individual to violate a law[.]

The Board readily affirms adverse action for insubordination. An employee's deliberate refusal to follow supervisory instructions constitutes serious misconduct that cannot be condoned. Redfearn v. Dept. of Labor, 58 MSPR 307, 316 (1993) (citing Sepulveda v. Dept. of Interior, 38 MSPR 449, 451-52 (1988), and Jefferson v. VA, 6 MSPR 348, 351-52 (1981)); see Murdock-Doughty v. Dept. of Air Force, 70 MSPR 119, 124 (1996) (failure, for no good reason, of a LPN to obey a supervisor's instructions relating to patient care constituted insubordination and serious misconduct); Holloman v. VA, 12 MSPR 445, 448 (1982) (failure to obey an order and follow instructions: "[A]ppellant's conduct was such as to seriously impair his working relationship with his supervisors, rendering him undependable as an employee."). A single instance of insubordination can support dismissal, particularly when the appellant had a poor disciplinary record with a relatively recent suspension for failure to follow instructions. See Montalvo v. USPS, 55 MSPR 128, 133 (1992); Haebe v. DOJ, 81 MSPR 167, 184 ¶ 32 (1999) (no de minimis exception to an otherwise proven charge of failure to follow instructions; the de minimis nature of the infraction goes to penalty determination), rev'd on other gnds, Haebe v. DOJ, 288 F.3d 1288 (Fed. Cir. 2002). Insolent disrespect toward a supervisor warranted removal when coupled with past, similar conduct, even though the employee was suffering from stress. Carson v. VA, 33 MSPR 666, 669 (1987); see Richard v. Dept. of Air Force, 43 MSPR 303, 309 (1990) (past discipline indicating a pattern of insubordinate and disrespectful behavior).

The courts show little patience with insubordination. Management directives should be followed. Webster v. Dept. of Army, 911 F.2d 679 (Fed. Cir. 1990), observed that the employee is not entitled to disregard instructions because the employee feels those instructions are foolish. Long before the Reform Act, the law was that "[i]nsubordination in itself is cause sufficient to justify discharge." May v. Civil Serv. Comm'n, 230 F. Supp. 659, 661 (W.D. La. 1963); see Stevenson v. United States, 155 Ct. Cl. 592, 595-96 (1962) ("One can readily see the chaos which would be created if subordinate employees were permitted to decide what should or should not be done in a given situation. If plaintiff had some sound reasons for remaining at the questionable desk, certainly a refusal to move was not the correct method of righting the wrong. She should have appealed through regularly provided channels and not have taken the law in her own hands.").

Even the best employee can be fired for insubordination that the employee honestly believes is justified. In Ferrone v. Dept. of Labor, 797 F.2d 962, 967 (Fed. Cir. 1986), Senior Judge Nichols remarked for the court, lamenting but sustaining the discharge for insubordination of an otherwise exemplary employee:

The criminal investigator for a federal agency has often, as here, contended with the most dangerous and ruthless kind of opponents, with the support of inadequate resources on the part of his own organization, and uncertain cooperation on the part of others. Any beginning associate in a large New York or Washington firm is paid better than he is, yet he must remember legal rights when the bullets are flying about his head. He must display personal courage, resourcefulness, initiative, persistence, patience, a contempt for regular hours, and a full set of intuitions. Above all, he must be lucky. Often, as with Ferrone and Walker, here, he may have the full force of the law behind him in theory, for whatever it is worth, but the apparent reality is a pair of Davids alone against platoons of Goliaths, and Goliaths much smarter than the Biblical original. If he represents the law, he often sees it as taking the side of his opponents with respect to critical issues. He must suffer a good deal of abuse and some overt contempt. Usually he tries to do right, and often succeeds, but it is small matter for wonder if occasionally he goes off the deep end. When he does, it is for such a case the compassionate Douglas factors were created. Were we not satisfied the agency and the MSPB had applied them here for all they were worth, we could not affirm as we do.

Another difficult case is Ryan v. DOJ, 950 F.2d 458, 462 (7th Cir. 1991). A long-time FBI agent with an unblemished record refused an assignment to investigate a pacifist group, because he believed this interfered with his religious beliefs. Although the agent was just months away from an early (law enforcement) retirement, he was dismissed:

Sympathy for a dedicated agent trapped between his career and his faith comes easily. Perhaps the FBI would have shown greater forbearance had Ryan promised to retire when he turned 50. But he did not want to retire. Even now he seeks reinstatement. It is difficult for any organization to accommodate employees who are choosy about assignments; for a paramilitary organization the tension is even greater. Conscientious objectors in the military seek discharge, which accommodates their beliefs and the military's need for obedience. Ryan received discharge but does not want it. He wants to be an agent and to choose his assignments too. With good will all around, and flexibility on the part of Ryan's fellow agents, it just might be possible to make a go of it. Title VII does not, however, compel the FBI to attempt this. Legal institutions lack the sense of nuance that will tell an experienced agent how far the rules may be bent without injury to the FBI's mission. Compelled, as it is by Title VII, to have one rule for all of the diverse religious beliefs and practices in the United States, the FBI may choose to be stingy with exceptions lest the demand for them overwhelm it.
