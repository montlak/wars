a. Hearing

Allegations must be supported by proof. An appellant need not prove his case to get a hearing. He need only make a nonfrivolous allegation of involuntariness. He may still lose at the hearing: allegations must be supported by proof. When an appellant's version of the facts raised issues of possible abuse of agency procedures and discretion, the AJ erred by deciding the case without a hearing on voluntariness. Claxton v. DOJ, Bureau of Prisons, 6 MSPR 47, 49, 6 MSPB 51 (1981). Another AJ erred by denying a hearing to an appellant who alleged that she was not advised of any appeal rights concerning a contemplated removal, that she was not advised of the consequences of a resignation with an immediate effective date, that she was given no choice as to the effective date, that she was home ill at the time she made her decision, immediately after being first advised that leave would no longer be granted, and that a removal action was being prepared. Robinson v. Dept. of Navy, 30 MSPR 12, 14-15 (1986).

Not every allegation of involuntariness is sufficient to warrant a hearing concerning an allegedly coerced resignation. Some facts demonstrating probable jurisdiction must be presented to the Board, Bermudez v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1993 NP No. 92-3546):

[I]n order for the Board to have jurisdiction to consider Bermudez's resignation, Bermudez must have proffered a nonfrivolous allegation of involuntariness.

That the employee faces a difficult choice between two unpleasant alternatives (resignation or removal) does not suffice to meet this requirement. Here, Bermudez failed to respond to the AJ's specific request for some evidence supporting the bare allegation that his resignation had been coerced and thus was involuntary. Accordingly, the AJ properly found that the MSPB lacked jurisdiction under 5 USC 7512-13.

The Federal Circuit noted the difference between allegations required to reach a "voluntariness hearing" and proof of an involuntary resignation in Braun v. VA, 50 F.3d 1005, 1007-08 (Fed. Cir. 1995), involving an allegation that the appellant with a previously excellent record was targeted by a new supervisor:

Before January 1988, Mr. Braun had no record of either conduct or performance problems over a twelve-year period of government service. He asserts that he had an impeccable record of fully successful or superior performance.

In January 1988, Mr. Braun received a new supervisor. The relationship between Mr. Braun and his new supervisor was not without friction. Over the next seventeen months, Mr. Braun's last seventeen months of government service, he received eleven disciplinary actions. The actions include two written admonishments, three notices of minimally successful performance, a warning of unacceptable performance, a letter of counseling, a verbal counseling, a written reprimand, and a fourteen-day suspension. We note that the suspension was canceled before Mr. Braun's resignation. Mr. Braun asserts that these disciplinary actions evidence the improper coercion that resulted in his resignation.

In response to the disciplinary actions, Mr. Braun filed at least two grievances, a complaint with the Department of Labor, and nine informal complaints alleging discrimination or reprisal for having filed his earlier complaints. He also enlisted the aid of his congressman.

The AJ held that Mr. Braun failed to make a non-frivolous allegation that, if proven, could establish that his resignation was involuntary. In so holding, the AJ stated, "at the time of his resignation[,] no current action by the agency could be viewed as a threatened or attempted removal." Specifically, at that time, the agency was not going to remove him, to discipline him, to suspend him, to demote him, or to withhold or deny his leave. Although "[a] resignation is either voluntary or involuntary on the date it is submitted and jurisdiction must be determined as of that date," Cruz, 934 F.2d at 1244, the AJ's reliance on this snapshot at the instant of Mr. Braun's resignation misses the point. "To determine whether a resignation or retirement is voluntary, a court must examine 'the surrounding circumstances to test the ability of the employee to exercise free choice.'" Scharf v. Department of the Air Force, 710 F.2d 1572, 1574 (Fed. Cir. 1983) (quoting Perlman v. United States, 490 F.2d 928, 933 (Ct. Cl. 1974)). The record suggests that the supervisor's disciplinary actions were not infrequent--eleven actions in seventeen months. The frequency of the supervisor's actions thus bears upon the voluntariness of Mr. Braun's resignation, as do Mr. Braun's prior successful evaluations and his successful grievances. The AJ should also consider the issues of possible abuse of agency procedures and discretion. An agency must have reasonable grounds for threatening to take disciplinary actions. Merely because problems manifest themselves with the arrival of a new supervisor, however, does not require a conclusion that the new supervisor has targeted the disciplined employee.

...

...In light of what we have stated above and our careful consideration of the record before us, we hold that the AJ's decision was unsupported by substantial evidence and not in accordance with the law. We further hold that Mr. Braun has made a non-frivolous allegation that, if proven, could establish that his resignation was coerced. This non-frivolous allegation is all that is required to trigger the Board's jurisdiction at this threshold stage.... It would be illogical to require a petitioner to prove in advance by preponderant evidence that a resignation or retirement was involuntary to secure a hearing on that very issue.[2] When there is a question as to the voluntariness of the petitioner's resignation or retirement, and the petitioner makes a non-frivolous allegation of the involuntariness of the resignation or retirement, an evidentiary hearing is required to determine whether the resignation or retirement was in fact involuntary. Mr. Braun's situation meets these criteria and a "voluntariness hearing" is thus required. At that hearing, Mr. Braun bears the burden of proving by preponderant evidence that his resignation was involuntary and that the board thus has jurisdiction to hear the merits.

[2] The AJ stated, "Since the voluntariness of the resignation is a jurisdictional question, the appellant bears the burden of proving the issue by preponderant evidence." Slip op. at 2 (citing 5 CFR § 1201.56(b)(2)(i) [sic, (a)(2)(i)] (1994)). To establish his right to a hearing, however, Mr. Braun need not make a showing by preponderant evidence.

We therefore vacate the AJ's dismissal and remand for an evidentiary hearing. At the hearing, the AJ should (1) redetermine the voluntariness of Mr. Braun's resignation; (2) make a determination of the timeliness of Mr. Braun's petition; and (3), if appropriate, make a determination on the merits.

The Board made clear in Hurwitz v. Dept. of Army, 61 MSPR 436, 439-40 (1994), that an appellant receives a hearing based on nonfrivolous allegations of unbearable working conditions. An AJ is not to deny the hearing by essentially requiring the appellant to prove her case before the hearing--a form of summary adjudication:

A resignation is presumed to be a voluntary action, falling outside the Board's jurisdiction. Gettings v. Department of the Air Force, 48 MSPR 502, 509 (1991). Such presumption may be rebutted, however, by evidence that the resignation was the result of duress or coercion, or was otherwise involuntary. Id. The Board has found that where an employee's working conditions are rendered so intolerable by alleged discrimination or harassment that he is "forced" into resignation, there has been a constructive discharge, and the employee's resignation is involuntary. See Burke v. Department of Treasury, 53 MSPR 434, 438 (1992); Wilson v. U.S. Postal Service, 32 MSPR 244, 247 (1987). The ultimate question to be decided in this regard is whether working conditions were so difficult that a reasonable person in the employee's position would have felt compelled to resign. See Collins v. Argonne National Laboratory, 757 F. Supp. 934, 937 (N.D. Ill. 1991). Furthermore, an appellant who raises nonfrivolous allegations of involuntariness is entitled to a hearing on the jurisdictional question. Wilson, 32 MSPR at 246.

In the present case, the administrative judge found that the appellant did not establish that she was forced to resign due to intolerable working conditions, noting that witness testimony taken during the agency's EEO investigation contradicted her claims of agency discrimination and harassment. However, in order to establish the right to a jurisdictional hearing, an appellant need only make a prima facie case of involuntariness, and whereas the administrative judge may properly consider unrebutted evidence from the agency pertaining to the appellant's right to appeal, she may not weigh evidence constituting "mere factual contradiction of the appellant's otherwise adequate prima facie showing of jurisdiction...." Ferdon v. United States Postal Service, 60 MSPR 325, 329 (1994). Accordingly, the administrative judge erred in considering the testimony of the agency's witnesses, factually contesting the appellant's allegations of discrimination and harassment, when determining the appellant's right to a hearing. The administrative judge went on to state that "even assuming that the appellant's allegations concerning her working conditions are true, I am unconvinced that they rose to the level of 'intolerable,' amounting to duress, forcing her resignation," again erroneously suggesting that in order to gain the right to a hearing, the appellant needed to prove duress, when in fact she was only required to nonfrivolously allege it.

We find that the appellant's specific contentions of agency discrimination and harassment, including the issuance of unwarranted letters of concern, the assignment of work outside her job description that she could not perform owing to her handicapping condition, threats of removal, and verbal abuse, were sufficient to constitute a nonfrivolous allegation that her resignation was involuntary, entitling her to a hearing. See Wilson, 32 MSPR at 247 (appellant's allegations of agency's humiliating, arbitrary, and willful conduct, including illegal reassignment, issuance of letters of warning without cause, wrongful ejectment from his office, and denial of annual/sick leave requests, cast sufficient doubt upon voluntariness of retirement to require hearing, where requested).

How are facts presented to support allegations in a manner to avoid characterization of the allegations as "naked" or frivolous? The best support for allegations will be through detailed sworn statements submitted to the Board in support of an appeal or in response to either an AJ 's show cause order or an agency motion to dismiss. The sworn statements should be specific and factual, supported, should they be available, by relevant documents attached as exhibits. The facts described in the statements should support a showing of involuntariness, as measured by the case law from the Board and the Federal Circuit.
