1. OPM Regulations, FPM

OPM regulations at Title 5 of the CFR are government-wide regulations within the meaning of § 7117(a) of the Statute. NFFE Local 943 and Dept. of Air Force, Hq. Keesler Technical Training Ctr., 19 FLRA 949, 954 n.4 (1985). OPM regulations concerning time-in-grade restrictions were deemed government-wide regulations in PATCO and Dept. of Transp., FAA, 4 FLRA 232, 233 (1980).

The Federal Personnel Manual (FPM) was abolished on December 31, 1994, DODDS and Overseas Educ. Ass'n, 51 FLRA 210, 211 n.2 (1995), although portions of the FPM were abolished earlier. Several FLRA decisions construed the FPM while it was still extant. FLRA gave effect to the FPM when circumstances required, as when ignoring the FPM would lead to an inequitable result. AFGE Local 1843 and VAMC Northport, 51 FLRA 444, 449 n.4 (1995), explained, reviewing exceptions to an arbitrator's award:

Although FPM chapter 430 is no longer in effect...its abolishment has no consequence in deciding this case. First, since the Agency has not excepted to the Arbitrator's conclusion that the FPM was violated, any changes in the FPM need not be considered. Moreover, even if the changes were relevant, we would not give retroactive effect to the abolishment of the FPM in the circumstances of this case because to do so would impair the grievant's rights under the chapter and there is no indication in the FPM Sunset Document that the Office of Personnel Management intended this result. See U.S. Department of Transportation, Federal Aviation Administration, Little Rock, Arkansas, 51 FLRA No. 24, slip op. at 8-10 (1995) (no retroactive effect given to abolishment of an FPM Letter because to do so would impair rights).

NFFE Local 1497 and Dept. of Air Force, Lowry AFB, 9 FLRA 151, 154 (1982), determined the FPM was a government-wide regulation, although other cases have only ruled upon specific provisions of the Manual. FPM provisions implementing specific regulations were given the effect of government-wide regulations. But a court held that the entire FPM was not a government-wide regulation; it contained both policy guidance and binding regulations. AFGE Local 2782 v. FLRA, 803 F.2d 737, 741 (D.C. Cir. 1986), noting Fed./Postal/Retiree Coalition v. Devine, 751 F.2d 1424 (D.C. Cir. 1985). FPM Chapter 335, concerning merit promotion requirements, was a government-wide regulation. AFGE and Hq., Air Force Logistics Command, Wright-Patterson AFB, 11 FLRA 238, 241 (1983); cf. AFGE Local 2782 v. FLRA, 803 F.2d 737, 741-42 (D.C. Cir. 1986) (applying principles of statutory or administrative interpretation to FPM). FPM Supp. 792-2 (concerning drug testing and treatment) was a government-wide regulation. AFGE Local 1759 and Dept. of Army, Hq., Ft. McPherson, 31 FLRA 21, 29 (1988). FPM Chapter 332, Appendix I, "Guidelines for Selection Interviews," constituted guidance to agencies rather than a regulation within the meaning of 5 USC 7122. AFGE Local 1941 and Dept. of Army, Ft. McClellan, 51 FLRA 998, 1001 (1996).

FPM letters did not constitute regulations. They were advisory and not binding on agencies. The FPM letter could not substitute for or contravene published regulations. AFGE Local 1568 and DHUD, 21 FLRA 781, 783 (1986) ("The Authority has previously noted the OPM guidance set forth in FPM letters is merely that and not binding on Federal agencies. National Treasury Employees Union and Department of the Treasury, U.S. Customs Service, 21 FLRA NO. 2, slip op. at 4 n.3 (1986), petition for review filed sub nom. Department of the Treasury, U.S. Customs Service v. FLRA, No. 86-1198 (D.C. Cir. Mar. 27, 1986). Similarly, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit has ruled that OPM advice and guidance is precisely that and not binding on agencies. Federal/Postal/Retiree Coalition v. Devine, 751 F.2d 1424, 1431 (D.C. Cir. 1985)."). Under NTEU Chap. 41 and Dept. of Treasury, IRS, 57 FLRA 640, 644 (2001):

The Authority normally defers to guidance from OPM on statutory matters that OPM is entrusted to interpret if that guidance constitutes a reasonable interpretation of the statutory language. See AFGE, Local 1978, 56 FLRA 894, 897 (2000). However, interpretations which lack the force of law, such as opinion letters, manuals and the like, do not warrant such deference. United States Dep't of the Air Force, 436th Airlift Wing, Dover Air Force Base, Dover, Del., 57 FLRA 304, 307 (2001) citing Christensen, 529 U.S. 576. Under those circumstances, deference is appropriate only to the extent that those interpretations have the power to persuade. Christensen, 529 U.S. at 587. See also Pitsker v. OPM, 234 F.3d 1378 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (OPM's informal interpretation of disability retirement statute is to be given deference only if it is persuasive). In this case, OPM's interpretation of the Act carries such persuasive weight.

See AFGE Local 2006 and SSA, 65 FLRA 465, 469 (2011) (considering effect of OPM guidance as to application of FLSA to employees with flexible or compressed work hours).

OPM prelitigation advisory opinions are entitled to deference. See VAMC Washington and AFGE Local 2798, 67 FLRA 194, 197-98 (2014) (reviewing arbitration award involving temporary promotion). Statements made in OPM amicus briefs did not rise to the level of official OPM guidance or the FPM. DHHS, SSA and AFGE Local 3610, 29 FLRA 1334, 1345 (1987) (ALJ Decision). But OPM's interpretations of its regulations may be given weight by an adjudicator attempting to ascertain the meaning or effect of a government-wide regulation. See, e.g., NTEU v. FLRA, 30 F.3d 1510, 1516-17 (D.C. Cir. 1994).

The FPM was abolished--ostensibly to simplify agency regulatory practice. Some decisions cited in this Guide depend in part on construction of sections of the FPM. It must be determined, if those cases are to be relied upon as precedent, whether the FPM provisions applied at the time of FLRA's consideration of the dispute. If the FPM provisions are no longer applicable, the precedent may no longer be reliable, as FLRA suggested when it reviewed an arbitrator's award, AFGE Local 2142 and Dept. of Army, Corpus Christi Army Depot, 50 FLRA 44, 47 (1994):

We also conclude that there is no basis for finding the award deficient under FPM chapter 307. In U.S. Department of the Navy, Mare Island Naval Shipyard, Vallejo, California and Federal Employees Metal Trades Council, 49 FLRA 802, 811 (1994), the Authority addressed an exception that relied on an abolished FPM provision. Citing Aaacon Auto Transport v. ICC, 792 F.2d 1156, 1161 (D.C. Cir. 1986) and Bradley v. School Board of Richmond, 416 U.S. 696, 711 (1974), the Authority followed the well-established principle of administrative law that, in general, agencies must apply the law in effect at the time a decision is made, even when that law has changed during the course of a proceeding, unless doing so would result in manifest injustice or there is statutory direction or legislative history to the contrary. In this case, FPM chapter 307 was abolished effective December 31, 1993. In our view, the failure to apply the abolished provision in this case results in no manifest injustice and there is no statutory direction or legislative history to apply the abolished provision.
