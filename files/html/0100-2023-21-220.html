c. Accommodation Requirement; Disability Discrimination

Cronin v. USPS, 2022 MSPB 13, ¶ 21 (2022), revised the Board's analysis of accommodation claims in appeals involving agency restoration efforts:

The appellant alleged that the agency's denial of restoration was based on prohibited disability discrimination. In Latham, we suggested that a claim of unlawful discrimination or reprisal for protected activity could serve as an "alternative means" of showing that a denial of restoration was arbitrary and capricious. 117 M.S.P.R. 400, ¶ 58 n.27; see Paszko v. U.S. Postal Service, 119 M.S.P.R. 207, ¶ 15 (2013). This holding was incorrect because, as explained above, a denial of restoration is arbitrary and capricious for purposes of section 353.304(c) only if an agency fails to meet its obligation under section 353.301(d). See Bledsoe, 659 F.3d at 1103-04. Determining whether an agency met its obligation under section 353.301(d) will turn on whether it "ma[d]e every effort" to restore a partially recovered employee "in the local commuting area" and "according to the circumstances in each case." If the agency makes the required effort but fails to find suitable work for the appellant, the denial of restoration is not arbitrary and capricious, and the agency's lack of success cannot be attributed to any improper motive on its part. If, on the other hand, the agency fails to make the effort required under section 353.301(d), the resulting denial of restoration is necessarily arbitrary and capricious, and no further analysis is required. While an agency's failure to comply with section 353.301(d) may well be the result of prohibited discrimination or reprisal for protected activity, whether that is so is immaterial to the question of whether denying restoration in a particular instance is arbitrary and capricious for purposes of section 353.304(c).[12]

[12] We do not decide at present whether and how the Board should address discrimination and reprisal claims in the event jurisdiction under 5 C.F.R. § 353.304(c) has been independently established. See Latham, 117 M.S.P.R. 400, ¶ 58 n.27 (observing that the concept of an "affirmative defense" fits better in matters such as adverse action appeals when the agency bears the burden of proof on the merits).

Desjardin v. USPS, 2023 MSPB 6, ¶¶ 20-21, 27-29 (2023), supra, provided guidance on how the Board adjudicates disability discrimination (failure to accommodate) claims associated with a restoration rights appeal of a partially recovered employee

The Board in Cronin declined to address how it should address claims of discrimination and retaliation in restoration appeals within its jurisdiction. Cronin, 2022 MSPB 13, ¶ 21 n.12. In Latham, the Board stated that, in restoration appeals, claims of discrimination and reprisal should be understood as "independent claims" rather than as "affirmative defenses." Latham, 117 M.S.P.R. 400, ¶ 58 n.27. The Board indicated that the concept of an "affirmative defense" fits better in matters such as adverse action appeals when the agency bears the burden of proof on the merits.[11] Id.

[11] The Board in Latham also noted the possibility that discrimination and reprisal claims could serve as alternative ways for an appellant to show that the denial of restoration was arbitrary and capricious. Latham, 117 M.S.P.R. 400, ¶ 58 n.27. However, as we explained previously, that aspect of Latham is no longer good law under Cronin. Supra ¶ 16.

Despite the Board's criticism in Latham of the use of the term "affirmative defense" to describe the adjudication of discrimination and retaliation claims in restoration appeals, the Board has continued to adjudicate those claims in the same manner as it did before Latham. See, e.g., Davis v. U.S. Postal Service, 120 M.S.P.R. 122, ¶¶ 15-18 (2013), overruled on other grounds by Cronin, 2022 MSPB 13. We see no reason to alter that approach. Under 5 U.S.C. § 7702(a)(1), if an employee "has been affected by an action which [he] may appeal" to the Board and "alleges that a basis for the action was discrimination prohibited by" certain enumerated anti-discrimination statutes, the Board is required to "decide both the issue of discrimination and the appealable action in accordance with the Board's appellate procedures." Thus, once the appellant has established that the challenged action is within the Board's jurisdiction, the Board is required by statute to adjudicate both the action itself and any claim that the action was based on one of the enumerated categories of prohibited discrimination.

...

The appellant alleged disability discrimination under both reasonable accommodation and disparate treatment theories. The Board adjudicates claims of disability discrimination raised in connection with an otherwise appealable action under the substantive standards of section 501 of the Rehabilitation Act. Pridgen v. Office of Management and Budget, 2022 MSPB 31, ¶ 35. The Rehabilitation Act has incorporated the standards of the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), as amended by the Americans with Disabilities Act Amendments Act of 2008 (ADAAA). Id. Therefore, we apply those standards here to determine if there has been a Rehabilitation Act violation. Id. In particular, the ADAAA provides that it is illegal for an employer to "discriminate against a qualified individual on the basis of a disability." 42 U.S.C. § 12112(a). The Board recently clarified that only an otherwise qualified individual with a disability is entitled to relief, whether the individual alleges disability discrimination based on a disparate treatment or reasonable accommodation theory. Haas v. Department of Homeland Security, 2022 MSPB 36, ¶¶ 28-29.

A qualified individual with a disability is one who can "perform the essential functions of the...position that such individual holds or desires" with or without reasonable accommodation. 42 U.S.C. § 12111(8). Thus, an appellant can establish that he is a qualified individual with a disability by showing that he can, with or without accommodation, perform either the essential functions of his position of record or those of a vacant funded position to which he could be assigned. See Clemens v. Department of the Army, 120 M.S.P.R. 616, ¶ 11 (2014).

The appellant does not allege that he could perform the essential functions of his City Carrier position with or without accommodation, and the medical restrictions submitted by his doctor precluded "route walking...for the delivery of mail." We therefore find that the appellant did not establish that he was qualified based on his ability to perform the essential functions of the position he held. Thus, he would need to identify a vacant funded position to which he could have been reassigned in order to establish that he is a qualified individual with a disability. In addressing the appellant's reasonable accommodation claim, the administrative judge found that the appellant failed to meet his burden to show that there was any vacant position he could perform within his medical restrictions, even with a reasonable accommodation. We agree with the administrative judge's finding because the record does not show any existing vacant position to which the appellant could have been reassigned.[13] We therefore find that the appellant's disability discrimination claims under both disparate treatment and reasonable accommodation theories fail because he has not established that he is a qualified individual with a disability.

[13] Our finding that the agency failed to conduct a proper search for available positions within the local commuting area, supra ¶ 18, does not relieve the appellant of his burden to establish the existence of a position to which he could have been reassigned in order to establish that he is a qualified individual with a disability. The agency argued before the administrative judge that the appellant had not identified a position he could perform within his medical restrictions. The appellant acknowledged that argument and indicated that he had requested information in discovery regarding the availability of positions he could perform.

Law predating Cronin considered disability accommodation in the context of agency restoration responsibilities. Restoration rights for partially recovered employees, along with agency placement obligations and disability accommodation requirements were described in Sapp v. USPS, 73 MSPR 189, 192-95 (1997) (Member Amador dissented) (following remand, again remanding for factual determination as the scope of the local commuting area, Sapp v. USPS, 82 MSPR 411 (1999)):

Agencies must make very effort to restore, according to the circumstances in each case, an employee or former employee who has partially recovered from a compensable injury and who is able to return to limited duty. See 5 C.F.R. § 353.304; Leach v. Department of Commerce, 61 M.S.P.R. 8, 15 (1994). A "partially recovered" individual is one who has recovered sufficiently to return to less physically demanding work. See Leach, 61 M.S.P.R. at 15. As noted above, the appellant is a former employee who suffered a compensable injury, and it is also undisputed that she has "partially recovered" from that injury because her physician (Dr. Miller) has released her for return to less physically demanding duty. The agency thus was obligated to make every effort to restore the appellant under 5 C.F.R. § 353.304. The Board will review an agency's actions with respect to that provision only to determine whether it acted arbitrarily and capriciously. See Leach, 61 M.S.P.R. at 15.

The agency's obligation to make every effort to restore the appellant included a duty to search throughout the agency but within the local commuting area for vacant positions to which she could have been restored and to consider her for any such vacancies. See Leach, 61 M.S.P.R. at 19; Farrell v. Department of Justice, 50 M.S.P.R. 504, 510-13 (1991), overruled on other grounds by Leach v. Department of Commerce, 61 M.S.P.R. 8 (1994). In denying her request for restoration, the agency stated that it had conducted a search for available positions within the Santa Ana District (where the appellant had once worked) but that it had found none that met her medical restrictions. The headquarters of the Santa Ana District is in Santa Ana, California. An agency official confirmed the scope of the search in his testimony at the hearing. The administrative judge found that the agency's job search throughout the Santa Ana District satisfied its obligation to search throughout the local commuting area but offered no explanation for this conclusion. On petition for review, the appellant challenges this finding on the proper geographic scope of the agency's job search. For the reasons stated below, we find that this challenge has merit.

The Code of Federal Regulations defines "local commuting area" to mean the geographic area that usually constitutes one area for employment purposes; it includes any population center (or two or more neighboring ones) and the surrounding localities in which people live and can reasonably be expected to travel back and forth daily to their usual employment. See 5 C.F.R. § 351.203.[1] The administrative judge made no specific findings regarding whether the Santa Ana District geographically encompasses the entire local commuting area or whether the local commuting area includes localities outside the Santa Ana District from which the appellant could have commuted to her former duty station. As noted above, he merely concluded without explanation that the Santa Ana District was an "appropriate" search area. The record also contains no specific information on this point.

[1] Although this definition falls under section 351 rather than section 353, we find that it is also instructive in this context and use it here. The broad nature of this definition is also consistent with the approach of the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission; it has held that a "commuting area" is determined on a case-by-case basis. See Santos v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC No. 05940573, slip op. at 4 (Jan. 23, 1995).

It is therefore necessary to remand the case to the regional office so that the parties may fully develop the record regarding whether the agency searched throughout the agency but within the local commuting area for positions to which she could be restored and considered her for such vacancies. See Leach, 61 M.S.P.R. at 19. If the administrative judge determines on remand that the scope of the agency's search was inadequate, he shall order the agency to conduct a broader search encompassing the entire local commuting area, and the agency would then consider whether or not it could restore the appellant to duty. See Farrell, 50 M.S.P.R. at 513.

One finding that the administrative judge made in the initial decision, however, does warrant clarification. He apparently determined that the restoration issue and the question of whether the appellant's undisputed disability of carpal tunnel syndrome could be reasonably accommodated were, in fact, only one issue, and that he did not need to decide them separately. This determination is not entirely correct. An agency's obligation to accommodate an employee's disability is similar in nature and purpose to its obligation to restore her after a compensable injury. See Leach, 61 M.S.P.R. at 20. In making that statement in Leach, however, the Board relied on a provision of the Federal Personnel Manual (FPM) stating that the obligation to restore partially recovered individuals meant "[a]t a minimum...treating these employees substantially the same as other handicapped individuals under the Rehabilitation Act of 1973..."See id. (citing FPM ch. 353, subch. 2-4(d)(1) (June 21, 1988)). The phrase "at a minimum" seems to imply that an agency's obligation to restore an employee might be broader than its duty under the Rehabilitation Act.

That implication is indeed borne out by one important difference between the scope of the two obligations which is relevant here. Relying on a decision by the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC), the Board has held that the scope of an agency's obligation to accommodate a disabled employee is limited to offering reassignment to a vacant position that is, inter alia, located within the same commuting area and serviced by the same appointing authority. See Joe v. Department of the Army, 62 M.S.P.R. 408, 412 (1994); Hurst v. Department of the Navy, 61 M.S.P.R. 277, 282 (1994). That standard is codified in the EEOC's regulations. See 29 C.F.R. § 1614.203(g). In analyzing an agency's duty to restore a partially recovered employee, the Board has relied on the FPM provision already quoted above; that provision stated that "agencies must make very effort to place an injured employee who is able to work in some capacity in the local commuting area." See Farrell, 50 M.S.P.R. at 511-12 (citing FPM ch. 353, subch. 2-4(d)(1)) (emphasis in original).

In contrast to the EEOC standard on reasonable accommodation, however, the FPM provision on restoration does not limit the agency's obligation by stating that the position to which an employee might be reassigned must be in the local commuting area and serviced by the same appointing authority. We therefore have no basis for imposing such a limitation on an agency's obligation to restore a partially recovered employee and decline to do so. The administrative judge should bear this distinction in mind if the need arises on remand to address the appellant's affirmative defense of disability discrimination.

In accordance with our discussion above, the case is remanded to the regional office for a determination regarding whether the scope of the agency's search for vacant positions to which the appellant could be reassigned was adequate. If it was, the administrative judge shall issue an initial decision setting forth that conclusion and incorporating his previous findings on the remaining issues raised by the appeal; if not, he shall issue an initial decision ordering the agency to conduct an adequate search within the local commuting area and to consider the appellant for restoration based on the results of that search.

Accommodative requirements for partially recovered employees seeking restoration were also described in Taylor v. DHS, 107 MSPR 306, 308-09 ¶ 7-8, 2007 MSPB 276 (2007):

The agency has not objected to the administrative judge's finding that the appellant was partially recovered from her compensable injury and that she therefore had restoration rights under 5 C.F.R. § 353.301(d). Moreover, we see no error in that finding. See Hicks v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 M.S.P.R. 137, 142 (1995) ("the Board has held that an individual's current receipt of partial disability compensation would generally suffice to establish that he is a partially recovered individual" under part 353), quoted in Gilbert v. Department of Justice, 100 M.S.P.R. 375, ¶ 17 (2005). Under 5 C.F.R. § 353.301(d), an agency "must make every effort to restore in the local commuting area, according to the circumstances in each case, an individual who has partially recovered from a compensable injury and who is able to return to limited duty." That regulatory paragraph provides further that these efforts require, "[a]t a minimum, treating these employees substantially the same as other handicapped individuals under the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, as amended."

The Rehabilitation Act standards that are relevant here are the standards that are applied under the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 (ADA), as amended, and they are set forth in 29 C.F.R. part 1630. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.203(b). Under those regulations, the agency must make reasonable accommodation of an employee's disability through means such as modifying or adjusting the duties of the position at issue, or reassigning the employee to a vacant position whose duties the employee can perform. See 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(o)(1)(ii), (o)(2)(ii). Although an employee has a right to be assigned to a position for which she qualifies at her current grade level or, if no such position is available, to a position at a lower level, the agency's obligation to offer reasonable accommodation of the employee's medical condition does not entitle the employee to a position at a higher grade level. See Narcum v. Department of the Interior, 98 M.S.P.R. 645, ¶ 8 (2005) (an agency's obligation to accommodate an employee's disability includes the obligation to assign the employee to a vacant position in the same commuting area, at or below the employee's current grade level); Clopton v. Department of the Navy, 36 M.S.P.R. 373, 379 (1988) (to afford reasonable accommodation, an agency is not required to promote a qualified disabled employee to a higher-level position).

The Board adjudicates claims of disability discrimination raised in connection with an otherwise appealable action under the substantive standards of section 501 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973. Pridgen v. Office of Management and Budget, 2022 MSPB 31, ¶ 35. The Rehabilitation Act has incorporated the standards of the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), as amended by the Americans with Disabilities Act Amendments Act of 2008. Id. Therefore, the Board applies those standards here to determine if there has been a Rehabilitation Act violation. Id. In particular, the ADA provides that it is illegal for an employer to "discriminate against a qualified individual on the basis of disability." 42 U.S.C. § 12112(a). A qualified individual with a disability is one who can "perform the essential functions of the...position that such individual holds or desires" with or without reasonable accommodation. 42 U.S.C. § 12111(8). An employer is also required to provide reasonable accommodations to an otherwise qualified individual with a disability. 42 U.S.C. § 12112(b)(5). Therefore, an appellant may establish a disability discrimination claim based on failure to accommodate by showing that: (1) she is a disabled person; (2) she is a qualified individual with a disability; (3) the action appealed was based on her disability; and (4) to the extent possible, that there was a reasonable accommodation under which she believes she could perform the essential duties of her position or of a vacant position to which she could be reassigned. See Haas v. Department of Homeland Security, 2022 MSPB 36, ¶¶ 28-29....
