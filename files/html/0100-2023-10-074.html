6. Permitting Record Development

The Board consistently rejects the notion that its scope of review is limited to consideration of the administrative record established before the agency. The Board is required by 5 USC 7701(a) to conduct a hearing if requested by the appellant and to consider de novo all the relevant evidence presented by both parties, whether offered at a hearing or transmitted as a part of the administrative record. Nothing in the law or Board regulations restricts an agency to its documentary administrative record or withholds from the agency a full and fair opportunity to cross-examine or otherwise rebut an appellant's evidence presented for the first time at the hearing before the Board. Foster v. DHHS, 18 MSPR 339, 341-42 (1983). [Refer to Chapter 1, "De Novo Review of Agency Actions."]

The AJ is authorized to limit submissions of the parties to avoid repetition and confusion, Fritz v. DHHS, 87 MSPR 287, 293-94 ¶¶ 14-16 (2000):

The appellant's submissions are all very lengthy, repetitive, and somewhat confusing. If the administrative judge should deem it necessary to permit him to submit additional material, he should be instructed to limit the length of the submissions. His consistent practice of providing voluminous answers to straightforward questions, and to detailing what often appear to be repeated claims in alternative ways has not served him or the adjudicatory process well, and has, instead, obfuscated the core issue of whether he has been subjected to reprisal for any genuine act of whistleblowing he may have done.

In this regard, we note the well-established rule that administrative judges have broad discretion in regulating the proceedings before them, see, e.g., Niswonger v. Department of the Air Force, 64 MSPR 665, 672 (1994), including but not limited to those enumerated at 5 CFR § 1201.41. Thus, we have approved an administrative judge's efforts to focus the evidence "on the specific issues of whistleblower reprisal before her instead of allowing a more general inquiry regarding the history of" the agency. Niswonger, id. We have also cautioned an appellant who had "continually submitted numerous extremely voluminous filings" that as to any additional opportunity he may be given to gather and submit evidence, "we emphasize that he is required to comply with the administrative judge's orders and is subject to sanctions for any future noncompliance." Wildberger v. Small Business Administration, 65 MSPR 673, 677 (1994) (emphasis in the original). Indeed, our main reviewing court has held that a party before the Board ignores a Board order at his peril. Mendoza v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 966 F.2d 650, 653-54 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (en banc).

Accordingly, should the administrative judge, in her discretion, direct the appellant to make any new, or synthesize any current submissions in a concise and efficient manner, he must comply.

Although an AJ may exclude repetitious testimony, he is obliged to "scrupulously avoid rulings which deny employees the fair and impartial trial guaranteed to them by the statute and regulation." Jahn v. USDA (Fed. Cir. 1989 NP No. 88-3358), citing Frampton v. Dept. of Interior, 811 F.2d 1486, 1489 (Fed. Cir. 1987). An AJ who cuts off the appellant's attempts to present his own testimony and supporting evidence or defenses risks reversal and remand. An AJ called on counsel to provide an offer of proof as to the defense of an appellant and precluded his testimony, over counsel's objections, when it was ruled insufficient. The Board remanded the appeal for rehearing, Long v. Dept. of Transp., 17 MSPR 384, 386 (1983):

The appellant under 5 USC 7701 has a statutory right to a hearing. Inherent in this right to a hearing is the right of appellant to testify and present witnesses in support of his or her contentions. Interstate Commerce Comm'n v. Louisville & Nashville R. Co., 227 U.S. 88 (1913); Londoner v. Denver, 210 U.S. 373, 386 (1908).... Also inherent in appellant's right to a hearing is the right to present every available defense. American Surety Co. v. Baldwin, 287 U.S. 156 (1932)....

The Federal Circuit remanded an appeal when an AJ who improperly precluded testimony, Frampton v. Dept. of Interior, 811 F.2d 1486, 1489-90 (Fed. Cir. 1987):

During the testimony of the agency's first witness, the presiding official announced that he was going to conclude the hearing in 2 days. There is nothing in the record to indicate that in pre-hearing proceedings, the parties had estimated that the presentation of their evidence could be finished in 2 days. Consequently, we find that the presiding official's determination to end the hearing in 2 days was arbitrary. As hereinafter shown, the presiding official pursued that determination in subsequent proceedings and thereby abused his discretion by denying petitioner the opportunity to complete the presentation of his testimony.

The agency's presentation consumed 1 1/2 days; it included the testimony of 8 witnesses, the offering of 27 exhibits, and a hearing file containing 23 documentary items. The petitioner presented 6 witnesses, including himself, and a number of documentary exhibits. The hearing of petitioner's evidence began after lunch on September 6, 1985, the second day of the hearing. At the close of that day's proceeding, the hearing was continued to September 10, 1985. About 2 hours into that third day of the hearing, the presiding official informed petitioner's attorney that he had 15 minutes to complete his evidence. A few minutes later, the presiding official advised petitioner's attorney "you have 5 minutes to wrap this up." At that point, petitioner's attorney stated that the agency's witnesses had produced evidence covering a 4-year period, thereby implying that he had the right to cover the same period with petitioner's evidence. The presiding official stated that the record would note the comments of the attorney, but that he would have only "5 minutes to wrap it up."

Following the cross-examination of petitioner on September 10, petitioner's attorney asked for permission to question his client. The presiding official refused the request, stating: "I will not allow you any redirect."

A fair hearing for employees who appeal to the MSPB from agency decisions is the basic cornerstone of employee rights. Under the statutory scheme, the hearing before the presiding official of the MSPB is the only opportunity which a discharged employee or one subjected to an adverse personnel action has to a de novo trial before an impartial judge.... This is so because the scope of judicial review is severely limited by the language of the statute....

Under 5 CFR 1201.41(b)(9), presiding officials have the authority to exclude truly irrelevant or overly repetitious testimony. Perkins v. VA, 21 MSPR 58, 60 (1984). However, they should scrupulously avoid rulings which deny employees the fair and impartial trial guaranteed to them by the statute and regulation. When, as in this case, a presiding official cuts off an employee's attempt to present his testimony, supporting evidence, or defenses, the presiding official risks reversal of his decision....

...

As previously stated, one of petitioner's principal defenses is that his termination was due to prohibited personnel practices on the part of his supervisor rather than to his unsatisfactory work performance as charged by his agency. Since petitioner was summarily prohibited from completing the presentation of his evidence on this issue, he was effectively denied the right to the full and fair hearing envisioned in the Board's decision in Long v. Dept. of Transp., 17 MSPR at 386.

...

Although we are remanding the case to the Board, we find it necessary to call attention to the fact that petitioner and his attorney were not without fault in the presentation of petitioner's case before the presiding official. Our review of the transcript shows that at times the testimony elicited from petitioner was repetitive and that many of his answers were rambling and unresponsive to the questions propounded to him. Also, it appears from the record that petitioner's attorney did not make adequate preparation in advance of the hearing regarding the evidence he intended to offer. It may well be that these incidents caused the presiding official to lose patience and to stop the petitioner's testimony. Therefore, at the additional hearing held in accordance with this order, the petitioner is strongly cautioned that when questions are propounded to him, his answers should be succinct and directly responsive to the questions, avoiding the narration of irrelevant matters. It will also be the responsibility of petitioner's attorney to prepare the case in advance of the additional hearing and to avoid burdening the presiding official with irrelevant testimony or repetitive evidence.

On remand, a different AJ took the omitted testimony and once again decided against the appellant. The case went back to the Federal Circuit on a question of whether the Board should have heard the entire case again. It was decided that the Board was correct in only supplementing the existing record. The AJ assigned to the remanded appeal correctly relied on the earlier record (hearing tapes and exhibits) without any indication that there were issues of credibility as to witnesses who earlier testified. Frampton v. Dept. of Interior, 880 F.2d 1314 (Fed. Cir. 1989).

After Frampton I, MSPB determined that an AJ may not exclude otherwise admissible testimony solely because of concern for the length of the hearing. But if testimony is limited, for reversible error to occur, it must be demonstrated that resolution of credibility on the issue under examination would likely have affected the outcome of the case. Taylor v. Dept. of Treasury, 34 MSPR 495, 499-500 (1987) (limitation of the right to examine a witness concerning prior inconsistent statements). If the AJ takes action, for example, excusing witnesses following their direct and cross-examination, and the appellant believes that the witnesses should remain to provide further testimony, the appellant or party must request that the AJ do what is necessary to ensure the witnesses remain available, i.e., the party must object to their being excused. The parties must make objections to the conduct of the hearing at the time when the objections will do some good. Curry v. Dept. of Air Force, 35 MSPR 301, 304 (1987); see Valverde v. Dept. of Army, 40 MSPR 380, 383-84 (1989) (failure to object during the hearing or in the posthearing brief to the restriction of the hearing to one rather than two days). [Refer later in this chapter to "Witness Request" and "Protecting Record; Missing Witness."]

Although the Board's position once was that discrimination issues need not be reached if an appealed decision was reversed on procedural grounds, that view was subsequently overruled. Under current law, an AJ must hear and decide issues of discrimination and prohibited personnel practices, irrespective of the existence and controlling effect of a procedural error. See Morey v. Dept. of Navy, 34 MSPR 97, 100 (1987). If the appellant raises a whistleblowing defense, it is the responsibility of the AJ to permit a full and fair hearing, including allowing evidence by the appellant concerning the bona fides of the agency action against him. Mongird v. Dept. of Navy, 33 MSPR 504, 507 (1987) (involving an appellant prevented from showing that the agency improperly initiated its investigation against him as a union official concerning comments published by the union in a newsletter).
