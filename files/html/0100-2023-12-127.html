2. What Evidence Is New

Ordinarily new evidence is evidence that develops after the initial decision becomes final. Evidence that is already in the record is not new, Johnson v. OPM, 61 MSPR 293, 295 (1994). Parker v. DLA, 4 MSPR 102, 103 (1980):

...Many of the items are duplicates of documents already made a part of the extensive record developed in this case. Other documents accompanying the petition for review merely recite information already introduced into the record. All of the documents predate the present initial decision. To the extent the documents relied on in the petition for review are not merely duplicative of items already a part of the record, they are either part of agency records or are apparently from the personal files of appellant. Petitioner makes no claim that he exercised due diligence in seeking to obtain and submit the documents prior to the closing of the record. Nor is there any claim that any of the documents were withheld from petitioner by the agency. Indeed, many of petitioner's submissions are documents either sent or received by petitioner himself and no explanation is given as to why petitioner was unable to present the evidence before the record was closed.

Evidence is new if, as a practical matter, it was received at a point when it could not readily have been submitted to the AJ. Becker v. VA, 112 MSPR 507, 510-11 ¶ 8, 2009 MSPB 204 (2009) (jurisdictionally significant letter from the Department of Labor in a VEOA case was dated one day before the issuance of the initial decision).

Some appellants submit with their PFR copies of exhibits already in the record. If evidence is already in the record, it may be referenced in the PFR. See Williams v. NLRB, 59 MSPR 640, 644 n.1 (1993). The Board will not, however, accept evidence as "new" when the appellant believes he submitted the evidence to the agency in response to a proposal notice but it was not included in the agency's response to the appeal. The appellant was on notice, after reviewing the agency response, that the document was not in the file before the Board. The appellant should then have submitted the material before the close of the record on appeal. See Robinson v. USPS, 32 MSPR 352, 354 (1987). If the appellant alleges that he unsuccessfully attempted to submit documents to the AJ, the evidence does not become "new" through submission with the PFR. For the Board to accept the documents, the appellant would need to establish that the AJ refused to accept those documents. Even then, the appellant's failure to timely object to the AJ's alleged refusal to accept the documents would preclude the evidence from being submitted with the PFR. See Pugh v. USPS, 91 MSPR 578, 582 ¶ 8 (2002). [Refer to Chapter 3, "Closing Record."]

Record evidence, although not new, must be justified as new and material evidence if it is expanded upon in the PFR. If portions of a collective bargaining contract are submitted to the AJ, the Board will disregard the submission of the entire contract with the PFR to the extent that the PFR submission adds to what was before the AJ. See Lowe v. DOD, 67 MSPR 97, 99 (1995). As to evidence submitted after the record closes and before the initial decision is issued, the Board is inclined to accept it if efforts were made to file the evidence prior to the record closing and if the submission was delayed through events beyond the control of the party who made the proffer, e.g., a delay in mail or delivery service. See Goshorn v. Dept. of Air Force, 31 MSPR 374, 375 (1986). Documentary evidence dated a day before the initial decision was issued was considered new. See Schneider v. OPM, 37 MSPR 181, 182 (1988) (SSA benefits award certificate affecting an overpayment recovery).

Lockard v. Dept. of Interior, 74 MSPR 576, 581 (1997), marked a departure from prior law, or at least an expansion of prior law, when it implied that under some circumstances, evidence bearing upon a significant issue could be submitted with a PFR if an AJ had not informed the appellant of the significance of the type of evidence proffered by the appellant at the PFR stage. Although an alternative reading of Lockard is that the Board permitted the appellant to submit with his PFR some evidence that apparently was part of the record before the AJ but somehow did not make its way into the file that reached the Board on PFR, the Board's holding was more expansive than a declaration that record evidence may be submitted with a PFR:

[T]he Board will consider evidence when the appellant was not put on notice of what the administrative judge considered to be the dispositive issue until the issuance of the initial decision. Williams v. USPS, 61 MSPR 213, 215 (1994). Here, the appellant was not on notice that his tenure in a previous position was a dispositive issue or that the record evidence did not include the documentation he submitted that showed this tenure until after the initial decision had been issued. Further, because his appeal was decided on the record, there was no hearing where he would have been made aware that the record did not include these documents. Accordingly, we find it appropriate to consider the documentary evidence submitted by the appellant. Id.

Lockard may signal a greater responsibility on the part of Board AJs to identify for appellants the issues and the types of evidence bearing on those issues that the appellant needs to make out a case. See McVicker v. SSA, 77 MSPR 324, 329 (1997) (when the AJ first raises a jurisdictional issue in an initial decision, the Board considers evidence first submitted with the PFR). [Refer in Chapter 2 to "Pro Se and Inadequately Represented Parties"; refer later in this chapter to "Timeliness of Appeal."]

Under MacMurdo v. USDA, 24 MSPR 388, 391-92 (1984), an initial decision in another appeal is not new evidence:

[T]he Board has previously found that an initial decision in another case before the Board does not constitute new and material evidence. See Taggert v. U.S. Postal Service, 3 MSPB 220, 221, 3 MSPR 108, 110 (1980). The decision of the Board is based on the evidence presented in each individual case and it is not improper to reach differing conclusions in cases involving the same issue where the evidence before the Board is not the same. Berry v. Department of Energy, 21 MSPR 95 (1984)....

See Brooks v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-14-0752-I-1 (NP 7/2/2015) ("Although the initial decision in Saiz was issued after the conclusion of the hearing in this appeal, and therefore was unavailable before the close of the record in the appellant's appeal, see 5 C.F.R. § 1201.58, the Board has long held that an initial decision issued in another case does not constitute new and material evidence, e.g., Taggart v. U.S. Postal Service, 3 M.S.P.R. 108, 110 (1980); Berry v. Department of Energy, 21 M.S.P.R. 95, 96 (1984).").

Evidence of nonpayment of back pay is new and material to a belated PFR alleging that the agency failed to restore the status quo when it rescinded an adverse action that was appealed and dismissed as moot. See Jasper v. USPS, 88 MSPR 27, 30-31 ¶¶ 9-10 (2001); cf. O'Connell v. Dept. of Navy, 73 MSPR 235, 241 (1997) (GAO decision concerning the appellant's request for waiver of the recovery of a debt was new because it was not transmitted to the appellant until after he filed a PFR). Evidence is new that, after the initial decision on a retirement appeal was issued, the agency removed an employee for physical inability to work. That evidence bears upon the proof of the employee's disability retirement application. See Booth v. OPM, 99 MSPR 13 (2005).
