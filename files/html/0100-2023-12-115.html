h. Mitigation or Cancelation of Suspension

If the appeal involves a suspension and the suspension is mitigated to a reprimand, no interim relief is to be provided if, by the date the initial decision is issued, the suspension was served and the appellant already returned to work and pay status. The AJ should not order interim relief, and failure of the agency to provide evidence of compliance with an interim relief order will not warrant dismissal of the appeal. McIntire v. FEMA, 55 MSPR 578 (1992), followed in Bruneau v. Dept. of Navy, 73 MSPR 308, 311 (1997); see Tellez v. DOJ, 70 MSPR 241, 242 (1996) (interim relief was improperly ordered when the AJ reversed a 30-day suspension that had already been served); Aiu v. DOJ, 70 MSPR 509, 515 (1996) ("Here, the appellant had been returned to work and restored to the pay, compensation, and benefits of his position by the time that the initial decision was issued because he had already served his suspension. Nothing else was required of the agency to provide interim relief under 5 USC § 7701(b)(2)(A)."); Kimm v. Dept. of Treasury, 64 MSPR 198, 203-04 (1994) (AJ erred by directing interim relief when a suspension was already served; the appellant was returned to work and restored to the pay, compensation, and benefits of his position by the time the initial decision was issued, so nothing else was required of the agency; back pay was not due as a component of interim relief); Wilson v. DOJ, 58 MSPR 96, 101 (1993) (no interim relief was appropriate when a suspension was mitigated to a shorter suspension and the suspension was served by the time the initial decision was issued); Weaver v. USDA, 55 MSPR 569, 576 (1992) (no interim relief was due when the AJ directed cancelation of the suspension; by the time of the initial decision, the suspension period had elapsed and "there is nothing that an agency can do to effect interim relief, for the disciplined employee has returned to duty and been restored to the pay, compensation, and benefits of his position").

Edwards v. Dept. of Navy, 62 MSPR 174, 177-78 (1994), concluded that when the AJ should not have ordered interim relief in the circumstances of a suspension mitigated to a reprimand, the agency's PFR was not mooted by the agency's cancelation of the suspension and issuance of a reprimand as "interim relief":

...The agency's apparent belief that it must do something more in light of the issuance of an interim relief order is understandable, however. We find that it would be unfair to dismiss an agency's petition for review for its good faith attempt to comply with an interim relief order that should not have been issued in the first place. We therefore hold that, when an administrative judge erroneously orders interim relief despite the appealed suspension having already been served, and the agency has attempted to comply by cancelling the suspension, the Board will not dismiss the agency's petition as moot.
