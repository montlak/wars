n. Step Increases

Arbitrators' awards concerning step-increase (AKA within grade increase) denials are not adverse or performance-based under 5 USC 7121(f); FLRA reviews the awards. Dept. of Educ., Div. of Civil Rights and AFGE Local 3887, 17 FLRA 997, 999 (1985). Procedurally, "an employee who has been denied a within-grade increase on the basis that he was not performing at an acceptable level of competence must be properly notified of the obligation to request reconsideration of the negative determination and the employee must then timely request reconsideration of the negative determination before filing a grievance challenging the denial of the increase." POPA and PTO, 34 FLRA 883, 886 (1990), reconsideration denied, 35 FLRA 1020, 1022 (1990); followed in DHHS, SSA and AFGE Local 1336, 35 FLRA 1167, 1169-70 (1990); cf. DHHS, Appalachian Lab., NIOSH, COC and AFGE Local 3430, 49 FLRA 1150, 1160 (1994) (although reconsideration request is mandatory, if request is made and agency does not timely respond to request, arbitrator may find agency constructively denied reconsideration).

For an arbitrator to award a retroactive within-grade increase, the arbitrator must find that agency action withholding or denying the increase was unwarranted and that but for the unwarranted action the grievant otherwise would have received the within-grade increase. The arbitrator must conclude either that the negative determination was not sustained or that due to some improper agency action or omission the work of the grievant was determined not to be at an acceptable level of competence when it otherwise would have been. Dept. of Educ., Div. of Civil Rights and AFGE Local 3887, 17 FLRA 997, 998 (1985). If the arbitrator directs the agency to provide a performance improvement period for the grievant and then to award a step increase upon a demonstration of acceptable performance, the award cannot be retroactive, for it would then mandate an increase for a period of performance at a less than acceptable level of competence. See DHHS, Appalachian Lab., NIOSH, COC and AFGE Local 3430, 49 FLRA 1150, 1161 (1994) (applying 5 CFR 531.412(b)).

In a step-increase case, there is no statutorily mandated standard of proof. The arbitrator establishes whatever standard he considers appropriate. Dept. of Educ., Div. of Civil Rights and AFGE Local 3887, 17 FLRA 997, 999 (1985). A caveat: although a contract may have strict requirements as to step-increase denials, before an arbitrator can award a retroactive within grade increase ("WIGI"), he must find that but for the improper management action the work of the employee would have met the acceptable level of competence standard. SSA, Office of Hearings and Appeals and AFGE Local 3506, 17 FLRA 1056, 1057 (1985). Because 5 USC 7701(c) applies to the MSPB but not to arbitrators' decisions involving step-increase denials, governed by 5 USC 5335(a), a retroactive step-increase cannot be awarded on the basis of a finding of harmful procedural error. There must be a determination that but for the agency error or contract violation the grievant's performance would have been at an acceptable level of competence. NTEU and IRS, 17 FLRA 1058, 1059 (1985). The failure of the agency to permit the grievant the full contract period to show improvement prior to the date he became eligible for a step-increase is, in itself, not a sufficient basis for the arbitrator to award the step increase. DHHS, SSA and AFGE, 17 FLRA 1061, 1062 (1985). Denial of an improvement period resulted in a valid award of a retroactive step increase when that procedural error was accompanied by a determination that the grievant's performance would have been satisfactory absent the procedural error. AFGE Local 3512 and Data Operations Ctr., SSA, 32 FLRA 940, 942-43 (1988) ("The Arbitrator ruled that the Activity had not properly determined that the grievant was not performing at an acceptable level of competence. He found that the Activity had violated Articles 21 and 22 of the parties' collective bargaining agreement. The Arbitrator further found that but for the violations of the agreement, the grievant's work would have been determined to have been at an acceptable level of competence and the grievant would have been granted the within-grade increase. Therefore, the award of a retroactive within-grade increase is consistent with 5 U.S.C. § 5335(a) and the Back Pay Act. See U.S. Department of Education, Division of Civil Rights, Atlanta, Georgia and American Federation of Government Employees, Local Union No. 3887, Atlanta, Georgia, 17 FLRA 997 (1985).").

SSA Region IX and AFGE Council 147, 29 FLRA 1331, 1332-33 (1987), reiterated the determinants of a step increase claimed through the grievance process:

[W]ith respect to the denying or withholding of a within-grade increase, the Authority has recognized under 5 U.S.C. 5335(a) that in order for an employee to be entitled to the increase, the work of the employee must be determined to be at an acceptable level of competence. Social Security Administration, 17 FLRA at 1064. Thus, in order for an award by an arbitrator of a retroactive within-grade increase to be authorized, the arbitrator must find that agency action in connection with the withholding or denying of the increase was unwarranted and that but for the unwarranted action, the grievant would have received the within-grade increase. The arbitrator must find either that the negative determination was not sustained or that due to some action or inaction on the part of the agency, the work of the grievant was determined not to be at an acceptable level of competence when it otherwise would have been. Id.

Although the Arbitrator found that the Activity had violated the parties' collective bargaining agreement, he did not make the findings necessary for awarding a retroactive within-grade increase. He did not invalidate the negative determination. More particularly, he did not find that but for the violation of the agreement in not providing the grievant with at least 60 days to improve, the grievant's work otherwise would have been determined to have been at an acceptable level of competence, and the grievant would have been granted the within-grade increase. Accordingly, the Arbitrator's awarding of a retroactive within-grade increase is deficient and must be struck from the award.
