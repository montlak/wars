4. Adequacy of RIF Notice

5 CFR 351.801 (2022), provides:

Notice period.

(a) (1) Each competing employee selected for release from a competitive level under this part is entitled to a specific written notice at least 60 full days before the effective date of release.

(2) At the same time an agency issues a notice to an employee, it must give a written notice to the exclusive representative(s), as defined in 5 U.S.C. 7103(a)(16), of each affected employee at the time of the notice. When a significant number of employees will be separated, an agency must also satisfy the notice requirements of §§ 351.803 (b) and (c).

(b) When a reduction in force is caused by circumstances not reasonably foreseeable, the Director of OPM, at the request of an agency head or designee, may approve a notice period of less than 60 days. The shortened notice period must cover at least 30 full days before the effective date of release. An agency request to OPM shall specify:

(1) The reduction in force to which the request pertains;

(2) The number of days by which the agency requests that the period be shortened;

(3) The reasons for the request; and

(4) Any other additional information that OPM may specify.

(c) The notice period begins the day after the employee receives the notice.

(d) When an agency retains an employee under § 351.607 or § 351.608, the notice to the employee shall cite the date on which the retention period ends as the effective date of the employee's release from the competitive level.

As to the specific content of a notice of a RIF, 5 CFR 351.802 (2022), requires:

Content of notice.

(a) (1) The action to be taken, the reasons for the action, and its effective date;

(2) The employee's competitive area, competitive level, subgroup, service date, and three most recent ratings of record received during the last 4 years;

(3) The place where the employee may inspect the regulations and record pertinent to this case;

(4) The reasons for retaining a lower-standing employee in the same competitive level under § 351.607 or § 351.608;

(5) Information on reemployment rights, except as permitted by § 351.803(a); and

(6) The employee's right, as applicable, to appeal to the Merit Systems Protection Board under the provisions of the Board's regulations or to grieve under a negotiated grievance procedure. The agency shall also comply with § 1201.21 of this title.

(b) When an agency issues an employee a notice, the agency must, upon the employee's request, provide the employee with a copy of OPM's retention regulations found in part 351 of this chapter.

5 CFR 351.805 (2022) adds:

New notice required.

(a) An employee is entitled to a written notice of at least 60 full days if the agency decides to take an action more severe than first specified.

(b) An agency must give an employee an amended written notice if the reduction in force is changed to a later date. A reduction in force action taken after the date specified in the notice given to the employee is not invalid for that reason, except when it is challenged by a higher-standing employee in the competitive level who is reached out of order for a reduction in force action as a result of the change in dates.

(c) An agency must give an employee an amended written notice and allow the employee to decide whether to accept a better offer of assignment under subpart G of this part that becomes available before or on the effective date of the reduction in force. The agency must give the employee the amended notice regardless of whether the employee has accepted or rejected a previous offer of assignment, provided that the employee has not voluntarily separated from his or her official position.

Other notice provisions are established in the OPM regulations, 5 CFR Part 351 Subpart H. Prior regulations, referred to in some decisions, had a shorter notice period. 5 USC 3502(d) requires a 60-day advance notice of a RIF, but the notification provision does not yet appear to have been interpreted in a final decision of the Board or in a decision by the Federal Circuit.

5 CFR 351.802 (2022) does not require an agency to use any specific language in describing the reason for a RIF. Cummings v. DOD, 6 MSPR 202, 204, 6 MSPB 178 (1981). A RIF notice does not have to state the specific reasons for the action. May v. ICC, 20 MSPR 557, 561 (1984). The RIF notice need only describe one of the reasons enumerated in 5 CFR 351.201(a) (2022). Rosen v. ICC, 20 MSPR 574 (1984). A RIF is not defective simply because the notice of the RIF fails to identify the regulatory basis for the RIF. See Precious v. TVA, 15 MSPR 176, 178 (1983). The RIF notice is sufficient if it states that the RIF is being conducted because of current and projected budgetary restraints and that the personnel office is available to respond to questions. Id.; see Bennally v. Dept. of Interior, 14 MSPR 132, 141 (1982) ("Although section 351.802 expressly itemizes many of the substantive rights that an employee who is affected by a RIF must be apprised of, it does not require that an agency expressly set forth the reasons for the RIF in the notice."). The reference to a "formal" announcement of a RIF in 5 CFR 351.201(a)(2) (2022) applies only to RIFs that are based upon reclassification of employees positions resulting from erosion of duties. According to Wreen v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1998 NP No. 98-3078), an agency may amend the reason stated for the RIF after the initiation of a Board appeal. In Wreen the court found no harmful error occurred when the agency cited a reorganization as the reason for the RIF and then, within a month from the filing of the appeal, the agency explained that the basis for the RIF (and the reorganization) was a shortage of funds.

The agency's burden is greater if the employee challenges the reason stated for the RIF, Rosen v. ICC, 20 MSPR 574, 577 (1984):

In the instant case, appellant has challenged the reason offered by the agency as the basis for the RIF in appellant's office. 3 Therefore, while the RIF notice offered by the agency may be considered as tending to show the RIF was undertaken for a valid reason, we cannot find that it alone establishes by a preponderance of the evidence that RIF regulations were properly invoked. The presiding official's application of the Copeland standard to the instant case was error. The evidence offered by the agency can be considered in evaluating the propriety of the RIF. We therefore must examine all of the evidence under Losure v. Interstate Commerce Commission, 2 MSPB 361, 365-66 (1980), to determine if the presiding official erred in finding that the agency failed to sustain its burden of showing that the RIF was based upon a shortage of work as permitted under 5 CFR 351.201(a).

Rosen cited the "Copeland standard," a reference to Copeland v. CSA, 6 MSPR 280 (1981), determining that an adverse action proposal is analogous to an indictment and not evidence of the charges it contains. Copeland was limited by other decisions construing the evidentiary value of proposal notices, but the point of Rosen was that the limitations then imposed by Copeland on adverse action notices are inapplicable to RIF notices.

Although the notice need not quote the language of 5 CFR 351.201(a) (2022) concerning the particular reason for the RIF, the RIF notice should provide a valid RIF reason. See Bacon v. DHUD, 20 MSPR 408, 414 (1984). Bacon was reviewed by the Federal Circuit. Bacon v. DHUD, 757 F.2d 265 (Fed. Cir. 1985). [Bacon is discussed earlier in this chapter, "Funding Decision"and "Reorganization Defined; Single Position RIF"]. The facts were summarized in the opinion of the court. In 1982 a general RIF notice was issued informing employees that "to resolve workload and skills imbalances," the Department was conducting a RIF. Later, specific RIF notices were issued setting the date of the RIF and reiterating the reason for the RIF as "workload and skills imbalances." On appeal it was argued that the stated reason was not one of the purposes authorized for a RIF by 5 CFR 351.201(a) (lack of work, shortage of funds, reorganization, reclassification due to change in duties, and exercise of reemployment or restoration rights). The court agreed with the Board's determination that the correction of workload and skills imbalances was within the scope of a reorganization, defined by regulation as the "planned elimination, addition, or redistribution of functions or duties in an organization." HUD, it was stated, sought to remedy imbalances in staffing at its headquarters caused by budget and program changes through the RIF, an action that the court concluded "clearly falls within the term 'reorganization.'" [Refer to Chapter 11, "Proposal Notice" for the effect of errors in a proposal notice.]

The agency's failure to use the term "reorganization" in the RIF notice does not conclusively determine whether, in fact, a reorganization occurred. The Board examines the evidence to determine whether the agency's action met the definition of reorganization. Holmes v. Dept. of Army, 41 MSPR 612, 615 (1989). The Board examined the validity of what constituted a reorganization in an appeal from a transfer of function. See Hasler v. Dept. of Air Force, 48 MSPR 207, 213 (1991) (remanding for consideration of whether the appellant was harmed by the use of transfer of function rather than RIF procedures).

Lack of an advance notice of release through RIF is subject to the harmful error rule. Washington v. TVA, 22 MSPR 377, 380 (1984) (had the employee not been terminated by an adverse action, he would have been subjected to a RIF; omission of a proper notice was harmless), aff'd, Washington v. TVA (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 85-660); cf. Barton v. Dept. of Educ., 20 MSPR 451, 453 (1984) (notice period is to be measured in calendar rather than working days; "Although the specific notice date cited by the presiding official is erroneous, appellant has not shown his substantive rights were affected by this error."); Schisler v. Dept. of Army, 9 MSPR 161, 163-64 (1981) (appellant was not harmed when the agency failed to state the effective date of the appellant's release from his competitive level but the agency provided the proper number of days in advance). Application of the harmful error rule to a claim that a RIF was invalidated by inadequate notice was considered in Martin v. Dept. of Navy, 61 MSPR 21, 26-27 (1994):

Contrary to the appellant's assertion, the agency's failure to provide her with timely notice of her separation by RIF was not harmful procedural error. The agency's belated RIF notice, while not meeting the technical requirements of 5 CFR § 351.802, was sufficient because it enabled the appellant, through her Board appeal, to assure that she received her substantive RIF entitlements. In cases where an issue is raised regarding an error in RIF procedures from which substantive rights are derived, the burden is on the agency to prove by the preponderance of the evidence that such error had no adverse effect on the employee's substantive entitlements. Here, the agency established that it afforded the appellant her full entitlements under RIF regulations, including her correct retention standing, release from competitive level, and bump and retreat rights. The appellant did not challenge the agency's actions in this regard. The agency's procedural error did not harm the appellant's substantive rights.

An employee is entitled to a new RIF notice period only if the agency decides to take a more severe action than that originally specified. 5 CFR 351.805(a) (2022); Metzger v. DHUD, 6 MSPR 434, 436, 6 MSPB 369 (1981) (applying the harmful error rule to a shortened notice period). The agency need not extend the notice period to accommodate an individual's interest in avoiding a break in service when the appellant desires to accept a position that will start after the effective date of the RIF. May v. ICC, 20 MSPR 557, 561-62 (1984). General reduction in force notices are not required by 5 CFR Part 351 (2022). The regulations permit agencies to issue them in order to meet notice requirements that might not be met by the specific notice alone. Saddler v. Dept. of Educ., 27 MSPR 636, 638 (1985).

Procedures followed concerning extension of general RIF notices are subject to the harmful error rule. The Board permitted OPM to approve an extension retroactively in Bayne v. Dept. of Energy, 34 MSPR 439, 446 (1987), after a House subcommittee requested that the agency delay its RIF until the subcommittee received information about it. On appeal, the general rule was stated: an error in the timing of a RIF notice is subject to the harmful error doctrine. Bayne v. Dept. of Energy (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 87-3545). If some employees receive a notice period that is longer than the appellant's, or if the notice period of certain other employees is extended, the disparity does not constitute reversible error if the other employees are not similarly situated to the appellant and if the appellant did not similarly request an extension of the notice period. Cox v. TVA, 41 MSPR 686, 690 n.3 (1989). If the agency improperly truncates the employee's (paid) duty status during the notice period by placing him on forced annual leave, the Board will correct the agency's error by requiring payment for the period of leave forced as a result of the agency's incorrect interpretation of the "emergency" proviso of 5 CFR 351.806 (2022). See Lerner v. Dept. of Interior, 7 MSPR 511, 512 (1981).

The RIF notice should accurately state the appellant's rights. A notice that gave the appellant a choice between a reassignment coupled with a downgrade and separation with severance pay, leading the employee to accept separation in reliance on the incorrect figure quoted for the severance allowance, caused the Board to require the agency to cancel the separation. Reed v. Dept. of Commerce, 18 MSPR 697, 699 (1984). An involuntary retirement appeal occurred in Wylie v. USDA, 99 MSPR 71 (2005), when the agency notified the appellant that his position would be abolished, leading to the appellant's acceptance of a discontinued service annuity, and the agency provided to the employee no RIF procedural protections and no information as to his RIF rights.
