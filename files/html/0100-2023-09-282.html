E. REVIEW OF DISCOVERY RULINGS

Discovery under Board rules loosely follows the discovery allowed under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure. An agency must provide relevant information even though the agency did not rely upon the information in making the decision. Relevance is not the same as admissibility: if evidence, though not admissible, is likely to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence, it is discoverable. The fairness of MSPB adjudications can only be enhanced by disclosure of the facts. Unless undue delay or hardship is caused by the request, uncertainty as to relevancy of requested evidence is resolved in the requestor's favor. McGrath v. Dept. of Army, 83 MSPR 48, 51 ¶ 7 (1999) (discovery relevance is to be liberally interpreted, uncertainty is to be resolved in favor of movant unless there is undue delay or hardship); Bize v. Dept. of Treasury, 3 MSPR 155, 164, 3 MSPB 261 (1980), followed, Johnson v. Dept. of Treasury, 8 MSPR 170, 175, 7 MSPB 649 (1981) ("[U]ncertainty as to the relevancy of requested evidence should be resolved in the favor of the movant, absent any undue delay or hardship caused by such request."). But cf. Brace v. DHUD, 13 MSPR 187, 190, 11 MSPB 451 (1982) ("[I]t has consistently been held by the Board that documents requested by an appellant must be relevant to the matter properly before the Board.").

Discovery disputes are brought to the attention of the AJ for adjudication by motion, e.g., a motion to compel discovery, motion for a protective order, or a motion in limine. Discovery disputes are resolved under the Board's regulations and decisions involving harmful adjudicatory error, not under a constitutional due process theory. See Burroughs v. Dept. of Army, 115 MSPR 656, 659 ¶ 7, 2011 MSPB 30 (2011) ("Even if the administrative judge should have ruled on this request, we find that his failure to do so did not violate the appellant's right to due process. See Johnson v. United States, 628 F.2d 187, 194 (D.C. Cir. 1980) (the Fifth Amendment only requires that a person receive his 'due' process, not every procedural device that he may claim or desire). We also find that the appellant has not shown how the administrative judge's failure to rule on his request might have affected the outcome of the appeal. See Karapinka v. Department of Energy, 6 M.S.P.R. 124, 127 (1981), aff'd, Burroughs v. Dept. of Army, 445 Fed. Appx. 347 (Fed. Cir. 2011 NP) (an administrative judge's procedural error is of no legal consequence unless it is shown to have adversely affected a party's substantive rights)."). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Motion to Compel," "Protective Order," and to Chapter 4, "Objection, Motions to Strike or in Limine."]

An AJ's ruling on a discovery dispute is reviewed for abuse of discretion, a deferential standard of review. Under the furlough appeal styled CCAD ARX6 v. Dept. of Army, DA-0752-14-0248-I-1 (NP 5/5/2015):

To the extent that the appellants are arguing that the administrative judge improperly limited discovery, the Board will not reverse an administrative judge's rulings on discovery matters absent an abuse of discretion. Wagner v. Environmental Protection Agency, 54 M.S.P.R. 447, 452 (1992), aff'd, 996 F.2d 1236 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (Table). The abuse of discretion standard is "a very high standard" and allows for "great deference." Pecard v. Department of Agriculture, 115 M.S.P.R. 31, ¶ 15 (2010) (citing Lipscomb v. Department of Defense, 69 M.S.P.R. 484, 487 (1996).
