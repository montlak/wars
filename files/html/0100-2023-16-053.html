d. Standard That Enumerates Tasks

With the caveat that, under Guillebeau, supra, the analysis may become insignificant, standards may still be considered unreasonable, if not invalid as absolute, particularly when applied to positions whose functions do not entail broad discretion, if they only describe what the appellant is supposed to do and do not measure how much work is supposed to be performed to achieve the level of acceptable performance. This result can be avoided by augmentation of the standard, but lacking augmentation, a performance action may fail. A standard considered in Bronfman v. GSA, 40 MSPR 184, 186-88 & n.4 (1989), involving removal of a GS-5 Accounting Technician, provided that "the employee shall analyze assigned accounts to determine the reason for nonpayment, help the customer resolve any problems, and, if necessary, discuss them with her supervisor." The standard also stated that an employee should "review the trial balance to identify delinquent accounts, making at least one telephone contact and sending a collection letter on each delinquent account monthly, and have a file on each account over seventy-five days old." The standard identified the documents to be included in the file to create a proper audit trail. A second standard stated that "all credit balances are to be investigated and cleared, that the agency will be notified of the credit, that, if the agency does not deduct the credited amount from the next payment, a refund will be processed, and that the credit should be cleared by the output of the second trial balance after its appearance." The standards were deficient because they provided no means for objective evaluation of a job that did not involve the degree of professional discretion allowing more subjective standards:

[T]he administrative judge...found that, while the standards were objective in the sense that they set forth how the appellant was to perform her duties, they did not, short of perfection, identify a level of acceptable performance toward which she could aim.... Although he recognized that an agency can give content to vague performance standards by informing the employee of specific work requirements through other methods...the administrative judge found that, despite being counseled as to how to perform her duties, the appellant was never notified of what would constitute an acceptable level of performance....

...

We agree with the administrative judge that, although the standards both describe how the appellant was to analyze accounts[4] and explain how she was to maintain orderly files, neither set forth a level of performance that she was required to meet in order to be considered acceptable. See Rogers v. Department of Labor, 28 MSPR 153, 156 (1985) (no evidence that the agency informed the appellant, either directly or indirectly, of the degree his performance would have to improve to avoid demotion). As written, the standards do not, to any extent, permit accurate evaluation of job performance on the basis of objective criteria. 5 USC § 4302(b)(2).

[4] For example, the first performance standard for critical element one states that the employee shall analyze assigned accounts to determine the reason for nonpayment, help the customer resolve any problems, and, if necessary, discuss them with her supervisor. The second performance standard states that all credit balances are to be investigated and cleared, that the agency will be notified of the credit, that, if the agency does not deduct the credited amount from the next payment, a refund will be processed, and that the credit should be cleared by the output of the second trial balance after its appearance.

While the agency is correct in asserting that performance standards are not required to be fully objective, we find that its reliance on a comparison of the appellant's standards with those in Shuman v. Department of the Treasury, 23 MSPR 620 (1984), is misplaced. The standards in Shuman, the Board found, encompassed a broad range of duties varying in complexity and significance. Moreover, the position at issue involved the exercise of a greater degree of professional discretion and fewer technical aspects, and the appellant's removal was premised on the qualitative judgments she made.

In the instant case, however...the agency in fact applied a quantitative standard to the appellant's performance but failed to inform her what it was. Absent evidence that the agency gave content to the appellant's performance standards by informing her of specific work requirements through written and oral instructions...the agency may not take a performance action based on an invalid standard....

As suggested by the administrative judge, the result of applying standards such as the ones at issue is that, only if the appellant were to perform her duties precisely as described therein and in the warning letter, with no mistakes, would she have met the standard. We agree that such standards are impermissibly absolute in nature. See Calloway v. Department of the Army, 23 MSPR 592, 600 (1984)....

Standards that define a job, meaning that there is little apparent room for error, were not impermissibly absolute pre-Guillebeau, if the agency set a higher level of performance for "outstanding," as in Hurd v. Dept. of Interior, 53 MSPR 107, 110-11 (1992), interpreting the standard for a GS-6 Realty Assistant:

Generally prepares Financial Plan-Operating Program for the Midwest Region Land Resources Division with little instruction. Generally monitors obligations and expenditures of Division to preclude over obligation and expenditure. Generally checks monthly expenditure report, undelivered orders report, work order package report, and progress report for correct allotments, expenditures, and unobligated balances. Takes corrective action when discrepancies are found. Generally checks to see that account numbers are in financial system before issuing obligating documents. If not in system, prepares with little instruction, work order plans. Generally responsible for payment of all services rendered to the Land Resources Division.

Of the standard the Board commented, Hurd, 53 MSPR at 111-12:

The administrative judge found that this standard was absolute because "the appellant was required to bring the reports current without regard to the amount of work necessary or the time it should take." The administrative judge has not provided any support for his conclusion that, even if this were true, it would render the standard absolute. An absolute standard is one which cannot be exceeded, and thus does not allow the agency to measure above-satisfactory performance. It provides that one incident of poor performance will result in an unsatisfactory rating on a job element, and thus does not provide a basis for evaluating an employee as exceeding the performance standard. Callaway v. Department of the Army, 23 MSPR 592, 599 (1984). Here, the standard clearly allows for the appellant to exceed it. Indeed, the agency has set forth a standard for outstanding performance that reflects a higher level of performance by the incumbent.

We further find that the performance standard is not so vague as to be ruled invalid without regard to the content given the standard by the agency.... The administrative judge found that the standard did not appear to set "any standard except that the incumbent is required to perform with 'little instruction.'" However, a review of the standard, as quoted above, shows that it specifically identified several tasks the appellant was expected to perform and explained the level of performance the appellant was expected to attain to receive a rating of fully successful performance on the critical element. Moreover, contrary to the administrative judge's implication, there is no requirement that a performance standard contain production or time standards.... Thus, we find that the administrative judge should not have invalidated this standard without considering whether the agency gave content to it by informing the appellant of specific work requirements through other means....

Here, the record shows that the appellant was aware of what she must do to bring her level of work in critical element 6A up to an acceptable level of performance. To begin with, the appellant had performed the duties of critical element 6A for several years in her position as a Secretary and for the first year in her position as a Realty Assistant. Moreover, the performance rating the appellant received for April through June 1990 informed her that her performance of critical element 6A was unsatisfactory because the financial reports were not current or correct. The July 2, 1990 performance improvement plan further notified her that she was expected to update the finance reports. Indeed, in a memorandum documenting his meeting with the appellant, Mr. Schenk stated that the appellant had indicated that she understood what the performance standards required. Thus, we find that the appellant's performance standard was not impermissibly vague and that the administrative judge should have determined whether the appellant's performance of the critical element was acceptable under the agency's performance standards. We find that this is a determination best made by the initial finder of fact.

See Lee v. EPA, 115 MSPR 533, 551 ¶ 31, 2010 MSPB 240 (2010) ("The measures, metrics, and focus areas listed under Critical Element #1 are consistent with the appellant's job description. Furthermore, the measures, metrics, and focus areas are in the nature of ongoing tasks, and thus were not rendered unreasonable by the shortened performance appraisal period."); cf. Johnson v. Dept. of Interior, 87 MSPR 359, 365 ¶¶ 12-13 (2000) (standards were invalid as absolute under a two-level performance appraisal system when the standards identified tasks but did not quantify expected performance (the decision is discussed later in this chapter, "Two-Level, Pass/Fail Appraisal System"); cf. Salmon v. SSA, 663 F.3d 1378 (Fed. Cir. 2011) (describing permissive augmentation of standards through supervisory instructions; Salmon is discussed earlier in this chapter, "Objectivity").

Although standards that are defined as tasks could be absolute under law before Guillebeau, performance can be based on completion of specific projects assigned during a PIP. An appellant may be assigned projects with deadlines, and if the appellant's performance standard calls for completion of the projects within the stated times, then the appellant's failure to complete the projects leads to a failure of performance. See Wilson v. DHHS, 770 F.2d 1048 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (case of Mr. Jackson: the failure to complete projects by deadlines supported a performance-based demotion); Guillebeau v. Dept. of Navy, 93 MSPR 379, 383-84 ¶ 11 (2003) (failure to complete projects when due led to removal).
