2. Determining if Issue or Argument Was Raised Below

An issue is preserved on appeal, and the agency is on notice of the issue--e.g., the harshness of a penalty--if it is mentioned in the appeal. An issue may be raised before the AJ by arguing facts that establish the issue, rather than by directly identifying and arguing the legal infirmity of the agency action. Brown v. USDA, 26 MSPR 173, 175 (1985). Issues need not be raised explicitly and often they are not explicitly raised in cases litigated by pro se appellants. The appellant need only allege some facts that should place the AJ on notice that a claim is being expressly or impliedly made, but, without the allegation of those basic facts, the claim cannot be asserted or litigated for the first time in a PFR. Johnson v. Dept. of Army, 23 MSPR 558, 560 (1984); cf. Keatting v. Dept. of Navy, 77 MSPR 642, 646 (1998) (although the appellant appeared to be raising a new claim in the PFR, the record on the underlying appeal demonstrated that the claim was untimely raised). If the appellant raises some facts that should have placed the AJ on notice that a claim of harmful error was being made, the Board could review the initial decision on that issue. McGhee v. DHHS, 11 MSPR 122, 124 (1982).

Ordinarily, issues are raised by the parties in the appeal and response to the appeal, by motions, and in prehearing submissions, and sometimes during the hearing and associated argument and briefing (although generally issues are supposed to be raised not later than the prehearing submissions and associated conference). An issue is considered raised if a party introduces related evidence into the record without objection, even though the issue is not affirmatively pleaded or offered as a defense, a result that parallels the principle that an appellate body may consider evidence that has been introduced without objection by either party, even if it was not considered by the trial court. Hubbard v. Walter Reed Army Med. Ctr., 29 MSPR 187, 189 (1985); see Jenson v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1995 NP No. 94-3520) (recognizing Hubbard rule). Hammonds v. USPS, AT-0752-10-0399-I-1 (NP 8/31/2011), noted of a harmful error issue raised on a PFR:

The appellant's argument on petition for review also appears to reiterate his contention on appeal that the alleged failure to provide a union representative on November 14, 2009, an alleged violation of his rights under National Labor Relations Board v. Weingarten, 420 U.S. 251 (1975), constituted harmful error. The appellant initially raised this argument in his prehearing submission, asserting that the denial was an unfair labor practice that constituted harmful error. The administrative judge correctly stated in the prehearing conference summary that the Board lacked jurisdiction to adjudicate the possible unfair labor practice claim. However, he did not separately address a harmful error allegation. Although the appellant did not object to the summary, we find under the circumstances of this case that he sufficiently preserved the issue when he testified regarding the matter at the hearing and asserted in his closing argument that he was disadvantaged by the lack of a union representative. [See, e.g., Davis v. Department of the Interior, 114 M.S.P.R. 527, ¶ 4 n.1 (2010). Because the administrative judge did not address the issue in the initial decision, we will do so on review.

To determine whether an issue was raised before the AJ, the Board may consider an AJ's order that defines the issues after the AJ reviews the appellant's initial pleading. The absence of an objection by the appellant to the AJ's statement of the issues is some evidence of whether the issue was raised below. See Rodriquez v. DOJ, 84 MSPR 685, 689 ¶ 7 (2000) (considering an acknowledgment order to a petition for enforcement). The Board considered on PFR arguments that have been raised as late as the appellant's closing argument following the hearing. See Biniak v. SSA, 90 MSPR 682, 685 n.1 (2002) (overruled on other grounds, Thomas v. USPS, 116 MSPR 453, 458-61 ¶¶ 8-13, 2011 MSPB 62 (2011), and Pickett v. USDA, 116 MSPR 439, 440-44 ¶¶ 2-12, 2011 MSPB 58 (2011)). If an issue is raised with the AJ, but the AJ properly refuses to consider it because the motion or pleading was not timely filed with the AJ, the Board will not consider the same issue as properly raised in a PFR. See Kirkland v. DHS, DC-0752-12-0104-I-1 (NP 10/24/2012). If there is a discovery dispute, the issue is preserved for PFR through a motion for reconsideration of the AJ's ruling on a motion to compel and through a request for reconsideration of that ruling along with an objection to the ruling on the latter request. See Figueora v. DHS, 119 MSPR 422, 2013 MSPB 33 (2013).) (overruled on other grounds, Singh v. USPS, 2022 MSPB 15 (2022)). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Preservation of Issue."]
