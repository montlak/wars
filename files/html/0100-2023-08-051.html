1. Current Continuous Service

One of the components of the definition of an employee under 5 USC 7511 is "current continuous service." The phrase is used somewhat differently in two parts of the statute. An employee in the competitive service has one year of current continuous service under other than a temporary appointment limited to one year or less. 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii). For the excepted service, matters are more complex. The preference eligible (individual with qualifying veterans service) must have one year of current continuous service in the same or similar position in an executive agency or the Postal Service (or Postal Regulatory Commission). 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(B). Other excepted service personnel gain status as employees who have completed two years of current continuous service in the same or similar positions in an Executive agency under other than a temporary appointment limited to two years or less. 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(C)(ii).

For service to be "current," i.e., the service must be continuous at the point of the action appealed, and it must be without a break of a workday, 5 CFR 752.402 (2022), "Definitions":

Current continuous employment means a period of employment or service immediately preceding an adverse action without a break in Federal civilian employment of a workday.

Roy v. MSPB, 672 F.3d 1378, 1380-81 (Fed. Cir. 2012), applied the statutory phrase:

Section 7511(a)(1)(C)(ii) unambiguously states that the individual must have "2 years of current continuous service in the same or similar positions...other than a temporary appointment limited to 2 years or less." Id. § 7511(a)(1)(C)(ii) (emphases added). Two phrases in the text of the statute are important to our analysis: "current continuous" and "other than a temporary appointment." Id. The word "current" indicates that the removal date is the key date for determining whether the continuity requirement is satisfied. The word "continuous" indicates that there cannot be a break in service. Thus, the phrase "current continuous" indicates that in order to determine the continuity requirement, we must look at the individual's employment at the time of removal and determine whether there has been a break in service during the two years that immediately precede the removal date. Moreover, the phrase "other than a temporary appointment" indicates that temporary appointments (limited to two years or less) do not count toward the two-year current continuous service requirement. Because at the time of removal, Ms. Roy had served less than two years in a permanent position, she is not an employee as § 7511(a)(1)(C)(ii) defines that term.

See Ellefson, 98 M.S.P.R. 191, ¶ 14; Samble, 98 M.S.P.R. 502, ¶ 9; see also 5 C.F.R. § 752.402(b).

There is a distinction in the significance of breaks in service for calculating current continuous service and tacking of prior service toward completion of a probationary or trial period, a matter subsequently discussed, Maibaum v. VA, 116 MSPR 234, 240 ¶ 14, 2011 MSPB 18 (2011):

The term "current continuous service" means a period of employment or service, either in the competitive or excepted service that immediately precedes an adverse action without a break in federal civilian employment of a workday. Fitzgerald v. Department of the Air Force, 108 M.S.P.R. 620, ¶ 10 (2008); 5 C.F.R. § 752.402(b). For purposes of tacking on prior service to complete a probationary or trial period, a single break in service of less than 30 days is permissible, provided the prior service is in the same agency and in the same line of work. McCrary v. Department of the Army, 103 M.S.P.R. 266, ¶ 12 (2006). Contrary to the initial decision, however, the 30-day rule does not apply when determining whether prior service may be counted toward the applicable current continuous service requirement under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1). See id., ¶ 8.

Dade v. VA, 101 MSPR 43, 44-48 ¶¶ 2, 6, 9-12 (2005), noted that a preference eligible's time in a prior competitive service appointment in a different agency may add service time to the employee's current competitive service appointment to establish a Board appeal right:

In 1994, the Department of the Army hired the appellant as an Audiologist. This appointment was in the competitive service and was subject to completion of a 1-year probationary period. The appellant served in this position until November 2, 2003, when he left the Army without a break in service for an Audiologist position with the agency. The SF-50 effecting the appellant's appointment with the agency indicated that the appointment was in the competitive service, that it was subject to a 1-year probationary period, and that the appellant was a 10-point preference-eligible veteran.

...

The agency...argued, for the first time, that the appellant's competitive appointment had been converted to a "hybrid" appointment in the excepted service on December 6, 2003, upon passage of the Veterans Health Care, Capital Asset, and Business Improvement Act of 2003, Pub. L. No. 108-170, 2003 U.S.C.C.A.N. (117 Stat.) 2042, amending 38 U.S.C. § 7401....

...

We find that the appellant's prior service as an Audiologist with the Army may be counted towards completion of 1 year of current continuous service, regardless of whether the appellant has remained in the competitive service or has been converted to the excepted service by operation of law under Public Law No. 108-170, and that the appellant is, therefore, an "employee" who may appeal his removal to the Board.

In order to appeal his removal to the Board, the appellant must meet the statutory definition of "employee." See McCormick v. Department of the Air Force, 307 F.3d 1339, 1340-41 (Fed. Cir. 2002). An individual in the competitive service who is either not serving a probationary or trial period under an initial appointment, or who has completed 1 year of current continuous service under other than a temporary appointment limited to 1 year or less is an "employee" who may appeal his removal to the Board. 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii); McCormick, 307 F.3d at 1341. Current continuous service, for purposes of section 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii), is a period of employment or service immediately preceding an adverse action without a break in Federal civilian employment of a workday. 5 C.F.R. § 752.402(b); see Ellefson v. Department of the Army, 98 M.S.P.R. 191, ¶ 13 (2005). This period of service need not be performed in the same agency nor in the same line of work. Id., ¶¶ 10, 14; see also Porter v. Department of Defense, 98 M.S.P.R. 461, ¶¶ 9-10 (2005).

As described above, the appellant served in the competitive service as an Audiologist with the Army for several years. The agency then appointed him to a permanent competitive service Audiologist position without a break in service. The appellant, therefore, completed 1 year of current continuous service under other than a temporary appointment limited to 1 year or less, and may appeal his removal to the Board if he is deemed to have remained a competitive service employee after enactment of Public Law No. 108-170. 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii); see Porter, 98 M.S.P.R. 461, ¶¶ 9-10; Ellefson, 98 M.S.P.R. 191, ¶¶ 10, 13-14.

Even if the agency is correct in its assertion that Public Law No. 108-170 changed the appellant from the competitive service to the excepted service, the appellant is still an "employee" with Board appeal rights. Under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(B)(i), an "employee" includes a preference eligible in the excepted service who has completed 1 year of current continuous service in the same or similar positions in an Executive agency. The Board has recently held that the term "an Executive agency" can include more than one agency. Greene v. Defense Intelligence Agency, 100 M.S.P.R. 447, ¶¶ 6-12 (2005). Because the appellant is a preference eligible, his prior service in the same position as an Audiologist with the Army may, therefore, be counted towards completion of his 1 year of current continuous service in his position as an Audiologist with the agency, even if Public Law No. 108-170 converted him to the excepted service.

The AJ is obliged to ensure notice to the appellant of the right to develop evidence concerning prior service that may confer Board jurisdiction if there is some indication of prior service, Dickens v. Dept. of Army, 99 MSPR 372, 375-76 ¶¶ 6-8 (2005):

An individual is entitled to appeal to the Board under 5 U.S.C. § 7513(d) if he is an "employee" as that term is defined at 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a), which provides, in pertinent part, "(1) "employee" means--(A) an individual in the competitive service--(i) who is not serving a probationary or trial period under an initial appointment; or (ii) who has completed 1 year of current continuous service under other than a temporary appointment limited to 1 year or less...." 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1). The Federal Circuit has held that an individual who is excluded from "employee" status under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(i) is an "employee" if the individual meets the definition of "employee" under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii). McCormick v. Department of the Air Force, 307 F.3d 1339, 1342-43 (Fed. Cir. 2002), pet. for reh'g en banc denied, 329 F.3d 1354 (Fed. Cir. 2003). Accordingly, a competitive service employee serving a probationary or trial period may appeal to the Board if he "has completed 1 year of current continuous service under other than a temporary appointment limited to 1 year or less." Id. We recently held that current continuous service need not be in the same or similar positions in order for an individual in the competitive service to qualify as an "employee" under this subsection. Ellefson v. Department of the Army, 98 M.S.P.R. 191 ¶ 14 (2005); see also Samble v. Department of Defense, 98 M.S.P.R. 502 ¶ 9 n.1 (2005). We held additionally that, for competitive service employees, "current continuous service" means a period of employment or service immediately preceding an adverse action without a break in Federal civilian employment of a workday. Ellefson, 98 M.S.P.R. 191 ¶ 14; Samble, 98 M.S.P.R. 502 ¶ 9.

We are unable to determine from the record whether the appellant has completed one year of current continuous service under other than a temporary appointment limited to one year or less. The appellant indicated on his initial appeal form that he had twelve years, two months, and fourteen days of government service and the service computation date on his SF-50 is June 4, 1992. It is not clear from the record whether the appellant has prior service that meets the current continuous service requirement of section 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii). Even if the appellant had a break in service, he may be an "employee" under subsection (i) of section 7511(a)(1)(A) if his prior service can be "tacked" to his trial period; however, the acknowledgement order did not inform the appellant how to establish that his prior service in competitive service positions should be credited towards completion of a later probationary or trial period in a competitive service position. See Ellefson, 98 M.S.P.R. 191 ¶ 16.

The appellant did not receive explicit information as to how he could show that his prior service can be "tacked" to his trial period. Nor did he receive explicit information as to how he could show that he meets the definition of an employee under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(1)(A)(ii). See Burgess v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 758 F.2d 641, 643-44 (Fed. Cir. 1985). Moreover, the record is not sufficiently developed for us to address the issue without a remand. The appeal must therefore be remanded for the administrative judge to determine whether, during the year immediately preceding his separation, the appellant's service was without a break in service of a workday and was under other than a temporary appointment limited to one year or less. The administrative judge must additionally determine whether the appellant's prior service can be "tacked" to his trial period. If the appellant meets either of these requirements, the Board would have jurisdiction over his appeal. See Ellefson, 98 M.S.P.R. 191 ¶¶ 15-16.
