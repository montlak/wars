13. Absence Caused by Fear for Safety

In Parker v. Dept. of Interior, 4 MSPR 97, 99-101, 4 MSPB 184 (1980), the appellant was discharged for absence without leave. He defended on the basis that the regulations implementing the Occupational Safety and Health Act (then 29 CFR 1977.12(b)(2)) allow an employee to choose not to perform his assigned task if he reasonably believes that he is in danger of imminent serious bodily injury and if no less drastic alternative is available. The regulation applies to private sector employers. There is no authority extending it to the federal sector. Assuming that the regulation applied, the appellant must present evidence demonstrating that he reasonably believed that his continued presence at his place of employment would result in imminent death or serious injury. Although the employee was concerned about contact with asbestos and tobacco smoke, there was no evidence that a less drastic remedy of requesting a safety inspection of the workplace or giving notice of an allegedly unhealthful working condition to a designated health official pursuant to the then governing version of 29 CFR 1960.31 ("Inspections by OSHA") would have been insufficient. The employee who refuses to perform work for a health-related reason must show the reasonableness of his concerns. The employee must at least be willing to listen to agency officials concerned with safety issues or read agency safety manuals that would demonstrate the employee's concerns are bona fide. Haymore v. Dept. of Navy, 9 MSPR 499, 504-05 (1982); see Filion v. Dept. of Navy, 11 MSPR 217, 219 (1982) (appellant's medical objections to performing light duty tasks were groundless; he was warned that failure to report to work could result in removal); cf. Clark v. DOJ, CH-0752-15-0288-I-2 (NP 10/21/2022) ("[W]e do not find persuasive the appellant's argument that he was absent because of a perceived danger. See Proctor v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 27 M.S.P.R. 163, 168-69 (1985) (finding that, when an employee failed to show danger of death or serious injury, the employee's AWOL and refusal to return to work warranted an adverse action).").

In Maxik v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 87-3491), appellant's fear of allergies in the workplace was outweighed by agency evidence that the workplace was safe. No medical evidence connected the work environment with appellant's illnesses, so the appellant's fear of imminent danger was not justifiable. Maulding v. DHHS, 42 MSPR 605, 611 (1989), rejected as an AWOL defense the employee's concerns about reactions to toxic chemicals. Noting that the regulation now codified at 5 CFR 339.302 (2022) permits, but does not require, agencies to offer medical examinations to employees, the Board concluded that the appellant failed to offer persuasive medical evidence that her sensitivity to chemicals warranted her absence from duty. Also rejected, for lack of a showing of sensitivity to chemicals, was the appellant's assertion of disability discrimination. Tucker v. VA, CH-0752-13-0421-B-1 (NP 12/12/2022), commented as to the reasons offered for an employee's refusal to report to her assigned work area:

We have considered that the appellant failed to meet with the agency's industrial hygienist when invited to do so to address her concerns about her work area. Although the appellant submitted a letter from a nurse practitioner, when the agency asked for additional information to determine the cause and nature of her health condition, including sending a letter to the nurse practitioner, the appellant did not provide the information. Accordingly, we find that the evidence does not show that the instructions were invalid or that the appellant was entitled to disregard them based upon a danger to her health. Thus, the administrative judge properly found that the agency proved by preponderant evidence the failure to follow instructions charge. See Maulding v. Department of Health & Human Services, 42 M.S.P.R. 605, 611 (1989) (sustaining a failure to follow instructions charge when the employee failed to report to the agency's laboratory as instructed due to her claimed chemical sensitivity and she did not present sufficient evidence in support of her claims), aff'd, 961 F.2d 694 (8th Cir. 1992); see also White v. Department of Housing & Urban Development, 95 M.S.P.R. 299, ¶¶ 21-25 (2003).

Peoples v. Dept. of Navy, 83 MSPR 216, 220-21 ¶ 11 (1999) (discussed in Chapter 6, "Temporary Absence Resulting from Intolerable Working Conditions"), legitimized the absence of workers who were suffering adverse psychological consequences from the presence of a coworker who engaged in bizarre and threatening behavior. The employees' absences were recommended by the agency's EAP counselor. [Refer later in this chapter to "Fear for Safety or Other Harm" under "Insubordination and Similar Misconduct."] In Moore v. USPS, 117 MSPR 84, 87 ¶ 12, 2011 MSPB 93 (2011), involving the analogous situation of a constructive suspension that occurred when the appellant absented herself because she was fearful of a customer who she thought was stalking her at the workplace, a hearing was warranted:

As set forth above, the appellant asserted that she worked alone much of the day at a secluded facility, and that one of her customers stalked her. The appellant has also asserted that the stalking caused her so much stress that she was unable to report for duty, and has presented her physician's statement that anxiety caused by her working conditions rendered her unable to work. Further, the appellant's psychologist subsequently diagnosed the appellant with PTSD as a result of her working conditions and recommended a transfer away from the Diamond Post Office as a remedy. The appellant has also alleged that she notified her supervisor of the problem, and told the agency that she could perform any and all of her duties in another location. We find that these specific allegations of intolerable working conditions constitute a nonfrivolous allegation of involuntary absence from duty. See Wegner, 89 M.S.P.R. 644, ¶ 13; Peoples, 83 M.S.P.R. 216, ¶¶ 11-13.

Citing Peoples, supra, Savage v. Dept. of Army, 122 MSPR 612, 629 ¶¶ 20-21, 2015 MSPB 51 (2015), determined as to a constructive suspension appeal:

Here, the appellant alleges that her absences beginning on August 18, 2008, were the result of psychological damage caused by intolerable working conditions. To establish jurisdiction over a constructive suspension on the basis of intolerable working conditions, an appellant must show that a reasonable person would have felt compelled to absent herself under the conditions and that the agency was culpable for these conditions. Peoples v. Department of the Navy, 83 M.S.P.R. 216, ¶ 5 (1999). Because no employee is entitled to leave work and remain absent without explanation, the appellant must inform the agency of the existence of the objectionable conditions and request assistance or remediation from the agency. Id., ¶ 8. The agency also must be notified of the specific nature of the conditions and the employee's inability to cope with them before the agency can be expected to investigate, attempt remediation of the conditions if necessary, or to consider finding other duties or positions for the employee pending resolution of the complaint. Id., ¶ 9.

Here, the administrative judge concluded that, because the appellant did not request reassignment as a reasonable accommodation for her medical restrictions, she had a meaningful choice as to her absences, and therefore had not been constructively suspended. W-1 ID at 24. However, a medical absence may be attributable to intolerable working conditions regardless of whether the employee subsequently requests accommodation under the Rehabilitation Act. If the conditions are such that a reasonable person would feel compelled to absent herself, and the agency is culpable for those conditions, to constitute a nonfrivolous allegation of involuntary absence from duty, it is sufficient for the appellant to notify the agency that she is medically incapable of returning to duty in her current work environment. See Peoples, 83 M.S.P.R. 216, ¶ 11.
