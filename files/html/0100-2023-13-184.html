5. Settlement of Complaint

ALJ cases, like others, can be settled. The Board favors the settlement of actions between an agency and its employees, including those actions initiated by the employing agency against one of its ALJs. SSA v. Biesman, 73 MSPR 82, 83 (1997); see SSA v. Lawson, 73 MSPR 275, 277 (1997) (Board accepted settlement, found cause for discipline, and authorized the imposition of a suspension); SSA v. Liebling, 71 MSPR 465, 466 (1996) (approving a 20-day suspension of an ALJ for charges related to noncompliance with time and attendance requirements, personal use of a government credit card, and failure to timely pay the card; the factual assertions accepted in the settlement included only facts relating to tardiness and attendance responsibilities); Office of Hearings & Appeals v. Liebling, 62 MSPR 12, 13 (1994) (settlement mitigating a proposed suspension: "the Board favors the settlement of actions between an agency and its employees, including those actions initiated against one of its administrative law judges"); Dept. of Commerce v. Dolan, 46 MSPR 282 (1990) (Table); SSA v. Givens, 27 MSPR 360, 362 (1985) (settlement imposing a suspension for the violation of a regulation governing the use of a GOV); SSA v. Pucci, 27 MSPR 358, 360 (1985) (settlement imposing a suspension for the use of government office for the sale of jewelry and clothing). In re King, 2 MSPR 507, 509-10 (1980), an early Board decision accepting the settlement of an ALJ complaint seeking the respondent's involuntary retirement, explained:

In their Stipulation of Settlement, the parties agree that the purpose of settlement is to protect the interests of the parties by saving each the considerable expense of a hearing before the Board and possibly further litigation in the courts, and to save the Board the expense and time of conducting a hearing. Public policy strongly favors settlement of disputes without litigation and the courts uphold settlement agreements whenever equity and public policy permit.

By such agreements are the burdens of trial spared to the parties, to other litigants waiting their turn before overburdened courts, and to the citizens whose taxes support the latter. An amicable compromise provides the more speedy and reasonable remedy for dispute.[5]

[5] Aro Corp. v. Allied Witam Co., 531 F.2d 1368, 1372 (1976). See also D. H. Overmeyer Co. v. Loflin, 440 F.2d 1213 (5th Cir. 1971).

Similarly, when parties seek Board approval of a settlement agreement, the Board must look to the complexity, expense, and likely duration of litigation, as well as the equity of the settlement as to each party. The time and expense incurred by both parties during the past four years, and the substantial likelihood of further and prolonged litigation to resolve the question of respondent's disability is a factor favoring settlement. The parties have been represented by counsel and each is satisfied with the terms of the settlement. The Board has found no law, rule, or regulation that forbids the agency's agreeing to settlement on the stipulated terms.

Settlements further the speedy, fair and reasonable resolution of the issues and set disputes to rest without the necessity of further litigation. DHHS, SSA v. Osinski, 45 MSPR 402 (1990) (Table). If the parties want their settlement to be part of the record, they submit it for acceptance into the record to the Board's ALJ, who issues an initial decision approving the settlement. The Board's ALJ goes through the same exercise as he would with other settled cases: he ensures that the agreement is lawful on its face, that it was freely entered into by the parties, that the subject matter of the agreement is within the Board's jurisdiction, see DHHS, SSA, OHA v. Whittlesey, 49 MSPR 423 (1991) (Table), that the agreement is consistent with law, equity and public policy, and that it resolves all matters involved in the complaint. Dept. of Commerce v. Dolan, 46 MSPR 282 (1990). In Dept. of Commerce v. Dolan, 56 MSPR 36 (1992) (Table), the Board added, as it approved a settlement, that the Recommended Decision suggesting approval of the agreement noted that the record represented "a sufficient showing of good cause to warrant authorization of the proposed suspension," a comment denoting that there was a good faith basis on the part of the agency to bring the action that led to the settlement. But see SSA v. Anyel, 66 MSPR 328, 331 (1995) ("Because the settlement agreement is the final resolution of the issues, we find that the CALJ should not have further adjudicated this case to sustain the charges. We therefore do not adopt the CALJ's recommendation to sustain the charges.").

Once a settlement agreement is presented to and approved by the Board, the Board retains authority to enforce its terms. DHHS, SSA v. Doyle, 45 MSPR 258, 259 (1990). Under Doyle, the Board may seal a settlement agreement, but it appears that the Board disfavors sealing agreements and may impose on its order the condition that the agreement can be disclosed to the extent necessary to "perfect its terms or where it is shown there is a legitimate need to know." Doyle essentially unsealed a record to permit an agency to use the settlement as evidence in a civil lawsuit brought by ALJ Doyle. Although the AJ may issue a protective order to incorporate the confidentiality provisions of a settlement agreement, and although the Board may agree as part of the settlement that it will dismiss the complaint and answer with prejudice, the Board has declined to expunge the complaint and answer from the record. The Board has noted that "other law protects confidentiality and personal privacy." CFTC v. Duncan, 62 MSPR 143, 144 (1994).
