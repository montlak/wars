2. Part-Time/Full-Time Employment

Antilles Consol. Educ. Ass'n and DOD, Domestic Dependent Elementary and Secondary Sch., 69 FLRA 281, 282-83 (2016), reviewing an arbitration award involving a RIF, said of the Part-Time Act:

In order to increase part-time opportunities in the federal government,[17] Congress passed the Part-Time Act. To address concerns that the promotion of part-time employment would threaten federal employees in full-time positions,[18] Congress included 5 U.S.C. § 3403, placing limits on the creation of part-time positions. As part of the Part-Time Act, § 3403 states that:

(a) An agency shall not abolish any position occupied by an employee in order to make the duties of such position available to be performed on a part-time career employment basis.

(b) Any person who is employed on a full-time basis in an agency shall not be required to accept part-time employment as a condition of continued employment.[19]

[17] S. Rep. No. 95-1116, at 1 (1978) (Senate Report); Exceptions at 4 (citing Senate Report).

[18] Senate Report at 16.

[19] 5 U.S.C. § 3403.

Therefore, the first subsection, § 3403(a), prevents an agency from eliminating an encumbered full-time position in order to create a part-time position with the same duties, while the second subsection, § 3403(b), prevents an agency from circumventing the first subsection by coercing an employee out of a full-time position into a part-time position "as a condition of continued employment."[20]

[20] Id.

A proposal was negotiable for part-time employees to be used only when it was not practical or prudent to use full-time employees. The proposal did not explicitly relate to the agency's decision as to the numbers of employees assigned and the proposal did not determine the numbers of employees assigned to an organizational unit. The proposal constituted a general, nonquantitative contractual requirement permitting management's use of part-time employees to be evaluated in a grievance by a full-time employee who thought that he or she was adversely affected by agency action. AFGE Local 987 and Hq., Warner Robins Air Force Logistics Command, Robins AFB, 8 FLRA 667, 667-69 (1982). That result was reversed in Dept. of Air Force, Hq., Warner Robins Air Force Logistics Command v. FLRA, 727 F.2d 1502, 1504-06 (11th Cir. 1984):

...The issue presented in this case...is whether a proposal is substantive and not subject to negotiation under 7106(a) or procedural and subject to negotiation under section 7106(b)....

...

...In this case...the FLRA properly applied the more stringent ["directly related"] standard. The proposal at issue is not one indirectly effecting substantive rights nor is it an "appropriate arrangement...." The proposal deals directly with the right of the agency under subsection (a) to "determine the personnel by which agency operations shall be conducted...." To the extent that the FLRA concluded that the proposal is an "appropriate arrangement," we hold that no reasonable basis supports that decision. Subsection (a) clearly states that the agency retains the authority to determine the "personnel by which agency operations shall be conducted" and to hire and fire employees. If this proposal is negotiable as an appropriate arrangement, we cannot envision what would remain for the agency to determine under subsection (a).

By the same reasoning, we cannot comprehend that the proposal is "procedural" under subsection (b)(2). We are not impressed by the contention of the FLRA and the Union that the "current proposal does not bind management in the exercise of the assignment of employees or determine the number and types of employees but simply requires management to justify by a reviewable prudent or practical standard their decision to use part-time employees."... Apparently, the contention is that while the agency may utilize its discretion in assigning, hiring, and firing employees, it must follow the procedure of favoring full-time employees when reasonable. This is not a procedure at all; it is a substantive decisionmaking tool.

...

Given the clear language of the statute and the clear effect of the proposal,...the only logical conclusion in this case is that the proposal is not bargainable. Despite the FLRA opinion, a proposal requiring the agency to utilize part-time employees only when full-time employees cannot practically or prudently be used is clearly, in the FLRA's words, "determinative of the numbers of employees assigned to an organizational unit...." We find the contrary opinions of the Authority...without rational basis and thus unpersuasive.

A proposal was not negotiable that permanent part-time employees be converted, on request, and at the earliest opportunity, to permanent full-time positions. The proposal was intended as an arrangement for part-time employees who previously worked a full-time schedule but who, because of funding problems, were restricted to working only the hours of their part-time schedule. Management retained the right to determine the number of positions. The proposal was interpreted to mean that the obligation extended only to those full-time positions vacated by another employee and available for those jobs created by the employer when the workload justified establishing other full-time jobs. Although the proposal interfered with agency rights, the interference was not excessive. But the proposal was not negotiable because it interfered with the right guaranteed by the late FPM Chapter 335, Requirement 4, of subchapter 1-4, permitting agencies to fill positions by making selections from any appropriate source. The proposal required the agency to select only permanent part-time employees for permanent full-time vacancies it decided to fill. AFGE Local 3186 and DHHS, Office of Social Security Field Operations, Philadelphia Region, 23 FLRA 230, 235-38 (1986). But see AFSCME Locals 2910 and 2477 and Library of Congress, 15 FLRA 717, 717-18 (1984) (requiring posting, although not selection, of a percentage of jobs as part-time positions). A proposal, not prompted by adverse effects on part-timers, was not negotiable when required management to select noncompetitively part-time employees for full-time positions. AFGE Local 1974 and Dept. of Air Force, Hq. 3415th Air Base Group (ATC), Lowry AFB, 27 FLRA 90, 93-94 (1987).

Proposals that restrict the manner the agency could approve of, recruit for, convert from, and promote from "on-call" positions were nonnegotiable because they interfered with the agency's right to hire and assign employees. FEMTC of Charleston and Dept. of Navy, Charleston Naval Shipyard, 29 FLRA 1422, 1425-27 (1987). A union proposal was nonnegotiable that required an employer to separate temporary, intermittent, or part-time employees before the employer could diminish the hours of employment for full-time personnel. The proposal directly and integrally infringed on the exercise of the management right to "remove" employees. AFGE Local 1603 and Navy Exch., Naval Air Station, Patuxent River, 3 FLRA 4, 5-6 (1980).
