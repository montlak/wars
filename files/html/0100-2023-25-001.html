RESEARCH, REFERENCE MATERIALS, AND FORMS

I. RESEARCH AND REFERENCES

We've reached the end of our Guide, the end of our line. But your journey just begins. There are many excellent sources of information about MSPB law and kindred subjects. Research aids appear with greater frequency as Board law becomes more complex. A few but not all of the available references are mentioned here. Publishers should be contacted, their websites combed, for their offerings. Many law firms and organizations offer instructional blogs, loosely defined as recent opinions on legal developments. YouTube videos abound. These Internet-based sources can be informative. Examine references from source materials of the MSPB, FLRA, EEOC, or the courts.

There are several sources of complete Board and related court decisions. The Board places precedential and nonprecedential decisions on its website, www.mspb.gov. MSPB carries precedential decisions on its website with the format keyed to a year of decision and decision number, e.g., 2022 MSPB 1. Nonprecedential Board decisions are on the website, each associated with a docket number. Board decisions are made available by West Group. More than 124 bound volumes and advance sheets report all Board precedential decisions from 1979 onward. West calls the series Merit Systems Protection Board Reporter, noted in the Guide as ___MSPR___. West also places Board decisions and other related materials on WESTLAW and the Personnet Federal Library. Information on West publications and online services is at https://legal.thomsonreuters.com/en/westlaw. Mead Data Central places Board decisions on LEXIS. Information about LEXIS may be found at https://www.lexisnexis.com/en-us/home.page. The Government Printing Office published a dozen or so bound volumes of Board decisions, but the GPO issuances were discontinued in 1983. Citations to the GPO series are referenced as ___MSPB___ in the Guide. The GPO citations are to a volume number rather than a year, e.g., 1 MSPB 18. LRP Publications produces federal sector publications and "CyberFEDS," an Internet-based subscription electronic case research system that carries MSPB, associated court decisions, and decisions of arbitrators and other agencies, including EEOC, concerned with federal personnel law and administration. For those who wish to probe for decisions in federal and state courts, be warned that the experience should not be undertaken lightly. You must achieve some familiarity WESTLAW, LEXIS, cyberFEDS, or with the National Reporter System, published by West Group. The National Reporter System contains published decisions of state and federal courts in thousands of volumes, all digested under a copyrighted digest system developed by West. Decisions that appear useful can lead to other decisions on the subject through the West digest system or another system, called Shepard's Citator. To describe the use of these systems would require replication of the first month or two of law school. That exercise will not be undertaken here. Assistance should be sought from a legal reference librarian. Most large municipal libraries, and all law libraries, carry treatises on legal research and legal writing.

Decisions of federal courts are also summarized and cited in two adaptations of the United States Code, home of the Civil Service Reform Act (part of Title 5). If you wish to try to find cases pertaining to adverse actions, you could examine the citations under 5 USC 7513 (adverse actions) and 5 USC 7701 (Board appeals procedures), among others. Following quotation of the statutes there appear case citations arranged according to categories of cases. The version of the Code most accessible is West's United States Code Annotated (USCA), available in many large public library reference sections and in all law libraries. The USCA also summarizes MSPB decisions by reference to West's MSPR and Federal Circuit cases by citation to the Federal Reporter and Federal Appendix. Many law libraries also carry the United States Code Service (LexisNexis), arranged in a format similar to the USCA, containing both statutes and notes of judicial interpretations of those statutes. Consider the Cornell Law School Search facility for the Code at https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text.

Legal research requires a little planning. It requires a lot of planning. The case you work on should be separated into problems and each problem may then be reviewed, cases summarized and synthesized. You need to know both the favorable and unfavorable cases. Though you need only recite those cases favorable to your position (there are some ethical restrictions concerning lawyers, who must cite controlling adverse precedent in court litigation), you should also know what cases oppose your position so that they can be distinguished, or at least weighed, in your evaluation or presentation of the case.

Each case has its peculiarities. There is no formula for analysis other than to identify and research the issues based on the facts that led to the action and, with research, to the defenses that are raised. Take a relatively simple appeal for a model. Assume a police officer working for the Department of Veterans Affairs becomes involved in an off-duty altercation with his son-in-law and that the incident results in the employee's arrest for misdemeanor assault, but not a conviction, and the VA learns of it. The employee happens to be a shop steward in his local union and recently had some harsh words with his supervisor concerning some grievances he presented on behalf of other employees. The supervisor, learning of the off-duty problem, wants to fire the employee. It seems likely that if adverse action charges are brought, the employee will raise as a defense reprisal for his union activities. The agency counsel or employee relations specialist and the employee's representative need to explore pertinent Board law. What are the issues?

First, we have the matter of proof: How do we demonstrate the fact of the off-duty arrest? What does it matter that the employee was arrested, but not convicted? Second, there is the problem of nexus: What difference does it make that the employee is involved in some type of off-duty fracas that seems to have no readily apparent impact on his daily job duties? Third, what factors mitigate against a disciplinary penalty? Are there comparable situations involving other employees? Then, what about the union business and the poor relationship between the employee and his supervisor, and the fair likelihood of a defense of reprisal before the Board or an arbitrator?

Reviewing all these problems, we construct a research outline. It might look something like this:

I. Evidence

a. Police Report; V.A. Investigation Report; Statement of Employee to V.A. Investigator

1. Research topics: hearsay; admissions; substantial evidence; official notice

II. Nexus

a. Off-duty misconduct with no conviction

1. Research topics: nexus--violent off-duty conduct; law enforcement positions; significance of arrest; burden of proof: presumptions and rebuttal of presumptions

III. Charge, Penalty and Mitigation

a. What charge is proper?

b. Possible provocation (from son-in-law); poor relationship with supervisor; lengthy service record

1. Research topics: mitigation--provocation; past work record; length of employment; prior discipline; potential for rehabilitation

c. Adverse action notice--mitigating or aggravating factors

1. Research topics: mitigation--articulation and weighing of relevant factors; significance of law enforcement position; Giglio impairment; deference to agency penalty determination; comparable penalties and disparate discipline; publicity or notoriety

IV. Affirmative Defense

a. Retaliation for union activities

1. Research topics: unfair labor practice or prohibited personnel practice and elements of proof (burden of proof, causation); mitigation (animosity of proposing or deciding official).

Once the case is reviewed and researched, it is recommended that a memorandum be developed that accurately and completely summarizes the facts (good and bad), with citations to documents in evidence or going into evidence, and with citations to statements or expected testimony from witnesses. There should then be a section of the memo containing governing case law (the good and the bad) followed by discussion of the application of the law to the facts. Then a conclusion stating the likely outcome of the case, where the weaknesses lie, and what further investigative work or research remains to be done. The memorandum, developing over the course of the case, should serve as a guide for the narrative supporting or responding to the appeal, for the prehearing submissions, for the adverse action hearing, and for the posthearing brief or closing argument--permitting a representative to ensure (through a check-off list) that every significant issue is raised and addressed, each pertinent document is entered into the record, every witness provides material testimony, and all important decisions and authorities are cited at the right stage of the case.
