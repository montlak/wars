A. CANCELATION OF APPOINTMENT, PROMOTION

Before an individual may be the object of an adverse action affecting his or her tenure in an appointive position, with its attendant due process protections, that individual must become an employee, that is, the individual must be appointed to a federal position. But the appointment process may go awry, and the question becomes whether the individual whose appointment never becomes final, or whose appointment is canceled, was subject to a constructive adverse action and whether that adverse action is procedurally invalid. The answer to that question lies in determining whether the appointment process was completed before the employee was separated from the government or before the appointment was canceled.

By statute, before an individual is appointed as an employee of the federal government, defined steps must be followed. The requisites of appointment are defined in 5 USC 2105:

Employee

(a) For the purpose of this title, "employee," except as otherwise provided by this section or when specifically modified, means an officer and an individual who is--

(1) appointed in the civil service by one of the following acting in an official capacity--

(A) the President;

(B) a Member or Members of Congress, or the Congress;

(C) a member of a uniformed service;

(D) an individual who is an employee under this section;

(E) the head of a Government controlled corporation; or

(F) an adjutant general designated by the Secretary concerned under section 709(c) of title 32;

(2) engaged in the performance of a Federal function under authority of law or an Executive act; and

(3) subject to the supervision of an individual named by paragraph (1) of this subsection while engaged in the performance of the duties of his position....

See Ainslie v. United States, 355 F.3d 1371, 1374 (Fed. Cir. 2004) (reviewing the requirements of § 2105(a) as to entitlement through 5 USC 3329(b) to a civil service position following involuntary separation under 32 USC 709 from a National Guard technician position).

The courts are inclined to require that formalities of the appointment process be followed because of the fiscal and other ramifications of an appointment. A court explained as to a classification challenge, Zucker v. Baer, 262 F. Supp. 528, 530-31 (S.D.N.Y. 1957):

...Quite clearly, one does not assume or hold a position until it has actually been assigned to him by competent authority, and, it is equally clear that one should not be permitted to apply for a reclassification of a position until he holds it. 5 USCA § 5112. Otherwise, there could be no effective organization of authority within the Civil Service. Employees would be free to assume unto themselves responsibilities they had no right to assume in an effort to upgrade their classifications. The result could be chaos, compounded by difficulties under Title 5 USCA, § 3101, which provides:

"Each Executive agency, military department, and the government of the District of Columbia may employ such number of employees of the various classes recognized by chapter 51 of this title as Congress may appropriate for from year to year."

If employees could attain reclassifications based on duties they themselves assumed, this could easily lead to there being more employees of a particular class in a given agency than Congress authorized. This, of course, would be intolerable.

The Board occasionally must determine if an individual has or has not been appointed to a position in an adverse action (removal or demotion) appeal filed by an individual who thought he was appointed to a position, only to find that the position was not his. Appointee status, by itself, does not offer the appellant statutory adverse action protections. An individual challenging cancelation of an appointment must still meet the statutory criteria governing the right to appeal, including sufficient qualifying service. See Scott v. Dept. of Treasury, 9 MSPR 263, 265 (1981) ("Even if appellant's appointment had not been revoked and he had entered on duty, as a temporary appointee, he would lack appeal rights to the Board from an adverse action. 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(l)(A). Moreover, the fact that appellant was an appointee for a short period of time does not establish his right to relief from the Merit Systems Protection Board. Appointee status, by itself, does not offer an appellant any of the statutory protections due federal employees under 5 U.S.C. § 7701 et seq."). [Refer to Chapter 1, "Appeal Rights of Probationer," for appeal rights of probationers; refer to appeal rights of veterans in Chapter 1, "Uniformed Service Employment and Reemployment Rights Act" and "Veterans Employment Opportunities Act."]

Most cases adjudicated by the Board involving falsification of employment applications are initiated by agencies under ordinary probationary termination or adverse action procedures under the "efficiency of the service" standard. [Covered in Chapters 1, 8.] Most of the case law that has evolved concerning cancelation of appointments is procedural. If an agency improperly circumvented ordinary hiring procedures, removal may be the appropriate remedy. The government has a legitimate interest in protecting the process, particularly if the appointment error prevents other individuals from competing for a position. Hatfield v. Dept. of Interior, 28 MSPR 673, 676 (1985).
