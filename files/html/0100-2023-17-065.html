a. Impact of Error

Whether the competitive level is properly constituted is not just a procedural right subject to harmful error analysis--it is a substantive right. The burden of proof is on the employer to demonstrate that the employee was placed in a proper competitive level. Speaker v. Dept. of Educ., 13 MSPR 163, 166 (1982). If an error is made as to retention standing, the agency has not properly conducted the RIF. The employee is not required to demonstrate that the agency error is harmful. Ray v. Dept. of Air Force, 3 MSPR 445, 447, 3 MSPB 516 (1980) (agency released an employee of higher retention standing, the appellant, while retaining an employee of lower retention standing in the competitive level). Because a substantive right is involved, it is immaterial that on reconstruction of the retention register for the appellant's competitive level, the agency determines that the appellant was not entitled to reinstatement. Lewis v. Dept. of Commerce, 25 MSPR 630, 631-32 (1985); Wright v. Dept. of Commerce, 20 MSPR 36 (1984) (same RIF; same result and reasoning as in Lewis). But see Copprue v. USDA, DA-0351-11-0305-X-1 (NP 2/23/2015) (an enforcement decision resulting from an AJ's initial decision that the agency improperly assessed the appellant's assignment rights, but the initial decision conditioned back pay on a subsequent determination by the agency that the appellant was qualified for another position under RIF regulations). [Copprue is discussed in this chapter, "Assessment of Qualifications."] However, if an agency releases employees from their competitive level without regard to their retention status and the appellant is the only individual on her retention register, her separation is proper. No deprivation of a substantive right occurred. Vickers v. Dept. of Labor, 20 MSPR 114, 117 (1984).

The law on the impact of RIF errors can be unclear. That may occur because, to note a case from federal sector labor relations, DOD, AAFES v. FLRA, 659 F.2d 1140, 1151 (D.C. Cir. 1981) "[t]he difficulty arises, of course, because the distinction between procedure and substance is not always crisp." At times the approach in Speaker, supra, and kindred cases is qualified by a modified harmless error rule. The agency's action will not be reversed if the Board can determine from the record that the positions that should have been included in a properly defined competitive level, and, further, if its review of those positions reveals that the agency's correct definition of the competitive level would not have affected the employee's substantive rights. Foster v. Coast Guard, 8 MSPR 240, 245, 7 MSPB 707 (1981) (reversing the RIF, however, when the record did not show that the appellant's position was so unique as to justify a one-person competitive level); see Hanks v. FEMA, 23 MSPR 567, 574 (1984) (agency need not establish competitive levels if all the employees of the agency are simultaneously released from their competitive levels); Wilburn v. Dept. of Transp., 19 MSPR 483, 484 n.* (1984) (citing Foster); Sarich v. Dept. of Interior, 12 MSPR 144, 147 (1982) (essentially applying the harmful error rule to the definition of competitive area and the development of a retention register). Under Foster, however, if the record demonstrates that a one-person competitive level is too narrow but does not reveal what the proper definition of the competitive level should have been, and if the Board cannot determine whether the agency would have arrived at the same decision had it correctly defined the competitive level, the agency fails to meet its burden of proving that the appellant was provided substantive rights. The RIF of the appellant is then reversed. [Refer later in this chapter to "Substantive Rights, Harmful Error."]

Hill v. Dept. of Commerce, 25 MSPR 205, 207-09 (1984), clarified Foster, supra:

In Losure v. Interstate Commerce Commission, 2 MSPB 361, 366, 2 MSPR 195, 201-02 (1980), we held that the nature of an employee's entitlement to continued employment under 5 USC 3502(a) is determined by application of the regulations set out in 5 CFR Part 351 and that with the exception of the notice provision in Subpart H, proper application of those regulations is a substantive right, rather than a procedural requirement. Thus, these substantive rights are not subject to the harmful error standard of 5 USC 7701(c)(1)(B), and the presiding official erred in utilizing a harmful error analysis.

While in Losure, the Board made clear that RIF entitlements were substantive rights and that it is the agency's burden to prove by a preponderance of the evidence that it afforded the appellant those rights, the Board subsequently explained that RIFs would not be reversed in those cases where it is shown that the agency's error in not precisely complying with the RIF regulations had no adverse effect on the employee's substantive entitlements. Foster v. Department of Transportation, 7 MSPB 707, 8 MSPR 240 (1981). The reason for such holding is that there is a difference between technical compliance with the rules governing RIF procedures and substantive entitlements of employees. An error in application of the procedures may affect the employee's rights but in many cases, because of the presence of particular facts and circumstances, there may be no effect on substantive entitlements. Reversals in the latter cases would lead to illogical and inequitable results which would work an injustice on the federal workforce and mission.

Foster, supra, and several cases which followed the Foster analysis, were decided on the basis of whether the Board could ascertain the effect on substantive entitlements from "the record." Questions have therefore arisen with regard to the burden of proof on this issue and we therefore find it desirable to further clarify the Foster holding. Because the agency must prove by a preponderance of the evidence that it afforded all rights to which an employee is substantively entitled, in cases where an issue is raised regarding an error in RIF procedures from which substantive rights are derived, the burden is on the agency to prove by preponderant evidence that such error had no adverse effect on the employee's substantive entitlements.

In the instant case, appellant contends that the agency's error in assigning her to a position which lasted for only two months adversely affected her substantive rights. Because the appellant has shown that an error in the RIF procedures occurred, utilizing the above analysis, we must determine whether the agency proved by preponderant evidence that such error did not adversely affect appellant's substantive entitlements.

The agency established in the unrebutted testimony of Ms. Sandra Turner Duckett that at the time of the first RIF, there were no positions, other than the one to which appellant was assigned, which were higher than the GS-9 level. Thus, had the agency known that the GS-11 position would not last for 3 months and therefore immediately assigned appellant to a position which would last 3 months, appellant would have been assigned to a GS-9 position initially. Therefore, we find that the agency has established by preponderant evidence that appellant's, substantive RIF rights were not adversely affected by the technical error in the application of 5 CFR Section 351.603(a). The presiding official's change in the effective date of the first RIF was thus in error.

RIF-related actions should be properly documented, but the documentation need not be perfect. Schroeder v. Dept. of Transp., 60 MSPR 566, 574 (1994), explained of a challenge to a competitive level:

Sections 293.101-103 provide that OPM will establish "minimum standards of accuracy, relevancy, necessity, timeliness, and completeness for personnel records it requires agencies to maintain." Although this regulation suggests that it is the general policy of the Federal government that agencies should maintain accurate personnel records, it does not itself set forth any standards of accuracy, nor have the appellants shown that the regulation provides for any penalty or remedy against the agency for its alleged failure to meet the demands of this policy. Thus, assuming for purposes of argument that the agency in this appeal previously failed to maintain accurate personnel records for appellants Schroeder and Whitwood, the appellants have not shown that such a failure is actionable before the Board in these RIF appeals. Moreover, the appellants have failed to show that the agency's alleged error provides a proper basis for depriving other employees of their statutory and regulatory retention rights during the RIF.
