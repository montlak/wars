broida flra Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices Chapter 08: Unfair Labor Practices, Subchapter 02: Management Violations, Section D: Unilateral Changes in Employment Conditions and Past Practice, Subsection 02: Past Practice, Part b: Historical Patterns
b. Historical Patterns

To make a determination that a past practice was changed, there must be evidence of the practice. FLRA dismisses ULP complaints when the record is so scant that it cannot support a finding that a particular practice existed in the past. See Dept. of Treasury, IRS, Louisville Dist. and NTEU, 42 FLRA 137, 143 (1991) (insufficient evidence to show past conditions governing assignment of GSA vehicles to revenue officers). To establish a change in practice, there must be evidence of the practice "before and after" a given time. Evidence from a few employees involving isolated incidents, without evidence showing how those incidents reflect either present practice or a departure from a defined past practice, likely will not make out a prima facie case of a ULP. VAMC, Memphis and NAGE Local R5-66, 42 FLRA 712 (1991); see Dept. of Labor, OWCP and AFGE Local 948, 56 FLRA 598, 603 (2000) (ALJ Decision) (past practice of using lottery to fill vacant offices was not established based on occasional use of lottery and occasional ad hoc management decisions); Letterkenny Army Depot and NFFE Local 1429, 34 FLRA 606, 612 (1990) (past practice of union representation at meetings between manager and employee to discuss the reasons why employee was not selected for promotion was not established by evidence of "a few, sporadic instances [when] Union representatives accompanied employees to non-selection meetings, compared to many such meetings conducted without the attendance of Union officials."); cf. VAMC Sheridan and AFGE Local 1219, 59 FLRA 93, 95 (2003) ("Although the General Counsel's evidence and arguments support a finding that there was an increase in the number of acute patients admitted to Unit 8, nothing in the General Counsel's exceptions or the record supports a finding that the Respondent changed its policy regarding the acuity of patients admitted to Unit 8, as alleged in the complaint.").

To constitute a past practice, that is, a term or condition of employment, the practice must be consistently exercised for an extended period of time and followed by both parties, or long followed by one party and not challenged by the other. For there to be a statutory violation, there must be a unilateral change in a condition of employment. A manual that was issued, reaffirming policies concerning breaks and luncheon periods, did not change past practice; it reaffirmed existing personnel policies and practices. That some managers previously deviated from those allotments of time was not controlling. SSA, Mid-Am. Serv. Ctr. and AFGE Local 1336, 9 FLRA 229, 240 (1982) (ALJ Decision). It is not an improper deviation from past practice for an agency that follows the pay and promotion rulings of the Comptroller General to modify its temporary promotion practices to follow a revision in case law by the CG. The agency followed its past practice of complying with CG rulings. HCFA and AFGE Local 1923, 17 FLRA 650, 653 (1985), aff'd, AFGE v. FLRA, 796 F.2d 530, 532-33 (D.C. Cir. 1986); see PTO and POPA, 57 FLRA 185, 191 (2001) (issue of whether past practice in negotiations occurred under parties' MOU: past practice requires consistent exercise over significant period of time, "followed by both parties, or followed by one party and not challenged by the other").

FLRA provided more elaborate explanation of what establishes a past practice in a water cooler case. Management supplied bottled water for a period of time after it discovered that the tap water was unsuitable. When the tap water problem was corrected, management sought to shut off the bottled water tap without bargaining. FLRA ruled a past practice was established, Dept. of Labor and AFGE Nat'l Council of Field Labor Locals, Local 1748, 38 FLRA 899, 909-10 (1990):

The record establishes that the Respondent furnished bottled water to bargaining unit employees for approximately 16 months before it unilaterally discontinued the practice. Further, the record shows that the Respondent furnished bottled water to bargaining unit employees for 3 months after the city water was certified as potable. There is no doubt that the practice of providing bottled water was known to both parties. In our view, the practice of providing bottled water was consistently exercised for a sufficient period of time so as to "establish" that practice.

More importantly, we reject the Judge's conclusion that the practice of providing bottled water was not established because that practice was of a "limited nature[.]"... As noted previously, for a condition of employment to be established by past practice, that practice must be followed by both parties or followed by one party and not challenged by the other. As such, the parameters of, or limitations on, the condition of employment must be understood by both parties.

The record before us does not provide a basis on which to conclude that the Union knew, or should have known, of the Respondent's intentions to limit the practice of providing bottled water. There is nothing in the record to show, for example, that at the time the practice was instituted, either the Union or employees were informed of any intended limitations on the practice. Moreover, shortly after the practice was initiated, the Union inquired about the water problem at a labor-management meeting. According to the Respondent's notes regarding that meeting, the Union was "advised that management has provided bottled drinking water on the floors where the problem exists."... Nothing in those notes shows that the Union was informed of any limitations on the practice.

The Respondent later stated to the Union that the bottled water was provided as an "interim" measure.... After that statement, however, the Respondent's notes indicate that, in response to another Union request at another labor-management meeting, the Union "was advised that the bottled drinking [water] will be provided indefinitely until more tests on the tap water can be done."... If the Respondent had a consistent intention regarding the practice of providing bottled water, it failed to so inform the Union. Accordingly, we are unable to conclude that the parties followed a known limited practice or that the Union should have, and failed to, challenge that practice.

We note, in this regard, that, consistent with the foregoing discussion, a respondent cannot by unilateral action define the parameters of its bargaining obligations. That is, a respondent cannot, by merely announcing that a practice is of a limited nature, establish limitations on the practice....

Finally, we reject the Judge's conclusion that the Respondent was privileged to terminate the practice of providing bottled water because "an agency [is not] required to bargain over the removal of a condition of employment which came into existence [sic] solely because of an emergency[.]"... There is no evidence in this case that the practice of providing bottled water was instituted because of an emergency. The Respondent did not argue before the Judge and does not now contend that it decided to provide bottled water for unit employees because of an "emergency." Although the record reveals that both the Respondent and the Union were concerned about the deterioration in the color, odor and taste of the drinking water, there is nothing in the record which suggests that the water problem was ever considered to be an "emergency" situation.

If management establishes both that a past practice was terminated and that it relied on voluntary compliance with directives preventing continuation of the practice, the union can establish a continuing practice only if the practice is "open to view" by management. The reaffirmation of an existing management policy is not a change in employment conditions. The union's failure to comply with the employer's expressed policy may not, without more, be used as proof of a past practice. Dept. of Treasury, IRS, Cleveland and NTEU, Chap. 44, 3 FLRA 656, 667-68 (1980) (ALJ Decision); see SSA, ODAR and AALJ, IFPTE, 68 FLRA 510 (2015) (ALJ Decision) (reaffirmation of what constituted past practice does not constitute unlawful unilateral change in working conditions). If management relies on a union's voluntary compliance with a policy that precludes use of office equipment for union business, and it is reasonable for management to assume union compliance, use of the equipment unobserved by management will not ripen into a past practice that cannot be stopped once discovered. If management intervenes to stop the prohibited practice, it has not changed past practice but reaffirmed prior practice, and it has not committed a ULP. Dept. of Treasury, IRS, Cleveland and NTEU Chap. 37, 6 FLRA 240, 248-49 (1981) (ALJ Decision). Although knowing acquiescence over a significant time may indicate past practice, acquiescence is not established if the agency makes prompt efforts to terminate a practice of union officials of using franked envelopes after that use was discovered. DHHS, SSA and AFGE, 17 FLRA 126, 140 (1985) (ALJ Decision).

Under SSA Mid-Am. Serv. Ctr. and AFGE Local 1336, 9 FLRA 229, 240 (1982) (ALJ Decision), occasional deviations from official policy by supervisors do not a past practice make: use of official time for certain purposes without the approval or knowledge of management or supervisors does not create a past practice or revive a past practice once that practice is terminated by consent of the union. If management then refuses to permit the use of the official time, its actions are a reaffirmation of past policy rather than a violation of past practice. Dept. of Navy, Polaris Missile Facility Atl. and AFGE Local 2298, 6 FLRA 372, 379 (1981) (ALJ Decision).

A past practice is deemed to continue, despite management's desires to discontinue it, if the attempt to change the practice is not communicated to the union and if, because the effort to discontinue the practice is not made clear to supervisors, not all supervisors discontinue the practice. Dept. of Navy, Portsmouth Naval Shipyard and IFPTE Local 4, 5 FLRA 352, 353 (1981). If a prior policy was ignored or often breached, a change in practice, as opposed to the official policy, requires bargaining and does not constitute reaffirmation of preexisting policy or practice. BLM, Richfield Dist. Office and AFGE Local 3790, 12 FLRA 686, 698-99 (1983) (ALJ Decision).

Local exceptions to a national policy do not ripen into an established past practice at the local level if there is no obligation to bargain at the local level. That would only occur if the agency at the national level had knowledge of and acquiesced in the local exception. Exceptions granted by local supervisors do not constitute acquiescence. DHHS, SSA and AFGE, 17 FLRA 1011, 1021-22 (1985).
