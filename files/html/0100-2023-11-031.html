a. Handwriting

If there is a challenge to the authenticity of a document, the party making the challenge may risk rejection of its challenge if it fails to provide expert testimony to back up its contentions. In one appeal, expert testimony on handwriting was lacking. See Raines v. USPS, 32 MSPR 56, 58 (1986) (rejecting, in the absence of expert testimony, an agency challenge to a letter from an individual withdrawing a complaint against an employee). It is the AJ who decides how the authenticity issue will be determined and whether a handwriting expert will be required.

An AJ may make a decision on the authenticity of handwriting even though he is not a handwriting expert. Authenticity is a question of fact within the province of the AJ. Boyling v. Dept. of Army, 6 MSPR 276, 278, 6 MSPB 236 (1981) ("Identification of handwriting is to be determined by the trier of fact, based upon lay or expert opinion or upon comparison with other handwriting samples in evidence."). Notes made by one supervisor can be authenticated and introduced through the testimony of another supervisor familiar with both the author's handwriting and the circumstances of the preparation of the notes. Once the notes are admitted, they have evidentiary weight even though they are hearsay. Powell v. Dept. of Treasury, 37 MSPR 78, 83-84 (1988).

Starr v. USPS, 80 MSPR 59, 61-62 ¶ 6 (1998), summarizes Board handwriting analysis requirements:

Identification of handwriting is to be determined by the trier of the fact, based upon lay or expert opinion or upon comparison with other handwriting samples in evidence. Boyling v. Department of the Army, 6 MSPB 236, 6 MSPR 276, 278 (1981). Handwriting comparisons may be made by the trier of fact, and conclusions drawn from them, in the presence or, as here, in the absence of expert opinion. Id. In comparing the signature on the return receipt card submitted by the agency, to the appellant's signatures contained on the appeal form, and the other documentation in the record, we conclude that the two signatures are not the same and, therefore, find that someone other than the appellant signed the return receipt card.

See Washington v. Dept. of Army, AT-0752-15-0138-I-1 (NP 9/21/2015) ("The Board has found that an administrative judge may identify handwriting based upon lay or expert opinion or upon comparison with other handwriting samples in evidence. Starr v. U.S. Postal Service, 80 M.S.P.R. 59, ¶ 6 (1998). The administrative judge may make handwriting comparisons and draw conclusions from them in the presence of, or in the absence of, an expert opinion. Id. Moreover, an administrative judge is free to reject the opinion of a witness offered as an expert. Mitchell v. Department of Defense, 54 M.S.P.R. 641, 644 (1992).").
