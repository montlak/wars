1. Custody and Control

If physical evidence is critical and if the appellant disputes the facts that the evidence is being offered to establish, an agency will not meet its burden of proof if it loses the evidence before the hearing through negligent handling of the physical evidence in its custody. Natividad v. USDA, 5 MSPR 415, 418, 5 MSPB 426 (1981). This point was illustrated in Banks v. FAA, 687 F.2d 92 (5th Cir. 1982), reversing Banks v. FAA, 10 MSPR 3 (1982), following remand, Banks v. FAA, 14 MSPR 283 (1982) (directing reinstatement of the appellant). The critical evidence comprised urine samples that allegedly showed drug use by the appellant. The lab samples were discarded by the lab before appellant's counsel could have them independently examined. When the Board failed to suppress the evidence of the lab results, the disciplinary action was sustained. The court concluded that the loss of the lab samples meant the loss of a meaningful opportunity for cross-examination and refutation of critical evidence. It was insufficient that secondary evidence was available, for example, through cross-examination of laboratory technicians. Although the government's failure to preserve, protect, and produce relevant evidence might be excused on a showing of good faith and reasonable effort, no showing was made. The court reversed the disciplinary action; there was no alternative evidence to sustain the action. See Dixon v. Dept. of Transp., FAA, 8 F.3d 798 (Fed. Cir. 1993) (reversing a dismissal because of the failure of chain of custody of urine samples). Ivery v. Dept. of Transp., 96 MSPR 119, 128 ¶ 21 (2004), reversed the discharge of an appellant when an agency failed properly to follow a split sample procedure: "an agency's procedural error may constitute harmful error when it effectively destroys, or precludes an appellant from acquiring, the only available evidence by which he can show that the agency likely would have reached a different conclusion in the absence of its error." But see Garza v. DOJ, 5 Fed. Appx. 894 (Fed. Cir. 2001 NP No. 00-3202) (declining to honor a chain of custody challenge raised by appellant as to a drug-test specimen when the appellant was familiar with the process and could have avoided the problem). But cf. Grimsrud v. Dept. of Transp., 902 F.3d 1364 (Fed. Cir. 2018) (denying rehearing en banc: compare majority and dissenting opinions relating to fundamental due process and denial to employee of his ability to have urine specimen tested by laboratory of employee's choice). The loss of physical evidence does not always mean a result favoring the appellant, particularly if the evidence can be described or portrayed in a manner that does not prejudice the appellant. Although destruction of evidence (a piece of stolen meat) before the hearing was considered regrettable, photographs and testimony, as well as a demonstration at the hearing, provided evidence sufficient to sustain the Board decision. Ingram v. Dept. of Air Force (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 85-674). Loss of lab results did not result in reversal of agency action when the appellant did not seek to secure those results for testing to rebut the agency allegations and when the results were not critical to the outcome of the case. Galloway v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 1986 NP No. 86-686); see Gamble v. Dept. of Navy, 54 MSPR 94, 97 (1992) (charge that a boiler plant operator slept on duty and rigged an alarm system of pipes positioned against the doors to fall and make a noise if the doors were opened, the failure of the agency to keep the pipe as physical evidence did not detract from the testimony of witnesses who observed some pipes rigged against doors and who heard the object fall when the door was opened). The Board's concerns about a "chain of custody" for evidence lessen if the appellant admits possession of the material at issue. See Averill v. Dept. of Navy, 30 MSPR 327, 330 (1986) (drug possession).

The AJ may take reasonable steps to ensure that an appellant secures access to physical evidence, and sanctions may be imposed if the agency does not make reasonable efforts to ensure that the appellant can review the evidence. See Crespo v. USPS, 53 MSPR 125, 131 (1992) (sanctions were denied because the agency made reasonable efforts to allow the appellant to inspect a check in the possession of either the Postal Inspector or the U.S. Attorney).
