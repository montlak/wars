b. Unfiled Settlement

When the parties settle but do not file their agreement, the agreement will not become part of the record, and it will not be enforceable by the Board when the initial decision becomes final. If a settlement agreement is not made a part of the record, and the appellant withdraws his appeal, the Board loses jurisdiction over the case. The agreement is reached free of Board intervention; it is between the parties; and it is treated as a contract beyond the enforcement authority of the Board. Banks v. USPS, 11 MSPR 100, 101 (1982); see Nolte v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 95-3704) (Board lacks jurisdiction over settlement that is not entered into the record) (relying upon King v. Reid, 59 F.3d 1215, 1218 n.2 (Fed. Cir. 1995)); Castro v. USPS, 51 MSPR 530, 533 (1991) ("A settlement agreement that is not made a part of the Board's record and is reached free of Board intervention, as the one at bar, is treated as a contract."); Harris v. USPS, 44 MSPR 547, 550 (1990). But see Caudill v. Dept. of Navy, 98 MSPR 228, 230 ¶¶ 5-7 (2005) (when an unfiled agreement resolved a constructive suspension on the representation by the AJ that the appeal could be refiled if the agency did not perform under the agreement, the implication was that the Board would resume jurisdiction of the underlying appeal when the appellant protested nonperformance and requested reinstatement of the appeal). The Board may consider a challenge to an unfiled settlement to determine the validity of a provision waiving the right to bring a Board appeal. See, e.g., Lee v. USPS, 111 MSPR 551, 553-56 ¶¶ 4-10, 2009 MSPB 121 (2009) (aff'd, Lee v. USPS, 367 Fed. Appx. 137 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP)). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Settlement Independent of MSPB Appeal"; "Waiver of Appeal Through Last Chance or Other Preappeal Agreement."]

An unfiled settlement leading to withdrawal of an appeal removes the case from MSPB jurisdiction, Thompson v. GSA, 38 MSPR 45, 47 (1988), although the Board would likely retain jurisdiction to enter the agreement into the record if it is through error of the AJ that the agreement is not placed into the record. Moore v. Dept. of Army, 38 MSPR 237, 240-41 (1988) ("[i]n the future, however, only the settlement terms which an administrative judge has approved and allowed to be entered into the record will be considered on a petition for enforcement of a settlement agreement"); see Gallagher v. USPS, 96 MSPR 677, 682 ¶ 14 (2004) (unfiled settlement led to dismissal of the appeal; the Board noted that the terms of the settlement were met); cf. Goulet v. Dept. of Commerce, 61 MSPR 336, 338 (1994) ("Because the initial decision had not yet become final at the time the administrative judge received the settlement agreement, she should have forwarded the agency's letter and the agreement to the Clerk of the Board as a motion to vacate the initial decision and reopen the record"); Lannen v. Dept. of Navy, 41 MSPR 662, 665 (1989) (Board recorded a settlement agreement that arrived at the regional office after an initial decision dismissed the appeal; the request to enter the settlement was not a PFR, but the region no longer had jurisdiction and the Board accepted the agreement to further public policy favoring settlement).

On occasion the Board receives what amounts to an enforcement petition based on a settlement that the AJ stated was accepted but that somehow did not find its way into the record. The Board then remands the appeal for proper review and entry of the agreement, if that is otherwise appropriate under Board case law, and for consideration of the enforcement petition. See DeGraaff v. Dept. of Navy, 55 MSPR 343, 347 (1992). Or the Board may be satisfied from the evidence presented with a PFR that the agreement was in fact submitted to the AJ, who dismissed the appeal, apparently without recognizing that the agreement was filed, and the Board will correct the problem by accepting the settlement into the record for enforcement if the other requisites for acceptance of the agreement are met. See Salas v. Dept. of Transp., 93 MSPR 688, 691 ¶¶ 9-10 (2003). The Board has the authority to enforce a settlement agreement that has been entered into the Board's record. Williams v. Department of Health & Human Services, 114 M.S.P.R. 11, ¶ 6 (2010). The appellant failed to show, or even allege, that the settlement agreement in question was entered into the Board's record in any previous proceeding.[2] In the absence of jurisdiction, the Board lacks any basis to consider her claim that the agency has acted in bad faith.
