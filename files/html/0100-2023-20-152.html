14. Mental Fitness Examination or Testing

In 1994 Congress amended the Whistleblower Protection Act through Pub. L. 103-424, § 5, to include as a covered personnel action "a decision to order psychiatric testing or examination." 5 USC 2302(a)(2)(A)(x). The 2012 Whistleblower Protection Enhancement Act amendments augmented compensatory damages proved to result from retaliatory investigations, under 5 USC 1221(g)(4):

Any corrective action ordered under this section to correct a prohibited personnel practice may include fees, costs, or damages reasonably incurred due to an agency investigation of the employee, if such investigation was commenced, expanded, or extended in retaliation for the disclosure or protected activity that formed the basis of the corrective action.

A psychiatric fitness examination may constitute a personnel action for purposes of an IRA appeal even if it is "offered" rather than ordered, Diefenderfer v. Dept. of Transp., 108 MSPR 651, 663-64 ¶¶ 30-32, 2008 MSPB 105 (2008):

...As the administrative judge acknowledged in his prehearing order describing the issues in this case, and again in his initial decision, a decision to order psychiatric testing or examination is a "personnel action" for purposes of 5 U.S.C. § 2302. 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(A)(x). The administrative judge found in his initial decision, however, that there was "no evidence to support [the appellant's] claim that she was given such an order"; he referred to documentary evidence he apparently believed supported a finding that the appellant's submission to such an examination was voluntary; and he found that the appellant had "implicitly conceded during her testimony that the agency did not order her to undergo a psychiatric examination."

The evidence on which the administrative judge relied in finding that the appellant had not been ordered to undergo a psychiatric examination appears to reflect a belief or assumption that 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(A)(x) covers only direct orders. That is, the administrative judge referred to the appellant's use of the word "asked" in a document in which she had stated that her supervisor "had asked me if I would be willing to undergo further medical evaluation," and he referred to cross-examination evidently intended to elicit the appellant's concession that no direct written order existed. The Board has indicated, however, that an action may be covered by that section even if the instructions at issue include language sometimes associated with offers or recommendations. In McCorcle v. Department of Agriculture, 98 M.S.P.R. 363, ¶¶ 3, 16 (2005), the Board found that a document in which the "agency advised the employee that it was 'offer[ing] ' him a paid 'psychiatric evaluation' to determine his fitness for duty," and in which it "further related that, if he did not accept the offer, the agency would 'consider initiation of appropriate disciplinary action...,'" provided a sufficient basis on which to require the administrative judge, on remand, to determine whether the agency had taken a personnel action under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(A)(x).

The appellant in this appeal denies that she made any concession that she voluntarily underwent the psychiatric examination at issue here; she refers to documents purportedly supporting her claim that she was ordered to undergo that examination; and she cites testimony in which she and another witness indicated that the only alternative to the examination would have been a suspension. Moreover, we see nothing in the evidence on which the administrative judge relied that amounts either to a withdrawal of this claim or to a concession that the examination was voluntary. In addition, we note that the testimony cited in the initial decision is followed by the appellant's testimony, on redirect, that she told her supervisor that she was unwilling to undergo an examination but would do so if ordered.

A recommendation for a psychological examination falls within the scope of a covered personnel practice in an IRA appeal, Agoranos v. DOJ, 119 MSPR 498, 512-13 ¶¶ 34-35, 2013 MSPB 41 (2013):

The agency argues on review that the administrative judge erred in finding that its recommendation that the appellant undergo a psychological examination constituted a personnel action under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2) because a mere recommendation to an employee to undergo a psychological examination is not a justiciable action under the WPA. The WPA provides that "a decision to order psychiatric testing or examination" is included under the definition of "personnel action." 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(A)(x). The Board has found that this provision covers not only direct order, but also language that may be associated with offers or recommendations when the agency further related that declination of the offer would result in consideration of disciplinary action. Diefenderfer v. Department of Transportation, 108 M.S.P.R. 651, ¶ 31 (2008). Thus, the Board has recognized that an implicit order to undergo a psychiatric examination, particularly when accompanied by a threat of repercussions if an offer or recommendation is declined, is within the scope of 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(A)(x). Id.

Here, the appellant alleged in his petition for appeal that, in reprisal for making his protected disclosures, the Special Agent in Charge and Associate Special Agent in Charge recommended that he undergo a psychological examination. Specifically, the appellant alleged that in the presence of four other management officials, the Special Agent in Charge said, "since you are not an agent I cannot mandate you into getting a psychological exam, but I am strongly recommending that you get one." Id. The appellant further alleged that he was treated in a demeaning, threatening and intimidating manner when he subsequently declined the offer to undergo a psychological examination. Id. The appellant also testified at hearing that he perceived the Special Agent in Charge's recommendation to be more than a mere offer. The administrative judge determined that the Special Agent in Charge's action constituted a "personnel action" under the WPA, and we see no reason to disturb his finding on this issue.

What the Board styled as a "standard" fitness exam was not a covered personnel practice, Huckel v. VA, AT-1221-15-0346-W-1 (NP 3/28/2016):

According to the appellant, the agency retaliated against him by ordering him to report for an "unprecedented" fitness-for-duty examination. While a psychiatric test or examination is a covered personnel action under the WPA, the appellant has not alleged, and the evidence he provided does not show, that the agency ordered him to report for a psychiatric test or examination. 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(A)(x). Rather, the record reflects that the agency ordered the appellant to report for a standard fitness-for-duty examination after he submitted a note stating that he could not stand or use his hands for more than 30 minutes at a time.

Before the statutory amendment, although fitness examinations (particularly psychiatric examinations) were considered (and acknowledged by the Board) to be subject to abuse and to have been used against disfavored employees, the Board declined in Caddell v. DOJ, 52 MSPR 529, 532 (1992), to characterize the fitness examination as a personnel action subject to review in an IRA appeal. Caddell was legislatively reversed by the 1994 legislation.
