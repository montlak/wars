8. Medical and Physical Qualification Requirements

Rindfleish v. OPM, 11 MSPR 498, 499-504 (1982), considered whether medical qualification standards set by OPM and administered by agencies could be challenged as unlawful employment practices. Because the agency had discretion to apply the regulations in a manner that avoided discrimination, the Board concluded that the medical standards were not "applied by OPM" in a manner that vested review jurisdiction in the Board:

Lisa A. Rindfleish (the appellant) petitioned the Board's Boston Regional Office for appeal of an employment practice under 5 CFR 300.101-300.104. She asserted that the visual acuity standard for Air Traffic Controller Specialist positions with the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) was an unlawful employment practice, imposed by the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) upon her as a candidate for such a position, in that it was not performance-related and it discriminated against her based on a physical handicap.

OPM filed a motion for dismissal of the appeal, contending that the Board lacked jurisdiction because the visual acuity standard in issue was not "applied...by the Office of Personnel Management" as required by 5 CFR 300.104(a) for the Board's jurisdiction to attach....

In an initial decision dated July 1, 1981, the presiding official found that the visual acuity standard was an "employment practice" under 5 CFR 300.101 which was applied to the appellant by OPM under 5 CFR 300.104(a) because it applied by its terms to every candidate for Air Traffic Controller Specialist positions. He concluded, therefore, that the Board had jurisdiction over the appeal. On the merits, the presiding official found that the standard was not performance-related because it was not effectively amended by FPM Letter 339-15 (September 24, 1979) to disqualify for appointment "only if there is a direct relationship between the condition and the nature of the duties of the specific position to be filled." He construed the minimum of 20/200 visual acuity in each eye without correction, as long as it is correctable to 20/20 in each eye, to limit the language in FPM Letter 339-15 providing that "a physical impairment will not disqualify an applicant for appointment if the condition is compensated for by a satisfactory prosthesis, mechanical aid, or by reasonable accommodation." He found, further, that this standard discriminated against the appellant, whose visual acuity was less than 20/200 in each eye without corrective lenses, because it failed to afford her the reasonable accommodation of wearing corrective lenses which corrected her vision to 20/20 in each eye. The presiding official concluded his initial decision with an order to OPM to rescind the visual acuity standard for Air Traffic Controller positions and to replace it with a general medical standard set forth in draft form in FPM Letter 339-16 (February 1981).

OPM then petitioned the Board for review of the initial decision, asserting that the presiding official erroneously interpreted 5 CFR 300.104(a) to find that the Board had jurisdiction over this appeal. We hereby GRANT the petition under 5 USC 7701(e)(1) and 5 CFR 1201.115.

...

OPM contended in its petition that, while it established the qualification standard relied on by the FAA in denying the appellant's application for employment, the FAA misapplied the standard in this case. OPM asserted that FPM Letter 339-15 amended the qualification standard for Air Traffic Controller Specialist positions, among others, to include the following paragraph:

The physical requirements of this standard are based on the arduous or hazardous nature of the duties typically performed by most of the positions covered by the standard. However, since individual positions may not include all such duties, a physical condition or impairment may be disqualifying for appointment only if there is a direct relationship between the condition and the nature of the duties of the specific position to be filled. In some instances, a physical impairment will not disqualify an applicant for appointment if the condition is compensated for by a satisfactory prosthesis, mechanical aid, or by reasonable accommodation. Reasonable accommodation may include, but is not limited to: the use of assistive devices, job modification or restructuring, provision of readers and interpreters, or adjusted work schedule.

OPM urged that this new paragraph was intended to and does in fact have the effect of modifying the visual acuity standard because maintaining an absolute medical qualification standard without reference to any relationship between the impairment and the duties of the position would be discriminatory on the basis of a handicapping condition under the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, 29 USC 701, et seq.

At the hearing on appeal, OPM's Medical Director testified as follows:

The changes reaffirm the federal medical employment policy of requiring job applicants and employees to possess only the minimum physical and mental abilities necessary for safe and efficient performance of the duties of the particular position. The effect of these changes will be enhancement of employment opportunities for handicapped persons by delegating more authority to federal employing agencies to determine the medical eligibility of an individual applicant for a position based on the actual tasks of that specific position and of the demonstrated abilities of the particular applicant and second, encouraging the use of reasonable accommodation.

This testimony is consistent with the following statements made by the Medical Director in an earlier letter to a Congressman concerning the appellant's situation:

The overall purpose of medical standards is to facilitate selection or retention of individuals whose ability to perform is not in question for medical reasons, or whose medical conditions do not present a degree of risk unacceptable to management. The failure of a particular applicant or employee to meet the performance related medical standard is an indication that the agency must carry out further evaluation of the person before making a final determination of his or her eligibility for selection or retention and shall not be taken to mean that the individual is unable to perform the essential duties of the position.

In a subsequent letter contained in the record, the FAA's Federal Air Surgeon acknowledged that the FAA met with OPM to discuss the effects of FPM Letter 339-15, including the concept for practical or functional testing of an applicant for employment who did not meet the qualification standards to determine whether he or she can nonetheless perform the duties of the position with reasonable accommodation. The Federal Air Surgeon indicated in that letter that the FAA had no employees trained to administer such a program and no standards by which to evaluate such testing. OPM urged in its brief, however, that FPM Letter 339-15 required the implementation of the new program forthwith, since it specified in the FPM Letter that the new language was "effective immediately."

In our view, the following language of FPM Letter 339-15 is controlling:

[A] physical condition or impairment may be disqualifying for appointment only if there is a direct relationship between the condition and the nature of the duties of the specific position to be filled. In some instances, a physical impairment will not disqualify an applicant for appointment if the condition is compensated for by a satisfactory prosthesis, mechanical aid, or by reasonable accommodation.

Contrary to the presiding official's construction, we find that this language delegated to the agencies the discretion to determine whether an applicant who does not meet a certain physical qualification standard is still qualified for appointment because, through reasonable accommodation of his physical condition, he can perform the duties of the position in question without endangering the health or safety of himself or others. This language thus constitutes a delegation of OPM's authority under 5 USC 7203 to determine whether the duties of a position can be performed efficiently by an individual with a handicapping condition, except where it would endanger someone's health or safety. Such delegation complements the requirement at 29 CFR 1613.704(a) that "[a]n agency shall make reasonable accommodation to the known physical or mental limitations of a qualified handicapped applicant...."

In light of these considerations, we find that although OPM originally promulgated the visual acuity standard for Air Traffic Controller Specialist positions, the ultimate determination of its applicability has rested with the FAA since FPM Letter 339-15 was issued on September 24, 1979. We concluded, therefore, that the visual acuity standard was not "applied...[to the appellant] by the Office of Personnel Management," as required under 5 CFR 300.104(a) for the Board's jurisdiction to attach, because her application was not denied until sometime in 1980 when its application was subject to the FAA's ultimate discretion. See Woolery v. OPM, 4 MSPB 227, 228, 4 MSPR 142, 144 (1980) (restriction on scope of competition for position in administration of merit promotion plan held not "applied" by OPM under 5 CFR 300.104(a) because its application was left to agency discretion).

In another case of a visual qualification standard imposed by an agency rather than by OPM, the Board explained that it lacked jurisdiction to consider a challenge to the application of that standard as an employment practice, Hall v. Dept. of Air Force, 12 MSPR 288, 289-90 (1982):

The record shows that in 1980, appellant was tentatively selected by the agency for a position of police officer subject to satisfying the physical requirements for that position. A medical examination revealed that he failed to meet the physical requirement of uncorrected vision of 20/70 (Snellen) in each eye. Consequently, the agency submitted a medical objection to OPM which upheld the objection and determined that appellant's eligibility should be cancelled. On appellant's appeal to OPM, the determination was changed to find appellant medically qualified, and OPM directed that his name be restored to the list of eligibles.

Under 5 CFR 300.104(a), a candidate for an agency position has the right to appeal to the Board when "an employment practice applied to him or her by the Office of Personnel Management violates a basic requirement of § 300.103," which requires, inter alia, a "rational relationship" between the duties of the position and the employment practice. That section also prohibits the basing of an employment practice on certain forms of discrimination "or other nonmerit factor."

Under 5 CFR 300.101, qualification standards constitute employment practices. However, the right of appeal to the Board conferred by 5 CFR 300.104(a) is limited to appeals from OPM violations of 5 CFR 300.103. Complaints regarding agency violations of that section may be made only to the agency through its grievance system. See 5 CFR 300.104(c)(1) and (2). Therefore, appellant had no right of appeal to the Board from the agency's nonselection action.

We note that the vision requirement here concerned was imposed by the agency and not by OPM. OPM has shown that its previous qualification standards for positions under the General Schedule were abolished and replaced by standards prohibiting medical disqualification unless a direct relation exists between the requirement and the duties of the position. See FPM Letter 339-15 (September 24, 1979). Thus, the vision requirement was an employment practice engaged in by the agency and not by OPM. See Cunningham v. ICC, 3 MSPB 473, 3 MSPR 394 (1980). The fact that OPM at first upheld the agency practice does not attribute the practice to OPM, particularly in this case where the evidence establishes otherwise.

McDown v. Dept. of Air Force, 25 MSPR 243, 244 n.* (1984), summarized Hall:

The appellant's contention of "erroneous certification" could be construed as an employment practice violation of qualification standards. Under 5 CFR § 300.101, qualification standards constitute employment practices. However, the right of appeal to the Board conferred by 5 CFR § 300.104(a) is limited to appeals from OPM violations of 5 CFR § 300.103. Complaints regarding agency violations of that section may be made only to the agency through its grievance system. Hall v. Department of the Air Force, 10 MSPB 810, 811, 12 MSPR 288, 289-90 (1982).
