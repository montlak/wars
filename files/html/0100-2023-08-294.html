f. Disability Incurred While in Military Service; Use of Leave for Disability

That the appellant suffered a disabling injury during military service and claims a current impact of that disability on the job is incidental to a claim of disability discrimination, but it does not establish a USERRA claim. McBride v. USPS, 78 MSPR 411, 415-16 (1998) (relying upon Leistiko v. Sec'y. of Army, 922 F. Supp. 66, 76 (D. Ohio 1996), aff'd, 134 F.3d 817, 821 (6th Cir. 1998)), followed, Ray v. VA, 84 MSPR 108, 109 ¶ 3 (1999) ("[A] claim of agency discrimination based on military-connected disability...is a claim of disability discrimination, not a claim covered under USERRA."); see London v. DHS, AT-315H-21-0601-I-1 (NP 4/22/2022) ("Regarding her possible USERRA claim, the appellant stated that she is a disabled veteran with a 30 percent service-connected disability and that the agency discriminated against her due to her service-connected disability. If the fact that the appellant incurred the injury during military service is incidental to her claim of disability discrimination, then it does not, on its own, make her claim a USERRA claim. McBride v. U.S. Postal Service, 78 M.S.P.R. 411, 415 (1998). Conversely, if the appellant is alleging the agency's actions, culminating in and including her termination, were motivated by her status as a disabled veteran, the Board may have jurisdiction over her claim. Lazard v. U.S. Postal Service, 93 M.S.P.R. 337, ¶ 8 (2003)."); Herrera v. USPS, DE-0752-15-0053-I-1 (NP 9/16/2016) ("Thus, it appears the appellant is arguing that the agency discriminated against him based on medical conditions resulting from his military service. However, an appellant's claim that he was discriminated against based on an injury or disability sustained while performing military service is not a cognizable USERRA claim. Mims v. Social Security Administration, 120 M.S.P.R. 213, ¶ 22 (2013)."); Jeanmarie v. Dept. of Air Force, DA-3330-14-0445-I-1 (NP 3/20/2015) ("Because of the injuries he sustained in the accident, the appellant asserts that he was taking pain medication, 'which had the sad side effect of creating changes in temperament.' The appellant believes that the side effects of the medication were responsible for the conduct that caused the agency to withdraw its tentative job offer. An allegation of discrimination based on a disability arising from military service is not a claim cognizable under USERRA. McBride, 78 M.S.P.R. at 415."); Bean v. USPS, AT-4324-12-0293-I-1 (NP 12/19/2012) ("Moreover, the administrative judge correctly noted that the appellant's attempt to reframe his disability discrimination claim as a USERRA claim based on the fact that his condition was service-connected does not constitute a viable USERRA claim. See Henson v. U.S. Postal Service, 110 M.S.P.R. [624] 629, ¶ 8 (2009) ('Thus, contrary to the appellant's argument on [petition for review], the fact that his disability is service-connected does not make the appellant's claim a USERRA claim.'); McBride v. U.S. Postal Service, 78 M.S.P.R. 411, 415 (1998) ('The fact that the appellant incurred a back injury while performing military service is incidental to her claim of disability discrimination. She has not, therefore, raised a USERRA claim.')"); Holmes v. DOJ, DA-4324-11-0661-I-1 (NP 7/25/2012) ("Nevertheless, as the administrative judge correctly found, the fact that the appellant's injury occurred during his military service does not transform his allegations into a USERRA claim. See, e.g., Daniels v. U.S. Postal Service, 88 M.S.P.R. 630, ¶ 8 (appellant's assertion that the agency took its action as a result of something that happened during his military service, as opposed to the fact of that service, failed to raise a claim under USERRA and his appeal was therefore properly dismissed for lack of jurisdiction), aff'd, 25 F. App'x 970 (Fed. Cir. 2001). Indeed, the fact that the appellant's claimed injury developed while he served on active military duty is irrelevant to his USERRA claim. See McBride v. U.S. Postal Service, 78 M.S.P.R. 411, 414-16 (1998) (USERRA proscribes the agency taking an action based on a veteran's performance of military duty, as opposed to an action based on an injury arising out of the performance of that duty; this interpretation is consistent with the statute's purpose, which mentions 'service,' and not injuries or disabilities arising from that service)."); Lazard v. USPS, 93 MSPR 337, 341 ¶ 6 (2003) (the Board distinguishes between appeals where the appellant alleges disability discrimination that happens to be service-connected and discrimination based on prior military service or veteran status); Daniels v. USPS, 88 MSPR 630, 633 ¶ 8 (2001) (citing McBride, supra: "In neither case does the appellant claim that the fact that he or she performed in a uniformed service or had an obligation to do so accounted for the agency's action; in both, they claim instead that it was as a result of something that happened during their service that accounts for the treatment they received from the agency.").

Although claims based on disability that are service connected are not cognizable under USERRA, under Jasper v. USPS, 73 MSPR 367 (1997), involving termination of a probationer, USERRA gives the Board jurisdiction to decide the appellant's allegation that the agency's termination action was discriminatory on the basis of his status as a disabled veteran, Henderson v. USPS, 95 MSPR 454, 458-59 ¶¶ 5, 7 (2004):

Under USERRA, the Board has jurisdiction over the appeal of "any person" alleging discrimination in federal employment on account of prior military service. See 38 U.S.C. §§ 4303(4)(A)(ii), 4311(a), 4324(b); McAfee v. Social Security Administration, 88 M.S.P.R. 4 ¶ 12 (2001); Petersen v. Department of the Interior, 71 M.S.P.R. 227, 231-40 (1996). USERRA states that "[a] person who...has performed...service in a uniformed service shall not be denied initial employment, reemployment, retention in employment, promotion, or any benefit of employment...on the basis of that...performance of service." 38 U.S.C. § 4311(a). Under USERRA, a person may submit an appeal against a federal executive agency alleging discrimination on the basis of prior military service directly to the Board, or he may submit such an appeal to the Board after he has filed a complaint with the Department of Labor and proceedings there have been exhausted. See 38 U.S.C. § 4324(b).

...An appellant's claim of discrimination based on his status as a disabled veteran is cognizable under USERRA. Jasper v. U.S. Postal Service, 73 M.S.P.R. 367, 369 (1997), overruled on other grounds by Fox v. U.S. Postal Service, 88 M.S.P.R. 381 ¶ 9 n.* (2001). An appellant must receive explicit information on what is required to establish an appealable jurisdictional issue. Burgess v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 758 F.2d 641, 643-44 (Fed. Cir. 1985).

Here, the appellant asserted in her petition for appeal and in her response to the agency's motion to dismiss that she was a disabled veteran and submitted evidence showing that she is "in receipt of disability compensation on account of service-connected disability rated at 30 percent or more." Further, the appellant expressly invoked USERRA below. Thus, we find that her allegations regarding her termination from employment, disparate treatment, and veteran's status can be reasonably viewed as raising a USERRA claim based on her status as a disabled veteran. We therefore find that the appellant's allegations should have been considered as a USERRA appeal. Thus, the administrative judge should have provided the appellant with adequate notice of what is required to establish Board jurisdiction under USERRA. See Muse v. U.S. Postal Service, 82 M.S.P.R. 164 ¶ 12 (1999).

The line between discrimination based on military service and discrimination against individuals disabled as a result of military service may be indistinct. In Bagunas v. USPS, 92 MSPR 5, 13-14 ¶¶ 17-18 (2002), (relief denied following remand, aff'd, Bagunas v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP No. 03-3184), reopening denied, Bagunas v. USPS, 100 MSPR 328 (2005)), members of a panel evaluating the applicant's qualifications were alleged to have ridiculed the appellant based on what they believed to be a combat-related disability. The Board remanded the appeal for a hearing on the appellant's USERRA claim after concluding that the ridicule may have suggested bias against veterans:

The AJ held that the appellant did not allege a cognizable claim under USERRA, interpreting the appellant's allegations as a claim of discrimination based on a disability arising out of military service. Relying on Morgan v. U.S. Postal Service, 82 M.S.P.R. 1 (1999), Jasper v. U.S. Postal Service, 73 M.S.P.R. 367 (1997), and Petersen v. Department of the Interior, 71 M.S.P.R. 227 (1996), the appellant argues that he has stated a claim of discrimination under USERRA based on his "status as a disabled veteran." Jasper does not support the appellant's argument because there the Board merely relied on evidence from the Department of Veterans Affairs regarding the appellant's service-connected disability in finding that the appellant established his status as a veteran. 73 M.S.P.R. at 370. Portions of Morgan could be read as implying that a claim of discrimination based on "disabled veteran status" can proceed under USERRA, 82 M.S.P.R. 1 ¶ 12 & n.3, but later precedent makes clear that a claim of discrimination based on a disability alone--even a disability arising out of military service--is not cognizable under USERRA. Daniels v. U.S. Postal Service, 88 M.S.P.R. 630 ¶¶ 7-9, aff'd, 25 Fed. Appx. 970 (Fed. Cir. 2001) (Table); McBride v. U.S. Postal Service, 78 M.S.P.R. 411, 415 (1998).

The Petersen decision, on the other hand, lends some support to the appellant's argument that he states a cognizable claim under USERRA. In Petersen, the appellant alleged that because of his military service he was referred to as a "psycho," "baby killer," and "plate head" at his employing agency. 71 M.S.P.R. at 235. The Board stated that Congress intended an "expansive interpretation" of the scope of the USERRA anti-discrimination provision, and accordingly held that a claim of discrimination based on a hostile environment marked by anti-veteran animus and remarks could proceed under USERRA. Id. at 239. Here, the appellant's allegation that the interview panel was skeptical and curious about his service-connected disability is not a hostile environment discrimination claim because the appellant was an applicant for employment, not an employee. Nonetheless, the remarks about the appellant "not look[ing] disabled" and having "bullets in his body" that the appellant attributes to the interview panelists, if made, could be some evidence of anti-veteran animus among the panelists. This evidence, if further developed, and if coupled with circumstantial evidence of discrimination, could permit a finding of discrimination.[5] Under an "expansive interpretation" of the USERRA anti-discrimination provision, and considering the appellant's repeated assertions in his petition for appeal and in response to the show-cause order that the agency discriminated against him because of his "veteran status" and "military status," we conclude that he has stated a claim of discrimination that can proceed under USERRA. In this connection, it must be emphasized that the relative weakness of factual allegations in support of a USERRA claim is not a ground for dismissing the claim summarily; instead, the claim should be denied on the merits if the appellant fails to develop those allegations. Cf. Duncan v. U.S. Postal Service, 73 M.S.P.R. 86, 92 (1997). As yet, the appellant has not been given an opportunity to develop his allegations.

[5] The appellant's allegations that he "scored very well on the written examination" and that he "was highly qualified for the position by virtue of educational achievement and work related experience" might be some circumstantial evidence of discrimination if the appellant could show that non-veterans who scored lower or were not as well-qualified were selected. While the appellant has not introduced such evidence, it does not appear that he has had a full opportunity to gather evidence in discovery.

In Davison v. VA, 115 MSPR 640, 646-47 ¶¶ 14-15, 2011 MSPB 25 (2011), the appellant could state a USERRA claim based on an assertion of adverse treatment resulting from use of leave as a disabled veteran under Executive Order 5396:

The appellant's allegation of agency retaliation for the exercise of his right as a disabled veteran to LWOP under Executive Order 5396 distinguishes this case from Bagunas v. U.S. Postal Service, 92 M.S.P.R. 5, ¶ 17 (2002), overruled on other grounds by, Garcia v. Department of Agriculture, 110 M.S.P.R. 371, 375-77 (2009), and its progeny, in which the Board held that a claim of discrimination based on a disability alone--even a disability arising out of military service--is not cognizable under USERRA, see also Daniels v. U.S. Postal Service, 88 M.S.P.R. 630, ¶ 8 (2001) aff'd, 25 Fed. Appx. 970 (Fed. Cir. 2001); McBride v. U.S. Postal Service, 78 M.S.P.R. 411, 414-15 (1998). In such cases, "the appellant [does not] claim that the fact that he...performed in a uniformed service...accounted for the agency's action"; rather he "claim[s] instead that it was as a result of something that happened during [his] service that accounts for the treatment [he] received from the agency." Daniels, 88 M.S.P.R. 630, ¶ 8. Here, the appellant alleges, not that his service-related disability accounted for the agency's action, but that its action was in reprisal for having exercised a right to LWOP granted only to those who have performed in a uniformed service. Indeed, as stated above, the appellant has indicated that, although he is a disabled veteran, the medical treatment that he received while on LWOP status was not "for treatment of a service connected condition," and he argues on review that although "[t]he Veteran needs to have a service connected condition to benefit from Executive Order 5396,...the medical treatment can be for any condition, service connected or not."

Based on the foregoing, we find that the appellant has made a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction under 38 U.S.C. § 4311. In so finding, we express no opinion on the merits of the appellant's USERRA claim, his interpretation of Executive Order 5396, or whether he was entitled to LWOP under that Executive Order. The strength or weakness of the assertions in support of a claim is not a basis to dismiss the USERRA claim for lack of jurisdiction; rather, if the appellant fails to develop his contentions, his USERRA claim should be denied on the merits. Tindall, 84 M.S.P.R. 230, ¶ 9; Melvin, 79 M.S.P.R. at 377. We therefore find it necessary to remand this appeal for adjudication of the appellant's USERRA claim.

Allegations of discrimination based on one's status as a disabled veteran are cognizable under USERRA. Kirkendall v. Department of the Army, 94 M.S.P.R. 70, ¶ 6 (2003). In addition, the Board has found that an appellant may establish Board jurisdiction over a USERRA claim by nonfrivolously alleging that the agency discriminated against him for using leave to which he was entitled only due to his status as a disabled veteran. Davison v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 115 M.S.P.R. 640, ¶¶ 12-15 (2011). Therefore, we find that the appellant has raised a cognizable USERRA claim and forward it to the regional office for docketing as a new appeal.
