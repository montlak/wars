a. Agency Restriction on Appearance of Employee as Witness

The subject of availability of agency personnel as witnesses is touched on in 5 CFR 1201.33 (2022):

Federal witnesses.

(a) Every Federal agency or corporation, including nonparties, must make its employees or personnel available to furnish sworn statements or to appear at a deposition or hearing when ordered by the judge to do so. When providing those statements or appearing at a deposition or at the hearing, Federal employee witnesses will be in official duty status (i.e., entitled to pay and benefits including travel and per diem, where appropriate). When a desired witness is employed by an agency who is not a party to the Board proceeding, the requesting party may avail itself of the provisions of sections 1201.81 to 1201.85 of this part regarding subpoenas to ensure the attendance of the witness. In addition, the Board and the parties will implement this provision, to the maximum extent possible, to avoid conflict with other regulations governing the production of Federal employees in matters in litigation.

Board commentary on the regulation, 77 Fed. Reg. 62349, 62354 (Oct. 12, 2012), states:

...However, we have revised the regulation to indicate that the Board and the parties will implement this provision, to the maximum extent possible, to avoid conflict with other regulations such as those issued pursuant to United States, ex rel. v. Touhy, 340 U.S. 462, 467 (1951) regarding the production of evidence from Federal employees in matters in litigation.

Many agencies that perform adjudicative functions have regulations that preclude their employees from testifying in other forums unless conditions of the regulations are met. Richardson v. Dept. of Interior, SF-4324-09-0913-I-1 (NP 9/9/2010), considered and honored a Labor Department regulation in a USERRA appeal:

The appellant asserts that the administrative judge erred in the manner that he treated the possible testimony of Department of Labor (DOL) employee Dale Langford regarding his processing of the complaint that the appellant filed with the DOL. The appellant requested Langford as a witness at the hearing and the administrative judge approved the appellant's request. Prior to the hearing, the DOL informed the administrative judge that DOL regulations prohibit DOL employees from providing information that relates to records contained in DOL files, that this regulatory prohibition has been upheld in United States ex rel. Touhy v. Ragen, 340 U.S. 462 (1951), and that DOL informed the appellant what he must do under the regulations to show that Langford's testimony was necessary in his appeal. The appellant failed to show that he had established to DOL that Langford's testimony was necessary. Further, as the administrative judge found, the appellant's offers of proof regarding Langford's anticipated testimony do not show that that testimony is relevant to any disputed material fact in the appeal. Thus, there is no merit to the appellant's assertion that the administrative judge improperly failed to consider Langford's testimony. Moreover, the administrative judge properly found that a credibility determination regarding Carla Perez's testimony was unnecessary because the misinformation Perez allegedly provided to Langford is immaterial to the adjudication of this appeal.

On the subject of agency control or interference with witnesses called by the appellant at a Board hearing, Oberman v. USPS, 13 MSPR 218, 219-20 (1982), explained that there is nothing fundamentally improper about the agency requesting that its witnesses follow Board directives concerning attendance rather than informal requests from the appellant or his counsel:

Appellant contends that the agency committed reversible error when, three days prior to the hearing, the agency notified several security police officers it employed that they were not to attend the hearing based on the sole request of appellant's counsel, but to attend the hearing only upon the request of the presiding official. Thereafter, upon the request of appellant's counsel, the presiding official directed the agency to provide the witnesses at the hearing and to inform them that no reprisals would be taken against them for testifying on appellant's behalf.

Although appellant urges that the agency interfered with his right to obtain the attendance of the agency's employees on his behalf, his counsel had not previously sought the assistance of the presiding official to order the appearance of the employees. See 5 CFR § 1201.41(b)(9). See also Gildersleeve v. U.S. Postal Service, 4 MSPB 465, 4 MSPR 409 (1980); Croken v. U.S. Postal Service, 3 MSPB 238, 240, 3 MSPR 128, 131 (1980). Under the Board's procedures, an agency "shall make its employees available to furnish sworn statements or to appear as witnesses at the hearing when requested by the presiding official...." 5 CFR § 1201.33. The agency was not obligated to make its employees available for the hearing until their presence was ordered by the presiding official.

Further, although appellant contends that the agency's original instruction to its security police officers inhibited them from testifying freely and fully, the transcript discloses that the presiding official and the agency took steps to assure the witnesses that they could testify free from reprisal. Additionally, the transcript discloses that the witnesses answered fully all questions put to them by appellant's counsel. The Board finds that appellant has not shown that his defense was prejudiced by the agency's conduct in this matter.

As to agency production of witnesses at a hearing, the AJ should ensure that the agency produces witnesses irrespective of internal agency procedures, Alford v. DOD, 118 MSPR 556, 561-62 ¶ 8-10, 2012 MSPB 110 (2012):

During the proceedings below, the appellant sought to present testimony from two of his treating doctors. On January 13, 2011, the administrative judge approved Drs. Erica Jarrett and John Massey to testify as witnesses for the appellant at the hearing which began on January 24, 2011. In his October 29, 2010 prehearing submission, the appellant had identified each as a "federal employee, Walter Reed Army Medical Center," who was to testify regarding the appellant's stress-related mental health issues during the performance improvement period. Following the prehearing conference, the appellant, through his counsel, made efforts to secure the presence of Jarrett and Massey. An attorney for Walter Reed Army Medical Center advised the appellant's representative that the Army had to authorize the appearance of its personnel at the Board hearing, and that the appellant had to make the request in writing. The appellant's attorney expressed to the agency representative on January 14, 2011, that he was not sure if it would be possible to work out all the details to get the Army doctors to the hearing and that, if not, he would consider asking the administrative judge to allow their testimony at a later time. By letter on January 21, 2011, the Walter Reed Army Medical Center attorney granted the appellant's request for the appearance of Jarrett and Massey. He further stated that the doctors' supervisors had to approve their absence from duty and he suggested to the appellant's attorney that "you conduct any necessary interviews at Walter Reed Army Medical Center." The doctors did not appear at the hearing, and the appellant objected to their absence. On January 31, 2011, the appellant filed a motion to reopen the hearing so that he could call the two witnesses. The agency opposed the motion, and the administrative judge denied it, reasoning that the appellant had failed to secure the attendance of the witnesses.

The Board's regulations provide that every federal agency must make its employees available to furnish sworn statements or to appear as witnesses at the hearing when ordered by the administrative judge to do so. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.33(a). Inasmuch as the administrative judge approved Jarrett and Massey to testify, and in light of the restrictions and delays imposed by Walter Reed Army Medical Center, the administrative judge should have ordered the appearance of these witnesses at the hearing. It was not the appellant's responsibility alone to ensure their appearance.

Because the approved witnesses were to provide medical testimony in support of the appellant's failure to accommodate claim of disability discrimination, and because our reviewing court has cautioned against disallowing witnesses whose testimony may bear on an appellant's affirmative defense, see Whitmore v. Department of Labor, 680 F.3d 1353, 1368-70 (Fed. Cir. 2012), we direct the administrative judge to conduct a supplemental hearing in order to obtain testimony from Jarrett and Massey and to make new findings on the appellant's failure to accommodate disability discrimination claim, and we therefore remand the appeal for that purpose.
