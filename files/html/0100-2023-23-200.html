a. Evidence Required

As with underlying appeals, resolution of compliance proceedings depends on evidence to support allegations and defenses. An appellant will not succeed in an enforcement action through conclusory allegations that the agency breached its settlement agreement; nor is the appellant likely to be able to rely on discovery to fill in the gaps of essential allegations of noncompliance, Kuykendall v. VA, 68 MSPR 314, 328-29 (1995):

Although an agency bears a heavy burden of production to submit all of the relevant, material, and credible evidence of its compliance responsive to an appellant's allegations, the appellant has the ultimate burden of establishing that the agency breached the settlement agreement. See Perry v. Department of the Army, 992 F.2d 1575, 1578 (Fed. Cir. 1993); Fredendall v. Veterans Administration, 38 MSPR 366, 371 (1988). We find it telling that the appellant here has not submitted any independent documentation, such as statements by witnesses, to support any of her numerous allegations of continual and overwhelming retaliation/harassment. Unlike the situation in Perry, the agency here was not "wholly possessed of the [relevant] evidence." Id. at 1578. Nor was she entitled to her requested discovery and hearing to establish her allegations. An employee is not entitled to them in enforcement proceedings, although the AJ may grant them in his discretion. See 5 CFR § 1201.183(a)(3); Forston v. Department of the Navy, 60 MSPR 154, 158 (1993); Covert v. Department of the Navy, 31 MSPR 376, 382 (1986). Because the appellant failed to specify below any genuine issue of material fact for which discovery and a hearing were warranted,[12] the AJ did not abuse his discretion in denying them. Cf. Briscoe v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 55 F.3d 1571, 1573 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (where the appellant presented no evidence to support her "bald allegations" of jurisdiction, those allegations were "frivolous" and thus did not warrant a jurisdictional hearing). We therefore find that the appellant failed to meet her burden of establishing that the agency breached the settlement agreement by failing to implement in good faith the reinstatement term.

[12] In requesting discovery below, the appellant simply contended that the agency had "generally denie[d]" her allegations, that the "true facts" could only be revealed through discovery, and that she wished to "ascertain what the Agency ha[d] done in similar situations regarding other employees who ha[d] not filed mixed case appeals with the Board."

Mann v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-11-0736-C-1 (NP 6/24/2014), returned an appeal to an AJ for development of evidence in a compliance proceeding:

The appellant filed only two pleadings in the compliance proceeding: the petition for enforcement itself and a response to the agency's statement of compliance with the terms of the settlement agreement. Both are signed by the appellant's representative; neither contains any document signed by the appellant himself. Nor do the appellant's pleadings contain documents signed by anyone else with first-hand knowledge of relevant events or information. Similarly, all of the agency's pleadings are signed by the agency's representative, and none contain documents signed by anyone else with first-hand knowledge of relevant events or information.

It is well established that the statements of a party's representative in a pleading do not constitute evidence in a Board proceeding. See, e.g., Felton v. Department of the Air Force, 106 M.S.P.R. 198, ¶ 7 (2007); Santos v. U.S. Postal Service, 77 M.S.P.R. 573, 577 (1998); Hendricks v. Department of the Navy, 69 M.S.P.R. 163, 168 (1995). Unfortunately, the administrative judge did not advise the parties of this well-established principle or advise them that statements by persons with first-hand knowledge of relevant events and information would be required in order to determine whether the agency was guilty of bad faith conduct in implementing the terms of the settlement agreement.
