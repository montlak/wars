h. Collateral Issues Following Rescission of Action; Claims for Attorney Fees or General Damages; Interest

When an action is canceled, the appeal is not moot until there is restoration of the employee to the status quo, and that may involve back pay and residual issues. Watson v. Dept. of Treasury, 38 MSPR 64, 66 (1988). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Restoration of Status Quo Ante," concerning incomplete restoration of status quo.] But a claim for counsel fees does not preclude dismissal for mootness. The fee claim should be presented after the final decision is issued dismissing the appeal. Currier v. USPS, 72 MSPR 191, 198-99 (1996) ("attorney fee issues are distinguishable from compensatory damages issues for purposes of determining mootness"). The chances of obtaining fees following cancelation of an action may be quite limited under current law, but whatever the ultimate entitlement the pursuit of a fee application does not preclude mootness of the underlying appeal following cancelation of the underlying action. See Uhlig v. DOJ, 83 MSPR 29, 32 ¶ 11 (1999) ("the appellant will not be barred by a finding of mootness from pursuing any outstanding motion for attorney fees"); Alleman v. Dept. of Army, 79 MSPR 233, 239-40 (1998) ("The Board has long held...that an appellant's intention to file a motion for attorney fees does not prevent dismissal of an otherwise moot appeal."); Hudson v. DHUD, 55 MSPR 639, 643 (1992) ("The appellant is also not entitled to attorney fees as a condition to being returned to the status quo ante."); Brown v. OPM, 51 MSPR 261, 263 (1991) (fee petition was not controlled by a mootness determination; the petition must be timely filed after the final date of the decision dismissing the underlying appeal as moot); cf. Young v. Dept. of Energy, 21 MSPR 541, 543 n* (1984) (declining to address the mootness issue when "the appellant has, appropriately, filed a motion for attorney fees").

Of the interplay between mootness of an appeal and a counsel fee claim, Tunik v. MSPB, 407 F.3d 1326, 1330-01 (Fed. Cir. 2005), noted:

As a preliminary matter, the Agency, as Intervenor, argues that Tunik's appeal is moot because he voluntarily retired. Tunik responds that his claim for attorney's fees remains alive and that he may choose to claim on remand that his retirement was involuntary. Moreover, he argues that there remains a risk that the Agency would take similar actions to impair his decisional independence in the future. First, we are unable to discern the claim for attorney's fees to which Tunik refers. He has presented no claim for attorney's fees to this court, nor did he present a claim for attorney's fees to the Board. Moreover, even if he had presented a claim for attorney's fees, "[an] interest in attorney's fees is, of course, insufficient to create an Article III case or controversy where none exists on the merits of the underlying claim." Lewis v. Continental Bank Corp., 494 U.S. 472, 480 (1990).

The right to petition for counsel fees has become illusory, perhaps moot, in cases involving complete rescission of the underlying action before a merits disposition and when the rescission did not result from a settlement incorporated into the Board's record for purposes of enforcement. To obtain a Board award of fees, settlement agreement aside, one must be a prevailing party. In Buckhannon Bd. v. West Virginia DHHR, 121 S. Ct. 1835 (2001), the Supreme Court implied that one does not become a prevailing party unless a shift in the parties' position results through an order of the adjudicator. Sacco v. Dept. of Justice, 90 MSPR 37, 39-42 (2001), aff'd, Sacco v. DOJ, 317 F.3d 1384 (Fed. Cir. 2003), makes plain the Board's current inability to award fees when agencies completely rescind underlying appealable actions without some formal action from the Board, e.g., an initial decision directing mitigation or reversal of the action, or a settlement accepted by the AJ. A claim for damages under the Civil Rights Acts or the Whistleblower Protection Act blocks the appeal from being mooted by an agency's unilateral rescission of the underlying action. Under the whistleblower statute, if the underlying action that motivated the appeal is canceled, the appeal is not moot if there is a claim for counsel fees and costs, amounting to a claim for damages that may be available to whistleblowers. See Turner v. VA, 96 MSPR 476, 478 ¶ 6 (2004). [Refer later in this chapter to "EEO Allegation; Claim for Compensatory Damages," "Whistleblower Protection Act: Claims for Damages, Other Relief" and Chapter 15, "Must Prevail Through MSPB Procedures" and "Cancelation of Action Appealed" on counsel fees.]

If the agency rescinds an action, it is not required to pay interest on the appellant's back pay, and the appeal may be dismissed as moot, Williams v. Dept. of Army, 97 MSPR 246, 250-51 ¶¶ 11-14 (2004):

In its response to the show-cause order, the agency states that it has not paid the appellant any interest on his back pay. The agency defends its decision not to pay interest on the back pay by arguing that it is not required by the Back Pay Act, codified at 5 U.S.C. § 5596(b), to do so because the removal action was not found to be unjustified or unwarranted by any appropriate authority. We agree.

The Back Pay Act is a waiver of sovereign immunity which allows the Board "to make employees whole for any losses they suffer as a result of an unwarranted or unjustified action." Mattern v. Department of the Treasury, 291 F.3d 1366, 1369 (Fed. Cir. 2002). As the Supreme Court of the United States held in Lane v. Pena, 518 U.S. 187, 116 S. Ct. 2092 (1996), "a waiver of the Government's sovereign immunity will be strictly construed, in terms of its scope, in favor of the sovereign." id. at 192, 116 S. Ct. at 2096 (citations omitted). The Court went on to explain that where a party is requesting money as part of the alleged damages, "the waiver of sovereign immunity must extend unambiguously to such monetary claims." id., 116 S. Ct. at 2097 (emphasis added); see also Department of the Army v. Blue Fox, Inc., 525 U.S. 255, 261, 119 S. Ct. 687, 691 (1999) (a waiver of sovereign immunity must be "'unequivocally expressed' in the statutory text") (citation omitted).

The only statutory basis for the payment of interest on back pay is, as the agency points out, the Back Pay Act as codified at 5 U.S.C. § 5596(b). Subsections 5596(b)(1) and (b)(2)(A) state unambiguously that an employee is entitled to back pay and interest on back pay only where an "administrative decision" is issued by an "appropriate authority under law, rule, regulation, or collective bargaining agreement to have been affected by an unjustified or unwarranted personnel action."

Here, no administration decision was issued finding that the agency subjected the appellant to an unjustified or unwarranted personnel action. Rather, the agency rescinded its removal action before the Board could find whether the action was unjustified or unwarranted. Thus, by its plain terms, the Back Pay Act does not apply to this situation. See Gray v. Office of Personnel Management, 771 F.2d 1504, 1513 n.13 (D.C. Cir. 1985) (the Back Pay Act, as a waiver of sovereign immunity is applicable only "in carefully limited circumstances" set out in the statute itself). Since the statutory waiver of sovereign immunity to provide interest on back pay must be strictly construed, the Board has no authority under the statute to order the agency to award the appellant interest on the back pay which it has already given him. Absent such authority, the Board must dismiss this appeal as moot. Accord, e.g., Buckhannon v. West Virginia Department of Health & Human Resources, 532 U.S. 598, 604-05, 121 S. Ct. 1835, 1840-41 (2001) (given the clear statutory language regarding the term "prevailing party," attorney fees cannot be awarded where the defendant voluntarily changes his conduct; rather, attorney fees can be awarded only where a tribunal has issued a judgment on the merits or the parties reach a settlement agreement enforceable through a court-ordered consent decree).
