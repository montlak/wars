d. Period of Non-Pay Status

If the employee occupies the same position before and after a compensable injury, the period that the employee receives compensation benefits under 5 USC 8151(a) is continuous service under 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(B). Strawbridge v. USPS, 31 MSPR 386, 388 (1986). Service during an indefinite suspension counts toward the "current continuous service" requirement. See Jackson v. USPS, 73 MSPR 512, 514 n.2 (1997). Military service is not qualifying. See McKay v. USPS, 14 MSPR 618, 619 n.* (1983). Under Campbell v. USPS, 88 MSPR 546, 549-50 ¶¶ 7-9 (2001), involving an excepted service employee, a period of absence counts toward current and continuous service:

The issue remains whether the appellant has completed one year of service in her Mail Processor position. Although it appears from the record that the appellant has not been at work since September 3, 1997, that is not entirely clear; however, it is apparent that the agency did not formally separate her from her Mail Processor position until July 9, 1999, more than one year after her August 2, 1997 entry into that position.

Under 5 CFR § 752.402(b), "[c]urrent continuous service means a period of employment or service immediately preceding an adverse action in the same or similar positions without a break in Federal civilian employment of a workday." The regulation does not address the circumstances here, i.e., an employee who has not been separated from Federal service but is absent from the workplace. In a somewhat similar set of appeals, a Postal Service employee, who was appointed to her Mail Processor position effective October 16, 1993, and suspended effective July 12, 1994, filed appeals of a constructive suspension and a subsequent removal. The Board, after joining the appeals, sustained the administrative judge's finding in the constructive suspension appeal that it had jurisdiction over the suspension beginning October 15, 1994, one year from the date of her entry into the Mail Processor position. Jackson v. U.S. Postal Service, 73 MSPR 512, 514 n.2 (1997), rev'd on other grounds, 79 MSPR 46 (1998). The Board also took jurisdiction over the removal action. The facts that the appellant in Jackson was not present at work or in a pay status did not affect her completion of the 1-year of current, continuous service requirement for Board jurisdiction to attach.

We note that Postal Service employees are not in the competitive service and consequently are not subject to the probationary period imposed by 5 CFR § 315.801, which applies only to competitive service employees. 5 CFR § 210.101(b). Individuals appointed to positions in the competitive service generally must serve a 1-year probation. 5 CFR § 2.4. Time in a nonpay status while on the rolls, unless absent because of a compensable injury or military duty, is creditable toward the 1-year probation up to a total of 22 workdays. Nonpay time in excess of 22 workdays extends the probationary period by an equal amount. 5 CFR § 315.802(c). This regulation applies only to the competitive service. 5 CFR § 210.101(b). The Office of Personnel Management has not similarly so limited the creditability of time in a nonpay status for excepted service employees, even assuming that such a regulation would apply to the Postal Service. Nor has the Postal Service indicated that its Employee and Labor Relations Manual contains a provision limiting the creditability of time in a nonpay status. We find that the appellant completed her one year of current, continuous service on August 2, 1998, while she was still employed by the agency before it removed her effective July 9, 1999.

See Spidel v. USDA, 113 MSPR 67, 71 n.2, 2010 MSPB 7 (2010) (time in a nonpay status while on the employee rolls is counted in calculating current continuous service under 5 USC 7511(a)(1)(C)(ii)).
