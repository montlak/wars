8. Other Deductions

Morones v. DOJ, 49 MSPR 212, 215 (1991) (following remand from 42 MSPR 642 (1989)), determined that agencies may deduct from back pay amounts garnished for certain child support orders:

The United States elected to waive its sovereign immunity under the provisions of 42 USC § 659 to permit states to garnish monies due and owing to employees of the Federal government based upon remuneration due and owing to those individuals. The term "remuneration" is further defined at 42 USC § 662(f)(1) as "compensation paid or payable for personal services of such individual, whether such compensation is denominated as wages, salary, commission, bonus, pay, or otherwise, and includes but is not limited to, severance pay, sick pay, and incentive pay...." Payment of garnishment is predicated upon receipt by the duly authorized agent of the government of appropriate legal process. 42 USC § 659(b).[3] Part 581 of title 5 of the Code of Federal Regulations, entitled "Processing Garnishment Orders for Child Support and/or Alimony," implements the objectives of 42 USC § 659.

[3] There is no dispute in this case that the legal process of garnishment was "regular on its face" and that the garnishment order was issued by a court of competent jurisdiction. See United States v. Morton, 467 U.S. 822, 836, 104 S. Ct. 2769, 2777, 81 L. Ed.2d 680 (1984) (the government cannot be held liable for honoring a properly-issued writ of garnishment).

The appellant contends that it was improper for the agency to deduct a portion of his back-pay award to satisfy the court-issued garnishment order because the applicable statutes and regulations do not specifically provide for the garnishment of back-pay awards. Nowhere in the relevant statutes or regulations are back-pay awards specified as excluded or exempt from garnishment, however. Further, those authorities provide for the garnishment of monies due and owing by the government to Federal employees, and such "monies" include, but are not limited to, compensation paid to those employees in the form of salaries, wages, and awards. See 42 USC § 662(f); 5 CFR §§ 581.101, 581.102(i), and 581.103. Because an award of back pay merely constitutes one type of compensation for an employee's personal services, we can discern no reason to exclude such awards from monies that are subject to garnishment. Finally, the mere fact that a back-pay award reflects compensation that accumulated over a period of time does not exempt it from the garnishment process.

The agency should deduct from back pay annuity payments made during a period of separation and any retirement incentive received, as well as appropriate deductions for retirement, Medicare, federal and state taxes, and payment the appellant received for accrued annual leave. Collins v. USPS, 64 MSPR 684, 691 (1994); see Lary v. OPM, 68 MSPR 206, 213-16 (1995) (explaining the nature of deductions for Old Age, Survivors, and Disability Insurance (OASDI) under the Social Security system). For discussion of the ability of the Postal Service to deduct all or portions of debts from employees from their back pay in accordance with the agency Employee and Labor Relations Manual, refer to Zuniga v. USPS, 56 MSPR 572, 575-76 (1993) (shortages from "vending machine accountability").

If an agency improperly calculates back pay and properly determines that the appellant was overpaid, the Board will not prevent the agency from recouping the overpayment from other portions of back pay yet due to the employee. See Johnson v. USPS, 71 MSPR 303, 308 (1996) (allowing the agency to recover excess payment for overtime back pay by deducting that amount from the appellant's interest award or "through other appropriate means"); Welber v. USPS, 70 MSPR 231, 233 n.1 (1996) ("The agency is entitled to take into account any overpayment that there may be in responding to the Board's order to show that it has paid the appellant the interest due on the back pay awarded by the Board."); Gay v. USPS, 49 MSPR 219, 228 (1991) (agency may deduct overpayment of night differential from additional Sunday premium pay it owed to the appellant). But see Welber v. USPS, 79 MSPR 139, 143 (1998) (agency was bound to a grievance settlement entered into with the appellant, setting a limit on the amount to be withheld from back pay to cover overpayment); Russell v. DOJ, 74 MSPR 289, 293 (1997) (if an agency improperly relocates an employee, the agency can be required to waive collection of the employee's relocation expenses).
