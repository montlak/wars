broida mspb Chapter 06: Adverse Actions Chapter 06: Adverse Actions, Subchapter 02: Service Efficiency, Section C: Adverse Action Related to Disability, Subsection 05: Effect of Removal for Inability to Work on OPM Disability Annuity Determination, Part a: Rebuttable Presumption of Annuity Entitlement
a. Rebuttable Presumption of Annuity Entitlement

The Federal Circuit did not receive simultaneous appeals of a termination for physical inability to perform work and the denial of a disability annuity. The court did, however, address the "tension" between a termination for physical inability to perform work and an appeal from an OPM denial to the same appellant of a disability retirement annuity in Bruner v. OPM, 996 F.2d 290, 291-94 (Fed. Cir. 1993). Instead of estopping the government from challenging the disability in the OPM proceeding, the court rearranged the burden of proof so that OPM would be required to overcome a presumption of disability arising from the removal action. The bottom line under Bruner is that "the government's action in separating an employee for disablement produces a presumption of disability that serves to shift to the government the burden of production. The government must come forward with enough evidence that a reasonable fact finder could conclude that the applicant did not qualify under 5 CFR 831.502(b)." Judge Newman, writing for the court, explained:

This court is without authority to review the substantive merits of disability determinations, or the factual underpinnings of such determinations. Lindahl v. OPM, 470 U.S. 768, 791.... However, it is our obligation to assure that the correct legal and procedural standards are applied. Id.

Mr. Bruner argues that as a matter of law the agency's determination that he is physically unable to perform the duties of his job qualifies him for disability benefits. In Lindahl this court recognized, but did not reach, the question of the relationship between an employee's termination for physical inability to do the job, and entitlement to a disability annuity:

[W]e are disturbed by the evident tension between (1) a removal for physical inability to do the job and (2) a subsequent denial to the same employee of disability retirement.

We expressly leave open, for a case in which the issue is properly before us, the resolution of problems inherent in the relationship between the two actions.

Lindahl, 776 F.2d at 280. The question was not answered in that case because the employee had appealed only the disability determination and not the termination of employment, and the court believed that it was unable to review both of these actions for possible conflict between them. We deem it unlikely that such a case of conflicting appeals will ever arise for judicial resolution, because of the fatally inconsistent positions and proofs that would be required of the petitioner. However, the "disturbing" question continues to arise, as in the case at bar.

Despite the facial inconsistency between the decision of the Veterans Administration that Mr. Bruner is too disabled to work, and the decision of the Office of Personnel Management that he is not entitled to a disability annuity, it may be that these rulings are not inconsistent in his case, when the correct standards are applied to each determination. See Piccone v. United States, 407 F.2d 866, 872, 186 Ct. Cl. 752 (1969) ("There is therefore the possibility that a person can be too disabled to continue work, but not disabled enough to qualify for disability retirement.")[.] Thus we decline to hold, as a matter of law, that the agency's action in separating Mr. Bruner on grounds of disability establishes his entitlement to a disability annuity. To this extent, Mr. Bruner's argument must be rejected.

However, the agency's action in separating Mr. Bruner from employment, based on its decision that Mr. Bruner is too disabled to work, is not irrelevant to the ensuing proceedings.

The Board placed upon Mr. Bruner the burden of proving that he is disabled. Mr. Bruner asserts that the burden of proof should have been upon the government, because a government agency had terminated his employment because of disability.

This court's review of disability-based actions is limited to determining whether "there has been a substantial departure from important procedural rights, a misconstruction of the governing legislation, or some like error 'going to the heart of the administrative determination.'" Lindahl, 470 U.S. at 791, 105 S. Ct. at 1633, (quoting Scroggins v. United States, 397 F.2d 295, 297, 184 Ct. Cl. 530, cert. denied, 393 U.S. 952, 89 S. Ct. 376, 21 L. Ed.2d 363 (1968)). Because the proper allocation of the burden of proof is an important procedural right that may have substantive consequences, we have considered the Board's assignment of the procedural burdens, in implementation of our review responsibility.

The burden in most disability compensation programs follows the general rules of evidence. As applied to disability retirement, the general rule is that the burden of proving entitlement is upon the person who asserts that he/she is disabled.... The placement of the burden of proof reflects social policy more than law, and tends to remain fixed despite procedural adjustments along the way....

We are not aware of, and OPM has not pointed to, any statutory or regulatory allocation of the burdens of proof or of production with respect to disability determinations made pursuant to the Civil Service Retirement Act. 5 CFR 831.502(b) states seven criteria that must be met for disability retirement, as follows:

No claim for disability retirement shall be allowed unless OPM determines that the claim should be granted based upon documentation provided by the applicant or the agency which demonstrates the following:

(1) A deficiency in service with respect to performance, conduct or attendance, or in the absence of any actual service deficiency, a showing that the medical condition is incompatible with either useful service or retention in the position;

(2) A medical condition which is defined as a disease or injury;

(3) A relationship between the service deficiency and the medical condition such that the medical condition has caused the service deficiency;

(4) The duration of the medical condition, past and expected, and a showing that the condition, in all probability, will continue for at least a year;

(5) The applicant became disabled while serving under the Civil Service Retirement System;

(6) The agency's inability to make reasonable accommodation to the employee's medical condition; and

(7) The absence of another available position within the employing agency and commuting area, at the same grade or pay level and tenure, for which the employee is qualified for reassignment. For this part, placement in the agency is limited to those facilities in the commuting area that are serviced by the same appointing authority.

Viewing these criteria, it is apparent that some involve evidence within the knowledge and control of the agency, and some within the purview of the employee. All are fact-dependent and subject to dispute.

In Lindahl this court held that the burden of proof, for purposes of eligibility for disability retirement, lies with the applicant, applying the general principle that an applicant for a benefit bears the burden of establishing eligibility for the benefit. The court in Lindahl did not discuss the allocation of the burden of production as to the seven criteria set forth in 5 CFR 831.502(b), as distinguished from the burden of proof. The burden of production, also called the burden of going forward, is initially upon the person with the burden of proof, and generally requires the production of sufficient evidence to support a finding in favor of that person.... The burden of production then shifts to the other party, who must, in turn, produce enough evidence to raise a question of material fact.... The Board in Chavez v. Office of Personnel Management, 6 MSPB 343, 6 MSPR 404 (1981), cited with approval in Lindahl, 776 F.2d at 278-79, setting the guidelines for disability appeals, assumed that the party with the relevant evidence will come forward to produce it:

Since it is OPM's decision that we review in such cases, OPM of course remains obliged to come forward with evidence and an explanation demonstrating the basis on which it reached its determination. Indeed, as to some material issues OPM or the employing agency may be in a better position than the applicant to adduce at least some of the relevant evidence, e.g., total creditable service or availability of other positions of the same grade or class in which the applicant might perform useful and efficient service. We hold only that the ultimate burden of persuasion is on the applicant "in the sense of answering the question of who wins if all the evidence is inadequate or unconvincing."

Chavez, 6 MSPB at 352-53, 6 MSPR at 416 (quoting 3 Kenneth Culp Davis, Administrative Law Treatise 16.9 at 258 (2d ed. 1980)) (footnotes omitted).

However, this allocation of the burdens of production and persuasion does not take account of the agency's decision and action taken in cases such as this one, wherein the agency has already separated the claimant from service because of disability, the agency having found that the claimant was physically unable to do the assigned job and that there was no other position that the claimant was able to do. Such agency action is presumptively correct, and is not here subject to collateral attack. Thus the Veterans Administration's finding and action based thereon, that Mr. Bruner was physically unable to work and that there was no job within the agency that he was qualified for and physically able to do, produces an evidentiary presumption which serves to shift the burden of coming forward to the government. That is, the applicant is deemed to have met his burden of proof prima facie. The burden of production then shifts to the government, to come forward with evidence sufficient to support a finding that the applicant is not disabled in terms of 5 CFR 831.502(b). See Fed. R. Evid. 301. Cf. Meneses v. Secretary of Health, Education and Welfare, 442 F.2d 803, 806 (D.C. Cir. 1971) (when the Social Security claimant shows physical impairment disabling him from his former employment, the government has the burden of coming forward with evidence that there are jobs the claimant can do).

Although the applicant is not absolved of coming forward with evidence and argument in rebuttal of any evidence of absence of entitlement adduced by the agency, the agency must first meet its burden of producing sufficient evidence to support a finding that the requirements of 5 CFR 831.502(b) have not been met. Cf. Kerner v. Flemming, 283 F.2d 916, 922 (2d Cir. 1960) ("[A]n applicant for a [Social Security] disability pension has the ultimate burden of persuasion.... However, it does not follow that the court is bound to sustain a denial of disability benefits where the applicant has raised a serious question and the evidence affords no sufficient basis for the Secretary's negative answer."). We do not hold that the ultimate burden of proof is shifted when the agency has separated the employee based on the agency's determination of disability, for we do not think that the presumption of disability based on agency action warrants this force. See Fed. R. Evid. 301 advisory committee's note.

In sum, the government's action in separating an employee for disablement produces a presumption of disability that serves to shift to the government the burden of production. The government must come forward with enough evidence that a reasonable fact finder could conclude that the applicant did not qualify under 5 CFR 831.502(b). Thus the initial presumption in favor of the claimant will control the result only in cases where the government does not introduce evidence sufficient to support a finding in its favor; in such cases, an applicant who has been dismissed for physical disqualification and absence of an alternative position will generally prevail. However, if the government's evidence is sufficient to support a finding that the applicant is not entitled to disability retirement in terms of 5 CFR 831.502(b), the totality of the evidence produced by both sides is weighed by the trier of fact, who may (but need not) infer disability from the facts giving rise to the presumption. Notes of Conference Committee, H.R. Rep. 1597, 93d. Cong., 2d Sess. (1974), reprinted in 1974 USCCAN 7098, 7099. The applicant, who retains the burden of persuasion, will then prevail only if he/she establishes entitlement by a preponderance of the evidence.

The Board held that Mr. Bruner had not introduced sufficient evidence to prove that he was disabled, thus basing its findings on the weakness of his case. Consequently, the Board did not consider the prima facie case of disability that is presumed from the agency's removal action, and did not discuss whether the government's evidence was adequate to support a finding in its favor. This was a procedural error that, as we have discussed, went "to the heart of the administrative determination," Lindahl, 470 U.S. at 791, 105 S. Ct. at 1633, for it is possible that this error affected the result....

The Board's decision is vacated, and the case is remanded for redetermination upon application of the correct procedural burdens.

The Board accepted and applied Bruner in O'Shea v. OPM, 62 MSPR 352, 354-56 (1994):

The regulations governing disability retirement require proof that an applicant has a medical condition expected to last at least a year that has either caused a deficiency in service with respect to performance, conduct, or attendance or is incompatible with useful service or retention in the position. See 5 CFR § 831.502(b)(1)-(6). The applicant must also show that the agency is unable to make reasonable accommodation to the employee's medical condition and that there exists no other available position within the employing agency and commuting area at the same grade or pay level and tenure for which the employee is qualified for reassignment. Id. at (6) and (7).

The ultimate burden of proof for purposes of establishing eligibility for disability retirement lies, as the administrative judge found, with the applicant. See Chavez v. Office of Personnel Management, 6 MSPB 343, 6 MSPR 404, 414-17 (1981). Three months before the issuance of the initial decision in this appeal, however, the Federal Circuit revisited the issue of the burden of proof in disability retirement cases where the applicant has been removed for physical inability to perform the duties of the position. Bruner v. Office of Personnel Management, 996 F.2d 290 (Fed. Cir. 1993). In Bruner, the court held that an employee's removal for physical inability to perform is prima facie evidence of disability for the purposes of the same employee's application for disability retirement. Id. at 294. That is, the agency's removal of the employee for disability establishes a rebuttable presumption that the employee was physically unable to work and that there was no job at the same grade or pay within the agency for which he was qualified and which he was physically able to do. Id. at 293-94. The burden of production then shifts to the agency to produce evidence that the applicant is not disabled under 5 CFR § 831.502(b). Id. at 294. If the agency produces such evidence, the applicant must then come forward with evidence to rebut the agency's assertion that he is not entitled to disability retirement. Id.

Here, OPM and the administrative judge based the decision to deny disability retirement benefits to the appellant on what they deemed to be the appellant's failure to offer sufficient objective medical documentation linking her symptoms and absence from work with a job-related disabling medical condition. In arriving at this decision, the administrative judge gave significant weight to the appellant's supervisor's failure to state that the appellant was medically unfit for duty and could not be accommodated or reassigned. It is true that, in completing the required statement relating to the appellant's application for disability retirement, the supervisor noted only that "[e]mployee has been on continuous LWOP since 7/18/89" as the explanation of why the appellant was unable to perform her duties. On the same form, however, the supervisor also noted that the appellant's conduct was not unsatisfactory and that her attendance stopped for apparent medical reasons. In addition, in its proposed removal notice the agency stated that the appellant was unable to return to her position "because of the continuing effects of [her] job-related disability," with her return to duty "not anticipated in the near future." Furthermore, the agency had granted the appellant leave without pay for more than two years and had cooperated with her successful application for OWCP benefits.

We find, therefore, that the agency had determined that the appellant was medically unfit for duty at the time of her separation and that the presumption established in Bruner should have been applied in this case. That is, the agency's removal of the appellant "because of the continuing effects of [her] job-related disability," produced a "presumption of disability that serves to shift to the government the burden of production." Bruner, 996 F.2d at 294. In this case, the government failed to "come forward with enough evidence that a reasonable fact finder could conclude that the applicant [for disability retirement benefits] did not qualify under 5 CFR § 831.502(b)." Id.

Not every individual who has been removed from service because of a medical condition is "disabled" and entitled to disability retirement benefits. The Bruner decision does not establish a non-rebuttable presumption. OPM can rightfully deny benefits if it can present proof that the appellant has insufficient objective medical evidence of disability under 5 CFR § 831.502(b). In this case, based on the record before us, we conclude that the government has failed in establishing that the appellant had insufficient medical evidence of disability. Accordingly, we conclude that the appellant has established her entitlement to disability retirement benefits.

Bruner was again applied, OPM's burden of proof again explained, Klein v. OPM, 71 MSPR 366, 370-72 (1996):

In a disability retirement case, an appellant's separation for disability establishes a prima facie case of disability and shifts the burden to OPM to produce enough evidence that a reasonable fact finder could conclude that he did not qualify for disability retirement. If OPM meets that burden, the Board must then consider the totality of the evidence in deciding the disability issue; the appellant retains the burden of persuasion at all times to establish his entitlement to disability retirement. See Trevan v. Office of Personnel Management, 69 F.3d 520, 526-27 (Fed. Cir. 1995); Bruner v. Office of Personnel Management, 996 F.2d 290, 294 (Fed. Cir. 1993). In stating that the appellant's removal was warranted based on his psychological inability to work for the Postal Service, then, USPS arguably raised a presumption of his disability, and OPM thus had to meet its burden of production to rebut that presumption. For the reasons set forth below, however, it appears that OPM met that burden and that the totality of the evidence does not establish the appellant's entitlement to disability retirement benefits.

...

OPM can meet its burden of production to rebut a presumption of disability by demonstrating a lack of objective medical evidence providing a reasoned explanation of how certain aspects of a particular condition render the employee unable to perform specific work requirements. See Trevan, 69 F.3d at 526-27; O'Shea v. Office of Personnel Management, 62 MSPR 352, 355 (1994). In this context, proof of stress, anxiety, and discomfort at work is not enough by itself to establish disability; the evidence must also demonstrate that these factors are so severe as to prevent an employee from performing the specific duties of the position in question. See Clarke v. Office of Personnel Management, 57 MSPR 30, 35-37 (1993), dismissed, 22 F.3d 1105 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (Table); Faragon v. Office of Personnel Management, 51 MSPR 63, 71 (1991); Sedor v. Office of Personnel Management, 48 MSPR 42, 47, aff'd, 950 F.2d 730 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (Table). That evidence must also establish the degree to which the appellant's condition can or cannot be controlled. See Clarke, 57 MSPR at 36-37; Faragon, 51 MSPR at 70-71.

The evidence discussed above shows that the appellant has a mental condition apparently brought on by a work environment that is unpleasant to him, but it does not thoroughly make clear the severity of that condition, nor does it establish the effect of that condition on his ability to satisfy specific work requirements. Similarly, it does not explain why Dr. Brachman has not used alternative means of treatment (i.e., antidepressant medication) in order to control the condition. Under the circumstances, then, the record shows that the appellant has a mental condition, but it lacks a reasoned explanation of how this condition is disabling. Proof of the appellant's stress, depression, and anxiety alone thus is not enough here to establish disability. See Clarke, 57 MSPR at 37; Faragon, 51 MSPR at 71. OPM met its burden of production by demonstrating this deficiency in some detail, with reference to a voluminous record, in its final decision. Trevan, 69 F.3d at 526-27. Because the totality of the evidence discussed above does not establish that the appellant is disabled, the administrative judge therefore did not err by affirming OPM's final decision. See Trevan, 69 F.3d at 527.

Marczewski v. OPM, 80 MSPR 343, 347-49 ¶¶ 6-10 (1998), also considered the relation of a removal based on the physical inability of the appellant to perform assigned duties, the inability of the agency to accommodate the appellant through reassignment, and an appeal from an OPM denial of a disability retirement application:

OPM may meet its burden of production under Bruner by submitting any information available to it, including medical and employment records submitted in the first instance by the applicant or the employing agency, as well as medical examinations and other information OPM directs the employee to provide. See Trevan v. Office of Personnel Management, 69 F.3d 520, 527 n.7 (Fed. Cir. 1995). OPM will meet its burden of production if these submissions demonstrate "a lack of objective medical evidence provid[ing] a reasoned explanation of how certain aspects of a particular condition render the employee unable to perform specific work requirements." See Trevan, 69 F.3d at 525.

In its finding that the appellant is not entitled to disability retirement, OPM reasoned that the appellant's medical diagnoses are merely situational, resulting from the interpersonal relationship with his supervisor. The Board has found that job-related stress resulting in physical and mental ailments that prevent an employee from performing the duties necessary in his job can warrant the granting of disability retirement. See Pugh v. Office of Personnel Management, 38 MSPR 184, 187-90 (1988); Bridges v. Office of Personnel Management, 21 MSPR 716, 718-19, recons. denied, 24 MSPR 112 (1984). Here, the record shows that it was not stress or anxiety alone that rendered the appellant incapacitated for purposes of work, but rather, that the work-related stress and anxiety that he suffered caused his mental condition which was, itself, incapacitating. See Pugh, 38 MSPR at 189-89. The appellant's treating psychiatrist, Tom J. Trannel, M.D., stated that, although, after the agency removed him, the appellant tried to work at a job sorting parts, the job precipitated nightmares, headaches, anxiety, and insomnia, and caused him to return to his "baseline level of being quite anxious throughout the day and socially withdrawn." Thus, OPM's evidence is insufficient to rebut the presumption that the appellant is disabled for the position of Supervisory Police Officer.

OPM also reasoned that the appellant is not entitled to disability retirement because, before the employing agency effected the appellant's removal, it attempted to accommodate him, offering him the position of Physical Security Specialist. OPM found that this position constituted an accommodation under the law of disability retirement because it was not under the supervision of the agency official whom the appellant perceived as mistreating him. As noted above, however, the appellant's disease impairs him in a broader scope than when he is at work under the supervisor who allegedly precipitated his psychiatric disease. Thus, to meet its burden to produce evidence to show that the appellant is not entitled to disability retirement, benefits, OPM must also produce evidence to show that the appellant can render useful and efficient service in that position with his psychiatric disorders. See 5 USC § 8337(a); 5 CFR § 831.1203(a)(2),(4); Eshelman v. Office of Personnel Management, 72 MSPR 173, 175 (1996); Gometz v. Office of Personnel Management, 69 MSPR 115, 118-19 (1995). OPM, however, did not submit a copy of the position description for the Physical Security Specialist into the record or otherwise show that the appellant could perform the duties of that position. Thus, OPM's evidence is also insufficient to show that the appellant is not entitled to disability retirement benefits because he could be accommodated by performing the duties of the position of Physical Security Specialist. Id.

In any event, even assuming that OPM has met its burden to produce evidence under Bruner, the appellant has nonetheless met his burden to come forward with evidence to rebut OPM's assertion that he is not entitled to the benefits that he seeks. As noted above, Dr. Trannel reported that the appellant could not work at any full-time position. If the appellant could not work at a full-time position, then he could not be reassigned to one. See Moran v. Office of Personnel Management, 72 MSPR 138, 144 (1996). We find therefore that the appellant has submitted evidence to rebut the presumption that he could perform full-time at the GS-9 level.

Accordingly, we find that the appellant has met his burden of proving that the agency was unable to accommodate him in his current position or through reassignment. See Rozar v. Office of Personnel Management, 61 MSPR 136, 143-44 (1994); Thomas v. Office of Personnel Management, 54 MSPR 686, 690-91 (1992); Biscaha v. Office of Personnel Management, 51 MSPR 304, 312 (1991). He has therefore established his entitlement to a disability retirement annuity by preponderant evidence.

Booth v. OPM, 99 MSPR 13, 16-18 ¶¶ 5-9 (2005), applied Bruner in a FERS appeal from the denial of disability annuity. The Board granted a PFR when, during the pendency of the PFR, the agency removed the appellant for physical inability to perform his job. The appeal returned to the AJ for reevaluation:

In an appeal from OPM's denial of a voluntary disability retirement application, an employee bears the burden of persuasion by preponderant evidence. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.56(a)(2) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b)(2)] ; Redmond v. Office of Personnel Management, 90 M.S.P.R. 4 ¶ 9 (2001); Chavez v. Office of Personnel Management, 6 M.S.P.R. 404, 417 (1981). To qualify for a disability retirement annuity under FERS, an employee must establish that: (1) He has completed 18 months of creditable civilian service; (2) while he was employed in a FERS position, he became disabled due to a medical condition, which has resulted in a deficiency in performance, conduct, or attendance, or if there is no such deficiency, the disabling medical condition is incompatible with either useful and efficient service or retention in the position; (3) the disabling medical condition is expected to continue for at least 1 year from the date the application for disability retirement is filed; (4) accommodation of the disabling medical condition in the position held is unreasonable; and (5) the employee has not declined a reasonable offer of reassignment to a vacant position. 5 C.F.R. § 844.103(a); Redmond, 90 M.S.P.R. 4 ¶ 9; see 5 U.S.C. § 8451. "Useful and efficient service means acceptable performance of the critical or essential elements of the position; and satisfactory conduct and attendance." 5 C.F.R. § 844.102.

An employee's removal for physical inability to perform the essential functions of his position constitutes prima facie evidence that he is entitled to disability retirement. Bruner v. Office of Personnel Management, 996 F.2d 290, 294 (Fed. Cir. 1993). Based upon this presumption, the burden then shifts to OPM to produce enough evidence from which a reasonable fact finder could conclude that the appellant did not qualify for disability retirement. Trevan v. Office of Personnel Management, 69 F.3d 520, 526 (Fed. Cir. 1995); Klein v. Office of Personnel Management, 71 M.S.P.R. 366, 370 (1996). OPM can meet its burden of production to rebut a presumption of disability "by demonstrating a lack of objective medical evidence providing a reasoned explanation of how certain aspects of a particular condition render the employee unable to perform specific work requirements." Trevan, 69 F.3d at 526-27 (citations omitted).

If OPM meets its burden of production, the appellant must come forward with evidence to rebut OPM's assertion that he is not entitled to benefits. Rivera v. Office of Personnel Management, 96 M.S.P.R. 665 ¶ 6 (2004). In spite of the shifting burdens of production, the appellant retains the burden of persuasion at all times to establish his entitlement to disability retirement. Trevan, 69 F.3d at 527; Klein, 71 M.S.P.R. at 370. The Board then considers the totality of the evidence in deciding the disability issue, including objective clinical findings, diagnoses and medical opinions, subjective evidence of pain and disability, evidence relating to the effect of the applicant's condition on his ability to perform in the grade or class of position he last occupied, and evidence that the applicant was not qualified for reassignment to a vacant position at the same grade or level as the position he last occupied. Dunn v. Office of Personnel Management, 60 M.S.P.R. 426, 432 (1994), dismissed, 91 F.3d 169 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table).

In its notice of proposed removal to the appellant, the agency wrote that it "propose[d] to remove [him] from [his] position of Lock & Dam Operator, WY-5426-08, at Robert C. Byrd Locks & Dam, due to [his] physical inability to perform the essential functions of [his] position and [his] inability to return to work from an extended period of Leave Without Pay (LWOP) and thus perform the duties of [his] position." In support of the proposed removal, the agency referenced a fitness-for-duty examination that indicated the appellant was unable to perform the physical requirements of his position, concluded that the appellant's functional abilities fell well below the guidelines required for his position, and determined that his personal safety and the safety of his co-workers would be at risk if he returned to work and performed the duties of his position. The proposed removal notice further stated that the agency had no position available at the same grade and pay with duties the appellant could perform with his physical and medical condition. Subsequently, the agency removed the appellant "due to [his] physical inability to perform the essential functions of [his] position and [his] inability to return to work" and "to promote the efficiency of the Federal service."

It appears that the appellant is entitled to the Bruner presumption. See Bruner, 996 F.2d at 294. However, because the appellant's removal occurred after the initial decision was issued, the appellant was never given the benefit of that presumption. The Board has found it appropriate to remand a case where the administrative judge placed the burden of proving entitlement to disability retirement on the appellant without first determining whether he was removed for physical inability to perform the essential functions of his position. Kardatzke v. Office of Personnel Management, 92 M.S.P.R. 74 ¶¶ 10-11(2002); Jones v. Office of Personnel Management, 75 M.S.P.R. 499, 502 (1997); see also Tarlaian v. Office of Personnel Management, 77 M.S.P.R. 247, 251, aff'd, 168 F.3d 1320 (Fed. Cir. 1998) (Table) (the administrative judge erred by failing to consider evidence of the appellant's removal for medical inability to perform in reaching his decision and by failing to analyze the evidence submitted consistent with Bruner and Jones). In such cases on remand, the administrative judge should determine the basis for the appellant's removal before establishing the parties' respective burdens of proof, and should then analyze the evidence accordingly. Kardatzke, 92 M.S.P.R. 74 ¶ 11; Jones, 75 M.S.P.R. at 502; Taralaian, 77 M.S.P.R. at 251.

If an agency removes an employee after an initial decision is issued denying a disability retirement, the Board may, upon receipt of information suggesting that the removal was based on medical inability to perform the job, remand the case to the AJ to consider the basis for the appellant's removal and to consider whether the Bruner presumption is applicable. See Kardatzke v. OPM, 92 MSPR 74, 79-80 ¶¶ 10-11 (2002).

For purposes of the Bruner analysis, an appellant's removal for extended LWOP resulting from the appellant's medical condition is equivalent to removal for inability to perform for medical reasons. Ayers-Kavtaradze v. OPM, 91 MSPR 397, 401 ¶ 10 (2002). Expanding on this concept, under McCurdy v. OPM, 96 MSPR 90, 93-94 ¶¶ 6-7 (2004):

An employee's removal for physical inability to perform the essential functions of her position constitutes prima facie evidence that she is entitled to disability retirement. Bruner v. Office of Personnel Management, 996 F.2d 290, 294 (Fed. Cir. 1993). Based on this presumption, the burden then shifts to OPM to produce evidence sufficient to support a finding that the applicant is not entitled to disability retirement. Id. If OPM meets this burden of production, the Board will consider the totality of evidence on both sides, and the applicant retains the burden of proving her entitlement by preponderant evidence. Id.

The Board has held that removal for extended absences is equivalent to removal for physical inability to perform where it is accompanied by specifications indicating that the decision to remove was based on medical documentation suggesting that the appellant was disabled and unable to perform her duties. Ayers-Kavtaradze v. Office of Personnel Management, 91 M.S.P.R. 397 ¶ 11 (2002). Such is the case here. In the notice of proposed removal, the proposing official wrote as follows:

I recognize that your absence is based upon a continuing medical condition that is beyond your control. You have documented that absence through medical statements from your physicians which have supported the fact that you have been unable to report for duty. You have also applied for disability retirement, further documenting the health conditions which have prevented you from working. Based upon the medical documentation you submitted, your supervisor approved leave without pay (LWOP) for your absence from November 27, 2001 through August 23, 2002. Your physicians' statements validate that there is no foreseeable end to your absence from work.

The Senior Auditor/Team Leader therefore, inter alia: "Develops audit proposals" and "survey/audit guides"; conducts the necessary audit work for these functions; oversees the audit work of his assigned teams; "[c]onducts entrance and exit conferences with audit clients to explain audit objectives and conclusions"; writes audit reports; "[p]rovides clear and concise verbal summaries of the survey/audit results to audit management" and "to audit team members"; and "[m]aintains security and accountability of classified information."

As noted above, Dr. Chaconas's March 11, 1996 letter indicates that the appellant discussed his job duties with him and that the appellant reported that "[w]riting audit reports and meeting with high level military officials to negotiate audit findings was very stressful" to him and that "[t]he constant pressure from various supervisors concerning his work performance and work habits put undue stress on him." Dr. Chaconas's March 11, 1996 letter merely reiterates what the appellant reported. That letter, as well as the other medical evidence of record, lacks a reasoned explanation of how the appellant's alleged chest pains related to his work and how they rendered him disabled for useful and efficient service in his position.

An employee's subjective evidence of pain that is supported by competent medical evidence must be seriously considered in a disability case. Rozar v. Office of Personnel Management, 61 MSPR 136, 141 (1994); Biscaha v. Office of Personnel Management, 51 MSPR 304, 309 (1991). Here, however, the appellant's subjective claim of chest pains, exacerbated by work-related stress, is not supported by competent medical evidence. All of the clinical findings are negative on the subject of the appellant's chest pains. They reveal no abnormality of the appellant's heart and provide no basis for his claimed chest pains. Further, even assuming that the appellant suffers from chest pains, neither the appellant nor the objective medical evidence of record explains how the appellant is stressed to the point of disability by merely performing the duties of his position. See Klein, 71 MSPR at 372 (proof of an appellant's stress, depression, and anxiety alone is insufficient to establish disability); cf. Rozar, 61 MSPR at 141-43 (the appellant proved her entitlement to disability retirement based on various congenital disorders, chronic hypertension and stress by presenting medical evidence to support her claimed conditions, and supplementing the objective clinical evidence with subjective evidence of how her conditions affected her ability to meet the requirements of her position, and with evidence that her employing agency strongly indicated that it lacked confidence in her medical ability to perform her duties).

Further, while the appellant asserts that he experienced some conflict with his supervisors, none of the evidence points to any unusual circumstances under which the appellant was required to work. Moreover, while Dr. Chaconas consistently recommended that the appellant not return to work, he also stated that he had been treating the appellant's chest pains with Zantac, apparently unsuccessfully, but did not indicate whether other treatment, either by medication or other means, was available to treat the appellant's condition and, if so, why he did not provide the appellant with such treatment. See Faragon v. Office of Personnel Management, 51 MSPR 63, 69 (1991).

We therefore find that the totality of the evidence, including evidence of the appellant's removal from his position for medical inability to perform his duties, the appellant's prior absence from his work, the appellant's subjective evidence of pain, and the objective medical evidence, does not establish that the appellant is disabled for useful and efficient service in his position. Because of this finding, we need not consider whether the employing agency could have accommodated the appellant in his position or reassigned him to an existing vacant position.

A settlement agreement may create a Bruner rebuttable presumption. That occurred in Bynum v. OPM, 89 MSPR 1, 5-6 ¶¶ 9-11 (2001), involving a removal for AWOL and other charges resolved by a settlement requiring that the basis for removal be changed to reflect "termination/disability":

OPM charges that the AJ erred in finding that the appellant established a prima facie case of her entitlement to a disability retirement because she considered that the true basis for the appellant's removal was physical inability to perform, when, in fact, that reason was "fictitious, fabricated, and fraudulent." In so arguing, OPM challenges the AJ's application of the Bruner presumption which provides that an employee's removal for disability, i.e., medical inability to perform her job duties, establishes a rebuttable prima facie case of her entitlement to disability retirement, and shifts the burden to OPM to produce sufficient evidence to support a finding that she is not entitled to disability retirement benefits. See Bruner v. Office of Personnel Management, 996 F.2d 290, 293-94 (Fed. Cir. 1993). The Bruner presumption further provides that, if OPM satisfies this burden of production, the totality of the evidence is considered to determine whether the employee satisfied her ultimate burden of establishing, by preponderant evidence, her entitlement to disability retirement. Id. at 294.

Although she did not specifically cite to Bruner, the AJ applied the presumption, based on the reason for the appellant's removal as agreed to by the parties in the document they signed in settlement of the appeal of her removal. The Board has held that it will generally give effect to the terms of a settlement agreement between an applicant for disability retirement and her employing agency in determining the applicant's entitlement to disability retirement. Jordan v. Office of Personnel Management, 77 M.S.P.R. 610, 614-17 (1998), recons. denied, 86 M.S.P.R. 144 (2000). The resolution of an appeal between an appellant and his employing agency based upon a settlement constitutes a final decision under the Board's appellate jurisdiction, and the Board has authority to enforce the settlement agreement once it becomes a part of the Board record. Id. at 615.
