j. Effect of Criminal Sentence

A criminal conviction, and the action of the sentencing court, is one element to be considered in determining a penalty. The Board may attempt to avoid extraordinary disparity between the penalty imposed by a criminal court and that of the federal agency-employer, as Hovanec v. Dept. of Interior, 67 MSPR 340, 342-45 (1995), implied through adoption of the AJ's mitigation analysis:

The appellant timely appealed from the agency's action demoting him from his GM-13 Supervisory Solid Minerals Analyst position to a GS-11 nonsupervisory Solid Minerals Analyst position based on charges that: (1) He "knowingly and unlawfully transported and caused to be transported in interstate commerce a fossil [he] knew to have been stolen or taken by fraud"; and (2) he "excavated or caused the excavation of approximately 300 fossil pieces from public or private lands without receiving valid permission."

Under the specification supporting the first charge, the agency alleged that the appellant pleaded guilty in Federal district court to an indictment charging that he "knowingly and unlawfully transported and caused to be transported in interstate commerce...merchandise having a value of more than $5,000, that is, the fossilized skeleton of a crocodilian reptile...knowing the same to have been stolen or taken by fraud, in violation of 18 USC § 2314 and 13 USC § 2." The agency stated that it, was provided with a copy of the judgment that documented that the appellant had been convicted of the criminal charge, was placed on probation for 2 years, and was ordered to pay the sum of $1,550, which included a $1,500 fine and a special assessment of $50.

...

The administrative judge found that the demotion penalty was unreasonable because: (1) While the deciding official testified that he demoted the appellant because he had lost confidence in the appellant's judgment and his performance as a supervisor, there was nothing in the appellant's performance appraisals since 1987, when his off-duty misconduct occurred, to support the deciding officials conclusions; (2) the agency promoted the appellant to the GM-13 supervisory position well after it became aware of his indictment and, at the time of his promotion, expressed no reservation regarding his judgment; (3) while the deciding official testified that he imposed the demotion to make an example out of the appellant, such a reason did not justify an unreasonable penalty; (4) there was no evidence to show that the appellant had been involved in any criminal activity since 1987, when the off-duty misconduct occurred; (5) the appellant has had "a long history of exceptional service with the agency"; (6) he "is considered an expert in his field and an asset to the agency"; (7) he has a clean prior disciplinary record; (8) he appeared sincere in his testimony that his misconduct would not recur; and (9) the agency's penalty was disproportionate to that imposed by the court, i.e., loss of pay of over $13,500 the first year of the demotion (plus additional future impact) versus $1,550 in a fine and court costs.

See Stuhlmacher v. USPS, 89 MSPR 272, 281 ¶ 24 (2001) ("[t]he fact that the appellant's misconduct was a criminal misdemeanor is but one factor to be considered in determining the maximum reasonable penalty.").

The Board may decline to credit acceptance of an appellant into a state accelerated rehabilitative disposition program as a measure of rehabilitation when acceptance in the program is not the same as a finding of innocence and instead is a component of a pending criminal proceeding. See O'Keefe v. USPS, 88 MSPR 475, 481 ¶ 14 (2001), vacated and remanded, O'Keefe v. USPS, 318 F.3d 1310 (Fed. Cir. 2002) (requiring reanalysis of facts and charges).
