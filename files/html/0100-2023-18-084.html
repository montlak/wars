broida mspb Chapter 11: Harmful Error Chapter 11: Harmful Error, Subchapter 03: Errors in Proposal Notice and Reply Process, Section F: Oral and Written Replies, Subsection 01: Proof of Harm; Failure to Consider a Reply; Due Process, Part a: Investigation of Information Provided by the Employee
a. Investigation of Information Provided by the Employee

A responsible, well-advised deciding official will consider and further inquire into or investigate new and material facts and circumstances provided by the appellant in the reply process--subject to due process protections for material information then developed. The deciding official's responsibility to seriously consider new information from the appellant goes to the central purpose of a meaningful reply opportunity. But the Board says little about the deciding official's responsibility concerning new and material information, e.g., information as to discipline against comparator employees, presented during the reply. One general statement is taken from Huntley v. USPS, CH-0752-13-0602-I-1 (NP 8/25/2014):

The appellant argues that the deciding official based her removal decision on the documentation from the OIG investigation and the response meeting with the appellant. The appellant alleges that the deciding official did not interview any of the witnesses involved in the charge against him. The appellant cites to no law, rule, or regulation that requires the deciding official to independently interview the witnesses and not rely on the agency's OIG, which is charged with conducting investigations such as the one involved in the present appeal. An agency is obligated to make reasonable inquiries into exonerating facts brought to its attention by an appellant before removing him. Uske v. U.S. Postal Service, 60 M.S.P.R. 544, 550 (1994), aff'd, 56 F.3d 1375 (Fed. Cir. 1995). The agency's OIG did investigate exonerating facts such as interviewing the woman who was alleged to be the intended recipient of the text messages. In addition, the appellant's written reply to the deciding official does not appear to contain any new facts that she needed to investigate prior to making her decision. We find that the deciding official was not required to conduct her own investigation and interviews over and above those conducted as part of the OIG investigation because the appellant did not provide any additional potentially exonerating facts that had not been investigated.

Uske v. USPS, 60 MSPR 544, 550 (1994), cited in Huntley, provided but general statements about the agency's duty to further develop facts raised during a reply:

Moreover, the administrative judge erred to the extent that he suggests that the agency had a duty to investigate the appellant's misconduct. An agency is obligated to make reasonable inquiries into exonerating facts brought to its attention by an appellant before removing him.[23] Here, however, the appellant made no reply to his proposed removal, and thus presented no exonerating evidence triggering a duty on the part of the agency to inquire. The appellant did not allege or show below that the agency had any affirmative duty to investigate or that he was harmed by a failure to more fully investigate his conduct.[24]

[23] See Cooper v. Department of the Navy, 26 M.S.P.R. 108, 111 (1985).

[24] Stephen, 47 M.S.P.R. at 681, 685; e.g., Lambert v. Department of the Army, 44 M.S.P.R. 688, 695 (1990), aff'd, 928 F.2d 410 (Fed. Cir.) (Table), cert. denied, 112 S. Ct. 180 (1991); Hatch v. Department of the Air Force, 40 M.S.P.R. 260, 264 (1989); Benton v. Department of Labor, 25 M.S.P.R. 430, 436 (1984); Schaules v. U.S. Postal Service, 19 M.S.P.R. 390, 392-93 (1984).

A more recent decision reflects a more substantive obligation on the part of the agency. See Webster v. Dept. of Energy, DC-0752-13-0280-I-2 (NP 7/6/2015) ("An agency is obligated to make reasonable inquiries into exonerating facts brought to its attention by an appellant before removing her. Uske v. U.S. Postal Service, 60 M.S.P.R. 544, 550 (1994), aff'd, 56 F.3d 1375 (Fed. Cir. 1995). In this case, however, the appellant chose not to respond to the notice of proposed removal. If she believed that there were exonerating circumstances for her misconduct, then it was incumbent on her to so inform the deciding official. Having failed to do so, she cannot fault the agency for not considering her purported justification for her misconduct as a mitigating factor in its penalty determination."). But see Trent v. USPS, DC-0752-20-0679-I-2 (NP 4/20/2023) ("[A]ppellant asserts that the agency violated his due process rights when the...deciding official allegedly failed to investigate the appellant's allegation that he was framed....Regarding his inadequate investigation claim, we find that the appellant has not identified any statute, regulation, or agency procedure that would require the agency to investigate his allegations, and he has failed to meet his burden to show that the agency likely would have reached a different conclusion if some additional procedure was followed. See Stephen v. Department of the Air Force, 47 M.S.P.R. 672, 681 (1991).").

Cooper v. Dept. of Navy, 26 MSPR 108, 111 (1985), noted in Uske, involved a counsel fee application based in part on the argument that the agency knew or should have known that it would not prevail, but fees were denied; the agency did further investigate based on the appellant's arguments:

The agency is obligated to make reasonable inquiries into exonerating facts brought to its attention by appellant before removing him. Steger v. Defense Investigative Service, 19 M.S.P.R. 462, 463-464 (1984). At his oral reply to the notice of proposed removal, appellant asserted that Mr „ Thomas was "a proven liar." The agency noted in its decision to remove appellant, however, that it further investigated and interviewed Mr. Thomas after appellant's oral reply. It concluded that Mr. Thomas was not absent without authorization on the days alleged and was not known to lie. Although the agency may have erred in its assessment of Mr. Thomas's credibility, nothing in the record shows that the agency should have known that appellant was more credible than Mr. Thomas when it decided to remove appellant.

Steger was another counsel fee case. It is discussed in Chapter 15, "Lack of Reasonable Inquiry" within "Knew or Should Have Known." The result reached by the Board in Steger followed a Federal Circuit decision, Steger v. Defense Investigative Serv., 717 F.2d 1402, 1407 (D.C. Cir. 1983):

Since its seminal opinion in Allen, the Board has determined that attorneys' fees may be warranted when an agency blindly continues with adverse personnel action despite the presentation of exonerating facts by the employee--facts which, had the agency only inquired into them, would have spared the employee the considerable expense of hiring an attorney. In the present case, the Board's unexplained failure to apply an agency's duty of prudent inquiry to the DIS forces us to reverse the Board's decision as arbitrary and capricious and remand to the Board for further consideration of whether, in light of this relevant factor, a fee award is due Steger and, if so, in what amount.

In another context, the failure of the agency to investigate circumstances developed by the appellant during a reply further convinced the Board that the appellant's allegations of reprisal were justified, Rhee v. Dept. of Treasury, 117 MSPR 640, 657 ¶ 33, 2012 MSPB 26 (2012):

We also note that the appellant claimed in her response to the agency's proposal notice that, upon her reinstatement in May 2009, Ammari "began creating a 'disciplinary record' against" her, and that she transferred out of his work group "after years of his harassment and retaliation." The agency's decision letter, however, makes no mention of the appellant's claims. The agency's failure to investigate the appellant's allegations concerning Ammari would also tend to indicate that the stated reason for its actions were a pretext for reprisal. Cf. Fulton v. Department of the Army, 95 M.S.P.R. 79, ¶ 14 (2003) (to have proceeded with personnel actions without investigating whether the appellant's allegations of disparate treatment were true would tend to indicate that the stated reasons for these personnel actions were a pretext for retaliation for whistleblowing).

[Rhee is discussed Chapter 12, "Convincing Mosaic; Evidence Considered as a Whole; Comparators."]

From all of this one concludes that the agency risks a merits reversal by the Board if it fails to investigate critical or determinative evidence provided during the reply--assuming that the information provided persuades the Board, if not the agency, of the lack of merit of the agency's case or the merits of the appellant's defenses. Accompanying a loss on the merits could be attendant liability for counsel fees and possible indirect evidence of reprisal or discrimination or whistleblowing retaliation and resultant compensatory or consequential damages.
