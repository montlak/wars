II. PETITION FOR REVIEW; RECONSIDERATION

The appeal results in an initial decision from a Board regional office. Once the initial decision issues, the AJ's jurisdiction over the case ends at the regional level, except to accept a settlement or allow transcript corrections during the time between the issuance of the initial decision and the date it becomes a final decision. Two procedures allow the Board to modify the initial decision. First, a petition for review (PFR) of the initial decision can be filed with the MSPB headquarters establishment by a party, including by OPM, or by OSC when those institutions are a party to a case. If the PFR is timely, or if the Board waives its timeliness regulation and accepts a belated PFR, the initial decision does not become final until the Board reviews the initial decision and accepts, modifies, or reverses the result reached by the AJ through the issuance of a Board final decision. The Board may also remand the case for additional adjudication by the AJ that will lead to another initial decision. Any party may file a petition for review (and, as will be later discussed, parties may file cross-PFRs), with the effect that the finality of the initial decision Is suspended pending MSPB review, and each PFR will be closed out by a decision from the Board. The PFR process is a matter of right, assuming the existence of an initial decision and assuming that the PFR is filed on time. Just as an AJ must close out an appeal with an initial decision, the Board closes out a PFR with a decision of its own, although the Board exercises its discretion as to whether to modify the initial decision or remand an appeal for additional development by the AJ. Previously many PFRs resulted in final decisions that summarily affirmed the initial decision by summarily rejecting arguments or objections raised in the PFR through a brief, pro forma, recitation--known as a "short form" decision--that the Board found no basis for reopening or modifying the initial decision. Short form decisions were discontinued following the expansion in 2010 of the Board's use of nonprecedential final orders. The PFR process is nondiscretionary: the Board must consider and decide a timely PFR.

The second procedure allowing the Board to modify an initial decision is discretionary with the Board. The Board may, at its discretion, decide to reopen or reconsider an initial decision, although any party may request the Board to exercise this initiative. Under 5 CFR 1201.118 (2022):

Regardless of any other provision of this part, the Board may at any time reopen any appeal in which it has issued a final order or in which an initial decision has become the Board's final decision by operation of law. The Board will exercise its discretion to reopen an appeal only in unusual or extraordinary circumstances and generally within a short period of time after the decision becomes final.

The Board may use the reopening authority before or after the initial decision becomes final; the PFR process must be invoked prior to the finality of the initial decision. A party can request the Board to exercise its authority to reopen or reconsider a case when the initial decision is final and there is no likely excuse for a belated PFR. Although there are a considerable number of cases defining when and for what reasons the Board will exercise its reopening authority, the exercise of this authority is discretionary and it is unusual. In addition to the PFR and the reopening process, under 5 CFR 1201.119 (2022), OPM may, under defined circumstances, file a petition for reconsideration of a Board final order. Review of arbitration awards involving EEO issues is initiated with the Clerk of the Board through a process similar to that for a PFR. [Refer to Chapter 5, "Review of Arbitration Awards."]

The nomenclature chosen by the Board to describe what is essentially an appeal from the initial decision that results from what is also called an appeal is a bit confusing. Many administrative agencies operating under the Administrative Procedure Act use adjudication procedures that result in recommended decisions issued by their judges. Those recommended decisions generally become final agency decisions unless exceptions are filed to those decisions. The exceptions trigger the appellate process leading to a final agency decision that is judicially reviewable. The Board, operating under procedures not ordinarily directly tied to the Administrative Procedure Act, set up the process so that a case is ordinarily begun at the regional office through an appeal (sometimes called a petition for appeal) adjudicated by an AJ to the point of issuance of an initial decision. Under 5 CFR 1201.113(a) (2022), peculiarly entitled "exceptions," the initial decision becomes final 35 days after it is issued unless any party files a timely petition for review or if the MSPB reopens the appeal on its own motion. Under 5 CFR 1201.113(b) (2022), "If the Board denies all petitions for review, the initial decision will become final when the Board issues its last decision denying a petition for review." Under 5 CFR 1201.113(c) (2022), if the MSPB grants a petition for review or a cross-petition for review, or if the Board "reopens or dismisses a case," the decision of the Board is final "if it disposes of the entire action." If the Board grants a PFR, then the case is "reviewed." If the Board reopens the case, then the case is "reopened." 5 CFR 1201.117(a) (2022).

The Board's description of the status of an initial decision and its transformation into an agency final decision is convoluted. In practice, parties file petitions for review or cross-petitions for review in the manner, and within the time limits, set by 5 CFR 1201.114-.115 (2022) to obtain review of an AJ's decision issued under 1201.111, the finality of that decision being controlled by 1201.113. Under 5 CFR 1201.115(d) (2022), the Board may grant a PFR when it is established that there is new and material evidence or that the AJ's decision was based on an erroneous interpretation of statute or regulation. Under 5 CFR 1201.117 (2022), in any case that is reopened or reviewed, the Board may issue a decision that grants a PFR or reopens the appeal and decides the case, or takes the action necessary for final disposition of the case; the Board may affirm, reverse, modify, or vacate the AJ's decision in whole or in part; and the Board may issue a final decision and set a compliance date.

The terms "reopen" and "review" are sometimes used interchangeably, but practitioners should keep in mind the distinction between a petition for review--that being the practitioners' means of appealing an initial decision and requiring the Board to consider the appeal through a petition for review, and a request to the Board to exercise its own discretion to reopen a case--a request that the Board exercise its discretion to consider to objections to an initial decision that has become final or to a Board final decision. Every PFR must be considered, although the PFR can be dismissed for procedural reasons such as timeliness or denied for substantive reasons; but few requests to the Board to reopen decisions are considered on the merits. Because the PFR triggers a nondiscretionary review process, as compared with the discretionary reopening process, the PFR is by far the favored tool to secure any chance of meaningful Board review. The PFR must be timely; half the effort is getting it to the Board on time.

Most Board decisions are issued on the basis of PFRs challenging initial decisions. The Board's FY 2011 Annual Report noted that of 849 PFRs decided that year, 2% were settled, 7% were dismissed, 69% were denied, 17% were granted, and 5% were denied but reopened on other grounds. Of the 145 PFRs that were granted, about 62% of the cases were remanded; in 17% of the cases was the initial decision reversed; and in about 15% of the cases, the initial decision was affirmed. The result: of 849 PFRs for FY 2011, 25 resulted in reversals; 89 resulted in remands. 628 PFRs were denied, with or without modification; some were settled or withdrawn. See MSPB's Issues of Merit (May 2012), p. 7.
