8. Burden of Proof

Settlement agreements pose different burdens of proof than cases involving the enforcement of Board orders directing remedial action. The burdens of proof governing a petition seeking enforcement of a settlement were summarized, Ortega v. USPS, 85 MSPR 422, 424-25 ¶ 6 (2000):

The Board has jurisdiction to enforce the settlement agreement in this case because the underlying appeal is within the Board's jurisdiction and the settlement was entered into the record for enforcement purposes. Fernandez v. Department of Justice, 80 MSPR 459, 461 (1998). Because a settlement agreement is a contract, the Board will adjudicate an enforcement proceeding relevant to a settlement agreement in accordance with contract law. Greco v. Department of the Army, 852 F.2d 558, 560 (Fed. Cir. 1988). In a breach of contract action, the burden of proof rests on the party claiming breach. Vaughan v. U.S. Postal Service, 77 MSPR 541, 546 (1998). Therefore, an appellant asserting noncompliance bears the burden of proving by preponderant evidence that the agency breached the settlement agreement; however, following the appellant's filing of a petition for enforcement, the agency must produce relevant, material evidence of its compliance with the agreement. Fernandez, 80 MSPR at 461; Gannon v. U.S. Postal Service, 67 MSPR 660, 664 (1995). Mere assertions of compliance are insufficient to make that showing. Edmonds v. U.S. Postal Service, 68 MSPR 677, 680 (1995). The agency must produce documentation or affidavits showing compliance. Fernandez, 80 MSPR at 461. In sum, both parties are required to produce documentation or affidavits to prove assertions in a petition for enforcement concerning compliance with a settlement agreement. Fernandez, 80 MSPR at 461; Edmonds, 68 MSPR at 680.

See Torres v. DHS, DA-0752-07-0066-C-2 (NP 4/4/2022) ("The appellant, as the party alleging noncompliance, bears the burden of proving by preponderant evidence that the agency breached the settlement agreement. Haefele v. Department of the Air Force, 108 M.S.P.R. 630, ¶ 7 (2008). In response to a petition for enforcement claiming breach of a settlement agreement, the agency should submit evidence of the measures it took to comply, but this is a burden of production only; the overall burden of persuasion on the breach issue remains with the appellant. Turner v. Department of Homeland Security, 102 M.S.P.R. 330, ¶ 5 (2006). An administrative judge should hold an evidentiary hearing if there is a genuine issue of material fact concerning the agency's breach of a settlement agreement. Id.; see 5 C.F.R. § 1201.183(a)(3)."); Azmat v. DHS, PH-0432-12-0020-C-1 (NP 12/18/2014) ("A settlement agreement is a contract, and, as such, will be enforced in accord with contract law. Allen v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 112 M.S.P.R. 659, ¶ 7 (2009), aff'd, 420 F. App'x 980 (Fed. Cir. 2011). The Board will enforce a settlement agreement which has been entered into the record in the same manner as a final Board decision or order. Id. Where the appellant alleges noncompliance with a settlement agreement, the agency must produce relevant material evidence of its compliance with the agreement or show that there was good cause for noncompliance. Id. The ultimate burden, however, remains with the appellant to prove breach by a preponderance of the evidence. Id.").

Flores v. USPS, 115 MSPR 189, 194-95 ¶¶ 9-11, 2010 MSPB 215 (2010), also described the requirements imposed on the parties in a settlement enforcement petition:

In order to prevail, the appellant bears the ultimate burden of showing material noncompliance by the agency with the terms of the settlement agreement. See Lutz v. U.S. Postal Service, 485 F.3d 1377, 1381 (Fed. Cir. 2007). A breach is material when it relates to a matter of vital importance, or goes to the essence of the contract. Id.; Torres v. Department of Homeland Security, 110 M.S.P.R. 482, ¶ 9 (2009). A party may establish a breach of an agreement "by proving that the other party failed to comply with a provision of the contract in a way that was material, regardless of the party's motive." Link v. Department of the Treasury, 51 F.3d 1577, 1582 (Fed. Cir. 1995). Where, as here, the facts are undisputed, the determination of whether there has been material noncompliance with the terms of the agreement necessarily reduces to a question of law. Lutz, 485 F.3d at 1381.

A settlement agreement is a contract, and, as such, it will be enforced in accordance with contract law. Allen v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 112 M.S.P.R. 659, ¶ 7 (2009); see Lutz, 485 F.3d at 1381. In construing the terms of a settlement agreement, the words of the agreement are of paramount importance. Greco v. Department of the Army, 852 F.2d 558, 560 (Fed. Cir. 1988); Galatis v. U.S. Postal Service, 109 M.S.P.R. 651, ¶ 10 (2008). The Board has no authority to unilaterally modify the terms of the parties' settlement agreement, Galatis, 109 M.S.P.R. 651, ¶ 10, or to read a nonexistent term into an agreement that is unambiguous, Hamilton v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 92 M.S.P.R. 467, ¶ 6 (2002). The Board will consider parol evidence only if the agreement is ambiguous. Galatis, 109 M.S.P.R. 651, ¶ 10; Haefele v. Department of the Air Force, 108 M.S.P.R. 630, ¶ 11 (2008). The terms of an agreement are ambiguous as a result of differing interpretations as to the meanings or intent given to those terms by the parties only when the respective interpretations are both reasonable. See Alexander v. U.S. Postal Service, 94 M.S.P.R. 237, ¶ 7 (2003). When an agreement's words and meaning are unambiguous, its terms are not subject to variation. Slattery v. Department of Justice, 590 F.3d 1345, 1347 (Fed. Cir. 2010).

The Board has the authority to enforce the terms of a settlement agreement that has been entered into the record in the same manner as any final Board decision or order. Lary v. U.S. Postal Service, 493 F.3d 1355, 1357 (Fed. Cir. 2007); Allen, 112 M.S.P.R. 659, ¶ 7. If an appellant alleges noncompliance with a settlement agreement, the agency must produce relevant material evidence of its compliance with the agreement, or show that there was good cause for noncompliance. Allen, 112 M.S.P.R. 659, ¶ 7; see Haefele, 108 M.S.P.R. 630, ¶ 7.

The AJ should explain to the parties, and the explanation is usually provided in an acknowledgment order issued after receipt of an enforcement petition, what is expected of them. Anthony v. DOJ, 76 MSPR 45, 51 (1997), stressed the significance of notice from the AJ concerning the need for affidavits or documentation to support an allegation that an agency breached a settlement:

Here, the administrative judge did not inform the appellant that, as the party asserting that the settlement agreement was breached, she has the burden of proving that the agency breached the agreement. See Reniere v. Department of Agriculture, 62 MSPR 648, 651 (1994); Fredendall v. Veterans Administration, 38 MSPR 366, 371 (1988), modified on other grounds, Jones v. Office of Personnel Management, 61 MSPR 252, 254 (1994). Nor did the administrative judge inform the appellant of what she must do to meet her burden of proof. That is, the administrative judge did not inform the appellant that mere assertions of the agency's noncompliance are insufficient to show noncompliance, and that, instead, the appellant must offer evidence in the form of documentation or affidavits to support her claim that the agency has not complied with the terms of the settlement agreement. See Vincent v. Department of Justice, 32 MSPR 263, 268-69 (1987) (party claiming noncompliance with a settlement agreement must offer evidence in the form of documentation or affidavits to support his claim of noncompliance); accord Edmonds v. U.S. Postal Service, 68 MSPR 677, 680 (1995)....

The party asserting breach of a settlement must prove by preponderant evidence that the other party breached the agreement, but the agency has a "heavy burden" of production regarding compliance with a settlement. See White v. USPS, 110 MSPR 461, 465 ¶ 7, 2009 MSPB 15 (2009); Golsby v. DHS, 100 MSPR 25, 29 ¶ 7 (2005) ("The agency has a heavy burden of production regarding compliance with the settlement agreement."); Alexander v. USPS, 94 MSPR 237, 239-40 ¶ 4 (2003) ("Thus, as the party asserting noncompliance, the appellant must prove by preponderant evidence that the agency breached the settlement agreement. Jones, 61 M.S.P.R. at 254. After the appellant has filed a petition for enforcement, however, the agency must produce relevant, material evidence of its compliance with the agreement. Ford, 85 M.S.P.R. 354, ¶ 5. The agency has a heavy burden of production regarding compliance with the settlement agreement. Joos v. Department of the Treasury, 69 M.S.P.R. 398, 402 (1996)."). The agency's assertions concerning compliance must include a clear explanation of its compliance efforts supported by understandable documentary evidence. See Vaughn v. USPS, 97 MSPR 97, 100 ¶ 7 (2004). Evidence is evidence. Statements by the representatives of parties in their pleadings do not constitute evidence of compliance. Alexander, supra ¶ 6. Under Golsby v. DHS, 100 MSPR 25, 29-30 ¶ 9 (2005), the Board notes that 5 CFR 1201.183(a)(1)(i) requires that an agency respond to an appellant's enforcement petition with evidence of compliance, "including a narrative explanation of the calculation of back pay and other benefits, and supporting documents." Under Golsby a declaration by an agency official relating to compliance may be deemed insufficient, and documentary evidence of compliance may be required, if the declaration does not fully meet the appellant's allegation of agency noncompliance with the agreement. [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Burden of Proof; Proof of Compliance," concerning compliance with Board remedial orders; and refer to Chapter 4, "Statements of Representative, Party."]

The party complaining of noncompliance has the initial burden of proving that the respondent committed a breach of the agreement, treated by the Board as a contract, Fredendall v. VA, 38 MSPR 366, 370-71 (1988):

Although not raised by the parties on petition for review, the Board notes that the enforcement decision in this case implicitly placed the burden on the appellant to prove her assertions of agency noncompliance with the settlement agreement. It is well settled that the resolution of an appeal on the basis of a settlement agreement that is made a part of the record "constitutes a final decision issued under the Board's appellate jurisdiction, and, as a result, the Board has the authority to enforce the settlement agreement." Richardson, 5 MSPR at 250. Should either party believe that the settlement agreement is not being properly implemented, the proper remedy is to petition for enforcement under 5 CFR § 1201.182 to the appropriate regional office. See Doster v. Agency for International Development, 9 MSPB 732, 10 MSPR 521, 522 (1982). The general requirements for the enforcement of settlement agreements are determined by federal law when, as here, the substantive rights and liabilities of the parties derive from federal law. See, e.g., Mid-South Towing Co. v. Har-Win, Inc., 733 F.2d 386, 389 (5th Cir. 1984); United States v. Orr Construction Co., 560 F.2d 765, 768 (7th Cir. 1977).

The courts have determined that a court-approved settlement agreement of the parties is subject to the general rules of construction and enforceability regarding contracts. See, e.g., Village of Kaktovik v. Watt, 689 F.2d 222, 230 (D.C. Cir. 1982); Sanchez v. Maher, 560 F.2d 1105, 1108 (2d Cir. 1977); Plymouth Mutual Life Insurance Co. v. Illinois Mid-Continent Life Insurance Co., 378 F.2d 389, 391 (3d Cir. 1967). An action to enforce a settlement agreement is analogous to an action for breach of contract. Lee v. Hunt, 483 F. Supp. 826, 832 (W.D. La. 1979), aff'd, 631 F.2d 1171 (5th Cir. 1980), cert. denied, 454 U.S. 834, 102 S. Ct. 133, 70 L. Ed.2d 112 reh. denied, 454 U.S. 1129, 102 S. Ct. 983, 71 L. Ed.2d 118 (1981). In the usual case, the burden of proof in an action for breach of contract rests on the plaintiff. See Browzin v. Catholic University of America, 527 F.2d 843, 849 (D.C. Cir. 1975). The courts, thus, require the plaintiff in an action based on the alleged breach of a settlement agreement to prove that the defendant committed such breach. See generally, e.g., Kashi v. Gratsos, 790 F.2d 1050, 1057 (2d Cir. 1986); Polish American Machinery Corp. v. R.D. & D. Corp., 760 F.2d 507 (3d Cir. 1985); Village of Kaktovik, 689 F.2d at 230-31.

The Board finds that this judicial precedent and analysis is generally applicable to the Board's enforcement proceedings. Therefore, the Board concludes that in a proceeding seeking the enforcement of a settlement agreement that was made a part of the record under Richardson, 5 MSPR 248, the burden rests on the party asserting that the settlement agreement has been breached to prove such noncompliance.

Fredendall v. VA, 38 MSPR 366, 371 (1988), also noted:

The Board acknowledges that the agency in this case has greater resources, expertise, and access to disputed records than does the appellant. Thus, the Board expects the agency in this case, and in all enforcement proceedings of a settlement agreement, to produce relevant, material, and credible evidence of its compliance with the agreement upon the request of the appellant.

See Perry v. Dept. of Army, 992 F.2d 1575 (Fed. Cir. 1993) ("Here, the agency charged with failing to comply with the settlement agreement is wholly possessed of the evidence. It, therefore, has the duty of producing all of the evidence that it has to show that the provision of the agreement at issue has been satisfied."); cf. Putnam v. DHUD, 44 MSPR 533, 536 (1990) ("The Agency makes a general statement that it has completed all of the items in the agreement. However, its evidence of compliance does not include any specific documentation of its compliance with the Board order. Nor does the agency's evidence of compliance include sufficient explanation regarding the steps it has taken to comply with the Board order.").

The Board's analysis of the burdens of proof in enforcement litigation concerning settlements was modified to a degree in Perry v. Dept. of Army, 992 F.2d 1575, 1577-78 (Fed. Cir. 1993), involving a dispute over whether the agency complied with a settlement calling for the appellant's priority consideration for a position and determining that the agency has the duty to produce all of the evidence that it possesses to show that the provision was satisfied:

...The board held that Perry was required to prove that the Army did not comply with the agreement, citing Fredendall v. Veterans Administration, 38 MSPR 366 (MSPB 1988). The board then found that the agreement did not require the Army to explain its reasons for not selecting Perry, but only required that the Army consider Perry before considering anyone else. The board also found no evidence of bad faith because, among other things, there was "no evidence that the selecting official knew [Perry], was aware of either the disciplinary action against her or the settlement agreement, or had any other reason to act in bad faith (or in retaliation) with respect to her referral as a candidate for priority consideration." The board thus concluded that Perry failed to carry her burden of proof. This appeal followed.

...

When a party to a settlement agreement petitions for enforcement, the petitioner "must describe specifically the reasons the [petitioner] believes there is noncompliance" with the settlement agreement. 5 CFR 1201.182(a). The alleged noncomplying party must then file one of the following: (1) evidence of compliance; (2) evidence of compliance actions that have been taken, that are in process, and that remain, and a reasonable schedule for full compliance; or (3) a statement showing good cause for failure to comply completely. 5 CFR 1201.183(a)(1)(i)-(iii); cf. Fredendall, 38 MSPR at 371 (discussing burden of production when agency asserted to be in noncompliance). Thereafter the petitioner may respond to the alleged noncomplying party's submissions. 5 CFR 1201.183(a)(1).

In this case the board placed on Perry the burden of proving the Army's noncompliance with the settlement agreement, citing the Fredendall case. There the board likened a petition for enforcement to an action for breach of contract, stating:

The courts have determined that a court-approved settlement agreement of the parties is subject to the general rules of construction and enforceability regarding contracts. An action to enforce a settlement agreement is analogous to an action for breach of contract. In the usual case, the burden of proof in an action for breach of contract rests on the plaintiff. The courts, thus, require the plaintiff in an action based on the alleged breach of a settlement agreement to prove that the defendant committed such breach.

...Therefore, the Board concludes that in a proceeding seeking the enforcement of a settlement agreement...the burden rests on the party asserting that the settlement agreement has been breached to prove such noncompliance.

Fredendall, 43 MSPR at 371 (citations omitted). In relying on these contract principles, the board made no mention of the requirements placed upon a noncomplying party by its own regulations, 5 CFR 1201.183(a)(1)(i)-(iii). It recognized, however, the greater resources, expertise and access to disputed records of the agency and said:

Thus, the board expects the agency in this case, and in all enforcement proceedings of a settlement agreement, to produce relevant, material, and credible evidence of its compliance with the agreement upon the request of the appellant. [Emphasis added.]

Fredendall, 38 MSPR at 371.

There is discernable tension between the board's regulations and its decision in Fredendall. The regulations at a minimum obligate the respondent (usually the agency) to produce in most cases "evidence of compliance" within 15 days after the filing of a petition that merely "describes specifically the reasons the petitioning party believes there is noncompliance." Yet Fredendall indicates that the petitioner must request agency records and evidence before they have to be produced. Read more expansively, the regulations place a greater burden on the respondent than on the petitioner, i.e., to prove complete compliance or when and how complete compliance will be achieved and to do so with "narrative explanation...and supporting documents," or to show good cause for noncompliance. Yet Fredendall expressly places the burden of proof on the petitioner.

Without deciding whether Fredendall is reconcilable with the board's regulations, a matter that we believe should be considered by the board in the first instance, we conclude that it is sufficient to say in this case that both Fredendall and the regulations place a heavy burden of production on the agency. Here, the agency charged with failing to comply with the settlement agreement is wholly possessed of the evidence. It, therefore, has the duty of producing all of the evidence that it has to show that the provision of the agreement at issue has been satisfied.

As discussed infra, we are convinced that the agency has failed in this regard; it has not produced "all of the relevant material and credible evidence of its compliance" that was available to it. Fredendall, 38 MSPR at 371. Thus, the board legally erred in denying petitioner's claim on the basis that she failed to satisfy her ultimate burden of proof as required by the Fredendall case.

Perry was followed in Hansson v. Dept. of Navy (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 96-3072). In Hansson, the appellant complained that the agency did not abide by the agreement to expunge from all personnel files any record of the action taken against him. The Board found the agency in compliance based upon an ambiguous and brief response to the appellant's enforcement petition. The agency did not submit evidence supporting the assertions in its response. Lacking a proper evidentiary showing by the agency, the court remanded the case to the Board to permit the agency to satisfy its burden of production of evidence for proper Board adjudication of the enforcement petition. See Sweet v. USPS, 89 MSPR 28, 35 ¶ 16 (2001) (agency representative's statement was not evidence of compliance with a settlement when it was not in the form of an affidavit or made under the penalty of perjury); Fernandez v. DOJ, 80 MSPR 459, 462 ¶ 9 (1998) (as to a promise in a settlement to ensure clean personnel files, agency's statement that it was 'in total compliance' with the agreement was not evidence of compliance because it was not in affidavit form or made under the penalty of perjury: "We find that the administrative judge erred by basing her compliance determination on the agency's unsworn, general statement of compliance and by not specifically addressing whether the agency had expunged the appellant's OPF of 'all other information relating to [the] demotion' as required by the settlement agreement. See Rickels v. Dept. of Treasury, 42 MSPR 596, 603 (1989)."); Warren v. Dept. of Navy, 70 MSPR 677, 680 (1996) (agency representative's unsworn statement that the record was purged did not overcome contrary allegations by the appellant supported by documentation).

As a result of Perry's requirement that the agency produce all the evidence it has to show compliance with a settlement, the Board modified the Fredendall analysis regarding the order of production of proof in compliance disputes involving settlements. Jones v. OPM, 61 MSPR 252, 254 (1994), explained that "to the extent that Fredendall holds that the agency does not have to produce evidence until requested by the appellant, Fredendall is modified to eliminate that holding":

The law of contracts applies to settlement agreements in Board cases. See Greco v. Department of the Army, 852 F.2d 558, 560 (Fed. Cir. 1988). In an action for breach of contract, the burden of proof rests on the plaintiff, who is required to prove by a preponderance of the evidence that the defendant committed breach. Thus appellant has the burden of proof here. See Fredendall v. Veterans Administration, 38 MSPR 366, 371 (1988); Kolassa v. Department of the Treasury, 59 MSPR 151, 154 (1993). See also Corbin on Contracts (1986), §§ 709 (one who asserts a breach is ordinarily required to prove facts establishing such a breach) and 1230 (the plaintiff must prove the fact of breach).

This burden must, however, be viewed in the context of the Board's procedural regulations. In Perry v. Department of the Army, 992 F.2d 1575, 1578 (Fed. Cir. 1993), the court pointed out that Fredendall is inconsistent with the Board's regulations at 5 CFR §§ 1201.56(a) [now 5 CFR 1201.56(b)] and 1201.183(a) to the extent it requires the appellant to request evidence of compliance before the agency's obligation to produce it arises. The agency's duty to produce evidence of compliance is triggered by the filing of a petition for enforcement, according to 5 CFR § 1201.183(a). Therefore, to the extent that Fredendall holds that the agency does not have to produce evidence until requested by the appellant, Fredendall is modified to eliminate that holding.

See Ruggieri v. USPS, 70 MSPR 92, 94 (1996) (concerning a dispute over the calculation of back pay required by a settlement: "In a proceeding to enforce a settlement agreement the party alleging noncompliance has the burden of proof. However, when an appellant makes specific allegations of noncompliance, it is the agency's burden to produce relevant evidence within its control showing the agency's compliance with the agreement or showing good reason for failure to comply.").

Reniere v. USDA, 62 MSPR 648, 650-53 (1994), was one of a series of decisions allowing an appellant to set aside a settlement after the employer breached a provision calling for a favorable employment reference. In Reniere, the agency was faulted for failing to fully counter appellant's hearsay evidence of the breach:

The appellant initially filed an appeal with the Board's Washington Regional Office from the agency's action removing her from her position as a secretary, GS-6. On March 23, 1992, the parties entered into a settlement agreement resolving their dispute and providing, in pertinent part, that the agency would make no statement to possible employers referencing unacceptable performance. The administrative judge found the agreement to be valid, entered it into the record for enforcement purposes, and dismissed the appeal as settled.

The appellant subsequently accepted employment with the National Headquarters of the Salvation Army and learned, upon her arrival, that the agency had not given her a good reference. She then filed a petition for enforcement of the settlement agreement, alleging that the agency breached the agreement and that the breach diminished her opportunities for employment in the Federal sector. The administrative judge denied the petition, finding that the appellant had failed to prove the agency's breach of the agreement because the evidence presented in support was unsigned and unauthenticated. In addition, she found that the appellant had not been harmed because the appellant's present employer hired her despite any negative reference from the agency....

In a proceeding seeking enforcement of a settlement agreement, current decisions of the Board provide that the party alleging breach of the agreement bears the ultimate burden of proof on the issue of noncompliance. Fredendall v. Veterans Administration, 38 MSPR 366, 371 (1988), modified on other grounds by Jones v. Office of Personnel Management, 61 MSPR 252 (1994). An action to enforce a settlement agreement is analogous to an action for breach of contract, and, generally, the burden of proof in an action for breach of contract rests upon the plaintiff. Id. Because the agency has greater resources, expertise, and access to disputed records than the appellant, it must produce relevant, material, and credible evidence of its compliance with the agreement upon the request of the appellant. Id.

We note, however, that, in a recent decision by the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, Perry v. Department of the Army, 992 F.2d 1575 (Fed. Cir. 1993), the court discussed the Board's findings in Fredendall. After noting that, under Fredendall, the party seeking enforcement bears the ultimate burden of proof, the court discussed the Board's regulations concerning the enforcement of settlement agreements. Perry, 992 F.2d at 1578-79. The regulations require that the petitioner for enforcement "must describe specifically the reasons [she] believes there is noncompliance" with the agreement. 5 CFR § 1201.182(a). The party allegedly in breach must then file either: (1) evidence of compliance; (2) evidence of compliance actions that have been taken, that are in process, and that remain, and a reasonable schedule for full compliance; or (3) a statement showing good cause for failure to fully comply. 5 CFR § 1201.183(a)(1)(i)-(iii).

While not deciding whether Fredendall is inconsistent with the Board's regulations concerning petitions for enforcement, the court held that "both Fredendall and the regulations place a heavy burden of production on the agency." Id.

[T]he regulations place a greater burden on the respondent than on the petitioner, i.e., to prove complete compliance or when and how complete compliance will be achieved and to do so with "narrative explanation...and supporting documents" or to show good cause for noncompliance.

Id. Finally, the court found that, when the agency charged with breach of the settlement agreement is wholly possessed of the evidence, it must produce all of the evidence that it has to show that it is in compliance with the agreement. Id.

This increased focus on the agency's burden of production in enforcement proceedings is especially important when the party petitioning for enforcement relies on hearsay evidence to show noncompliance. While hearsay is admissible in administrative proceedings before the Board, the assessment of the probative value of such evidence depends on the circumstances of each case. Biberstine v. Department of Defense Dependents Schools, 37 MSPR 248, 258 (1988); Borninkhof v. Department of Justice, 5 MSPB 150, 5 MSPR 77, 83-87 (1981). Hearsay evidence may constitute substantial evidence, but it may not be sufficient to constitute preponderant evidence. Tamburello v. US Postal Service, 45 MSPR 455, 466 (1990). The administrative judge must assess the probative value of the hearsay evidence. See Scroggins v. U.S. Postal Service, 48 MSPR 558, 565 (1991).

...Here, the agency was clearly possessed of the evidence of compliance with the agreement, through the testimony of its employee, Dr. Stafford. Under the rationale of Perry, the agency was required to produce all of its evidence showing compliance, e.g., a straightforward statement from Dr. Stafford regarding the negative responses rather than the agency's attempt to circumvent the issue through its admission that Dr. Stafford "responded to some specific questions asked." Perry decrees that the burden of proof does not rest entirely on the appellant; the agency must produce all of the evidence in its possession bearing on compliance. Perry, 992 F.2d at 1579. In this case, the agency's tacit admission that it had responded negatively to the request for a reference is sufficient to establish the appellant's allegations, and, therefore, we find that the administrative judge erred in concluding otherwise.

Dept. of Treasury, 74 MSPR 684, 695 (1997) ("Under contract law, the party alleging breach of a settlement agreement has the burden of proving such breach."); Reinert v. Additionally, part of the appellant's burden is to at least plead a breach of an agreement; without this argument the appellant fails to meet his burden of showing that the agency breached the settlement. If the appellant fails to meet his burden of proving noncompliance with a settlement, his case is not rescued through an attack on the agency's evidence of compliance, Allen v. VA, 420 Fed. Appx. 980, 986-87 (Fed. Cir. 2011 NP):

Mr. Allen also argues that, even if the agency was required to purge removal-related documents only from his OPF, the declarations submitted by the agency to demonstrate compliance with this obligation were insufficient to prove that the agency actually removed all such documents. This argument is unpersuasive, however, because it was Mr. Allen, and not the agency, who had the burden of proving breach.

Pursuant to 5 C.F.R. § 1201.183(a), which requires an agency accused of non-compliance with an agreement to come forward with evidence of compliance, the agency submitted two sworn declarations. In the first, Ms. Anderson averred that she "instructed the Health Resource Center" ("HRC"), which maintains the agency's OPFs, "to remove from the Appellant's OPF all documentation related to his removal from the VA, and to replace this information with an SF-50" reflecting Mr. Allen's resignation. The agency also submitted a declaration from Assistant Human Resources Officer Jennifer LuttJohan of HRC confirming that "the information contained in [Mr. Allen's] OPF which referenced his removal from [the agency] was replaced with an SF-50 stating that [Mr. Allen] resigned from the VA." Ms. LuttJohan further averred that "there is no other documentation in [Mr. Allen's] OPF that shows he was disciplined or removed." Mr. Allen, on the other hand, presented no evidence showing that the agency failed to expunge all removal-related documents from his OPF other than the fact that the agency disclosed removal documents to OWCP.

Mr. Allen's contention that the agency's declarations were insufficient to prove compliance conflates the agency's burden of moving forward with evidence of compliance with the ultimate burden of proving breach. While the agency was required to produce evidence "of the compliance actions that the party ha[d] completed, and a statement of the actions that are in process and the actions that remain to be taken," see 5 C.F.R. § 1201.183(a), the Board found that the agency discharged this obligation by submitting declarations averring that: (1) the agency instructed HRC to remove the relevant documents from Mr. Allen's OPF; and (2) an inspection of Mr. Allen's OPF revealed that information related to his removal had been removed and was replaced with an SF-50 reflecting Mr. Allen's resignation. Once the agency satisfied this burden of production, it was Mr. Allen who had the burden of proving that the relevant documents were not removed from his OPF. See Jones v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 61 M.S.P.R. 252, 254 (M.S.P.B. 1994) (party asserting breach bears the ultimate burden of proving facts establishing breach notwithstanding 5 C.F.R. § 1201.183(a)) (citing Corbin on Contracts (1986), §§ 709 (one who asserts a breach is ordinarily required to prove facts establishing such a breach) and 1230 (the plaintiff must prove the fact of breach). The Board properly weighed the evidence presented by both sides and concluded that Mr. Allen failed to prove that the agency maintained removal related documents in his OPF, rather than in some other file. Because substantial evidence supports the Board's conclusion, we find no error as to this issue.

Impossibility of performance may be a defense to an asserted breach of a settlement. The burden of showing impossible performance rests with the party making that assertion. Nance v. Dept. of Transp., 55 MSPR 68, 71 n.2 (1992). Assuming a dispute over compliance rather than the impossibility of performance, the basic rule on compliance was stated in Lyon v. OPM, 45 MSPR 179, 183 (1990):

In a proceeding seeking enforcement of a settlement agreement, the agency must produce relevant, material, and credible evidence of its compliance with the agreement upon the request of the appellant. The ultimate burden, however, is on the appellant, as the party seeking enforcement, to show that the agency has failed to fulfill the terms of the agreement....

If the agency's defense to enforcement proceedings is the appellant's noncompliance with a provision of a settlement agreement, the appellant may need to demonstrate compliance to obtain the quid pro quo from the agency. See Johnson v. FEMA, 41 MSPR 561, 564 (1989).
