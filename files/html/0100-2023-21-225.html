broida mspb Chapter 14: Retirement and Restoration Rights Chapter 14: Retirement and Restoration Rights, Subchapter 02: Restoration Rights Upon Recovery From Occupational Injury or Illness, Section C: Meaning of "Recovery", Subsection 04: Physically Disqualified Employee, Part a: Geographic Extent of Consideration
a. Geographic Extent of Consideration

For the physically disqualified employee, under Estate of Kravitz v. Dept. of Navy, 110 MSPR 97, 106-07 ¶¶ 21-22, 2008 MSPB 221 (2008), the agency's search requirements are extensive:

On remand, the AJ should determine whether the agency failed or refused to restore the appellant pursuant to the standard set forth in 5 C.F.R. § 353.301(c):

An individual who is physically disqualified for the former position or equivalent because of a compensable injury, is entitled to be placed in another position for which qualified that will provide the employee with the same status, and pay, or the nearest approximation thereof, consistent with the circumstances in each case. This right is agencywide and applies for a period of 1 year from the date eligibility for compensation begins. After 1 year, the individual is entitled to the rights accorded individuals who fully or partially recover, as applicable.

The record shows that on August 7, 2002, the agency completed "a qualification determination...to ascertain if [the appellant was] eligible for shoreside employment with the Afloat Personnel Management Center (APMC) and if so, what position [the appellant] qualif[ied] for, and if any vacancies exist[ed] . The agency found that the appellant was "not eligible for shoreside employment within [its] appointing authority at the APMC." The APMC is the personnel and training arm for the MSC.

The agency has not averred to this point, however, that it undertook a review of opportunities agencywide, which would have included other commands and facilities within the Department of the Navy, nor has it shown that it was precluded from making such a search. See Farrell v. Department of Justice, 50 M.S.P.R. 504, 510 (1991) ("[a]n examination of the relationship between the restoration to duty regulations and the reemployment priority list regulation demonstrates...that the term 'agency'...includes all component parts of an agency."), overruled on other grounds by Leach v. Department of Commerce, 61 M.S.P.R. 8, 13 (1994); cf. Moore v. U.S. Postal Service, 33 M.S.P.R. 77, 82 (1987) ("A former employee covered by 5 U.S.C. § 8151(b)(2) is entitled to agencywide consideration for reemployment, and is not limited to consideration only at the facility at which he was last employed, unless the agency can show such circumstances that would preclude an agencywide search. The basis for affording such a agencywide right of priority consideration for reemployment...is to provide qualifying former employees as full an opportunity as possible under the particular circumstances to be considered for reemployment with their agency.") (citations omitted). Accordingly, the parties should be allowed to present evidence and argument regarding agencywide opportunities for restoration during the period the appellant was eligible for restoration.

Pettus v. Dept. of Navy, DC-0353-13-0409-B-1 (NP 4/4/2022), explained:

An employee who is physically disqualified from her former position as the result of a compensable injury has an agency-wide right to restoration to an equivalent position or the "nearest approximation thereof" within the first year of her injury. 5 C.F.R. § 353.301(c). Under the applicable regulation, the appellant only needs to prove that the agency failed to restore her, or improperly restored her, following a leave of absence.[3] 5 C.F.R. § 353.304(a).

[3] To the extent that the administrative judge found that the appellant had to prove that the agency's denying restoration was arbitrary and capricious, we modify that finding. The arbitrary and capricious burden does not apply to employees seeking restoration within 1 year after compensation begins. See 5 C.F.R. § 353.304 (setting forth an appellant's burden of proof based on the degree of her recovery).
