4. Pay and Benefits; Classification; Debt; Leave

Defined as adverse actions, under 5 USC 7512, are:

(3) a reduction in grade;

(4) a reduction in pay;...

According to 5 USC 7511:

(3) "grade" means a level of classification under a position classification system;

(4) "pay" means the rate of basic pay fixed by law or administrative action for the position held by an employee;...

"Pay," defined by 5 CFR 752.402 (2022), is:

...the rate of basic pay fixed by law or administrative action for the position held by the employee, that is, the rate of pay before any deductions and exclusive of additional pay of any kind.

For the record, "grade" is defined by 5 CFR 752.402 (2022) as "a level of classification under a position classification system."

A reduction in compensation through reduction or elimination of an allowance or other fringe benefit does not necessarily equate to a reduction in pay. Statutory language and purpose must be considered, Nelson v. FDIC, 68 MSPR 1, 3-4 (1995):

Under 5 USC 7512(4), a reduction in pay is a matter appealable to the Board. "Pay" means the rate of basic pay fixed by law or administrative action for the position held by an employee. 5 USC 7511(a)(4). An employee's rate of basic pay does not include "additional pay of any kind." 5 CFR 531.202(k); McLaughlin v. USPS, 55 MSPR 192, 197 (1992).

In Fox [v. FDIC], 62 MSPR at 451, the Board was interpreting the statutory requirements of the Whistleblower Protection Act (WPA) regarding interim relief, by which an agency must afford an employee "pay, compensation, and all other benefits as terms and conditions of employment," 5 USC 7701(b)(2)(B), when it makes a determination not to comply with the interim relief provisions by returning the employee to the workplace. In finding that "compensation" included an RDA [rent differential allowance] in that appeal, we relied on analysis in McLaughlin v. USPS, 55 MSPR at 197-98, in which we found that "compensation" means something other than "pay." The appellant in Fox was entitled under the provisions of the WPA to an RDA as "compensation." That finding does not support a finding that discontinuing the appellant's RDA in this appeal is a reduction in "pay" and thus an action within the Board's jurisdiction under 5 USC 7512(4).

"The Civil Service Reform Act does not specifically confer jurisdiction over a 'demotion,' but rather over reductions in grade and pay. See 5 USC 7512(3)-(4), 7513(d). However, a demotion is defined as a reduction in either grade or pay. See 5 CFR 210.102(b)(4)." Malan v. Dept. of Air Force, 55 MSPR 283, 290 n.5 (1992). "A reassignment to a lower-graded position is an appealable adverse action." Marcheggiani v. DOD, 90 MSPR 212, 217-18 ¶ 11 (2001) ("Alternatively, a reassignment to a lower-graded position that constitutes a release from the employee's competitive level because of a reorganization or some other reason listed at 5 C.F.R. § 351.201(a)(2) is an appealable RIF demotion."); cf. Ronso v. Dept. of Navy, AT-3443-14-0157-I-1 (NP 9/25/2014) (Board lacks jurisdiction that the appellant was denied on-call pay or that the appellant should have been employed under Title 38 rather than under Title 5).

If it is not presented with an IRA (whistleblower reprisal) appeal, or in a USERRA appeal, the Board ordinarily does not have appellate jurisdiction over allegations concerning holiday pay, premium pay, differential pay, or the agency's failure to mail timely pay checks. The pay problems must be tied to the Board's statutory jurisdiction over adverse actions arising from reductions in basic pay (downgradings) or suspensions. See Shifflett v. Dept. of Navy, 83 MSPR 472, 474 n.2 (1999) ("[S]ince locality pay is clearly an addition to basic pay, its loss would not be considered an appealable reduction in pay."); cf. Matsuo v. United States, 586 F.3d 1180 (9th Cir. 2009) (briefly describing the locality pay system): Scott v. USDA, 108 MSPR 177, 181-82 ¶ 12, 2008 MSPB 41 (2008) (defining locality pay); Bell v. Dept. of Transp., 39 MSPR 210, 212 (1988) ("The appellant should have been afforded an opportunity to support his allegations with evidence or argument and to show that any of the pay discrepancies resulted in an action over which the Board would have jurisdiction, i.e., a constructive involuntary suspension or a reduction in pay."). But see Kile v. Dept. of Air Force, 104 MSPR 49, 53, 2006 MSPB 330 (2006) (questioning the result in Shifflett as to whether locality pay is part of basic pay under the 2004 Federal Workplace Flexibility Act), [following remand, 107 MSPR 160, 2007 MSPB 260 (2007)] .

On September 18, 2008, OPM proposed rules at 73 Fed. Reg. 54075 to clarify, at 5 CFR 752.401(b)(15), that a reduction in an employee's basic pay resulting from the application of the Federal Workforce Flexibility Act of 2004, Pub. L. 108-411 (October 30, 2004), and implementing regulations, is excluded from adverse action coverage. The regulatory change was made permanent, 74 Fed. Reg. 63531 (Dec. 4, 2009), so that the regulation, 5 CFR 752.401(b)(15) (2022) excludes from the definition of adverse action:

Reduction of an employee's rate of basic pay from a rate that is contrary to law or regulation, including a reduction necessary to comply with the amendments made by Public Law 108-411, regarding pay-setting under the General Schedule and Federal Wage System and regulations implementing those amendments...[.]

An appealable pay reduction must occur in the course of a personnel action. There is no reduction in pay when an employee is reinstated to a lower step in a job that he resigned six months earlier. Reduction in pay exists "only when an ascertainable lowering, at the time of the personnel action, of an employee's present or future pay occurs." Curtin v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1992 NP No. 92-3129) (citing Chaney v. VA, 906 F.2d 697, 698 (Fed. Cir. 1990)); cf. Brumfield v. SSA, AT-3443-16-0093-I-1 (NP 4/15/2016) ("As the administrative judge found, the appellant's assertion that the agency required her to take leave when not in her work area does not constitute a nonfrivolous allegation that the agency reduced her rate of basic pay or that she otherwise suffered an appealable reduction in pay or grade."). Even when a reduction in pay occurred as a result of a RIF, subsequent salary disputes stemming from claims to pay retention were not within the Board's jurisdiction, unless those pay disputes arose at the time of the underlying personnel action, the RIF. Chaney v. VA, 906 F.2d 697 (Fed. Cir. 1990); see Starnes v. Dept. of Interior, 6 MSPR 90, 92, 6 MSPB 88 (1981) ("[P]ay retention is not an issue which is appealable in a RIF case."). If the reduction in pay occurs while the appellant is a probationer, the Board lacks jurisdiction because the appellant is not a covered employee. Pratt v. VA, 3 MSPR 288, 289, 3 MSPB 376 (1980) (it was immaterial that after an appeal was filed the appellant completed probation); cf. Mulligan v. USPS, 81 MSPR 1, 4 ¶ 9 (1999) (remand to determine whether the Board had jurisdiction over a postal inspector's challenge to pay reduction; at issue was whether the appellant's position was managerial, supervisory, or involved personnel work in other than a purely nonconfidential clerical capacity).

A reduction in pay of an appellant who did not successfully complete a "career enhancement program," with a statutory special pay rate, was not within the Board's appellate jurisdiction. Perrault v. USDA, DA-0752-15-0522-I-1 (NP 2/10/2023).
