c. Sworn Written Statement; Form of Attestation

Affidavits are often encountered when the agency submits its response to the appeal. The investigation file that forms part of the response can include sworn statements or affidavits. Depositions--written transcriptions of sworn verbal testimony--are often taken after the appeal and response have been filed but before the hearing. Depositions are taken "subject to penalties for intentional false statements," 5 CFR 1201.75 (2022):

Taking depositions.

Depositions may be taken by any method agreed upon by the parties. The person providing information is subject to penalties for intentional false statements.

Under 18 USC 1001:

(a) Except as otherwise provided in this section, whoever, in any matter within the jurisdiction of the executive, legislative, or judicial branch of the Government of the United States, knowingly and willfully--

(1) falsifies, conceals, or covers up by any trick, scheme, or device a material fact;

(2) makes any materially false, fictitious, or fraudulent statement or representation; or

(3) makes or uses any false writing or document knowing the same to contain any materially false, fictitious, or fraudulent statement or entry;

shall be fined under this title, imprisoned not more than 5 years or, if the offense involves international or domestic terrorism (as defined in section 2331), imprisoned not more than 8 years, or both. If the matter relates to an offense under chapter 109A, 109B, 110, or 117, or section 1591, then the term of imprisonment imposed under this section shall be not more than 8 years.

The party taking the deposition may then seek to enter it into the appeal as an exhibit with the prehearing submission. [Refer to Chapter 2, "Deposition," for guidance on MSPB deposition practice.]

An affidavit or deposition is a sworn statement made outside a hearing room. The threat of a perjury conviction or the moral obligation of an oath, it is believed, makes these statements more probative than those that are unsworn. The use of affidavits or sworn statements is significant. They constitute evidence when they are appended to the appeal or included with the agency response to the appeal. The hearing then becomes an opportunity for one party to rebut the other's sworn statements.

An affidavit is a statement that is executed before a notary public. The notary signs a statement to the effect that the affiant swore to and signed the statement in the presence of the notary. As to the significance of a notary's certification, Kelsey v. OPM, DC-0831-09-0804-I-1 (NP 12/10/2010):

..."the well-established rule that to overcome the presumption arising from [a notary's] certificate there must be proof of gross concurrent mistake or fraud, through strong and disinterested evidence." Marden v. Hopkins, 47 App. D.C. 202, 206-07 (D.C. 1918). Thus, the appellant has failed to rebut the notary's certificate, which is prima facie proof of the evidence that it contains. See Ford v. Ford, 27 App. D.C. 401, 408 (D.C. 1906).

Under 28 USC 1746, unsworn statements made under penalty of perjury are considered the equivalent of affidavits. Donato v. DOD, 34 MSPR 385, 389 (1987). Section 1746 provides:

Unsworn declarations under penalty of perjury

Wherever, under any law of the United States or under any rule, regulation, order, or requirement made pursuant to law, any matter is required or permitted to be supported, evidenced, established, or proved by the sworn declaration, verification, certificate, statement, oath, or affidavit, in writing of the person making the same (other than a deposition, or an oath of office, or an oath required to be taken before a specified official other than a notary public), such matter may, with like force and effect, be supported, evidenced, established, or proved by the unsworn declaration, certificate, verification, or statement, in writing of such person which is subscribed by him, as true under penalty of perjury, and dated, in substantially the following form:

(1) If executed without the United States: "I declare (or certify, verify, or state) under penalty of perjury under the laws of the United States of America that the foregoing is true and correct. Executed on (date).

(Signature)."

(2) If executed within the United States, its territories, possessions, or commonwealths: "I declare (or certify, verify, or state) under penalty of perjury that the foregoing is true and correct. Executed on (date).

(Signature)."

The Board's appeal form, at block 12, the signature block, contains the statement the statement "I certify that all of the statements made in this form and any attachments are true, complete, and correct to the best of my knowledge and belief." The statements provided by the appellant may or may not constitute sworn evidence, depending on how those statements are presented to the Board. If the appeal is signed by the appellant rather than only by a designated representative, under some circumstances the statements in the appeal may be considered sworn, Edem v. Dept. of Commerce, 64 MSPR 501, 505 n.5 (1994):

The appellant claims in her petition for review that her allegations of marital status discrimination were sworn, as they were contained in an attachment to a Merit Systems Protection Board appeal form, which contains a statement that "I certify that all of the statements made in this appeal are true, complete, and correct to the best of my knowledge and belief." While the appeal form does contain this statement, we note that the statements therein are neither sworn under oath nor made under penalty of perjury. We further note that the certification was signed by the appellant's representative, not by the appellant personally.

Those who are e-filers may submit sworn statements by selecting a box that the e-filed submission contains information within the personal knowledge of the filer and that the information provided is sworn to be true, McCauley v. VA, CH-1221-14-0721-W-1 n.6 (NP 2/18/2016):

See Cannon v. DHHS, DA-0752-12-0289-I-1 (NP 11/5/2013) ("In his petition for appeal, which included a verification that his statements were true, complete and correct to the best of his knowledge and belief, the appellant denied that he ever drank alcohol on duty.").

In Edem, 64 MSPR at 505, supra, the Board observed that sworn statements are not essential to establish a right to a jurisdictional hearing. See Felton v. Dept. of Air Force, 106 MSPR 198, 200 ¶ 6 (2007) (appearing to credit a statement made by the appellant in his appeal concerning the status of an EEO complaint when the appeal form was signed with the certification noted in Edem, supra). But, under current Board regulations, sworn statements appear to be required to establish jurisdiction when jurisdiction is in dispute. When jurisdiction is disputed, nonfrivolous allegations of jurisdiction must be supplied to the Board. Under 5 CFR 1201.4 (2022):

(s) Nonfrivolous allegation. A nonfrivolous allegation is an assertion that, if proven, could establish the matter at issue. An allegation generally will be considered nonfrivolous when, under oath or penalty of perjury, an individual makes an allegation that:

(1) Is more than conclusory;

(2) Is plausible on its face; and

(3) Is material to the legal issues in the appeal.

Sworn statements are often included in the agency's record supplied with its response to the appeal. An appellant may also include affidavits or sworn statements with the appeal that initiates her case with the Board. As to affidavits offered during the hearing phase of the case, section 14.a.(2) of Chapter 10 of the Judges' Handbook states of affidavits and depositions:

Affidavits and depositions may be accepted into evidence at a hearing, despite objections, upon a reasonable showing by the offering party of the unavailability of the affiant to testify. There is generally no need to play recorded or videotaped depositions at the hearing unless one of the parties wishes the AJ to rule on an objection made at the deposition.

Affidavits that are not rebutted are competent evidence of the information they contain. DeDonato v. USPS, 25 MSPR 286, 289 n.6 (1984); see Walters v. Dept. of Transp., 18 MSPR 234, 237 (1983) ("Sworn affidavits which are not rebutted are competent evidence of the matters asserted therein."); cf. Zimmerman v. OPM, 80 MSPR 512, 516 ¶ 10 (1999) ("We note that his testimony under oath was based on personal knowledge of the events at issue, was consistent, and was not inherently improbable."). "Sworn statements that are not rebutted are competent evidence of the matters asserted therein." Jordan v. DOJ, 54 MSPR 609, 611 (1992); see Craft v. VA, 78 MSPR 374, 381 (1998) (crediting sworn statement of affiant who was deceased by the time of hearing); Vercelli v. USPS, 70 MSPR 322, 327 (1996) ("The declaration was made under penalty of perjury, entitling it to considerable weight unless rebutted."); Manzan v. USPS, 68 MSPR 616, 619 (1995) ("Sworn statements that are not rebutted are competent evidence of the matters contained therein."); Staats v. USPS, 68 MSPR 65, 68 (1995) ("An unrebutted affidavit is considered competent evidence of the matters asserted therein."); Schaefer v. USPS, 42 MSPR 592, 595 (1989) ("Affidavits that are not rebutted are competent evidence of the matters asserted therein, including the timely filing of pleadings, and, if uncontested, such affidavits prove the facts they assert."); Woodall v. FERC, 30 MSPR 271, 273 (1986) (declaration submitted pursuant to 28 USC 1746, subscribed as true under penalty of perjury, proves the facts it asserts if uncontested). An affidavit was substantial evidence of the charged misconduct when there did not appear to be much of a dispute over the facts at issue and when the appellant could have deposed the affiant to challenge his version of the events. See Kulkin v. VA (Fed. Cir. 1986 NP No. 85-1880); cf. Martin v. DHS, 855 Fed. Appx. 691, n.2 (Fed. Cir. 2021 NP) ("Mr. Martin argues that the arbitrator's credibility determination as to Mr. Van-Zile's written testimony is not afforded any deference because Mr. Van-Zile did not testify in person. But the deference to credibility findings is not limited to live testimony. See Joseph v. Dep't of Homeland Security, 497 F. App'x 26, 29 (Fed. Cir. 2012) (per curiam). The arbitrator also found Mr. Martin to be a less than credible witness based on, among other things, his 'several versions' of the events of May 18, 2017."). But "Statements made under the penalty of perjury are entitled to little weight if they are inherently incredible or if they are disputed or rebutted by other evidence." Davis v. SSA, SF-0432-12-0763-I-1 (NP 9/29/2014) (vacated following court remand, Davis v. SSA, SF-0432-12-0763-M-1 (NP 5/3/2016)).

Affidavits or statements present problems because they are selective as to content and because, as hearsay, they are not subject to cross-examination. They are frequently prepared by investigators or lawyers who may believe their job is to support a case against the employee. The statements may be signed by employees who fear reprisal from, or desire to curry the favor of, supervisors or managers known to be anxious to rid themselves of the nettlesome appellant. The potential for abuse is apparent. The subject of witness coercion was addressed in Bonet v. USPS, 712 F.2d 213, 217 (5th Cir. 1983), including the comments of a dissenting judge about affidavits offered in adverse action cases:

I accept those affidavits at face value, although I must comment it is very difficult for an employee not to give such an affidavit when confronted with a superior who indicates that he or she is trying to build a case against an employee for this particular kind of misconduct....

Affidavits should not displace live testimony if the affidavits were retracted or contradicted by the witnesses who made them. See Jefferson v. DLA, 22 MSPR 10, 13 (1984) (citing Walls v. USPS, 10 MSPR 274, 278 (1982)). An appellant's affidavit may carry less weight if the appellant decided not to have a hearing and present testimony subject to cross-examination on its content, Potosky v. USPS, AT-0752-09-0957-I-2 (NP 1/11/2011):

During proceedings before the administrative judge, the appellant withdrew his request for a hearing. He relied on his affidavit, which is hearsay evidence, to show that he did not take a microphone speaker amp from a postal customer. The Board assesses the weight to be given hearsay evidence under Borninkhof v. Department of Justice, 5 M.S.P.R. 77, 83, 87 (1981). Applying the Borninkhof criteria to the appellant's affidavit, its value is diminished because he was available to testify at a hearing, but chose not to do so, and is not a disinterested witness as his employment was at stake. Further, his account is inconsistent with other evidence in the case. The appellant asserts that he did not take a microphone speaker amp from a postal customer and that what he took was trash. However, there is nothing in the record to corroborate the appellant's assertion, including the arresting officer's affidavit. The appellant had the opportunity to clarify any discrepancy in the affidavit through the testimony of the officer, who the administrative judge approved as a witness for the appellant, before the appellant withdrew his hearing request. If the Board were to assess the appellant's credibility as the declarant of the affidavit under Hillen v. Department of the Army, 35 M.S.P.R. 453, 458 (1987), his credibility would be weak for reasons similar to those that diminish the value of the affidavit under the Borninkhof criteria, notably, his bias on his own behalf and the contradiction of his version of events by other evidence or record. Hillen, 35 M.S.P.R. at 458.

Affidavits have limited reliability if they are made to agency investigators outside of the presence of the appellant or agency representative. Walls v. USPS, 10 MSPR 274, 278 (1982); cf. Washington v. USDA, 10 MSPR 465, 470 (1982) ("We would add that even though the agency apparently did not attempt to obtain the witnesses, transcript evidence was preferable even to a sworn affidavit. See Kowal v. United States, 412 F.2d 867, 873 ([Ct. Cl.] 1969)."). A party should not be sanctioned for producing an affidavit rather than a witness whom the party stated would be produced at a hearing, but the weight of the affidavit may be diminished if a sufficient reason is not provided for the absence of the witness and if there are other, notable circumstances, e.g., the affidavit was not witnessed. See Williams v. Dept. of Air Force, 41 MSPR 173, 177 (1989).

Although the use of affidavits in a Board hearing is proper if the material is probative and its use is fundamentally fair, O'Brien v. In re Kass, 2 MSPR 79, 100, 2 MSPB 251, 269 (1980). The Board may be required to weigh conflicting statements, and the Board can technically evaluate the format of written statements offered into evidence. The more formal the declaration--the more closely the statement follows an affidavit format--the more weight the Board gives the critical substance of the statement when there are conflicts among written submissions. In Haebe v. DOJ, 81 MSPR 167, 173 ¶ 10 & n.1 (1999), rev'd on other grounds, 288 F.3d 1288 (Fed. Cir. 2002), involving an issue of interim relief, the appellant's statement to the Board included the following assertion: "I hereby certify that the foregoing statements made by me are true. I am aware that if any of the foregoing statements made by me are willfully false, I am subject to punishment." The Board's evaluation of the certification was that it constituted an unsworn statement:

The appellant's "certification" is not a sworn affidavit or a declaration made under penalty of perjury. A declaration made under penalty of perjury must at least include the phrase "penalty of perjury" and state that the document is true. See, e.g., In re Muscatell, 106 B.R. 307, 309 (Bankr. M.D. Fla. 1989); Edem v. Department of Commerce, 64 MSPR 501, 505 n.5 (1994) (a certification that certain statements are true, complete, and correct is not a sworn statement or a declaration made under penalty of perjury). A declaration made under penalty of perjury is entitled to considerable weight unless rebutted. See Vercelli v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 MSPR 322, 327 (1996). Nevertheless, although the appellant's certification is unsworn and not made under penalty of perjury, it is still admissible hearsay evidence. See Scott v. Department of Justice, 69 MSPR 211, 228 (1995), aff'd, 99 F.3d I 1 60 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table).

The form of a declaration was of significance as to proof of OPM approval of an agency appraisal system, Adamsen v. USDA, 116 MSPR 331, 339-41 ¶¶ 14-17, 2011 MSPB 49 (2011) (Member Rose dissenting):

It would be proper for the agency to support its contention that OPM approved its November 1995 performance appraisal system with an affidavit or declaration from a credible witness with actual knowledge of the approval. Indeed, probative hearsay evidence may meet the substantial evidence standard. Griffin, 23 M.S.P.R. at 663 n.5, citing Borninkhof v. Department of Justice, 5 M.S.P.R. 77 (1981). The agency provided an "affidavit" by Anita Adkins, senior policy Human Resources Specialist, who states that "[b]y letter dated January 31, 1996, Doris Hausser, Chief Performance Manager and Incentive Awards Division, OPM acknowledged approval of USDA's system." However, Ms. Adkins did not become responsible for the agency's non-SES performance management policy until April 2006, and she plainly was not personally involved in the relevant events of 1995 and 1996. Although Ms. Adkins states she reviewed "available files," she does not identify what files were or were not available, she does not assert that she personally saw or examined the alleged January 1996 OPM approval letter, nor does she otherwise explain the factual basis for asserting that OPM approved "USDA's [performance management] system" in a letter dated January 31, 1996.

We are cognizant that, in appropriate circumstances, an unrebutted affidavit or declaration under penalty of perjury may prove the facts it asserts. See Schaefer v. U.S. Postal Service, 42 M.S.P.R. 592, 595 (1989) (affidavit); Woodall v. Federal Energy Regulatory Commission, 30 M.S.P.R. 271, 273 (1986) (declaration under penalty of perjury). However, Ms. Adkins's alleged "affidavit" woefully fails to meet the requirements of an affidavit because it was not sworn before a notary public or other person authorized to administer oaths. The statement also fails to meet the requirements of a declaration pursuant to 28 U.S.C. § 1746, as it was not signed "under penalty of perjury"; Ms. Adkins's statement merely states: "I hereby declare that the information contained in the above Affidavit is true to the best of my knowledge, information and belief." 28 U.S.C. § 1746(2); see e.g., Caretolive v. Food & Drug Administration, 2011 WL 43646 (6th Cir., January 6, 2011), slip op. at 7. It is therefore nothing more that an unsworn statement, which we find entitled to little weight for the reasons we explain below.

Although hearsay evidence such as Ms. Adkins's statement may be admissible in Board proceedings, assessment of the probative value of hearsay evidence necessarily depends on the circumstances of each case. Borninkhof, 5 M.S.P.R. at 83-87. The Board generally evaluates the probative value of hearsay by considering various factors that include the availability of persons with firsthand knowledge to testify at the hearing, whether the out-of-court statements were sworn, whether the declarants were disinterested witnesses to the events and whether their statements were routinely made, the consistency of the out-of-court statements with other statements and evidence, whether there is corroboration or contradiction in the record, and the credibility of the out-of-court declarant. Vojas v. Office of Personnel Management, 115 M.S.P.R. 502, ¶ 13 (2011); see Social Security Administration v. Long, 113 M.S.P.R. 190, ¶¶ 26-27 (2010) (citing Borninkhof, 5 M.S.P.R. at 87).

Ms. Adkins's unsworn statement, identifying no firsthand knowledge of the relevant events and no factual basis to support a claim that OPM had issued a letter in January 1996 approving the agency's November 1995 performance appraisal system, is on its face unreliable hearsay. We have considered the statement in light of the agency's significant, unexplained failure to produce any letter from OPM approving the updated performance appraisal system described in Personnel Bulletin 430-1. Especially also considering that the agency was able to produce OPM approval letters for both the appraisal system that originally covered the appellant and the system in effect after the appellant's removal, we do not find Ms. Adkins's unsworn, unsupported statement to be probative in establishing the requisite OPM approval for the agency's November 1995 appraisal system. Therefore, we give little weight to Ms. Adkins's statement.[10]

[10] Ms. Adkins has identified no personal knowledge of the critical fact she asserts, i.e., OPM approval of the agency's performance appraisal system. She admittedly was not present during the relevant time period, she does not state she has personally seen the January 1996 letter, she does not identify any other basis for her to have personal knowledge of this crucial fact, and her statement was not given under oath or in the proper form for a declaration under penalty of perjury. Federal Rules of Evidence 602, providing guidance to the Board, states, in part: "A witness may not testify to a matter unless evidence is introduced sufficient to support a finding that the witness has personal knowledge of the matter." We therefore give little weight to Ms. Adkins's similarly weak unsworn statement, lacking an adequate description of her personal knowledge. We further note that we have considered Ms. Adkins's unsworn, unsubstantiated assertion that OPM had approved the agency's performance appraisal plan in light of the agency's failure to produce the alleged January 31, 1996 OPM letter of approval, even though the administrative judge below granted the appellant's motion to compel the agency to produce such documentation.
