8. Procedure for Introducing Document; Demonstrative Evidence

Often the AJ will have required the parties to mark and identify exhibits through a prehearing submission that contains a list of exhibits the party desires to introduce into the Board record. During a prehearing conference, the parties may agree that some or all of the exhibits will be admitted. If the exhibits are admitted during the prehearing process, they are part of the appeal record and can be discussed without further ado at hearing as witnesses are examined and as closing arguments or posthearing briefs are offered. Assuming, however, that at the time of hearing a document is not already marked and admitted into evidence, the procedure for introducing the document into evidence is simple. The process is described below (the questions described below are nearly the same for documents already marked for identification in the prehearing submission but not admitted into evidence during the prehearing conference; the attorney using the document simply refers to it by the exhibit number or letter).

Documents offered into the record as exhibits during a hearing are first marked for identification as an exhibit for the party by number or letter (as specified by the prehearing order). Each party (or counsel, if the party is represented), the AJ, and the witness should be given a copy of the proposed exhibit (if the exhibit was already provided in the prehearing submission, it need not again be provided to the AJ and opposing counsel). When the witness is about to be given the document, there is a customary incantation of "May I approach the witness?" or, more succinctly, "May I approach?" Counsel presents the witness with the document and counsel then treads back to counsel's table. The attorney offering the exhibit briefly describes it, e.g., a proposed removal to the appellant dated December 25. The exhibit is examined and briefly described by the witness through counsel's questions. Counsel then offers the exhibit into evidence. The AJ asks for and rules on any objection. If the exhibit is determined to be relevant and otherwise admissible, the AJ states that it has been received in evidence. Counsel may then question the witness further about the exhibit. If the exhibit is rejected, the AJ states why and describes the document for the record. The offeror protects the record by objecting to the ruling, asking for reconsideration of the ruling, and objecting to the denial of the request for reconsideration. The rejected document should go into the Board appeals file as a rejected exhibit. The AJ may ask counsel to withdraw the exhibit or offer it back to counsel. Assuming the document has some significance, counsel should request that the document be included in the file, or that it be maintained in a file of rejected exhibits to be preserved along with the remainder of the Board file. If the AJ refuses to keep a copy of the document, ensure that it is described on the record.

The procedure for offering a document seems to cause about as much confusion among some representatives as the definition of hearsay, so here it is. Assume we have on the stand the supervisor of an appellant who was fired for poor performance and the agency representative wishes to put into evidence the appellant's performance standards (having omitted to place these standards into the response to the appeal):

Q. Mr. Supervisor, on what basis did you evaluate the appellant's performance?

A. I didn't think it was very good.

Q. No, I mean on what document did you evaluate the performance of the appellant?

Objection: Leading question.

Administrative Judge: Overruled.

A. May I answer now?

Q. Sure, go ahead.

A. Why, now that you mention it, I used performance standards that were on an agency form.

Q. Excellent. Judge may I approach the witness?

Administrative Judge: Yes.

Q. I am providing to you and opposing counsel what I've marked as Agency Exhibit 1 for identification. Now, Mr. Supervisor, I am handing you a document identified as Agency Exhibit 1. Would you tell us what it is.

A. Why, it says...

Q. No, No. Just tell us what it is. Don't read it.

A. Oh. These are the appellant's performance standards.

Q. Are these the standards you used in this case?

A. Well, yes, now that you mention it, in fact, they were.

Agency representative: I offer into evidence Agency Exhibit 1.

Administrative Judge: Objections?

Appellant's representative: Yes. I object because the exhibit should have been included in the response to the appeal.

Administrative Judge: What is the Agency response?

Agency representative: The appeals response was prepared by my technical advisor and he forgot to include it, but the appellant has had the standards all along and the standards were identified in our prehearing submission.

Administrative Judge: There doesn't seem to be any question that the appellant had these standards and they were identified in the prehearing submission from the agency, so I do not see any prejudice to the appellant simply because the agency should have included the standards in the response to the appeal. So I am going to overrule the objection and admit these standards. They will be received as Agency Exhibit 1. Proceed.

Appellant's representative: I ask that you note my objection to your ruling and reconsider. The Agency has prejudiced my client by waiting until the last moment to introduce these standards.

Administrative Judge: Your exception is noted and I deny your request for reconsideration. Let's move along. What is the next question, please?

Not every document will be accompanied with all this fanfare as it goes into the record. Whatever procedure is used, remember that if documents are going to be introduced into the hearing, have copies available for everyone and provide a copy to the opposing representative before the exhibit is handed to the AJ or witness.

At times a witness provides demonstrative evidence, for example, the witness sketches out a diagram or chart. If that drawing is to be considered as part of the evidence, it has to make its way into the record either through a copy or a photograph.

The Judges' Handbook offers informal guidance to AJs concerning treatment of exhibits at Chapter 10, section 14:

f. Exhibits.

(1) General. All documents offered for introduction into the record are first marked for identification as an exhibit for the party (e.g., Appellant's Exhibit A, Agency's Exhibit 1, etc.). This procedure applies whether or not the exhibit is received into evidence. Generally, the parties should be required to mark and index their own exhibits before the hearing. The AJ may choose another method of marking the exhibits if it is more efficient. The relevance and admissibility of the exhibits should have already been determined during the prehearing conferences. Parties must provide each other with copies of their exhibits. Evidence introduced solely at the direction of the AJ must be identified and numbered as an exhibit of the AJ.

(2) Ruling on Exhibits. The AJ must state on the record that the exhibit has been marked as (insert identification of party's) exhibit number (insert number or letter) and that it has been admitted into evidence.

(3) Rejected Exhibits. Except under extraordinary circumstances, physical objects (tools, weapons, drugs, or other contraband, etc.) should not be received into evidence. When physical objects are of such probative value as to be material or relevant to a party's burden of proof, the party seeking to admit the physical object into evidence should use an alternative method (photographs, verbal descriptions, stipulations, etc.). Particularly as the Board moves closer to all-electronic records, what might otherwise be a physical exhibit must be entered into the record in an electronic format, through description, testimony, photograph, etc. If a physical object is proffered and rejected by the AJ on evidential grounds, other than its suitability for inclusion in the record, the AJ should verbally describe the object on the record immediately following the ruling on admissibility. All other rejected exhibits, must be maintained in a "Rejected Exhibit" section of the appeal file, properly tabbed and identified as such. When a rejected exhibit is too voluminous or bulky or otherwise unsuitable for enclosure in a "Rejected Exhibit" section of the file, the AJ should describe the rejected exhibit on the record, immediately following the ruling on admissibility, and substitute a brief verbal description for the inclusion in the "Rejected Exhibit" section of the appeal file. Exhibits that are rejected as duplicates of material already contained in the appeal file may simply be returned to the proffering party and need not be included in the "Rejected Exhibit" section of the appeal file.

(4) Withdrawn Exhibits. The AJ must state that the exhibit (identifying party and number or letter) is withdrawn, and then return the withdrawn material to the party who originally submitted it. The AJ may retain exhibits when controversy is likely or removal from the record may cause confusion.

The Handbook also explains, at Chapter 10, section 15(b), as to exhibits identified but not yet in the record at the conclusion of a hearing:

b. Exhibits. When an exhibit was going to be introduced into the record but is still outstanding at the conclusion of the hearing, arrangements must be made for its subsequent receipt. The document should be identified and assigned an exhibit number or letter, and when it is submitted, provided to the other party for inspection and written comment, if appropriate.

The Board's Hearings Procedures Guide (a source of guidance still used by the Board for training and orientation, although it has been superseded by the Judges' Handbook), adds at 33:

J. EXHIBITS AND DEMONSTRATIONS

1. Exhibits

The parties should be advised in the prehearing order that in presenting evidence at the hearing, including exhibits, they will be limited by their prehearing submissions, except for good cause shown.

If an exhibit has been received into evidence at the hearing, the AJ should so state for the record. Evidence introduced solely at the direction of the AJ must be identified and numbered as an exhibit of the AJ.

If an exhibit has been rejected, the AJ shall state that it has been rejected, give the reasons for the rejection, and describe the excluded evidence for the record. See 5 CFR Sec. 1201.62(b).

Accepted and rejected exhibits shall be clearly marked "accepted" or "rejected" and placed in the appeal file. See Edmond v. TVA, 23 MSPR 489, 491 (1984).

2. Quoted Materials

The AJ shall receive into evidence testimony from witnesses which is based on information in the record file, especially where the opposing party has an opportunity to examine the witnesses regarding any relevant document or report, and has access and opportunity to ascertain what material evidence the opposing side has. McLaughlin v. Dept. of Interior, 4 MSPR 70, 73 (1980).

3. Demonstrative Evidence

The AJ shall accept demonstrative evidence into the record when the party introducing the evidence establishes its accuracy. See Davis v. Dept. of Transp., 17 MSPR 373, 375 n.3 (1983).

Demonstrative evidence was used in the form of a recording of a radio transmission. Manning v. Dept. of Defense, SF-0752-13-0632-I-2 (NP 12/20/2016) ("This radio transmission was played during the hearing. ...The administrative judge, having had an opportunity to hear the radio transmission, analyze witness demeanor, and make credibility determinations, concluded that the appellant's language during this contact with dispatch 'gave the impression that he was observing a nondescript motorcycle on agency premises' and intended to give a false impression to the dispatcher. We discern no error with her conclusion in this regard.").
