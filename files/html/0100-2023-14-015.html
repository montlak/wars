9. Rest Periods and Other Short Breaks

The granting of rest periods is within the discretionary authority of department heads under 5 USC 301. DOJ, INS and AFGE, Nat'l Border Patrol Council, 9 FLRA 253, 256-57 (1982). FLRA summarized its law, concluding that break periods are negotiable and that notification requirements concerning breaks are not negotiable, except as to impact, DHHS, SSA, Baltimore and AFGE Local 3342, 34 FLRA 765, 769-70 (1990):

The establishment of break periods is negotiable.... Scheduling breaks to which employees are entitled under a collective bargaining agreement is within the duty to bargain.... Employees must remain in a duty status during their breaks, however, and management retains the right to assign work to them during their breaks under section 7106(a)(2)(B)....

In addition, management has the right under section 7106(a)(2) of the Statute to take actions to ensure that the work assigned to employees is accomplished. Management exercises its right to assign work...and its right to direct employees...by holding employees accountable for the performance of their work....

The break policy involved in this case requires employees to notify supervisors when they take their breaks....

...By requiring employees to notify their supervisors before they go on breaks, or when they want to take their breaks at a time different from the schedule, management is requiring employees to be accountable for their use of duty time. Therefore, the Respondent's decision to change its break policy is an exercise of management's rights to direct employees and to assign work and is outside the duty to bargain.

Determinative of negotiability is whether the proposal prohibits management from assigning work to employees during rest periods. New York State Nurses Ass'n and VA Bronx Med. Ctr., 30 FLRA 706, 743 (1987) (Proposal 15, § 10.05); see NFFE, Council of VA Locals and VA, 31 FLRA 360, 364-97, 411-37 (1988) (discussing series of proposals concerning shift changes and leave arrangements for VA medical staff). A proposal is nonnegotiable that a rest period be free of work. AFGE Local 1770 and Dept. of Army, Ft. Bragg Dependent Sch., 28 FLRA 493, 522 (1987) (Provision 8). In Overseas Educ. Ass'n and DODDS, 29 FLRA 628, 639 (1987) (Proposal 5a), a proposal was nonnegotiable that management agree to duty-free lunch periods. A nonnegotiable proposal fixed the length of standby tours in relation to rest hours between tours for firefighters. It precluded assignment of work to an employee who had been on standby duty for a period of hours. Int'l Ass'n of Firefighters and 4392d Aerospace Support Group, 9 FLRA 700, 701 (1982).

There may be other reasons why a proposal concerning rest breaks is nonnegotiable. In IAM and Dept. of Treasury, Bureau of Engraving & Printing, 33 FLRA 711, 730 (1988), Proposal 9, allowing an employee to leave the activity premises during a break, was nonnegotiable because it interfered with agency internal security practices. Employees had some responsibility for safeguarding large quantities of stamps printed by the activity. Employees normally spent their entire workday on the job site, enjoying a paid lunch hour to accomplish that result. The adverse effect on agency operations of changing security practices to accommodate the effect of the proposal interfered with internal security practices.

A proposal providing two uninterrupted rest periods during each eight-hour tour of duty could be negotiated. The agency had discretion to authorize those rest periods under 5 USC 301, allowing the promulgation of regulations for the conduct of agency employees and extending to the grant of rest periods. Despite management's argument that the proposal required increased staffing, the proposal did not directly interfere with the numbers, types, and grades of employees or positions assigned a tour of duty; the employees remained in duty status during the rest periods and management could assign work to them, notwithstanding that the proposal mandated that the rest periods be uninterrupted. AFGE Local 3511 and VA Hosp., San Antonio, 12 FLRA 76, 85-87 (1983). The union could require negotiations over a proposal that VDT operators be given scheduled breaks of fixed duration: there was no prohibition against management assigning them different work when they were not occupied with their VDT duties during their rest periods. AFGE Nat'l Council of Social Security Field Office Locals and DHHS, SSA, 24 FLRA 842, 843-44 (1986). A proposal was negotiable that each unit employee have two 15-minute breaks, one during each four hours of duty time, with an additional requirement that management make every reasonable effort to provide relief for employees so the breaks could be taken. The proposals allowed employees to take breaks depending on the work to be done and the availability of resources to do the work. Nothing in the proposals precluded management from assigning work to employees on a break if that were necessary to ensure coverage. It was noted that FLRA has consistently determined that break periods are negotiable, as long as they are on duty time and employees remain subject to the assignment of work. Nat'l Union of Hosp. and Health Care Employees, Dist. 1199 and VAMC, Dayton, 28 FLRA 435, 451 (1987).

A proposal that effectively permitted employees to decide when to take breaks (established by the negotiated agreement) and precluded management from instituting a break schedule did not infringe unduly on the right to assign work. The union's intent concerning the proposal was to permit the employees flexibility in scheduling as long as the mission of the agency was not significantly disrupted. AFGE Local 3342 and DHHS, SSA, 19 FLRA 1100 (1985). AFGE Local 1808 and Dept. of Army, Sierra Army Depot, 30 FLRA 1236, 1254 (1988), confirmed that the union may negotiate a proposal that the exact timing of rest periods be mutually agreed by representatives of the union and management (on a departmental basis). A proposal for employees to take a "working smoke break" was negotiable. The agency made no showing that the proposal interfered with its right to assign work. AFGE Local 3369 and SSA, Cypress Hills Dist. Office, 31 FLRA 1110, 1112 (1988); see AFGE Local 3369 and SSA, Midtown Dist. Office, 31 FLRA 1114, 1115-16 (1988) (petition for review of negotiability of proposal concerning smoke breaks was dismissed because it was not apparent that a negotiability rather than bargaining duty issue was raised). A duty-free break of reasonable duration to take care of personal business for employees working 10 or more consecutive hours was negotiable as an appropriate arrangement for employees adversely affected by long shifts. The record suggested that the determination of whether to grant the break and its timing remained within management's discretion. AFGE Council of Prison Locals, Local 1661 and DOJ, Fed. Bureau of Prisons, FCI Danbury, 29 FLRA 990, 1019 (1987). There are some regulatory restrictions on the length of paid break periods. One, noted in NAGE, SEIU and VAMC, Newington, 32 FLRA 206, 208 (1988), is that a break may not exceed 20 minutes under 5 CFR 551.411(b) (1988).

Nonnegotiable was a proposal that management make every effort to relieve employees, through assignment of other personnel, when work demands would otherwise preclude an individual from taking a break. The proposal interfered with the right of management to assign work to an employee on a break. The union did not sufficiently develop arguments for FLRA to consider whether the proposal constituted an appropriate arrangement. AFGE Local 1760 and DHHS, SSA, Office of Hearings and Appeals, Region II, 46 FLRA 1285, 1290 (1993). Resolved as nonnegotiable was a proposal that added 20 minutes paid time for banking purposes on paydays, apparently because it was concluded that a paid break may not be attached to an unpaid lunch period if conflict with 5 USC 6101, establishing a 40-hour workweek is to be avoided. AFGE Nat'l Council of SSA Field Operations Locals and DHHS, SSA, 17 FLRA 924, 925-26 (1985). But see NAGE Local R1-109, post.
