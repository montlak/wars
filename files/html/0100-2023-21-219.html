broida mspb Chapter 14: Retirement and Restoration Rights Chapter 14: Retirement and Restoration Rights, Subchapter 02: Restoration Rights Upon Recovery From Occupational Injury or Illness, Section C: Meaning of "Recovery", Subsection 03: Partially Recovered, Part b: Arbitrary or Capricious Restoration Effort
b. Arbitrary or Capricious Restoration Effort

Under 5 CFR 353.301 (2022):

(d) Partially recovered. Agencies must make every effort to restore in the local commuting area, according to the circumstances in each case, an individual who has partially recovered from a compensable injury and who is able to return to limited duty. At a minimum, this would mean treating these employees substantially the same as other handicapped individuals under the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, as amended. (See 29 U.S.C. 791(b) and 794.) If the individual fully recovers, he or she is entitled to be considered for the position held at the time of injury, or an equivalent one. A partially recovered employee is expected to seek reemployment as soon as he or she is able.

Chism v. USPS, 85 MSPR 436, 439-42 ¶ 5-12 (2000) (partially overruled, Chen v. USPS, 97 MSPR 527 (2004), post), considered what restoration efforts are required for the partially recovered employee:

A partially recovered employee is one who has recovered sufficiently to return to less physically demanding work. Leach v. Department of Commerce, 61 M.S.P.R. 8, 15 (1994). Under 5 C.F.R. § 353.301(d), agencies are required to "make every effort to restore in the local commuting area, according to the circumstances in each case, an individual who has partially recovered from a compensable injury and who is able to return to limited duty." Where an individual is entitled to seek and does seek restoration as a partially recovered employee, the agency is continuously obligated to make restoration efforts, to include searching throughout the agency but within the local commuting area for vacant positions to which he could be restored and to consider him for such vacancies. Leach, at 15-16, 19.

Jurisdiction

An individual who is partially recovered from a compensable injury may appeal to the Board "for a determination of whether the agency is acting arbitrarily and capriciously in denying restoration." 5 C.F.R. § 353.304(c). In order to establish Board jurisdiction over such an appeal, the appellant must show that: (1) he was separated from his position due to a compensable injury; (2) he partially recovered from the injury; (3) he requested restoration within certain limitations; and, (4) the agency denied that request. See As'Salaam v. U.S. Postal Service, 85 MSPR 76 ¶ 14 (2000); Besemer v. U.S. Postal Service, 77 M.S.P.R. 260, 264 (1998). We need not further discuss the first element, since it is undisputed that the appellant was separated from his EAS-17 Maintenance Supervisor position due to his compensable injuries. In order to resolve the jurisdictional question, however, we must address each of the other elements.

The appellant established that he partially recovered from his injuries

Title 5 C.F.R. § 353.102 defines partially recovered as follows: "An injured employee, though not ready to resume the full range of his or her regular duties, has recovered sufficiently to return to part-time or light duty or to another position with less demanding physical requirements." In the instant case, the record contains a letter from one of the appellant's physicians, dated May 15, 1991, that indicates that the appellant was able to work 8 hours per day within specified restrictions. There are similar letters from physicians, dated from 1994 through 1997, that also indicate that the appellant was ready to return to work. However, the record also contains a November 4, 1995 Medical/Rehabilitation Status Questionnaire in which the appellant indicated that he could not work at that time due to a "total knee replacement." In addition, a witness from the Department of Labor testified that the appellant's status as a partially recovered employee dated only from June 2, 1997. Finally, an agency Human Resources Specialist indicated on an April 8, 1998 form requesting the appellant's Official Personnel File (OPF) that the appellant was "able to return to work." The AJ found that the appellant had partially recovered from his compensable injuries "at some point." We agree that the record evidence cited above clearly establishes that the appellant partially recovered from his injuries, but also that it is unclear when the partial recovery occurred. Accordingly, although we find that the appellant satisfied this element of his jurisdictional burden, since we are remanding this appeal for adjudication on the merits we leave it to the AJ to further develop the record as necessary and to determine the date on which the appellant was partially recovered.

The appellant has shown that he requested restoration

The appellant testified that he began to seek restoration as early as 1991, and that he periodically spoke with a number of agency employees about returning to duty, both over the phone and in person, between 1991 and 1999. The agency does not dispute that the appellant requested restoration, and, in fact, other record evidence supports it. Nevertheless, again, as above, it is unclear when the appellant first requested restoration, and whether he was partially recovered at that time. The AJ must also make these determinations on remand.

The agency denied the appellant restoration

As stated above, the AJ based his dismissal of the appeal on his finding that the appellant had failed to demonstrate that the agency "expressly denied" the appellant restoration. The AJ further stated that he could not "review the appellant's claim that the agency's conceded delay in restoring him constituted a constructive denial of restoration." The appellant argues on review that the AJ's holding would allow an agency to divest an employee of Board review by indefinitely ignoring restoration. For the reasons discussed below, we agree.

First, we note that neither the statute nor the regulations covering restoration rights state a specific form in which a denial of a restoration request must be made in order to trigger a former employee's Board appeal rights. 5 U.S.C. § 8151(b); 5 C.F.R. part 353. Nor has the Board ever specified that such a denial must be formal, or in writing. In contrast, in fact, the Board has held that an agency's delay in restoring a partially recovered employee may constitute a denial of restoration. See Taylor v. U.S. Postal Service, 69 M.S.P.R. 479, 483 (1996). In Taylor, because the record was insufficiently developed, the Board was unable to decide whether the agency had delayed the appellant's restoration to such an extent that it was tantamount to a denial. Id. In the instant appeal, however, although we cannot determine the exact dates on which the appellant was first partially recovered and requested restoration, even the agency does not dispute that at the latest, those conditions were fulfilled by April, 1998. In fact, there is some support for a finding that the appellant may have been partially recovered and may have requested restoration much earlier. Moreover, an agency employee admitted that, in 1998, he told an OWCP official that the agency would not restore the appellant to duty because doing so would be too expensive. Under these circumstances, we find that the agency's extensive delays in restoring the appellant, coupled with its admission that, at least as of 1998, it had no plans to attempt to do so, were sufficiently equivalent to a denial of restoration to bestow jurisdiction over this appeal upon the Board.

The Merits

Although we have found that the Board has jurisdiction over this appeal, we must remand the appeal to the regional office for further adjudication on the merits, including a full evidentiary hearing. We instruct the AJ to determine when the appellant was partially recovered from his compensable injuries and when he first requested restoration, and then to determine whether the agency's subsequent denial of restoration was "arbitrary and capricious." See 5 C.F.R. § 353.304(c). If the AJ finds that the denial was arbitrary and capricious, he should order the agency to pay the appellant back pay and benefits as appropriate.

An employee who rejects a suitable offer of restoration cannot show that the agency's offer was so unreasonable as to amount to an appealable denial of restoration because OWCP, by finding the offer suitable, necessarily determined that it was reasonable. The Office of Personnel Management's regulations provide:

Agencies must make every effort to restore in the local commuting area, according to the circumstances in each case, an individual who has partially recovered from a compensable injury and who is able to return to limited duty. At a minimum, this would mean treating these employees substantially the same as other handicapped individuals under the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, as amended.

5 C.F.R. § 353.301(d). As discussed in Sanchez v. U.S. Postal Service, the reassignment obligation under the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, which mandates reasonable accommodation for persons with disabilities, is not necessarily limited geographically to the local commuting area. 2010 MSPB 121, ¶ 18. Under 5 C.F.R. § 353.301(d), however, an agency's responsibility in the restoration context is limited to the local commuting area. Id. The Board has interpreted this regulation as requiring agencies to search within the local commuting area for vacant positions to which an agency can restore a partially recovered employee and to consider him for any such vacancies. See Sapp v. U.S. Postal Service, 73 M.S.P.R. 189, 193-94 (1997). "For restoration rights purposes, the local commuting area is the geographic area in which an individual lives and can reasonably be expected to travel back and forth daily to his usual duty station." Hicks v. U.S. Postal Service, 83 M.S.P.R. 599, ¶ 9 (1999). It includes any population center, or two or more neighboring ones, and the surrounding localities. Sapp, 73 M.S.P.R. at 193. The question of what constitutes a local commuting area is one of fact. The extent of a commuting area is ordinarily determined by factors such as common practice, the availability and cost of public transportation or the convenience and adequacy of highways, and the travel time required to go to and from work. See Beardmore v. Department of Agriculture, 761 F.2d 677, 678 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (defining "local commuting area" in the context of reassignment).

In this case, the agency's documentary submissions show that its job search was limited to installations within a single district. Because the agency limited its search to a single district, whether it searched the entire commuting area remains an unanswered question of material fact. See Sanchez, 2010 MSPB 121, ¶ 14. Evidence that the agency failed to search the commuting area as required by 5 C.F.R. § 353.301(d) constitutes a nonfrivolous allegation that the agency acted arbitrarily and capriciously in denying restoration. See Barachina v. U.S. Postal Service, 113 M.S.P.R. 12, ¶ 7; Urena v. U.S. Postal Service, 113 M.S.P.R. 6, ¶ 13. We therefore find that the appellant has met all of the criteria to establish Board jurisdiction over her restoration appeal, which entitles her to adjudication on the merits. See Barrett v. U.S. Postal Service, 107 M.S.P.R. 688, ¶ 8 (2008).

Cf. Askew v. USPS, AT-0353-16-0757-I-1 (NP 10/25/2022) ("Regarding the fourth jurisdictional criterion, evidence that the agency failed to search the local commuting area, as required by 5 C.F.R. § 353.301(d), would constitute a nonfrivolous allegation that the agency acted arbitrarily and capriciously in denying restoration. Rodriguez-Moreno v. U.S. Postal Service, 115 M.S.P.R. 103, ¶ 17 (2010). The 'local commuting area is the geographic area in which an individual lives and can reasonably be expected to travel back and forth daily' to her usual duty station. Id., ¶ 13. As to the remedy for denial of restoration rights to a partially recovered employee, the Board favors a retroactive search for an appropriate assignment, Corum v. USPS, 118 MSPR 288, 297 ¶ 21, 2012 MSPB 81 (2012):

The Board will not order the appellant restored to an assignment, nor will it order back pay based on such an assignment, because that would put the appellant in a better position than if the wrongful action had not occurred. Cf. Hagan v. Department of the Army, 99 M.S.P.R. 313, ¶ 8 (2005) ("A status quo ante remedy does not require that the appellant be placed in a better position than he was in at the time of the agency's action."). Rather, in a case like this one where the denial of restoration was arbitrary and capricious for lack of a proper job search, the Board has found that the appropriate remedy is for "the agency to conduct an appropriate search within the local commuting area retroactive to...the date of the appellant's request for restoration, and to consider her for any suitable vacancies." Sapp v. U.S. Postal Service, 82 M.S.P.R. 411, ¶ 21 (1999). The remedy of a retroactive job search will be sufficient to correct the wrongful action and substitute it with a correct one based on an appropriate search. However, it will not put the appellant in a better position than she was in before the wrongful action because it leaves open the possibility that the agency might still be unable to find an appropriate assignment available as of May 11, 2006.

The remedial authority to correct a denial or restoration was restated, Harris v. USPS, AT-0353-13-0459-I-1 (NP 6/20/2014):

...The Board will not order the appellant restored to an assignment that was properly discontinued, nor will it order back pay based on such an assignment because that would place the appellant in a better position than if the wrongful action had not occurred. Davis, 120 M.S.P.R. 122, ¶ 14; Scott v. U.S. Postal Service, 118 M.S.P.R. 375, ¶ 14 (2012). Rather, in a case like this one, in which the denial of restoration was arbitrary and capricious for lack of a proper job search, the appropriate remedy is for the agency to conduct an appropriate search within the local commuting area retroactive to the date of the appellant's request for restoration and to consider her for any suitable work. Davis, 120 M.S.P.R. 122, ¶ 14; Scott, 118 M.S.P.R. 375, ¶ 14. The remedy of a retroactive search for available tasks will be sufficient to correct the wrongful action and substitute it with a correct one based on the appropriate search. Davis, 120 M.S.P.R. 122, ¶ 14. It will not, however, put the appellant in a better position than she was in before the wrongful action because it leaves open the possibility that the agency might still be unable to find appropriate available tasks. Id. The appellant may be entitled to back pay only if the agency's restorative search uncovers available work to which it could have restored her. Id.
