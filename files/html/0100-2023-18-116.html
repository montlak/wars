a. Biased Proposing Official

Evidence of bias of a proposing official is relevant, Edmond v. TVA, 23 MSPR 489, 491-92 (1984):

Appellant attempted to introduce additional testimony to substantiate his claim of bias by his supervisor, arguing that the testimony would be material in determining whether or not his supervisor proposed his removal because his supervisor reasonably believed Raulston's allegations to be true or because he disliked the appellant. The presiding official ruled that the testimony would be irrelevant and refused to permit appellant to present those witnesses.

Although the provisions of 5 C.F.R. § 1201.62(b) permit a presiding official to exclude evidence or testimony from consideration if it is "irrelevant, immaterial or unduly repetitious," we conclude that the presiding official erred in excluding the witnesses proffered by appellant.

In this case, the issue of whether or not the attack occurred is but one of the issues raised during the course of the hearing. The appellant claimed that his supervisor's action was motivated not by the allegation of an attack but by his personal animosity towards the appellant. The proffered testimony could have established a possible alternative explanation for the action taken against the appellant. The existence of an alternative explanation is certainly a possibility when the evidence supporting the occurrence of an unprovoked attack is considered. The only direct testimony concerning the altercation comes from the alleged participants, and it is directly contradictory. Furthermore, the appellant introduced unrebutted testimony impeaching the alleged victim's credibility and reputation for veracity.

We also find that the reasons for the selection of the penalty of removal are not entirely clear. Both the proposing official and the deciding official stated that they considered lesser penalties but that the proposing official felt that a lesser penalty would not have been effective. The deciding official's testimony indicates that he chose the penalty of removal based upon the proposing official's lack of confidence in the effectiveness of a lesser penalty. Under these circumstances, testimony concerning alleged bias by the proposing official is highly relevant and should not have been excluded....

A couple observations from the Court of Claims, predecessor to the Federal Circuit, shed some light on the significance of the choice of a proposing official and that individual's motivation. The proposing official's direct involvement in the actions leading to the proposal penned by the official was questioned by the late Judge Oscar Davis in Burkett v. United States, 402 F.2d 1002, 1007 (Ct. Cl. 1968):

There is another questionable aspect of the notice upon which we do not pass because it was not presented administratively or in this court. Mr. Milne signed the notice himself as charging party, and the letter is phrased in first-person terms. In a case involving the writer's personal interests so directly and strongly, this was an insensitive practice which could better have been avoided. Cf. Offutt v. United States, 348 U.S. 11, 75 S. Ct. 11, 99 L. Ed. 11 (1954). Perhaps if another official had had the responsibility of deciding whether to proceed formally against plaintiff, and of framing the notice of charges, this matter would have taken a different course.

From Bilanow v. United States, 309 F.2d 267, 270 (Ct. Cl. 1962):

A man is going to lose his job, his means of support. At such times it is not unreasonable that he ask his superiors to exercise care and use every available and reasonable means open to secure a just and honorable decision.

Setting aside the matter of the "cat's paw" motivational analysis in EEO and whistleblower and USERRA cases, it has been said that even if a proposing official was directed to remove the employee, an independent decision from a deciding official makes the error harmless. Williams v. DLA, 34 MSPR 54, 58 (1987). But see McCourt v. Hampton, 514 F.2d 1365, 1368 (4th Cir. 1975) ("[N]otice [of proposed action] is inadequate if, at the time it is given, it is not notice of proposed action but is notice of a final action."). Allegations of bias are not sufficient to require the recusal of a proposing official. See Adams v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 00-3020) ("We find no authority to suggest that the mere pendency of racial discrimination allegations--unproven claims--against [the proposing official] disqualifies him from taking part in employment decisions involving other black female employees.").
