a. Overlapping Fee Entitlement Standards

It should be apparent, or soon will be, to the thoughtful reader that the categories of fee entitlement overlap. See SSA v. Goodman, 33 MSPR 325, 332 n.5 (1987) ("[t]hese categories are related and may sometimes overlap and merge."); cf. Coffman v. OSC & DHS, 2022 MSPB 18, ¶ 15 (2022) ("However, both the Board and the court have recognized that Allen factors 2 and 5 are related and may sometimes overlap. Id. at 1457 n.5; Social Security Administration v. Goodman, 33 M.S.P.R. 325, 332 n.5 (1987). Indeed, the court in Yorkshire noted that, if an agency ‘possesses no credible evidence prior to the hearing before the Board ([Allen factor] 5), the result of the case will usually be in favor of the employee ([Allen factor] 2).' Yorkshire, 746 F.2d at 1457 n.5 (emphasis in original)."). It can fairly be said that there is no clear demarcation between the standards announced in Allen: essentially the Board described agency bad faith in several different ways and degrees. Agency conduct can be characterized under one or all of these categories. In O'Donnell v. Dept. of Interior, 2 MSPR 445, 451, 2 MSPB 604 (1980), the Board felt that the agency demonstrated ill will by pressing on with a removal based on an ill-founded belief that an employee was faking an illness. Had the agency paid attention to the employee's reply, it would have been clear that the employee could not be charged with misconduct. The Board affirmed an award of fees: the improper motivation for the action warranted the award irrespective of whether the agency's conduct was labeled as being clearly without merit, wholly unfounded, a gross procedural error, in bad faith, or based on charges the agency should have known it would lose. Appellants' counsel will fare best by claiming fees under all possible theories. A multipronged fee petition forces the AJ to perform some analysis. Favorable court cases should be liberally cited.

Under current Board practice, every theory supporting a fee claim should be presented to the AJ. The Board reissued its regulations in 1986, and again in 1997, and the regulations now require the appellant to state why he believes he is entitled to a fee award. 5 CFR 1201.203(a) (2022) (formerly 5 CFR 1201.37(a)(3)). The Board expects specific identification by the appellant of each basis for fee entitlement. See Levenson v. DLA, 49 MSPR 659, 664 (1991) ("Under the Board's current regulations, when an employee files a motion for attorney fees, he must state why he believes he is entitled to an award under the applicable statutory standard."). The Federal Circuit expects the same. A counsel fee motion ought to provide a reasonably specific basis for the fees claimed. The Board is not required to wade through a lengthy record to determine if evidence demonstrates any basis for granting fees. Bruning v. VA (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 88-3125). The appellant's "mere unsupported allegation" in his motion for fees that "he was entitled to an award of fees under each of the Allen criteria" is an insufficient basis for the Board to undertake the analysis of each criterion. See Garcia v. Dept. of Labor, 45 MSPR 28, 33 n.* (1990). The appellant should do more than mention one of the basic entitlement standards. He should explain why he was entitled to fees under that standard. See Mattox v. Dept. of Navy, 46 MSPR 578, 581 n.3 (1991). The Board noted, however, that a counsel fee petition that omits a specific statement concerning the statutory category supporting the fee request will be excused--and the petition entertained under the appropriate basis for fees--if the initial decision on the underlying appeal does not refer the appellant to the regulation governing fee awards. O'Brien v. OPM, 38 MSPR 131, 133 n.2 (1988).

Previously, some forgiveness may have been shown if an appellant missed one of the several possible theories of recovery. Wilson v. OPM, 33 MSPR 371, 374-75 (1987), determined that, under Cruz v. USPS, 32 MSPR 565, 567-68 (1987), if a fee request was submitted before the publication of the Board's 1986 regulation requiring the appellant to state why he believes he is entitled to an award and to identify the applicable statutory standard, the AJ should have analyzed the motion under all of the appropriate Allen criteria. See Chiodo v. OPM, 36 MSPR 479, 482 (1988) (it was appropriate for the AJ to look beyond the parties' pleadings to decide whether the interest of justice requirement was met); Butler v. OPM, 35 MSPR 407, 409 (1987) ("[I]t is appropriate to look beyond the parties' pleadings to decide whether the interest of justice requirement has been met because that determination is primarily controlled by the findings of the prior Board decision.... [I]t was proper for the administrative judge to analyze the appellant's attorney fee motion under all the criteria set forth in Allen..."). One appellant claimed that fees should be awarded under the substantial innocence test, and the government countered in court that the particular issue was not raised before the Board and therefore could not be argued in court. The court concluded, however, that by filing a motion for attorney fees, the appellant asserted that each of the statutory requirements of 5 USC 7701(g)(1), as delineated in Allen, have been met. The court went on to state that an appellant seeking fees should file a memo in support of a fee motion setting out all the considerations relied upon to demonstrate that the statutory requirements for an award have been met, substantiated by affidavits or other documentary evidence if factual questions must be resolved. Samuel v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 84-1258).

Although the Board requires counsel to state the bases for fee petitions, applying past regulations one case suggested that the AJ has the discretion to award fees on an unstated basis, Madera v. OPM, 53 MSPR 310, 315 (1992):

[A]n administrative judge has substantial discretion with respect to ruling on motions. See 5 CFR 1201.41(b)(8). Levenson v. Defense Logistics Agency, 49 MSPR 659, 664 (1991). An administrative judge may "look beyond" the parties' pleadings where, as here, a basis for granting the fee motion is apparent without extensive review of the evidence....

Under Madera, if the AJ raises a theory of recovery not raised by the appellant, the agency ought to be given a chance to respond. If this does not occur, the agency can raise its arguments to the Board on a PFR. Chairman Levinson dissented in Madera, 53 MSPR at 317, arguing:

Agencies cannot be expected to frame a response to motions that do not explain the basis for entitlement. To respond to such a motion, agencies would have to undertake the impossible burden of raising and countering every possible justification that might occur to the administrative judge. This they are not required to do. The burden for establishing entitlement to fees falls on the appellant, not the agency or the administrative judge.

The Board followed Madera in Babakitis v. OPM, 60 MSPR 35, 36-37 (1993):

[W]here an "appellant did not expressly allege that attorney fees were warranted in the interest of justice, and did not identify a specific basis for such finding," it may still look beyond the appellant's pleadings and award fees where a basis for granting the fee motion is apparent without extensive review of the record. See Madera v. Office of Personnel Management, 53 MSPR 310, 315 (1992).

See Pacheco v. OPM, 68 MSPR 129, 133 (1995) (same holding as Babakitis); DiGiulio v. Dept. of Treasury, 66 MSPR 659, 667 n.3 (1995) ("The Board may look beyond the parties' pleadings where an alternative basis for granting the fee motion is apparent without extensive review of the evidence."); Dunn v. USPS, 49 MSPR 144, 147 n.* (1991) (awarding fees under the "knew or should have known" criterion when the appellant argued that the agency's penalty was "grossly excessive": "We find that this argument is sufficient under 5 CFR 1201.37(a)(3) [1991] to raise the issue of whether the agency 'knew or should have known' that its removal penalty would not be upheld on appeal. The Board looks to the substance of the appellant's arguments, not to the labels that he may attach to them.").
