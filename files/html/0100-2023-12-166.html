8. Procedure on Late Submission

The regulation on late filings provides, 5 CFR 1201.114(g) (2022):

Any pleading described in paragraph (a) of this section that is filed late must be accompanied by a motion that shows good cause for the untimely filing, unless the Board has specifically granted an extension of time under paragraph (f) of this section, or unless a motion for extension is pending before the Board. The motion must be accompanied by an affidavit or sworn statement under 28 U.S.C. 1746. (See Appendix IV.) The affidavit or sworn statement must include: The reasons for failing to request an extension before the deadline for the submission, and a specific and detailed description of the circumstances causing the late filing, accompanied by supporting documentation or other evidence. Any response to the motion may be included in the response to the petition for review, the cross petition for review, or the response to the cross petition for review. The response will not extend the time provided by paragraph (e) of this section to file a cross petition for review or to respond to the petition or cross petition. In the absence of a motion, the Board may, in its discretion, determine on the basis of the existing record whether there was good cause for the untimely filing, or it may provide the party that submitted the document with an opportunity to show why it should not be dismissed or excluded as untimely.

After a PFR is filed, the Board's Clerk generally sends out a letter acknowledging the PFR, and if the PFR is late, the letter from the Clerk may call on the filing party to explain, that is, show good cause for, the delay and state generally in what form the explanation should be provided. But the correspondence from the Clerk is not required by regulation and its absence does not provide good cause for the belated PFR, Barnes v. DOD, DC-0752-13-0357-I-1 (NP 9/3/2014):

Additionally, the Clerk of the Board's acknowledgment letter did not address the timeliness issue, and normal processing of the petition continued, possibly suggesting that the appellant had shown good cause for the untimely submission.[4] Conversely, the Clerk did not issue an order granting the appellant's motion.

[4] In another context, however, our reviewing court has explained that a communication from the Clerk of the Board does not constitute a final decision of the Board. See McCormack v. Merit Systems Protection Board, No. 91-3547, 1994 WL 746298, at *2 (Fed. Cir. June 3, 1994); see also Vaughn v. Department of the Treasury, 119 M.S.P.R. 605, ¶ 8 (2013) (although the Clerk of the Board customarily issues a letter to the parties acknowledging the receipt of the petition for review, this letter is not required by law, rule, or regulation; it is issued as a courtesy to the parties and to facilitate an orderly adjudication on review).

The Board is more likely to grant a reasonable extension request than excuse a belated filing. Many petitions for review are filed late without an extension request, and the Board dismisses many of those petitions as untimely. In 1988 the Board reminded parties of what the Board then described as its adjudicatory rule that "[i]n the interest of judicial efficiency and fairness, regardless of how minimal the delay, the Board will not waive its timeliness requirements in the absence of a showing of good cause." Cayabyab v. OPM, 38 MSPR 469, 471 (1988); see Bass v. Dept. of Air Force, 66 MSPR 425, 427 (1995) (stating the rule). That rather Draconian rule has been relaxed to a degree in recent years, but to avoid dismissal there must be presented good cause justifying the delay in filing. "Silence does not constitute a showing of good cause for the delay." Feltz v. VA, 68 MSPR 263, 265 (1995); see Lanes v. OPM, 43 MSPR 364, 365 (1990) (denying unexplained motion to waive the time limit for filing PFR).

There must be a showing of good cause to justify any late filing, whether it is made in support of an extension request or whether it is made to justify a late submission not preceded by an extension request. Golden v. Dept. of Navy, 20 MSPR 185, 187 (1984); see Smith v. Dept. of Army, 105 MSPR 433, 2007 MSPB 104 (2007) (rejecting a PFR late by a day from a pro se appellant who failed to respond to the Clerk's notice directing that the appellant show cause for the filing delay); May v. USPS, 5 MSPR 552, 553, 6 MSPB 13 (1981) (appellant requested an extension that was not supported by good cause and the appellant then filed a late PFR and failed to supply a showing of good cause in support of the late filing). The Board's show cause order should be reasonably calculated to explain to an appellant what he needs to establish to show that his PFR is timely. If, for example, the timeliness of the PFR depends on the date that the appellant received the initial decision, the show cause order should inform the appellant that he must explain when he received the initial decision. See Griffin v. MSPB, 163 Fed. Appx. 895, 897-98 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP) (considering the various factual scenarios of how the PFR filing deadline might have been met, depending on when the appellant received the initial decision, and remanding the case to the Board to obtain and analyze information on the date of the appellant's receipt of the initial decision). The response to the Clerk's request for a showing of good cause for a late PFR should be filed on time, although the Board will consider explanations filed after the time allotted for the submission if good cause is shown. Wallace v. OPM, 37 MSPR 431, 433 (1988). [Refer to Chapter 2 concerning treatment of untimely appeals.]

Note the requirement of sworn statements to secure waiver of the time limit for a belated PFR. Many cases throughout this discussion reject belated PFRs accompanied by unsworn statements asserting facts purportedly providing an excuse for the delayed filing. If the appellant provides an explanation for an untimely filing, and if the appellant then fails to provide a supporting sworn statement in response to the Board's PFR acknowledgment letter requiring the statement, the PFR will be dismissed for failure to show good cause for the delayed PFR.

Many decisions that follow discuss many reasons considered by the Board to excuse, or not excuse, untimely PFRs. Summarized, the factors applied by the Board were noted, with citation to Morman v. Dept. of Army, 68 MSPR 60 (1995) (involving a late appeal), Smith v. OPM, DC-0845-10-0323-I-1 (NP 3/17/2011):

...To establish good cause, a party must show that she exercised due diligence or ordinary prudence under the particular circumstances of the case. Alonzo v. Department of the Air Force, 4 M.S.P.R. 180, 184 (1980). To determine whether a party has shown good cause, the Board will consider the length of the delay, the reasonableness of her excuse and her showing of due diligence, whether she is proceeding pro se, and whether she has presented evidence of the existence of circumstances beyond her control that affected her ability to comply with the time limits or of unavoidable casualty or misfortune which similarly shows a causal relationship to her inability to timely file her petition. Moorman v. Department of the Army, 68 M.S.P.R. 60, 62-63 (1995), aff'd, 79 F.3d 1167 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table).

The Board emphasized the need for compliance with its regulation governing waiver of the PFR time limit, Gonzales v. DOJ, 70 MSPR 648, 650-51 (1996):

These reasons do not constitute good cause for the untimely filing of the agency's petition for review. The Board takes official notice of the partial government shutdown from December 18, 1995 to January 8, 1996 and of the closure of Federal government offices in the Washington, D.C. area on January 8-10, and January 12, 1996, due to snow. 5 CFR § 1201.64. However, the agency's representative admits in her declaration that she was at work on January 11, several days before the petition for review was due. The Board's regulations provide that an untimely petition for review must be accompanied by a sworn statement or affidavit showing good cause for the untimely filing, and that the affidavit or sworn statement "must include: (1) The reason for failing to request an extension before the deadline for the submission...." 5 CFR § 1201.114(f)(1). The agency's representative has not explained why she could not contact the Clerk of the Board on January 11 to request an extension. Moreover, the agency's representative has not explained why she could not have requested an extension on January 16, the date the petition for review was due. In fact, there is no evidence that the agency's representative ever sought an extension of time either before or after the deadline, until she actually filed the petition for review. See Averella v. USPS, 64 MSPR 441, 443 (1994).

Accordingly, we find that the agency has failed to show that it exercised due diligence or ordinary prudence and, therefore, it has not shown good cause for the untimely filing of its petition for review. Thus, we dismiss the agency's petition for review as untimely filed....

It was the practice that if a PFR appeared to be filed late, the Board asked for a submission from the appellant and representative demonstrating good cause for the late petition. The Clerk now may send out a form, "Motion to Accept Filing as Timely or to Waive the Time Limit," allowing the appellant to show good cause for the late PFR. See, e.g., Ollado v. OPM, 98 MSPR 618, 619 ¶ 3 (2005). If no response is provided, the PFR is dismissed as untimely. See Smith v. DOD, 89 MSPR 20, 22 ¶ 5 (2001) ("Even construing appellant's asserted newly-discovered evidence as relating to the merits of his appeal, such a merits claim is inapposite to the issue of timeliness."); De Vero v. OPM, 75 MSPR 12, 14 (1997) (with no explanation for a delay of two months in filing a PFR; it was dismissed as untimely); Brown v. Dept. of Navy, 71 MSPR 19, 21 (1996) (noting the absence of a response to the show cause order and also noting a lack of grounds in an untimely PFR for waiver of the deadline); Sufrir v. OPM, 70 MSPR 528, 530 (1996) (if the appellant's response to a show cause order on timeliness addresses only the merits of a PFR and not the timeliness issue, "[s]uch a response does not establish good cause for the filing delay and thus warrants dismissal of the appellant's petition"); Schmid v. Dept. of Army, 29 MSPR 31, 32 (1985) (PFR was dismissed when the appellant failed to respond to the Board's order directing him to show cause why the petition was not untimely); Burnett v. OPM, 25 MSPR 450, 451 (1984) (insufficient response to a show cause order for the appellant to request "help" but include no explanation for the late PFR); Green v. DHEW, 2 MSPR 351, 2 MSPB 513 (1979) (neither the appellant nor the representative responded to the Board's show cause order).

If the response to the show cause order is late, and no good cause excuses the delay, the Board need not consider it. See Dull v. Dept. of Navy, 76 MSPR 31, 34 (1997). One decision seemed to suggest that an appellant is supposed to file a motion for waiver of the time limits for a PFR if the Clerk directed the appellant to show cause why a late petition should be accepted. Jones v. OPM, 41 MSPR 146, 148-49 (1989). That requirement--if that is what the decision held--is unnecessary: if the party explains the lateness, then the explanation should be sufficient to meet the Board's procedural needs without the formality of a motion. If the Board directs the appellant to show cause why his PFR should not be dismissed as untimely, subsequent correspondence from the appellant to the Board will not be considered sufficient if it does not respond to the order. Williams v. USPS, 29 MSPR 165, 166 (1985). A party responding to a motion for waiver of a time limit may provide evidence in support of its position. When an appellant sought to show that her medical condition precluded her from filing a timely PFR, OPM responded with its own evidence suggesting that the appellant was not as impaired as she claimed. Chaney v. OPM, 43 MSPR 276, 280 (1990).

The same rules apply to dismissals based on the failure to timely perfect through refiling a deficient PFR that was originally timely filed. If the appellant claims that he timely refiled, when the Board has no record of receiving the refiled PFR, the appellant ought to specifically state on what date he refiled, or produce evidence concerning the allegedly timely refiling, or show other due diligence relative to the processing of the refiled PFR. See Robinson v. OPM, 56 MSPR 325, 327 (1993).

On occasion the Board may decide not to rule on a timeliness issue if there is another clear basis for denial of a PFR, e.g., the failure of the appellant to demonstrate the existence of new evidence or error by the AJ sufficient to set warrant reconsideration of the initial decision. See Shauf v. DOD, 83 MSPR 384 (Table) (1999).
