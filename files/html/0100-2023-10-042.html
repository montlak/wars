5. Employees Who Administer Title VII of the CSRA; 5 USC 7112(b)(4)

5 USC 7112(b)(4) renders a unit inappropriate if it includes employees engaged in administering the provisions of Chapter 71 of Title 5, U.S. Code, the Federal Service Labor-Management Relations Statute (FSLMRS). Mediators employed by the Federal Mediation and Conciliation Service did not constitute an appropriate unit because of the statutory exclusion. FMCS, Region 7 and Council of Fed. Mediators, Region 7, 3 FLRA 138, 139 (1980), followed in FMCS and FMCS Ass'n of Fed. Mediators, 52 FLRA 1509, 1514-15 (1997). Labor Department lawyers who could be called on to administer the standards of conduct for labor organizations under the Reform Act were not appropriate for inclusion in a unit. Dept. of Labor, Office of the Solicitor and Nat'l Council of Field Labor Locals, 8 FLRA 286, 287-88 (1982). Investigators of the Department of Labor Office of Pension and Welfare Benefits Programs, who investigated officials of federal sector unions and who administered statutes relating to labor-management relations within 5 USC 7112(c), could not be included in units represented by federal sector unions. Dept. of Labor and Nat'l Council of Field Labor Locals, AFGE, 23 FLRA 464, 471 (1986); see Dept. of Labor, Pension and Welfare Benefits Admin. and Nat'l Union of Compliance Officers, Indep., 30 FLRA 1229, 1234 (1988) (under § 7112, union cannot represent unit excluded from § 7120 (standards of conduct for labor organizations) and concurrently represent unit subject to § 7120).

The exclusion of employees who administer labor relations provisions of the law will be reasonably and realistically construed, as was explained in Dept. of Labor, Pension and Welfare Benefits Admin. and Nat'l Union of Pension Investigators and Auditors, 38 FLRA 65, 71-72 (1990), approving a unit that would include, among others, Pension and Welfare field investigators and auditors who enforce ERISA:

[T]he purposes of ERISA are to regulate and investigate the funding and administration of employee benefit plans.

Consistent with the foregoing, we find that ERISA is not a "law relating to labor-management relations" within the meaning of section 7112(c). It is clear that accomplishing the purposes of ERISA may involve the regulation and investigation of labor union officials. That fact, however, does not mean that ERISA is a law relating to labor-management relations. In our view, a law relates to labor-management relations when the law is intended to affect or regulate, in some way, the collective bargaining process or other matters directly affecting the labor-management relationship.

Labor unions as institutions, and labor union officials in their individual and official capacities, are subject to many laws and regulations, including laws pertaining to labor unions as employers. To hold that ERISA constitutes a law relating to labor-management relations solely because it applies to labor unions and their officials would require the same result with respect to such laws as those pertaining to discrimination in employment, wage and hour standards, contributions to political parties and candidates, and payment of taxes, among others. We decline to read the Statute in a manner which would lead to such an incongruous result.
