13. Agency Bad Faith, Provocation, Manipulation; Improper Pressure on Appellant; Entrapment

Bias of a proposing official is not a factor that should be analyzed under the harmful error standard; it is a factor going toward whether the penalty imposed by the agency is reasonable under Douglas. Coons v. Dept. of Navy, 15 MSPR 1, 3 (1983). [There are cases reaching an opposite result discussed in Chapter 11, "Deciding Official Who Is Biased, Whose Decision Is Directed, Who Lacks Discretion."] In Coons, the proposing official maligned the appellant, opposed his initial appointment, attempted to have the appellant transferred out of the department ten months before the proposed removal, and was not considered a good manager. The Board determined that it would reevaluate the penalty, because the agency deciding official accepted the recommendation of the proposing official without critical evaluation.

The degree of fault and bad faith on the part of the agency in proposing the adverse action is complicated by the need to know at what point the bad faith must occur to affect the decision to remove the employee. The Board suggested, but did not explicitly determine, that a deciding official can purify an action that was proposed with a degree of ill will. See Kling v. DOJ, 2 MSPR 464, 470 n.4, 2 MSPB 620 (1980). The AJ concluded that the action was proposed in bad faith, reversed the action, and awarded counsel fees based on the Allen standards involving bad faith. The Board remanded the case, noting that there must be more in the record than differences between the appellant and the proposing official to support a finding of bad faith. On remand the Board directed the AJ to consider the effect of the ill will of the proposing official, i.e., to determine whether that official sought to influence the deciding official who made the removal decision, and to determine whether that deciding official had before him a fair and complete summary of the evidence favorable to appellant.

In Webster v. Dept. of Army, 911 F.2d 679 (Fed. Cir. 1990), a dissenting opinion suggested that a deciding official ought to consider the animus of a proposing official in determining whether to sustain or mitigate a penalty. See House v. USPS, 80 MSPR 138, 143-44 ¶¶ 13-15 (1998) (mitigating a penalty for falsification of time entry; the deciding official, who investigated at least one underlying specification, may have been predisposed against the appellant based upon the intensity of that official's efforts to obtain evidence against the appellant) (citing Svejda v. Dept. of Interior, 7 MSPR 108, 111-12, 7 MSPB 36 (1981)). [Refer to Chapter 11, "Sullivan; Regulatory Prohibitions; Adverse External Influences" and "Deciding Official Who Is Biased, Whose Decision Is Directed, Who Lacks Discretion"; refer to Chapter 13, "Significance of Retaliatory Investigation; Appellant Initiates Investigation; Marano" and "Whistleblowing Reprisal," discussing motivation in whistleblower reprisal cases; refer to Chapter 12, "Prima Facie Case; Accommodation Requirements," as to motivation in EEO affirmative defenses.]

Provocation is not a defense to a charge, but it is a factor to be considered in assessing a penalty. Kokkinis v. VA, 81 MSPR 26, 28 ¶ 6 (1998); see Gill v. DOD, 92 MSPR 23, 34 ¶ 27 (2002) (penalty for a charge of disrespectful conduct was mitigated by a supervisor contributing to the conflict by raising his voice and failing to conduct himself in an appropriate manner). As to a clerk-typist whose removal for poor performance was mitigated to a demotion on a finding by the AJ that the appellant's supervisor had harassed and harangued her and made unreasonable determinations that her work was unacceptable, the Board modified the result from a demotion to full reinstatement to the original position or to a position of the same grade and pay in the same geographical area. Peterson v. Dept. of Energy, 6 MSPR 728, 731, 6 MSPB 608 (1981). In a case brought in federal district court, an employee was reinstated whose job performance suffered because of anti-Semitic remarks and harassment by the supervisor after the employee complained of discriminatory treatment and remarks. The court decided that an employer cannot use an employee's diminished work performance as a legitimate basis for removal if the diminution is the direct result of the employer's discriminatory behavior. Nor could agency managers avoid responsibility for the employee's problems once they were alerted to the discriminatory practices: the employer, once on notice of the practices, was required to do more than announce the existence of official policy against discrimination. Weiss v. United States, 595 F. Supp. 1050 (E.D. Va. 1984). After an employee was removed for a poor attendance record and refusal to submit to a psychiatric fitness for duty examination, the employee's defense that his supervisor was harassing him was deemed irrelevant without an allegation of a prohibited personnel practice under 5 USC 2302(b)(10). Hodgson v. Dept. of Air Force, 20 MSPR 541, 544 (1984). The Hodgson analysis is inconsistent with the mitigation factors enumerated in Douglas; mitigation does not depend on proof of a prohibited personnel practice. In another appeal there was no demonstration that claimed harassment caused the conduct leading to the appellant's discipline. Pope v. Dept. of Navy, 44 MSPR 289, 294 (1990).

Associated with bad faith is pressure on an employee to take an action that he otherwise would not. Removal was mitigated to a 90-day suspension because the appellant, a construction specialist, was pressured by management to violate regulations concerning benefits to DHUD grantees in order to mollify the grantee and bring to an end a controversy over the grantee's case. Sakellairos v. DHUD, 22 MSPR 59, 63 (1984); see Rose v. DHUD, 26 MSPR 356, 360 (1985) (it would be grossly inequitable to suspend an employee for responding to a supervisor's directive, regardless of the employee's perception of the propriety of the order). Although discipline was imposed, a consideration in mitigation was pressure the appellant was under to find some way to satisfy the desire of a superior to appoint a politically well-connected applicant for employment. When the means of appointment chosen were incorrect, discipline was imposed because the employee had sufficient expertise to recognize the error in the appointment process and to discuss it with management. Special Counsel v. Byrd, 59 MSPR 561, 583 (1993); cf. Miller v. Dept. of Navy, 27 MSPR 227, 231 (1985) (mitigating to a reprimand suspension of a ship's officer who failed to make log entries when the evidence showed that the captain ordered that no log entry be made). But see Black v. Dept. of Air Force, 29 MSPR 133, 137 (1985) (meat cutter foreman was removed for the falsification of inventory; although actions were performed at the behest of a supervisor, the appellant's actions were for "his personal benefit in that he gained favor with his supervisor and bolstered his own managerial reputation."); Pitts v. Dept. of Air Force, 29 MSPR 108, 114 (1985) (sustaining removal of a meat cutter foreman charged with falsifying inventory reports, resulting in losses exceeding $19,000; setting aside a mitigation directed by the AJ and avoiding discussion of the AJ's finding that the appellant acted at the direction of a superior and out of fear for his job).

Without becoming involved in an analysis of bad faith, the Board may examine seemingly arbitrary practices of an agency that impact penalty selection. If the agency selects a penalty following the appellant's adverse action reply, then cancels the decision, appoints a new deciding official, and issues a more severe penalty, the original and lesser penalty may be considered as a point of mitigation if the agency fails properly to explain the necessity for the switch in deciding officials. See Rose v. Dept. of Navy, 31 MSPR 232, 235-38 (1986). [Refer to Chapter 11, "Changes in Proposing, Deciding Official," and "Lack of Authority."]

If an appellant is involved in conduct involving contact with the police and he defends his conduct on the basis that he was entrapped, the lack of deterrent effect on police of the entrapment defense raised in administrative proceedings makes the defense unavailable to the appellant in an adverse action case. The Board had said there is nothing wrong with giving an appellant an opportunity to engage in wrongdoing. Middleton v. DOJ, 23 MSPR 223, 226 n.3 (1984) ("In this case, the informant merely afforded appellant the opportunity to engage in wrongdoing and appellant accepted. Hampton v. United States, 425 U.S. 434 (1976). There is no evidence that his conduct was the result of anything other than his own predisposition. [Refer to Chapter 4, "Misconduct of Investigator" and Chapter 11, "Entrapment."]
