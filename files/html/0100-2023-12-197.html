z. Other Problems

Personal problems, e.g., foreclosure of the appellant's home, generally do not sway the Board to waive lengthy delays in filing a PFR. Schwartz v. OPM, 34 MSPR 217, 219 (1987); see Tanter v. Dept. of Interior, DC-0752-19-0755-I-1 (NP 2/28/2023) ("The appellant also argues on review that, due to the COVID-19 pandemic, she was unable to go to the public library to use the computer, printer, or scanner because she is an 'at-risk patient.' We acknowledge that the appellant appears to live in Greenbelt, Maryland, and that, during the early months of the pandemic, including in April and May of 2020, it is possible that public facilities such as libraries were closed to the public in Maryland. However, the appellant has not explained why she was unable to pursue a timely filing by other avenues. For example, she has not explained why she was unable to submit a handwritten petition for review or request for an extension."); Keys v. OPM, 113 MSPR 173, 2010 MSPB 20 (2010) (general assertions of death of sister, illness, stress); Crozier v. Dept. of Transp., 93 MSPR 438 (2003) (duties as a scout leader, back problems, and the appellant's pro se status did not excuse a 13-day delay in filing a PFR); Stewart v. Dept. of Navy, 93 MSPR 147, 149 ¶¶ 4-5 (2002) (delay in filing a PFR was not excused when the appellant's possessions, including case files, were in storage while she conducted a job search); Waldo v. Dept. of Air Force, 93 MSPR 58, 60-62 ¶¶ 6-9 (2002) (time spent on the administration of a relative's estate was insufficient to explain a delay); McCray v. USPS, 92 MSPR 182, 184 ¶ 7 (2002) (assertion insufficient that the appellant was attending to a terminally ill father); Greenberg v. DOJ, 91 MSPR 42, 45 ¶ 5 (2002) ("[H]e has not specified when he was taking care of the sick relative or how that situation or the relative's death prevented him from pursuing his appeal."); Garcia v. OPM, 85 MSPR 576, 578 ¶ 4 (2000) (wife's illness was not good cause for a belated PFR), aff'd Garcia v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 00-3327); Gosey v. Smithsonian Inst., 85 MSPR 342, 344 ¶ 7 (2000) ("[H]e offered no evidence or argument to show that his care-giving responsibilities so occupied his time that he was prevented from filing a timely petition for review or from requesting an extension of time."); Hilton v. Dept. of Army, 78 MSPR 690, 692 (1998) (declining to excuse a lengthy delay caused by the appellant's inability to access records stored at a distant location); Key v. USPS, 77 MSPR 513, 515 (1998) (pendency of a criminal case did not justify a delay when the appellant failed to show that it affected his ability to file a PFR); Alexander v. VA, 76 MSPR 285, 288 (1997) (family and financial difficulties did not justify delay); Crisp v. VA, 73 MSPR 231, 234 (1997) ("general personal difficulties," e.g., divorce proceedings and difficulties in finding new employment and a lawyer, did not provide cause for excusing a late PFR); Peria v. OPM, 69 MSPR 559, 562 (1996) (lengthy delay was not excused by the appellant's explanation that he had been shot and wounded; he did not describe the extent of his wounds and did not identify the period of his incapacitation); Sembach v. OPM, 68 MSPR 199, 201 (1995) ("A general allegation of family difficulties does not provide a basis for waiver of a filing deadline."); Johnson v. USPS, 66 MSPR 604, 608 (1995) (a combination of financial problems, inability to obtain representation, and discouragement at the likely outcome of a PFR did not constitute good cause for a delayed PFR); Ganley v. USPS, 65 MSPR 70, 72 (1994) (late PFR was not excused by frustration, anger, or a lack of funds to employ counsel); Johnson v. VA, 64 MSPR 257, 259-60 (1994) (auto accident and other circumstances separating the appellant from his case records did not justify an untimely PFR); Ganenas v. OPM, 64 MSPR 253, 256 (1994) (no waiver for an appellant who was 84 years old and sickly); Pine v. Dept. of Army, 63 MSPR 381, 383 (1994) (as to "general reference[s] to her mother's illness and the need to care for her...a general claim of family difficulties that does not specifically account for the period of untimeliness does not establish good cause for waiver of the filing deadline"), followed in Virgil v. USPS, 75 MSPR 109, 113 (1997) ("[A] general reference to family difficulties or the need to care for a critically ill family member is not sufficient in itself to justify a delay in filing[.]"). Issues of health or death of family members did not excuse delayed filing of a PFR by amount a month in Lilly v. USPS, CH-0353-16-0244-I-1 (NP 8/25/2022):

The appellant's motion states that the deaths of her aunt and father, the attendant circumstances, and her subsequent grieving were factors in her untimeliness and constitute good cause. She submitted evidence of her father's death on April 22, 2016, id. at 3, which was just under 1 month before the issuance of the initial decision, 2 months before the deadline to file a petition, and 3 months before she ultimately filed her petition for review. While we are sympathetic toward her situation, we find that her explanation does not provide good cause for the delay in filing her petition. See Stephens v. Department of Health & Human Services, 95 M.S.P.R. 600, ¶ 6 (2004) (finding that family difficulties that predated the initial decision did not establish good cause for a 1-month delay in filing a petition for review), aff'd, 128 F. App'x 147 (Fed. Cir. 2005).

Further, the appellant has failed to show that her father's death, while unfortunate, created any circumstances beyond her control that affected her ability to comply with the time limits. See Alonzo v. Department of the Air Force, 4 M.S.P.R. 180, 184 (1980). She did not assert any details, such as whether she had to leave the area, and, if so, when she returned. She also has not explained why no one else could have assumed the duties of making the appropriate arrangements for filing a petition for review in her absence. See Taylor v. U.S. Postal Service, 53 M.S.P.R. 27, 28 (1992). Furthermore, while the appellant's distress from losing family members is understandable, we find that such distress does not itself establish good cause for the 27-day filing delay. See Cunningham v. Department of Transportation, 35 M.S.P.R. 674, 677-78 (1987) (finding that, in the absence of corroborating evidence, an appellant's allegation that he was emotionally upset over a friend's death is insufficient to establish good cause for an untimely filing); Ruoff v. Federal Aviation Administration, 16 M.S.P.R. 249, 250-52 (1983) (finding that the appellant's bare assertion that he was in "serious mental trauma" due to his daughter's death was insufficient to establish good cause for failing to follow orders and timely prosecuting his appeal).

Concerns about the outcome of another person's case do not excuse a belated PFR. See Durio v. VA, CH-3443-15-0224-I-1 (NP 9/21/2015) ("Finally, the appellant's suggestion that his appeal be consolidated with a pending appeal filed by a coworker does not serve to excuse his own late filing. Cf. Carpenter v. Department of Health & Human Services, 40 M.S.P.R. 55, 56 (1989) (holding that the appellant's decision to await developments in another Board action did not constitute good cause for untimely filing of petition for review).").

The Board's analysis of weather-related problems is taken from Griffin v. DOD, 99 MSPR 208, 210-11 ¶ 5 (2005):

The appellant argues that Hurricane Ivan affected his ability to file his PFR on time. The appellant also argues that he believed November 19, 2004, was the filing deadline. The Board has held, however, that a general claim of a natural disaster does not demonstrate good cause to excuse an untimely filed PFR. Corpus v. Office of Personnel Management, 65 M.S.P.R. 315, 317 (1994), appeal dismissed, 52 F.3d 344 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table) (appellant failed to show good cause for an untimely filing based on her statement that her petition was late due to a typhoon, where she did not state with any specificity when the typhoon occurred, or how it caused her to file her petition 12 days after the deadline for filing had passed); Carabeo v. Office of Personnel Management, 52 M.S.P.R. 454, 456 (1992) (while a volcanic eruption that occurred 3 months before the appellant refiled his corrected petition constituted the existence of circumstances beyond his control at that time, he presented no facts to establish good cause for his 2-year filing delay in refiling his corrected petition). Thus, because the appellant has not stated with any specificity how Hurricane Ivan caused him to file his petition 1-month late, we find that his argument fails to show good cause for the filing delay. Corpus, 65 M.S.P.R. at 317; Carabeo, 52 M.S.P.R. at 456.

As to any personal calamity or natural misfortune, if the event falls after the period allowed for filing the PFR, the event will not likely persuade the Board to permit a belated petition without some other cause preventing the PFR from being timely filed. See Menchaca v. USPS, 56 MSPR 479, 483 (1993). A general claim that a natural disaster was responsible for an untimely filed PFR does not show good cause for waiver of the Board's time limit. Corpus v. OPM, 65 MSPR 315, 317 (1994) (appellant did not state with specificity when a typhoon occurred or how it caused her to file her PFR 12 days late) (citing Carabeo v. OPM, 52 MSPR 454, 456 (1992) (volcanic eruption three months before the filing of a corrected PFR did not establish good cause for a two-year delay in refiling the corrected petition)). Duran-Arcelay v. OPM, 70 MSPR 13, 16 (1996), declined to excuse a PFR allegedly delayed by hurricanes:

The appellant's sole explanation for the delay appears to be natural phenomena; to wit, the passage of hurricanes near Puerto Rico. The Board has held that a general claim that a natural disaster was responsible for an untimely filing does not constitute good cause for the delayed filing in the absence of specific evidence relating the occurrence of the natural disaster to the delayed filing. See Corpus v. OPM, 65 MSPR 315, 317 (1994) and cases cited therein. Here, while we note that numerous hurricanes affected the Caribbean this season, there is no showing in the record on petition for review that any storm severely affected Puerto Rico; nor has the appellant specified how he was prevented from timely filing his August 15 and September 9 filings with the Board.

The Board can show compassion. A missed filing date was excused for one day because on "the date on which the response was due, the agency was forced to evacuate its office in Chicago because a tunnel beneath the city gave way and flooded the city with water from the Chicago River, resulting in a loss of electrical power to the agency's office." Del Marcelle v. Dept. of Treasury, 59 MSPR 251, 253 n.1 (1993). But see Walton v. USPS, 54 MSPR 510, 512 (1992) (Great Chicago Flood, occurring 20 days after the filing deadline, did not prevent the appellant from providing the initial decision to his counsel prior to the deadline or from filing his own PFR).

An ice storm causing power losses was insufficient reason to excuse a delayed PFR when the appellant should have been aware of the need for a PFR and did not show he could not use a means other than electronic filing, such as fax or mail or commercial delivery. Rivera v. SSA, 111 MSPR 581, 2009 MSPB 127 (2009); see Taylor v. USPS, DA-0752-14-0106-I-1 (NP 8/6/2014) ("Furthermore, in the absence of specific evidence relating the occurrence of the natural disaster to the delay, a general claim that a natural disaster was responsible for an untimely filing does not constitute good cause for a delayed filing. See Duran-Arcelay v. Office of Personnel Management, 70 M.S.P.R. 13, 16 (1996) (generally asserting that a hurricane was the cause for the appellant's untimely filing of his petition for review was insufficient to show good cause where he failed to state with any specificity how it caused the delay); Corpus v. Office of Personnel Management, 65 M.S.P.R. 315, 316-17 (1994) (asserting recurrence of a weekly typhoon and loss of electricity was insufficient to show good cause for an untimely petition for review where the appellant failed to specify when the typhoon occurred or how these conditions affected her ability to timely file her petition).").

Other problems surfaced but did not convince the Board to waive its PFR deadline, McDonald v. USPS, 109 MSPR 100, 102 ¶ 6, 2008 MSPB 123 (2008):

The fact that he has filed claims before other fora does not excuse the lateness. See Janini v. Department of Labor, 106 M.S.P.R. 111, ¶ 7 (2007). Also, his money problems are insufficient reason to excuse the delay. See Robey v. U.S. Postal Service, 105 M.S.P.R. 539, ¶ 16, aff'd, 253 Fed. Appx. 933 (Fed. Cir. 2007) (NP). Likewise, "vehicle problems" do not explain why he could not file a petition for review before more than 8 months had elapsed. See, e.g., Laboy v. U.S. Postal Service, 103 M.S.P.R. 570, ¶¶ 8-9 (2006) (the appellant failed to show good cause based on her representative's automobile accident); Davis v. Department of the Treasury, 95 M.S.P.R. 207, ¶ 6 (2003) (the appellant's assertions that trauma suffered as the result of being robbed at a bus stop and that his typewriter broke down did not provide a basis for waiving the Board's time limit).

Varied personal problems failed to excuse a late PFR. See Anderson v. Dept. of Navy, DA-0752-13-0106-I-1 (NP 11/2/2022) ("Similarly, her attempt to obtain assistance from other agencies, rather than simply following the straightforward instructions she was provided, militates against a finding of due diligence. Agbenyeke v. Department of Justice, 111 M.S.P.R. 140, ¶ 8 (2009) (stating that the appellant's failure to contact the Board to attempt to remedy her confusion weighed against a finding of good cause); Johnson v. Department of the Air Force, 92 M.S.P.R. 370, ¶ 10 (2002) (deciding to pursue claims in another forum did not show good cause)."); Anderson v. Dept. of Treasury, DE-315H-15-0003-I-1 (NP 4/15/2022) (destruction of cellphone and data it contained; substantial delay between recovery of data and PFR); Mitchell v. USPS, 111 MSPR 346, 348 ¶ 7, 2009 MSPB 93 (2009) (pursuit of unemployment compensation did not excuse a delay of three months in filing a PFR) (aff'd, Mitchell v. MSPB, 361 Fed. Appx. 132 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP) (also noting child care problems, home eviction, utilities termination)); Vaughn v. OPM, 102 MSPR 1, 3 ¶ 7, 2006 MSPB 97 (2006) (being out of the country did not excuse the late PFR; nor did pursuit of a remedy in another forum); Johnson v. DHHS, 98 MSPR 1, 3 ¶ 6 (2004) (delay of two years in filing PFR was not excused because the appellant "was attempting to correct his employment situation through other means"); Smith v. USDA, 61 MSPR 309, 312 (1994) (a one-day delay was not excused because "the appellant may have been working long hours during the filing period"); Williams v. OPM, 59 MSPR 399, 401 (1993) ("[G]eneral family difficulties do not constitute good cause for a waiver of the filing deadline."; loss of the inquiries from the Board's Clerk did not excuse prolonged delays in filing responses to those requests for information about a late PFR); Grant v. DOD, 59 MSPR 386, 388-89 (1993) (eight-month delay was not excused by poor financial condition, inability to find employment, repossession of car, confusion, and difficulties in communicating with the Board by reason of a lack of telephone); Purk v. NASA, 58 MSPR 139, 141 (1993) (delay of a day was not excused by the assertion that the appellant was "under 'pressure' to find work following his wife's hospitalization"; the appellant did not explain how his having undertaken a job search prevented him from making a timely filing); Fields v. USPS, 56 MSPR 78, 80-81 (1992) (general financial and family difficulties, and a belated recognition of grounds for a PFR, do not constitute good cause for a waiver of the deadline for filing a PFR); Pedrajas v. OPM, 55 MSPR 667, 669-70 (1992) (delay was not excused by financial circumstances, stress, and failure to understand the complexities of MSPB law); Suclan v. OPM, 51 MSPR 360, 361 (1991) (delay not excused by claimed financial distress and inability to hire an attorney); Sweeting v. DOJ, 48 MSPR 36, 38 (1991) (delay of years was not excused because the appellant lost "contact" with the Board); Carreon v. OPM, 46 MSPR 16, 19 (1990) (lack of finances does not constitute good cause for an untimely filing); Taganas v. USPS, 45 MSPR 244, 246 (1990) (delay was not excused by reason of inability to find a job or the foreclosure of a home); Parker v. USPS, 44 MSPR 437, 439 (1990) (Board refused to waive the filing deadline for an appellant who delayed filing a PFR while attempting to secure money to hire a lawyer through the distribution of retirement funds from an agency that he maintained had prevented him from accessing those funds while any grievance or appeal was pending); Harrell v. VA, 43 MSPR 281, 284 (1990) (delay was not excused by the inability to pay for certified mail; the Clerk's notice and the Board regulations did not require certified mail); Bryant v. VA, 40 MSPR 4, 6-7 (1989) (separation from paperwork through the theft of a briefcase containing the PFR was an insufficient explanation for delay when the waiver motion was unsigned, undated, and submitted without a sworn statement, without a service certificate, and without supporting evidence); Bacalso v. OPM, 27 MSPR 1, 1 n.1 (1985) (transportation strike causing a foreign mail delay excused a PFR delay); Graham v. OPM, 26 MSPR 586, 587-88 (1985) (the illness of a spouse did not justify a seven-month delay in the PFR).

Failure to appreciate the financial consequences of retirement (until retirement) did not provide cause for a late PFR challenging a settlement agreement that led to the retirement. Chiarella v. USPS, 41 MSPR 16, 19 (1989). A combination of factors--stress, depression, confusion, inability to obtain assistance from a union representative, attempts to remain sober, difficulties in obtaining pertinent documents--did not sway the Board to excuse a late PFR in Holt v. USPS, 36 MSPR 240, 243 (1988); Reaves v. VA, 92 MSPR 352, 354 ¶ 7 (2002) (inadequacy of union representation did not excuse an 18 day delay in the filing of a PFR); see Kinan v. DOD, 89 MSPR 407, 410 ¶ 6 (2001) (vague statement concerning "difficulty and hardship" as a result of the initial decision did not justify a five-month delay in the PFR); Nemoga v. USPS, 48 MSPR 439, 440 (1991) (another personnel action against the appellant and disability retirement did not explain the belated corrected PFR); cf. Sykes v. DOJ, 19 MSPR 565, 567 (1984) (if an employee has a back pay problem tied to the validity of an initial decision involving an indefinite suspension, the employee should not continue pursuit of the issue with the agency once the employee is aware that the agency is not going to award back pay; instead, the employee should timely file a PFR).

The appellant's delay in filing a PFR from an initial decision dismissing his appeal as moot was not excused because the appellant was waiting for the agency to complete the actions required to make him whole, Randazzo v. USPS, 108 MSPR 258, 260 ¶ 9, 2008 MSPB 47 (2008):

We further note that this reasoning is consistent with other precedent finding that an appellant's decision to wait for various events to unfold does not constitute good cause for a filing delay. For example, an appellant's decision to wait for a determination on his grievance before filing his petition for review does not constitute good cause to waive the regulatory time limit for filing a petition for review. See Eaglehart v. U.S. Postal Service, 102 M.S.P.R. 672, ¶ 9 (2006). Similarly, an appellant's decision to wait for a decision by the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission before filing a petition for review, and a delay to determine whether the agency followed required procedures, did not constitute good cause to waive the regulatory time limit for an untimely petition for review. See Zamot v. U.S. Postal Service, 91 M.S.P.R. 475, ¶ 6 (2002), aff'd, 332 F.3d 1374 (Fed. Cir. 2003); Williamson v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 61 M.S.P.R. 66, 68-69 (1994)....

Cf. Kroeger v. USPS, 112 MSPR 488, 491 ¶ 7, 2009 MSPB 208 (2009) ("[W]e find that the appellant's argument that the agency was uncooperative in proceedings on his removal does not show circumstances beyond his control or unavoidable casualty or misfortune that would constitute good cause for his failure to timely file a PFR regarding his suspension.").

Irresoluteness may engender but not excuse delay. The Board declined to extend by six and one-half years the period to file a PFR from an initial decision accepting a settlement agreement resolving a removal. The appellant's explanation was that he had taken six years to "make up his mind to appeal...[and had] been trying to forget the Postal Service job, but [he] can't." Young v. USPS, 34 MSPR 594, 596 (1987); see Mavritte v. OPM, 10 MSPR 268, 270 (1982) (the appellant's distrust of the MSPB or OPM does not constitute a reasonable excuse for the delay in a PFR); cf. Holley v. USPS, 97 MSPR 20, 24 ¶¶ 10-11 (2004) (appellant's challenge to the validity of a settlement, including his assertion that he was mentally unable to understand or agree to its terms, did not establish cause for waiving the PFR deadline when medical evidence did not demonstrate that the appellant was unaware of his right to file a PFR). An election not to pursue further remedies because of a belief that further review would be fruitless does not constitute a showing of good cause for the delayed filing of a PFR. Zamot v. MSPB, 332 F.3d 1374, 1378 (Fed. Cir. 2003). Unsuccessful attempts to work a problem out with an agency did not establish good cause for a delay of five months in filing a PFR. Carriker v. OPM, 106 MSPR 516, 518 ¶ 7, 2007 MSPB 206 (2007).

Discouragement and low expectations do not justify delay. See Murphy v. DHHS, 73 MSPR 287, 289 (1997) (neither discouragement about the review process nor a belief that the AJ was biased served to excuse a belated PFR); Marasco v. USPS, 66 MSPR 555, 558 (1995) (neither discouragement at the likely outcome of the PFR nor the issuance of a precedential decision after the time allowed for the PFR constitute good cause for waiving the time limit); Bellinger v. USPS, 65 MSPR 318, 320 (1994) (citing Devine v. USPS, 64 MSPR 289, 291 (1994) (appellant's discouragement at the likely outcome of a PFR did not establish good cause for late filing; the discovery of additional legal arguments, or the issuance of a precedential decision after the time period for filing the PFR expired, did not justify waiving the time limit; decisions issued well before the appellant's PFR established the impropriety of Postal Service restructuring decisions)); Devine v. USPS, 64 MSPR 289, 291-92 (1994) (discouragement of the appellant at the likely outcome of a PFR does not establish good cause for a belated filing; the discovery of an additional legal argument, or the issuance of a precedential decision after the time for filing a PFR has expired, does not by itself constitute good cause for waiving time limits; good cause for waiver is not present if an appellant who is aware of his right to appeal, chooses to not exercise that right until he learns that someone else has successfully pursued an argument he could have made himself); Somosky v. DOD, 56 MSPR 665, 667 (1993) (pessimism as to the likely outcome of a PFR did not excuse its untimeliness). Delay was also not excused because the process of appealing to the Board's regional office was an ordeal and the result of that appeal discouraged the appellant. Nuss v. OPM, 45 MSPR 247, 248 (1990); see Heard v. USDA, 90 MSPR 402, 404 ¶ 5 (2001) (delay was not excused by nonspecific charges of agency harassment); Wells v. Dept. of Treasury, 86 MSPR 683, 686 ¶ 9 (2000) ("The appellant's admission that 'he elected to let the matter die and just put it behind me' similarly does not demonstrate good cause for the delay."); Dungan v. USPS, 79 MSPR 249, 251 ¶ 5 (1998) (delay was not excused because the appellant believed that the Board might be biased against him); Dooley v. Dept. of Air Force, 57 MSPR 684, 690 (1993) (as to the appellant's concern about the complexity of his case: "If the appellant thought that prosecuting a petition for review was beyond his competence, he was free to retain an attorney to represent him[.]"); Hunt v. Dept. of Air Force, 27 MSPR 14, 15 (1985) (late PFR was not excused when the "attorney whom she consulted about her case told her nothing could be done").

Disruptions caused by a funeral for a staff member did not provide sufficient cause for a late PFR when the decedent had no connection to the processing of the appeal. Elvrum v. OPM, 41 MSPR 545, 546 (1989); see Broyles v. OPM, 90 MSPR 297, 299-300 ¶ 6 (2001) (death of a spouse did not excuse a four-month delay in filing a PFR from a survivor annuity appeal); Estate of DePalermo v. OPM, 53 MSPR 4, 6 (1992) (lateness was not excused by distress caused by the death of a father two years before the initial decision became final); Moles v. OPM, 43 MSPR 89, 90 (1989) (family death was not a reason for delay without further specifics). A group of personal problems, including health care requirements for the appellant's father, was insufficient to excuse a PFR delayed beyond an extension granted by the Board. Stephens v. DHHS, 95 MSPR 600, 603 ¶ 6 (2004).

The Board found itself unable to excuse a late PFR filed by an appellant who said that she misread the deadline because she was wearing an old pair of eyeglasses when she first read the Board's notice. Mata v. Dept. of Army, 43 MSPR 250, 252-53 (1990); see Barreyro v. OPM, 56 MSPR 623, 625 (1993) (delay was not excused on the basis of a statement that the appellant is "76 years old, lives alone, and receives the equivalent of $45 a month in income"); Facal v. OPM, 43 MSPR 294, 295 (1990) (late PFR was not excused because the appellant was "housebound" and had difficulty walking because of arthritis). An employee whose visual problems may have contributed to sending the PFR to the wrong fax number did not show just cause for a delay of approximately four years in making inquiry about the status of the PFR--"not the act of a reasonably prudent person under the circumstances." Bollar v. DHUD, 105 MSPR 491, 494-95 ¶¶ 11-12, 2007 MSPB 112 (2007) ("We also note that the appellant's incorrect filing here is distinguishable from cases where the Board has found good cause for waiving the time limit where the appellant timely sent a filing to the wrong agency. See e.g., Maggard v. Office of Personnel Management, 102 M.S.P.R. 75, ¶ 9 (2006); Killian v. Office of Personnel Management, 100 M.S.P.R. 583, ¶ 6 (2005); Hunter v. Department of the Interior, 79 M.S.P.R. 456, ¶ 8 (1998). In this case, there was no way of knowing that the appellant's filing had been misdirected.").

Incarceration was not sufficient to excuse an untimely PFR when the appellant had notice of the initial decision and could have filed the PFR himself from jail. Cox v. OPM, 93 MSPR 402, 403-04 ¶ 6 (2003) (incarceration did not excuse the late PFR); Clark v. Dept. of Army, 82 MSPR 93, 94-95 ¶ 6 (1999) (six-year delay in filing a PFR was not excused by incarceration in "boot camp"; the appellant failed to convince the Board that he was precluded from communicating from the camp by telephone or correspondence); Waller v. USPS, 78 MSPR 696, 699 (1998) (incarceration was no excuse when there was no showing of how the appellant was prevented from filing a PFR). An assertion of homeless status, resulting in inability to receive Board decisions, was insufficient to warrant waiver of the PFR filing deadline, Jones v. SSA, 111 MSPR 498, 502-03 ¶¶ 10-11, 2009 MSPB 112 (2009):

The appellant's claims, that he did not receive documents, that there was confusion regarding his address, and that he was homeless for some of the time in question, do not show good cause for his filing delay. The record shows that the vast majority of pertinent documents in his appeals, including both initial decisions, were served on the appellant's residence of record. Further, by the appellant's own admission, he did not become homeless until after the deadline for filing a petition for review had passed in both cases. While the appellant claims that he did not receive any of the pertinent documents concerning his second removal appeal, we note that the appellant has significant prior experience with Board procedures, having appealed a prior action concerning another agency and pursued three compliance appeals stemming from that appeal. Jones v. Department of Health & Human Services, MSPB Docket No. PH-0752-02-0381-C-3 (Apr. 22, 2009); Jones v. Department of Health & Human Services, MSPB Docket No. PH-0752-02-0381-C-2 (June 6, 2005); Jones v. Department of Health & Human Services, MSPB Docket No. PH-0752-02-0381-I-2 (June 6, 2005); Jones v. Department of Health & Human Services, 96 M.S.P.R. 537 (2004) (Table); Jones v. Department of Health & Human Services, MSPB Docket No. PH-0752-02-0381-I-1 (Nov. 19, 2002) (Initial Decision). This experience should have alerted the appellant to possible problems regarding service of documents, and his failure to note these problems breached his personal duty to monitor the progress of his appeals at all times. Miller v. Department of Homeland Security, 110 M.S.P.R. 258, ¶¶ 12, 14 (2008).

The appellant's arguments, that he needed to await the results of his FOIA request before filing his petition for review and that he timely filed his petitions upon receiving the FOIA response, are also without merit. The appellant admits that he initiated his FOIA requests after the regional office informed him in June of 2008 that his appeals were closed and that he needed to contact the Board's headquarters. The decision to delay contacting the Board after receiving notice regarding the problems with the status of the appeal does not show due diligence warranting a finding of good cause. See Miller, 110 M.S.P.R. 258, ¶ 13; Groesbeck v. Office of Personnel Management, 109 M.S.P.R. 1, ¶ 4 (2008). Further, even though the appellant was pro se, his belief that he needed to obtain information pursuant to a FOIA request does not excuse the delay, nor does it explain the failure to seek an extension of time to file a petition for review in advance. See Bocock v. Department of the Navy, 85 M.S.P.R. 241, ¶ 5 (2000); Comer v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 78 M.S.P.R. 633, ¶ 6 (1998), aff'd, 178 F.3d 1313 (Fed. Cir. 1999) (Table); Criddell v. U.S. Postal Service, 60 M.S.P.R. 30, 33 (1993) (an appellant's effort to gather information that was believed to support the case does not establish good cause for a waiver of the filing deadline).

See Gomez v. VA, NY-0752-10-0238-I-1 (NP 6/15/2011) ("[T]he fact remains that the initial decision was mailed to the appellant's address of record, and that he failed to notify the Board of any change in his address. As to his assertion that he did not have an address because he was homeless, he has provided no evidence that he was homeless at the time the initial decision was issued or when he received it. See Sutton v. Office of Personnel Management, 113 M.S.P.R. 576, ¶ 9 (2010)."); Sutton v. OPM, 113 MSPR 576, 581, 2010 MSPB 88 (2010) (various conditions, including homelessness and transition among residences, were insufficient to justify a delayed PFR without a showing of how these conditions prevented timely filing).

Confusion by the appellant when the AJ who signed the initial decision was not the same AJ who heard the case did not excuse a delayed PFR: "irrespective of which administrative judge signed the initial decision, the decision itself clearly stated that a petition for review must be filed with the Clerk of the Board no later than May 20, 2005." Jimenez v. SSA, 100 MSPR 287, 291 ¶ 8 (2005).

One case found the appellant filing a timely PFR but omitting a certificate of service. The notice from the Board to cure the deficiency and the time allowed for its cure fell within a short period while the appellant was away on vacation. Within a day of his return, he corrected the service deficiency. The Board adjudicated the PFR. Bianco v. OPM, 48 MSPR 291, 293 (1991); Gethers v. Dept. of Navy, 59 MSPR 196, 198 (1993) (appellant was on notice of the need to correct a deficient PFR before he left town for a vacation: the appellant has not "explained why he was unable to timely file his corrected petition for review in the time available before going out of town or why he did not request additional time to respond.").

Holidays do not excuse the untimely PFR. See Dooley v. Dept. of Air Force, 57 MSPR 684, 690 (1993) ("We believe...that 35 days is ample time both to prepare a petition for review and to celebrate the holidays. Moreover, we expect a party who has filed an appeal before the Board to take the time necessary to meet Board filing deadlines, even if it means taking time away from holiday celebrations.").

Travel may not persuade the Board to excuse a delayed PFR. See Purifoy v. OPM, 11 MSPR 228, 229 (1982) (delay in a PFR was excused; the appellant was on a trip when the initial decision was received by someone handling mail during his absence; that individual "put it with appellant's other second class mail to hold until his return."). But see Sembach v. OPM, 68 MSPR 199, 201 (1995) ("[A]n appellant's assertion that he was out of town during part of the filing period and left 'his papers' behind does not establish good cause for waiver of the time limit, where he has not explained why he was unable to timely file before leaving town or why he did not request an extension of time to file.").
