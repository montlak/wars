c. Reversal of Criminal Conviction

Substantial delay between the date of reopening and the underlying Board decision occurred in Payne v. USPS, 69 MSPR 503, 506-08 (1996), involving reversal of a criminal conviction underlying the appellant's removal:

The Board may reopen or reconsider a case on its own motion to correct its own errors or to modify its judgment, decree, or order. A determination of whether to reopen a case is discretionary, and involves balancing the desirability of finality and the public interest in reaching what ultimately appears to be the right result. A case may be reopened in the interests of justice, particularly where the evidence is of such weight as to warrant a different outcome. The Board's authority to reopen an appeal is limited by the requirement that such authority must be exercised, within a reasonable amount of time, which is generally measured in weeks rather than years. See, e.g., Welber v. USPS, 62 MSPR 98, 101-02 (1994); Golden v. USPS, 60 MSPR 268, 272 (1994); Woodall v. Federal Energy Regulatory Commission, 28 MSPR 192, 194-95 (1985).

This case involves a delay of more than just weeks, as over one year elapsed between the issuance of the Board's final decisions and the appellant's request for reopening. We find, however, that it can be distinguished from other cases in which reopening has been denied on the basis of the appellant's delay in requesting reopening. Here, there has been a material change in the controlling facts of the case. The dispositive evidence--the reversal of the appellant's conviction--did not exist, and therefore, was not available to the appellant at an earlier date through no fault of the appellant. See Welber, 62 MSPR at 102-03. Thus, the Board may reopen its decisions, notwithstanding the appellant's 13-month "delay" in seeking reopening, because of the event that occurred after the Board issued its final decisions. See id.

The Board will reopen a case, however, only if the appellant exercised due diligence in seeking reopening. See, e.g., Guzy v. Dept. of the Interior, 64 MSPR 470, 472 (1994); Welber, 62 MSPR at 103; Lopez v. Dept. of Justice, 55 MSPR 644, 646 (1992). We find that the appellant did so. The record shows that the appellant first asked his former employing agency to reinstate him on September 13, 1993, which was within days of his receipt of the court's August 27, 1993 reversal of his conviction. The agency refused this request on September 15, 1993. The appellant again sought reinstatement on November 8, 1993, within days of his receipt of the November 4, 1993 letter from the U.S. Attorney to the lawyer representing him in the criminal case informing him of the prosecutor's decision not to reprosecute him. Moreover, roughly one month after learning of the prosecutor's decision, the appellant filed a petition with the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, which was the last forum that reviewed his appeal of his 1992 removal. Finally, after receiving the agency's November 29, 1993 letter refusing his second request for reinstatement, the appellant filed his request to reopen with the Board on January 4, 1994, which was apparently 35 days or less after he received the agency's letter. Thus, under the circumstances of this case, we find that the appellant exercised due diligence in seeking reversal of his removal based on the reversal of his conviction and the subsequent decision of the prosecutor not to reprosecute. Cf. 5 CFR § 1201.114(d) (a petition for review must be filed within 35 days after the initial decision is issued).

The agency asserts that it would be prejudiced if the Board excused the appellant's delay in filing and that the appellant should have filed a petition for review of the October 22, 1992 initial decision affirming his removal if he wanted Board review. However, the agency has cited no authority for its argument that prejudice to the agency should be the overriding consideration in determining whether to reopen a final Board decision. Cf. Stephens v. OPM, 33 MSPR 292, 296-99 (1987) (the Board decided to reopen the appeals and apply a new rule of law retroactively, in part, because the burden on the affected agency would not be great). Moreover, as previously noted, the basis for the appellant's motion to reopen, the Sixth Circuit's overturning of his criminal conviction, did not occur until after the period for filing a timely petition for review in Payne, 55 MSPR 317 (Initial Decision, Oct. 22, 1992), had expired. Thus, the agency has not explained how the appellant's decision not to file a petition for review of that initial decision, or to file a petition for judicial review of Payne, 55 MSPR 317, with the Federal Circuit is relevant to the issue of whether the Board should reopen these decisions. Therefore, we reopen the appeals and vacate the Board's decisions in Payne, 55 MSPR 317, and Payne, 55 MSPR 317 (Initial Decision, Oct. 22, 1992).

We find that the court's decision overturning the appellant's criminal conviction constitutes new and material evidence in the appellant's appeal of his removal. See Underwood, 18 MSPR at 710-11. The agency asserts that Underwood should not apply because, in that decision, the Board applied pre-Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 standards concerning requests to reopen and reconsider decisions of the former Civil Service Commission. As already indicated by previously cited decisions, and as acknowledged by the agency itself, the Board has always recognized and exercised its authority to reopen final decisions in appropriate situations. The agency also contends, in effect, that the court's decision is not material evidence because the record indicates that the agency based its decision to remove the appellant on "facts and evidence other than the criminal process." The record belies the agency's contention. It clearly shows that the appellant was removed solely based on the fact of his conviction, not on the underlying conduct.... The Board has held that the reversal of a criminal conviction on judicial appeal warrants reopening a final decision to reverse an adverse action based solely on the conviction. Underwood, 18 MSPR at 711. Accordingly, we reverse the agency's action removing the appellant from his position.

See Pawn v. USDA, 82 MSPR 284, 286-87 ¶¶ 5-6 (1999) (appellant was entitled to restoration and back pay upon the reversal of the criminal conviction underlying removal and related charges of an inability to carry a firearm and unavailability for work); Palomo v. DOJ, 73 MSPR 306, 308 (1997) ("[R]eversal of a criminal conviction on judicial appeal warrants reopening a final decision in order to reverse an adverse action based solely on the conviction"; motion to reopen is premature during the pendency of an appeal from a criminal conviction). The situation differs if the job is lost because a necessary security clearance is lost by reason of a criminal conviction. That the criminal conviction is later expunged or overturned does not vitiate the removal based on the clearance loss. See Nowlin v.
