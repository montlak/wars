E. FURLOUGHS; SENIORITY

A nonnegotiable proposal required the agency to use certain weights in the evaluation of temporary employees and then to use those evaluations equitably to furlough and recall those employees. The proposal directly interfered with management's right to select among employees to lay off under § 7106(a)(2)(A). NTEU and IRS, 7 FLRA 275, 286-88 (1981). A provision that management consider furloughing union representatives rather than separating them by RIF was nonnegotiable. It was contrary to government regulations that do not permit RIFs of employees while employees with lower retention standing are on furlough. NTEU Chap. 202 and Dept. of Treasury, Bureau of Gov't Fin. Operations, 22 FLRA 553, 557-58 (1986). Another proposal was nonnegotiable, as a violation of government-wide OPM regulations on furloughs, that precluded employees on notice of adverse action from being released on furloughs without regard to their standing on a retention register. Coordinating Comm. of Unions and Dept. of Treasury, Bureau of Engraving & Printing, 29 FLRA 1436, 1442-44 (1987). NAGE Local R1-144 and Dept. of Navy, Naval Underwater Sys. Ctr., 29 FLRA 471, 478 (1987), determined it was not negotiable to require the employer to place an employee who would otherwise be furloughed in a vacant position that the agency decided to fill. The proposal required management to place an employee in a vacant position irrespective of the employee's qualifications, in violation of management's right to detail employees.

A proposal that furloughed employees have the option to choose continuous furloughs rather than a day or two at a time, so as to qualify for unemployment compensation, or to receive leave without pay, was negotiable although it interfered with management's right to assign work. The proposal made it more difficult for management to secure some savings in its budget through the furlough process, but that detriment did not rise to the level of excessive interference. AFGE Local 32 and OPM, 22 FLRA 307, 311-13 (1986). FLRA suggested that another proposal was negotiable that permitted discontinuous furloughs. AFGE Nat'l VA Council and VA, 41 FLRA 73, 75-76 (1991) (Proposal 5, dismissing proposal, however, as moot); see NAGE Local R1-203 and Dept. of Interior, Fish and Wildlife Serv., 55 FLRA 1081, 1096-98 (1999) (Proposal 9) (proposal was negotiable that permitted employees choice of taking their furloughs on discontinuous or continuous basis so as to qualify for unemployment compensation; OPM regulations governing furloughs and recall of employees from furlough were not inconsistent with proposal). AFGE Nat'l VA Council approved as negotiable proposals allowing administrative leave to be granted by the agency to employees seeking to apply for unemployment benefits and to contact job placement and employment agencies. FLRA allowed negotiation of a proposal that required an agency to provide persuasive reasons for a furlough if some but not all employees in a competitive level were selected for furlough. It concluded that the requirement did not permit an arbitrator to substitute his judgment for management but only required the agency to follow the regulatory requirement of then-applicable 5 CFR 752.404(b)(2) that the agency include in its furlough notice the basis for selecting particular employees as well as the reasons for the furlough. NAGE Local R1-144 and Dept. of Navy, Naval Underwater Sys. Ctr., 29 FLRA 471, 473-74 (1987). The same decision declared negotiable a proposal that furloughs of employees be the last resort when administrative leave could not be given due to budgetary restraints. The proposal only called on management to exercise its discretion in allowing administrative leave under the conditions described in the FPM; the employer could avoid the use of administrative leave, even for brief periods, if budgetary constraints precluded its use.

FLRA approved as negotiable a proposal that "[e]mployees who are furloughed during a lapse of appropriation will be retroactively paid and otherwise compensated when appropriations are approved to the extent permitted by law and regulation." Nat'l Weather Serv. Employees Org. and Dept. of Commerce, Nat'l Weather Serv., 39 FLRA 1279, 1279 (1991). AFGE Nat'l VA Council and VA, 41 FLRA 73, 79-81 (1991), approved the Proposal 4, that "[t]he agency will grant employees who suffer loss of pay through furlough administrative leave equal to the lost time if an appropriation is enacted":

The Union asserts that the proposal would not limit the Agency's right to place employees in a furlough, or nonpay, status when the Agency has insufficient appropriated funds to pay them for a period of time. Rather, the Union contends, the proposal would be effective only after appropriated funds subsequently were made available for the period covered by the furlough. The Union argues that the proposal would ameliorate the effect on employees of having been placed in a furlough status by requiring the Agency to retroactively put them on administrative leave for a period equal to the length of time they were furloughed.

We find that the plain wording of Proposal 4 is consistent with the Union's statement of intent. Therefore, we adopt the Union's interpretation of Proposal 4 for the purposes of this decision.

Based on that interpretation, we conclude that the proposal does not violate the Anti-Deficiency Act because it does not require the Agency to spend funds when they are not available. Instead, the proposal requires that the funds necessary to provide the administrative leave granted employees under the proposal have been appropriated before the proposal is effectuated. Our determination is consistent with the Comptroller General's decision at 62 Comp. Gen. 1, 3 (1982), which states that an agency, in its discretion, has the authority to grant excused absences to its employees' and that, therefore, an agency may grant administrative leave retroactively to its employees to the extent to which funds have been appropriated and were available and adequate on the date in question to cover the costs incurred by the granting of administrative leave.

We further conclude that Proposal 4 does not interfere with the Agency's right to lay off employees under section 7106(a)(2)(A) of the Statute. The proposal would not affect the ability of the Agency to furlough employees when it is without adequate appropriated funds. Rather, the proposal addresses only retroactive compensation of those employees who may have been furloughed as the result of inadequate appropriations.

A proposal that does not permit management to consider qualifications of employees but requires seniority to govern retention of employees within the same employment categories when hours are decreased was a nonnegotiable infringement on the right of management to assign work. SEIU Local 556 and Dept. of Army, Army Support Command, Ft. Schafter, 29 FLRA 1553, 1560-61 (1987) (Proposal 8).
