broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 19: Critical Elements and Performance Standards, Section E: Development, Modifications of Standards and Elements

E. DEVELOPMENT, MODIFICATIONS OF STANDARDS AND ELEMENTS

An agency must negotiate over the manner that unit employees will participate in establishing performance standards under 5 USC 4302(a)(2). SSA, Baltimore and AFGE, 9 FLRA 909, 911 (1982). Proposals establishing procedures for development and implementation of performance standards and critical elements, or establishing appropriate arrangements for employees adversely affected by the application of performance standards and elements, are within the duty to bargain under § 7106(b)(2) and (3). AFGE Local 1923 and DHHS, Office of Sec'y, Hq., Office of Gen. Counsel, Social Security Div., 21 FLRA 178, 179-87 (1986). A proposal was approved for bargaining that the employer provide the union with all statistical data relied upon to develop performance standards for certain unit positions. AFGE, Nat'l Council of Field Assessment Locals and DHHS, SSA, 32 FLRA 982, 985-88 (1988).

Although a union may require negotiations on how employees will be involved in the development of standards, the standards remain substantively nonnegotiable, AFGE Local 940 and VA, Philadelphia, 52 FLRA 1429, 1435 (1997):

Although 5 C.F.R. § 430 states that agencies "shall...[c]ommunicat[e] performance plans to employees," this language was not intended to create bargaining obligations over and above those required by the Statute. See OPM Final Rule on 5 C.F.R. Parts 430 et al., 60 Fed. Reg. 43,935, 43,936 (1995) (clarifying that regulation does not require bargaining over performance standards); id. at 43,937 (explaining that agencies must involve employees in the design and implementation of performance standards only to the extent required by the Statute)....

It was acceptable to require negotiations over a proposal that the employer provide the union with a full explanation of how standards were arrived at, along with supporting documentation. AFGE Local 1760 and DHHS, SSA, Baltimore, 25 FLRA 16, 27-28 (1987). But a union could not compel negotiation of proposals requiring management to provide justification for use of "negative" performance standards. The proposal was substantive rather than procedural. The proposed requirement authorized an arbitrator to review the reasons given by management for establishing its standards and rating levels. The arbitrator would be required to substitute his judgment for that of management in establishing standards. NFFE Local 1798 and VAMC, Martinsburg, W.Va., 27 FLRA 239, 246, 299-51 (1987) (relying on POPA and PTO, 25 FLRA 384 (1987) (union could not compel management to explain to satisfaction of arbitrator why management's standards should be accepted if union demonstrated that the standards it proposed were more objective)).

It was negotiable that the identification of standards and critical elements be a joint effort between the employee and the supervisor. The proposal did not specify the form of the supervisor's participation. NFFE and Dept. of Interior, BLM, 29 FLRA 1491, 1491-93 (1987) (relying upon POPA and PTO, 29 FLRA 116 (1987)); see NTEU and USDA, Food and Nutrition Serv., W. Region, 42 FLRA 964, 977 (1991) ("The second sentence of Provision 3...allows for employee participation in the formulation of performance standards without requiring negotiation over the content of those standards."). An acceptable procedure was proposed when a union requested identification of performance elements and establishment of performance standards through a joint planning and communication process between employees and their supervisors, meeting at least annually to discuss standards and elements for the next rating year, with the shop steward entitled to be present and a party to those discussions. The proposal permitted employee participation without preventing the agency from establishing standards. NFFE Local 1430 and Dept. of Navy, N. Div., Naval Base, Philadelphia, 15 FLRA 45, 45-46 (1984). POPA and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 47 FLRA 10, 30-31 (1993), approved as negotiable Provision 9, requiring that employee meetings be completed within a two-week period prior to the establishment or modification of appraisal plans.

POPA and PTO, Dept. of Commerce, 29 FLRA 1389, 1389-92 (1987), considered a proposal that when a proposed performance appraisal plan applies to no more than 15 employees, the supervisor shall hold a meeting with the involved employees so they can provide input on the plan. Under the proposal the union would have an opportunity to have a representative present at the meeting and the supervisor would make a written report providing reasons for rejecting any suggestions prior to implementing the plan. Portions of the proposal were negotiable:

In National Treasury Employees Union and Department of Treasury, Bureau of the Public Debt, 3 FLRA 769, 778 (1980), aff'd sub nom. NTEU v. FLRA, 691 F.2d 553 (D.C. Cir. 1982), the Authority stated that 5 U.S.C. § 4302(a)(2) "encouraged employee participation in establishing performance standards" but did not specify the form which such employee participation must take. Thus, the Authority found that the manner in which a particular agency provides for such employee participation was within that agency's discretion and within the duty to bargain to the extent that it would not prevent the agency from exercising its right to establish performance standards and critical elements.... [T]he Authority determined in National Federation of Federal Employees Local 1430 and Department of Navy, Northern Division, U.S. Naval Base, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, 15 FLRA 45 (1984), that Proposal 1 in that case which required, among other things, that the identification and establishment of performance standards would be a "joint planning communication process between the employee and the aid of the steward and the supervisor" was negotiable. The Authority found that Proposal 1 in that case did not prevent the agency from establishing performance standards but, rather, was concerned with the manner in which the agency would provide for employee participation in the establishment of those performance standards.

Similarly, the first sentence of Proposal 1 in this case also concerns the manner in which the Agency will meet the requirement...to include employee participation in the establishment of performance standards. Further, the form of the employee participation required by the first sentence of this proposal is entirely consistent with Government-wide regulations promulgated by the Office of Personnel Management implementing 5 U.S.C. § 4302(a)(2). For example, 5 C.F.R. § 430.204(c)(1) (1987) provides that employee participation in the development of performance standards may be achieved by several means, specifically including that "[e]mployee and supervisor discuss and develop performance plan together." In addition, the Federal Personnel Manual states that employee participation may be accomplished by discussions between "a representative sample of employees and their supervisors." FPM Chapter 430, subch. 2-3.d. Thus, the requirement that supervisors be involved in a joint process with employees or their representatives in the development of performance standards results from the application of statute and implementing Government-wide regulations and not from the first sentence of this proposal.

Moreover, the first sentence of this proposal does not specify the form of participation or assign any particular tasks....

In holding the first sentence of Proposal 1 to be negotiable, we note that it does not prevent management from designating other Agency officials to participate in meetings regarding proposed performance appraisal plans or in any manner prevent the Agency from establishing performance standards.

...

As contrasted with the first sentence of Proposal 1, the third sentence of the proposal requires that a supervisor make a written report giving reasons for rejecting employee suggestions regarding performance standards....

...

[I]t interfere(s) with management's right to assign work....

The D.C. Circuit affirmed FLRA's reasoning in POPA v. FLRA, 873 F.2d 1485, 1488 (D.C. Cir. 1989):

The third sentence of this proposal, which provides that "[t]he supervisor shall make a written report," on its face requires the agency to assign a specific work task to a particular management official. Because it dictates who must make the written report, we agree with the Authority that this sentence of the proposal interferes with management's discretion under 7106(a)(2)(B) of the Statute to select the person who should receive the assignment.

...The union notes that Congress required agencies, as part of the performance appraisal process, to encourage employee participation, broadly defined, in the development of performance standards.... We find, however, that the union's argument is misplaced, for the Authority held only that this proposal interferes with management's right to assign work, not that "employee participation" necessarily includes meetings between employees and supervisors but not reports by supervisors....

NAGE Local R1-144 and Dept. of Navy, Naval Underwater Sys. Ctr., Newport, 35 FLRA 642, 642-43 (1990), resolved to be nonnegotiable, as an infringement of management's right to assign work, a proposal that "[t]he final decision as to the employee's elements and standards rests with the employee's supervisor." A proposal was not negotiable that required the union to participate on an equal basis in the development or revision of standards and studies. The proposal interfered with management's right to assign work. AFGE Local 3804 and FDIC, Chicago Region, 7 FLRA 217, 227-28 (1981). A proposal was not negotiable providing that if the employee and his supervisor cannot agree on standards the differences will be referred up the supervisory chain for resolution. The proposal invaded the right of management to set standards: it was couched in terms of actions that would occur absent agreement over the standards. NFFE Local 1623 and S. Carolina NG, Columbia, 28 FLRA 633, 636-38 (1987).

A negotiable proposal compelled establishment of a joint study committee to explore the feasibility of quality circles to serve as a forum for evaluating employee concerns and suggestions regarding new systems of work performance. AFGE Local 12 and Dept. of Labor, 17 FLRA 674, 676-77 (1985) (Proposal 3). But a proposal was nonnegotiable that required management to permit an observer to be present during the development or revision of performance measures and studies. The proposal interfered with the agency exercise of its discretion to direct employees and assign work. AFGE Local 1940 and USDA, Plum Island Disease Ctr., 16 FLRA 816, 818-20 (1984) (Proposal 2). The union could not compel negotiation of a provision that allowed it to sit as an observer in meetings of management concerning development or revision of measures of performance. That presence interfered with the right of the agency to freely engage in internal discussion and deliberation, and it interfered with the decisional process of management concerning the right of the agency to direct employees and assign work. AFGE Local 2302 and Army Armor Ctr., Ft. Knox, 15 FLRA 17, 18-19 (1984).
