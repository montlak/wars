a. EEO Complaint

The standard that adverse actions, including downgradings, be taken for such cause as will promote the efficiency of the service does not authorize a demotion of an employee because an agency selected a particular course of action to implement another employee's personnel action. In Quinney v. Dept. of Army, 3 MSPR 1, 4-5, 3 MSPB 123 (1980), the appellant was the victim of discrimination when she failed to obtain promotion to a GS-7 position. The Army downgraded the employee who successfully obtained the GS-7 position so that the appellant could be promoted following the determination on the EEO case. The individual demoted then filed an appeal with the Federal Employee Appeals Authority of the Civil Service Commission (predecessor to MSPB). The appeal was successful. The demotion was ordered canceled. Faced with two GS-7 employees and the FEAA order, the Army demoted the appellant who previously demonstrated discrimination. On appeal, the Board determined that there was no cause to demote the appellant; the Army established no wrongdoing on her part. The Army was left to determine what to do with the two GS-7 employees and to ascertain who should have the job in contention. The lesson of the tale is that an agency should not, by remedying discrimination through the promotion of one employee, place another employee at a disadvantage if the second employee is not at fault and has the status or tenure to appeal. See Harman v. Dept. of Navy, 3 MSPR 530, 531 n.1, 4 MSPB 48 (1980) (citing Patterson v. American Tobacco Co., 535 F.2d 257, 268 (4th Cir. 1976), suggesting that Title VII was not to be used to displace incumbent workers).

Carter v. Dept. of Army, 11 MSPR 532, 532-33 (1982), provided additional analysis concerning the use of an adverse action against a blameless employee to establish the basis for a remedy for an employee adversely affected by discriminatory practices:

Appellant was demoted from the position of Supervisory Quality Inspection Specialist, GS-8, to Quality Inspection Specialist, GS-7, by the Department of the Army (agency) after it decided to retroactively promote a female employee to the same GS-8 position as part of a corrective personnel action to remedy sex discrimination in its promotion procedures. Appellant did not participate in the prohibited discrimination and was not charged with improper performance of his duties. On appeal to the Board's Atlanta Regional Office, the presiding official held that the demotion was not for such cause as would promote the efficiency of the service and that the action should be reversed.

In its petition for review, the agency argues that the presiding official erred in reversing appellant's demotion. The agency claims that its decision to promote a female and demote appellant was justified, in that the female was the superior candidate for the position and there was no alternative method available for correcting the effects of the prohibited sex discrimination on the selection process. Moreover, the agency contends that a reasonable basis exists for the demotion and that it is not required to show misconduct on the part of appellant in order to justify its adverse action.

We find the agency's arguments to be without merit. While the agency was obligated to correct its discriminatory conduct, its determination to promote a female does not establish proper cause for appellant's demotion. This Board has held that the phrase "such cause as will promote the efficiency of the service" does not authorize a demotion of an employee simply because an agency selected a particular course of action to implement another employee's personnel action. Quinney v. Department of the Army, 3 MSPB 123, 125, 3 MSPR 1, 3 (1980).

In the instant case, the agency informed appellant that the demotion did not reflect "unfavorably" on his performance. In fact, appellant received an "Outstanding" performance rating while serving as GS-8. Therefore, the agency was faced with the dilemma of having two employees required to be in GS-8 positions. Once the agency decided to promote the EEO complainant retroactively, it had the responsibility of treating both fairly, through the exercise of discretion in making assignments. Quinney, supra. Implicit in the agency responsibility was the obligation to place both employees in positions without affecting either of them adversely.

Our review of the record reveals that no valid "cause" for the demotion that would promote the efficiency of the service (5 USC § 7513(a)) exists in the absence of a showing that appellant violated any of his duties or obligations. The agency cannot correct its discriminatory conduct by taking an adverse action against an innocent employee. Under these circumstances, appellant was entitled to a GS-8 position notwithstanding the agency's determination that it was obligated to promote a female in order to correct its discriminatory conduct. Accordingly, the agency has to determine where the two employees should be assigned. Quinney, supra.

Shelton v. USPS, 53 MSPR 483, 486 n.2 (1992), helpfully added: "The dilemma facing the agency as to how to determine which of the two employees should be assigned to [that] ...position will not be settled by the Board. The resolution of [that] ...problem is the responsibility of agency management." In Gibbs v. Dept. of Army, 19 MSPR 127, 129 (1984), the appellant was demoted following a charge of discrimination that successfully challenged his promotion; a second rating panel used different criteria. Appellant was not selected by the second panel, and he appealed. The Board concluded that there was no proof that the criteria the first panel used were incorrect; there was no fault on the part of the appellant and no enhancement of service efficiency through his demotion. In Morgan v. Dept. of Air Force, 13 MSPR 246, 250 (1982), demotion to remedy discrimination against another employee was so clearly without merit and wholly unfounded that an award of counsel fees was warranted under the Board's "interest of justice" standard. For discussion of the considerations allowing "bumping" to remedy discrimination, a review of Lander v. Lujan, 888 F.2d 153 (D.C. Cir. 1989), is recommended. See Notification and Federal Employee Antidiscrimination and Retaliation Act of 2002, Pub. L. 107-174, 5 USC 2302, note (§ 102(1)): "Federal agencies should not retaliate for court judgments or settlements relating to discrimination and whistleblower laws by targeting the claimant or other employees with reductions in compensation, benefits, or workforce to pay for such judgments or settlements.").
