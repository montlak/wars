f. Long Service

Although longevity of service and an otherwise good service record are relevant considerations in determining the reasonableness of a penalty, Sterling v. DOD, 46 MSPR 177, 184 n.15 (1990), if an offense is serious enough, e.g., a false claim, prior long service may not mitigate the offense. Hanna v. Dept. of Army, 42 MSPR 233, 241 (1989). Under Stanford v. Dept. of Navy, SF-0752-15-0444-I-1 (NP 9/15/2016):

As the deciding official recognized, the appellant's 37 years of service, lack of prior discipline, and satisfactory performance are significant mitigating factors. However, the Board has found that falsification is a serious offense that may warrant removal, even for employees with extensive Federal service, satisfactory performance, and no prior disciplinary record. See Hylick v. Department of the Air Force, 85 M.S.P.R. 145, ¶¶ 22-24 (2000) (finding that the penalty of removal was reasonable for 2 charges of deliberate misrepresentation when an appellant had 25 years of service, good performance, and no prior disciplinary record); Tanner v. Department of Transportation, 65 M.S.P.R. 169, 174 (1994) (finding that the penalty of removal was reasonable for charges of falsification and misrepresentation when an appellant had 12 years of Federal service, no prior disciplinary record, and numerous commendations); Walcott v. U.S. Postal Service, 52 M.S.P.R. 277, 284 (finding that the penalty of removal was reasonable for a falsification charge when an appellant had 27 years of Federal service and no prior disciplinary record), aff'd, 980 F.2d 744 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table); Pitts v. Department of the Air Force, 29 M.S.P.R. 108, 110 (1985) (finding that the penalty of removal was reasonable for charges of falsification and deliberately falsifying a material fact in connection with a matter under official investigation when an appellant had 30 years of Federal service, outstanding performance, and no prior disciplinary record), aff'd, 795 F.2d 1020 (Fed. Cir. 1986). Here, in light of the seriousness of the appellant's misconduct and the other aggravating factors discussed above, we find that the penalty of removal is reasonable.

Yet long and otherwise successful service may mark a penalty for mitigation as a result of the potential for rehabilitation. See Halper v. USPS, 91 MSPR 170, 173 ¶ 9 (2002) (mitigating for reasons that included a substantial miscalculation by deciding the official of the length of the appellant's service); Brown v. Dept. of Army, 96 MSPR 232, 236 ¶ 13 (2004) (considering whether the deciding official evaluated the appellant's long record of good service as a mitigating or aggravating factor in the choice of removal as a penalty for misuse of a government credit card); Shelly v. Dept. of Treasury, 75 MSPR 677, 684 (1997) (rejecting argument that long service supports a stiff penalty since the appellant arguably should have known better: "Under [that] approach, the longer someone works, the more likely it is that a single misstep will be fatal to his or her career."), followed, Wentz v. USPS, 91 MSPR 176, 186 ¶ 19 (2002); Bishopp v. Dept. of Air Force, 75 MSPR 33, 40 (1997) ("Still, all the instances of aberrant behavior, including the misconduct at issue in this appeal, occurred in a relatively short period of time in what was otherwise a long and distinguished career in federal service, thus indicating that the appellant has good rehabilitation potential.").

Violation of agency regulations concerning case processing, with an inference in the Board's decision of favoritism toward some claims handled by an SSA operations supervisor, did not warrant removal of an employee of 29 years of unblemished service who was given no opportunity to rehabilitate his conduct. Presley v. DHHS, 15 MSPR 291, 295 (1983). But long service may be countered by other factors: the severity of the offense or the employee's supervisory status. See Baker v. DHHS, 41 MSPR 363, 366 n.2 (1989) (removal was sustained of a supervisory employee with exemplary service of over 28 years; the employee accepted a meal from a contractor and provided the contractor with confidential information); cf. Hornbuckle v. Dept. of Army, 45 MSPR 50, 53 (1990) (prior long service was before the assumption of a supervisory position and the agency lost confidence in the appellant's supervisory abilities as a result of problems concerning the misuse of a government vehicle).

A claim of long service will be placed into context. Military service is taken into account. Seven months of supervisory service out of a career of about seven years with the agency did not qualify as the type of service record warranting mitigation of demotion to a nonsupervisory position resulting from an incident of property destruction following a disagreement over the appellant's exercise of supervisory authority. McDowell v. Dept. of Navy, 39 MSPR 179, 183 (1988); see Smith v. Dept. of Army, SF-0752-21-0090-I-1 (NP 9/29/2022) ("An employee's military service is relevant when analyzing length of service under Douglas. Tartaglia v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 858 F.3d 1405, 1409 (Fed. Cir. 2017)."); Purifoy v. VA, CH-0752-14-0185-M-1 (NP 5/16/2022) (military service of more than two years a mitigating factor); Boo v. DHS, 122 MSPR 100, 112 ¶ 21, 2014 MSPB 86 (2014) ("We further find that, although the appellant's tenure with the agency was short--just over 1 year--he had over 22 years of service with the Air Force and that his lengthy military service is a considerable mitigating factor."); Adam v. USPS, 96 MSPR 492, 495 ¶ 6, 8 (2004) (nine years of service was within the description (by the agency) of a short-term employee); Negron v. DOJ, 95 MSPR 561, 573 ¶ 35 (2004) (adopting determination of AJ that the appellant's eight years of service, although not minimal, was not substantial); Betz v. GSA, 55 MSPR 424, 431 (1992) (although an appellant had 19 years of federal service, only three-and-a-half of those years were with the employing agency). But see Wentz v. USPS, 91 MSPR 176, 185 ¶ 19 (2002) ("While 13 years of discipline-free service would not justify mitigation of removal for every act of misconduct, under the circumstances of this appeal, we believe it is a significant mitigating factor.").

If a deciding official looks at long service as an aggravating rather than mitigating factor, the Board may correct the mistake by reconsidering the penalty with due regard to the appellant's service record, as in Brison v. VA, CH-0752-10-0869-I-1 (NP 12/3/2012), aff'd, Brison v. VA, 549 Fed. Appx. 976 (Fed. Cir. 2013 NP); cf. Smith v. Dept. of Army, SF-0752-21-0090-I-1 (NP 9/29/2022) (affirming AJ's decision although reevaluating the length of appellant's service, including military).
