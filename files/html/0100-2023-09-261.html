2. Privileged Information

Not all information is discoverable. Public policy dictates that some communications remain undisclosed without a demonstration of extraordinary circumstances. Communications between attorney and client, priest and penitent, and husband and wife; matters of state secret; trade secrets; and information that can be characterized as essential to high-level governmental deliberations may be exempted from discovery. These privileges often involve complex legal formulations beyond the scope of the Guide. The Board, without extensive expositions on the law of privilege, cautions that evidentiary privileges should not be lightly granted. Vogel v. DOJ, 9 MSPR 382, 387 n.4 (1982) (allowing an in camera inspection to review documents claimed to be privileged and suggesting that, if relevant, even documents that might be included within the "deliberative process" privilege should be released). Assuming the information is privileged, it is doubtful that an adverse inference can be drawn from the assertion of the privilege. See Beamon v. Dept. of Labor, 35 MSPR 15, 19 n.1 (1987).

The Board's Hearings Procedures Guide (superseded by the Judges' Handbook but still used by the Board for training and orientation) offers guidance on privileged material, at 34-35:

b. Privilege

On occasion, a party will seek to limit the introduction of material on the grounds that it is privileged and may not be disclosed.

The person asserting the privilege must demonstrate that the information sought to be introduced is, in fact, privileged. Danko v. Dept. of Defense, 5 MSPR 426, 429 (1981).

The Federal Rules of Evidence make it plain that state law concerning privilege will govern federal civil trials, while federal law will control in federal criminal prosecutions. See Fed. R. Evid. 501.

The Comments to Rule 501 list nine typical non-constitutional privileges, including required reports, lawyer-client, psychotherapist-patient, husband-wife, communications to clergymen, political votes, trade secrets, secrets of state and other official information, and the identity of an informer, as potential areas of conflict in a trial.

Added to these recognized common law privileges, Board cases may involve other claims of privileged, confidential information, including: the deliberative processes privilege, see United States v. Nixon, 418 U.S. 683, 705-6 (1974) and Soucie v. David, 448 F.2d 1067, 1077 (D.C. Cir. 1971); the proscription against divulging any confidential information learned during the course of federal employment, found in 18 USC § 1905; the guarantee of privacy accorded alcohol and drug treatment records under 42 USC § 4582; and the sanctity of taxpayer records collected by the Internal Revenue Service.

In each instance, the claim must be examined in light of the reason for its assertion, and ruled upon in keeping with the appropriate common law.

For example, an appellant claiming handicap discrimination on the basis of alcohol or drug addiction may assert the privilege as to his treatment records; he should be cautioned that such an assertion may undercut the validity of his affirmative defense.

Questions of privilege can become complex. "Executive" privilege, "governmental" privilege, "deliberative" and "work product" privilege, and "informants" privilege are terms liberally cast about in some appeals. Thousands of court cases concerning claims of privilege explain, in a manner not entirely consistent, the nature of the privileges available to the government. At the very least, the Federal Circuit suggested (in an appeal coming from the International Trade Commission) that "any government claim of privilege should be made with regard to specific documents and communications and specify the particular privilege claimed and the basis for its assertion." Zenith Radio Corp. v. United States, 764 F.2d 1577, 1581 (Fed. Cir. 1985).

As to privilege claims, common sense rather than hypertechnical legal arguments should prevail. A deciding official should be able to state what conversations he had with others about a decision to discharge an appellant without the case turning into an intense exploration of the meaning of the law governing FOIA exemptions for predecisional deliberative documents. If an appellant desires the names and addresses of witnesses known to the agency, these should be provided, subject to a reasonable protective agreement or order, without committing considerable resources to an exploration of the interstices of the Privacy Act. Board proceedings are not the place for elaborate and time-consuming disputes over the availability of clearly pertinent information.
