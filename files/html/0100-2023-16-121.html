1. Interference With Grievance

An incorrect statement to an employee by management that the employee had no recourse under a contract grievance procedure (thought by management to have expired) constituted interference; no finding of unlawful motivation was required. Dept. of Air Force, 35th Combat Support Group, George AFB and NFFE Local 977, 4 FLRA 22, 30 (1980) (ALJ Decision). It was a ULP, a violation of §§ 7116(a)(1) and (8) and 7121(a)(1), for the employer to advise an employee that she could grieve a reprimand through either the agency grievance procedure or the contract grievance procedure. Section 7121(d) made the contract procedure the exclusive means for protesting the reprimand by a grievance. DOD, Dept. of Army and Air Force, AAFES, Altus AFB Exch. and AFGE Local 2586, 22 FLRA 559, 563 (1986). But an agency did not impede or obstruct submission of unresolved grievances to arbitration by maintaining, in processing an employee's grievance, that the employee was a supervisor, even if the agency was later determined to have erred. Hq., Army Training Ctr., Ft. Jackson and Howell, 23 FLRA 635, 637 (1986). The agency committed a ULP by refusing to process a grievance from an incumbent union during the pendency of a question concerning representation. See Morale, Welfare and Recreation Directorate, Marine Corps Air Station and AFGE Local 2065, 48 FLRA 686, 690 (1993). FLRA declined to enforce a grievance settlement under very unusual circumstances. See SBA and AFGE Council 228, Local 2532, 38 FLRA 386, 406-09 (1990) (ULP case: declining to enforce a settlement reached during arbitration that was highly favorable to union and that was beyond authority of the management representative to negotiate or approve); cf. Ind. Union of Pension Employees for Democracy & Justice and PBGC, 70 FLRA 820 (2018) (Member DuBester concurring) (ULP for union to interfere with processing of grievances by failing to give effect to procedures for selecting arbitrators) (recon. den., Ind. Union of Pension Employees for Democracy & Justice and PBGC, 71 FLRA 60 (2019) (Member DuBester concurring) (aff'd, Ind. Union of Pension Employees for Democracy & Justice v. FLRA, 961 F.3d 490 (D.C. Cir. 2020) (discussed under "Refusal to Arbitrate")). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Lack of Authority to Settle."]

The grievance procedure under an expired contract continues until it is replaced by a successor agreement. In Dept. of Air Force, Davis-Montham AFB and AFGE Local 2924, 70 FLRA 876, 877 (2018) (Member DuBester dissenting), AFGE represented one group of agency employees, while another group was represented by NFFE. Both unions had contracts with the agency. AFGE was later certified as the exclusive representative for the group of employees earlier represented by NFFE, but no new contract was negotiated to replace the NFFE agreement. Meanwhile, both contracts were relied on to support grievances initiated by AFGE for both groups of employees. Eventually the agency rejected grievances filed by AFGE under the AFGE contract for employees previously represented by NFFE. That led to arbitration, and the arbitrator determined that a grievance under the AFGE contract was arbitrable for an employee in the prior NFFE unit. FLRA vacated the award and determined that the NFFE contract continued in effect and was the proper vehicle to present the grievance:

The Agency contends that, as a matter of law, AFGE was required to file the grievance under the NFFE agreement's negotiated grievance procedure. In this regard, the Authority has stated that "when a negotiated agreement expires, personnel policies, practices, and matters affecting working conditions," including negotiated grievance and arbitration procedures, "continue to the maximum extent possible absent either an express agreement to the contrary or the modification of those conditions of employment in a manner consistent with the Statute."[8] Further, "such provisions survive and remain in full effect...even following the decertification of one exclusive representative and the installation of a new one."[9]

[8] Indep. Union of Pension Emps. for Democracy & Justice, 68 FLRA 999, 1004 (2015) (Pension) (citing NTEU, 64 FLRA 982, 985 n.4 (2010)). We note that in 2018 the Authority issued U.S. DHS, U.S. CBP, El Paso, Tex., 70 FLRA 501, 503-04 (2018), which clarified the meaning of "working conditions" and "conditions of employment."

[9] Pension, 68 FLRA at 1004 (citing U.S. Nuclear Regulatory Comm'n, 6 FLRA 18, 19-20 (1981)).

Here, even though the NFFE agreement has expired, the parties have neither reached an express agreement regarding a new grievance procedure nor modified the NFFE agreement in a manner consistent with the Statute. Therefore, the NFFE agreement's grievance procedure remains in effect and is binding on the parties. Consequently, the Arbitrator erred, as a matter of law, in finding that the NFFE agreement is defunct and not binding.
