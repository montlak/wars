3. Notes of Witness; Memorandum of Conversation

Notes of a supervisor or another witness are often used during hearings, or offered as evidence, either to buttress testimony (showing that it is not a recent fabrication), to jog the memory of a witness, or, if there is no memory, to serve as a recordation of past recollection. It is not unusual for the appellant or a supervisor to keep a daily log of events occurring when adverse action appears a possibility. Those notes may be offered into evidence.

Supervisors' notes may be used as evidence; they may be probative if they are corroborated or unchallenged. Gamble v. Dept. of Army, 23 MSPR 11, 16 (1984). A memorandum made immediately after a conversation carries substantial weight and probative value, especially if statements contained in the memo are corroborated by independent sources. Rodriguez v. USDA, 27 MSPR 79, 86 (1985); see West v. VA, DE-0752-12-0429-I-1 (NP 3/28/2014) (reports of contact: "The appellant has cited no legal authority to support her contention that these documents are inadmissible because they were unsigned, and we are unaware of any such authority. Any questions arising from the lack of signatures go to the probative value of this evidence and not to its admissibility. Cf. Scott v. Department of Justice, 69 M.S.P.R. 211, 228 (1995) (an unsworn statement by an appellant at the initial appeal level is admissible hearsay evidence), aff'd, 99 F.3d 1160 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table); Ford v. Office of Personnel Management, 69 M.S.P.R. 73, 75 (1995) (the fact that the appellant's assertions are not sworn or in affidavit form goes to the weight, and not to the admissibility, of her evidence and arguments)."). Nevertheless, the AJ may credit testimony over handwritten notes. See Wirth v. SSA, 21 MSPR 691, 693 (1984). Or notes may be of no evidentiary value. See Swapsy v. USPS, CH-0353-13-0311-I-2 (NP 9/13/2022) ("The exhibit is a series of OWCP records of telephone contact. One document is a statement that an employee informed the appellant that OWCP found some unidentified position not suitable. Thus, it would have been hearsay evidence that OWCP found unsuitable a job offer that may or may not have been the offer at issue in this appeal. We do not know the job title of the person who took the appellant's telephone call, so we do not know if she was the Claims Examiner handling the appellant's case or a customer service representative working a phone bank and reading information from a computer screen. The Board has found that a document missing indicia of reliability to a similar degree as the record of telephone contact in this appeal constituted at best a mere scintilla of evidence and did not rise to the level of even substantial evidence. Adamsen v. Department of Agriculture, 116 M.S.P.R. 331, ¶¶ 13-18 (2011).").

Notes that are created after litigation appears to be coming may be given weight if there is no basis for discrediting them. See McGuire v. DOJ, 48 MSPR 659, 664 (1991). In Brown v. Coast Guard, 10 MSPR 573, 576 (1982), a Chapter 75 performance appeal, the agency placed principal reliance on notes prepared by a supervisor. The Board discounted the notes: they were prepared by an individual who clearly could not be considered disinterested, the statements could not be considered routinely made, and the notes were not statements traditionally accepted by the judiciary at hearings. Contradicted by live testimony, the notes did not rise to a level of probative value sufficient to resolve factual disputes in the agency's favor. If uncorroborated and unexplained notes are challenged through direct testimony, supervisors' notes may have little probative value:

By holding that the agency did not prove by a preponderance of the evidence the 35 examples cited in the petition for review, the presiding official presumably did not accord these documents any probative value. We have reviewed the whole record and find that in light of our decision in Borninkhof, the presiding official did not err.

In the face of contradictory live testimony presented by appellant at the hearing, the presiding official refused to accept the agency's hearsay statements, without corroboration, as dispositive of the disputed facts that the agency needed to prove. Much of this material consisted of unsigned or unwitnessed handwritten memoranda and notes written on appellant's alleged work product by Lieutenant Commander Ken Allington, appellant's supervisor. Although the agency presented Allington as a witness, it did not explain why he did not testify as to his firsthand knowledge of the events in dispute but merely authenticated that he was the preparer of the notes in the file. The statements relied upon by the agency were prepared by an actor who clearly cannot be considered disinterested; the statements cannot be considered routinely made; nor have statements of this kind traditionally enjoyed judicial acceptance at hearings.

Thus, on the basis of the whole record, including appellant's sworn, contradictory testimony, the Board finds that the agency's hearsay evidence does not rise to a level of probative value sufficient to resolve the factual disputes in the agency's favor and that the presiding official did not err in his determination that the agency failed to sustain its burden of proof in the 35 examples.

If the appellant denies allegations of unacceptable performance, supervisors' memos--unexplained and unauthenticated--may not be sufficiently probative to meet the substantial evidence standard. Stephens v. DHHS, 23 MSPR 302, 304 (1984). Notes of conversations with another, unverified by the author, are entitled to little probative value if they conflict with direct testimony presented to the AJ. Gipson v. VA, 682 F.2d 1004, 1010 n.10 (D.C. Cir. 1982). [Refer to Chapter 5, "Contemporaneous Notations, Statement; Post Hoc Explanation" and "Credibility Findings."]

An agency action that relies on supervisors' notes will not be reversed because a negotiated agreement bars their use: there must be a showing of harmful error through their admission, e.g., a showing that the appellant's belated knowledge of those notes hampered her ability to respond to the agency charges and prejudiced her ability to defend herself. Brown v. VA, 32 MSPR 696, 697-98 (1987). [Refer to Chapter 11 for Harmful Error.]
