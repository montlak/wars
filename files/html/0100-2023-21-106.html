a. Effect of Remarriage After Retirement

Rakowsky v. OPM, 705 Fed. Appx. 986, 988-89 (Fed. Cir. 2017 NP), describes what must be done to establish a survivor annuity when an annuitant remarries after retiring:

The election of a survivor annuity in cases where a retired employee remarries after retirement is governed by 5 U.S.C. § 8339(j)(5)(C)(i), which states, in relevant part, that:

Upon remarriage, a retired employee or Member who was married at the time of retirement...may irrevocably elect during such marriage, in a signed writing received by [OPM] within 2 years after such remarriage..., a reduction in the employee or Member's annuity under paragraph (4) of this subsection for the purpose of providing an annuity for such employee or Member's spouse in the event such spouse survives the employee or Member.

The statutory provision does not require an election of a survivor annuity in any particular form, but it must show an "unmistakable intent" to make such an election. Harris v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 985 F.2d 549, 550 (Fed. Cir. 1993). Any signed writing that manifests the annuitant's intent to make an election will suffice. Dorsey v. Office of Pers. Mgmt., 587 F.3d 1111, 1114 (Fed. Cir. 2009).

Following remarriage, the survivor annuity is no longer automatically established, Murphy v. OPM, 50 MSPR 407, 409-10 (1991):

The appellant contends in his petition for review that, under Burrell v. Office of Personnel Management 33 M.S.P.R. 546 (1987), his current wife is entitled to survivor benefits, regardless of whether he made a timely election of a reduced annuity with survivor benefits for her. In Burrell, the former wife of an annuitant applied for survivor benefits, contending that, because the annuitant had elected a reduced annuity with survivor benefits for her when he retired in 1972, she, rather than the annuitant's current wife, was entitled to the survivor benefits. See Burrell at 547. The Board noted that the law prior to October 1, 1978, was that when an annuitant elected a reduced annuity with survivor benefits on retirement and then remarried after retirement, the current spouse of the annuitant was automatically entitled to the survivor benefits, without a specific election or designation. See id. The Board then found that, because the annuitant married his current wife in 1974, she, rather than his former wife, was entitled to survivor benefits under the law in effect prior to 1978, even though the evidence did not establish that the annuitant specifically elected survivor benefits for his current wife. Id. Unlike the annuitant in Burrell, the appellant in the present appeal married his current wife after October 1, 1978, when the law was changed to eliminate the automatic, indeed mandatory, restoration of a reduced annuity on remarriage, and allowed the annuitant to decide, on remarriage, whether to provide a survivor annuity for the new spouse. See id.; Pub. L. No. 95-317, 92 Stat. 382 (1978). We therefore find that Burrell does not apply in this appeal.
