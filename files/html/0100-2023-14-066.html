i. Contrition or Remorse; Admission of Misconduct

The Board admonished that "it is the efficiency of the service, not whether the appellant deserves sympathy, that is the ultimate criterion for determining whether a particular penalty may be sustained." Singletary v. Dept. of Air Force, 94 MSPR 553, 561 ¶ 16 (2003) (citing Betz v. GSA, 55 MSPR 424, 432 (1992)). But, as Brenda Lee suggested, it may help to say you're sorry. See Seigars v. Dept. of Air Force, DA-0752-15-0091-I-1 (NP 9/14/2016) ("[A]ppellant was aware that his actions would be considered inappropriate, that he lacked rehabilitative potential based on his refusal to take responsibility for his actions, and that there was a lack of alternative sanctions to deter such actions in the future."); Gilliam v, VA, AT-0752-14-0039-I-1 (NP 2/4/2015) ("[a]n appellant's rationalizations for his behavior and lack of remorse for his misconduct reflect a lack of rehabilitative potential and may properly be considered as an aggravating factor. Neuman v. U.S. Postal Service, 108 M.S.P.R. 200, ¶ 26 (2008)."); Hankins v. Dept. of Army, DA-0752-13-0423-I-1 (NP 9/8/2014) ("Although the agency failed to prove that the appellant was actively involved in misappropriating the furniture, it does not appear that the appellant has ever apologized or otherwise expressed any remorse for his failure to account for the furniture, or for his conduct during the investigative interviews. This does not bode well for his rehabilitative potential. See Neuman v. U.S. Postal Service, 108 M.S.P.R. 200, ¶ 26 (2008) (the appellant's rationalizations and lack of remorse indicated little potential for rehabilitation and were aggravating factors)."); Redschlag v. Dept. of Army, 89 MSPR 589, 637 ¶ 93 (2001) (the appellant's "unwillingness to acknowledge that she engaged in any misconduct indicate[s] that she exhibited little potential for rehabilitation"); Bailey v. VA, 13 MSPR 435, 436 n.2, 437 (1982) (sustaining a removal for insubordinate or threatening remarks; the Board notes that the agency offered to reduce the proposed removal to suspension if the appellant apologized to his supervisor).

In Pfaehler v. DHHS, 20 MSPR 159, 161 (1984), the appellant was discharged when she copied agency documents and improperly disclosed them to a grant applicant that supported the practice of holistic medicine. The Board sustained the removal. The appellant took her case pro se to the Federal Circuit. She told the court during argument that she would not release confidential or privileged agency documents if she were returned to her position. The court remanded to the Board for reconsideration. The Board then modified the penalty, reducing it to a 45-day suspension, noting that although the offense was very serious, there was a lack of training concerning office procedures and Pfaehler showed contrition during the reply process. In its nonprecedential opinion, the court suggested that the Board could not disregard a promise to avoid misconduct in the future on the basis of a subjective belief that the appellant would continue to offend. See Pfaehler v. MSPB, 783 F.2d 187 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (an opinion concerning a counsel fee petition referencing a nonprecedential decision). The result here is difficult to square with the Douglas concept that if there is substantial misconduct, the agency's penalty determination will be sustained unless it is unreasonable; in fact, the Board did not even state that the agency penalty determination was unreasonable. The result is better understood when considered in light of the natural inclination of the Board to avoid confrontation with an otherwise deferential reviewing court.

Timing has a lot to do with the weight given expressions of contrition, Singletary v. Dept. of Air Force, 94 MSPR 553, 560 ¶ 15 (2003):

Although the Board has found that an appellant's failure to bring a mitigating factor to an agency's attention does not prohibit an AJ from considering that factor, the Board has noted that such failure merely affects the weight of the factor. See Bryant v. General Services Administration, 23 M.S.P.R. 425, 427 (1984). The Board has found that an employee's admission of his misconduct and his expression of remorse are indicative of his rehabilitative potential and constitute a significant mitigating factor when the employee notifies an agency of his wrong doing of his own volition, prior to the agency's initiating an investigation into the misconduct. See Casarez v. Department of the Army, 70 M.S.P.R. 131, 134 (1996). The Board has also found that an employee's immediate admission of misconduct and expression of remorse upon an initial inquiry by an agency is of some mitigating weight. See Schoeffler v. Department of Agriculture, 47 M.S.P.R. 80, 91 (1991), vacated in part on other grounds, 50 M.S.P.R. 143, 146 (1991); Kruger v. Department of Justice, 32 M.S.P.R. 71, 76-77 (1987). However, where an employee's admission of misconduct and expression of remorse do not come until after the agency conducts its investigation, the Board has found that the employee's contrition is entitled to little or no mitigating weight. See Capeless v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 78 M.S.P.R. 619, 625 (1998) ("The...AJ properly did not consider the factor of the appellant's demonstration of remorse at the hearing. The...AJ correctly concluded that the appellant's contrition was not exhibited until long after the agency had reached its decision."); Padilla v. Department of Justice, 64 M.S.P.R. 416, 424 (1994) (the appellant's expression of regret for "letting the Warden down" only after the agency started to investigate the "weapons incident" was not a mitigating factor that outweighed the seriousness of his offense), aff'd, 64 F.3d 676 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table). Because the appellant's expressions of remorse, no matter how genuinely made, were not made until after the agency had conducted its investigation and had proposed to take disciplinary action against her, we find them be of little mitigating weight.

See Fushikoshi v. USDA, SF-0752-12-0318-I-1 (NP 9/11/2013) ("The appellant's expression of remorse when the agency investigated his misconduct demonstrates rehabilitative potential. See Schoeffler v. Department of Agriculture, 47 M.S.P.R. 80, 91 (1991), vacated in part on other grounds, 50 M.S.P.R. 143, 146 (1991)."); Boo v. DHS, 122 MSPR 100, 112 ¶ 22, 2014 MSPB 86 (2014) (that the appellant expressed immediate remorse for his actions serves as a mitigating factor).

Lack of recognition of the nature of misconduct suggests lack of rehabilitative potential. See Clark v. DHS, DC-0752-13-0661-I-1 (NP 2/21/2023) ("appellant has not taken responsibility for her actions, and she does not seem to appreciate the gravity of her misconduct, which is made more egregious by her SES status and her CCHCO position"); Phillips v. Dept. of Air Force, AT-0752-09-0043-I-2 (NP 8/4/2010) ("Given the appellant's supervisory responsibilities and the seriousness of the sustained misconduct, the appellant's inability to recognize why his conduct was inappropriate shows poor rehabilitation potential. Under these circumstances, the mitigation of his removal to a demotion was reasonable. See Little v. Department of Transportation, 112 M.S.P.R. 224, ¶ 32 (2009); Jinks v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 106 M.S.P.R. 627, ¶¶ 27-28 (2007); Mingledough v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 88 M.S.P.R. 452, ¶¶ 11-16 (2001), review dismissed, 35 F. App'x 873 (Fed. Cir. 2002); Lowe v. Department of Justice, 63 M.S.P.R. 73, 76-79 (1994)."); Jeanquart v. VA, CH-0752-14-0565-I-1 (NP 8/17/2022) ("The administrative judge and deciding official properly found that the appellant's attempts to rationalize and minimize his violent and abusive behavior reflected poor potential for rehabilitation. See also Neuman, 108 M.S.P.R. 200, ¶ 26 (finding that an appellant's rationalizations and lack of remorse indicated little potential for rehabilitation and were aggravating factors)"); cf. Sipe v. VA, DE-0752-15-0513-I-1 (NP 8/24/2022) ("[T]he deciding official found that the appellant otherwise demonstrated a lack of rehabilitative potential because he minimized his involvement in one of the situations in question in contrast to others' accounts of the incident. Accordingly, we find that the record otherwise demonstrates a lack of rehabilitative potential.").

Wiley v. USPS, 102 MSPR 535, 543 ¶ 18, 2006 MSPB 206 (2006), noted the significance of the appellant's failure to accept responsibility for misconduct:

The agency did not present any evidence that the appellant had been involved in any prior disciplinary actions or misconduct before this incident. Thus, the appellant appears to have had 8 years of unblemished agency service at the time of his removal. We find, however, that these mitigating factors are outweighed, not only by the seriousness of the offense, but by the appellant's failure to take responsibility for his misconduct and his lack of remorse. See Render v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 90 M.S.P.R. 441, ¶ 17 (2001) (considering lack of remorse in a penalty determination and finding that the appellant's failure to recognize that her acts were improper demonstrates that she lacks rehabilitation potential). At the hearing, the appellant continued to deny stating that he "felt like getting his gun and shooting up the place," even when confronted with the testimony of his "friend," Elias, who stated that she specifically heard him make that statement. He went on to admit, however, that he did ask Elias, "[I]f I lose my job, what am [I] supposed to do, want to kill her?" We find that this statement provides a greater justification for the removal penalty.

Similarly, Mann v. Dept. of Navy, 54 MSPR 693, 698-99 (1992), sustained a suspension for some sexually degrading remarks to female subordinates:

[A]ppellant's attitude following both the counseling and the current charges does merit consideration in our analysis of the reasonableness of the agency's penalty selection.

In his response to the agency's petition for review, the appellant still maintains that he does not feel that he has violated the agency's policy on sexual harassment, although he concedes now that the language he used was "inappropriate and unnecessary." He has repeatedly indicated his displeasure and resentment that the agency should bring any action at all against him, stating his belief that the government hired him to do a job and that he should have been left alone to do it, regardless of what formal agency rules or regulations might have required; he stated that there was "no reason" for bringing a sexual harassment charge against him. Although he admitted several times that he may have used abusive and offensive language in referring to Ms. Steele and Ms. Odeski, he never expressed remorse for this offense at the hearing and denied, heatedly and at length, that it might have been serious or might have constituted sexual harassment. His statements thus indicate that his potential for rehabilitation in this area is, at best, limited, and provide support for the agency's penalty determination.

A removal for facilitating a drug sale was mitigated to a 120-day suspension for an employee who gained nothing from the transaction, acknowledged his misconduct, testified that he had led a difficult life, and made assurances that his future behavior would be different. Schaffer v. USPS, 39 MSPR 153, 159 (1988).

If one is to obtain mitigation through contrition, the Board expects the proper type of contrition. Dolezal v. Dept. of Army, 58 MSPR 64, 71 (1993), rejected a mitigation argument with the observation that "[t]he deciding official specifically testified that the appellant expressed remorse for the 'hassle' involved in pursuing the investigation against him, but did not apologize for the underlying misconduct." See Brown v. Dept. of Army, 96 MSPR 232, 237 ¶ 14 (2004) (remorse not properly demonstrated by an appellant who said, as to misuse of a government credit card, "it's a violation if they catch you"); Viens v. Dept. of Interior, 92 MSPR 256, 266-67 ¶ 21 (2002) (appellant's remorse was directed to concerns about the loss of his job); Render v. VA, 90 MSPR 441, 447 ¶ 17 (2001) ("[A]ppellant's evident lack of remorse or recognition that her acts were improper, as well as her view that this action is entirely attributable to a widespread and entirely unjustified conspiracy against her [demonstrates] she lacks rehabilitation potential."); O'Keefe v. USPS, 88 MSPR 475, 481-82 ¶ 15 (2001) ("There is no showing that the appellant expressed remorse for his misconduct, took any responsibility for the misconduct, or made any other showing that would give confidence that he would not engage in the charged misconduct in the future."), vacated and remanded, O'Keefe v. USPS, 318 F.3d 1310 (Fed. Cir. 2002) (requiring reanalysis of facts and charges); Shaw v. Dept. of Air Force, 80 MSPR 98, 117 (1998) ("Moreover, the appellant's failure to express remorse at any time during these proceedings supports a finding that he is a poor prospect for rehabilitation."); Wynne v. VA, 75 MSPR 127, 137 (1997) (appellant's statement to the administrative judge that he handled incidents poorly was "belated, lukewarm expression of remorse...insufficient by itself to show that he has good rehabilitative potential"; his continued incidents of misconduct subsequent to the apology belied his verbal expression of remorse); Thomas v. DOD, 66 MSPR 546, 553 (1995) (record did not support a demonstration of remorse; instead the appellant attributed his misconduct to his medication and a medical condition); Padilla v. DOJ, 64 MSPR 416, 424 (1994) (remorse was insufficient to outweigh the severity of the use of unauthorized law enforcement credentials in the purchase of a firearm). But see Shelly v. Dept. of Treasury, 75 MSPR 677, 684 (1997) (Board recognized rehabilitative potential because the appellant testified that she would not repeat her misconduct even though she contended in the reply to the proposed removal that her conduct was acceptable); Hillen v. Dept. of Army, 66 MSPR 68, 97 (1994) ("[A]fter the appellant learned that a complaint against him had been filed with IG, it is undisputed that he reevaluated his behavior, changed his manner of interacting with employees, and that there were no further complaints."); Andrus v. IRS, 14 MSPR 500, 503, 13 MSPB 150 (1983) (although the appellant could not guarantee that he would not again strike a subordinate, his conduct was mitigated by long service and by the fact that he brought the matter to the attention of the agency and admitted to total responsibility for the incident).

Admission of wrongdoing before its disclosure through an agency investigation suggests the potential for rehabilitation, Casarez v. Dept. of Army, 70 MSPR 131, 134 (1996):

...The Board has specifically found that an employee's action in notifying the agency of his wrongdoing on his own volition constitutes a mitigating factor. Lampack v. U.S. Postal Service, 27 MSPR 468, 470 (1985). Moreover, we find that it is a significant mitigating factor. By coming forward, the appellant showed that he recognized, albeit belatedly, that his failure to report his sick leave usage was wrong. His decision to admit his misconduct also supports a finding that he shows good potential for rehabilitation. See, e.g., Lazenby v. Dept. of the Air Force, 66 MSPR 514, 520 (1995), citing Kruger v. Dept. of Justice, 32 MSPR 71, 76-77 (1987) (the employees' truthful admission of their misconduct on initial inquiry by the agency was a mitigating factor); cf. Brown v. Dept. of the Air Force, 67 MSPR 500, 509 (1995) (the employee showed no potential for rehabilitation where he attempted to rationalize his actions without acknowledging that they were inappropriate, and his lack of comprehension of wrongdoing raised serious concerns about his judgment, reliability, veracity, trustworthiness and ethical conduct).

See Stuhlmacher v. USPS, 89 MSPR 272, 282 ¶ 28 (2001) (when the appellant brought earlier misconduct to the agency's attention, there was a suggestion of rehabilitative potential); cf. Chavez v. SBA, 121 MSPR 168, 180 ¶ 17, 2014 MSPB 37 (2014) ("We find, moreover, that the agency's attempt to use the appellant's acknowledgment of his wrongdoing to counter the administrative judge's finding of an approbatory environment is a misapplication of Douglas. See Casarez v. Department of the Army, 70 M.S.P.R. 131, 134 (1996) (the truthful admission of misconduct is a mitigating, not an aggravating, factor).").
