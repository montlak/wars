4. Annuity Appeal

In a disability retirement case the preponderant evidence rule still applies and, OPM overpayment recovery and agency-initiated disability retirement cases aside, the burden of proof is on the appellant, Thomas v. OPM, 54 MSPR 686, 688-89 (1992):

The appellant bears the burden of proving by preponderant evidence that he is entitled to disability retirement.... The final determination must be based on all probative value of all of the evidence submitted, taking into account objective clinical findings, diagnoses and medical opinions, subjective evidence of pain and disability, and all evidence relating to the effect of the appellant's condition on his ability to perform in the position last occupied....

Armstrong v. OPM, 7 MSPR 412, 415 (1981), added:

...The presiding official's analysis in this case is similar to the now well established procedures for adjudicating Title VII complaints of prohibited discrimination. See McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792, 93 S. Ct. 1817, 36 L. Ed.2d 668 (1973). Essentially, the presiding official first examined the evidence of record to determine whether there was some substance to appellant's claim of total disability. The presiding official then found that appellant had established a prima facie case that his sensitivity to tobacco smoke effectively disabled him from performing the duties of his position, and that while the burden of persuasion remained on appellant, it now became OPM's burden to show by "objective evidence" that appellant's claim did not warrant disability retirement. The presiding official next examined the arguments and evidence presented by OPM, but found that OPM had failed to rebut appellant's case and concluded: "Therefore, I find that a preponderance of the evidence presented supports a finding that appellant is totally disabled...."

The Board finds this approach to the adjudication of a voluntary application for Civil Service disability retirement benefits to be in error. Murray v. Office of Personnel Management, 7 MSPB 235, n.3, 7 MSPR 352, n.3 (1981). As we held in Chavez v. Office of Personnel Management, 6 MSPB 343, 6 MSPR 404 (1981), it is the applicant who bears the burden of persuasion by a preponderance of the evidence in an appeal before the Board from an OPM disability retirement decision. Id., 353, 6 MSPR 417. Moreover, because of the nature of a voluntary application for disability retirement benefits, i.e, that the applicant is seeking entitlement to a statutory benefit, the burden of persuasion remains on the applicant to demonstrate that he or she meets the statutory criteria for entitlement. Id., 351-52, 6 MSPR 414-16. The applicant is not entitled to the benefits of a presumption, or a prima facie case as it was described by the presiding official, in meeting his or her burden. The ultimate determination of whether the applicant met his or her burden of proof should have been based solely on the weight of the relevant evidence presented. Id., 358-59, 6 MSPR 422-24.

Thus, in the case at hand, appellant was required to show by a preponderance of the evidence that he satisfied the criteria for total disability as set forth at 5 USC § 8331(6). The inadequacy of the OPM response had no bearing on appellant's burden of persuasion....

See Cohron v. OPM, 96 MSPR 466, 469 ¶ 10 (2004) (OPM must prove an overpayment by preponderant evidence); Balkovec v. OPM, 83 MSPR 621, 622 ¶ 4 (1999) (a claimant to an annuity must prove his entitlement by preponderant evidence); Arcinas v. OPM, 82 MSPR 603, 608 ¶ 13 (1999) ("The appellant has the burden of proving his entitlement to the annuity he seeks."); Wilkey-Marzin v. OPM, 82 MSPR 200, 204 ¶ 4 (1999) ("In an appeal from OPM's denial of a voluntary disability retirement application, an employee bears the burden of persuasion by a preponderance of the evidence."); Prestien v. OPM, 8 MSPR 698, 704-05 (1981) (burden is on an annuitant to demonstrate by preponderant evidence his continuing entitlement to disability retirement); cf. McLaughlin v. OPM, 62 MSPR 536, 549-50 (1994) ("The appellant has failed to identify any single specific issue that the chief administrative judge left unaddressed. Thus, his allegation fails to set forth sufficient specificity to warrant Board review. See, e.g., Tines v. Department of the Air Force, 56 M.S.P.R. 90, 92 (1992).").

The burden is on the agency if it initiated the retirement application on grounds of mental disability. 5 USC 8347(d); Lindahl v. OPM, 718 F.2d 391 (Fed. Cir. 1983), rev'd on other grounds, 105 S. Ct. 1620 (1985). Although the agency that initiates a disability retirement case has the burden of proof before the Board, the employee may fully participate as a party, Daley v. OPM and DHHS, 11 MSPR 601, 604 (1982):

Once the employee's role in an agency initiated disability retirement application is determined to be that of a party, and noting again that the agency may complete the disability retirement application process, without that party's involvement, it becomes clear that the party initiating the action is the employing agency. The employing agency...has the burden to prove disability by a preponderance of the evidence. The appeal to the Board by the employee will bring before the Board all the parties to the disability retirement application process, and will subject that process to de novo review by the Board. The employing agency may be assisted or opposed by the employee in carrying its burden of proof.

When annuity entitlements, or overpayment disputes, are involved in the appeal, OPM is supposed to explain the basis for its figures. See Cohron v. OPM, 96 MSPR 466, 475 ¶ 22 (2004) ("Although the Board does not require actual calculations if a formula is given, in this case, OPM provided neither."). If an issue in a retirement case, e.g., a presumption of death, requires analysis of foreign law, and the Board has jurisdiction over the retirement appeal, "the fact that one or more issues in the case should be decided under the substantive law of another state or country does not preclude the forum from adjudicating the case or from applying its own procedures in doing so." Alvarez v. OPM, 64 MSPR 534, 536 (1994). [Refer to Chapter 14 for retirement appeals; refer to Chapter 6, "Adverse Action Related to Disability," for analysis of burdens of proof.]
