g. Reassignment With Pay Reduction in Anticipation of RIF

Timing of the steps taken by the agency and the response by the employee may determine whether an employee was subjected to a personnel action constituting an appealable RIF. An employee may appeal a RIF if she receives a notice that she will be separated through a RIF and, before separation, she applies for and accepts a lower-graded position in another competitive area through the agency's voluntary placement program. Swift v. Dept. of Educ., 23 MSPR 210, 211-12 (1984). A voluntary reassignment with a downgrading is not a RIF, even if the reassignment is undertaken during a RIF, if the appellant was offered a job that would entail no pay loss, Perez v. DOD, NY-0351-09-0349-I-1 (NP 9/30/2010):

The Board's jurisdiction over RIF-related appeals is provided in 5 C.F.R. § 351.901, which states that an employee who has been furloughed for more than 30 days, separated, or demoted by a RIF may appeal to the Board. Demotion is defined in pertinent part as a change of an employee, while serving continuously in the same agency, to a lower grade when both the old and new positions are under the General Schedule or to a position with a lower rate of pay when both the old and new positions are under the same type ungraded wage schedule, or are in different pay method categories. 5 C.F.R. § 210.102(b)(4). In general, voluntary changes in employment do not constitute RIF demotions. See Torain v. U.S. Postal Service, 83 F.3d 1420, 1423 (Fed. Cir. 1996). In Harants v. U.S. Postal Service, 130 F.3d 1466, 1469 (Fed. Cir. 1997), the court established an exception to the general rule, holding that an assignment to a lower-graded position constitutes a RIF demotion, even when the employee voluntarily applies for or is offered an assignment to that position, as long as the assignment was made after the agency had informed the employee that his original position had been abolished and that he had not been selected for an assignment to a position at his former grade level.

In this case, as the administrative judge properly found, at the time the appellant asked to be placed in the lower-graded Education Technician position, he had been notified that his placement under the RIF would be to a Teacher (Elementary) position. The agency submitted evidence in the form of a sworn declaration from a Human Resources (HR) Officer explaining that, prior to the RIF, the appellant encumbered the position of Education Technologist, AD-1701-11; that "AD" indicated that his salary was "Administratively Determined"; that he was a bargaining unit member of the Antilles Consolidated Education Association (ACEA) which includes all nonsupervisory/nonmanagerial professional employees, i.e., Teachers, Psychologists, Education Technologists, Physical Therapists, Guidance Counselors; that all such members are paid according to the same pay schedule that was negotiated between the agency and ACEA; that "1701" referred to the appellant's occupation and that "11" indicated Bachelors Degree, his pay lane level; that he was notified on May 28, 2009, of his reassignment under the RIF to the position of Teacher (Elementary), AD-1710-00; that, once he was placed in the position, the "00" pay lane designator would have been changed to "11" to reflect his academic level of Bachelors Degree; and that therefore the reassignment, had he not rejected it, would not have affected his grade level, pay level, pay system, hours of work, or benefits. The appellant did not challenge the information contained in the HR Officer's declaration (apart from suggesting his own definition of "demotion"). Under these circumstances, the administrative judge correctly found that the Harants exception to the general rule does not apply here, that the appellant's acceptance of the lower-graded Education Technician position was voluntary, and that therefore the Board lacks jurisdiction over this appeal.

The appellant's "alternate view of what is a demotion" does not compel a different result. He suggests that an Education Technologist requires more training and technical skills than an elementary School Teacher, and he appears to suggest that his limited experience teaching first-graders was brief and long ago, such that his assignment would not have been in the best interest of the students. Neither of these arguments is availing since demotion depends on whether there has been a reduction in grade or pay, not responsibilities, see Hayes v. U.S. Postal Service, 390 F.3d 1373, 1379 (Fed. Cir. 2004), and the appellant's reticence about returning to classroom teacher duties is not evidence that he lacks the qualifications to do so.

Because the appellant voluntarily accepted the Education Technician position, the administrative judge correctly found that the Board lacks jurisdiction to consider the appellant's argument that, based on events that occurred in 2004, the individual who displaced him should not have been an Education Technologist in the first place. She also correctly found that, in the absence of Board jurisdiction, she lacked the authority to consider the appellant's allegation that the agency committed prohibited personnel practices. Wren v. Department of the Army, 2 M.S.P.R. 1, 2 (1980), aff'd, 681 F.2d 867, 871‑73 (D.C. Cir. 1982).

An agency must, when a downgrading or pay reduction is involved, carefully explain options to an employee when the downgrade or pay reduction is offered as an option connected with a reassignment. The unexplained option could include a RIF with its attendant placement rights, Jones v. USDA, 117 MSPR 276, 281 ¶¶ 14-15, 2012 MSPB 10 (2012):

[T]he agency, upon notifying the appellant that her GS-6 MA position had been abolished, effectively offered her the following four options: reassignment to the GS-6 Secretary position in Illinois; downgrade to the GS-5 RMA position in Beltsville; resignation; or retirement. The agency did not advise the appellant that she could refuse all four options, or what the consequences of such a refusal would be. Given that the agency repeatedly characterized its Work Reduction Procedures as a mechanism for avoiding a RIF, the appellant's refusal of the positions offered pursuant to those procedures might have resulted in the appellant's release from her competitive level pursuant to a RIF action. Conversely, the appellant might have been subjected to an adverse action removal for refusing a directed reassignment to the GS-6 Secretary position in Illinois. The agency likewise failed to advise the appellant of her Board appeal rights in the event of either such outcome.

An agency is required to provide an employee with information that is not only correct in nature, but adequate in scope to allow the employee to make an informed decision. Miller v. Department of Homeland Security, 111 M.S.P.R. 325, ¶ 8 (2009), aff'd, 361 Fed. Appx. 134 (Fed. Cir. 2010). Where, as here, an employee decision is made with "blinders on," based on misinformation or a lack of information, such a decision is involuntary. Id. Accordingly, we find that the appellant's "acceptance" of a downgrade to the GS-5 RMA position was involuntary. Moreover, as the administrative judge further noted, where the Board finds that an employee-initiated action is involuntary, it must be reversed by operation of law. See Baldwin v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 111 M.S.P.R. 586, ¶ 46 (2009).
