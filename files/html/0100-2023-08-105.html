4. Preappointment Conditions

5 CFR 315.805 (2022), establishing the conditions for termination of probationers for preappointment conditions, is reprinted earlier in this chapter, "Appeal of Probationary Termination." That regulation limits the matters that may be raised on appeal to procedures (notice, reply, and consideration of the reply) and discrimination (partisan political reasons and marital status). A probationer seeking to appeal termination based on a preappointment condition must allege a procedural regulatory deficiency. Coleman v. VA, 3 MSPR 274, 276 (1980), noted as to a claim that the appellant was hired contrary to an examining physician's recommendation:

Thus, in order for the Board to acquire jurisdiction under 5 CFR section 315.806(c), the appellant must allege that his termination was not effected in accordance with the procedural requirements of 5 CFR section 315.805, which provides that the employee is entitled to: (a) advance written notice of the proposed adverse action and the reasons therefor; (b) a reasonable time to respond thereto and to have his response considered by the agency in making its decision; and (c) written notice of and reasons for the adverse decision at or before the effective time of the action, information pertaining to appeal rights, and time limitations involved in exercising those rights.

Petitioner has made no allegations of procedural error committed by the agency in her termination under 5 CFR section 315.805. In fact the record reflects that the agency has complied with all the procedural requirements imposed by that section. Petitioner has simply alleged that, because the agency appointed her prior to receipt of the results of a physical examination upon which her appointment was conditional and which included a recommendation by the examining physician against her hiring, the agency committed procedural error in her appointment and subsequent termination. Such actions by the agency do not constitute procedural errors in termination under 5 CFR section 315.805 for which petitioner is afforded a right of appeal under 5 CFR section 315.806(c). Therefore, the Board was without jurisdiction to entertain the appeal....

Summarized by West v. DHHS, 122 MSPR 434, 437-38 ¶ 7, 2015 MSPB 34 (2015):

When an agency terminates a probationary employee for reasons based in whole or in part on conditions arising before her appointment, it must follow the procedures set forth in 5 C.F.R. § 315.805. A probationer whose termination is subject to 5 C.F.R. § 315.805 may appeal her termination to the Board on the ground that it was not effected in accordance with these procedural requirements. 5 C.F.R. § 315.806(c). In such appeals, the merits of the agency's decision are not before the Board. Hope v. Department of the Army, 108 M.S.P.R. 6, ¶ 7 (2008). Rather, if an appellant nonfrivolously alleges that she was terminated based in whole or part on pre-appointment reasons, and that the agency failed to afford her the procedural protections of 5 C.F.R. § 315.805, then the Board has jurisdiction to determine whether the agency, in fact, failed to follow the procedures of 5 C.F.R. § 315.805, and, if so, whether such procedural error was harmful. See Hope, 108 M.S.P.R. 6, ¶ 7; see also Pope v. Department of the Navy, 62 M.S.P.R. 476, 479 (1994). If there was harmful error, the agency action must be set aside. Banghart v. Department of the Army, 96 M.S.P.R. 453, ¶ 6 (2004), aff'd, 137 Fed. Appx. 350 (Fed. Cir. 2005).

The regulatory requirements for termination of a probationer for preappointment conditions were noted in Winn v. MSPB, 335 Fed. Appx. 970 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP). The procedure available to the probationer to challenge termination for a preappointment condition is only for the competitive service employee, unless some other statute or regulation incorporates those protections by reference. DeSantis v. Dept. of Transp., NY-0752-14-0074-I-1 (NP 10/10/2014) ("However, nonpreference-eligible excepted service probationary employees do not have the right to appeal to the Board on this basis. See Barrand v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 112 M.S.P.R. 210, ¶ 13 (2009)."). [Refer to Chapter 11 on harmful error.]

An employee complaining of a procedurally defective probationary termination should specify the procedural defect in the appeal. See Hunter v. DOJ, 73 MSPR 290, 295 (1997). To obtain a Board hearing on an allegation of improper termination for preappointment conditions, the appellant must make a bona fide allegation of a preappointment condition leading to termination, Seubert v. Dept. of Treasury, 15 MSPR 510, 512 (1983):

...Although appellant asserted that two persons who, the record shows, recommended appellant's termination, considered the prior "incident," there is nothing in those recommendations raising an inference of such consideration, nothing in the decision notice raising such an inference, and no assertion by appellant that the decision making official considered or was even aware of that incident. Thus, we conclude that no hearing was required under the circumstances of this case.

Although an agency may refer to current working conditions or unsatisfactory performance in a probationary termination letter, the appellant is entitled to a hearing on whether he was denied preappointment protections if the allegations in his appeal bring into question the basis for the termination. In Milanak v. Dept. of Transp., 90 MSPR 219, 223-24 ¶ 10 (2001), the appellant's probationary termination letter referred to poor performance, but just a few weeks before the termination the appellant's supervisor informed the personnel office that the appellant was doing good work. The appellant asserted that just prior to the termination, his supervisor completed an investigation that disclosed adverse information concerning the appellant's prior government employment. The evidence warranted a jurisdictional hearing.

The appellant need not prove that an agency procedural defect constituted harmful error to establish Board jurisdiction under 5 CFR 315.806(c); she need only make a nonfrivolous allegation that the agency did not comply with the procedural requirements of 5 CFR 315.805. Parker-Stuckey v. Dept. of Treasury, 46 Fed. Appx. 625, 628 (Fed. Cir. 2002 NP) (once jurisdiction is established, to prevail the appellant must demonstrate the procedural error was harmful error (citing Pierce v. GPO, 70 F.3d 106, 109 (Fed. Cir. 1995)). If a hearing is conducted, the agency may defeat Board jurisdiction if witnesses credibly testify they did not consider preappointment reasons but instead terminated the probationer for performance problems. See Milanak v. Dept. of Transp., 76 Fed. Appx. 323, 326 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP). Probationers removed for preappointment reasons are entitled to MSPB review to ensure that they have received their proper procedural rights, but they are not entitled to have the MSPB review the sufficiency of the grounds relied upon for removal. 5 CFR 315.805, 315.806(c) (2022). The merits of a case or the rehabilitation or performance of an employee will not be considered as a defense. Gaxiola v. Dept. of Air Force, 6 MSPR 515, 518, 6 MSPB 432 (1981). Because the probationary employee has no property right in his position, the limited review grounds are constitutionally sufficient. Webb v. NLRB (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 87-3140).
