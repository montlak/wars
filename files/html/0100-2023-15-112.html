1. Guard, Correctional Officer, Police, Investigator, Security Official

"[C]orrectional or law enforcement officers are held to a higher standard of conduct with respect to the seriousness of their offenses." Todd v. DOJ, 71 MSPR 326, 330 (1996) (removal for a violation of the laws against drunken driving and the failure of the appellant to report his own arrest; appellant misused official credentials in an attempt to obtain "professional courtesy" in a DWI arrest); see Welch v. VA, 226 Fed. Appx. 982, 986 (Fed. Cir. 2007 NP) (recounting arbitrator's award for facts that included misuse of credentials by an agency police officer); Compton v. DOJ, 198 Fed. Appx. 969, 972 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP) (demotion of prison computer services manager based on the violation of agency standards of conduct by being under the influence of alcohol on duty or immediately prior to reporting for duty).

The Board recognizes that there is considerable concern in a prison environment about how employees interact with inmates. A correctional employee's acceptance of an unauthorized gift of money from an inmate warranted dismissal. The appellant's actions "showed a susceptibility to manipulation by inmates, thus posing a threat to the security of the institution." Perrodin v. DOJ, 55 MSPR 407, 412 (1992). A correctional officer was properly discharged when he did not follow institutional rules and engaged in irresponsible behavior in the presence of inmates during an escorted town trip. Albin v. DOJ, 25 MSPR 305, 309 (1984). It was proper to remove a correctional officer who had an improper relation with an inmate. The relation jeopardized inmate discipline. Bain v. DOJ, 15 MSPR 515, 518 (1983); see Smith v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 1995 NP No. 94-3353) (removal for an improper relationship between a correctional officer and an inmate). Removals of a correctional officer and a recreational specialist were sustained after they became involved in a business transaction with an inmate. Bolton v. DOJ, 26 MSPR 658, 663 (1985) ("[T]he Board has recognized the sensitivity involved in personnel-inmate relationships in correctional institutions and the delicate balance which must be maintained in the interest of propriety."); see Tucker v. DOJ, 70 Fed. Appx. 548, 551 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP) (demotion of correctional supervisor sustained for authorizing the improper meeting of two inmates); Plath v. DOJ, 12 MSPR 421, 425 (1982) (removal of a cook foreman for failing to supervise an inmate properly during an escorted trip and failing to report the unauthorized absence of the inmate: "[C]onduct by any employee, which facilitates a breakdown in security or discipline of the inmates, represents a danger to the health and safety of employees in the institution, as well as that of the inmates[.] "); cf. Young v. DOJ, 48 MSPR 585, 589 (1991) (suspension of a legal technician whose inappropriate handling of inmate property caused a security breach).

If there is a custom that permits the acceptance of a painting from an inmate, the painting cannot be considered contraband in violation of prison regulations that would otherwise justify the dismissal of the employee. Elkins v. DOJ, 4 MSPR 230, 231, 4 MSPB 306 (1980). The failure of a prison correctional officer to consult with supervisors concerning conduct of escorted inmate trips to a convention did not warrant removal. The employee consulted with coworkers concerning past practice; there was no showing that the conduct of the escorted trip was contrary to prison policy; and other individuals with similar offenses were not removed. Albin v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 1986 NP No. 85-2043); Albin v. DOJ, 30 MSPR 572, 572 (1986) (remand following judicial review; penalty mitigated to a 14-day suspension).

A Bureau of Prisons recreational specialist was removed for removing contraband from the grounds of a federal correctional institution. Hickman v. DOJ, 11 MSPR 153, 156 (1982) ("It is essential that the Department of Justice be able to exercise wide discretion in controlling the work-related conduct of those employees charged with maintaining the integrity of our prison system."). A correctional officer was removed for leaving his post and the facility without permission. There was recent past discipline; the offense was not mitigated by the officer's attempt to have another officer relieve him from his post. Porties v. DOJ, 22 MSPR 181, 183 (1984); see Boatman v. DOJ, 66 MSPR 58, 64 (1994) (suspension of a correctional officer charged with violating post orders by leaving a vehicle unattended with a weapon in it); Harrison v. Dept. of Treasury, 42 MSPR 614, 622 (1989) (discipline of a customs patrol officer who left a surveillance area without permission); Barger v. DOJ, 11 MSPR 170, 175 (1982) (removal of a correctional officer who refused to report to his work station because of an unjustified fear for his safety); Speigel v. DOJ, 8 MSPR 50, 51 (1981) (charge that a physician assistant failed to carry out an order to respond to a medical emergency in agency jail during other than normal duty hours). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Transaction With Patron, Vendor, Inmate."]

Demotion was mitigated to a 15-day suspension when a supervisory correctional officer was verbally abusive to an inmate. Davis v. DOJ, 63 MSPR 360, 369 (1994) (testimony that such incidents were generally treated as performance rather than misconduct problems). Removal of a detention enforcement officer was sustained after an incident involving verbal and minor physical abuse of a detainee. Valdez v. DOJ, 65 MSPR 390, 399 (1994) (charges also involved the implication of improper use of the appellant's position to gain access to the detainee); see Gilley v. DOJ, 11 MSPR 316, 320 (1982) (demotion and reassignment of a supervisory correctional officer based on an altercation with an inmate and failure to preserve evidence of the incident).

Possession of an unauthorized weapon (a lead-filled rubber pipe) by an appellant who apparently was in on-duty status warranted removal of a GS-5 Police Officer. Agency policy prohibiting possession of the weapon was based in part on the danger that a psychiatric patient might obtain access to the weapon. Torres v. VA, 22 MSPR 83, 85 (1984); see Caronia v. DOJ, 78 MSPR 201, 216 (1998) (overruled on other grounds, Southerland v. Dept. of Defense, 119 MSPR 566, 2013 MSPB 46 (2013)) (combination of factors, including depression causing inattention to duty, resulted in the mitigation of removal to suspension of a supervisory security officer who unintentionally brought a weapon in his car into a nonsecured area of a correctional facility); Filip v. VA, 15 MSPR 329, 333 (1983) (mitigating to a reprimand the removal of a police officer for bringing a firearm onto hospital grounds; supervisors tolerated officers carrying handguns to and from work). Repeated improper use of agency credentials to obtain firearms warranted removal of a Bureau of Prisons electronics technician with an otherwise good record. Padilla v. DOJ, 64 MSPR 416, 423 (1994). A security guard, occupying a "position where the utmost honesty and dependability are essential," was removed for theft of several sheets of lead worth about $100. Gilmore v. Dept. of Army, 7 MSPR 253, 255-56, 7 MSPB 155 (1981).

Inappropriate display of agency credentials and a service weapon during an off-duty argument sustained a suspension. Chandler v. Dept. of Treasury, 13 MSPR 90, 96 (1982) (customs pilot); see Anderson v. DOJ, 62 MSPR 611, 615 (1994) (border patrol agent was suspended for the improper display of credentials); Connolly v. DOJ, 18 MSPR 39, 41-42 (1983) (false or improper off-duty arrest by deputy marshal, with the display of a weapon, warranted removal); Majette v. USPS, 16 MSPR 419, 426 (1983) (removal of a police officer for a carrying pistol without a license and misrepresenting his status as a postal inspector at the time of arrest); Haldeman v. Dept. of Treasury, 15 MSPR 482, 484-85 (1983) (removal of a criminal investigator who, without cause, conducted a search of a hotel lounge patron and "gave the impression of public drunkenness"; a prior disciplinary history existed). But see Stines v. DOJ, 22 MSPR 511, 514-15 (1984), rev'd, Stines v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 1985 NP No. 85-630) (court overturned removal of a supervisory correctional officer based on an off-duty incident involving the display of a weapon during an altercation). [Stines is discussed in Chapter 7, "Law Enforcement Officer; Giglio Impairment" and "Conduct Contrary to Agency Mission."]

Removal is appropriate for a guard who sleeps on duty. It is not necessary for the offense to cause demonstrable harm. See Hyatt v. Dept. of Army, 30 MSPR 256, 260 (1986) (sleeping on the job and improper or no entries in log); Broadnax v. Dept. of Navy, 22 MSPR 538, 540 (1984) (appellant was on notice of the possible consequences of his actions); Hipolito v. Dept. of Navy, 11 MSPR 157, 158 (1982) (guard was removed for sleeping on duty); cf. Gmitro v. Dept. of Army, 95 MSPR 89, 96 ¶ 20 (2003) (removal of police officer who slept in his vehicle instead of assisting guards conducting vehicle searches during period of high security precautions; the agency need not prove that harm resulted from the appellant's inattention). Removal of a border patrol officer for sleeping on the job was justified. The situation encompassed potential danger to others, Ramirez v. Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv. (Fed. Cir. 1983 NP No. 83-879), aff'g Ramirez v. Dept. of Treasury, 14 MSPR 291, 293 (1983):

Although there is evidence in the record that drowsiness is a common problem for Customs Patrol Officers and that sleeping on duty has occurred when officers, who have not been removed, were working in pairs, the instant situation constitutes a much more serious offense. The appellant fell asleep while he was alone and while he was serving as the backup and radio link for other officers who were patrolling a border area on foot and in the dark. His sleeping, under these circumstances, occurred in a potentially dangerous situation in which the safety of others depended upon his attention to duty.

[Refer earlier in this chapter to "Loafing; Wasting Time" and "Inattention to Duty; Sleeping on Job."]

Removal of a police officer was sustained for unauthorized entry into carpentry and paint shops during an evening, for use of an unauthorized master key, and for failing to note his entry into the secured areas in official duty logs. Perryman v. VA, 14 MSPR 17, 20 (1982). Removal was sustained of a Secret Service uniformed patrol officer who, while on duty, engaged in the nonwork-related activity of touring an exhibit in a hotel. Perez v. Dept. of Treasury, 26 MSPR 546, 549 (1985). The Board sustained removal of a criminal investigator who was involved in an off-duty altercation with his wife leading to a criminal sentence of three years of probation. Of concern was the appellant's lack of judgment and impulse control, his lack of candor, and the agency's concern that the appellant's guilty plea could be used against him if he were called to testify in a trial as an investigator. Carlton v. DOJ, 95 MSPR 633, 638 ¶ 8 (2004). A federal air marshal was removed for charges that included inappropriate confrontations in nonwork related civilian settings. Ayres v. DHS, 280 Fed. Appx. 991 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP). [Refer to Chapter 7, "Conduct Contrary to Agency Mission" and "Law Enforcement Officer; Giglio Impairment"; refer earlier in this chapter to "Lautenberg Amendment."]
