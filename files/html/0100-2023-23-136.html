3. Enforcement Procedure

Enforcement can only be initiated as to a final Board decision order; initial decisions pending challenge by PFRs are not within the regional enforcement authority. 5 CFR 1201.181(a) (2022), restating 5 USC 1204(a)(2), providing interim relief authority, does not expand upon regional enforcement authority. See Sparrow v. USPS, 48 MSPR 246, 251 (1991). If a PFR of an initial decision is filed by an agency, the initial decision is not final; a petition for enforcement is premature. Thomas v. USPS, 65 MSPR 268, 271 n.* (1994) (AJ should have dismissed as premature an enforcement petition for a decision that was not final because of the pendency of the appellant's PFR); Peebles v. Dept. of Navy, 5 MSPR 488, 489 n.1, 5 MSPB 486 (1981) ("Because a petition for review had been timely filed, the initial decision was not final and the respondent's petition for enforcement was premature.").

Enforcement proceedings, or an appeal, may also be used to challenge the agency's failure to properly conclude an indefinite suspension. Compare Rhodes v. MSPB, 487 F.3d 1377 (Fed. Cir. 2007) (separate appeal), with Hofmann v. USDA, 31 MSPR 399, 401 (1986) ("[a]ppellant should not be foreclosed from challenging the continuing nature of the suspension now because he recognized that an earlier challenge would waste his, the agency's, and the Board's time and resources[.]"). [Refer to Chapter 1, "Indefinite Suspension" and to Chapter 2, "Indefinite Suspension; Enforced Leave."]

Enforcement is initiated at the regional level. The AJ assigned to the case decided on the merits ordinarily decides the enforcement petition. See Wright v. Dept. of Transp., 28 MSPR 419, 421-22 (1985) (under the Board's delegation scheme, AJ rather than Board regional director may adjudicate an enforcement petition). The appellant's enforcement claims will not ordinarily be heard for the first time before the Board or during review of an AJ's enforcement recommendations when they could and should have been earlier raised in enforcement proceedings before the AJ. Edwards v. DOJ, 90 MSPR 537, 542 ¶ 8 (2002); see Lowe v. VA, 85 MSPR 219, 223 ¶ 8 (2000) (Board declines to consider an enforcement allegation that was not first raised with the AJ); Unhoch v. USPS, 66 MSPR 651, 658 (1995) ("even if the matter had been clearly raised as evidence of noncompliance, we find that it was untimely, inasmuch as it was not presented to the administrative judge"); see also Bowden v. USPS, 95 MSPR 505, 510-11 ¶ 13 (2004) ("Generally, the Board will not consider contentions of noncompliance raised for the first time in response to a compliance recommendation where there has been no showing that the appellant was previously unaware of the alleged noncompliance.").

As to the appellant, compliance issues must be raised promptly so that they do not become collateral matters in subsequent appeals. The point was illustrated in Gammal v. MSPB, 671 F.2d 654 (1st Cir. 1982). The employee was removed after failing to accept a position offered in response to a Board decision in a prior case. The employee defended the AWOL charges by arguing that he had not taken the position because it was not assigned to him in compliance with the initial Board reinstatement decision. The court decided that the employee was properly removed for AWOL: he had the chance to provide more evidence concerning compliance with the initial Board decision and he elected not to do so. Appellant failed to offer evidence to show lack of compliance with the Board decision ordering his reinstatement; the Board's decision on the subsequent AWOL case was supported by substantial evidence. The Board's decision could not be collaterally attacked on the basis of a compliance challenge that should have been earlier raised. [Refer to Wright v. Dept. of Transp., 24 MSPR 550, 553-54 (1984), and 28 MSPR 419, 421-22 (1985), later in this chapter, "Reinstatement to the Same Position; Status Quo Ante Remedy."]

The rule that enforcement issues should be raised at the regional level is subject to practical considerations. Although enforcement is normally first addressed at the regional level, if an AJ fails to address a compliance issue and other issues are before the Board in an enforcement proceeding, the Board may attempt to resolve the remaining compliance issue rather than remand it to the AJ. See Rose v. USPS, 77 MSPR 139, 144 n.5 (1997); Shelton v. USPS, 53 MSPR 483, 486 (1992) (remedying a compliance problem, without remand, by ordering the restoration of the employee).

Compliance issues raised initially through a PFR will be remanded to the region. Gallegos v. Dept. of Interior, 60 MSPR 5, 9 (1993) (remanding compliance issues raised by appellant in response to untimely agency PFR of initial decision approving a settlement); Mascarenas v. DOD, 57 MSPR 425, 433 (1993) (issue was remanded that was raised for the first time in appellant's PFR from a compliance initial decision); Keith v. USPS, 56 MSPR 668, 670 (1993) (remanding issue of alleged noncompliance with a settlement to the region when the issue was raised in a PFR challenging an initial decision dismissing appeal as settled); Dati v. Dept. of Navy, 41 MSPR 397, 401 (1989) (new enforcement issues raised during briefing of PFR on an enforcement dispute arising from a settlement will be remanded to the region for adjudication). But see Singletary v. USPS, 79 MSPR 76, 80 (1998) (Board advised the appellant of the option of filing a new enforcement petition with the regional office; AJ would then determine the timeliness of the petition); Jardine v. USPS, 68 MSPR 544, 547-48 (1995) (no remand when appellant made reference to enforcement in PFR but provided no further detail; appellant could raise the compliance issue with the region).

If an enforcement petition is filed with the Board, it will be remanded to the regional office for adjudication. See Allen v. USPS, 39 MSPR 38, 40 (1988). If it is unclear whether the appellant is pursuing a PFR or an enforcement petition, the appeal may be remanded to the region for clarification of the appellant's intent. But if the appellant clearly raises issues of noncompliance through a PFR, the case goes back to the region for processing as a petition for enforcement. See Shaishaa v. Dept. of Air Force, 65 MSPR 273, 276 (1994) (if the parties do not settle their differences and the Board cannot resolve the disputes on the existing record, the Board may remand an enforcement case back to the region for additional evidence and analysis of the unresolved issues); Keenan v. USPS, 59 MSPR 130, 133 (1993) ("Where a settlement agreement has been entered into the record for enforcement, and the appellant includes issues of noncompliance with the settlement agreement in a petition for review, those issues relating to noncompliance will be forwarded to the regional office...for processing as a petition for enforcement.")).

There are constraints on how and when an agency may raise issues during enforcement proceedings. An agency may not await the PFR stage of litigation to present the argument that the agency will suffer economic prejudice because of the appellant's delayed enforcement petition. Hoover v. Dept. of Navy, 57 MSPR 545, 551-52 (1993) (enforcement petition processed prior to the Board regulation setting a deadline for petitions).

A Board-accepted settlement is subject to enforcement. 5 CFR 1201.41(c)(2)(i) (2022). It is immaterial that the appellant seeks enforcement with a settlement accepted by the Board rather than a final decision issued at the conclusion of an appeal. Weiss v. Dept. of Army, 36 MSPR 230, 232 (1988). [Refer below to "Enforcement and Interpretation of Settlement Agreements."] An appellant does not have a right to compel the Special Counsel to seek Board enforcement of OSC recommendations. Patrick v. Dept. of Transp., 6 MSPR 247, 250, 6 MSPB 213 (1981). [Refer to Chapter 13, "Cases Brought by OSC."]

Board policy calls for a modicum of cooperation between the parties in enforcement matters, Petrecz v. OPM, 33 MSPR 21, 23 (1987):

...The parties are reminded that, under the Board's regulations, the agency is obligated to communicate promptly with appellant and appellant is to cooperate with the agency concerning compliance. 51 Fed. Reg. 25,164 (1986) (to be codified at 5 CFR § 1201.181(b)). If appellant believes that the agency has not complied with this Board order, he may file a petition for enforcement with the Philadelphia Regional Office in accordance with 5 CFR § 1201.182.

Should cooperative efforts fail, a party seeks relief from the Board through an enforcement petition. The AJ to whom an enforcement petition is assigned should ensure that the parties understand what is expected of them during the proceedings. The burden of proof should be clearly defined for the parties (a task normally undertaken through the acknowledgment order issued by the AJ upon assignment of the case to him or to her). The appellant should identify areas of noncompliance. If the appellant seeks compliance in particular areas, but not others, e.g., a reassignment to a different shift, the AJ should not attempt to require actions, e.g., reversal of the shift assignment, that are not desired by the appellant. Wittl v. Dept. of Air Force, 36 MSPR 20, 25-26 (1987).

The AJ should rule on procedural issues involving a compliance dispute, such as requests for discovery or a hearing, before closing the record. The AJ's recommendations or decision on the enforcement issues should fully analyze the issues and the facts. See Burford v. USPS, 56 MSPR 460, 464-65 (1993). It is insufficient for the agency to report progress toward settlement of the dispute; the agency must specifically inform the Board concerning appellant's challenge to the agency's compliance obligations. See Alston v. DHHS, 33 MSPR 218, 223 (1987). If the agency reports that it is in compliance and the appellant fails to respond to the agency submission, the Board may dismiss the petition for enforcement. See Singletary v. USPS, 84 MSPR 211, 214 ¶ 5 (1999); Elbert v. VA, 79 MSPR 441, 442 (1998) (when the appellant failed to respond to the agency's assertion of compliance, the Board accepted the agency's representation as unrebutted evidence of compliance); Abernethy v. OPM, 65 MSPR 404 (1994) (Table) (directing, as part of enforcement proceedings, that OPM issue a reconsideration decision; when, following the agency's assertion that it had complied, the appellant made no response, the enforcement petition was dismissed). The AJ may request periodic status reports, but the reporting requirement should not become so confusing that the failure to respond to one status request will result in a concession over a compliance point that is clearly in contention. See Collins v. USPS, 64 MSPR 684, 690 (1994) (dispute over back pay calculations). The AJ has the authority to dismiss an enforcement proceeding without prejudice to refiling. See Hinton-Morgan v. Dept. of Army, 81 MSPR 62, 64-65 ¶ 6 (1999) ("The administrative judge did not commit abuse of discretion in dismissing without prejudice the appellant's petition for enforcement of the settlement agreement because there was an alleged material question of fact regarding the appellant's allegation of agency breach and the parties could consult and discuss the outstanding issues and attempt to resolve those issues without the assistance of the administrative judge."); cf. Espinoza v. Dept. of Navy, 69 MSPR 679, 682 (1996) (abuse of discretion review standard was applied to the dismissal without prejudice of an enforcement petition).

Submissions to the AJ on the merits of an underlying enforcement dispute are properly rejected by the AJ if he receives them after he issues his initial or recommended compliance decision. See Peck v. OPM, 35 MSPR 175, 179 (1987) (appellant may file another enforcement petition, if it is timely to do so). If the agency does not respond to a PFR from a compliance decision, the Board may determine the entitlement of the appellant based on his submission and the AJ's decision. See El-Amin v. USPS, 46 MSPR 516, 517 (1990). It may occur that the Board decides during the processing of a PFR that an appellant has demonstrated his right to prevail on the merits of one claim, e.g., harmful procedural error. The Board then directs the agency to reinstate the appellant at the same time that the Board remands the case for further regional consideration of other issues, e.g., discrimination and reprisal. See Johnson v. Dept. of Interior, 56 MSPR 549, 553 (1993). When this occurs, the Board clearly states in its order what is required of the agency.
