4. Successive MSPB Cases

The result on a given issue in one appeal generally forecloses litigation on issues that were resolved, or could have been resolved, through a final decision on an earlier appeal between the same parties. A final decision in an earlier appeal precludes through res judicata a subsequent appeal on the same personnel action but challenged on a different affirmative defense. Under Johnson v. USPS, AT-0752-11-0464-I-1 (NP 1/31/2012):

The doctrine of res judicata thus bars the appellant from challenging his removal from the agency for a second time. See Rosado v. Department of the Air Force, 68 M.S.P.R. 662, 665 (1995) (holding that the appellant was precluded from appealing a removal which the Board had sustained in an earlier appeal), dismissed, 79 F.3d 1164 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table).

Moreover, the affirmative defenses the appellant raises in his second appeal cannot be interposed as a defense to his removal because any further challenge to the removal is barred. See Rosado, 68 M.S.P.R. at 665. Even if the appellant did not raise these claims in his prior appeal, he is barred from doing so now because he could have raised them in his first appeal. See Zgonc v. Department of Defense, 103 M.S.P.R. 666, ¶ 8 n.* (2006), aff'd, 230 F. App'x 967 (Fed. Cir. 2007) (because the appellant could have raised a whistleblower reprisal claim as an affirmative defense in the first appeal of his removal, he is barred from filing an individual right of action appeal in which he reasserted that his removal was the result of the agency's whistleblower reprisal); see also Sabersky v. Department of Justice, 91 M.S.P.R. 210, ¶¶ 7-8 (2002), aff'd, 61 F. App'x 676 (Fed. Cir. 2003) (the appellant's failure to file a retaliation claim in the first appeal of his removal does not entitle him to bring a second appeal based on the same personnel action).

If a claim or issue could not have been addressed through an earlier appeal, res judicata does not govern the later appeal. See Luecht v. Dept. of Navy, 87 MSPR 297, 306 ¶ 17 (2000) (a USERRA appeal did not bar an IRA appeal; the USERRA appeal only involved issue of whether discrimination was based on appellant's prior military service); Marshall v. Dept. of Navy, 81 MSPR 305, 312 (1999) (earlier settlement of a Board appeal did not bar through res judicata subsequent personnel actions that neither were, nor could have been, raised in a prior action). On occasion the appellant becomes confused and files what amounts to the same appeal at different Board offices. The Board sorts out the record and the resulting procedural complications at the PFR level when the multiple appeals are joined. See Smith v. DOD, 106 MSPR 228, 2007 MSPB 163 (2007).

To have preclusive effect, the Board's decision must be a final decision. If an AJ considers two appeals with similar issues, an initial decision on one issue in the first appeal does not have res judicata effect on the same issue in the second appeal unless the initial decision first treating the issue becomes final before issuance of the initial decision in the other appeal. See Luna v. Dept. of Air Force, 87 MSPR 232, 235 ¶ 6 (2000) ("[D]octrine of collateral estoppel may not be applied to a finding in an initial decision that is not final."); Schramm v. Dept. of Transp., 52 MSPR 191, 194 (1992) ("Because a final decision has been reached regarding the issue of whether the appellant's disclosure constituted whistleblowing, the appellant is now precluded from further litigating this matter.");Wagner v. EPA, 51 MSPR 326, 329 n.* (1991) ("An administrative judge...may not apply the doctrine of res judicata to an allegation that has been litigated in a prior appeal, but upon which the Board has not issued a final decision at the time of the second appeal."); Tiffany v. Dept. of Treasury, 48 MSPR 334, 337 (1991) (same result as to the application of collateral estoppel); West v. USPS, 44 MSPR 551, 561 (1990) (AJ erred by applying res judicata when the Board had not issued a final decision in a prior appeal on the allegation at issue).

That issue or claim preclusion through collateral estoppel depends on the existence of an earlier final decision was reiterated by Wade v. Dept. of Air Force, 70 MSPR 396, 398-99 (1996):

We have reopened this appeal to address the administrative judge's incorrect application of the doctrine of collateral estoppel. As the administrative judge noted, she could not apply the doctrine of res judicata to this appeal when she issued her December 28, 1995 initial decision because a petition for review was pending before the Board in Wade I and a final decision thus had not yet been issued in that case. See Holloway v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 56 MSPR 422, 425 (1993) (absent a final judgment on the merits of an action, the doctrine of res judicata cannot be applied). Instead, she applied the doctrine of collateral estoppel. This was error. The doctrine of collateral estoppel also applies only to valid and final judgments. See Ashe v. Swenson, 397 U.S. 436, 442-44, 90 S. Ct. 1189, 1194, 25 L. Ed.2d 469 (1970); see also Astoria Federal Sav. & Loan Ass'n v. Solimino, 501 U.S. 104, 106-08, 111 S. Ct. 2166, 2169, 115 L. Ed.2d 96 (1991) (the doctrines of res judicata and collateral estoppel apply to final determinations of administrative bodies acting in a judicial capacity). Our reviewing court has likewise followed this rule. See U.S. Philips Corp. v. Sears Roebuck & Co., 55 F.3d 592, 598 (Fed. Cir.) (collateral estoppel bars the relitigation of factual issues that were fully and fairly litigated and were the basis of a final judgment)....

The Board has used similar reasoning in addressing situations where a petition for review of the initial decision in an appeal was pending when a second appeal, based on the same action or issues, was filed. Under those circumstances, the Board has held, the administrative judge should not apply the doctrines of res judicata or collateral estoppel to bar adjudication of the second appeal on the merits. See Holloway, 56 MSPR at 425; Wagner v. Environmental Protection Agency, 51 MSPR 326, 329 & n.* (1991). The administrative judge therefore erred by dismissing the instant appeal based on the doctrine of collateral estoppel when the Board had not yet issued a final decision in Wade I.

We find, however, that this error has not harmed the appellant's substantive rights because dismissal of the instant appeal is now appropriate. See id. Under the doctrine of res judicata, a valid, final judgment on the merits of an action bars a second action involving the same parties or their privies based on the same cause of action. The doctrine precludes the parties from relitigating issues that were, or could have been, raised in the prior action, and it is applicable if: (1) The prior judgment was rendered by a forum with competent jurisdiction; (2) the prior judgment was a final judgment on the merits; and (3) the same cause of action and the same parties or their privies were involved in both cases. See Peartree v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 MSPR 332, 337 (1995). Those conditions have been met here. The initial decision in Wade I, which became the Board's final decision on January 26, 1996 (while the petition for review in this appeal was pending), addressed the propriety of the appellant's December 28, 1991 demotion and decided the issue against him. He has sought to contest the merits of that demotion again in the instant appeal, and the parties to the two actions are the same. The doctrine of res judicata thus bars the appellant from relitigating the issue of his demotion, and we dismiss the appeal on that basis. See id.

Separate actions, e.g., successive periods of enforced sick leave and involuntary LWOP status, even if similar, require separate decisions if separate periods of time and distinct types of leave are involved. Pletten v. Dept. of Army, 12 MSPR 595, 596 (1982) (citing Compton v. Dept. of Energy, 12 MSPR 283, 284 (1982) (agency's loss in an appeal of a prior RIF action did not collaterally estop it from running a second RIF; res judicata was not a bar because the RIFs were separate actions involving differing management reasons and lacking an identity of issues)). Dismissals of appeals with prejudice are generally considered final, and relitigation of those appeals is barred by res judicata. See Staranowicz v. Dept. of Treasury, PH-0752-11-0383-C-1 (NP 7/5/2012) ("dismissal with prejudice is a judgment on the merits for purposes of claim preclusion. Sullivan v. Department of Justice, 282 F. App'x 828 (Fed. Cir. 2008 NP); Brown v. Department of the Navy, 102 M.S.P.R. 377, ¶ 10 (2006). Therefore, the appellant's challenge to the validity of the settlement agreement is barred by the doctrine of res judicata...."). But when an appeal has been settled, the merits of the agency's action have not been examined, and the doctrine of res judicata cannot properly be applied as to matters not resolved or waived through that settlement. Brown v. Dept. of Navy, 102 MSPR 377, 383 ¶¶ 11-12, 2006 MSPB 177 (2006). Res judicata principles generally seem to arise during the consideration of appeals, but the principles apply as well to PFRs. If the Board issued a final decision through the PFR process, a later PFR will not be entertained as to an issue finally decided in the earlier PFR. See Polonchak v. USPS, 52 MSPR 405, 407 n.1 (1992). Successive enforcement petitions may be barred by res judicata. See Hambrick v. USPS, DC-3443-17-0481-I-1, n.2 (NP 7/14/2022) ("To the extent that issues raised by the appellant have been adjudicated in prior petitions for enforcement, the Board has found that such a situation is properly addressed under the doctrine of res judicata. See Senyszyn v. Department of the Treasury, 113 M.S.P.R. 453, ¶¶ 9, 12 (2010)"); Slocum v. USPS, AT-0752-07-0157-C-2 (NP 1/8/2015) ("The Board has expressly held that the doctrine of res judicata applies to issues that were raised, or could have been raised, in a petition for enforcement."); Parker v. VA, PH-0752-06-0478-C-2 (NP 2/13/2012) ("Accordingly, the appellant is precluded from reasserting her claim for loss of a shift differential because she litigated this claim in her first compliance action, and the Board denied her request. See Zgonc v. Department of Defense, 103 M.S.P.R. 666, ¶ 8 (2006) (res judicata precluded the appellant from litigating a claim that she had an opportunity to litigate in a previous appeal), aff'd, 230 F. App'x 967 (Fed. Cir. 2007)."). [Refer to Chapter 6, "Effect of Removal for Inability to Work Upon OPM Disability Annuity Determination."]

Regulation implementation review proceedings may be barred by earlier Board appeals, particularly those involving retirement appeals, raising the same issues. See Fabros v. OPM, 85 MSPR 400, 401-02 ¶ 2 (2000) (res judicata blocked a regulation implementation petition challenging the exclusion of employees serving under nonpermanent indefinite appointments from CSRS when those employees, or those in privity with them, were previously excluded from civil service retirement coverage in the course of appeals from OPM's denial to them of civil service retirement benefits); Mandadero v. OPM, 82 MSPR 302, 303-04 ¶ 3 (1999) (same result as to an appellant who lost a retirement case and then sought to raise a dispositive issue again through a regulation implementation review proceeding); Francisco v. OPM, 80 MSPR 684, 687 (1999) (regulation review was barred by an earlier retirement appeal raising the validity of regulation excluding employees serving under certain nonpermanent indefinite appointments from retirement coverage).

If because of a due process error the Board sets aside an initial decision sustaining an adverse action after a Board hearing, the agency is free to take discipline again, with due regard for due process, and the appellant may again be before an AJ--likely the same AJ who heard the first appeal. That AJ then may adopt earlier findings, with due regard for due process. Lopes v. Dept. of Navy, 119 MSPR 106, 111 ¶ 10, 2012 MSPB 135 (2012) ("While the administrative judge may incorporate testimony from the prior appeal into the record in this appeal, he must also afford the parties the opportunity to fully develop the record in this appeal, consistent with his authority to exclude irrelevant and duplicative testimony. Accordingly, while the administrative judge may incorporate hearing testimony from the first appeal into the record in this appeal, he must also allow the parties to further develop the record to the extent that it is relevant and nonduplicative of the evidence that is already in the record."); cf. Rosario-Fabregas v. Dept. of Army, NY-0752-13-0142-I-2 (NP 7/1/2016) ("Here, as in Lopes, 119 M.S.P.R. 106, ¶ 8 n.4, the concepts of claim preclusion and issue preclusion do not apply because the Board's November 30, 2011 decision vacating the first initial decision and reversing the first removal action did not address the agency's charges and did not constitute a final decision on the merits of the charges.").
