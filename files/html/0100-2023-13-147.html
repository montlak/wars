2. Medical Impairment

A removal is not defined in terms of the disciplinary motivation; it may be taken for such cause as will promote service efficiency. Although a removal is normally thought to be disciplinary, it need not be. An agency may terminate an employee for inability to perform a position. This action is not truly disciplinary, but the Board takes jurisdiction because the action is a removal resulting from the employee's inability to perform work due to a disabling condition. See Royal v. Dept. of Air Force, 21 MSPR 609, 611 (1984) (setting aside a termination for the lack of a proper disability accommodation); cf. Doe v. Hampton, 566 F.2d 265 (D.C. Cir. 1977) (a pre-Reform Act case discussing the nexus between a psychiatric illness and removal proceedings). [Refer earlier to "Enforced Leave; Constructive Suspension."]

Care should be taken to distinguish temporary from permanent disability. See Morgan v. USPS, 48 MSPR 607, 613 (1991). The Board suggested in Joyner v. Dept. of Navy, 57 MSPR 154, 161 n.7 (1993), that an agency may act on the basis of absence:

[A]n agency may initiate a non-disciplinary removal action where an employee is absent and there is no foreseeable end to such absence. See Ward v. General Services Administration, 28 MSPR 207, 209 (1985). In Schultz v. United States, 810 F.2d 1133, 1137 n.* (Fed. Cir. 1987), where the employee refused to report for duty because of alleged mental incapacitation for the position and the court found her resignation coerced, the court stated that the proper procedure would have been for the agency to have granted leave, including LWOP, and later to have removed the employee for reasons of inability to perform the duties of her position, or alternatively for excessive excused absence. Schultz is distinguishable from this case, however, because it appeared that Ms. Schultz was only temporarily incapacitated; she expressly requested only thirty days' leave. Thus, there was a foreseeable end to her absence.

If the charge is medical incapacity to work, poor performance or absence alone does not prove charges of medical incapacity justifying removal, Ellshoff v. Dept. of Interior, 76 MSPR 54, 70 (1997):

That the appellant may not have adequately performed her job duties during the six-week period between her return to work and her removal is insufficient to prove the charge, in the absence of supporting medical evidence. Although nonmedical evidence regarding deficiencies in job performance may be significant in corroborating any medical evidence of in capacity, cf., e.g., Yates v. U.S. Postal Service, 70 MSPR 172, 176-77 (1996) (removal was not sustained where, despite specific medical restrictions, the appellant continued to fully and adequately perform his job duties), such nonmedical evidence is insufficient alone to establish a charge of inability to perform job duties, as discussed above. Moreover, even aside from the lack of supporting medical evidence here, the six-week period between the appellant's return to work and her removal was simply too short to adequately gauge her ability to perform her job duties after recovering from a serious illness, particularly as she was concurrently dealing with the agency's notice of proposed removal.

The agency argues on review that: Because the appellant's medical condition is psychiatric in nature, it was more difficult to establish a nexus between the condition and the deficiencies in her job performance than if she had a physical condition; the appellant was not forthcoming with medical information; she admitted that her depression negatively affected her work performance; and there was substantial evidence that her performance was unsatisfactory. The agency then argues that, when these facts and circumstances are considered, it established that the appellant's depression and her performance deficiencies were "intrinsically related." We find that the agency's arguments are wide of the mark and otherwise without merit.

As discussed above, the record establishes that the appellant was indeed incapacitated by depression for several months. The issue, however, is whether she was incapacitated as of the date of her removal, and the record does not establish that she was, as discussed above. The agency refers to, and we are aware of, no legal support for its apparent suggestion that a lesser degree of medical evidence is required when the allegedly disabling condition is psychiatric, rather than physical, in nature. Cf. Lassiter, 60 MSPR at 145-46 (involving a charge of medical incapacity based on a psychiatric condition). Nor was the appellant required to submit medical documentation to assist the agency in proving its charge against her. See generally, e.g., Perez v. Railroad Retirement Board, 65 MSPR 287, 289 (1994) (the agency bears the burden of proving its charges by a preponderance of the evidence). We therefore concur in the AJ's determination that the agency failed to prove this charge. See Sebald, 32 MSPR at 165 (in removing an employee for medical incapacity, the agency cannot rely solely on the existence of a disabling condition or on speculation that it will affect the employee's ability to perform his job).

As to proof of the employee's ability to perform work, the Board does not consider itself bound by an OWCP determination that the employee has "fully recovered," i.e., that the employee's workers' compensation has been terminated because the employee is able to perform all the duties of the position he left or of an equivalent one. Kimble v. USPS, 47 MSPR 251, 254 (1991). As to an employee demoted for medical inability to perform his position based upon some medical evidence from the agency, under Collins v. Dept. of Navy, 41 MSPR 256, 258-59 (1989), the agency was not required to direct a medical examination as part of its burden of proof:

We find that the administrative judge erred in holding that a comprehensive medical examination was an absolute requirement of proof that the appellant was medically incapable of performing the duties of his position. Rather, we find that the agency was neither required nor authorized to order a medical examination under 5 CFR Part 339. Section 339.301(a) of Title 5, CFR, provides that "[a]n agency may require that an individual who has applied for or occupies a position which has physical/medical standards for selection or retention" to report for a medical examination under certain circumstances. Sections 339.301(b) through 339.301(d) permit an agency to order a medical examination in certain instances involving an employee's receipt of workers' compensation benefits, release from a competitive level by reduction-in-force action, or request for change in duty status, assignment, working conditions or other benefit or special treatment, based on medical reasons. The revised regulations were intended to limit significantly the authority of agencies to order medical examinations for employees in light of previous abuses, particularly in cases involving psychiatric examinations and disability retirement....

The position from which the appellant was demoted had no physical or medical standards. The agency therefore could not have required a medical evaluation under 5 CFR 339.301(a). Further, the appellant's situation did not fall within those circumstances described in 5 CFR 339.301(b)-339.301(d). Thus, although a medical examination might have provided the best evidence of the appellant's medical inability to perform his duties, we find that there was no basis on which the agency could have properly ordered the appellant to submit to such a medical examination.

Further, we note that neither the administrative judge nor the appellant pointed to any authority supporting an absolute requirement for a medical examination in cases, such as the instant, where an employee is charged with medical inability to perform his duties, and we are aware of none. While the administrative judge relied on Abatecola, as well as Tracy v. Department of the Army, 23 MSPR 115 (1984), in finding that the agency could have ordered a fitness-for-duty examination for the appellant, we find those cases distinguishable from the present case. Abatecola [v. VA, 29 MSPR 601 (1986), aff'd, 802 F.2d 471 (Fed. Cir. 1986)], unlike this case, involved the exception provided in 5 CFR § 339.301(b) for certain situations related to the receipt of workers' compensation benefits. With respect to Tracy, we note that the agency action in that case became effective on January 7, 1983, prior to the promulgation of 5 CFR § 339.301(b). Thus, the agency action there was permissible under the existing regulation.

In an unusual case, the Board determined that although an appellant did not establish a defense of mental disability to a charge that involved threats against a supervisor, her conduct seemed to be aberrant; she dressed and acted "strangely" on the day of the incident; and her conduct and past history of hospitalization should have raised the possibility with agency officials that the misconduct was due to a mental condition. The Board reduced the penalty from removal to a 90-day suspension. The appellant's return was conditioned on her providing the agency with a medical certification that she "[did] not constitute a danger to herself or other agency employees." The Board may have been suggesting that mental impairment may be a mitigating factor if it does not rise to the level of a disability as defined by EEOC regulations. Cochran v. INS, 16 MSPR 343, 352-53 (1983). [Refer to Chapter 7, "Mitigation or Aggravation of Penalties"; Chapter 8, "Medical Inability to Perform."]
