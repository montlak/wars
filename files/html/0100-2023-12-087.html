g. Pay Entitlement; Overtime; Allowances

The appellant is entitled to pay consistent with the grade and pay level held as a result of the initial decision, that is, the grade and pay level that was held at the time of the adverse action that was reversed or that is to be held as a result of mitigation effective on the date of the adverse action. The appellant is not entitled to the pay level he believes to would have received through promotion that might or could have occurred had the adverse action not occurred. See Tisdell v. Dept. of Air Force, 94 MSPR 44, 49 ¶ 9 (2003). Interim relief involving restoration of the appellant to work need not place her pay at a level that, absent her separation, would have been attained pursuant to the terms of a collective bargaining agreement. Fernand v. Dept. of Treasury, 100 MSPR 259, 263 ¶ 7 (2005).

Under McLaughlin v. USPS, 55 MSPR 192, 197-200 (1992), interim relief does not include overtime compensation unless the employee proves that he is entitled to overtime as a term or condition of employment by law, rule, regulation, collective bargaining agreement, or binding agency policy:

The WPA provides that, when an agency makes the determination not to return an employee to the place of employment, "such employee shall receive pay, compensation, and all other benefits as terms and conditions of employment pending the outcome of any petition for review...." 5 USC § 7701(b)(2)(B). The statute itself does not define either the words "pay, compensation, and...benefits," or the words "terms and conditions of employment," however. Related Federal regulations and statutory provisions nonetheless afford us some guidance.

Under 5 USC § 7511(a)(4), the term "pay" is defined as "the rate of basic pay fixed by law or administrative action for the position held by an employee." Further, according to 5 CFR § 531.202(i), an employee's basic rate of pay does not include "additional pay of any kind." See Keim v. USPS, 19 MSPR 124, 126 (1984). Under the provisions of 5 CFR parts 870-73, 890, and 831-46, the term "benefits" reasonably includes life insurance, health insurance, and retirement benefits that an employee elects and that are attendant to the position he holds. There are, however, no definitions of the term "compensation" in any Title V provision.

An elementary rule of statutory construction requires that effect be given, if possible, to every word, clause, and sentence of a statute so that no provision will be rendered superfluous, inoperative, or insignificant. See American Radio Relay League v. Federal Communications Commission, 617 F.2d 875, 879 (D.C. Cir. 1980); Ginocchi, 53 MSPR at 68. Thus, interpreting the phrase "pay, compensation, and...benefits" so as to give meaning to each word compels us to find that "compensation" means something other than "pay" or "benefits."

We conclude that the term "compensation" refers to those amounts by which an employee's "pay" is regularly augmented, including premium pay, night differential, and overtime pay. See ELM § 434.13, which includes overtime pay under "Types of Compensation." The legislative history of the WPA supports this conclusion. It provides, by way of explanation, that an employee not returned to the workplace will nevertheless receive the pay and benefits he would receive if he were working. S. Rep. No. 413, 100th Cong., 2d Sess. 35 (1988); see Ginocchi, 53 MSPR at 69-70. This interpretation is further supported by the legal definition of "compensation" which is "[r]emuneration for services rendered, whether in salary, fees, or commissions." Black's Law Dictionary 283 (6th ed. 1990).

The question in each case then becomes whether overtime is awardable as part of an interim relief order because it is a term and condition of employment under 5 USC § 7701(b)(2)(B). We note that an agency's authority to assign overtime work to an employee is discretionary in nature. See Federal Personnel Manual (FPM), ch. 550, subch. 1-3 (May 7, 1981); FPM, ch. 610, subch. 1-1 (revised July 1969). No employee is guaranteed that overtime work will be available, and no employee is entitled to overtime assignments as a matter of right. See FPM, ch. 550, subch. 1-3; FPM, ch. 610, subch. 1-1. Management retains the discretion to assign overtime duties based on the circumstances of the workplace, such as the amount of work to be done, the human resources available, and budgetary concerns. See Wells v. FAA, 755 F.2d 804, 808 (11th Cir. 1985). Because no employee has an expectation of overtime, and we discern no law, rule, or regulation that limits an agency's authority to assign overtime, we conclude that, as a general matter, overtime is not payable as a term and condition of employment under law, rule, or regulation.

The United States Supreme Court, however, in Hishon v. King & Spalding, 467 U.S. 69, 104 S. Ct. 2229, 81 L. Ed.2d 59 (1984), considered the meaning of the words "terms, conditions, or privileges of employment" as used in Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, 42 USC § 2000e. Where "the underlying employment relationship is contractual," the Court said, "'terms, conditions, or privileges of employment' clearly include benefits that are part of an employment contract." Hishon, 467 U.S. at 74, 104 S. Ct. at 2233. On the other hand, an employer-provided benefit that is not "a contractual right of employment, may qualify as a 'privileg[e]' of employment." Id. at 75, 104 S. Ct. at 2233 (original emphasis).

Although Federal employment is not contractual, Hishon suggests that the phrase "terms and conditions of employment" should be construed to include compensation to which the appellant is entitled not only under law, rule, or regulation, but also under the terms of a collective bargaining agreement or binding agency policy, but not any that is wholly within the discretion of management. The Board has long held that it will enforce the provisions of a collective bargaining agreement to which an agency is a party in the same manner as it treats provisions of the agency's regulations. See, e.g., Giesler v. Dept. of Transp., 3 MSPB 367, 3 MSPR 277, 280 (1980), aff'd sub nom., Giesier v. MSPB, 686 F.2d 844 (10th Cir. 1982). Thus, even though an agency may have complete control over whether any overtime is worked in a given period, a collective bargaining agreement or binding agency policy may control how available overtime is allocated among the employees. Where that is true, the overtime to which an employee would be entitled under the collective bargaining agreement or binding agency policy is compensation paid as a "term" or "condition" of employment.

In enforcing a final Board decision, we will include overtime pay in our back pay order when the appellant proves that, during the time period that he was improperly separated from his position, he would have received overtime pay based on the average overtime pay received by similarly situated employees. See Spezzaferro v. FAA, 807 F.2d 169, 173 (Fed. Cir. 1986); Blackmer v. Dept. of Navy, 47 MSPR 624, 628 (1991); Bonacchi v. USPS, 46 MSPR 531, 535 (1990); see also Trinity Valley Iron & Steel Co. v. NLRB, 410 F.2d 1161, 1177 n.29 (5th Cir. 1969). We believe, however, that generally requiring an agency to provide an appellant with overtime pay to be deemed in compliance with an interim relief order would unduly complicate the interim relief process and may be deemed to interfere inordinately with the agency's on-going management of its work force, particularly since the appellant can recoup any overtime pay to which he may be entitled, if and when we issue a final decision in his favor.

We therefore hold that, as a general rule, overtime pay is compensation that is not required to be provided under an interim relief order.[4] We also hold that, as an exception to the rule, in those instances in which an employee proves that he is entitled to overtime as a term or condition of employment by virtue of law, rule, regulation, collective bargaining agreement, or binding agency policy, the overtime that he would have worked had he been returned to duty is payable under an interim relief order.

[4] The Board recognizes that the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) has recently issued regulations interpreting the interim relief provision of the WPA. 57 Fed. Reg. 3707, 3712-13 (1992) (to be codified at 5 CFR part 772, subpart A). It appears that OPM's interpretation may differ from that set forth in this opinion. Id. The Board, however, is not required to defer to OPM's interpretation. See Ginocchi, 53 MSPR at 71 n.7. In enacting the interim relief provision, Congress chose to codify it in 5 USC § 7701, a section which authorizes only the Board to issue implementing regulations. 5 USCA § 7701(k) (West Supp. 1991). As a result, it is the Board, not OPM, that is the principal administrative agency charged with administering and interpreting the interim relief provision. Accordingly, OPM's interpretation of the interim relief provision is not entitled to deference where it conflicts with the Board's interpretation. See Ginocchi, 53 MSPR at 71 n.7.

In this appeal, the appellant asserts that he is entitled to approximately 15-20 hours of overtime pay per week because he worked an average of approximately 15-20 hours of overtime per week prior to his removal. He does not claim, nor does he present any evidence to show, that his purported entitlement to overtime pay stems from law, rule, or regulation, or from the provisions of a collective bargaining agreement or binding agency policy. We find that the appellant has failed to establish that entitlement to overtime is a term or condition of his employment requiring the agency to pay him for overtime he allegedly would have worked as part of the administrative judge's interim relief order and, thus, he is not entitled to overtime as part of the administrative judge's order of interim relief.

See Archerda v. DOD, 121 MSPR 314, 321 ¶ 14, 2014 MSPB 49 (2014) ("Overtime pay is only to be provided pursuant to an interim relief order when the appellant proves that he is entitled to it as a term or condition of employment by virtue of law, rule, regulation, collective bargaining agreement, or binding agency policy. Bahrke v. U.S. Postal Service, 98 M.S.P.R. 513, ¶ 8 (2005). While the appellant claims he was entitled to overtime pay pursuant to a collective bargaining agreement, the provisions of the agreement he submitted do not guarantee overtime. Rather, they state that "overtime shall be assigned on a rotational basis" and that "[m]andatory overtime will be required anytime that a shift is below minimum staffing requirements and no other employee has volunteered to work the overtime." A mere assertion of entitlement to overtime pay, such as the appellant has made, is insufficient to show that the agency is in noncompliance with the interim relief order. See id.").

The exception to McLaughlin, overtime entitlement stemming from a provision of a negotiated agreement, was applied in Rios v. Dept. of Treasury, 77 MSPR 602, 605-07 (1998), defeating an agency's PFR because of its failure to provide interim relief when it denied a customs inspector overtime pay:

The critical issue presented by the parties' argument and evidence on compliance is whether the agency failed to satisfy [the] interim relief requirements when it detailed the appellant to the Seaport but precluded him from earning the overtime pay there that he earned at LAX. The appellant asserts in his motion to dismiss and accompanying declaration that the agency failed to provide him overtime pay because it barred him from overtime work at LAX and relegated him to fourth rather than first pick of overtime assignments at the Seaport. Specifically, he claims that while assigned to LAX he earned an average of approximately $956 in overtime pay every two weeks. In support of his entitlement to that same overtime pay at the Seaport, he submits an affidavit from Lori Gray, Assistant Counsel for the National Treasury Employees Union (NTEU), who stated that the collective bargaining agreement with the agency provided that overtime was a condition of employment and that both the collective bargaining agreement and the National Inspectional Assignment Policy (NIAP) delineated the process for distributing overtime to customs inspectors.

The agency responds that the appellant was not entitled to overtime because while the collective bargaining agreement used the term "condition of employment," it did not require the agency to assign overtime work at all; rather the agreement left the assignment of overtime within the agency's discretion with only the directive that the agency assign overtime in an equitable manner. In this regard, the agency asserts that it provided him Seaport overtime assignments on an equitable basis pursuant to the collective bargaining agreement, that it did not restrict his overtime at the Seaport, and that he had not alleged that he was barred from overtime work at the Seaport.

Further, the agency asserts that calculating and paying the appellant for overtime work would be unduly burdensome and impracticable because overtime work is largely voluntary, making it difficult to quantify per inspector on a pay period-to-pay period basis. In support of this argument it submits a declaration from Supervisory Customs Inspector Joanie Schoney, who stated that it was "virtually impossible" for her to predict the amount of overtime earned by a customs inspector largely due to the inconsistency in his or her willingness to volunteer for overtime work. The agency neither rebuts the appellant's estimation of his overtime earnings at LAX, nor offers evidence of the actual or estimated overtime earnings of customs inspectors currently at the Seaport or LAX.

In McLaughlin v. U.S. Postal Service, 55 MSPR 192, 199-200 (1992), we held that overtime pay is compensation required to be provided under an interim relief order only in those instances in which an employee proves that he is entitled to it as a term or condition of employment by virtue of law, rule, regulation, collective bargaining agreement, or binding agency policy. In reaching this holding, we explained that even if an agency has complete control over whether overtime is worked, any overtime provided by a collective bargaining agreement or binding agency policy is compensation paid as a "term" or "condition" of employment when either agreement controls "how available overtime is allocated among the employees." Id. at 199.

Upon reviewing the collective bargaining agreement and the pertinent portions of the NIAP submitted by the agency, we reject its assertion that overtime was not a condition of the appellant's employment. On its face, Article 22, Section 2.A of the collective bargaining agreement explicitly states that overtime is a condition of employment. Section 3.A. of Article 22, states that "[s]ubject to the needs and requirements of the Service for differing types, grades, skill and skill levels, and within the limits placed upon the permissible amount of overtime earnings of Customs employees, overtime earnings will be equalized to the maximum extent possible." Id. Read together, Sections 2.A and 3.A clearly satisfy our rule set forth in McLaughlin for determining whether agency-controlled overtime is compensation which amounts to a term or condition of employment. Similarly, we find that the NIAP, Sections 3-6, which set forth the rules for allocating overtime, renders overtime a condition of employment by binding agency policy. Furthermore, as we implicitly did in McLaughlin, we reject the agency's claim that the calculation of overtime pay would be so arduous and problematic as to relieve the agency of its statutory obligation to provide the appellant with overtime pay where such compensation is a term and condition of employment pursuant to a collective bargaining agreement or binding agency policy, as is the case here. 55 MSPR at 199-200.

We conclude, then, that while the agency acted within its discretion in assigning the appellant to the Seaport after making an undue disruption determination, it remained obligated to provide him the pay, compensation, and benefits of his previous position during the pendency of the petition for review. King v. Jerome, 42 F.3d 1371, 1373 (Fed. Cir. 1994). From our review of the collective bargaining agreement, the compensation to which the appellant was entitled by statute necessarily included overtime pay, which in this case was undisputedly an average of $956 per pay period that the appellant earned at LAX. The agency's refusal to grant the appellant such overtime earnings amounts to a denial of the compensation of his former position. See Johnson v. Department of Justice, 71 MSPR 59, 67 (1996) (agency could not avoid paying the appellant a differential, compensation that he would have received at his former duty station in Guam, by detailing him to another position in Washington, D.C.). Indeed, we note that consistent with our holding in Johnson, 71 MSPR at 67, the agency complied with interim relief requirements regarding the payment of night differential when, in response to the appellant's claim that it had failed to provide him with a 15% night differential, it promptly corrected that error. Accordingly, based on the foregoing evidence of the agency's noncompliance with interim relief because of its failure to provide the appellant with overtime pay, we grant the appellant's motion to dismiss the agency's petition for review.

As part of her submission on interim relief, the appellant has presented a copy of the governing collective bargaining agreement. On its face, Article 22, Section 2.A of the collective bargaining agreement states that overtime is a condition of employment; section 3.A. of Article 22 states that "[s]ubject to the needs and requirements of the Service for differing types, grades, skill and skill levels, and within the limits placed upon the permissible amount of overtime earnings of Customs employees, overtime earnings will be equalized to the maximum extent possible." Section 3.B of Article 22 provides that overtime assignments shall be made and rotated in a fair and impartial manner. Taken together, these provisions of the collective bargaining agreement clearly satisfy our rule set forth in McLaughlin for determining whether agency-controlled overtime is compensation which amounts to a term or condition of employment; we therefore find that overtime is a condition of employment by agreement and binding agency policy.

The appellant and counsel from her union (Ms. Gray) have submitted declarations made under penalty of perjury that Customs Inspectors assigned to duty at LAX (as the appellant once was) received, on the average, approximately $17,000 to $18,000 annually from overtime earnings; the appellant herself averaged $17,000 annually in overtime earnings. Customs Inspectors assigned to the Seaport, by contrast, have averaged approximately $5,500 in overtime earnings. They argue that the agency has not complied with the interim-relief order by denying her the overtime earnings at LAX that she would have received but for her assignment to the Seaport. The agency concedes that it has denied the appellant the opportunity for overtime earnings that she would have received at LAX; it contends, however, that it properly assigned the appellant to the Seaport and barred her from working at LAX after making an undue disruption determination, and that she is only entitled to the overtime opportunities available at the Seaport.

We disagree with the agency's position. While the agency properly assigned the appellant to the Seaport after making an undue disruption determination, it was still obligated to provide her the pay, compensation, and benefits of her previous position during the pendency of the petition for review. See Jerome, 42 F.3d at 1375; Briley v. National Archives & Records Administration, 71 MSPR 211, 216 (1996) (the purpose of interim relief is to grant the appellant the pay, compensation, and benefits of the position awarded in the initial decision while the petition for review is pending). From our review of the collective bargaining agreement and the undisputed evidence submitted by the appellant, it appears that she has received approximately $17,000 in annual overtime earnings from work at LAX as a term and condition of employment by virtue of the operation of the agency's binding overtime policy. The agency certainly had the discretion not to return the appellant to duty at LAX, but it could not use that discretion to avoid paying her all that she would have been entitled to had she remained working at LAX; overtime earnings at LAX were part of the compensation of the appellant's former position that the administrative judge awarded in the initial decision, and the agency's refusal to grant her such overtime earnings amounts to a denial of compensation of her former position. See Johnson v. Department of Justice, 71 MSPR 59, 67 (1996) (agency could not avoid paying the appellant a differential, compensation that he would have received at his former duty station in Guam, by detailing him to another position in Washington, D.C.). Accordingly, we dismiss the agency's petition for review for failure to award the appellant interim relief to which she was entitled. See id. at 67-68.

As noted, the appellant is entitled to all "pay, compensation, and all other benefits as terms and conditions of employment during the period pending the outcome of any petition for review...." 5 USC § 7701(b)(2)(B). The Board has held that the term "compensation" refers to those amounts by which an employee's "pay" is regularly augmented, including premium pay, night differential, and overtime pay. McLaughlin v. USPS, 55 MSPR 192, 198 (1992). In McLaughlin, the Board also considered the phrase "terms and conditions of employment" and construed it to include compensation to which the appellant is entitled not only under law, rule, or regulation, but also under the terms of an agency's collective bargaining agreement or binding agency policy, but not any that is wholly within the discretion of management. Id. at 199. Allowances and differentials, as described in 5 CFR § 501.210, are clearly included both as compensation and as terms and conditions of employment. See also Fox v.
