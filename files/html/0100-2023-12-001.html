CHAPTER 5

INITIAL DECISION AND PETITION FOR REVIEW

I. INITIAL DECISION

Once an appeal is filed, the appeal must be resolved, even if it is withdrawn, settled, or dismissed by the appellant, through an initial decision issued by an MSPB AJ. The initial decision is critical to the Board's adjudication process. An initial decision must be based on the record evidence and pleadings. The decision summarizes the facts, resolves credibility disputes, and decides legal issues. Either party or both may seek review of the initial decision by the MSPB (the Board Chair, Vice Chair and Member, assisted by headquarters legal staff) through a petition for review (PFR) of the initial decision. In unusual circumstances, the MSPB may reopen the appeal on its own volition. If the Board does not review or reopen the initial decision, it becomes the final agency decision from the MSPB, subject to judicial review or, with EEO affirmative defenses, to review by EEOC. If the initial decision provides inadequate analysis, the Board may, on review, remand the case to the AJ for further analysis or the Board may attempt to close the analytical gaps itself and issue a final decision without a remand. If neither party files a timely PFR of the initial decision, the initial decision becomes a Board final decision as a matter of law. Review of that decision may then be sought in the courts or with EEOC. [Refer to Chapter 12 for the EEO process applicable to Board cases and to Chapter 17 for judicial review.] There is an exception to the usual process of issuance and review of Board decisions from AJs: the Office of Personnel Management may seek reconsideration of an AJ's final (or a Board final) decision. OPM reconsideration petitions are few. [Refer later in this chapter to "Petition for Review; Reconsideration."]

The nature of an initial decision was noted in Jackson v. VA, 768 F.2d 1325, 1330 (Fed. Cir. 1985):

...The board itself (which is composed of three members) normally does not conduct the evidentiary hearing. Under the statute (5 USC 7701(b)), the board is authorized to delegate this part of its responsibility to administrative law judges or other qualified officials who conduct the trial-like proceedings as "presiding officials" and who make an "initial" or recommended decision to the board. This initial decision, however, is not simply a "draft." It is served on the parties and the board and automatically will become the final decision of the board unless modified by the board sua sponte or pursuant to a petition for review. 5 CFR 1201.113(a) and 1201.114(a). See Connolly v. Dept. of Justice, 766 F.2d 507, 511-512 (Fed. Cir. 1985)

An initial decision, even if undisturbed by Board review, is always nonprecedential. See Clark v. Dept. of Navy, 12 MSPR 428, 429 (1982); cf. SSA v. Abell, 47 MSPR 98, 100-101 (1991) (recommended decisions involving ALJs, and procedural orders issued in the normal course of litigation, have no precedential value). But cf. Holt v. USPS, 63 MSPR 198, 202-03 (1994) (unusual result: initial decisions may be employed to demonstrate comparable (or, presumably, disparate) penalties within the same agency; initial decisions are, on finality, reliable statements of the facts of a particular case). Initial decisions may have some indirect significance. The Board has canvassed initial decisions to check a range of reasonableness in attorney fee applications. In the Board's early years, some initial decisions were reprinted with the Board final decisions in the Board case reporters.

Only final decisions are officially reported, although commercial services may carry initial decisions (it can be useful to see if the AJ assigned to your case has dealt with your appeal's issues (or with your client) in the recent past. Through 2010 a Board final decision was ordinarily precedential (with an exception for decisions issued when, by reason of recusal or a vacancy or other reason Board members could not decide a case by majority vote, leaving the initial decision undisturbed). Through 2010, the Board often used short form final decisions that had no precedential value: they affirmed the initial decision with little more than a recitation that a petition for review did not meet regulatory review criteria. Starting in 2010 the Board began making extensive use of nonprecedential final orders written in about as much detail and with as much analysis as precedential final decisions, an approach that leaves the Board with a smaller base of binding precedent than would be established were there no distinction between the nonprecedential and precedential decisions. Nonprecedential orders are cited throughout this Guide; despite the author's disagreement with the Board's bifurcation of its law, nonprecedential final orders remain, well, nonprecedential.
