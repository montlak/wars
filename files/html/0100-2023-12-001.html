CHAPTER 4

SUBJECTS OF BARGAINING:
PROCEDURES FOR NEGOTIABILITY DETERMINATIONS

I. INTRODUCTION

It is difficult to treat separately the duty to bargain and the subjects of bargaining. The difficulty is compounded by negotiability issues addressed through negotiability appeals, unfair labor practice cases, and disputes over arbitration awards reviewed through the exception process. The web has many strands--the strands are not well-woven. Discussion of bargaining responsibilities continues in Chapter 8, "Unfair Labor Practices," and negotiability issues are also referenced in Chapter 9, "Review of Arbitration Awards." FLRA provides an overview of the procedural aspects of negotiability determinations in its publication, Guide to the Federal Labor Relations Authority Negotiability Appeals Process, www.flra.gov/102599, including a fascinating block diagram of the process. FLRA also provides guidance on procedures for resolving negotiability disputes, www.flra.gov/cases/negotiability. FLRA provides forms for use in negotiability appeals, www.flra.gov/authority_forms_negotiability_appeal. Refer also to the June 17, 2013, Guide to Negotiability Under the Federal Service Labor-Management Relations Statute. https://www.flra.gov/system/files/webfm/Authority/NG%20Forms,%20Guide,%20Other/Negotiability%20Guide%206-17-13.pdf.

We begin with the statute defining the parameters of bargaining, 5 USC 7114(a)(4):

Any agency and any exclusive representative in any appropriate unit in the agency, through appropriate representatives, shall meet and negotiate in good faith for the purpose of arriving at a collective bargaining agreement. In addition, the agency and the exclusive representative may determine appropriate techniques, consistent with the provisions of section 7119 of this title, to assist in any negotiation.

Add to the mix 5 USC 7102:

Each employee shall have the right to form, join, or assist any labor organization, or to refrain from any such activity, freely and without fear of penalty or reprisal, and each employee shall be protected in the exercise of such right. Except as otherwise provided under this chapter, such right includes the right--

(1) to act for a labor organization in the capacity of a representative and the right, in that capacity, to present the views of the labor organization to heads of agencies and other officials of the executive branch of the Government, the Congress, or other appropriate authorities, and

(2) to engage in collective bargaining with respect to conditions of employment through representatives chosen by employees under this chapter.

For good measure, 5 USC 7103 informs us that:

(a) For the purpose of this chapter--

...

(12) "collective bargaining" means the performance of the mutual obligation of the representative of an agency and the exclusive representative of employees in an appropriate unit in the agency to meet at reasonable times and to consult and bargain in a good-faith effort to reach agreement with respect to the conditions of employment affecting such employees and to execute, if requested by either party, a written document incorporating any collective bargaining agreement reached, but the obligation referred to in this paragraph does not compel either party to agree to a proposal or to make a concession;

...

(14) "conditions of employment" means personnel policies, practices, and matters, whether established by rule, regulation, or otherwise, affecting working conditions, except that such term does not include policies, practices, and matters--

(A) relating to political activities prohibited under subchapter III of chapter 73 of this title;

(B) relating to the classification of any position; or

(C) to the extent such matters are specifically provided for by Federal statute[.]

Within the basic duty of the parties to meet and negotiate, there are subsidiary duties and rights, established at 5 USC 7114:

(b) The duty of an agency and an exclusive representative to negotiate in good faith under subsection (a) of this section shall include the obligation--

(1) to approach the negotiations with a sincere resolve to reach a collective bargaining agreement;

(2) to be represented at the negotiations by duly authorized representatives prepared to discuss and negotiate on any conditions of employment;

(3) to meet at reasonable times and convenient places as frequently as may be necessary, and to avoid unnecessary delays;

...

(5) if agreement is reached, to execute on the request of any party to the negotiation a written document embodying the agreed terms, and to take such steps as are necessary to implement such agreement.

(c)(1) An agreement between any agency and an exclusive representative shall be subject to approval by the head of the agency.

(2) The head of the agency shall approve the agreement within 30 days from the date the agreement is executed if the agreement is in accordance with the provisions of this chapter and any other applicable law, rule, or regulation (unless the agency has granted an exception to the provision).

(3) If the head of the agency does not approve or disapprove the agreement within the 30-day period, the agreement shall take effect and shall be binding on the agency and the exclusive representative subject to the provisions of this chapter and any other applicable law, rule, or regulation.

(4) A local agreement subject to a national or other controlling agreement at a higher level shall be approved under the procedures of the controlling agreement or, if none, under regulations prescribed by the agency.

The Statute outlines the negotiability review procedure, 5 USC 7117(c):

(1) Except in any case to which subsection (b) of this section applies, if an agency involved in collective bargaining with an exclusive representative alleges that the duty to bargain in good faith does not extend to any matter, the exclusive representative may appeal the allegations to the Authority in accordance with the provisions of this subsection.

(2) The exclusive representative may, on or before the 15th day after the date on which the agency first makes the allegation referred to in paragraph (1) of this subsection, institute an appeal under this subsection by--

(A) filing a petition with the Authority; and

(B) furnishing a copy of the petition to the head of the agency.

(3) On or before the 30th day after the date of the receipt by the head of the agency of the copy of the petition under paragraph (2)(B) of this subsection, the agency shall--

(A) file with the Authority a statement--

(i) withdrawing the allegation; or

(ii) setting forth in full its reasons supporting the allegation; and

(B) furnish a copy of such statement to the exclusive representative.

(4) On or before the 15th day after the date of the receipt by the exclusive representative of a copy of a statement under paragraph (3)(B) of this subsection, the exclusive representative shall file with the Authority its response to the statement.

(5) A hearing may be held, in the discretion of the Authority, before a determination is made under this subsection. If a hearing is held, it shall not include the General Counsel as a party.

(6) The Authority shall expedite proceedings under this subsection to the extent practicable and shall issue to the exclusive representative and to the agency a written decision on the allegation and specific reasons therefor at the earliest practicable date.

Unlike the private sector, permitting an almost unlimited scope of bargaining (subject to the constraints of economic power), the scope of federal sector bargaining, although broadly defined by statute, is limited by numerous exceptions. The statutory limitations on the negotiability of federal sector proposals create innumerable disputes over the boundaries of what is and is not within the scope of bargaining. This chapter addresses the procedural means established to resolve those disputes. Negotiability appeals, technically called petitions for review, may be filed by unions in disputes with agencies concerning what matters may be collectively bargained. Chapter 6 provides detailed coverage of the negotiability of specific proposals classified by subject matter. The appeal may be closed procedurally or decided on the merits of the case. A negotiability decision is subject to judicial review, discussed in Chapter 10. If the parties cannot reach agreement, mediation and impasse resolution procedures are available under 5 USC 7119, discussed in Chapter 7.

An exclusive representative may propose that a particular matter be negotiated. An agency may declare that the matter is nonnegotiable because it conflicts with federal law (including statutory management rights), government-wide rules or regulations, an agency regulation supported by a compelling need, or because the matter is permissively negotiable and the agency has elected not to bargain. Alternatively, the agency head may disapprove a contract that is presented to the agency for approval under 5 USC 7114(c); if the contract is disapproved, the disapproval is treated as a declaration of nonnegotiability. However the negotiability issue arises, the exclusive representative may file a negotiability appeal with FLRA.

Employers may not submit negotiability appeals, even if a union declares an employer's proposal nonnegotiable. 5 USC 7117(c)(1), (2); 5 CFR 2424.20 (2022); VAMC, Salisbury, N.C. and AFGE Local 1738, 2 FLRA 405, 405-06 (1980). The corollary of that proposition is that only an agency may make an allegation of nonnegotiability that is subject to FLRA review. SSA and Nat'l Council of SSA Field Operations Locals, AFGE, 25 FLRA 238, 239-40 (1987). There is no legislative history to suggest why Congress did not make the expedited procedure available if the union asserts that an issue is nonnegotiable. A possible explanation is that unions' assertions of nonnegotiability are so rare that Congress thought no special review procedure was necessary. AFGE v. FLRA, 778 F.2d 850, n.4 (D.C. Cir. 1985). The employer may file an unfair labor practice charge based on a refusal to bargain under 5 USC 7116(b)(1), (5). AFGE and Air Force Logistics Command, Wright-Patterson AFB, 4 FLRA 272, 272 (1980). An interest arbitrator may not ordinarily consider a union allegation of nonnegotiability. DOJ, INS and AFGE, Nat'l Border Patrol Council, 31 FLRA 1123, 1124 (1988).

Negotiability resolutions are made on a case-by-case basis. An agency may have a valid concern over what it hypothesizes to be the extended effect of a bargaining proposal. A negotiability dispute, concerned only with the consistency of a particular proposal with applicable law and regulation, is not the forum to address that concern. Under the Statute, the agency protects its interests on the merits of proposals at the bargaining table or, if a mutually satisfactory agreement as to the proposal cannot then be reached, before the Federal Service Impasses Panel (FSIP). NFFE Local 1153 and Army, Seventh Signal Command and Ft. Ritchie, 26 FLRA 505, 510 (1987). Negotiability issues are decided by the Authority according to the law in effect at the time of the Authority's decision, not on the basis of the law at the time that the agency refused to bargain. Dept. of Transp. and PASS, 40 FLRA 690, 710 (1991).

There is a distinction between those matters that are negotiable, unless negotiability is precluded by law, and those matters that are clearly not negotiable except at the unreviewable option or election of the agency--permissive subjects of bargaining. Under 5 USC 7106(b)(1), defining the permissive scope of bargaining, the agency need not bargain, unless it so elects, on the numbers, types, and grades of employees or positions assigned to any organizational subdivision, work project, or tour of duty, or on the technology, methods, and means of performing work. If a matter is negotiable at the election of the agency and, following and during a negotiability appeal, the agency does not advise FLRA that it refuses to negotiate for that reason, FLRA decides the negotiability issue. The agency may terminate bargaining on elective (permissive) topics at any point, even if it initially agrees to negotiations. Nat'l Maritime Union and Dept. of Navy, Military Sealift Command, 25 FLRA 105, 109 (1987). Some matters are not negotiable because they are excluded from the statutory definition of conditions of employment under 5 USC 7103(a)(14): policies, practices, and matters relating to: political activities prohibited under subchapter III of chapter 73 of Title 5; the classification of any position; matters specifically provided for by federal statute.

Add to the equation disputes over the underlying obligation to bargain. An agency may assert that negotiations are precluded because bargaining has been waived through contract or through union inaction as to bargainable matters. Negotiability is not reached until there is an obligation to negotiate. Bargaining issues--more particularly, bargaining failures--are considered in Chapter 8 of this Guide, involving unfair labor practices.

The basic framework of bargaining in the federal sector was described in AFGE v. FLRA, 778 F.2d 850, 851-52 (D.C. Cir. 1985):

In enacting the Labor-Management Act, Congress sought to provide federal civilian employees with some of the rights enjoyed by employees in the private sector. Specifically, Congress found that statutory protection of employees' rights to form labor unions and bargain collectively was in the public interest. 5 U.S.C. § 7101(a)(1). At the same time, Congress recognized that it could not merely transplant private employment statutes to the public employment context. Rather it would have to "establish procedures which are designed to meet the special requirements and needs of the Government." 5 U.S.C. § 7101(b). Thus, the Act strikes a balance between the need to strengthen employees' bargaining rights and the need not to unduly interfere with government operations.

Notwithstanding the broad rights the new law conferred on federal employees to engage in collective bargaining, certain subjects were excluded from the scope of bargaining. See, e.g., 5 U.S.C. §§ 7106(a) (management rights); 7117(a)(2) (agency regulation for which "compelling need" exists). To adjudicate "negotiability" disputes arising out of these provisions, Congress set up mechanisms by which a union can appeal an agency's assertion of nonnegotiability. See, e.g., § 7117(c) (expedited review of negotiability issues); § 7118 (unfair labor practice proceedings). Additionally, although strikes continued to be forbidden, § 7116(b)(7), Congress established an Impasses Panel, whose job it is to suggest, and if necessary, order, terms of settlement between agencies and unions when they cannot agree. § 7119.
