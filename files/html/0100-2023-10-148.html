1. Control of Questioning

AJs have considerable latitude in the conduct of the hearing. They may preclude a party from offering testimony that provides information already established through documents or that is irrelevant. Ford v. Dept. of Navy, 43 MSPR 495, 500 (1990). Through their rulings, AJs arrange the order of examination and cross-examination of witnesses and define the subject matter suitable for inquiry. Pay attention to, and ensure that you understand, the directions provided by the AJ. In Franks v. Dept. of Army, 14 MSPR 387, 390 (1983), the AJ split questioning into two parts: one concerning jurisdiction, the other concerning the merits of a demotion. The agency representative, not an attorney, apparently did not fully understand the order of presentation of testimony and did not develop evidence concerning the merits. In Franks, the Board determined that the hearing was fairly conducted; the agency's failure to develop evidence was not attributable to the AJ. An AJ may allow a pro se appellant to testify in narrative, rather than question and answer format. See Render v. VA, 90 MSPR 441, 444 ¶ 7 (2001).

The Board occasionally faults an AJ for limiting the development of evidence. But AJs' rulings are ordinarily not disturbed on appeal. Before a complaint is made about the control of testimony by the AJ, there should be a ruling from the AJ. In Madison v. Dept. of Air Force, 32 MSPR 465, 485-86 (1987), some dispute occurred as to whether the AJ cut off questioning on an EEO issue or discouraged the production of evidence; clearly, the Board was displeased with the conduct of the hearing, noting the AJ's "discouragement of the presentation of evidence of the appellant's allegation of agency reprisal with regard to her physical ailment." Chairman Levinson's dissent made some significant comments concerning the development of evidence by advocates:

The question of whether the administrative judge erred in "discouraging" the presentation of evidence of alleged acts of reprisal...is also not properly before the Board. It is fundamental that "[n]o assignment of error can be made unless there is a ruling on the objection." R. Hunter, Federal Trial Handbook, 2d, § 40.7 (1984). Yet, as the majority recognizes and the hearing tapes clearly disclose, the administrative judge never ruled on the agency's objection to such evidence. Instead, the administrative judge made a good faith effort to understand the explanation of relevancy given by appellant's counsel, which itself was not a model of clarity. During the course of that colloquy, the administrative judge told appellant's counsel he would not "have any problem" if appellant took "a question or two about the medical to state [her] position," after which he would have to analyze it along with the rest of the record to determine its legal significance. Appellant's counsel then propounded three additional questions to his client, to which she gave fairly lengthy answers, and then turned to another topic. The agency never renewed its objection, and the administrative judge issued no ruling.

Under these circumstances, if appellant did not introduce all of the evidence she wanted on this subject, the responsibility falls squarely on her counsel's shoulders. I will not presume that because he was initially skeptical of the topic, the administrative judge would have excluded relevant evidence if subsequent questions and answers clarified the relationship between the evidence and issues in the case. Appellant's counsel should have continued to adduce evidence he thought necessary to his case until the administrative judge sustained an objection or, sua sponte, ordered the inquiry terminated, rather than resort to self-censorship. I would not hold that persistence in the face of a definite ruling is required, or even appropriate, but, in the absence of plain error, the Board should not criticize its administrative judges for improperly limiting evidence unless either the proponent or opponent have elicited a firm ruling.

See Evono v. DOJ, 69 MSPR 541, 548 (1996) (appellant's representative did not acquiesce in the AJ's ruling limiting evidence when the representative reiterated to the AJ the appellant's claim of discrimination, even though the representative did not object to or ask for reconsideration of the AJ's ruling). Assuming a ruling that is adverse concerning an intended question, there should be a showing that the question, if answered, would have produced relevant and material evidence concerning the charge against the appellant. Box v. USPS, 51 MSPR 401, 405 n.2 (1991). That showing will require an offer of proof.
