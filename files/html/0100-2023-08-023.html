D. BOARD DECISIONS; PRECEDENTIAL SIGNIFICANCE

Once an appeal is filed, it must be adjudicated by the AJ through the issuance of an initial decision. It matters not that the appeal is late, not within the Board's jurisdiction, withdrawn, settled, or abandoned: an initial decision closes the case out. Initial decisions become final Board decisions after 35 days if no earlier petition for review (PFR) is filed with Board headquarters and if the Board does not, on its own motion, reopen an initial decision before it becomes final. The Board may reopen an initial decision after it becomes final, a process described in Chapter 5.

If the Board itself becomes involved in the adjudication of the appeal through a PFR or reopening, it issues its own decision, and if that decision terminates the Board's processing of the case, that decision is the final decision or order of the Board. Or the Board may decide to remand the appeal to the AJ for further analysis (then the AJ would issue another initial decision, subject to another PFR). When the Board issues a final decision on review or reopening of an initial decision, the Board's final decision is precedential unless the final decision states otherwise. If only two members participate in a Board decision and their decision is split, the AJ's decision stands; the decision is not precedential. See Board Order 1060.17 ¶ 5 (May 17, 1996). Precedential and nonprecedential decisions, discussed below, are found in digital form on the Board's website, www.mspb.gov Board's website. They are available through some commercial legal databases, and they are available through a FOIA request to the Board.

To describe the role of initial decisions and final Board decisions on the ultimate issues in appeals emphasizes the importance of those decisions in the adjudication scheme. Other orders or rulings are issued by AJs, and reviewed by the Board in final decisions through the PFR process, involving matters of procedure and discovery, compliance or enforcement, counsel fees, awards of damages, interlocutory appeals, remands, rulings on motions, sanctions, and all manner of miscellaneous issues discussed in the thousands of pages to follow. Stay alert.

The opinions of the former Civil Service Commission (generally through 1978) are not binding on the Board. The value of those opinions "depends solely on the logic and persuasiveness of their holdings" in light of the Reform Act. Special Counsel v. DeMeo, 77 MSPR 158, 173 (1997) (CSC decisions as instructional); Brown v. OPM, 18 MSPR 670, 672 (1984) ("The opinions and past adjudicatory practices of the former [Civil Service] Commission...are not binding on the Board. The value of the Commission's opinions depends solely on the logic and persuasiveness of their holdings in light of the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978."); Chiodo v. Dept. of Treasury, 6 MSPR 155, 157, 6 MSPB 139 (1981) (value of CSC decisions depends on their logic and persuasiveness under CSRA). The Board is bound to follow decisions of the Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, the Board's reviewing court. Rogers v. OPM, 77 MSPR 620, 624 (1998); see Cobert v. Miller, 800 F.3d 1340, 1349 (Fed. Cir. 2015) (Judge Wallach concurring) ("Ketterer's approach thereby became law that must be followed by the Board and panels of this court until overruled by either the Supreme Court or by this court en banc."). [Refer to Chapter 17, "Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit," concerning review procedures of the Federal Circuit.] Decisions of federal district courts do not bind the Board, although they may be followed if their reasoning is persuasive. Nigg v. USPS, 91 MSPR 164, 169 n.3 (2002), aff'd on other grounds, Nigg v. MSPB, 321 F.3d 1381 (Fed. Cir. 2003).

Initial decisions, including those that become final only by the passage of time, lack precedential value, although once in a while they warrant mention in a Board decision. Roche v. Dept. of Transp., 110 MSPR 286, 291 ¶ 13, 2008 MSPB 247 (2008), said of an appellant's reliance on an initial decision as precedent:

In arguing that section 7511 is not applicable, the appellant relies on Jewell v. Department of Homeland Security, 2005 MSPB Lexis 2860 *4 (Initial Decision, May 19, 2005), petition for review denied, 100 M.S.P.R. 63 (2005) (Table), an initial decision in which a Board administrative judge found that the definitions of section 7511 did not apply to an appeal under 49 U.S.C. § 40122(g), and that those in the FAA's PMS applied instead. He notes that the Board denied a petition for review of that initial decision, and he seems to argue that the denial reflects the Board's conclusion that the decision was correct. Initial decisions of the Board have no precedential value, however, and the petition for review in this case was denied by nonprecedential final order, rather than by a precedential opinion and order. E.g., Fitzgerald v. Department of the Air Force, 108 M.S.P.R. 620, ¶ 15 (2008); Jewell, 100 M.S.P.R. 63. Furthermore, the Board's denial of a petition for review does not necessarily indicate that the Board concurs in every aspect of the initial decision. The Board generally does not consider issues that are not raised on review; and, even when the petitioning party establishes that the administrative judge made an error in adjudicating the case, the Board may deny the petition if the error does not affect the outcome of the case. See Panter v. Department of the Air Force, 22 M.S.P.R. 281, 282 (1984) (an adjudicatory error that is not prejudicial to a party's substantive rights provides no basis for reversal of an initial decision).

See Nelson v. FDIC, 83 F.3d 1375, 1377 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (citing "Harris v. Department of the Navy, 15 M.S.P.R. 464, 467 n.4 (1983) ('Initial decisions are of no precedential value and therefore are not binding on the Board.'); Acfalle v. Dept. of Army, SF-0752-15-0305-I-1 (NP 11/22/2016) ("Parties may cite nonprecedential orders, but such orders have no precedential value; the Board and administrative judges are not required to follow or distinguish them in any future decisions"; "Board initial decisions are of no precedential value and cannot be cited or relied on as controlling authority, Rockwell v. Department of Commerce, 39 M.S.P.R. 217, 222 (1988)."); Durling v. OPM, 77 MSPR 429, 432 (1998) ("Nor does issuance of the Smith decision provide good cause for the lateness of the appellant's petition for review. First, that decision was an initial decision, and not a decision of the full Board. As such, it lacked precedential authority, even after it became final. See Stewart v. U.S. Postal Service, 55 M.S.P.R. 362, 365 (1992)."); Stewart v. USPS, 55 MSPR 362, 365 (1992) ("This apparently erroneous finding in the October 4, 1991 initial decision has no bearing on the present appeal, however, because initial decisions, even after they become final, do not have any precedential effect. See, e.g., 5 C.F.R. § 1201.113; Blevins v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 46 M.S.P.R. 239, 244 n.5 (1990)."); Roth v. USPS, 54 MSPR 290, 296 n.3 (1992) (declining to compare analysis of penalties arrived at through initial decisions involving different appellants before the same regional office); NLRB v. Beddow, 47 MSPR 103, 105 (1991) (applying the same rule to a recommended decision in an action against an ALJ); SSA v. Abell, 47 MSPR 98, 101 (1991) ("It has long been held that initial decisions have no precedential value. Rockwell v. Department of Commerce, 39 MSPR 217, 222 (1988); Clark v. Department of the Navy, 12 MSPR 428, 429 (1982)."); Jones v. Dept. of Army, 43 MSPR 508, 510 (1990); Grant v. District of Columbia Gov't, 16 MSPR 462, 463 (1983) (initial decisions have no precedential value); Clark v. Department of the Navy, 11 MSPB 71, 12 M.S.P.R. 428, 429 (1982) ('The agency argues that its decision to remove appellant was reasonable and supported by several Board initial decisions. These decisions, however, have no precedential value....')."). But see Tanner v. DOD, DC-0752-12-0209-A-1 (NP 8/1/2014) (examining initial decisions to determine the reasonableness of an hourly rate claimed in a counsel fee petition); Holt v. USPS, 63 MSPR 198, 202-03 (1994) (initial decisions were used to show comparable discipline imposed by the deciding official, since they accurately reflected the facts of the comparable cases).

Initial decisions may be useful to locate analyses employed by AJs who confronted issues or factual patterns of interest. It may help to examine initial decisions of an AJ assigned to your appeal to learn how that AJ approaches issues or treats Board or judicial precedent. Reference to an initial decision may be helpful when the Board's analysis in a final decision can be better understood through the AJ's analysis of the appeal. Although initial decisions have no precedential value, they may be cited in Board litigation, principally for the strength of analysis applied in the initial decision.

A Board final decision--whether the result of an unchallenged or unreviewed initial decision or the product of Board review--is res judicata as to the issues in that appeal. Should the appellant again attempt to appeal the same personnel action and issues, the Board will likely dismiss the appeal through an initial decision based on res judicata. Ellis v. USPS, 33 MSPR 685, 686 (1987) (Board may, however, [under procedures discussed in Chapter 5, "PFR Process"], reopen a case on the motion of a party or on its own motion). [Refer to Chapter 4, "Previous Determinations of Disputed Facts, Issues, Claims: Res Judicata, Collateral Estoppel, and Law of the Case--The Doctrines."]

Through mid-2010, most final decisions were summary in nature, described as "short form" by the Board, "one-liners" by the bar. Many of the older Board final decisions, often providing analysis in but one paragraph or a footnote, cannot be fully understood without reference to the initial decisions. Commercial reporting systems used by practitioners may provide both initial and final decisions. Board final decisions can be found on the Board's Internet webpage, and initial decisions can be obtained from the Board through a FOIA request, although some antiquated initial decisions cannot be obtained from the Board because it destroys old files in accordance with its document retention schedule. The FOIA request should include the name of the case, the caption, docket number, and, if available, the date of the initial decision. The Board' website provides for electronic FOIA requests.
