b. Nonstatutory Vehicle Misuse Charge; Impaired Driver

An individual may be disciplined for vehicle misuse even if there is no statutory proscription. Although most vehicle misuse cases arise under the statutes earlier discussed, agencies may have their own regulations concerning vehicle use. If those regulations are violated, the employee can be subjected to discipline without regard to whether the charged conduct would also constitute a violation of the statute governing use of official vehicles. See Els v. Dept. of Army, 82 MSPR 27, 31-32 ¶¶ 7-10 (1999) (AJ erred by dismissing a charge of misuse of a government trailer as unsupported by the vehicle misuse statute; the agency did not rely on the statute and could take action for misuse of the trailer without regard to whether it constituted a GOV within the meaning of the statute; removal was mitigated to a 30-day suspension based on a past good record and supervisory condonation of some of the appellant's actions); Campbell v. DHHS, 40 MSPR 525, 535 (1989) (transportation of family member from an airport, after taking a supervisor to the airport, violated an agency regulation prohibiting GOV use for other than official business: "the kind of misconduct at issue here is potentially serious, since it results not only in increased expenses to the government, but also in exposing the government to liability claims that might arise from an accident in which a vehicle might be involved. In addition, the seriousness of the appellant's conduct is increased by the showing that the appellant engaged in the same kind of misconduct on more than one occasion. Of even greater significance is the fact that the appellant held a prominent supervisory position involving frequent contacts with the public"); Hedgecock v. Dept. of Army, 20 MSPR 333, 336-37 (1984) (removal of an aerial photographer for use of a government-chartered plane for unauthorized personal services; the appellant operated without supervision and set his own itinerary: "Thus not only did appellant cause the agency to incur actual expenses of $300, but in addition, he exposed the agency to far greater potential harm in terms of possible liability incurred by involving the two youngsters."). If the employee is charged only under an agency regulation, rather than under 31 USC 1349, the agency may be able to avoid imposing the minimum statutory mandatory penalty of a 30-day suspension. See SSA v. Givens, 27 MSPR 360, 362 n.2 (1985).

Operating a government vehicle under the influence (of alcohol) while on official business on a public highway warrants removal. See Davis v. Dept. of Army, 56 MSPR 583, 586 (1993) (sustaining removal, overturning mitigation by the AJ, despite a long and otherwise unblemished record: the vehicle was involved in an accident and the "appellant endangered the public safety and exposed the government to liability for any deaths or injuries that may have ensued"); Tenorio v. DHHS, 30 MSPR 136, 139 (1986) (removal; past history of vehicle and alcohol-related problems); Quander v. DOJ, 22 MSPR 419, 423 (1984) (DEA agent removed for, among other charges, driving a GOV under the influence and the resultant auto accident); Brown v. USDA, 26 MSPR 173, 175-76 (1985) ("The seriousness of appellant's offense must not be understated. He was returning from a lengthy trip away from his facility, and during early evening hours was operating a large vehicle on a public highway. He admitted that he was intoxicated while he was driving, and that he was operating the vehicle on the wrong side of the road. Furthermore, he allowed incorrect time and attendance records to be submitted showing that he was working during the time he was in jail. Appellant's offense was clearly intentional and reflects a total disregard for his own safety and the safety of other highway travelers."), rev'd and remanded, 785 F.2d 322, on remand, 29 MSPR 698 (1986) (Table) (mitigating removal to a 60-day suspension); Hilderbrand v. DOJ, 22 MSPR 233, 237 (1984) (DEA agent removed for involvement in a fatal hit and run accident while using a GOV on duty time); cf. Hoofman v. Dept. of Army, 118 MSPR 532, 537-38 ¶¶ 9-10, 2012 MSPB 107 (2012) (aff'd, Hoofman v. Dept. of Army, 526 Fed. Appx. 982 (Fed. Cir. 2013 NP)) (misuse of a vehicle coupled with an offense under state law of operating the vehicle while under the influence of alcohol). The defense that impaired driving resulted from a reaction to some medication did not excuse for the effects of the alcohol to the extent they may have been aggravated by the medication. It was immaterial that the suspension was calculated and sustained by the Board, in part, upon the basis of a statutory infraction requiring intent to misuse a vehicle, for the penalty was adequately supported by the sustained charges. Coleman v. Secret Serv., 749 F.2d 726 (Fed. Cir. 1984). But see Rodriguez v. CPSC, AT-0752-14-0655-I-1 (NP 5/29/2015) (setting aside one component of a "conduct unbecoming" charge when the appellant's impairment while operating a GOV resulted from medication rather than alcohol, but sustaining removal; "In light of the appellant's job duties, we agree and find that removal based on the sustained specification concerning the appellant's failure to report her DUI arrest alone is within the tolerable limits of reasonableness in this case. Accordingly, we affirm the removal action.").
