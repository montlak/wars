4. Federal Circuit Commentary on Transcript

Appellants' counsel hoped that the Federal Circuit would provide some help as to transcripts when the issue came to fore in a mandamus proceeding brought by Robert Beard, Miscellaneous Docket 57 (1985) (precedential), who sought a ruling that Federal Rules of Appellate Procedure 16 and 17 required the Board to file a transcript when a case was brought to the court. Unreceptive to the argument, the court ruled:

As defined by Rule 16, FRAP, the record on review or enforcement of agency orders consists of the order under review, the findings or report on which the order is based, and the pleadings, evidence and proceeding before the agency.

Pursuant to Rule 17, FRAP, the agency whose order is the subject of the petition for review must file the record with the Clerk of this court within 40 days of service of the petition for review. Rule 17(b), FRAP, sets forth alternative ways in which the agency may file the record here.

Petitioners, however, contend that because the rules place upon the agency the responsibility to file the record, the agency is therefore also required to prepare and file a transcript of the proceedings before it in order to comply with Rule 17, FRAP. Petitioners further rely on the fact that Rules 10(b) and 13(d), FRAP, which govern appeals from district courts and tax courts respectively, specifically require that an appellant shall order and pay for the transcription of proceedings from the reporter within a specified time period for inclusion in the record on appeal. Petitioners therefore theorize that because the rules governing agency review do not contain a specific provision requiring that the party seeking review arrange for the transcription of agency proceedings, it is the agency's responsibility to do so as part of its duty to file the record.

Initially, we note that petitioners' contention is contrary to the provisions of Rule 11, Rules of the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, which supplement the Federal Rules of Appellate Procedure before this court. Pursuant to our Rule 11(a)(2), the provisions of FRAP 10(b) and (c), and the first paragraph of FRAP 11(c), shall govern the preparation and filing of the transcript of proceedings in the tribunal from which an appeal is taken. Unlike the Federal Rules of Appellate Procedure, our rules are not divided into separate titles based upon the source of the appeal, and therefore they are applicable to petitions for review of agency proceedings as well as appeals from the district courts or tax courts. Thus, in accordance with our rules, petitioners bear the burden of ordering and paying for the transcripts in their appeals from orders of the Board before this court.

The transcript issue was again addressed, in an objection to the Board's treatment of a PFR, in Gonzales v. DLA, 772 F.2d 887, 890 (Fed. Cir. 1985):

Pursuant to section 7701(j) the board has prescribed 5 CFR 1201.53 concerning the transcript requirement. Hearings are recorded on tape and the regulation provides that a transcript will be made available to the parties upon payment of costs. "Exceptions may be granted in extenuating circumstances for good cause shown," upon written motion. The tape, of course, is available to the board at all times. Does this satisfy the statute in this case? The answer depends on whether the agency regulation reasonably interprets the legislative intent behind the statute. National Muffler Dealers Ass'n v. United States, 440 U.S. 472, 477 (1979).

The legislative history of the Reform Act contains no comment on the transcript requirement. Although commonly thought of as a writing, the primary definition of a transcript is "a copy of any kind." Black's Law Dictionary 1342 (5th ed. 1979). Thus, we believe that the board's keeping of a tape recording satisfies the section 7701(a)(1) requirement and that petitioner's speculation that he was prejudiced is without merit.

In addition, we note that, as opposed to section 7701(b) which requires the board to furnish a copy of its decision to each party in an appeal and to the Office of Personnel Management, section 7701(a)(1) merely requires the board to keep a transcript. This distinction is not without a difference. Although the board originally provided copies of the hearing transcript free of charge, it began its current practice on December 21, 1981 [reprinted above] ....

Petitioner did not furnish a written transcript to the board with his application [petition] for review nor has he done so to the court on this appeal. He says he could not afford it. This becomes significant because if petitioner wishes to rely on any part of the record of the hearing he is required to provide it to us under Federal Rule of Appellate Procedure 10(b) and Local Rule 11. Petitioner has not sought production of a written transcript by relying on the provisions of 28 USC 1915 (1982), by bringing the appeal in forma pauperis, which might have satisfied both the board and the court of the good cause and extenuating circumstances which would result in production at the government's expense. 5 CFR 1201.53. We think that the regulation is entirely reasonable and lawful....

Toyama v. Leavitt, 408 Fed. Appx. 351, 353 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP), added:

...As for the hearing transcript, we have previously held that hearing tapes satisfy the transcript requirement. Gearan v. Dep't of Health & Human Servs., 838 F.2d 1190, 1191-92 (Fed. Cir. 1988). Ms. Tayoma's [sic] contention that the hearing CDs are "impermissibly vague for citation and rebuttal purposes" is inconsistent with her numerous citations to those recordings throughout her briefs.

The Federal Circuit reaffirmed that the Board's treatment of tapes and transcripts complies with the administrative record requirements of the Federal Rules of Appellate Procedure. Gearan v. DHHS, 838 F.2d 1190 (Fed. Cir. 1988). But the preference is for a transcript, not a recording. See Harris v. SEC, 972 F.3d 1307, 1315 n.2 (Fed. Cir. 2020) ("Although Ms. Harris provided neither a written transcript nor an audio recording of the administrative hearing, we have obtained from the MSPB a copy of the full administrative record--including the audio recordings of the hearing. We decline the government's suggestion to disregard all of Ms. Harris's arguments supported only by citation to the audio recording. See Kinslow v. Dep't of the Treasury, 315 F. App'x 286, 288 (Fed. Cir. 2009) (unpublished per curiam) (accepting audio recording of administrative hearing as a substitute for a written transcript). Nonetheless, we strongly urge litigants to provide written hearing transcripts whenever possible.").

If an appellant wants a transcript for use in the circuit court, he must arrange for its preparation and delivery through the Board or through the stenographic firm that recorded the hearing. Should the appellant seek a transcript at public expense, the appellant will need to file a motion with the AJ, or the Board (at the PFR stage) explaining the need for the transcript and the inability to pay for it. The matter would then be up to the AJ or Board, with consideration of any available funds. A prior regulation, 5 CFR 1201.53(c), provided a process for a party to request a transcript at public expense by request to the AJ or the Board. 5 CFR 1201.53(c) (2011). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Current MSPB Practice," concerning waiver of transcript costs.; refer to Chapter 17, "Record and Issues Below."] For those appellants who cannot afford transcription costs, there is the remedy of 28 USC 1915, permitting use of an application in forma pauperis to support a request to a reviewing court to have the Board prepare the transcript at public expense. The Federal Circuit Rules should also be consulted. The "Practice Note" to Rule 24 of the Federal Circuit appears to require that requests for transcripts by in forma pauperis appellants be directed to the Board. When in doubt as to the correct procedure, call the court's Clerk for guidance. Federal Circuit Practice Notes to Rule 17 suggest that transcript requests are to be addressed to MSPB.
