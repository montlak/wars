b. Fee Agreement

There are few cases involving production of fee agreements, probably because their production is routine. Alternatively, counsel describe in their affidavits supporting fee motions the terms of oral agreements. An oral agreement, or written agreement later incorporating an oral agreement, is sufficient, Miller v. VA, 84 MSPR 692, 696 ¶¶ 6-7 (2000):

Where an administrative judge ignores an appellant's failure to submit a copy of a fee agreement or a summary of that agreement with his motion for fees, he commits adjudicatory error that provides a basis for reversal of a FAID if the absence of the fee agreement prejudiced the agency's substantive rights. Williams v. Department of the Air Force, 64 MSPR 37, 41 (1994); Panter v. Department of the Air Force, 22 MSPR 281, 282 (1984). The agency's substantive rights are not prejudiced where an administrative judge ignores the appellant's failure to submit a copy of the fee agreement if the appellant establishes that fees were incurred and the agency does not argue that fees were not incurred. Williams, 64 MSPR at 41.

Here, the appellant's counsel indicated that initially he represented the appellant to respond to a jurisdictional show-cause order under an oral agreement, that the appellant incurred fees under the oral agreement, and that, before the hearing, on August 18, 1998, he and the appellant entered into a written agreement that incorporated the oral agreement. The appellant submitted a copy of that written fee agreement. In addition, the appellant's counsel submitted a detailed accounting of his hours worked in this case, including the hours he worked prior to entering into the August 18, 1998, written fee agreement with the appellant. Further, the agency did not dispute that the appellant incurred the attorney fees identified in the accounting. Thus, the evidence of record shows that the appellant incurred attorney fees prior to the execution of the August 18, 1998, fee agreement. Williams, 64 MSPR at 41. Thus, the administrative judge, by not discussing the evidence of a written fee agreement prior to August 18, 1998, did not harm the agency's substantive rights. Id.

Should the fee agreement be oral, its terms should be described in counsel's affidavit. See Sprenger v. Dept. of Interior, 34 MSPR 664, 668 (1987) (Board does not require fee agreements to be written; counsel's affidavit describing the nature of the fee agreement is sufficient); SSA v. Goodman, 33 MSPR 325, 336 (1987) (referring to an affidavit that described an oral fee agreement); cf. Martinez v. USPS, 89 MSPR 152, 158 ¶ 14 (2001) (acknowledgment order required a copy of the fee agreement or a summary of its terms). A fee agreement may be established by affidavits reciting an oral agreement that supplements a written agreement concerning application of a portion of back pay toward legal fees. Metz v. Dept. of Treasury, 34 MSPR 625, 629 (1987). If the description of the oral terms of the agreement vary from the billing practices under the agreement, the Board may be inclined to accept the practice as the proper description of the retainer agreement. See Lizut v. Dept. of Army, 51 MSPR 549 (1991) (practice of billing at an hourly rate caused the Board to disregard the assertion that the retainer was purely contingent).

If the fee agreement is not included with the fee application, the Board has remanded the case to the regional office following consideration of a PFR so that the region may consider the fee agreement that the Board requires counsel to submit. Phillips v. Dept. of Transp., 30 MSPR 356, 359-60 (1986). But fee awards do not require a fee agreement and the Board considers other evidence of the reasonableness of an hourly rate, Kilpatrick v. VA, CB-7121-13-0181-A-1 (NP 1/16/2015):

To establish the appropriate hourly rate, the attorney fee application must be accompanied by a copy of the fee agreement, if one exists, as well as evidence of the attorney's customary billing rate for similar work. Guy v. Department of the Army, 118 M.S.P.R. 45, ¶ 15 (2012). As noted above, the appellant has not provided a copy of the applicable fee agreement for his request for review of an arbitration award by the Board. However, the customary billing rate may be established by showing the hourly rate at which the attorney actually billed other clients for similar work during the period for which the attorney seeks fees or, if the attorney has insufficient billings to establish a customary billing rate, then by affidavits from other attorneys in the community with similar experience stating their billing rates for similar work. Id. The appellant has submitted evidence of his attorney's billing rate for other clients so the Board can determine the appropriate hourly rate despite the absence of a written fee agreement. The relevant market rate for the determination of reasonableness of an attorney fee request is the prevailing community rate for similar services in the community in which the attorney ordinarily practices. 5 C.F.R. § 1201.203(a)(3).

...

The appellant's attorney submits that his hourly rate has been $450.00 since January 1, 2009. The appellant also has submitted the following documents in support of the reasonableness of this rate: a statement from his attorney summarizing his background, experience, and prior federal sector cases where he was awarded fees; a 2006 rate sheet for attorney fees from the Community Legal Services of Philadelphia; an attorney fee award from the Eastern District of Pennsylvania in 2005, granting a rate of $425 per hour; and a declaration from another local attorney stating that Mr. Friedman's hourly rate is appropriate in the Philadelphia market. The appellant's attorney states in his submission that he has over 25 years of experience handling federal labor and employment matters. The agency has not contested the hourly rate for the appellant's attorney. We find no evidence to support a finding that the rate was not based upon market considerations. Thus, we find the $450.00 hourly rate to be reasonable.
