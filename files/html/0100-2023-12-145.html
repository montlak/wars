a. Jurisdictional Issue

There is an exception to the rule that an issue must be raised below if it is to be preserved for later review, and that exception concerns the jurisdiction or authority of the tribunal to hear the case at all. The tribunal's jurisdiction to hear the case, we are told, is always at issue, Pennington v. VA, 57 MSPR 8, 9-11 (1993):

The Board's jurisdiction is limited to that conferred by statute or regulation. See Miller v. Dept. of Treasury, 47 MSPR 223, 227 (1991). Jurisdiction cannot be conferred by any action or inaction of the parties, and the issue of jurisdiction may be raised at any time in the course of a Board appeal. See id.; Walton v. Dept. of Navy, 42 MSPR 244, 249-50 (1989).

...

Generally, an appellant should be given specific notice of the jurisdictional issue, and an opportunity to reply to the notice, before his appeal is dismissed for lack of jurisdiction. See Mitchell v. Dept. of Defense, 46 MSPR 154, 159-60 (1990). In the present appeal, the issue of jurisdiction was not raised below by the parties or the administrative judge. However, the agency clearly raised the issue in its petition for review, and the appellant responded to it. We note, in any event, that the lack of a jurisdictional notice below did not prejudice the appellant's substantive rights, because the record plainly shows that the Board is without jurisdiction over this appeal. See Mitchell, 46 MSPR at 160....

Jackson v. OPM, 62 MSPR 6, 10 (1994), said of a jurisdictional issue raised in OPM's PFR:

Under most circumstances, the Board will not consider an argument raised for the first time on review. See Miller v. Department of Justice, 41 MSPR 353, 356 (1989). Here, though, OPM's arguments challenge the Board's jurisdiction. As a limited-jurisdiction federal tribunal, the Board must satisfy itself that it has jurisdiction over the matter before it, cf. Hambsch v. United States, 857 F.2d 763, 764 (Fed. Cir. 1988), and may raise the matter of its own jurisdiction sua sponte at any time, Marren v. Department of Justice, 51 MSPR 632, 636 (1991), aff'd, 980 F.2d 745 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table). The Board "will not act where it lacks statutory or regulatory authority." 51 MSPR at 636 (citation omitted). Thus, OPM will not be deemed to have waived the contentions it raises in its petition for review, even though it did not raise them below. Cf. Phillips v. General Services Administration, 924 F.2d 1577, 1579 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (the government was permitted to argue before the Court of Appeals that the Board, whose decision was under appeal, lacked jurisdiction, even though it did not contest jurisdiction in the Board appeal).

See Conley v. Dept. of Treasury, 8 MSPR 118, 121, 7 MSPB 603 (1981) (Board viewed with "extreme disfavor" a submission of agency documentation for the first time in a PFR, but the Board nonetheless remanded the case for reconsideration in light of agency evidentiary submissions on probationary status); Fassett v. USPS, 76 MSPR 137, 139 (1997) (jurisdiction may be raised, sua sponte, by the Board at any time); Waldrop v. USPS, 72 MSPR 12, 15 (1996) (Board permitted the agency to raise for the first time in its PFR argument, contrary to the position taken on the appeal, that the employee was not a supervisory employee with MSPB appeal rights. "[T]he agency cannot be estopped from arguing that the Board lacks jurisdiction, notwithstanding the position it took before the administrative judge, and notwithstanding the findings and testimony of the deciding official.").

When the Board started issuing decisions on PFRs in 2022 after a hiatus of five years when it lacked a quorum, a series of nonprecedential decisions, often involving IRA cases, demonstrated a willingness of the Board to receive new evidence and new arguments at the PFR level if the evidence and arguments implicated jurisdiction (often an issue in IRA appeals and appeals of constructive adverse actions or enforcement of last chance agreements). The Board did not revise its PFR review authority, but it seemed more liberal in its application. See, e.g., Searcy v. Dept. of Air Force, AT-0752-17-0083-I-1 (NP 2/10/2023) ("[T]there is an exception for arguments regarding the Board's jurisdiction because this issue may be raised at any time during the Board proceedings. Pirkkala v. Department of Justice, 123 M.S.P.R. 288, ¶ 5 (2016)."). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Review of New Evidence" and "Jurisdictional Issue."]

Agencies may be more successful than are appellants in raising jurisdictional arguments for the first time in a PFR. Washington v. Dept. of Navy, 77 MSPR 525, 528 (1998), noted:

Although it is well established that the issue of jurisdiction is always before the Board, see Becker v. Department of Veterans Affairs, MSPB Docket No. CH-1221-97-0190-W-1, slip op. at 4 (Sept. 17, 1997), the Board generally will not consider arguments raised for the first time in a petition for review absent a showing that they are based on new and material facts not previously available despite the party's due diligence. Banks v. Department of the Air Force, 4 MSPR 268, 271 (1980). The appellant has made no such showing here, although he was afforded that opportunity in response to the administrative judge's jurisdictional order. Thus, we have not considered these arguments on petition for review. See Timberlake v. U.S. Postal Service, 76 MSPR 172, 175 (1997).

Compare the result in Washington with Fishbein v. DHHS, 102 MSPR 4, 8 n.2, 2006 MSPB 96 (2006), when an agency reversed its position at the level of the PFR and supported jurisdiction over an employee's IRA case:

The agency did not raise these arguments below, and, in fact, indicated that it believed that the Board lacks jurisdiction over this appeal. Generally, the Board will not consider new arguments on petition for review absent a showing that they are based on new and material evidence not previously available despite the party's due diligence. See Lovoy v. Department of Health & Human Services, 94 M.S.P.R. 571, ¶ 30 (2003). However, we will consider the agency's new arguments on the issue of jurisdiction because that issue is always before the Board and may be raised by any party or sua sponte by the Board at any time during Board proceedings. See id.

But Simmitt v. VA, NY-315H-09-0345-I-1 (Final Order 3/19/2010), involving the appeal by a terminated probationer, considered on a PFR an issue that related to jurisdiction even though the issue or argument was not first presented to the AJ:

A probationary employee terminated for pre-employment reasons is entitled to notice and an opportunity to respond before the agency issues a decision on the termination. 5 C.F.R. § 315.805. The appellant alleges for the first time on petition for review that the agency should have afforded her the procedural protections of 5 C.F.R. § 315.805. Further, she submits evidence dated prior to her employment. Generally, the Board will not consider evidence and argument submitted for the first time with the petition for review absent a showing that it was unavailable before the record was closed despite the party's due diligence. Banks v. Department of the Air Force, 4 M.S.P.R. 268, 271 (1980); Avansino v. U.S. Postal Service, 3 M.S.P.R. 211, 214 (1980); 5 C.F.R. § 1201.115. However, the issue of the Board's jurisdiction is always before the Board and may be raised by either party or sua sponte by the Board at any time during a Board proceeding. See Edwards v. Department of State, 98 M.S.P.R. 481, ¶ 4 (2005). The appellant fails to make a nonfrivolous allegation of any pre-employment basis for the agency's action rather than the agency's stated basis for its action, the appellant's performance and conduct after her appointment. Thus, the appellant has failed to establish Board jurisdiction over the agency's termination action for pre-appointment reasons.

A whistleblower's appeal, Muhleisen v. VA, DE-1221-13-0345-W-1 (NP 11/10/2014) (Member Robbins dissenting), considered a jurisdictional issue raised first on PFR by a pro se appellant:

We agree with the administrative judge that the record below does not identify when the appellant made these disclosures. Nevertheless, in her petition for review, the appellant specifies that she made these disclosures to her immediate supervisors in "early 1999" and that shortly thereafter her supervisors increased their alleged retaliatory practices, thus causing her to leave employment with the agency in September of that year. Generally, the Board will not consider a new argument on review absent a showing that it is based on new and material evidence not previously available despite the party's due diligence. See Young v. U.S. Postal Service, 115 M.S.P.R. 424, ¶ 11 (2010). Under the limited circumstances of this case, however, where the appellant is proceeding pro se,[5] and her new arguments address whether she has made a nonfrivolous allegation establishing an element of the Board's IRA jurisdiction over her appeal, we find it appropriate to consider the new argument for the first time on petition for review. See, e.g., Hudson v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 104 M.S.P.R. 283, ¶ 6 (2006) (the WPA is a remedial statute and should be broadly construed); Smith v. Department of the Army, 80 M.S.P.R. 311, ¶ 7 (1998) (pro se pleadings not held to same standard as those submitted by counsel). Based upon her allegations that she made her disclosures to her supervisors in 1999 and that she thereafter endured an increased campaign of harassment which led to her decision to resign, we find that the appellant has nonfrivolously alleged that her protected disclosure was a contributing factor in her alleged involuntary resignation. See Jessup v. Department of Homeland Security, 107 M.S.P.R. 1, ¶ 10 (2007) (an appellant's allegation that a supervisor was aware of a protected disclosure is enough to satisfy the nonfrivolous allegation standard for contributing factor).

[5] The record reflects that the appellant was unrepresented in her proceedings before the Board and OSC, specifically with regard to her whistleblower retaliation claims.

See Trabue v. USPS, 102 MSPR 14, 16 ¶ 6, 2006 MSPB 99 (2006) (as to evidence submitted with the PFR concerning the appellant's veterans preference status, the Board reopened the appeal on its own motion "because the evidence the appellant has submitted with his petition for review implicates the Board's jurisdiction and warrants an outcome different from that in the initial decision").

The Board occasionally distinguishes treatment of a new claim of jurisdiction and evidence concerning a claim of jurisdiction first raised in an appeal. A probationer may not confer jurisdiction on the Board by alleging partisan political discrimination for the first time in a PFR. O'Connor v. Dept. of Navy, 5 MSPR 338, 339, 5 MSPB 362 (1981); see Hammond v. VA, 98 MSPR 359, 361 n.* (2005) (as to an issue of marital status discrimination raised in the PFR: "the appellant failed to state below sufficient facts to demonstrate marital status discrimination, and the Board will not consider such an argument raised for the first time in a PFR, as it is here, absent a showing that it is based on new and material evidence that was not previously available despite due diligence. See Banks v. Department of the Air Force, 4 M.S.P.R. 268, 271 (1980)."). But see Conley v. Dept. of Treasury, 8 MSPR 118, 121, 7 MSPB 603 (1981) (agency could supply with its PFR information concerning the appellant's probationary status). The Board may decide to omit discussion of what once was a jurisdictional issue if the issue is no longer of decisional significance. That approach was announced in a decision in a VEOA appeal (referring to the same approach in Individual Right of Action (whistleblower) appeals) when so much time has passed since the effective date of the underlying remedial statute than it is pointless to address the matter of statutory retroactivity. See Davis v. DOD, 2022 MSPB 20, n.1 (2022). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Jurisdictional Issue."]
