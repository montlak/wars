5. Informal Discovery; Control by Agencies of Employees' Time

Nothing in Board practice requires depositions or other forms of discovery. Some counsel prefer to obtain information by telephone or personal interviews of cooperative witnesses. However, interviews may be hindered by the agency if its employees are instructed by agency counsel or managers not to speak with the appellant or her counsel. Pippinger v. Rubin, 129 F.3d 519, 534 n.8 (10th Cir. 1997), considered unobjectionable in Privacy Act litigation a directive to agency employees that they not participate in interviews by plaintiff's counsel. Discovery was available for that purpose:

...The Secretary of the Treasury, "under the regulations as to the custody, use, and preservation of the records, papers, and property appertaining to the business of his department, may take from a subordinate...all discretion as to permitting the records in his custody to be used for any other purpose than the collection of the revenue, and reserve for his own determination all matters of that character." Boske v. Comingore, 177 U.S. 459, 470, 20 S. Ct. 701, 705-06, 44 L. Ed. 846 (1900); see also United States ex rel. Touhy v. Ragen, 340 U.S. 462, 71 S. Ct. 416, 95 L. Ed. 417 (1950). The Boske decision was based on a provision of the U.S. Code that has been revised several times, but presently provides that:

The head of an Executive department...may prescribe regulations for the government of his department, the conduct of its employees, the distribution and performance of its business, and the custody, use, and preservation of its records, papers, and property. This section does not authorize withholding information from the public or limiting the availability of records to the public.

5 USC § 301 (1994).

Thus, the magistrate judge did not abuse its discretion in denying Pippinger's motion to compel. Pippinger had no legal right to speak "informally" to any particular witness. Fed.R.Civ.P. 26(b)(1) provides that "[p]arties may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, which is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action...." (emphasis added). Rules 26 through 36 enumerate the formal mechanisms of discovery. Nowhere do the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure provide litigants with a right to conduct informal interviews.

Cf. Smith v. Cromer, 159 F.3d 875 (4th Cir. 1998) (considering 5 USC 301, cited in Pippinger, supra, as to an agency's directive to its employees not to comply with a state court subpoena for documents and testimony in a state criminal proceeding).

Although an agency may take steps, including issuance of regulations, to prevent its personnel from conveying information outside of discovery channels to appellant or her counsel, the Board will not police enforcement of those regulations by preventing appellant from obtaining information outside of regular discovery proceedings, Christofili v. Dept. of Army, 81 MSPR 384, 389-93 ¶¶ 12-22 (1999):

The agency asserts that the appellant must use the Board's discovery procedures as the sole method of obtaining evidence within the agency's control in a Board appeal. We disagree.

The Board's regulations at 5 CFR §§ 1201.71-.75 set forth the Board's discovery procedures. See Heath v. Department of Transportation, 64 MSPR 638, 651 (1994). Discovery is "the process, apart from the hearing, by which a party may obtain relevant information,...from another person or party, that the other person or party has not otherwise provided." 5 CFR § 1201.72(b).

The Board, however, has no regulatory requirement that a party obtain evidence only through the Board's discovery procedures. Rather, the parties are free to obtain relevant information from each other informally through any lawful means without using the Board's discovery process. Thus, an appellant may obtain relevant information through any lawful means, including the cooperation of sympathetic agency employees. The Board's job is not to supervise the agency's management of its own workforce.

The Board encourages "in the interest of adjudicatory economy" the parties to reach "an informal resolution of [an]appellant's request for information." Stein-Verbit v. Department of Commerce, 72 MSPR 332, 337 (1996). In this regard, the Board encourages its administrative judges when problems arise from an appellant's informal request for information from an agency "simply to remind the appellant of the Board's discovery procedures, to answer any questions about discovery that the parties may have...and to remove [himself]from the discovery process until [his]participation [is]necessary to resolve any motions that might arise." Id.[3]

[3] Of course, if a party attempts to obtain relevant information from another person or party informally without using the Board's discovery procedures, and the other person or party does not agree to such "informal" discovery, the party that failed to timely use the Board's discovery process and file a motion to compel discovery with the administrative judge has no basis for asserting error or prejudice to his substantive rights by the other party's or person's failure to produce the information. See, e.g., Buscher v. U.S. Postal Service, 69 MSPR 204, 210 (1995); Head v. Office of Personnel Management, 53 MSPR 421, 422 (1992).

Additionally, the Board's discovery procedures are not the sole procedures by which a party may obtain documentation from an agency to defend against an action appealable to the Board.

"Procedures are available under both the Board's discovery regulations...and under the Freedom of Information Act, 5 USC § 552 [(FOIA)], to obtain documents to defend against such an action." Heath, 64 MSPR at 651. The Board's consistent practice has been to consider relevant and material evidence obtained by an appellant from an agency under FOIA. See, e.g., King-Roberts v. Office of Personnel Management, 80 MSPR 431, 433 n.2 (1998) (in response to the appellant's FOIA requests, the Office of Personnel Management provided her with numerous materials regarding its criteria for review in disability retirement cases); Granger v. Department of Justice, 71 MSPR 487, 491 (1996) (the Board considered the appellant's late-discovered evidence which he obtained from the agency under FOIA that was material to the issue of Board jurisdiction where he exercised due diligence in submitting his FOIA request to the agency even prior to filing his Board appeal and the administrative judge closed the record before the appellant could obtain discovery from the agency that he had been actively pursuing during the proceedings); Armas v. Department of Justice, 71 MSPR 244, 247, 249-50 (1996) (the Board considered evidence regarding the Board's jurisdiction obtained by the appellant in response to his FOIA requests to the Office of Special Counsel made while his Board appeal was pending).

An administrative judge may exercise his sound discretion to impose sanctions upon the appropriate party for its failure to follow the Board's regulations or its failure to respond to the administrative judge's orders as necessary to serve the ends of justice if the party has failed to exercise due diligence or has exhibited negligence or bad faith in its efforts to so comply. See, e.g., 5 CFR § 1201.43; Ellshoff v. Department of the Interior, 78 MSPR 615, 1 4 (1998); Williams v. Office of Personnel Management, 71 MSPR 597, 603 (1996), aff'd, 119 F.3d 16 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (Table). We find that the imposition of sanctions under the circumstances of this case would not serve the ends of justice. As stated above, the Board has no regulatory requirement that a party obtain evidence only through the Board's discovery procedures; rather, the parties are free to obtain relevant information from each other informally through any lawful means without using the Board's discovery process. Indeed, in the interest of adjudicatory economy the parties are encouraged to resolve an appellant's request for information informally. See Stein-Verbit, 72 MSPR at 337. Although the agency argues generally that the disclosure of information regarding this case by Firkin and Turse to the appellant could put the agency in danger of noncompliance with the Privacy Act, 5 USC § 552a, the agency has made only bare assertions.

Accordingly, under the circumstances of this case, we find that the imposition of sanctions against the appellant are not necessary to serve the ends of justice because the agency has not shown that he has failed to exercise due diligence or has exhibited negligence or bad faith in his efforts to comply with the Board regulations. See, e.g., Ellshoff, 78 MSPR 14; Williams, MSPR at 603.

In summary, we find that an appellant may obtain the disclosure of relevant information from the agency and agency employees through any lawful means other than the Board's discovery procedures, including informal requests for information from the agency and agency employees and by using FOIA....
