a. Incomplete Motion

Thousands of individual lawyers handle Board cases. Internet searches show that their numbers increase weekly. They come from every village, hamlet, and town. For some attorneys, one and only one Board case will ever come their way. The Board recognizes that it cannot reasonably expect a large and varied group of lawyers to fully fathom the complexities of the MSPB fee review procedures. If fees are warranted but the fee petition is incomplete as presented to the AJ, before the Board employs the extreme sanction of denial of the petition, the record should show that the hourly rate claimed is unreasonable or that the hours charged are excessive. If the petition is not unreasonable on its face but only incomplete, it will be adjudicated subject to disallowances by the AJ, who is not precluded from requesting additional information for adjudication of the petition. Johnson v. IRS, 24 MSPR 202, 203-04 (1984) (citing Jordan v. DOJ, 691 F.2d 514 (D.C. Cir. 1982), and Brown v. Stackler, 612 F.2d 1057 (7th Cir. 1980)); accord Rumsey v. DOJ, 866 F.3d 1375, 1379 (Fed. Cir. 2017) ("a petitioner who is a prevailing party is entitled to attorney's fees and costs, even if the supporting documentation is in some way deficient");Obremski v. OPM, 31 MSPR 554, 558 (1986) (Board will not deny fees where they have been found to be warranted in the interest of justice but there is insufficient evidence regarding reasonableness; "rather, the Board will determine the minimum reasonable fee on the basis of the record before it").

Under Wilson v. DHHS, 834 F.2d 1011 (Fed. Cir. 1987), the AJ should request additional information before penalizing the appellant. The Board applies the Wilson rule when it finds a deficiency in a fee application; it will remand the case to the region for additional information from the appellant or the attorney to meet the deficiency noted by the Board. See Ford v. Dept. of Navy, 48 MSPR 298, 303 (1991); Rose v. Dept. of Navy, 47 MSPR 5, 12 (1991) ("The record shows that the appellant was afforded the opportunity to respond in writing to any alleged deficiencies and to supply reasons and new materials to address any concerns raised."); Peters v. OPM, 34 MSPR 570, 575 (1987) (remanding for counsel to submit evidence and argument concerning the reasonableness of fees and evidence of the fee agreement).

The rule under Johnson, supra, is that if fees are warranted in the interest of justice, complete denial of fees will be approved only where there is something in the record suggesting the request is outrageous. McLane v. Dept. of Navy, 33 MSPR 404, 407 (1987); Blumenson v. DHHS, 30 MSPR 644, 651 (1986) (where the appellant's request was not outrageous but fell short of case law proof requirements, a fee petition was subject to disallowances based on the Board's judgment in similar cases before it). The Board elaborated on Johnson in Harris v. USDA, 48 MSPR 189, 195-96 (1991):

In Johnson...[t]he Board held that, before it employs the extreme sanction of denial of attorney fees in a case where they are warranted in the interest of justice, there should be something in the record suggesting that the hourly rate is unreasonable or that the hours claimed are excessive.... The Board further held that, where it awards fees in such a situation (i.e., the appellant's request was not outrageous, but it fell short of case law proof requirements), the fee petition will be subject to disallowances based on the Board's judgment in similar cases before it....

When the Board cannot make sense of portions of a fee petition that comes to it through a PFR, it may remand the matter to the AJ to develop additional evidence for issuance of a supplemental decision. See Woodall v. FERC, 33 MSPR 127, 136 (1987).
