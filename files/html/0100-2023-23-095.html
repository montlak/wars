1. Employee Other Than Postal Service

There seems to be a recognition, if not a clear consensus, that an appellant need not attempt to mitigate the employer's back pay liability by securing other employment during the course of the employee's appeal, Power v. United States, 597 F.2d 258, 264 (Ct. Cl. 1979):

Under the portion of this court's decision in the Schwartz case, supra, dealing with the question of when the obligation of the claimant in that case to seek other employment began, 181 F. Supp. at 410, 149 Ct. Cl. at 148, the proper view seems to be that a government employee who has been improperly removed from his position is not required to seek other employment while appellate administrative proceedings are in progress and the employee is endeavoring to obtain reinstatement; but that the obligation to seek other employment does arise when the adverse action removing him from his position becomes final at the administrative level. In the present case, the plaintiff's removal from his position actually did not become final until the Board of Appeals and Review of the Civil Service Commission rendered the final administrative decision on April 5, 1974, upholding the plaintiff's removal and finally concluding the series of administrative appeals to which the plaintiff was entitled.

Urbina v. United States, 428 F.2d 1280, 1287, 192 Ct. Cl. 875 (Ct. Cl. 1970), cited in Powers, noted:

...An illegally discharged employee will not be denied his back salary for failure to work during periods when his time is reasonably consumed in prosecuting appeals from such discharge. Schwartz v. United States, 181 F. Supp. 408, 149 Ct. Cl. 145 (1960).

From Schwartz v. United States, 181 F. Supp. 408, 410 (Ct. Cl. 1960), cited in Power and Urbina:

...Pursuant to this decision the Post Office removed plaintiff from his employment effective September 15, 1954. During this period when plaintiff was preparing for the hearing and while awaiting the Board's decision, we think the plaintiff had reasonable grounds for not making an effort to secure other extra employment. His time was undoubtedly taken up in the effort to secure favorable action by the Board. We think, therefore, that plaintiff had reasonable grounds for not seeking the added employment until the final adverse action removing him from his position. Therefore, plaintiff is entitled to recover for the period from March 12, 1954, through September 15, 1954.

...

After that final action there was no reasonable excuse for plaintiff's refusal to make any effort to secure other employment and thereby mitigate the damages resulting from the action taken by the Post Office Department.

See Andress v. USPS, 56 MSPR 501, 505 (1993) (noting AJ's finding that a removed employee was not required to seek replacement employment during the pendency of an appeal (citing Naekel v. Dept. of Transp., FAA, 850 F.2d 682, 685 (Fed. Cir. 1988))); Schultz v. USPS, 89 MSPR 123, 127 n.5 (2001) ("[t]he Back Pay Act does not require that a back pay award be offset by what an employee reasonably should have earned during the relevant period; rather, 5 U.S.C. § 5596 requires only that an award of back pay be offset by amounts actually earned by the employee through other employment during the period at issue. See 5 U.S.C. § 5596(b)(1)(A)(i)."); cf. Marren v. DOJ, 34 MSPR 520, 524 (1987) ("Mr. Marren was free to spend the entire period of his unwarranted removal performing union business, internal or otherwise, or any other nonremunerative pursuit of his choice, without adversely affecting a potential back pay award.").

There is some contrary authority. Woodall v. FERC, 33 MSPR 127, 131 n.3 (1987), a counsel fee decision, cryptically commented:

In most circumstances, there is a requirement for back pay awards that the employee make diligent efforts to secure other employment after a suspension or removal. Karanek v. United States, 184 Ct. Cl. 125, 394 F.2d 525 (1968); cert. denied, 394 U.S. 1013 (1969). See also cases cited in 5 USCA § 5596, note 85.

Some Pre-Reform Act law implied a necessity for an appellant to mitigate his damages. See, e.g., Mazaleski v. Treusdell, 562 F.2d 701 (D.C. Cir. 1977). Some clarification arrived in Marshall v. GPO, 45 MSPR 62, 66 (1990):

[T]he agency cites to cases for the proposition that a discharged employee has the duty to diligently seek other employment in order to qualify for back pay when the cases, in fact, do not so hold. See Naekel v. Department of Transportation, 850 F.2d 682, 685 (Fed. Cir. 1988), citing Power v. United States, 597 F.2d 258, 264-65, 220 Ct. Cl. 157 (1979)...; Urbina v. United States, 428 F.2d 1280, 1287, 192 Ct. Cl. 875 (1970). These cases hold that an improperly discharged employee is not required to seek other employment while appellate administrative proceedings are in progress and the employee is endeavoring to obtain reinstatement. The duty to seek other employment only arises when the adverse action removing him from his position becomes final at the administrative level....
