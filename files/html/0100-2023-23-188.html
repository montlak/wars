broida mspb Chapter 16: Settlement, Remedies, and Enforcement Chapter 16: Settlement, Remedies, and Enforcement, Subchapter 03: Enforcement, Section B: Enforcement and Interpretation of Settlement Agreements, Subsection 02: Agency Enforcement Initiative, Part b: Agreement Not to Reapply for Employment With Agency
b. Agreement Not to Reapply for Employment With Agency

McFadden v. USDA, 95 Fed. Appx. 332, 336 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP), suggested that an agency could make a choice between self-help and enforcement proceedings to implement a clause barring an appellant from applying for an agency job. Campbell v. Broadcasting Bd. of Gov., 96 MSPR 86, 88 ¶ 6 (2004), authorized the agency to file a copy of the Board's order with EEOC to show that the Board enforced a settlement provision requiring the appellant to withdraw an EEO complaint.

The Board permits agencies to reject applications from appellants who violate settlements that include an agreement not to reapply for employment. If the appellant succeeds in obtaining reemployment, is discovered, and is then fired for violating the agreement not to reapply, the Board adjudicates the ensuing appeal if it was not waived through the settlement, Herbert v. USPS, 86 MSPR 80, 83-84 ¶¶ 5-6 (2000):

...As noted above, the AJ dismissed the appeal for lack of jurisdiction, on the basis that the agency effected the appellant's removal in order to accomplish his compliance with the May 1998 settlement agreement. In support of this determination, the AJ relied on Bales v. Department of Transportation, 54 MSPR 187, 191 (1992), Eaton v. U.S. Postal Service, 50 MSPR 377, 380 (1991), and Moseley v. U.S. Postal Service, 48 MSPR 667, 668 (1991). For the reasons discussed below, we find that the AJ erred by dismissing this appeal for lack of jurisdiction on the basis that the removal action was pursuant to the settlement agreement.

In Bales, involving a petition for enforcement of a settlement agreement in which the appellant agreed to resign, the Board found that the agency properly resorted to "self-help" to effect the appellant's resignation, in the face of his refusal to do so, in order to accomplish his compliance with the settlement agreement. 54 MSPR at 188, 191. In Eaton, 50 MSPR at 380, and Moseley, 48 MSPR at 668, also involving petitions for enforcement of settlement agreements, the Board held that the agency was not required to obtain the Board's prior authorization to remove the appellant for breach of the settlement agreement. These cases did not address the issue of the Board's jurisdiction over a removal action, as is involved here. We therefore find that they do not support the AJ's determination that the Board lacks jurisdiction over this removal appeal.

The AJ found, and the appellant does not dispute, that he violated the settlement term in which he "agreed not to seek employment with the Postal Service." This finding is immaterial to the issue of Board jurisdiction here, however. Where an employee is removed for violating a settlement agreement with the agency, the Board is not deprived of jurisdiction based on the settlement agreement unless the agreement includes a provision waiving the appellant's right to file a Board appeal. See Ellis v. U.S. Postal Service, 77 MSPR 675, 677 (1998); Fassett v. U.S. Postal Service, 76 MSPR 137, 139-40, review dismissed, 132 F.3d 49 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (Table); Rogers, 59 MSPR at 651. Thus, the material jurisdictional issue, not addressed by the AJ, is whether the May 1998 settlement agreement contained a provision waiving the appellant's right to file a Board appeal from his removal. The settlement agreement here includes no provision whatsoever waiving appeal rights. See Ellis, 77 MSPR at 677 (that a settlement agreement is silent regarding a particular matter does not mean that it is ambiguous); Fassett, 76 MSPR at 139-41 (same). We therefore find that the settlement agreement here does not deprive the Board of jurisdiction over this appeal.

Roane v. USPS, 53 MSPR 1, 3 (1992), allowed the agency to decide for itself how to deal with an appellant who allegedly violated a settlement that included a provision that the appellant not again apply for a job with the agency:

We agree with the administrative judge that the appellant was in noncompliance with the settlement agreement because he submitted an application for employment with the agency. However, it is also true that the agency had properly rejected the appellant's application under the terms of the settlement agreement before it filed its petition for enforcement. Because there is no pending employment application, there is nothing for the Board to enforce in this proceeding. The matter is accordingly moot. An issue becomes moot when "the parties lack a legally cognizable interest in the outcome...." Jefferson v. Abrams, 747 F.2d 94, 96 (2d Cir. 1984), quoting Murphy v. Hunt, 455 U.S. 478, 481, 102 S. Ct. 1181, 1183, 71 L. Ed.2d 353 (1982). Because there is no pending employment application, there is no legally cognizable interest in the outcome of this issue. The petition for enforcement relating to the appellant's employment application is therefore DISMISSED AS MOOT.[2]

[2] The agency also requests the Board to order the appellant not to seek employment with the Postal Service in the future. There is no evidence that the appellant has filed or intends to file another employment application with the agency. Even if an application is filed, the agency has the authority to reject it based on the terms of the settlement agreement. See Moseley v. US Postal Service, 48 MSPR 667 (1991) (agency filed petition for enforcement requesting the Board to remove the appellant because he violated terms of settlement agreement; the Board found that the authority to remove an employee was vested in the agency and that enforcement of the settlement agreement did not require Board involvement). See also Eaton v. US Postal Service, 50 MSPR 377 (1991). Therefore, we decline to order the relief requested.

Winston v. Dept. of Treasury, 114 MSPR 594, 2010 MSPB 180 (2010), declined an agency's initiative to enforce a settlement by having the Board declare the appellant to be terminated and for the Board to affirmatively deny her the right to appeal. The appellant's settlement provided, id. at 596 ¶ 3:

...The appellant agreed to withdraw her Board appeal based on the terms of the settlement agreement, in pertinent part, as follows:

1. The agency agrees:

a. to rescind and remove from appellant's Official Personnel Folder (OPF) the Standard Form 50 reflecting her removal effective December 7, 2007, and replace it with a Standard Form 50 reflecting her resignation for personal reasons, effective December 7, 2007; and

* * *

2. In consideration for paragraph 1, appellant agrees:

* * *

b. she will neither seek, nor apply for, nor accept any employment whatsoever with the Internal Revenue Service. Appellant understands that if she applies for or accepts such employment, she will be considered in breach of this agreement, and the agency will reinstate her termination for the reasons set forth in the decision letter of December 4, 2007, sustaining the October 4, 2007 proposal letter, and Appellant will have no right of appeal in the event the agency takes such action.

The Board found the relief requested either beyond its jurisdiction or beyond the terms of the settlement, id. at 597-98 ¶¶ 6-9:

In recommending that the Board (1) order the agency to convert the appellant's resignation into a removal, and (2) declare that she has no right of Board appeal from such a removal action, the administrative judge has erred. See Eaton v. U.S. Postal Service, 50 M.S.P.R. 377, 380 (1991); Moseley v. U.S. Postal Service, 48 M.S.P.R. 667, 668 (1991). As the Board held in Eaton and Moseley, that relief is not within the Board's jurisdiction to grant, and, consistent with the Board's limited jurisdiction, that relief was not specified in the settlement agreement.

First, the relief requested--permission to reinstate the appellant's termination--is not within the Board's jurisdiction to grant. The jurisdiction of the Board is not plenary. It has only that jurisdiction granted it by law, rule, or regulation. See Noguera v. Office of Personnel Management, 878 F.2d 1422 (Fed. Cir. 1989). Although the Board has broad authority to enforce the terms of a settlement agreement entered into the record, McClain v. United States Postal Service, 40 M.S.P.R. 66, 70 (1989), the Board's appellate jurisdiction does not include the power to remove an employee of another agency, the relief requested here. 5 U.S.C. § 7501 et seq.; 5 U.S.C. § 7701 et seq. Moreover, because the power to remove in this case is outside the Board's appellate jurisdiction, any decision pertaining to the appellant's removal would be advisory. Such advisory opinions are prohibited by 5 U.S.C. § 1204(h) (West Supp. 1991). Shaw v. Department of the Navy, 39 M.S.P.R. 586, 591 (1989), overruled on other grounds, Joyce v. Department of the Air Force, 74 M.S.P.R. 112 (1997).

Second, the settlement agreement does not contemplate the relief requested by the agency. In regard to the agency's request that the Board order the agency to convert the appellant's resignation into a removal, the settlement agreement provides only that "the agency"--not the Board--"will reinstate her termination." Specific performance of a contract may be denied if the party requesting it has an adequate remedy available through non-judicial means, including some type of self-help, whether provided by the terms of the contract itself or by some other means. Bales v. Department of Transportation, 54 M.S.P.R. 187, 191 (1992); 5A Corbin on Contracts, § 1158 (1964). Because the settlement agreement gives the agency the remedy to itself reinstate the appellant's termination, the Board's involvement is not required. However, even if the settlement agreement had specified that the Board would reinstate the appellant's removal in these circumstances, such a provision would be void as contrary to the principles discussed in Eaton. The Board does not have the authority to remove an employee or to advise an agency to do so, but only to review an agency's decision to take such action. Eaton, 50 M.S.P.R. at 380. In sum, an order from the Board to reinstate the appellant's termination would be neither authorized by the settlement agreement nor within the Board's jurisdiction.

In regard to the agency's request that the Board declare that the appellant has no right of Board appeal from such a removal action, the settlement agreement states only that if the agency reinstates the appellant's removal, the "Appellant will have no right of appeal in the event the agency takes such [removal] action." Even if the agency had already converted the appellant's resignation into a removal, the agreement does not specify that the Board would then preemptively declare the appellant to have no right of MSPB appeal. Moreover, even if the settlement agreement had specified that the Board would issue such a direction, a provision of that kind would be void because the Board lacks the authority to issue advisory opinions. Eaton, 50 M.S.P.R. at 380.
