g. Federal Reserve Board

The Federal Reserve Board was beyond the reach of the WPA. The Reserve Board's statutory appointment authority excluded protections provided by other laws, Carney v. Bd. of Governors of Fed. Reserve Sys., 64 MSPR 394, 395-96 (1994):

Even assuming arguendo that the FRB does qualify as an "agency" under 5 USC § 2302(a)(2)(C), we find that the Federal Reserve Act, 12 USC § 221 et seq., operates to exclude Board jurisdiction over the appellant's IRA appeal. The Act provides that the "employment, compensation, leave, and expenses" of the members and employees of the FRB "shall be governed solely by the provisions of this Act...and rules and regulations of the [FRB] not inconsistent therewith...." 12 USC § 244.

The appellant argues that the delimiting language of the Federal Reserve Act was superseded by the subsequent passage of the WPA, noting that 5 USC § 2302(a)(2)(C) does not list the FRB among the executive agencies specifically excluded therein. We find no indication in the legislative history of either the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 (CSRA) or the WPA that Congress intended such a result, however. In the absence of a clear intention otherwise, a statute dealing with a narrow, precise, and specific subject, will not be controlled or nullified by a subsequent statute of general application. See Radzanower v. Touche Ross & Co., 426 U.S. 148, 153, 96 S. Ct. 1989, 1992-93, 48 L. Ed.2d 540 (1976); Morton v. Mancari, 417 U.S. 535, 550-51, 94 S. Ct. 2474, 2483, 41 L. Ed.2d 290 (1974). See also 1A Sutherland Statutory Construction § 23.15 (5th Ed.) ("Where the later general statute does not present an irreconcilable conflict, the prior special statute will be construed as remaining in effect as a qualification of or exception to the general law"). Moreover, the Board has found that the WPA does not extend Board jurisdiction over matters exclusively committed to agency review by a prior statute. See Ockerhausen v. New Jersey Department of Military and Veterans Affairs, 52 MSPR 484, 489-90 (1992) (provisions of National Guard Technicians Act restricting Board jurisdiction in cases involving National Guard technicians remain unaffected by the enactment of the CSRA or WPA). See also Chan v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 53 MSPR 617, 620 (1992) (because civil service laws do not apply to employee appointed as physician under 38 USC § 4114(a)(1)(A), Board lacked jurisdiction over employee's IRA appeal). Accordingly, we find that the specific provisions of the Federal Reserve Act regarding the FRB's personnel matters govern over the general provisions of the WPA extending whistleblower protection to federal employees.
