broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 02: Petition for Review; Reconsideration, Section G: Timeliness of PFR, Subsection 09: Justification for Late Filing, Part m: Failure to Receive Initial Decision; E-Filer, Subpart (i): Responsibility to Notify Board of Physical or Email Address Change
(i) Responsibility to Notify Board of Physical or Email Address Change

Parties need to inform the Board of address changes, electronic (for e-filers) and physical, during the pendency of Board proceedings. See, e.g., 5 CFR 1201.14(b)(7), (e)(6); 1201.26(b)(2)(2022).

The Board is not inclined to excuse a late PFR because an initial decision was not received on time when the nonreceipt is due to the appellant's error. Henderson v. Dept. of Army, 102 MSPR 198, 200-01 ¶¶ 8, 2006 MSPB 158 (2006), involving an initial decision that was transmitted by electronic filing, noted:

We note that, assuming arguendo that the appellant's unsworn statement on PFR had been sufficient to satisfy the Board's regulations at 5 C.F.R. 1201.114(f), he has failed to show good cause for his delay. The appellant claims that he did not find out until February 23, 2006, that a decision had been made on his appeal. However, he did not at that time request an extension to file a PFR. The appellant also asserts that the ID had "erroneously been sent to the wrong file" in his e-mail account. However, it is the appellant's responsibility to ensure that the Board has his correct e-mail information. See Bennett v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 97 M.S.P.R. 1, ¶ 5 (2004) (5 C.F.R. 1201.114(d) was not intended to postpone the deadline for filing a PFR where the delay in receipt of the ID was due to the appellant's own negligence). Moreover, he offers no evidence in support of his assertion.

If the appellant informs the AJ that he wishes to convert from electronic to mailed filings and service of materials from the Board, the Board excuses a delayed PFR when the initial decision is mistakenly electronically delivered to the appellant and the appellant acts quickly to file his PFR after receiving a mailed copy. See Blatt v. Dept. of Army, 121 MSPR 473, 475-76 ¶ 5, 2014 MSPB 65 (2014). If the employee, previously represented by another, represents himself in an adverse action hearing, the AJ ought to secure the employee's current address rather than send the initial decision to the former representative. A delay in filing a PFR caused by the inability of the employee to obtain the initial decision permits the filing of an otherwise untimely PFR. See Lester v. VA, 10 MSPR 359, 361 (1982).

As to the necessity for an appellant to ensure proper forwarding of his mail, May v. USPS, 108 MSPR 557, 560 ¶ 10, 2008 MSPB 89 (2008), declined to waive the filing period for a PFR delayed by years:

We find that the appellant has failed to show that he exercised due diligence or ordinary prudence that shows good cause for his untimely filing. See McPherson, 104 M.S.P.R. 547, ¶ 5. Although the appellant is pro se on review, his nearly 8-year delay in filing his petition for review is lengthy and militates against waiving the filing deadline. See, e.g., Uson v. Office of Personnel Management, 105 M.S.P.R. 402, ¶ 5, aff'd, 250 Fed. Appx. 326 (Fed. Cir. 2007). Further, in asking the Board to waive the time deadline, the appellant asserts that he changed his address and did not receive the initial decision, but he does not claim that he acted with due diligence by forwarding his mail through the Postal Service or by contacting the Board to update his mailing address. See Lima v. Department of the Air Force, 101 M.S.P.R. 64, ¶ 4 (2006). We find that the appellant's reasons supporting his request to extend the filing deadline are not persuasive because the Board has long established that an appellant is responsible for ensuring the receiving and forwarding of his mail. See Lima, 101 M.S.P.R. 64, ¶ 4; Bennett v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 97 M.S.P.R. 1, ¶ 5 (2004). The appellant's claimed failure to receive the initial decision notifying him of the dates for re-filing his appeal and filing his petition for review was the direct result of his failure to provide the Board with a current address.

See Belandres v. DOD, SF-315H-07-0430-I-1 (NP 12/6/2011) ("In asking the Board to waive the filing deadline, the appellant asserts that she changed her address and did not receive the initial decision, but she does not claim that she forwarded her mail through the Postal Service or contacted the Board to update her mailing address. The appellant's failure to fulfill these responsibilities does not constitute excusable negligence but, rather, indicates a lack of due diligence on her part.").

The need for the appellant to keep the Board informed of his or her current address was again emphasized, Moore v. OPM, 112 MSPR 512, 514-15 ¶¶ 6-8, 2009 MSPB 209 (2009):

The regulatory deadline for filing a petition for review is not intended to postpone the deadline for filing a petition for review where delayed receipt of the initial decision is due to a party's own negligence. Laboy v. U.S. Postal Service, 103 M.S.P.R. 570, ¶ 5 (2006); Lima v. Department of the Air Force, 101 M.S.P.R. 64, ¶ 4 (2006). The certificate of service reflects that the initial decision was mailed to the appellant's address of record in Washington, D.C. The Board has held that service on an appellant at the last address of record is proper. D'Aquin v. Office of Personnel Management, 65 M.S.P.R. 499, 503 (1994). We find that the appellant's delayed receipt of the initial decision was the product of his failure to provide the Board with a current address. It is well established that an appellant is responsible for ensuring the receipt and forwarding of his mail. Lima, 101 M.S.P.R. 64, ¶ 4; Johnson v. Department of the Navy, 73 M.S.P.R. 431, 433 (1997). Thus, we find that the deadline for filing the petition for review in this case was July 31, 2009, 35 days after the initial decision was issued. The appellant's petition for review was therefore untimely filed by 18 days.

To establish good cause for the untimely filing of a petition for review, a party must show that he exercised due diligence or ordinary prudence under the particular circumstances of the case. See Alonzo v. Department of the Air Force, 4 M.S.P.R. 180, 184 (1980). To determine whether an appellant has shown good cause, the Board will consider the length of the delay, the reasonableness of his excuse and his showing of due diligence, whether he is proceeding pro se, and whether he has presented evidence of the existence of circumstances beyond his control that affected his ability to comply with the time limits or of unavoidable casualty or misfortune which similarly shows a causal relationship to his inability to timely file his petition. Moorman v. Department of the Army, 68 M.S.P.R. 60, 62-63 (1995), aff'd, 79 F.3d 1167 (Fed. Cir. 1996) (Table).

In this case, the filing delay of 18 days is not minimal. See Rodriguez v. Department of the Navy, 71 M.S.P.R. 396, 398 (1996). In his motion to waive the deadline for filing his petition, the appellant states that because the initial decision was sent to a prior address, he was unaware of the time limit for filing a petition for review. That explanation does not show that the appellant exercised due diligence or ordinary prudence. As stated above, the appellant does not dispute that the initial decision was sent to the address of record that he provided to the regional office, and he does not contend that he advised the Board of any change of address. Even though the appellant is acting pro se, his failure to ensure that his mail was sent or forwarded to the proper address indicates that he has failed to act with due diligence under the circumstances of this case.

"It is well settled that an appellant is responsible for notifying the Board of any change of address and is also responsible for ensuring the timely forwarding of his own mail." Hairston v. Smithsonian Institution, 95 MSPR 397, 400 ¶ 6 (2004); see Jacks v. Dept. of Air Force, 114 MSPR 355, 358 ¶ 8, 2010 MSPB 133 (2010) ("settled law that an appellant is responsible for notifying the Board of any change of address"). The rule on notice to the Board concerning changes of physical mailing addresses applies as well to changes in email addresses for those using MSPB e-filing procedures. See Walker v. Dept. of Air Force, 109 MSPR 261, 263-64 ¶¶ 6-8, 2008 MSPB 144 (2008) (rejecting PFR as untimely when the appellant's attorney did not change her email address to timely receive an initial decision).

A change of address is supposed to be in writing. Vandegriff v. Dept. of Army, 106 MSPR 446, 448 ¶¶ 5-6, 2007 MSPB 201 (2007) (discounting the appellant's statement that he advised the Board of the change in address by phone and that he did not have the funds to send a letter; also noting that the appellant was served a copy of the initial decision by e-filing). A brief delay will likely be excused if the appellant notified the Board of a new address and if the delay is caused by a mailing delay. See Santiago v. USPS, 98 MSPR 179, 183 ¶ 7 (2005). An untimely PFR is not excused because the appellant or his counsel fails to provide the Board with a change in address, causing a delay in the arrival of the initial decision. The Board has no duty to determine the current address of the appellant or counsel. Corbett v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-1101), aff'g Corbett v. Dept. of Army, 19 MSPR 368, 369 (1984) (ordinary prudence was not shown when neither the appellant nor her counsel supplied the regional office with a change of address during the five weeks between the change and issuance of the initial decision). The Board's petition for appeal form states that the appellant must notify the Board of any change of address or telephone number while the appeal is pending. Triplett v. OPM, 105 MSPR 575, 580 ¶ 8, 2007 MSPB 126 (2007); see Ball v. Dept. of Air Force, 91 MSPR 323, 325 ¶ 5 (2002) (appellant or representative was responsible for informing the Board of an address change); Greenberg v. DOJ, 91 MSPR 42, 45 ¶ 5 (2002) (appellant's failure to notify the Board of an address change is not "excusable negligence but rather a failure to exercise due diligence"); Small v. GSA, 88 MSPR 187, 191 ¶ 8 (2001) ("It is settled law...that appellants are responsible for notifying the Board of any change of address."); Leslie v. USPS, 83 MSPR 361, 365 ¶ 11 (1999) (involving a delayed appeal: "It is the appellant's obligation to keep the agency apprised of her change of address"; failure to apprise the agency of an address change could be excused if the appellant was not mentally competent); Peria v. OPM, 69 MSPR 559, 562 (1996) (dismissing an untimely PFR: "[A]n appellant is responsible for ensuring that he is in receipt of his mail and that the Board knows where to reach him."); Smith v. Dept. of Army, 68 MSPR 607, 610 (1995) ("[N]othing in the record [showed] that the appellant ever provided the Postal Service with a change of address form, or that he advised the Board of his new mailing address[.]"); Szafranski v. New York State College of Optometry, 68 MSPR 183, 185 n.2 (1995) (failing to excuse untimeliness due to the misdirection of Board orders: under 5 CFR 1201.26(b)(2) (2022), parties must notify the Board and each other of any change in address); Granada v. OPM, 55 MSPR 81, 82 (1992) (the appellant failed to obtain a Board deficiency notice concerning a PFR because she failed to notify the Board of her change in address); Gentry v. OPM, 40 MSPR 46, 49 n.3 (1989) (failure to notify the Board of an address change to ensure receipt of the initial decision was not excusable neglect in the late filing of a PFR); Wilson v. USPS, 35 MSPR 399, 401 (1987) ("The appellant had been informed, in an earlier order from the regional office to which he responded, that he was required to notify the Board and the agency in writing of any changes in his address."); Graham v. USPS, 32 MSPR 572, 574 (1987) ("Although appellant asserts that he was emotionally upset at the time he moved away from his address of record, he has failed to establish that his mental condition was such that he could not have called or otherwise informed the Board's regional office of his new address."); Gibilisco v. DOJ, 22 MSPR 584, 585 (1984) (appellant failed to notify the AJ of his change of address; a copy of the initial decision was sent to his attorney); cf. Wrighten v. Dept. of Army, 92 MSPR 71, 73 ¶¶ 5, 6 (2002) (appellant did not provide support for the contention that she notified the Board by fax of an address change; assuming notice was provided, several months elapsed from when the appellant learned of the initial decision to her filing of the PFR); Pierce v. Dept. of Air Force, 19 MSPR 548, 551 (1984) (good cause for delay was established by the appellant's representation that he moved and received the initial decision shortly before he filed a PFR); cf. Trauner v. Dept. of Army, 12 MSPR 512, 514 (1982) (late response in counsel fee proceedings was not excused when the attorney notified the Postal Service but not the AJ of his change of address). But see Sumrall v. Dept. of Air Force, 85 MSPR 597, 605 ¶ 14 (2000) ("[W]e find good cause to waive the short delay based on the appellant's pro se status and his change of address shortly before the initial decision was served.").

If the appellant expects to be away from his residence for an extended absence during a period that might involve receipt of a decision that could lead to a PFR, the appellant should, as a matter of due diligence, make arrangements to have his mail forwarded. Dajoyag v. OPM, 44 MSPR 217, 220 (1990); see LaGreca v. USPS, 114 MSPR 162, 166 ¶ 9, 2010 MSPB 109 (2010) (appellant's assertion concerning the distance she lives from her post office mailbox did not excuse an untimely PFR); Talas v. Dept. of Interior, 99 MSPR 325, 328 ¶ 9 (2005) (appellant's late PFR was not excused because she was out of town, and did not receive the initial decision, for a week during the Christmas season; "the appellant has a personal obligation to diligently monitor the progress of her appeal at all times"); Stewart v. Dept. of Navy, 93 MSPR 147, 149-50 ¶ 5 (2002) (appellant has an obligation to notify the Board of any change of address and to ensure the timely forwarding of correspondence); Small v. GSA, 88 MSPR 187, 192 ¶ 10 (2001) (appellants were on detail, but "[t]o the extent that they failed to make arrangements to have their mail forwarded to them, they failed to exercise due diligence"); McDonagh v. GSA, 82 MSPR 679, 682 ¶ 6 (1999) (late PFR was not excused by a delay in the appellant's receipt of the initial decision: the appellant was responsible for notifying the Board of an address change and ensuring the timely forwarding of mail; the appellant was responsible for the neglect of his agent relative to mail forwarding), aff'd, McDonagh v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 99-3345); Johnson v. Dept. of Navy, 73 MSPR 431, 433 (1997) (incarceration did not excuse the belated receipt of the initial decision; "[i]t is his responsibility to ensure the timely forwarding of his own mail, and he is held responsible for any neglect on the part of the agent in this regard"); Campa v. OPM, 65 MSPR 321, 323 (1994) (appellant is accountable for her failure to receive a procedural notice from the Clerk; her current absence from her address of record does not constitute good cause for waiver of the filing deadline); Galleno v. OPM, 63 MSPR 440, 442 (1994) ("The appellant has not shown why, if she expected to be absent for an extended period of time, she could not have made arrangements to have her mail forwarded to her."); Ellerbee v. USPS, 58 MSPR 48, 49 (1993) (late refiling of a corrected PFR: it was insufficient that the appellant was working 90 miles from home; the appellant was not prevented from timely receiving mail or making arrangements for refiling before leaving home); Smith v. OPM, 57 MSPR 663, 666 (1993) (delay not excused because the appellant was "out of town" during the period for filing a PFR); Brady v. Dept. of Labor, 57 MSPR 341, 345 (1993) (if the appellant requests an extension for filing a PFR and then leaves his residence for an extended period, he should inform the Clerk that correspondence should be directed to a different address); Hutton v. Dept. of Navy, 56 MSPR 165, 168 (1992) (corrected PFR was untimely when the appellant claimed he did not have 24-hour access to his post office box and therefore did not immediately obtain correspondence from the MSPB Clerk; the appellant was responsible for considering the schedule of his post office); Tiongco v. OPM, 51 MSPR 177 (1991) (untimely submission was not excused when the appellant was away from her residence for an extended time, did not notify the Board of a change of address, and had not arranged for the forwarding of her mail); Zideb v. OPM, 28 MSPR 409, 410 (1985) (appellant did not exercise due diligence when he took an extended trip without making arrangements for the forwarding of mail or providing the Board with an explanation of his return date); Gugliotto v. Dept. of Labor, 19 MSPR 621, 622 (1984) (a late PFR was not excused because the appellant lived in a rural area and the address changed during the pendency of appeal, "creating difficulty in his receiving his mail"). But see Buniff v. USDA, 79 MSPR 118, 121 ¶ 6 (1998) (excusing a PFR delay caused when the AJ failed to send the initial decision to the new address provided by the appellant).

If during a residential move, the employee misplaces an initial decision that he received, lengthy delay in filing a PFR will not be excused if the employee could have, with due diligence, attempted to obtain another copy of the initial decision or if he could have requested an extension of time to file a PFR. Maranan v. OPM, 57 MSPR 377, 379 (1993); see Spann v. Soldiers' & Airmen's Home, 80 MSPR 602, 605 ¶ 7 (1999) (PFR late by two weeks was not excused because the appellant's wife set aside mail from the Board without informing the appellant). [Refer to Chapter 2, "Problem With or Delayed Receipt of Domestic, Certified, International Mail, or Commercial Delivery; Address Change," for decisions involving late appeals caused by appellants' relocations and consequential delays in receipt of agency decision letters.]

Bradshaw v. VA, 113 MSPR 650, 654-56 ¶¶ 12-14, 2010 MSPB 97 (2010), changed earlier law as to reasons for accepting arguably late appeals by determining that the appellant will not be faulted for failing to keep an agency on notice of a new address prior to the time the agency issues an appealable decision (resulting in belated receipt of the decision). That changed with the Board's 2012 regulatory revisions at 5 CFR 1201.22(b)(3) requiring, in part, that "An appellant is responsible for keeping the agency informed of his or her current home address for purposes of receiving the agency's decision, and correspondence which is properly addressed and sent to the appellant's address via postal or commercial delivery is presumed to have been duly delivered to the addressee." But under the law that follows, the appellant in Board proceedings is required to inform the Board of changes in address during the pendency of those proceedings. [Refer to Bradshaw in Chapter 2, "Problem With or Delayed Receipt of Domestic, Certified, International Mail, or Commercial Delivery; Address Change."] The distinction in treatment of appellants' failures to keep agencies or the Board updated as to address changes (under prior regulations) was the subject of a remand decision from the Board's reviewing court, Gomez v. MSPB, 482 Fed. Appx. 540, 542 (Fed. Cir. 2012 NP):

Before 1997, the Board's regulations required that petitions for review be filed within 35 days of the issuance of the initial decision. In that year, the Board amended its regulations to provide that petitions for review would be deemed timely if filed within 30 days of when the petitioner received the initial decision if the initial decision was received more than five days after it was issued. See 62 Fed. Reg. 59,991 (codified at 5 C.F.R. § 1201.114(d)). In subsequent cases, the Board has interpreted that regulation to require the petitioner to notify the Board of changes of address and take other reasonable measures to ensure the receipt of mail from the Board. See Bennett v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, 97 M.S.P.R. 1, 3 (2004); McDonagh, 82 M.S.P.R. at 682.

In a letter to the Board, we asked for the Board's view on an issue that had not been discussed either in the Board's opinion or in the briefs to this court. Specifically, we noted that timeliness regulations regarding appeals to the Board from an agency's decision, which are similarly worded to 5 C.F.R. § 1201.114(d), have not been interpreted to require the employee to show that he has taken reasonable measures to ensure that he will receive notice of the decision to be appealed. See Saddler v. Dep't of the Army, 68 F.3d 1357, 1360 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (interpreting 5 C.F.R. § 1201.154(b)(1), which addresses the timeliness of appeals to the Board alleging that a personnel action was based, in whole or in part, on prohibited discrimination); Bradshaw v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, 113 M.S.P.R. 650, 655 (2010) (interpreting 5 C.F.R. § 1201.22(b), which addresses the timeliness of appeals to the Board challenging an adverse agency action).

In response to our letter, the Board requested that we vacate and remand this case to give the Board an opportunity to "consider more closely the legal issues surrounding the Board's jurisdiction, as well as the evidence that implicates the Board's jurisdiction, and issue a decision accordingly." Because the Board, in its decision under review, did not address the similarly worded regulations cited above, we agree that it is appropriate for the Board to have an opportunity to consider whether it should interpret the timeliness regulation for petitions for review in a manner consistent with the interpretation given to the regulations for review of agency action. Accordingly, we vacate the Board's order dismissing Mr. Gomez's petition for review and remand this case for further proceedings before the Board.
