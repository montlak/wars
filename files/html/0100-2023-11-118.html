3. Acquittal; Reversal of Criminal Conviction

Acquittal of criminal charges is not binding in agency proceedings seeking to remove the employee from the civil service. Collateral estoppel through acquittal is foreclosed in a subsequent administrative proceeding because of the different standards of proof. The determination that the government did not prove its criminal case beyond a reasonable doubt does not preclude an agency from attempting to prove the same set of facts in an appealed adverse action by a preponderance of evidence. Wilson v. DHS, 208 Fed. Appx. 876, 879 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP) (dismissal of criminal charges does not weaken the agency's removal case or estop the agency from taking the removal action); see Polcover v. Secretary of Treasury, 477 F.2d 1223, 1231 (D.C. Cir. 1973) ("The difference between proof to a 'preponderance' of the evidence, the burden assumed by the agency in administrative proceedings of this nature, and proof 'beyond a reasonable doubt,' the burden assumed by the government in criminal proceedings, is critical."); Finfer v. Caplin, 344 F.2d 38, 40 (2d Cir. 1965) (removal of IRS officer; on judicial review: "The acquittal of a civil service employee on an indictment charging him with commission of a criminal offense does not invalidate the action removing him from his position for the good of the service when there is substantial evidence to support the decision dismissing him from service."); Alsbury v. USPS, 392 F. Supp. 71, 75 (C.D. Cal. 1975) (aff'd, Alsbury v. USPS, 530 F.2d 852 (9th Cir. 1976)) ("Certainly, the agency, in making its own determination, cannot be constrained to blindly follow the results of a criminal trial which has a stricter standard of proof and far different consequences for the employee. This is not to say that an agency should totally ignore the results of a criminal trial of its employee, but rather that it should consider all of the evidence concerning the charges made."); Rodriguez-Ortiz v. Dept. of Army, 46 MSPR 546, 548 (1991) ("A determination by a court that the government had insufficient evidence to prove its criminal case beyond a reasonable doubt will not preclude an agency from attempting to prove the same set of facts by a preponderance of the evidence in a related administrative action."); Markland v. USPS, 23 MSPR 495, 496 (1984) ("Since the agency supported its charge of theft of mail by the required standard of preponderance of the evidence, appellant's acquittal in a criminal proceeding on the same charge does not entitle him to reinstatement since the court decision was made under the higher criminal standard than that applicable to Board proceedings."); Adams v. Dept. of Transp., 16 MSPR 158, 161 (1983) ("Collateral estoppel use of a criminal acquittal is foreclosed in a subsequent civil suit because of the differing standards of proof. Thus a determination that the government did not prove its case beyond a reasonable doubt will not preclude an agency or a private party from attempting to prove the same set of facts by a preponderance of the evidence.");

If a removal is based on misconduct rather than the fact of arrest and indictment (grounds for an indefinite suspension), court action favorable to the appellant (probation) does not control the result of the Board case. See Eilertson v. Dept. of Navy, 23 MSPR 152, 158 (1984); Zimmerman v. DHHS, 19 MSPR 454, 456 (1984) ("where a removal action is based on the employee's misconduct, rather than on his arrest and indictment, the administrative action is not affected by the court action"); Ball v. GSA, 9 MSPR 439, 440 (1982) (although the appellant argued that entry of probation before judgment after a nolo plea did not constitute a finding of guilt by a court, the AJ made an independent determination on the assault charge); McCray v. Dept. of Army, 1 MSPR 460, 461, 1 MSPB 445 (1980) (acquittal). The acquittal is not relevant to misconduct as long as misconduct, and not the existence of a criminal charge or investigation, is the basis of the action against the employee. See Pflanz v. Dept. of Transp., 21 MSPR 71, 73 (1984); see also Lopez v. Dept. of Army, 95 Fed. Appx. 1010, 1011 (Fed. Cir. 2004) ("It is not necessary for Lopez to be convicted of a criminal offense for the Army's charge of larceny to be sustained. See Serrano v. United States, 612 F.2d 525, 530 (Ct. Cl. 1979) (noting that an acquittal of charges at court martial did not preclude the agency from independently determining whether an employee acted improperly."). Even if a criminal plea is vacated, and the criminal charge dismissed with prejudice, the statements or admissions given by the defendant in support of the plea may be used to support a related misconduct charge leading to a Board appeal. See Pierce v. DOD, DC-0752-15-0355-I-1 (NP 9/6/2016) ("Further, while the charge still stood, the prosecutor refused to let the appellant enter an Alford plea, and instead required her to state under oath that she had committed the conduct upon which the charge was based. Additionally, while the court vacated her plea, the appellant never repudiated her sworn stipulation to the facts underlying the plea agreement."). As to an indefinite suspension, acquittal of criminal charges does not entitle an employee to immediate reinstatement. The agency may be able to justify termination by proving the employee's misconduct by a preponderance of the evidence. Daniels v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1984 NP No. 84-704); cf. Keppinger v. Dept. of Air Force, 21 MSPR 323, 324 (1984) (res judicata may not properly be applied to a removal action on the basis of an earlier decision sustaining an indefinite suspension since the cause of the actions differ). [Refer to Chapter 6, "Indefinite Suspension."]

The converse is that if the appellant is charged with misconduct, demonstrated only by a criminal conviction later reversed, the action fails for lack of preponderant evidence of misconduct. Robinson v. Dept. of Army, 21 MSPR 270, 274 (1984). If the removal is based on only a conviction, later reversal of the conviction can lead to reopening of the Board appeal and reversal of the removal. See Pawn v. USDA, 82 MSPR 284, 286 ¶ 5 (1999) (reversal of a criminal conviction on judicial appeal warrants reversal of an adverse action based solely on conviction); Palomo v. DOJ, 73 MSPR 306, 308 (1997) (reopening was warranted when a judicial appeal reverses the criminal conviction constituting the sole basis for an adverse action); Payne v. USPS, 69 MSPR 503, 508 (1996) (when the removal is based only on a conviction and that conviction is reversed by a court, the appellant will be awarded full back pay following his timely petition to reopen his appeal). The Robinson case makes an important point for agencies: a stronger case may result if the underlying misconduct is charged and established than if the conviction alone is relied upon. Collateral estoppel has advantages, but it also entails risks if the estoppel is jeopardized through continuing litigation. If an employee is removed on the basis of his conduct, not on the basis of an arrest and indictment, an agency loss before the Board is unaffected by subsequent court action (even a conviction). See DePappe v. USPS, 27 MSPR 565, 566 n.* (1985); Robinson, supra. In Marshall v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 88-3398), the issue was the effect of a pending appeal on a Board adjudication that was based on a guilty plea and underlying misconduct. Because the Board decision was not based on a conviction, the result of a criminal appeal on appellant's conviction or sentence did not affect the charge leading to his removal.
