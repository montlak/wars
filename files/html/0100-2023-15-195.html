b. Against Supervisor

Assault on a supervisor is within the class of offenses considered conduct that carries on its face prejudice to the service. Smith v. DLA, 4 MSPR 425, 426, 4 MSPB 477 (1980). The seriousness of striking another employee so severely as to cause injury is self-evident. When added to this serious and dangerous act is the fact that the victim is the appellant's acting supervisor, the threat to the continuing operation of the office is clear. Buggs v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 86-1605); see Ruffin v. Dept. of Army, 35 MSPR 499, 505 (1987) (removal for slapping a supervisor's hand, among other charges; conduct following the slapping was immaterial; the poor judgment of the supervisor, without provocation, was immaterial); Gooch v. USPS, 30 MSPR 90, 91 (1986) (distribution clerk was removed for slapping a supervisor in the face); Washington v. Dept. of Navy, 21 MSPR 370, 373-74 (1984) (removal was sustained for striking a supervisor; the offense was committed intentionally and maliciously and "must necessarily affect the supervisor's confidence in appellant's ability to perform"; it was a serious breach of the employer-employee relationship); Hall v. Dept. of Navy, 8 MSPR 356, 358 (1981) (removal was sustained for striking and harming a supervisor when there were no extenuating circumstances).

The unprovoked striking of a supervisor is a serious offense directly affecting an agency's obligation to maintain a safe workplace for its employees. A discharge was sustained for striking a supervisor with some papers during a confrontation over a within-grade increase denial. Nordell v. DHHS, 15 MSPR 37, 41 (1983) (sustaining removal; the Board declined to find provocation when the appellant directed her supervisor to deliver within-grade increase papers to her attorney and the supervisor instead presented them to the appellant). Striking a supervisor without provocation was sufficient to warrant removal. Deutsch v. USPS, 39 MSPR 386, 390 (1989); see Picariello v. USPS, 80 MSPR 126, 131 ¶ 10 (1998) (removal sustained; management's actions did not constitute provocation warranting physical and verbal assault). Removal was well within the bounds of reasonableness for an employee of 14 years of service who struck his supervisor. Harris v. Dept. of Treasury, 10 MSPR 618, 621 (1982). Removal was reasonable for a threat and an unprovoked minor assault against a supervisor. No harassment occurred, and there was no medical problem impairing the appellant's judgment. Muldrow v. VA, 22 MSPR 586, 588-89 (1984).

Even when a supervisor created some job tensions, removal was appropriate where an appellant, in view of other employees, started a fight that produced minor injuries to both the supervisor and the appellant, who continued to strike his supervisor "after he was down." Wills v. Dept. of Navy, 46 MSPR 70, 74 (1990) (distinguishing Wilburn v. USPS, 28 MSPR 524, 527 (1985), and Andrus v. IRS, 14 MSPR 500, 503 (1983), involving provocation by the victim). Removal was also sustained for an off-duty altercation at a restaurant. The appellant slapped the supervisor after they had both been drinking. There was no injury, but the offense was "extremely serious" because the supervisor was a highly placed employee, the incident was unprovoked, and the assault resulted because of the victim's position and not for a reason unrelated to the workplace. Andrus v. IRS, 14 MSPR 500, 502-03, 13 MSPB 150 (1983), post, was distinguished because there was an element of provocation and no residual hard feeling. Fowler v. DLA, 38 MSPR 174, 177 n.3 (1988). A 15-day suspension was warranted for an employee with a good record of 10 years of service against whom a charge of disorderly conduct was sustained for throwing a wrench in the vicinity of a supervisor after an argument concerning overtime. Yoshida v. Dept. of Navy, 14 MSPR 300, 302 (1983). Assault must be distinguished from an accident causing injury to a supervisor, although the accident might be chargeable negligence. See Rose v. Dept. of Navy, 25 MSPR 273, 274 (1984). [Refer later in this chapter to "Negligence."]
