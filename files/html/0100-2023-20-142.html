7. Decision Concerning Leave; Change in Duties; Idling; Overtime; Harassment

A covered personnel action may include a decision concerning benefits. 5 USC 2302(a)(2)(A)(ix). Covered benefits include leave. See Greenspan v. VA, 94 MSPR 247, 252 ¶ 11 (2003) (cancelation of approved leave is a personnel action under the WPA) (on later review, Greenspan v. VA, 464 F.3d 1297 (Fed. Cir. 2006)); Coons v. Dept. of Treasury, 85 MSPR 631, 641 ¶ 18 (2000) (threat to place an employee on AWOL was a covered personnel action); Lawley v. Dept. of Treasury, 84 MSPR 253, 259 ¶ 14 (1999) ("Placing an employee in a leave without pay or AWOL status are decisions concerning pay or benefits, and are therefore personnel actions under 5 USC 2302(a)(2)(A)(ix)"; the appellant should demonstrate that the regulatory requisites for taking leave were met before the Board will assess an argument that its denial resulted from reprisal); cf. Staley v. VA, ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2021 NP) (FMLA denial in context of whistleblower appeal). A refusal to permit an employee whose removal was proposed to remain in work duty status, as required by 5 CFR 752.404(b)(3), is a personnel action because it is a decision concerning pay or benefits. McCollum v. NCUA, 417 F.3d 1332, 1340 (Fed. Cir. 2005). Ordinarily not covered, unless there is an agency regulation to the contrary, is official time granted for labor relations activities under 5 USC 7131 but not statutorily granted for the preparation of MSPB pleadings. See Marren v. DOJ, 50 MSPR 369, 372-73 (1991) (without a regulatory or statutory authorization, "official time for the appellant to prepare a petition for review to the Board was not a decision concerning benefits"; an agency decision to deny the appellant annual leave was, however, "a decision concerning a benefit").

Arauz v. DOJ, 89 MSPR 529, 537-39 ¶¶ 17-20 (2001) (following remand, Arauz v. DOJ, 93 MSPR 166 (2002)), concluded that covered personnel actions include the denial of administrative leave, if administrative leave is routinely granted by the agency for personnel in circumstances comparable to those of the appellant. Arauz also decided that the denial of sick leave is within the ambit of covered personnel actions:

In Marren v. Department of Justice, 50 MSPR 369, 373 (1991), the Board found that an agency's denial of a request for annual leave to prepare a petition for review in a Board appeal was a decision concerning a benefit, and that it therefore constituted a personnel action under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(A)(ix). The Board also found, however, that the agency's denial of a request for official time did not constitute such a decision or personnel action. Id. at 372-73. In making these findings, it noted that, while annual leave was a benefit provided by law that accrued automatically, the appellant had failed to show that he was entitled to the official time he had requested. Id. at 373.

In the present case, we find that the denial of the appellant's request for administrative leave is comparable to the denial of the request in Marren for official time. That is, the appellant in this case, like the employee in Marren, was asking to be paid, without charge to annual or sick leave, for time spent on matters other than her official duties. The basis for her request was that an anonymous threat had been delivered to her home, and that this threat caused her to be fearful and suffering from emotional stress. The appellant has cited no authority showing that these circumstances entitled her to administrative leave. Moreover, administrative leave, like official time, does not accrue automatically as annual and sick leave do.

However, we find that a showing that the agency denied a request for administrative leave in circumstances in which it had a general practice of granting such leave would be sufficient to establish the denial of a benefit under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(ix). Here, the appellant was not on notice of the need to establish an agency practice of granting administrative leave in the circumstances she relied on in requesting the leave. Further, there appeared to be some confusion regarding whether the appellant was claiming a denial of sick leave, administrative leave, or both. We therefore find that the appellant should be afforded an opportunity on remand to show that there was a general practice of granting administrative leave under the circumstances here....

The appellant also alleges that the administrative judge failed to address her claim regarding the agency's denial of her sick leave request. In his initial decision, the administrative judge found that the appellant's protected disclosures were a contributing factor in the denial of this request, which the agency contended was based on the fact that she had no accrued sick leave it could grant.[6]. He also found that the agency would have taken the personnel actions at issue in the appeal in the absence of the protected disclosures. He did not, however, specifically address the appellant's contention that the agency denied her sick leave request because of her protected disclosures nor did he refer to her allegation that she had accrued sick leave at the time the agency denied her request. Moreover, documents in the record indicate that, near the beginning of the period for which the appellant had requested leave, she accepted, a settlement offer providing for the restoration of 350 hours of sick leave. Thus, the appellant's claim that the agency delayed restoring her sick leave for 6 months after the parties agreed to this settlement is inextricably intertwined with her claim that the agency denied her sick leave because of her disclosures.--On remand, therefore, the administrative judge should address the appellant's allegations regarding the agency's denial of her sick leave request and the effect that the agency's apparent delay in restoring her sick leave had on its denial.

[6] Because sick leave, like annual leave, is a benefit that is provided by law and that accrues automatically, we find that a denial of sick leave constitutes a "decision concerning...benefits" under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(A)(ix), that it therefore constitutes a "personnel action" that may be the subject of an IRA appeal, and that the Board has jurisdiction over the appellant's appeal as it concerns this matter. See 5 U.S.C. § 6307; cf. Marren, 50 M.S.P.R. at 373.

Placement of an employee on administrative leave is a personnel action because it is a significant change in duties, responsibilities, or working conditions. Brown v. Dept. of Navy, 102 MSPR 377, 385 ¶ 16, 2006 MSPB 177 (2006); see Usharauli v. DHHS, 116 MSPR 383, 390 ¶ 14, 2011 MSPB 54 (2011) ("Placement on administrative leave is a 'personnel action' under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(A) for purposes of an IRA appeal. See e.g., Hagen v. Department of Transportation, 103 M.S.P.R. 595, ¶ 13 (2006)."); Special Counsel v. IRS, 65 MSPR 146, 148 (1994) (involving an OSC stay application; again finding that because the appellant's placement on administrative leave closely followed a proposed removal, his placement on leave was a disciplinary or corrective action under 5 USC 2302(a)(2)(A)(iii), making it a personnel action with the Board's stay authority). Monitoring of an email account does not qualify as a covered personnel action for an IRA case. Woodworth v. VA, NY-1221-13-0037-W-1 (NP 5/20/2014) ("monitoring a government email account on a government-owned computer does not constitute a 'personnel action' under the WPA because there is no showing that such monitoring constitutes a significant change in duties, responsibilities, or working conditions.").

5 USC 6329b-d, "Investigative Leave and Notice Leave," adds as to placement of an employee on investigative leave--akin to administrative leave:

(g) Recourse to the Office of Special Counsel.--For purposes of subchapter II of chapter 12 and section 1221, placement on investigative leave under subsection (b) of this section for a period of not less than 70 work days shall be considered a personnel action under paragraph (8) or (9) of section 2302(b).

A covered personnel action includes a change in duties. Shivaee v. Dept. of Navy, 74 MSPR 383, 388 (1997), explained a statutory change expanding coverage of whistleblower reprisal cases involving changes of employees' duties:

...Prior to October 29, 1994, the definition of "personnel action" included "any other significant change in duties or responsibilities which is inconsistent with the employee's salary or grade level...." 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(A)(x) (1988). We find that the described change in duty site was not a change in duties or responsibilities, and therefore, does not constitute a personnel action under the version of 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(A)(x) that was in effect through October 28, 1994, assuming that that version applies.

Effective October 29, 1994, the definition above was amended to state: "[a]ny other significant change in duties, responsibilities, or working conditions." Pub. L. No. 103-424 § 5(a)(1), (2), 108 Stat. 4363 (codified as amended at 5 USCA § 2302(a)(2)(A)(xi) (1996)). The 1994 amendment's deletion of the qualifying language "which is inconsistent with the employee's salary or grade level" enlarged the category of actions encompassed in the definition of "personnel action." See Briley v. National Archives and Records Administration, 71 MSPR 211, 223 (1996). The legislative history of the amendment shows that,

[c]onsistent with the Whistleblower Protection Act's remedial purpose, the provision adding "any other significant change in duties, responsibilities, or working conditions" to listed personnel actions should be interpreted broadly. This personnel action is intended to include any harassment or discrimination that could have a chilling effect on whistleblowing or otherwise undermine the merit system, and should be determined on a case-by-case basis.

140 Cong. Rec. H11,421 (daily ed. Oct. 7, 1994) (statement of Rep. McCloskey). Assuming that the move took place after October 28, 1994, the appellant's mere assertion that he was moved to another location does not, even under a broad interpretation, show a "significant" change in working conditions. The appellant does not say whether other Contract Specialists work outside the base, or whether it is common for Contract Specialists or agency employees generally to be moved from inside the base to outside and vice versa; without more information, we have no way of knowing whether the appellant's move was "significant." Thus, we find, under the circumstances of this case, that the alleged change in duty site does not constitute a personnel action. In addition, "physical pain and emotional stress" are not, in and of themselves, personnel actions. 5 U.S.C. § 2302(a)(2)(A).

Before a 1994 amendment, a renumbered § 2302(a)(2)(A)(x) required a "significant change in duties or responsibilities which is inconsistent with the employee's salary or grade level." See Briley v. Nat'l Archives and Records Admin., 71 MSPR 211, 223 (1996), aff'd, Briley v. NARA, 236 F.3d 1373, 1379 (Fed. Cir. 2001).

Covarrubias v. SSA, 113 MSPR 583, 589 n.4, 2010 MSPB 90 (2010), provided the Board's interpretation of the 1994 legislative change to include infliction (as whistleblowing reprisal) of harassment or discriminatory working conditions:

...The legislative history of the 1994 amendment to the WPA indicates that the term "any other significant change in duties, responsibilities, or working conditions" should be interpreted broadly, to include "any harassment or discrimination that could have a chilling effect on whistleblowing or otherwise undermine the merit system." 140 Cong. Rec. H11,421 (daily ed. Oct. 7, 1994) (statement of Rep. McCloskey); see Roach v. Department of the Army, 82 M.S.P.R. 464, ¶ 24 (1999). We find that the appellant's claims of harassment and disability discrimination constitute a non-frivolous allegation that she was subjected to a personnel action within the meaning of the WPA.

Retaliatory idling of an employee may constitute a prohibited personnel practice. See Harvey v. MSPB, 802 F.2d 537 (D.C. Cir. 1986); cf. Special Counsel v. Harvey, 28 MSPR 595, 601 (1984) (deliberate idling of an SES employee violated 5 USC 3131(7)). Preventing an employee from performing any duties and eliminating all of his responsibilities is inconsistent with the employee's grade level. See Carey v. VA, 93 MSPR 676, 680 ¶ 9 (2003) (undesired placement of appellant on administrative leave was a personnel action under the WPA); Coons v. Dept. of Treasury, 85 MSPR 631, 641 ¶ 18 (2000) (placement on AWOL constituted a covered change in pay; a significant change in duties included the allegation that the appellant was assigned to a work cubicle with no meaningful duties, no telephone, no support staff, no title, no position description, no expectations or performance plan).

Denial of the opportunity to earn overtime pay because of whistleblowing disclosures is a decision concerning pay and is covered by the WPA. Also covered would be a complaint that appellant was denied work assignments that would have led to the ability to earn overtime pay. DiGiorgio v. Dept. of Navy, 84 MSPR 6, 14-15 ¶ 19 (1999); see Taylor v. Dept. of Navy, 101 MSPR 478, 481 ¶ 8, 2006 MSPB 80 (2006) (denial of overtime is a covered personnel practice in an IRA case); cf. Gonzales v. Dept. of Navy, 101 MSPR 248, 251-52 ¶ 9, 2006 MSPB 41 (2006) (loss of the opportunity for work providing a night-pay differential would be a covered personnel action).

Tello v. DHS, DA-1221-10-0137-W-1 (NP 7/18/2011), determined that "stripping the appellant of his law enforcement authority and placing him on administrative duties constitutes a significant change in duties." But a shift change was not a covered personnel action. Kochanoff v. Dept. of Treasury, 54 MSPR 517, 521 (1992). A personnel action did not embrace removal of the employee from coverage under a compressed workweek plan, a requirement that the employee report to individuals designated by his supervisor, and the delegation for approval of the employee's evaluation to someone who was not the merit pool manager. Wagner v. EPA, 51 MSPR 337, 344 (1991); see Wagner v. EPA, 53 MSPR 300, 306 (1992) ("personnel action" did not include the switch of an appellant to a new supervisor without a significant change in duties; "reassignment" was properly defined with reference to OPM's definitional regulations at 5 CFR 210.102(b)(12) and did not include minor changes in job description or function or the switch to another supervisor).

A "personnel action" did not include appellant's exclusion from an off-site staff conference and his temporary supervision by a lower-graded employee during the period of the conference. These matters did not constitute a significant change in the appellant's salary or grade level: the temporary supervision had no effect on the appellant's daily duties and responsibilities; the conference was not a regularly recurring event and it was not a training event that constituted a personnel action. The appellant's argument that the conference could have an effect on a future performance rating was deemed tenuous. Wagner v. EPA, 51 MSPR 326, 328 (1991). Administrative adjustments in an ALJ's case docket did not rise to a significant change in duties constituting a personnel action. White v. SSA, 76 MSPR 447, 462 (1997). But covered personnel actions included a false accusation of lateness coupled with a requirement to punch a time clock and denial of permission to attend job-related conferences. Sood v. VA, 88 MSPR 214, 222-23 ¶ 16 (2001). [Refer later in this chapter to "Hostile Environment."]
