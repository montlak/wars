d. Whistleblowing Prohibited Personnel Practice Complaint or Appeal

The application of collateral estoppel in appeals involving whistleblowing reprisal is complicated. An employee may assert that reprisal has infected a variety of consecutive personnel actions within the scope of an Individual Right of Action. [Refer to Chapter 13 for Individual Right of Action appeals and whistleblower defenses to adverse actions.] The law is structured so that a particular personnel action may be the subject of a whistleblowing complaint to the Office of Special Counsel and then subject to an appeal to the Merit Systems Protection Board. Once the appeal is at the MSPB, the appellant may seek to amend the whistleblower case to add another personnel action or the failure to take a personnel action, or the threat to take or not take a personnel action. Unless the new matter is separately appealable to the Board, if the matter that is the subject of the amendment has not been first raised with the Office of Special Counsel, the amendment will be rejected and the appellant is left to reinitiate the IRA process as to the new claim with OSC, subject to a later appeal on that matter to MSPB. If the initial MSPB appeal proceeds to a merits determination, and a subsequent MSPB appeal is initiated, the agency may, if it received a favorable result in the first appeal, argue that the Board's determination on reprisal collaterally estops the appellant from litigating reprisal in the second appeal. Or the first appeal may not have reached a merits determination and instead been dismissed for lack of jurisdiction, for example, because the appellant failed to make a nonfrivolous allegation of whistleblower reprisal. In the subsequent appeal on a different personnel action raised as an IRA, the agency may seek to invoke collateral estoppel as a result of the jurisdictional determination of the first appeal. Then there is the question of whether collateral estoppel applies to bar an appellant from pursuing a merits determination in an IRA case if the Office of Special Counsel pursued and did not prevail on a related corrective action case.

The Special Counsel does not bring many corrective action cases, but if it does bring a case an adverse result would not bar an appellant's separate case (assuming the appellant was not intervenor in the OSC case). Collateral estoppel will not be applied if public policy will be compromised, as would occur through the application of collateral estoppel against an appellant with the effect of compromising the purpose and policy behind the appellant's right to a hearing before the Board. The appellant is not a party to an OSC case; further, the responsibilities of OSC to the appellant are not the same as those of an employee's advocate. Under Frazier v. MSPB, 672 F.2d 150, 162 (D.C. Cir. 1982), OSC is not viewed as the employee's representative, but rather the protector of merit system principles (a status that may have changed following the 1989 Whistleblower Amendments [discussed in Chapter 13, "Prohibited Personnel Practices"] ). Application of collateral estoppel would force appellants to intervene in OSC cases, a result that might hamper OSC's prosecution abilities. See Special Counsel v. Dept. of Energy, 14 MSPR 333, 337-38 (1983) (denial of an OSC stay extension does not determine any appeal by the employee once the agency's adverse action is undertaken).

If the agency is a party to the earlier proceeding and had a full and fair opportunity to litigate its case, it is collaterally estopped from relitigating issues decided against it in the prior proceeding, Mortensen v. Dept. of Army, 27 MSPR 433, 435-37 (1985), with the result that if the agency lost the issue of reprisal when OSC brought a case, the agency would be collaterally estopped from relitigating the underlying reprisal issue during a separate appeal brought by the appellant:

This case originated when the agency proposed appellant's removal based upon charges of insubordination, unsatisfactory performance, and misuse of employee protective procedures resulting in a disruption of general services. The proposed action, however, was stayed pending the Office of Special Counsel's pursuit of appellant's allegations of reprisal.

In Special Counsel v. Department of the Army, 16 M.S.P.R. 178 (1983), the Board found that although retaliation played a significant role in appellant's proposed removal, the agency demonstrated legitimate managerial reasons for the action. Since dual motivation was shown, the Board applied the rule established in Mt. Healthy City School District v. Doyle, 429 U.S. 274 (1977), to determine whether the agency would have taken the action anyway. In making this determination, the Board found that the agency proved several of the specifications of insubordination and unsatisfactory performance, and concluded that the proposed removal was not the product of a prohibited personnel practice. The agency then effected appellant's removal based upon the charges and specifications sustained in the Special Counsel proceeding. Appellant appealed the action to the Board's Denver Regional Office.

The presiding official found that the doctrine of collateral estoppel precluded relitigation of the charges sustained in the previous Special Counsel proceeding and appellant's affirmative defense of reprisal. The presiding official concluded, based upon additional evidence submitted by the parties, that appellant's removal promoted the efficiency of the service and was within the bounds of reasonableness. The presiding official, therefore, sustained the agency action.

In her petition for review, appellant contends that the doctrine of collateral estoppel does not apply in this case because the legal analysis employed in the prior proceeding has since been overruled. In this regard, appellant contends that the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit rejected the Mt. Healthy dual motivation approach in the case of Sullivan v. Department of the Navy, 720 F.2d 1266 (Fed. Cir. 1983).

Under the doctrine of collateral estoppel, the judgment on the merits in one suit precludes the relitigation of the same issues in a second suit, regardless of whether the first and second suits were based on the same cause of action. As the Board recognized in Chisholm v. Defense Logistics Agency, 3 MSPB 273, 3 M.S.P.R. 171 (1980), aff'd 656 F.2d 42 (3d Cir. 1981), it may apply collateral estoppel to any issue previously adjudicated assuming the following prerequisites for the doctrine exist: the issue in the second suit must be identical to that invoked in the prior action; the issue must have been actually litigated in the prior action; and the determination in the prior action must have been necessary to the resulting judgment. See Chisholm, supra.

In the present case, the charges at issue and appellant's allegation of reprisal are identical to the issues presented in the previous Special Counsel proceeding and were actually litigated in that action. Further, the determinations on those issues in that proceeding were necessary to the resulting judgment. The prerequisites for applying collateral estoppel are therefore satisfied.

Even if the prerequisites for collateral estoppel exist, however, the Board may refrain from applying the doctrine if injustice would result or considerations of public policy would be compromised. See Payer v. Department of the Army, 19 M.S.P.R. 534, 538 (1984). Applying collateral estoppel against appellant in the present removal proceeding due to the previous Special Counsel adjudication would compromise the purpose and policy behind an appellant's right to a hearing before the Board. Congress provided this right in order to provide employees with the opportunity to rebut agency charges personally and to guarantee a full and fair consideration of their case. See Callahan v. Department of the Navy, 748 F.2d 1556 (Fed. Cir. 1984). Further, the Board has held that an appellant's right to a hearing constitutes more than a mere opportunity to testify but includes the opportunity to observe proceedings and provide guidance to her representative. See Starkey v. Department of the Air Force, 3 MSPB 377, 3 M.S.P.R. 289 (1980).

These opportunities, however, were not provided to appellant in the previous Special Counsel proceeding. Appellant was not a party to the proceeding and the Special Counsel duties are not equivalent to those of an employee's advocate since there is a significant distinction between the purpose of a Special Counsel corrective action and a Chapter 77 appeal. Frazier v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 672 F.2d 150, 162 (D.C. Cir. 1982). Appellant, therefore, was not afforded the opportunity to personally rebut the charges or provide guidance to her representative. Further, affording collateral estoppel effect to Special Counsel corrective actions would force appellants to intervene in those actions in order to assure that their interests are satisfactorily represented. Such intervention, however, may hamper the Special Counsel's pursuit of the case. Under these circumstances, we will not apply collateral estoppel to bar appellant's statutorily provided right to a hearing on the issues in question.

The agency, however, was a party to the prior proceeding and had a full and fair opportunity to litigate its case. It is therefore collaterally estopped from relitigating issues which were decided against it in the prior proceeding. See Montana v. United States, 440 U.S. 147 (1979); Parklane Hosiery Co. v. Shore, 439 U.S. 322 (1979). In so finding, we recognize that applying the doctrine against the agency promotes the policies of judicial economy and avoidance of inconsistent findings, and gives conclusive effect to our prior determinations.

See Hamel v. DHS, DE-1221-16-0046-W-1 (NP 1/31/2023) (IRA case: when a final Board decision in a prior removal appeal found appellant's disclosures protected, collateral estoppel precluded relitigation of their protected status in a subsequent, related IRA appeal).

If the appellant presented allegations of reprisal under the Whistleblower Protection Act in an earlier appeal and lost on whether those allegations were protected, a reprisal claim stemming from nonselection by a subsequent employer based on disclosure to that employer of information from the first employer is barred by collateral estoppel if the second appeal would relitigate IRA coverage of the employee's disclosures. See Daniels v. MSPB, 306 Fed. Appx. 567 (Fed. Cir. 2009 NP) (results in an earlier appeal involving the alleged disclosures compelled the same result in the current appeal, involving the same disclosures).

If the appellant appealed earlier, and lost, an adverse action appeal, a later IRA appeal is barred by res judicata because the appellant could have raised the whistleblower reprisal allegations as an affirmative defense in the earlier appeal, Rose v. DOD, 705 Fed. Appx. 983, 985-86 (Fed. Cir. 2017 NP):

The doctrine of res judicata "prevents parties from litigating issues that could have been raised in a prior action" and arise from the same operative facts. Carson v. Dep't of Energy, 398 F.3d 1369, 1375 (Fed. Cir. 2005); see Renville v. Dep't of Health & Human Servs., 632 Fed. Appx. 611, 613 (Fed. Cir. 2015) ("Res judicata serves to limit...claims that the party could have raised in an earlier action arising from the same transaction or occurrence."). The doctrine applies when: "(1) the prior decision was rendered by a forum with competent jurisdiction; (2) the prior decision was a final decision on the merits; and (3) the same cause of action and the same parties or their privies were involved in both cases." Carson, 398 F.3d at 1375 (citation omitted). "In order that a final judgment shall be given res judicata effect in a pending action, it is not required that the judgment shall have been rendered before that action was commenced." Restatement (Second) of Judgments § 14 cmt. a (Am. Law. Inst. 1982); see, e.g., Ellis v. Amex Life Ins. Co., 211 F.3d 935, 937-38 (5th Cir. 2000) (applying Restatement (Second) of Judgments § 14 and affirming a dismissal based on res judicata). The "same cause of action" clause of the third Carson factor is met when the same event gave rise to both suits, even if a different legal theory is pled in each appeal. See, e.g., N.J. Inst. of Tech. v. Medjet, Inc., 47 Fed. Appx. 921, 923-25 (Fed. Cir. 2002). Whether the MSPB properly dismissed an action based on res judicata raises a question of law that we review de novo. See Renville, 632 Fed. Appx. at 613.

II. The Doctrine of Res Judicata Bars Mr. Rose's Appeal

Mr. Rose contends that the MSPB did not properly apply the doctrine of res judicata. Specifically, he argues that neither the AJ nor the MSPB reached the merits of the whistleblower claim, deciding instead that they did not have jurisdiction over that claim.

The MSPB appropriately determined that res judicata barred Mr. Rose's whistleblower claim based on its decision in Rose II. As to the first Carson factor, the MSPB has jurisdiction over Mr. Rose's original appeals challenging "a suspension of more than [fourteen] days" or "a removal." 5 U.S.C. § 7512; see id. § 7513(d). As to the second Carson factor, the MSPB issued a final decision on the merits regarding Mr. Rose's prior appeals before giving preclusive effect to those decisions in the instant IRA appeal. See Rose II, 2015 WL 4736787, at ¶ 1.

As to the third Carson factor, even though Mr. Rose attempts to distinguish his current whistleblowing claim, this appeal involves the same parties and the same operative facts as Rose II. In Rose II, Mr. Rose challenged the DOD's actions that led to his suspension and removal. See id. ¶¶ 1-2. The same operative facts were discussed in his prior appeal. See id. ¶ 2 (reviewing Mr. Rose's supervisor's false statements and Mr. Rose's subsequent barment). As such, Mr. Rose could have raised a whistleblower claim based on his suspension or removal in Rose II. See 5 C.F.R. § 1209.2(b) (2017) (providing jurisdiction to the MSPB over certain appeals relating to whistleblowing and other protected activities). Although Mr. Rose attempted to raise a whistleblower claim before the MSPB in Rose II, the MSPB refused to consider it "because he did not identify such a claim for adjudication below or in his prior petition for review." Rose II, 2015 WL 4736787, at ¶ 13. Mr. Rose's failure to properly raise a whistleblower claim as a defense in Rose II prevents him from raising it now to re-challenge his removal. Cf. Sabersky v. Dep't of Justice, 61 Fed. Appx. 676, 677 (Fed. Cir. 2003). Thus, the doctrine of res judicata bars Mr. Rose's appeal.

If the IRA case is dismissed for lack of jurisdiction, then it is important to keep in mind the distinction between res judicata and collateral estoppel, explained in Johnson v. Dept. of Air Force, 92 MSPR 370, 375-76 ¶¶ 12-14 (2002) (a USERRA case):

Finally, the appellant attempts to renew his USERRA claim that he initially raised in his appeal below. We first note that there is no statutory or regulatory time limit for filing a USERRA appeal. See 5 C.F.R. § 1208.12; Tierney v. Department of Justice, 89 M.S.P.R. 354, ¶ 6 (2001). We also find that the appellant's renewed USERRA claim is not barred by the doctrines of res judicata or collateral estoppel. "under the doctrine of res judicata, a valid, final judgment on the merits of an action bars a second action involving the same parties or their privies based on the same cause of action." Peartree v. U.S. Postal Service, 66 M.S.P.R. 332, 337 (1995). Res judicata precludes parties from relitigating issues that were, or could have been, raised in the prior action, and is applicable if: (1) the prior judgment was rendered by a forum with competent jurisdiction; (2) the prior judgment was a final judgment on the merits; and (3) the same cause of action and the same parties or their privies were involved in both cases. Id. The doctrine of res judicata, however, does not apply to bar a prior claim that has been dismissed for lack of jurisdiction. Peartree, 66 M.S.P.R. at 338. Thus, res judicata does not bar the USERRA claim here because the administrative judge dismissed the appeal below for lack of jurisdiction. Further, the initial decision did not include a final judgement on the merits of the appellant's USERRA claim, as required for applying res judicata.

The doctrine of collateral estoppel, however, may preclude a second action in the same forum that dismissed a prior appeal for lack of jurisdiction. Peartree, 66 M.S.P.R. at 338. Collateral estoppel is appropriate when the following conditions are met: (1) an issue is identical to that involved in the prior action; (2) the issue was actually litigated in the prior action; (3) the determination on the issue in the prior action was necessary to the resulting judgment; and (4) the party precluded was fully represented in the prior action. Kroeger v. U.S. Postal Service, 865 F.2d 235, 239 (Fed. Cir. 1988); Peartree, 66 M.S.P.R. at 341. The "actually litigated" element is satisfied when the issue was "properly raised by the pleadings, was submitted for determination, and was determined." Banner v. United States, 238 F.3d 1348, 1354 (Fed. Cir. 2001); see Luna v. Department of the Air Force, 87 M.S.P.R. 232, ¶ 8 (2000).

In the present case, we find that the appellant's USERRA claim was not "actually litigated" in the prior action, and that the administrative judge's mention of the USERRA claim was not necessary to the resulting judgment, as required for applying collateral estoppel. While the appellant first raised the USERRA issue in his petition for appeal, he did not submit any further evidence or argument on it for determination. Further, it does not appear that the administrative judge determined the issue because she only discussed the procedural history of the issue and then dismissed the resignation and retirement claims for lack of jurisdiction. Thus, under these circumstances, collateral estoppel does not preclude the USERRA claim. See Luna, 87 M.S.P.R. 232, ¶¶ 7-9; Serrao v. Department of Commerce, 69 M.S.P.R. 475, 477-78 (1996).

The doctrine of res judicata is inapplicable here because the appellant's prior Board appeals did not involve the personnel action appealed here--his nonselection for promotion in April 1992. That is, the prior Board appeals did not involve the same cause of action. See Roque v. Office of Personnel Management, 55 MSPR 50, 52-53 (1992); Lowy v. Department of the Army, 50 MSPR 671, 674-75 (1991). In dismissing this appeal as res judicata, the administrative judge relied on Byrd v. Department of the Air Force, 56 MSPR 629, 632 (1993). See id. at 630-31.

We find, instead, that the doctrine of collateral estoppel is applicable here.[1] The Board's authority to employ the doctrine of collateral estoppel, also called issue preclusion, is well-recognized. See, e.g., Thomas v. General Services Administration, 794 F.2d 661, 664 (Fed. Cir. 1986). The doctrine precludes relitigation of the same issues that were adjudicated in a prior case, regardless of whether the prior case involved the same cause of action. See, e.g., Raymond v. Department of the Army, 34 MSPR 476, 480 (1987). The Board may apply this doctrine if: (1) the issue previously adjudicated is identical to that now presented; (2) that issue was actually litigated in the prior case; (3) the previous determination of that issue was necessary to the resulting judgment; and (4) the party precluded by the doctrine was fully represented in the prior case. See Kroeger v. U.S. Postal Service, 865 F.2d 235, 239 (Fed. Cir. 1988); Thomas, 794 F.2d at 664; Mycka v. Office of Personnel Management, 56 MSPR 675, 678-79 (1993). We find that each of these four requirements exists in the present appeal, as discussed below.

[1] The doctrine of res judicata precludes relitigation of all issues that were or could have been raised in the prior proceeding, while the doctrine of collateral estoppel only precludes relitigation of issues that were actually litigated and decided in the prior proceeding. See Chisholm v. Defense Logistics Agency, 656 F.2d 42, 47-48 (3rd Cir. 1981).

First, the issue previously adjudicated is identical to the issue involved here. The ten alleged disclosures the appellant raised in this appeal were previously raised in his removal appeal. The appellant argues on petition for review that the administrative judge erred by failing to address in her initial decision his eleventh disclosure, "that management had made statements evidencing discrimination on the basis of sex and age against a coworker." The record indicates that the appellant did mention, both in this appeal and in his prior removal appeal, his participation in the coworker's pursuit of an equal employment opportunity complaint based on sex and age. However, the appellant did not include this matter in his list of ten alleged disclosures, and confirmed below that in "the instant appeal...[he was] relying only on the ten disclosures" specified in his petition for appeal. We therefore find that the administrative judge did not err in limiting her consideration to the ten alleged disclosures specifically raised by the appellant. Thus, the issue in the present appeal of whether the ten alleged disclosures constitute protected whistleblowing activity under 5 USC § 2302(b)(8) is identical to that adjudicated in the appellant's removal appeal. See Fisher, 59 MSPR at 173-74.

Second, this issue was actually litigated in the prior appeal. The "actual litigation" criterion requires that the issue must have been contested by the parties and resolved by the adjudicator. See Mother's Restaurant, Inc. v. Mama's Pizza, Inc., 723 F.2d 1566, 1570 (Fed. Cir. 1983); Raymond, 34 MSPR at 480. Our review of the removal appeal file reveals that the parties contested this issue and that the Board resolved it by concluding that none of the alleged disclosures constituted protected whistleblowing activity under 5 USC § 2302(b)(8). See Fisher, 59 MSPR at 173-74.

Third, the previous determination of this issue was necessary to the resulting judgment. The appellant argues on petition for review that this issue was merely part of his affirmative defense in his removal appeal and was therefore irrelevant to the ultimate disposition of that appeal. This argument is without merit. Had the appellant prevailed on his affirmative defense of whistleblower reprisal in the removal appeal, his removal would not have been sustained. Moreover, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit has stated that the purpose of this third requirement is "to prevent the incidental or collateral determination of a nonessential issue from precluding reconsideration of that issue in later litigation" and "does not mean that the finding must be so crucial that, without it, the judgment could not stand." Mother's Restaurant, Inc., 723 F.2d at 1571.

Finally, the appellant was "fully represented" in his removal appeal. This fourth requirement looks to whether the party to be issue-precluded has had a "full and fair chance to litigate" the issue in question. Mother's Restaurant, Inc., 723 F.2d at 1569-70 n.4. This requirement addresses situations where, e.g., the party to be issue-precluded was not a party in the prior case or there were more than two opposing parties in the prior case, so that it is not immediately apparent whether the party's interests with respect to the issue to be precluded were fully represented in that prior case. See Mother's Restaurant Inc., 723 F.2d at 1572; Mortensen v. Department of the Army, 27 MSPR 433, 437 (1985). Thus, contrary to the appellant's contention below, this requirement does not pertain to whether he was represented by an attorney in the removal appeal, and the fact that he was pro se there does not prevent our application of the doctrine of collateral estoppel to the issues adjudicated in that appeal.

Even if all of the requirements for the application of the collateral estoppel doctrine are met, the Board may refrain from applying the doctrine if injustice would result or other public policy considerations would be compromised. See Williams v. U.S. Postal Service, 35 MSPR 581, 585 (1987). Under the circumstances of this appeal, we find no basis to refrain from applying the doctrine to prevent the appellant's relitigation of the previously adjudicated issue of whether the ten disclosures raised in this appeal constitute protected whistleblowing activity under 5 USC § 2302(b)(8). Because this issue forms part of the jurisdictional requisites for an IRA appeal, see Geyer v. Department of Justice, 63 MSPR 13, 16 (1994), and because this issue has been previously adjudicated against the appellant in the removal appeal, we find that he has failed to establish the Board's jurisdiction over this IRA appeal. See Horton v. Department of Transportation, 61 MSPR 421, 422-23 (1994).

Again applying collateral estoppel in the jurisdictional context of an IRA appeal, Luecht v. Dept. of Navy, 87 MSPR 297, 305-06 ¶ 16 (2000), determined:

The appellant...asserts that his demotion in 1998 to the GS-6 Contact Representative position was the result of retaliation for whistleblowing. He previously filed an appeal with the Board alleging that he involuntarily accepted the demotion, and raised a whistleblowing claim in that appeal. The administrative judge dismissed the appeal for lack of jurisdiction, finding that the appellant voluntarily accepted the GS-6 position.... The Board denied the appellant's petition for review for failure to meet the review criteria, and the initial decision became the Board's final decision. Luecht v. Department of the Navy, 86 MSPR 41 (2000) (Table). Thus, the appellant is collaterally estopped from asserting in this action that the demotion is a personnel action under 5 USC § 2302(a)(2)(A)(111), an action under chapter 75 of Title V or other disciplinary or corrective action. See Kroeger v. U.S. Postal Service, 865 F.2d 235, 239 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (collateral estoppel, or issue preclusion, is appropriate when (1) an issue is identical to that involved in the prior action, (2) the issue was actually litigated in the prior action, (3) the determination on the issue in the prior action was necessary to the resulting judgment, and (4) the party precluded was fully represented in the prior action).

Schneider v. DHS, 98 MSPR 377, 386 n.4 (2005), resolved, without much decisional clarity, that collateral estoppel did not preclude agency litigation involving a 2003 suspension of a prior whistleblowing reprisal determination because the finding of a protected disclosure was a "matter of mixed fact and law and stipulations relating to such matters are not binding":

In 1999, the appellant filed an IRA/adverse action appeal of the agency's actions suspending her for 30 days and placing her in absence without leave (AWOL) status, which appeal was docketed as Schneider v. Department of Justice, AT-1221-00-0263-W-2. The administrative judge found that the appellant established that the agency took these actions in retaliation for the appellant's whistleblowing activity (disclosing, e.g., that agency officials accepted bribes from aliens to grant immigration benefits and were derelict in their duty to prevent marriage fraud), to which the parties had stipulated. He ordered corrective action. The Board denied the agency's petition for review of the initial decision, which thus became the final decision in the case. Schneider v. Department of Justice, 92 M.S.P.R. 228 (2002) (Table). Despite the finding in Schneider v. Department of Justice, AT-1221-00-0263-W-2, that the disclosures, iterated to form the basis of the appellant's affirmative defense of whistleblowing retaliation in this appeal, were protected, we decline to apply the principle of collateral estoppel to find that these disclosures were protected here. The question of whether a disclosure is protected under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8) is a matter of mixed fact and law and stipulations relating to such matters are not binding. See Wojcicki v. Department of the Air Force, 72 M.S.P.R. 628, 634 (1996).

See Jensen v. USDA, 104 MSPR 379, 384 ¶ 7, 2007 MSPB 3 (2007) ("Further, regardless of any concessions by the agency, '[T]he question of whether a disclosure is protected under 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8) is a matter of mixed fact and law and stipulations relating to such matters are not binding.' Schneider v. Department of Homeland Security, 98 M.S.P.R. 377, ¶ 17 n.4 (2005) (citing Wojcicki v. Department of the Air Force, 72 M.S.P.R. 628, 634 (1996))").

Wojcicki v. Dept. of Air Force, 72 MSPR 628, 634, 640 (1996), relied on in Schneider and Jenson, involved an IRA appeal that followed a grievance heard by an arbitrator. Both cases appeared to involve assertions of protected disclosures. The arbitrator apparently did not find whistleblower reprisal, although the Board did. There is no discussion in Wojcicki of issue preclusion through res judicata or collateral estoppel, other than the statement at 637 n.4: "The appellant's election to grieve his nonselection, allegedly in reprisal for whistleblowing activities, under the collective bargaining agreement did not divest the Board of jurisdiction under the Whistleblower Protection Act. See Augustine v. Department of Justice, 50 M.S.P.R. 648, 653 (1991)."

Boechler v. Dept. of Interior, 109 MSPR 619, 628 ¶ 17, 2008 MSPB 188 (2008) (overruled, Hau v. DHS, 123 MSPR 620, 625-26 ¶¶ 13-15, 2016 MSPB 33 (2016) (applying collateral estoppel to preclude relitigation of an assertion in a forced retirement appeal of a hostile environment claim earlier raised and not credited in a USERRA case), post), provided analysis avoiding application of collateral estoppel when the issue in the earlier appeal was whether the appellant proved, as a merits determination, that a protected disclosure caused a challenged personnel action and the issue in the later appeal is whether the appellant made a nonfrivolous allegation of a protected disclosure:

Collateral estoppel, or issue preclusion, is appropriate when: (1) The issue is identical to that involved in the prior action; (2) the issue was actually litigated in the prior action; (3) the determination on the issue in the prior action was necessary to the resulting judgment; and (4) the party against whom issue preclusion is sought had a full and fair opportunity to litigate the issue in the prior action, either as a party to the earlier action or as one whose interests were otherwise fully represented in that action. McNeil v. Department of Defense, 100 M.S.P.R. 146, ¶ 15 (2005). Here, the application of collateral estoppel fails at the first prong of this test. Although the appellant has raised the identical alleged protected disclosure to Senator McCain's staff in both appeals, the issue in the earlier action was whether, after a hearing, the appellant proved by preponderant evidence that the disclosure was protected. The issue in this appeal is whether, on the written record, the appellant has made a nonfrivolous allegation that his disclosure was protected. One may make a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction and ultimately be unable to prove the allegation. In an IRA appeal, however, a nonfrivolous allegation that a disclosure is protected is sufficient to satisfy the threshold issue of jurisdiction, provided that the other jurisdictional criteria are met. In order to establish jurisdiction over an IRA appeal, an appellant is not required to prove that he made protected disclosures, merely to make a nonfrivolous allegation that the disclosures were protected. See Grubb, 96 M.S.P.R. 377, ¶ 11; Rusin, 92 M.S.P.R. 298, ¶ 10-11. The administrative judge's finding in the earlier appeal that the appellant did not prove that his disclosure was protected does not preclude a finding in this appeal that he made a nonfrivolous allegation that his disclosure was protected.

The reasoning in Boechler was applied in Wadhwa v. VA, 111 MSPR 26, 30-31 ¶ 6, 2009 MSPB 34 (2009) (overruled, Hau v. DHS, 123 MSPR 620, 625-26 ¶¶ 13-15, 2016 MSPB 33 (2016) (collateral estoppel precluded relitigation of an assertion in a forced retirement appeal of a hostile environment claim earlier raised and not credited in a USERRA case)).

Parikh v. VA, 110 MSPR 295, 2008 MSPB 248, 301 ¶¶ 15-17 (2008) (following remand, Parikh v. VA, 116 MSPR 197, 2011 MSPB 1 (2011)) (overruled, Hau v. DHS, 123 MSPR 620, 625-26 ¶¶ 13-15, 2016 MSPB 33 (2016) (collateral estoppel precluded relitigation of an assertion in a forced retirement appeal of a hostile environment claim earlier raised and not credited in a USERRA case)), involved the IRA appeal of a removal alleged to be in reprisal for whistleblowing. One issue was whether the result in an earlier appeal, presenting a corrective action request denied in an initial decision on the merits and involving a particular disclosure characterized as gross mismanagement, precluded raising the same disclosure, characterized as a violation of law, rule, or regulation, in the subsequent removal appeal that was dismissed for lack of IRA jurisdiction. The Board concluded, again distinguishing a merits determination from the existence of a nonfrivolous allegation in a subsequent case, that the issue could be raised and that it was not barred in a jurisdictional determination through collateral estoppel:

On review, the appellant does not dispute that he raised this disclosure in his prior appeal, and that the administrative judge there found that it was not protected. He argues, however, that the prior inquiry was limited to whether the disclosure evidenced gross mismanagement or gross waste of funds, while he argues here that the disclosure also evidences fraud and a violation of law, rule, or regulation.

Collateral estoppel, or issue preclusion, is appropriate when: (1) An issue is identical to that involved in the prior action; (2) the issue was actually litigated in the prior action; (3) the determination on the issue in the prior action was necessary to the resulting judgment; and (4) the party against whom issue preclusion is sought had a full and fair opportunity to litigate the issue in the prior action, either as a party to the earlier action or as one whose interests were otherwise fully represented in that action. McNeil v. Department of Defense, 100 M.S.P.R. 146, ¶ 15 (2005).

We find that these requirements are not satisfied here. Although the appellant has raised the identical alleged protected disclosure in both appeals, the issue in the earlier action was whether, after a hearing, the appellant proved by preponderant evidence that the disclosure was protected. The issue in this appeal, however, is whether, on the written record, the appellant has made a nonfrivolous allegation that his disclosure was protected. An appellant may make a nonfrivolous allegation of jurisdiction and ultimately be unable to prove that allegation. In an IRA appeal, however, a nonfrivolous allegation that a disclosure is protected is sufficient to satisfy the threshold issue of jurisdiction, provided that the other jurisdictional criteria are met, regardless of whether the appellant can ultimately prove that the disclosure is protected. See Boechler v. Department of the Interior, 109 M.S.P.R. 619, ¶ 17 (2008). Thus, the administrative judge's finding in the earlier appeal, that the appellant did not prove on the merits that his disclosure was protected, does not preclude a finding here that the appellant made a nonfrivolous allegation that his disclosure was protected for purposes of jurisdiction. Id.

Boechler, Wadhwa, and Parikh were overruled by Hau v. DHS, 123 MSPR 620, 625-26 ¶¶ 13-15, 2016 MSPB 33 (2016) (aff'd, sub nom. Bryant v. MSPB, 878 F.3d 1320 (Fed. Cir. 2017), post):

The administrative judge in the appellant's prior USERRA appeal found jurisdiction under the liberal jurisdictional standard for that statutory scheme, but denied the appellant's request for corrective action, finding in pertinent part that the appellant failed to prove that he was subjected to a hostile work environment. The issue of whether the agency subjected the appellant to a hostile work environment based on his military service was actually litigated in his prior USERRA appeal, and the administrative judge's findings on that issue were necessary to the resulting judgment. The appellant thus is barred by collateral estoppel from arguing that the agency subjected him to a hostile work environment based on his military service, which is the sole basis of his constructive discharge claim in this matter.

Because jurisdiction was found in the appellant's initial USERRA appeal, this case is distinguishable from Noble. Nevertheless, we conclude that, in light of our finding that collateral estoppel bars relitigation of the hostile work environment claim, and the fact that the appellant has not alleged any acts of harassment that differ from those he relied on in his prior appeal, the appellant cannot in this matter articulate a nonfrivolous allegation that the agency denied him retention in employment based on his military service. See Mintzmeyer v. Department of the Interior, 84 F.3d 419, 424 (Fed. Cir. 1996). Accordingly, dismissal for lack of jurisdiction in this matter is appropriate.

We recognize that the Board has held previously that, although an appellant raised identical issues in two separate appeals, an earlier decision that the appellant did not prevail on the merits did not preclude a finding that he made a nonfrivolous allegation establishing jurisdiction in a subsequent appeal. Boechler v. Department of the Interior, 109 M.S.P.R. 619, ¶ 17 (2008), aff'd without opinion, 328 Fed. Appx. 660 (Fed. Cir. 2009); see Wadhwa v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 111 M.S.P.R. 26, ¶ 6 (citing Boechler), aff'd without opinion, 353 Fed. Appx. 434 (Fed. Cir. 2009); Parikh v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 110 M.S.P.R. 295, ¶¶ 13-17 (2008) (same). Conversely, "as [the U.S. Supreme] Court and other courts have often recognized, res judicata and collateral estoppel relieve parties of the cost and vexation of multiple lawsuits, conserve judicial resources, and, by preventing inconsistent decisions, encourage reliance on adjudication." Allen, 449 U.S. at 94. To find jurisdiction and allow this matter to proceed to the merits phase of the case when the appellant is barred by collateral estoppel from presenting any of his merits arguments during such proceedings would defy logic.[*] Furthermore, to do so would cause unnecessary costs to the parties, make the Board complicit in the vexation of multiple lawsuits, waste the Board's resources, and potentially could lead to inconsistent decisions--all evils the U.S. Supreme Court, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, and the Board have declared should be avoided. See, e.g., id.; MGA, Inc. v. General Motors Corporation, 827 F.2d 729, 732 (Fed. Cir. 1987); Kavaliauskas, 120 M.S.P.R. 509, ¶ 5. Accordingly, we hereby overrule our decisions in Boechler, Wadhwa, and Parikh.

[*] Pursuant to Kirkendall v. Department of the Army, 479 F.3d 830, 845 (Fed. Cir. 2007) (en banc), once an appellant establishes jurisdiction over his USERRA appeal, he is entitled to a hearing. Downs v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 110 M.S.P.R. 139, ¶¶ 17-18 (2008). The appellant already had received a hearing when his hostile work environment claim was heard in connection with his case designated as MSPB Docket No. SF-4324-13-0300-I-1. However, were a merits hearing held in this matter, the appellant would be precluded from presenting evidence and argument concerning his allegation of a hostile work environment, which is the entire basis of his claim in this case. In other words, an administrative judge would have to convene a hearing during which the appellant could not present any content, which would be a waste of time and resources.

Hau was affirmed in the consolidated appeals (including that of Mr. Hau), Bryant v. MSPB, 878 F.3d 1320, 1327-29 (Fed. Cir. 2017), declining to avoid the Board's revision in the application of collateral estoppel on the basis of a "change of law" exception:

...Petitioners primarily rely on Dow Chemical Co. v. Nova Chemicals Corp. (Canada), 803 F.3d 620, 629 (Fed. Cir. 2015), for the proposition that the change-of-law exception to collateral estoppel should apply to their current appeals. In Dow, we noted that the change-of-law exception to collateral estoppel is applicable when (1) the governing law is changed; (2) the prior decision applied the old law; and (3) the change in law compels a different result in the current case. 803 F.3d at 629-30.

Dow does not support the outcome sought by Petitioners. As noted by the Board and the DHS, the substantive USERRA law did not change. Even if we look to the change in the Board's precedent on its jurisdiction, the Board applied the new law to their current USERRA appeals, and Petitioners are seeking to apply the old law instead. Moreover, the change in law did not make a difference to Petitioners because under the Board's precedent either before or after the change, Petitioners would have been ultimately precluded from relitigating the identical issue.

This case does not present a situation in which pursuing a certain course of action was unavailable or otherwise futile under the then-controlling precedent. See Blonder-Tongue Labs., Inc. v. Univ. of Ill. Found., 402 U.S. 313, 350 (1971). Although the Board overruled its own precedent and changed its law on jurisdiction in Hau II, which may have been unexpected, that change does not justify Petitioners' abandoning their claims in Bryant I. Seeking review of the first AJ's decision was available under the then-existing Board's precedent. Petitioners did not abandon review of their USERRA appeals in Bryant I out of any futility of such pursuit under the old law, but out of their apparent belief that their best course of action was to file separate USERRA appeals including the constructive discharge claims. Simply put, the change-of-law exception does not save Petitioners from their deliberate choice of action.

Similarly, Petitioners' appeal to general equity considerations also fails. Although we have noted that the USERRA statutes are "interpreted broadly in favor of individuals returning from military service," Crowford v. Dep't of Army, 718 F.3d 1361, 1367 (Fed. Cir. 2013), such interpretive canon favoring military members is not relevant here. First, determining the Board's jurisdiction in the instant case does not involve any statutory interpretation. What Petitioners are actually arguing is that they should be given a chance to bring their second USERRA appeals to the full Board, in view of the apparent leniency afforded to military members under the overall USERRA scheme, in further view of the first AJ's footnote in Bryant I and the change of the Board's precedent regarding the effect of collateral estoppel on its jurisdiction. However, as conceded during oral argument, Petitioners do not contend that the Board's decision in Hau II overruling its precedent to address preclusion at the jurisdictional stage was incorrect. Rather, Petitioners are urging us to find an exception to the Board's admittedly correct law on jurisdiction and collateral estoppel due to the peculiar circumstances of their appeals. We decline to do so.

In general, courts do not liberally invoke exceptions to collateral estoppel based on the particular circumstances of a case. See 18 Charles Alan Wright & Arthur R. Miller, Fed. Prac. & Proc. § 4426 (3d ed. 2017). Furthermore, the Board's precedential decision concerning its jurisdiction in Hau II, albeit a departure from its own precedent, does not constitute the kind of unforeseeable event or compel a finding of lack of "incentive" to litigate that courts have found may warrant applying an exception to collateral estoppel. Id. §§ 4423, 4424. If anything, Petitioners' filing of the second, identical USERRA appeals, with a bare recitation of "constructive discharge" entirely predicated on the previously heard and decided hostile work environment issue, demonstrates that they acted deliberately and were not unsuspecting parties unjustly precluded from being heard. In view of the admittedly identical nature of Petitioners' successive appeals and their voluntary abandonment of their first appeals, we do not find that an exception should apply here.

We conclude that Petitioners' second USERRA appeals are barred by collateral estoppel and agree with the Board that an appellant cannot make a nonfrivolous allegation of a USERRA violation if its contentions are wholly precluded. Indeed, this case illustrates the flaw in the Board's now-overruled precedent. As noted by the Board, even if Petitioners had been granted a hearing in the second, identical USERRA appeals, because they did not make any additional allegations beyond those in Bryant I, Petitioners would not have been able to present any content at the hearing. See Hau II, 123 M.S.P.R. at 627 n.*. Such a hearing would have been a waste of resources, contrary to the policies underlying the doctrine of collateral estoppel. See Montana v. United States, 440 U.S. 147, 153 (1979); see also Morgan v. Dep't of Energy, 424 F.3d 1271, 1275 (Fed. Cir. 2005) (finding the issue precluded when there was "no new conduct with respect to his claim for relief").

We therefore conclude that the Board correctly dismissed Petitioners' appeals for lack of jurisdiction because Petitioners' constructive discharge claims under USERRA are precluded by collateral estoppel. We have considered the remaining arguments and find them unpersuasive.

DISCUSSION

...

"Under the doctrine of res judicata (or claim preclusion), '[a] final judgment on the merits of an action precludes the parties or their privies from relitigating issues that were or could have been raised in that action.'" Ammex, Inc. v. United States, 334 F.3d 1052, 1055 (Fed. Cir. 2003) (quoting Federated Dep't Stores, Inc. v. Moitie, 452 U.S. 394, 398 (1981)). As long as the prior decision was rendered by a court with competent jurisdiction, see Gillig v. Nike, Inc., 602 F.3d 1354, 1361 (Fed. Cir. 2010), res judicata applies when "(1) the parties are identical or in privity; (2) the first suit proceeded to a final judgment on the merits; and (3) the second claim is based on the same set of transactional facts as the first." Id. (citing Parklane Hosiery Co. v. Shore, 439 U.S. 322, 326 n.5 (1979)); see Phillips/May, 524 F.3d at 1268.

It is uncontested that the earlier judgment against Detrich involved identical parties and was in a court with competent jurisdiction. The earlier case was also a final judgment "on the merits" because it was based on the parties' substantive arguments at the time, rather than dismissed on jurisdictional or other procedural grounds.

The only issue, then, is whether Detrich's WPA claim is based on the same set of transactional facts as the prior case. Detrich argues that because of the typographic error in the inappropriate-use-of-email charge, his present claim is based on a different set of facts: "the set of facts that only became available to the appellant after the error in the charge was revealed." Newly discovered facts relating to a previously litigated claim may on rare occasions form the basis for a new claim, such as in the case of negligent misrepresentation by the other party. See Restatement (Second) of Judgments § 26 cmt. j (1982). Here, however, the error was discovered during the prior case, and the error was found to be harmless. See Detrich, 251 F. App'x at 680 n.1. Although Detrich argues that this error was not harmless because it prevented him from questioning witnesses about the email, this issue cannot be relitigated now. Detrich's WPA claim is thus "based on the same, or nearly the same factual allegations" as the prior case--namely, the email he sent on May 31, 2004--which is sufficient to satisfy the "same set of transactional facts" element. Ammex, 334 F.3d at 1056. Detrich's claim is barred by res judicata.

The preclusion of an IRA case after an adverse action appeal that could have raised a whistleblowing defense is inapplicable if the earlier Board appeal was untimely--not adjudicated on the merits, Calvetti v. Dept. of Air Force, 107 MSPR 480, 482-83 ¶¶ 7-9, 2007 MSPB 306 (2007):

Under 5 U.S.C. § 7121(g), an employee who is subject to a personnel action that he is entitled to grieve under a collective bargaining agreement and who alleges a prohibited personnel practice that is not covered by 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(1), such as section 2302(b)(8) whistleblowing reprisal, may elect one of the following remedies: (1) an appeal to the Board under 5 U.S.C. § 7701; (2) the negotiated grievance procedure; or (3) a complaint with OSC which, upon exhaustion of Special Counsel proceedings, may be followed by an appeal to the Board under 5 U.S.C. §§ 1214 and 1221. Shannon v. Department of Homeland Security, 100 M.S.P.R. 629, ¶ 15 (2005); Cooper v. Department of Defense, 98 M.S.P.R. 313, ¶ 11 n.1 (2005). An employee is deemed to have elected to file an appeal with the Board when he has timely filed a notice of appeal under the Board's applicable appellate procedures; an employee is deemed to have elected the negotiated grievance procedure when he has timely filed a grievance in writing, in accordance with the provisions of the parties' negotiated procedure; and an employee is deemed to have elected corrective action under chapter 12 when he has made an allegation to OSC under 5 U.S.C. § 1214(a)(1). See 5 U.S.C. § 7121(g)(4); Brundin v. Smithsonian Institution, 75 M.S.P.R. 332, 336 (1997).

We note initially that the appellant has asserted, and the agency has not disputed, that he was covered by a collective bargaining agreement when the agency removed him from Federal employment. As an employee covered by a negotiated grievance procedure who raised allegations of whistleblower reprisal, the appellant therefore could have elected one of the three remedies set forth in 5 U.S.C. § 7121(g). Further, we have found that an employee has not elected under 5 U.S.C. § 7121(g)(4)(A) to pursue a Board appeal unless he has "timely filed a notice of appeal under the applicable appellate procedures." See Brundin, 75 M.S.P.R. at 336.

Since the appellant's prior removal appeal was untimely filed, as discussed above, the appellant did not "elect" the Board remedy by filing that appeal.[2] Id. at 336. In addition, he apparently has not challenged his removal by using the negotiated grievance procedure. Thus, the appellant did not timely elect either of the first two remedies identified in 5 U.S.C. § 7121(g), and he could properly elect the remaining remedy of raising allegations of whistleblower reprisal with OSC in connection with his removal and subsequently filing an appeal. See Shannon, 100 M.S.P.R. 629, ¶ 17. Accordingly, the 65-day limit applicable to otherwise appealable action (OAA) appeals, which are first filed before OSC, was the appropriate timeliness standard. See 5 C.F.R. § 1209.5(b). Because the record shows that the appellant met this requirement, his appeal should not have been dismissed as untimely filed. See Westmoreland v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 77 M.S.P.R. 315, 317 (1998); Brundin, 75 M.S.P.R. at 336.

The Whistleblower Protection Act (WPA) provides that, unless a personnel action is otherwise directly appealable to the Board, an employee affected by such action shall first seek corrective action from the OSC. 5 USC § 1214(a)(3). If unsuccessful before the OSC, he may seek corrective action from the Board. See, e.g., Marano v. Department of Justice, 2 F.3d 1137, 1139 (Fed. Cir. 1993). Since a personnel transfer or reassignment is not an adverse action over which the Board would otherwise normally have jurisdiction, the action is reviewable by the Board only when the appellant asserts the existence of a prohibited personnel practice under the WPA. Id. The appellant, therefore, in order to appeal his directed reassignment to the Board, had to first seek corrective action from the OSC. Any alleged issues not raised in his complaint to the OSC were outside the Board's jurisdiction and could not be considered by the administrative judge. See, e.g., Ellison v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 7 F.3d 1031, 1037 (Fed. Cir. 1993).

The doctrine of res judicata provides that matters, including questions of jurisdiction, that were settled in a prior appeal in which there has been a final decision are precluded from being brought before the Board in a subsequent appeal.... In the appellant's 1992 IRA appeal, the administrative judge determined that three of the claims which are also at issue here had not been presented to OSC and, accordingly, the Board had no jurisdiction over them. That determination made the administrative judge's additional determination that the matters were not "protected disclosures" unnecessary because he had already determined that the Board lacked jurisdiction over the case. Under these circumstances, the administrative judge improperly applied the doctrine of res judicata to the "protected disclosure" issue because that issue was not properly before the Board in the 1992 IRA appeal.
