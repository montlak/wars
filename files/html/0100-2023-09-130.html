m. Consecutive Adverse Actions

Assuming that the agency rescinded an adverse action and divested the Board of appellate jurisdiction, the agency is free to try again by instituting a new adverse action based on the same charges. See Dellera v. DHUD, 65 MSPR 636, 643 (1994) ("The fact that an agency takes a new adverse action following rescission does not affect the rescission of the earlier action"; the Board is without pendent jurisdiction to address issues that are capable of repetition yet evade review because of mootness; citing Fairley v. USPS, 63 MSPR 10, 12-13 (1994)); Fairley v. USPS, 63 MSPR 10, 13 (1994) ("If the administrative judge finds that the action has been completely rescinded and that the Board thus lacks jurisdiction over it, she may adjudicate the merits of the appeal from the second removal action.... However, if she finds that the first removal action has not been completely rescinded, she must adjudicate the appeal from that action on the merits; in that event, the merits of the appeal from the second removal action may not be reached until a final decision of the Board or a decision of a court has been issued reversing the first removal action."); Bracy v. Dept. of Navy, 52 MSPR 598, 601 (1992) (agency cancels a resignation claimed to be involuntary and then proposes a removal based upon the violation of an earlier last chance agreement; a PFR challenging the dismissal of the first appeal based on rescission, followed by a removal, was treated as a new appeal and remanded to the region for adjudication). But if the agency fails to properly cancel one action, e.g., a suspension, and the agency then removes the appellant for reasons substantially related to the acts predicating the suspension, the Board may direct the agency to cancel the removal and restore the appellant. See Storm v. Dept. of Army, 64 MSPR 14, 27 (1994); cf. Frias v. USPS, 57 MSPR 691, 697 (1993) ("When an appeal has been dismissed on the basis that the agency has completely rescinded its adverse action, the agency is not precluded from instituting a new adverse action based on the same charges. See Mavronikolas v. U.S. Postal Service, 39 M.S.P.R. 442, 444-45 (1989)."). [Refer earlier in this chapter to "Cancelation of Action Appealed," "Whistleblower Protection Act: Claims for Damages, Other Relief," for discussion as to the likelihood of repetition of threatened actions, and refer to Chapter 11, "Double Jeopardy"; refer to Chapter 16, "Enforcement" on compliance.]

An agency may remove an employee, have the removal reversed by an AJ, and then remove the employee a second time. If the first removal is subsequently sustained by the Board, an appeal from the second removal is moot, Jackson v. USPS, 79 MSPR 144, 146-47 (1998):

The Board has held that where an agency has taken two consecutive removal actions, the second can have no force or effect unless the agency rescinds or modifies the first, or a final decision of the Board or a court reverses it. See Parker v. U.S. Postal Service, 46 MSPR 214, 219 (1990). Based on that precedent, we have also dismissed as moot the appeal of a second removal action after we determined, on appeal of the first, that the earlier action was sustained. Hanner v. Department of the Army, 62 MSPR 677, 687 (1994), aff'd, 48 F.3d 1236 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table). See also, e.g., Social Security Administration v. Carr, 78 MSPR 313 (1998), (appeals growing out of the appellant's employment were dismissed as mooted by a decision sustaining the agency's proposal to remove her); Occhipinti v. Department of Justice, 61 MSPR 504, 508 (1994) (where effective relief cannot be granted to the appellant from an adjudication of an appeal, he has no legally cognizable interest in its outcome, making dismissal of that appeal appropriate). We find that this is the situation here. Having now held that the appellant's February 25, 1995 removal was proper, the later removal is of no effect.

A dispute whether the contested mootness of one personnel action was in turn mooted by a second action removing the appellant arose in White v. Int'l Boundary Water Comm'n, 59 MSPR 62, 64-65 (1993):

When an agency completely rescinds a personnel action after an appeal is filed--that is, when it restores the employee to the status quo ante--the appeal is rendered moot and the Board is divested of jurisdiction. McCulley v. U.S. Postal Service, 54 MSPR 207, 209 (1992). See also Taylor v. Department of Education, 54 MSPR 406, 410 (1992) (mootness doctrine applies in an IRA appeal). Reinstatement followed immediately by a detail or reassignment, however, may not constitute a return to the status quo ante; the Board will carefully scrutinize the legitimacy of the agency's rationale for the detail or reassignment. Mann v. Veterans Administration, 29 MSPR 271, 274 (1985).

The petition for review raises the questions as to whether the appellant was restored to the status quo ante after his appeals were dismissed, but on the present record we cannot determine whether the change in the appellant's responsibilities was either material or improper under Mann. Normally under such circumstances we would remand the appeal to the regional office for further proceedings. See McCulley, 54 MSPR at 210. To remand these appeals, however, would be pointless. Assuming arguendo that the administrative judge found on remand that the first removal action was not completely rescinded, and even given the fact that the agency concedes that the first removal was procedurally invalid, the appellant would gain nothing more than a declaration that he was temporarily treated improperly. He would not be restored to his former position, other than on paper perhaps, because on September 4, 1992, he was again removed.

The petition for review is therefore moot, because "the Board cannot grant effective relief." Conrad v. Department of Justice, 24 MSPR 452, 454 (1984). See also 5 USC 1204(h) ("[t]he Board shall not issue advisory opinions"); Villarreal v. Department of the Treasury, 13 MSPR 82, 84 (1982) (the Board will dismiss a petition for review if a disposition ostensibly in favor of the party filing the petition "would have no effect").
