1. FLSA Computation

Under Int'l Ass'n of Firefighters, Local F-61 and Philadelphia Naval Shipyard, 3 FLRA 438, 441 (1980), the union could compel negotiations on a proposal that a seven-day week control application of the FLSA to unit firefighters. See 29 USC 207(k). The use of a seven-day work period resulted in different overtime entitlement than a 14-day work period. Nothing in the statute precluded negotiation of the length of the pay period for FLSA purposes, and because the proposal did not directly infringe on management authority to set its budget, the argument that the proposal would result in increased costs for the agency was not a bar to its negotiation. The proposal, involving a calculation formula for overtime pay, did not affect the agency's organization or the number of employees assigned to the organization. Subject to bargaining, there being no statutory or regulatory conflict, was Proposal 3 in Int'l Bhd. Of Police Officers and GSA, Region 2, 47 FLRA 397, 401-02 (1993), that "[a]bsence on sick leave, holidays, non-work days established by executive order, or absence on compensatory time during the basic work week does not reduce the amount of overtime pay to which an employee may be entitled during an administrative workweek." The proposal was consistent with provisions of 5 CFR 551.401(b) and (d), that time spent in a paid nonwork status is considered hours of work for determining employee entitlement to FLSA overtime pay.

A union could compel negotiations on inclusion in a collective bargaining agreement of the types of time that would be compensable under FLSA for pre- and post-shift work, avoiding characterizations of the time as noncompensable preliminary and postliminary time if OPM rules were applied. The complex analysis under FLSA and OPM rules interpreting FLSA will not be attempted here. AFGE Local 987 and Dept. of Air Force, Robins AFB, 37 FLRA 197, 207-12 (1990), rev'd Dept. of Air Force v. FLRA, 952 F.2d 446, 453 (D.C. Cir. 1991), provides interpretation of OPM regulations implementing FLSA to exclude compensation for the postliminary activities that were the subject of the proposal. See NAGE Local R14-22 and Dept. of Army, Hq., Army Air Defense Artillery Ctr., 45 FLRA 949, 970 (1992) (nonnegotiable: second sentence of Proposal 8, mandating overtime pay for employees whose departure from worksite at end of workday is delayed due to gate inspection: postshift activity was not closely related to principal activities during hours of work; overtime would be contrary to 5 CFR 550.412); cf. Akpeneye v. US, 990 F.3d 1373, 1374 (Fed. Cir. 2021) (reviewing FLSA requirements for overtime and duty-free meal breaks interrupted by some work: "Thus, the mere fact that Plaintiffs had ongoing security duties during their breaks does not, in and of itself, transform them into standby employees who were hired to wait or render their meal breaks primarily for the government's benefit. This does not suggest that the officers' standby obligations were irrelevant. The existence of standby duties of sufficient significance in this context could mean that the break time was primarily for the government's benefit."). [Refer to Chapter 9, "Overtime Compensation."]

Nothing in FLSA authorized employees to negotiate for double time on Sunday. The phrase in FLSA, at 29 USC 207(a)(1), requiring pay at not less than one and one-half the regular rate for overtime work was consistent with the limitation in the Federal Employees Pay Act, 5 USC 5544, setting time and a half as a standard in the federal sector. Because the rates of pay were set by statute, they were not bargainable. 5 USC 7103(a)(14)(C). FLRA distinguished situations allowing for bargaining of pay for certain categories of employees whose bargaining rights were preserved by statute. AFGE Council of Fed. Grain Inspection Locals and USDA, Fed. Grain Inspection Serv., 3 FLRA 530, 532-34 (1980).

A union could not compel bargaining on a proposal that required overtime in at least 15-minute increments and precluded the agency from extending the regular work period for less than 15 minutes. The proposal violated the right to assign work. AFGE Local 1815 and Army Aviation Ctr., Ft. Rucker, 28 FLRA 1172, 1174 (1987) (citing AFGE Mint Council 157 and Dept. of Treasury, Bureau of Mint, 19 FLRA 640 (1985)).
