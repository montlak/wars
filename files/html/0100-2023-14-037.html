c. Exemplary Penalty

Douglas requires agencies to consider the individual circumstances and offenses of the employee being disciplined. Exemplary punishment, that is, punishment designed to deter other employees, is generally contrary to the Douglas principles. Perez v. USPS, 48 MSPR 354, 357-58 (1991); see Blake v. DOJ, 81 MSPR 394, 415 ¶ 40 (1999) ("Importantly, the administrative judge and the deciding official both failed to consider the mitigating factors of the appellant's rehabilitation potential and the exemplary nature of the penalty"; a penalty commensurate with a substantial lapse of sound managerial judgment was appropriate rather than a penalty commensurate with an active, malevolent design to deceive Congress); Crouse v. Dept. of Treasury, 70 MSPR 623, 631 (1996) ("Exemplary punishment is generally contrary to the Douglas principles."), reconsideration denied, 75 MSPR 57, 66 (1997); Fowler v. USPS, 32 MSPR 559, 563 (1987) (exemplary penalties fail to focus on individual circumstances and offense of employees being disciplined; further, exemplary penalties should not be imposed by the Board if the agency does not assert their necessity).

Yet the Board hedges on exemplary penalties, explaining in Harper v. Dept. of Air Force, 61 MSPR 446, 448 (1994), that exemplary penalties may be appropriate if other circumstances are considered. Harper involved a removal for theft of agency property:

The administrative judge found that the agency's deciding official testified that he was aware of the appellant's years of service, his lack of a prior disciplinary record, and his satisfactory performance record. She also noted that he testified that those factors had no effect on his decision because, if the agency had imposed a punishment of less than removal, such an action would have sent a signal that the agency condoned theft and would have undermined the credibility of the organization. As noted by the administrative judge, the Board has held that exemplary punishment is generally contrary to the Douglas principles, which focus on the individual circumstances and offenses of the employee being disciplined. See Perez v. U.S. Postal Service, 48 MSPR 354, 357-58 (1991), citing Fowler v. U.S. Postal Service, 32 MSPR 559, 563 (1987), rev'd on other grounds, 846 F.2d 77 (Fed. Cir. 1988) (Table). We note, however, that our decision in Douglas, 5 MSPR at 305-06, provides that one of the factors to be considered in determining the appropriateness of a penalty is "the adequacy and effectiveness of alternative sanctions to deter such conduct in the future by the employee or others." Thus, our decisions in Perez and Fowler should be understood to mean that deterrence is one of the factors that may be considered by an agency in making a penalty determination, but an agency may not decide to make an example of an appellant irrespective of the other Douglas factors. Upon consideration of all of the relevant Douglas factors and the particular circumstances in this appeal, we find that removal was not an excessive penalty.

Board Chair Erdreich dissented in Harper at 451:

In DeWitt v. Department of the Navy, 747 F.2d 1442, 1445 (Fed. Cir. 1984), cert. denied, 470 U.S. 1054 (1985), the court held that it was improper for an agency to impose the maximum penalty for theft of government property without considering the unique circumstances of the case, noting that "[i]t is this refusal to consider the particular facts of the case that is the essence of the abuse." Moreover, as noted by the administrative judge in this appeal, the Board has held that exemplary punishment is generally contrary to the principles set forth in Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 M.S.P.R. 280, 306 (1981), which focuses on the individual circumstances and offenses of the employee being disciplined. See Perez v. U.S. Postal Service, 48 M.S.P.R. 354, 357-58 (1991). Thus, although the agency's need in this appeal to set an example for other employees may be one of the considerations in selecting the penalty, it should not be controlling and override other valid Douglas considerations.

Deterrence was a consideration in the imposition of a removal, rather than a lengthy suspension, of an employee who held the equivalent of a management ALJ position and who was charged with sending inappropriate email traffic to some colleagues and who made insufficient efforts to control more egregious email traffic of those individuals, VA v. Chiappetta, CB-7521-16-0014-N-1 (NP 12/2/2022):

In his initial decision, the ALJ found that, if the respondent were to return work at the agency, he probably would amend his email behavior, and that a lengthy suspension would be sufficient to deter such misconduct by the respondent and others. He further found, however, that the penalty of removal would be even more effective in deterring others from committing similar misconduct. On petition for review, the respondent argues that the ALJ is attempting to make an example of him and that the Board has traditionally frowned upon exemplary punishment. He argues that, in light of the nature of his misconduct, his rehabilitative potential, and the agency's policy of progressive discipline, demotion to a nonsupervisory position would be more appropriate.

We agree with the respondent that exemplary punishment is generally contrary to the principles set forth in Douglas, which call for the Board to focus on the individual circumstances and offenses of the employee being disciplined. Perez v. U.S. Postal Service, 48 M.S.P.R. 354, 357-58 (1991). Nevertheless, it is appropriate under Douglas to consider the deterrent effect that a lesser penalty would have on others. Harper v. Department of the Air Force, 61 M.S.P.R. 446, 448 (1994); Douglas, 5 M.S.P.R. at 305-06. This means simply that deterrence is one of the factors that may be considered in selecting a penalty but that the Board may not decide to make an example of an individual irrespective of the other Douglas factors. See Harper, 61 M.S.P.R. at 448. Reviewing the initial decision as a whole, we find that the ALJ considered all of the appropriate penalty factors in arriving at his decision. In any event, even excluding deterrence as a factor entirely, upon consideration of all the relevant Douglas factors and the particular circumstances, we find that removal is the appropriate penalty for the respondent's misconduct.

The respondent's misconduct is severe and, as explained in the initial decision, raises serious questions about his capability to render fair, impartial, and unbiased decisions as an administrative adjudicator. Given the bigoted overtones of the FOH activity, the respondent's unwillingness to stop it despite his position of prominence, the negative publicity that this case has already garnered, and the deleterious effect that the respondent's retention would have on the agency's reputation if he continued on in any sort of adjudicatory role, we find that the agency has shown good cause for his removal.
