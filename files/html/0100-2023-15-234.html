a. Criminal Record

Failure to list criminal offenses on the portion of the Form SF-171 requesting the information suggests that the appellant falsified the records intentionally and with purpose to deceive or mislead. Specific intent to make a false or fraudulent statement may be inferred if the alleged misrepresentation is made with reckless disregard for the truth. This intent, given findings that the appellant understood the form, are not dispelled by the appellant's statement that the offense was not important, that he forgot about it, that he listed a more serious offense, or that he knew that OPM would discover the offenses and therefore had no motive to make a false statement. Intervening performance is immaterial. Spielman, supra, was distinguished because it was pre-Reform Act. McCreary v. OPM, 27 MSPR 459, 462 (1985); see Hamilton v. Dept. of Air Force, 52 MSPR 45, 47-48 (1991) (failing to list on an application a prior conviction for possession and sale of marijuana; a disparity argument was disregarded because the penalty was "appropriate"; a showing of remorse did not sway the Board); cf. Jones v. DOJ, 13 MSPR 425, 427 (1982) (removal for failing to disclose during a preemployment interview that during military service the appellant was involved in possession and sale of drugs). The failure to disclose a record of conviction on an employment application justifies discharge; considering whether the employee would have been hired had the disclosure been made is speculative. Dunbar v. Dept. of Treasury, 6 MSPR 100, 101, 6 MSPB 96 (1981); see Stevenson v. DOD, 55 MSPR 625, 631 (1992) ("the Board has consistently held that an employee's performance in a position to which she has been appointed based on a falsified employment application has no relevance to the falsification charge or the consequent removal"); cf. Brown v. USPS, 3 MSPR 61, 62 (1980) (dismissal for the failure to list on an application a conviction by special court-martial).

If an employee has doubts concerning whether the facts of arrest and incarceration for a civil offense ought to be placed on an employment application, he should list the events with an explanation rather than omitting them. Pichot v. DOJ, 29 MSPR 477, 480-81 (1985) (citing Wilcox v. USPS, 9 MSPR 493, 495 (1982)). Incomplete disclosure of a criminal record may suggest falsification, Christopher v. Dept. of Army, 107 MSPR 580, 588-89 ¶ 17, 2008 MSPB 2 (2008):

The administrative judge's finding that the appellant's partial disclosure on his OF 306 indicated that he lacked any intent to deceive is not in accord with prior Board cases which have found that an employee intended to mislead the agency where he knowingly revealed some, but not all, requested information when completing employment forms. See, e.g., Reed v. Office of Personnel Management, 74 M.S.P.R. 616, 620-21 (1997) (finding that the appellant knowingly submitted incorrect information on his employment forms when he disclosed only a single warrant and not all of his prior arrests, criminal charges, and convictions of which he was aware at the time he completed the forms); Harmon v. General Services Administration, 61 M.S.P.R. 327, 332 (1994) (finding that the appellant's intent to deceive the agency could be inferred from his reckless disregard for the truth when he deliberately and repeatedly claimed in his application for federal employment that he had obtained a college education when he had actually only attended a few general classes on a college campus), aff'd, 47 F.3d 1181 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (Table); Devitto v. Office of Personnel Management, 61 M.S.P.R. 297, 299-302 (1994) (sustaining the charge of intentional falsification of employment documents where the appellant disclosed some, but not all, of his prior arrests and convictions in reckless disregard for the truth); Buhl v. Office of Personnel Management, 37 M.S.P.R. 305, 311 (1988) (finding that the appellant deliberately concealed two of his prior arrests despite having listed his most recent arrests on his application for federal employment). Further, we find that the appellant's own testimony does not support the administrative judge's finding that the appellant did not intend to deceive the agency when he failed to disclose his prior convictions because the appellant testified that he recalled his 1995, 1997, and 1998 convictions for assault (specifications 1-3) at the time he completed the OF 306.

See Box v. USPS, 51 MSPR 401, 406 (1991) ("The question on the employment application asks that applicants list all convictions and pending criminal charges except for certain traffic violations and juvenile offenses. As the administrative judge found, the appellant admitted that he incorrectly answered that question in the negative, but asserted that he believed that he only needed to acknowledge convictions for which he was incarcerated. There is nothing in the question or elsewhere in the record, however, to suggest that the question applies only to incarcerations."). But see Lopez-Matos v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 2002 NP No. 02-3143) (remanding for further examination of whether a traffic violation constituted an "offense" within the meaning of the SF-86), following remand and further appeal, Lopez-Matos v. DOJ, 113 Fed. Appx. 387 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP) (reversing the Board and setting aside removal); Weinberg v. Macy, 360 F.2d 816 (D.C. Cir. 1965) (suggesting the need for a close examination of the circumstances when the government charges that the appellant failed to disclose an arrest and the evidence points to a legal entanglement short of an arrest); Pelicone v. Hodges, 320 F.2d 754 (D.C. Cir. 1963) (appellant should not have been dismissed when he correctly disclosed an arrest but the government incorrectly thought that the arrest should have been characterized in a different manner).

The Board implied that an applicant has a duty to respond that he is under charges for a felony if he is aware that charges are likely to be filed against him soon after he completes his SF-171. See Johnson v. Dept. of Army, 37 MSPR 95, 97 (1988), aff'd, Johnson v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 88-3350) (also sustaining dismissal on the basis of erroneous information concerning the appellant's prior addresses--information that the Board thought would prevent the agency from learning about criminal charges).

Reliance on advice of counsel may influence the determination of whether an appellant intentionally falsified an employment application. If an employee fails to disclose a criminal proceeding based upon the advice of counsel given on full disclosure of facts and followed in good faith, the appellant will be considered to have rebutted the mental element requisite to the offense of intentional falsification, even though the employee is ordinarily responsible for actions taken on the advice of counsel. Gillis v. USPS, 24 MSPR 642, 645 (1984); see Naekel, 782 F.2d 975 (Fed. Cir. 1986), supra. [Refer in this chapter to "Reliance on Advice of Counsel or Other Individual."]

Disclaimer of recall of one's past may be insufficient to dispel an inference of intent. An intentional false statement case may be sustained if the evidence of intent to deceive comprised omission of facts from the employment form itself, and if evidence shows that the employee left out information about prior criminal convictions when he completed applications seven months earlier, particularly when three of the five omitted criminal convictions resulted in jail sentences. DeLessio v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1987 NP No. 87-3450); see Weirich v. OPM, AT-0731-15-0629-I-1 (NP 3/18/2016) ("As the administrative judge noted, the appellant was terminated from H.A. Sack just 1 month before he first submitted an inaccurate OF-306 in May 2014, and his termination was still recent when he again attested to inaccurate information in both June and July 2014. We therefore find it implausible that the appellant inadvertently failed to review the accuracy of the forms he was submitting, on multiple occasions, particularly given that he had so recently experienced the significant event of termination. See Devitto v. Office of Personnel Management, 61 M.S.P.R. 297, 301-02 (1994) (refusing to credit the appellant's claim that his failure to disclose information on an employment form was an honest mistake, finding that explanation implausible)."); cf. Reed v. OPM, 74 MSPR 616, 620-21 (1997) (embarrassment about a criminal record does not justify the falsification of an employment document); Devitto v. OPM, 61 MSPR 297, 302 (1994) (in a suitability disqualification appeal, the Board examined in some detail various statements on forms calling for information about convictions and arrests; certain misrepresentations by the appellant evidenced "at best, his reckless disregard for the truth," discrediting the defense that the omissions constituted an "honest mistake"); Curry v. Dept. of Air Force, 35 MSPR 301, 306 (1987) (discounting a defense that epilepsy caused mental lapses that in turn caused false statements on employment documents relating to past criminal and employment records). An individual's honest mistake as to whether a traffic offense should be reported as a criminal offense on a SF-86 did not warrant a negative suitability determination, Lopez-Matos v. DOJ, 113 Fed. Appx. 387, 389-90 (Fed. Cir. 2004 NP):

To find an appellant unsuitable based on false statements, an agency must show by a preponderance of the evidence that the appellant provided false information with the intention of defrauding the Government. See Naekel v. Dep't of Transp., 782 F.2d 975, 977 (Fed. Cir. 1986); see also Brown v. Def. Logistics Agency, 65 M.S.P.R. 436, 442 (1994), aff'd, 67 F.3d 319 (Fed. Cir. 1995). The fact of an incorrect response cannot control the question of intent. Naekel, 782 F.2d at 979. "Were a bare inaccuracy controlling on the question of intent, the 'intent' element of the charge would be subsumed within the distinct inquiry of whether the employee's answer adheres to the true state of facts." Id. Intent may also be inferred when a misrepresentation is made with reckless disregard for the truth or with a conscious purpose to avoid learning the truth. See Walcott v. U.S. Postal Serv., 52 M.S.P.R. 277, 282, aff'd, 980 F.2d 744 (Fed. Cir. 1992).

In the present case, the Board erred by allowing an incorrect response to control the "intent" element of the false statements test. While undisputed evidence exists that Mr. Lopez-Matos incorrectly answered question 23f on Form SF-86, no substantial evidence of record establishes his intention to deceive or mislead the INS given the confusing nature of the question. In response to INS's allegation that he made an intentional false statement, Mr. Lopez-Matos explained his oversight stemmed from the similarity of form SF-86 to form 306, which did not require the disclosure of "traffic fines of $300 or less." While the Board dismissed Mr. Lopez-Matos' response as "unreasonable" and "unworthy of belief," it did not point to any supporting evidence, other than Mr. Lopez-Matos' "incorrect response." Lopez-Matos, MSPB Docket No. AT-00731-00-0786-M-1 at 9. Given the confusing nature of the application question, it could have reasonably been interpreted as covering only traffic fines that were not dismissed. Because neither INS nor the Board presented substantial evidence of intent, this court sets aside INS's action because it was arbitrary, capricious, and unsupported by substantial evidence. The decision of the Board is reversed.

State law may affect requirements on employment and security applications for disclosure of information about arrests or prosecutions that state law shields from compulsory disclosure. McKenna v. INS (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 98-3366), gave effect to New York law concerning alleged failure to reveal a felony charge on a SF-86 form:

Mr. McKenna admits that he was arrested in 1979 for allegedly committing a felony, but asserts that he did not have to reveal this information on the SF 86's because the charge had been dismissed and his record sealed under N.Y.C.P.L. § 160.60. That statute provides in pertinent part as follows:

Upon the termination of a criminal...proceeding against a person in favor of such person...the arrest and prosecution shall be deemed a nullity and the accused shall be restored, in contemplation of law, to the status he occupied before the arrest and prosecution. The arrest or prosecution shall not operate as a disqualification of any person...to pursue or engage in any...occupation.... Except where specifically required or permitted by statute or upon specific authorization of a superior court, no such person shall be required to divulge information pertaining to the arrest or prosecution.

N.Y.C.P.L. § 160.60 (McKinney 1992) (emphasis added).

The arbitrator found that this state law was "not binding on the Federal sector in relation to determining the fitness of an employee for a Federal government job." Although it is true, as the arbitrator notes, that state laws can be preempted to achieve federal purposes, the arbitrator did not base his conclusion on any federal law or regulation that might preempt the New York statute at issue. Cf. City of New York v. FCC, 486 U.S. 57, 63 (1988) (noting that the Supremacy Clause applies to both federal statutes and property adopted federal regulations). The arbitrator's conclusion appears to be based on the assumption that the statute before us interferes with the federal government's ability to evaluate the fitness of personnel for national security positions. Congress has addressed this issue, however, and has determined that it will defer to state decisions to seal criminal records. 5 USC § 9101 requires states to provide criminal history records to the federal government when personnel are being investigated for national security purposes. Specifically excluded from the records states must provide are those that have been sealed from access by state and local criminal justice agencies. See 5 USC § 9101 (a)(2) (1994). Aside from whether Mr. McKenna's position involved national security, this statute prevents us from finding that N.Y.C.P.L. § 160.60 has been preempted by general federal interests in national security.
