F. RELIGIOUS OBSERVANCES

NTEU and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 52 FLRA 1265, 1289-90 (1997) (Article 18), explained the nature of government-wide regulations on religious compensatory time:

The sole basis advanced by the Agency for finding the provision nonnegotiable is its asserted inconsistency with 5 C.F.R. § 550.1002(b), governing the use of compensatory time for religious observances.[19] The Agency does not dispute the fact that the use of compensatory time for religious purposes can encompass a death in an employee's immediate family.

[19] 5 C.F.R. § 550.1002(b) provides as follows:

To the extent that such modifications in work schedules do not interfere with the efficient accomplishment of an agency's mission, the agency shall in each instance afford the employee the opportunity to work compensatory overtime and shall in each instance grant compensatory time off to an employee requesting such time off for religious observances when the employee's personal religious beliefs require that the employee abstain from work during certain periods of the workday or workweek.

Under 5 C.F.R. § 550.1002(b), an employee must be given the opportunity to earn compensatory time in order to be granted time off for religious observances when two criteria are satisfied: (1) the employee's religious beliefs require abstention from work at certain times; and (2) modifications in work schedules do not interfere with the efficient accomplishment of the agency's mission. The Agency has not explained how the provision would create conflicting requirements for granting religious compensatory time. As to the first criterion, the Agency makes no argument that an employee would be given time off without regard to whether the employee's religious beliefs require abstention from work. As to the second criterion, the provision expressly states that such leave shall be granted in accordance with applicable regulations; given the Union's explanation, which we have adopted, that the modification of work schedules will not interfere with the efficient accomplishment of the Agency's mission, it is clear that the provision is consistent with 5 C.F.R. § 550.1002(b). Accordingly, it is not contrary to that regulation. See American Federation of Government Employees, AFL-CIO, National Immigration and Naturalization Service Council and U.S. Immigration and Naturalization Service, 27 FLRA 467, 476-77 (1987).

Approved as negotiable in POPA and Dept. of Commerce, PTO, 48 FLRA 546, 549-50 (1993), was the proposal that the agency grant compensatory time off for religious observances in accordance with OPM regulations:

For the following reasons, we find that the provision is consistent with 5 C.F.R. § 550.1002(b), a Government-wide regulation, and conclude, therefore, that the provision is negotiable.

Provision 1 provides that, in accordance with the Office's published policy, the Agency will grant reasonable time for religious observances that is requested by an employee. We agree with the Union that the provision must be read to include the prefatory phrase "in accordance with the Office's published policy."... Interpreted in this manner, the provision conditions any grant of time for religious observance on Office policy. The record demonstrates that, in explaining Office policy, the Agency notified employees that, under applicable statutes, compensatory time would be granted to accommodate employees' religious needs when such an accommodation does not interfere with the efficient accomplishment of the Agency's work. By requiring the Agency to grant reasonable compensatory time in accordance with Office policy, therefore, the provision requires the Agency to grant employees' requests for reasonable time for religious observances where doing so would not interfere with the efficient accomplishment of Agency work.

Under 5 C.F.R. § 550.1002(b), compensatory time for religious observances will be granted "[t]o the extent that...modifications in work schedules do not interfere with the efficient accomplishment of an agency's mission[.]" 5 C.F.R. § 550.1002(b). As interpreted above, the disputed provision in this case requires the Agency to grant reasonable time that is requested by an employee for religious observances where doing so would not interfere with the efficient accomplishment of the Agency's work. We find that, interpreted in this manner, the provision incorporates the conditions governing compensatory time for religious observances that are prescribed by 5 C.F.R. § 550.1002(b). We conclude, therefore, that the provision is consistent with 5 C.F.R. § 550.1002(b).

Because the provision in this case incorporates the conditions prescribed by 5 C.F.R. § 550.1002(b), the provision is distinguishable from the provision in Office of Chief Counsel, IRS, 39 FLRA at 35-36. The provision in that case precluded the agency from denying an employee's request for leave for religious observances unless the employee's presence was absolutely essential and no replacement was available. We found that the provision in that case was inconsistent with 5 C.F.R. § 550.1002(b) because it established a standard for denial of employees' requests for leave for religious observances which was more restrictive than the applicable Government-wide regulation. By contrast, the provision in this case incorporates the regulatory conditions governing such leave requests.

Nonnegotiable was a provision that set more liberal standards for granting time off for religious observances than did OPM government-wide regulations at 5 CFR 550.1002 ("Compensatory Time Off for Religious Observances"). AFGE Local 1923 and DHHS, SSA, 17 FLRA 543, 545 (1985); see NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, IRS, 38 FLRA 1366, 1370 (1991) (Proposal 2). Also nonnegotiable was a proposal that, to the maximum extent possible, an agency would accommodate requests of employees for compensatory time off or for religious compensatory time, unless the modification in schedules would seriously interfere with the agency mission. The proposal was inconsistent with 5 CFR 550.1002, requiring an agency to grant an employee's request for religious compensatory time and to modify the employee's work schedule to the extent that the modification does not interfere with efficient accomplishment of the agency mission. FUSE, Local R1-144 and Dept. of Navy, Naval Underwater Sys. Ctr., 26 FLRA 568, 569-70 (1987); see NFFE and Dept. of Interior, BLM, 29 FLRA 1491, 1508-09 (1987) (proposal was not negotiable that required compensatory time for religious observances except in emergencies).

A proposal was not negotiable that the employer grant employees time off from their regular work for religious observances. The proposal precluded consideration of the agency's need for the employee's services. AFGE Local 1458 and DOJ, U.S. Attorney, S. Dist. of Florida, 29 FLRA 3, 9 (1987) (Provision 3); see AFGE Local 2024 and Dept. of Navy, Portsmouth Naval Shipyard, 37 FLRA 249, 251-52 (1990) (discussed earlier in this chapter, "Maternity, Paternity, and Family Leave"). Proposals concerning time off for religious holidays were considered as to VA employees in NFFE, Council of VA Locals and VA, 31 FLRA 360, 380 (1988) (Proposal 6). A requirement that management grant administrative leave or other accommodations for religious observances was not negotiable. It did not consider management's needs for the employee's services and infringed on the right to assign work; it was inconsistent with 42 USC 2000e(j), defining reasonable accommodation in terms of undue burden upon the employer. AFGE Local 2263 and Dept. of Air Force, Hq., 1606th Air Base Wing (MAC), Kirtland AFB, 15 FLRA 580, 584-85 (1984).

Addressed in AFGE Local 2024 and Dept. of Navy, Portsmouth Naval Shipyard, 37 FLRA 249, 253 (1990), was the propriety for negotiations of a provision (Proposal 1) that the employer grant leave requests, unless the leave would prohibit the employer from accomplishing a critical job, if the leave requested was for a religious holiday of the employee's faith, in the case of the death of a member of the employee's immediate family, or if the employee was a delegate to a labor organization convention or union meeting. The proposal interfered with the right of management to assign work by requiring a grant of leave. The proposal was not an appropriate arrangement:

...The proposal...would establish a single, very strict criterion that would be determinative of whether management could exercise its right to assign work in the face of a leave request falling under this proposal. The express wording of the proposal allows denial only for "accomplishing a critical job." As previously indicated, the Union's statement that the proposal is intended to allow management to exercise discretion in deciding whether to approve leave requests is inconsistent with the wording of the proposal and is not persuasive.

The extent to which the denial by management of an employee's request for leave, as would be covered under the proposal, would adversely affect the employee would depend on the particular employee's circumstances. The effect could be significant in some cases and minimal in others. The proposal, however, does not take account of such inevitable variations and would uniformly apply the proposal's stringent restriction of the Agency's ability to assign work in all instances, even if the adverse effects on the employee were, in fact, minimal. On balance, we find that the Union has not shown that this proposal necessarily would benefit employees to an extent that would outweigh the proposal's negative impact on the Agency's right to assign work. Therefore, we find that Proposal 1 excessively interferes with management's right to assign work and does not constitute an appropriate arrangement....

Approved for negotiations were two proposals concerning leave for religious reasons in AFGE Nat'l INS Council and INS, 27 FLRA 467, 475-77 (1987). The first was that the employer make every effort to accommodate the practice of religious beliefs by individual employees consistent with the needs of the employer; the second was that the employer grant compensatory time off to an employee requesting the time off, although the employer could deny the request if it interfered with the ability of the agency to accomplish its mission:

Considering the disputed subsections together, we conclude that subsection D(1) does not mandate that management grant leave or compensatory time off at a particular time to an employee to accommodate the practice of religious beliefs. Rather the language "make every effort"...is limited by the standard...which states that leave may be disapproved if the requested change would interfere with the ability of the organization to efficiently accomplish its mission. Moreover, the language...refers to the "needs of the Service."...

Also, contrary to the Agency's assertions, we see no conflict between the standard in the disputed provision and the "undue hardship" test in 42 U.S.C. § 2000e(j), the Civil Rights Act of 1964. The latter standard requires employers, including Federal agencies, to "reasonably accommodate to an employee's...religious observance or practice without undue hardship on the conduct of the employer's business." In addition, in 1978 Congress required that OPM establish regulations providing for compensatory time off for religious observances. See 5 U.S.C. § 5550a. The standard in subsection D(2) is essentially the same as that established by OPM in 5 C.F.R. § 550.1002(b) which states as follows:

To the extent that such modifications in work schedules do not interfere with the efficient accomplishment of an agency's mission, the agency shall in each instance afford the employee the opportunity to work compensatory overtime and shall in each instance grant compensatory time off to an employee requesting such time off for religious observations when the employee's personal religious beliefs require that the employee abstain from work during certain periods of the workday or workweek. [Emphasis added.]

In our view, the Government-wide regulation concerning work schedule changes to accommodate employees' personal religious beliefs applies the broader religious accommodation provision of the Civil Rights Act. Inasmuch as Provision 4 incorporates the standard found in a Government-wide regulation, the Agency's argument that the standard is inappropriate cannot be sustained.

...

Additionally, the Agency argues that the provision is nonnegotiable because it would subject management decisions regarding accommodation for religious observances to the grievance procedure and permit the substitution of an arbitrator's judgment for that of management. We reject this argument based on section 7103(a)(9)(C)(ii) of the Statute which defines a grievance to include "any claimed violation, misinterpretation, or misapplication of any law, rule, or regulation affecting conditions of employment." As discussed above, provision for a change in work schedules for religious observances and a standard to evaluate management's decisions on employees' requests is part of a Government-wide regulation. Therefore, regardless of whether the disputed language becomes part of the contract, a management decision concerning leave or a change in work schedule is a grievable matter under section 7103.

A proposal required the agency allow an employee to engage in compensatory overtime work for time lost as a result of religious requirements compelling abstention from work. Nonnegotiable, as inconsistent with regulation, was a proviso that the employer could only decline the arrangement for overtime work if the employee's presence on the job "is deemed absolutely essential and there is no suitable replacement." NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Office of Chief Counsel, IRS, 39 FLRA 27, 35-36 (1991) (Proposal 2). Negotiable was a proposal that compensatory time off for religious observances earned but not used within a set time would be converted to normal compensatory time off. No premium pay was involved, and no OPM regulation was violated by the proposal. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., 46 FLRA 696, 733-34 (1992) (Provision 17).
