broida flra Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations Chapter 06: Subjects of Bargaining: Specific Applications of Negotiability Determinations, Subchapter 18: Reductions in Force, Section C: Retention Standing; Retreat Rights, Subsection 01: Service Credit; Seniority; Performance Rating Levels

1. Service Credit; Seniority; Performance Rating Levels

A proposal was negotiable that management base first consideration of any reassignment or bumping right or separation involving more than one employee on skill requirements and total length of service. AFGE Local 987 and Hq., Warner Robins Air Force Logistics Command, Robins AFB, 8 FLRA 667, 673-75 (1982). But reversion or RIF retreat rights based on seniority were nonnegotiable in AFGE Local 1603 and Marine Corps Exch., Henderson Hall, Arlington, 12 FLRA 543, 543-44 (1983).

Proposals concerning the amount of seniority earned by various appraisal levels during a RIF were negotiable. Under OPM regulations there was discretion with the agency to provide from zero to less than four years of credit. Army Eng'r Ctr. and AFGE Local 1052, 13 FLRA 707, 712 (1984); see IFPTE, Local 4, and Army Engineer Research & Dev. Ctr., 71 FLRA 1135 (2020) (Member DuBester concurring) (proposal negotiable that RIF retention standing that by statute depended in large part on performance factors take into account long-term performance). But a proposal was nonnegotiable that required the agency to add four years of service credit for RIF purposes for each employee with an outstanding rating on any job element. Controlling OPM regulations, then at 5 CFR 351.504(c), required that additional credit be granted only on the basis of a summary rating of outstanding, not just an outstanding rating on a single element of that rating. AFGE Local 32 and OPM, 16 FLRA 948, 948-49 (1984). A union proposal establishing service credit of a certain number of years for outstanding performance was nonnegotiable. It was at variance with the credit directed by OPM regulations. NTEU and DHHS, 21 FLRA 428, 428-29 (1986); see NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Fin. Mgmt. Serv., 29 FLRA 422, 430, 447 (1987) (proposal was nonnegotiable that called for service credit in different amounts than specified by OPM RIF regulations governing computation of service credit based on performance appraisal levels). A proposal was not negotiable requiring, for RIF retention purposes, that credit for four additional years of service be given to an employee who received an overall rating of satisfactory and who was rated outstanding in any performance element. OPM regulations required an additional four years of credit for employees receiving the rating of outstanding or the highest appraisal under the agency system. 5 CFR 351.504 (1984). Another proposal requiring service credit awards for levels of ratings was rejected as nonnegotiable because it conflicted with OPM directives. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Fin. Mgmt. Serv., 29 FLRA 422, 430 (1987). OPM made clear through the late FPM that the service credit decision is to be made only on the basis of the summary appraisal of the employee's performance. A proposal that service credit be based on an overall rating of satisfactory for an employee who was rated as outstanding in any performance element was contrary to the government-wide regulation of OPM. AFGE Local 32 and OPM, 18 FLRA 93, 93-94 (1985). Also nonnegotiable, because of a conflict with OPM regulations, was a Provision 4, requiring management to use performance ratings without regard to when they were assigned. NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, Customs Serv., 46 FLRA 696, 709-11 (1992).

Nonnegotiable was Proposal 10 in AFGE Local 1547 and Dept. of Air Force, Luke AFB, 64 FLRA 813 (2010):

With respect to the abolishment of specific position job series, encumbered positions will be abolished based on employees' RIF Service Computation Dates (SCD); positions held by employees with the lowest RIF SCD will be abolished in inverse order.

Luke AFB explained the result that the proposal was contrary to OPM regulations governing retention standing and tenure groups and that the proposals did not fall into the category of a negotiable arrangement, id. at 814-16:

1. Proposal 10 is outside the duty to bargain because it is inconsistent with government-wide RIF regulations.

For the reasons that follow, we find that Proposal 10 is inconsistent with government-wide RIF regulations, and is therefore outside the duty to bargain pursuant to § 7117(a)(1) of the Statute.

Proposal 10 affects the RIF retention rights of Agency employees. Proposal 10 would require the Agency to make an initial selection of positions that would be subject to a RIF based only on employees' seniority, i.e., employees' SCD. Once these positions, encumbered by employees with the lowest SCDs, have been identified, regular RIF procedures under 5 C.F.R. §§ 351.501 and 351.502 would apply.[6]

[6] 5 C.F.R. § 351.501 concerns employees in the competitive service and 5 C.F.R. § 351.502 concerns employees in the excepted service.

RIF regulations set forth requirements for determining employees' RIF retention rights that differ from Proposal 10's requirements. An agency that is preparing to conduct a RIF affecting a particular job series must establish a retention register. Employees are listed in the retention register in the order of their relative retention standings. Employees' retention standings are determined based on the application of four retention factors listed in the regulations applied "in descending order." 5 C.F.R. §§ 351.501(a), 351.502(a). These factors are: 1) tenure group; 2) veterans' preference; 3) length of service; and 4) performance. Id. Furthermore, as the order of the factors indicates, the initial determination distinguishing among employees for retention purposes is based on employees' tenure group; e.g., whether an employee is a career employee not serving a probationary period, whether an employee is serving a probationary period because the employee was newly appointed, or whether the employee is serving under a term appointment. See 5 C.F.R. §§ 351.501(b) & 351.502(b).

As is apparent from the foregoing discussion, Proposal 10 would change how employees are initially distinguished for RIF retention purposes. Proposal 10 would make an employee's SCD the initial determinant of the employee's retention standing, rather than tenure group. Proposal 10 is therefore inconsistent with government-wide RIF regulations and is outside the duty to bargain under § 7117(a) of the Statute.

This conclusion is consistent with Authority precedent. Proposal 10 is similar to sentences two and three of Proposal 1 in Laborers' International Union of North America, AFL-CIO-CLC, Local 1267, 14 FLRA 686, 688 (1984) (Laborers' Int'l), which the Authority found to be outside the duty to bargain. In Laborers' Int'l, the Authority found that sentences two and three of the proposal were intended to govern RIF retention rights of employees because they required the agency, during a RIF, to offer vacant shifts to employees based only on seniority. The Authority held that this was inconsistent with RIF regulations [7] because the proposal barred consideration of all the factors affecting retention standing set forth in the regulations. Laborers' Int'l, 14 FLRA at 688. Proposal 10 has a substantially similar effect, establishing employee seniority as the primary factor in determining how a RIF will affect an employee. Based on the above, we find that Proposal 10 is not within the duty to bargain.

[7] The RIF at issue in Laborers' Int'l concerned employees in the competitive service. Therefore, while they were not specifically cited, the RIF regulations at issue in that case included the requirements set forth in 5 C.F.R. § 351.501.

2. Proposal 10 is outside the duty to bargain because it affects management's right to layoff under § 7106(a)(2)(A), and is not an appropriate arrangement under § 7106(b)(3) of the Statute.

In addition to the reasons discussed above, we also find that Proposal 10 is outside the duty to bargain because of its effect on management's rights.

a. Proposal 10 affects management's right to layoff under § 7106(a)(2)(A) of the Statute.

Management's right to layoff employees under § 7106(a)(2)(A) of the Statute includes the right to conduct a RIF and to exercise discretion in determining which positions will be abolished and which retained. See, e.g., AFGE, Local 1827, 58 FLRA 344, 345 (2003). As discussed in § III.C.1., above, Proposal 10 would prevent the Agency from conducting a RIF as prescribed in government-wide RIF regulations, and from determining which positions to abolish and which to retain. The proposal therefore affects management's right to layoff employees under 5 U.S.C. § 7106(a)(2)(A).

b. Proposal 10 is not an appropriate arrangement under § 7106(b)(3) of the Statute.

When a proposal affects a management right under § 7106(a) of the Statute, the proposal is nevertheless within the duty to bargain if it constitutes an appropriate arrangement under § 7106(b)(3) of the Statute. AFGE, Local 1156, 63 FLRA 340, 341 (2009). A proposal constitutes an appropriate arrangement if it is: (1) intended as an arrangement for employees adversely affected by the exercise of a management right; and (2) appropriate because it does not excessively interfere with the exercise of management's rights. E.g., NAGE, Local R14-87, 21 FLRA 24, 31 (1986) (KANG). The Authority determines whether a proposal excessively interferes with the exercise of a management right by weighing the benefits afforded employees under the proposed arrangement against the burden on the exercise of the right. See NTEU, 59 FLRA 978, 981 (2004).

Proposal 10 excessively interferes with management's right to layoff employees under § 7106(a)(2)(A). Focusing first on the proposal's benefits to unit employees, as described by the Union, the proposal is intended to benefit senior employees who might be affected by a RIF by requiring that less senior employees be RIFed first.

As is apparent, the proposal is not an unqualified benefit to unit employees. It is true that the proposal clearly would benefit some employees by protecting them from the adverse effects of a RIF. However, those benefits are offset in equal measure by the proposal's adverse effect on other employees whose positions the proposal would require the Agency to abolish pursuant to RIF procedures, in place of the positions of employees whom the proposal would protect.

With regard to the burden on management's rights, the proposal totally eliminates the Agency's discretion in exercising its right to layoff. The proposal would require the Agency to abolish positions of less senior employees even though the Agency might choose other positions to abolish after applying the other factors specified in §§ 351.501(a) and 351.502(a).

Weighing the benefits and burdens, we find that the proposal interferes excessively with management's right to layoff. The proposal's burden on management's rights, removing the Agency's discretion as to the positions to be abolished, outweighs the proposal's uncertain benefit to unit employees. Cf. Fed. Union of Scientists & Eng'rs, 22 FLRA 731, 734 (1986) (holding nonnegotiable a proposal that would have eliminated the agency's discretion to determine which positions to abolish in a RIF by requiring that part-time employees be RIFed before full-time employees). Accordingly, we conclude that Proposal 10 is not an appropriate arrangement and is outside the duty to bargain as contrary to § 7106(a)(2)(A) of the Statute.
