a. Premature Motion

A motion for counsel fees cannot be entertained or acted on by an AJ if it is filed before the final date of the initial decision. If the appellant (or agency) files a PFR, the initial decision does not become final until the PFR is decided; any fee motion submitted before the final date of the decision is premature. Motions filed before the final date of the decision may be dismissed without prejudice. Corey v. Dept. of Labor, 23 MSPR 581, 582 (1984); see Acker v. GSA, 14 MSPR 560, 561 (1983) (fee petition premature when a PFR was pending challenging the initial decision).

In unusual circumstances, the Board allowed an AJ's adjudication of a technically premature fee petition to stand. The petition was timely when filed, but became "premature" when the respondent was permitted to file an otherwise untimely PFR. Gonczi v. OPM, 41 MSPR 671, 674-76 (1989) (denying agency PFR). In the ordinary case of a premature fee petition, counsel must refile the fee petition at the proper time (allowing for the possibility that the premature fee petition is dismissed without prejudice to later refiling, with an automatic refiling date set by the AJ). But the Board may recognize (as when there is a PFR from denial of a fee motion as premature because of a pending PFR from an initial decision on the merits) intervening events and remand the fee petition to the regional office for adjudication. See Gorman v. OPM, 46 MSPR 542, 543 (1991); Chiodo v. OPM, 36 MSPR 479, 481 (1988) (AJ properly adjudicated a fee petition that was premature when filed; the appellant filed a "reapplication" for fees after the initial decision became final; additionally, the AJ stated that he would start processing the case immediately because the agency submitted written notice that it intended to comply with the initial decision); cf. Carson v. Dept. of Energy, 86 MSPR 192, 197 ¶ 10 (2000) (AJ erred by adjudicating a fee petition while a compliance initial decision was pending on a PFR; while the PFR of an addendum decision was pending, the Board denied the PFR on the compliance decision; the Board proceeded to adjudicate the fee dispute).

An AJ should not ordinarily delay adjudicating a fee petition when the appellant appeals to EEOC seeking to enlarge or change the relief granted by the Board. The pendency of the EEOC appeal does not make the fee petition premature, Baker v. USPS, 86 MSPR 349, 353-54 ¶¶ 11-12 (2000):

To arrive at an answer to this issue, we must weigh the benefits of administrative efficiency against the interests of fairness. On the one hand, it would save the administrative judge time if he were only required to determine the amount of attorney fees once. On the other hand, by dismissing the motion for attorney's fees, the administrative judge is delaying payment of these fees on the portion of the appeal that is final. In the present situation, it appears that delaying a determination on the attorney fees motion would not significantly decrease the amount of work for the administrative judge.

There is no dispute that the appellant is a prevailing party as to part of his constructive suspension claim. And, as we made clear above, the appellant is not a prevailing party in his restoration appeal. Assuming, without deciding, that the appellant also meets the second criterion of section 7701(g)--that an award of attorney fees is warranted in the interest of justice--the administrative judge will have to segregate the hours spent by the appellant's attorney on the restoration appeal from the hours spent on the adverse action appeal, regardless of the outcome of the appellant's Equal Employment Opportunity Commission appeal. Similarly, regardless of the outcome before the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, the administrative judge will have to determine the amount of attorney fees warranted for the constructive suspension claim since the appellant only prevailed as to a portion of it. Thus, regardless of the result of the appellant's appeal to the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, the administrative judge will need to make several determinations regarding the amount of attorney fees warranted, and most of such findings will not be affected by the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission's decision. If the appellant prevails before the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, then the administrative judge would award the remainder of the reasonable attorney fees in the adverse action appeal which did not relate to the constructive suspension. If, however, the appellant does not prevail, then the administrative judge would not award any further attorney fees. Thus, we believe that the interests of fairness outweigh the negligible administrative efficiency in this appeal, and we GRANT the appellant's petition for review and VACATE the initial decision.

If a counsel fee motion is dismissed as prematurely filed, and an untimely fee motion is then filed, the latter motion does not amend the earlier motion to make it timely. Even v. Dept. of Interior, 27 MSPR 567, 568 (1985). But the Federal Circuit determined that rejection of the counsel fee motion as untimely was unreasonable. The attorney earlier filed a nearly identical motion that became premature once the agency filed a PFR; the second motion was only a week late; counsel had a small office and the filing period fell on the day after Christmas, when staff was on vacation. Even v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1986 NP No. 85-2745). The Board maintains its original position, however, that a premature fee petition will not excuse a fee petition filed about 30 days late. Ballentine v. DOJ, 33 MSPR 28, 29-30 (1987), aff'd, Ballentine v. DOJ (Fed. Cir. 1988 NP No. 87-3407), followed in Sanders v. EEOC, 45 MSPR 693, 698 (1990). At least, however, a premature filing may be considered among the circumstances showing good cause for untimely filing. Brackins v. OPM, 66 MSPR 535, 541 (1995).
