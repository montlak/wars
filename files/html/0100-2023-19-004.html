2. Multiple Filing Options; Pleading Requirements; Timing of MSPB Appeal

The period for filing a mixed case appeal initiated through the EEO process is not the same as the deadline that applies to an appeal taken directly to the Board from an agency decision. An employee may initiate a mixed case directly with MSPB, through agency EEO procedures, or through arbitration procedures under a collective bargaining contract. See 29 CFR 1614.301, .302 (2022). Chapter 5 includes discussion of the procedures to be followed when the Board is requested to consider an EEO issue raised in arbitration. If the arbitration route is chosen, the appellant must petition for Board review of the resulting award before EEOC may accept jurisdiction over the discrimination issues. See Marenus v. DHHS, 39 MSPR 498, 502 (1989). [Refer in Chapter 2 to "Lack of Notice of Appeal Rights," and to Chapter 5 "Review of Arbitration Awards."]

As to appeals cases originating with the Board or through agency EEO complaints, Board regulations provide, 5 CFR 1201.154 (2022):

For purposes of this section, the date an appellant receives the agency's decision is determined according to the standard set forth at 1201.22(b)(3) of this part. Appellants who file appeals raising issues of prohibited discrimination in connection with a matter otherwise appealable to the Board must comply with the following time limits:

(a) Where the appellant has been subject to an action appealable to the Board, he or she may either file a timely complaint of discrimination with the agency or file an appeal with the Board no later than 30 days after the effective date, if any, of the action being appealed, or 30 days after the date of the appellant's receipt of the agency's decision on the appealable action, whichever is later.

(b) If the appellant has filed a timely formal complaint of discrimination with the agency:

(1) An appeal must be filed within 30 days after the appellant receives the agency resolution or final decision on the discrimination issue; or

(2) If the agency has not resolved the matter or issued a final decision on the formal complaint within 120 days, the appellant may appeal the matter directly to the Board at any time after the expiration of 120 calendar days. Once the agency resolves the matter or issues a final decision on the formal complaint, an appeal must be filed within 30 days after the appellant receives the agency resolution or final decision on the discrimination issue.

(c) If the appellant files an appeal prematurely under this subpart, the judge will dismiss the appeal without prejudice to its later refiling under § 1201.22 of this part. If holding the appeal for a short time would allow it to become timely, the judge may hold the appeal rather than dismiss it.

The regulations require a degree of specificity in pleading discrimination in an appeal. The appellant is supposed to state in his or her appeal that discrimination is an issue, 5 CFR 1201.153 (2022):

(a) Contents. An appeal raising issues of prohibited discrimination must comply with § 1201.24 of this part, with the following exceptions:

(1) The appeal must state that there was discrimination in connection with the matter appealed, and it must state specifically how the agency discriminated against the appellant; and

(2) The appeal must state whether the appellant has filed a grievance under a negotiated grievance procedure or a formal discrimination complaint with any agency regarding the matter being appealed to the Board. If he or she has done so, the appeal must state the date on which the appellant filed the complaint or grievance, and it must describe any action that the agency took in response to the complaint or grievance.

(b) Use of Board form or Internet filing option. An appellant may comply with paragraph (a) of this section by completing MSPB Form 185, or by completing all requests for information marked as required at the e-Appeal site (https://e-appeal.mspb.gov). MSPB Form 185 can be accessed at the Board's website (http://www.mspb.gov).

Bourbour v. NLRB, CH-0432-10-0192-I-1 (NP 5/5/2011), suggests that a discrimination claim should be plainly raised during an appeal:

In her petition for review, the appellant asserts that the administrative judge "wrongly concluded on discrimination issue, because [the appellant] did not file yet." At the hearing, the appellant testified that the agency did not like her because she "was not born here." As the administrative judge correctly noted in her initial decision, the appellant was given numerous opportunities prior to the hearing in this case to raise an affirmative defense of discrimination. Our review of the appellant's prehearing pleadings reveals that she failed to assert an express or implied claim of national origin or other types of unlawful discrimination. Nonetheless, in response to her petition for review, the Clerk of the Board issued the appellant a show-cause order to allow her an opportunity to state whether she was attempting to raise the issue of prohibited discrimination on the basis of national origin through her statement at the hearing and to show that she timely raised the issue after she learned of the basis for such an allegation. See Weslowski v. Department of the Army, 80 M.S.P.R. 585, ¶ 12 (1999) (generally, an appellant may raise an allegation of discrimination at any time during the Board's consideration of the appeal of the agency's action, including on petition for review, if the appellant did not know of the existence of a basis for the allegation at the time that the petition for appeal was filed).

The appellant's response to the show-cause order shows that she was raising the issue of national origin discrimination. It also shows, however, that she was aware of the basis of that allegation prior to her removal and that she was informed that she must raise the issue when she filed her appeal. Thus, the Board finds that the appellant's allegation of national origin discrimination was untimely filed. See Weslowski, 80 M.S.P.R. 585, ¶ 12.

If discrimination is not raised when the appeal is first filed, the EEO allegation may be raised during the subsequent development of the case. The regulations provide that issues of discrimination may be raised after the appeal has been initiated. 5 CFR 1201.24(b) (2022), the regulation governing all appeals, explains:

An appellant may raise a claim or defense not included in the appeal at any time before the end of the conference(s) held to define the issues in the case. An appellant may not raise a new claim or defense after that time, except for good cause shown. However, a claim or defense not included in the appeal may be excluded if a party shows that including it would result in undue prejudice.

5 CFR 1201.156(b) (2022) adds:

Issue not raised in appeal. When an appellant has not alleged prohibited discrimination in the appeal, but has raised the issue later in the proceeding, the judge will decide both the issue of discrimination and the appealable action within 120 days after the issue is raised.

The Federal Circuit recognized but avoided a regulatory ambiguity in 5 CFR 1201.154 (2022) that was unsuccessfully argued to permit a filing with the Board at any time, with no limitation, once an EEO complaint had been pending with the agency for 120 days and a final agency decision was issued after that 120-day period. Paine v. MSPB, 467 F.3d 1344 (Fed. Cir. 2006).

If the appellant initiates a mixed-case complaint with the agency through the EEO process, a notice from the agency that the matter is not a mixed case does not trigger the period for filing a Board appeal. The appellant must await the issuance of a FAD and then file within 30 days or, if no FAD is received earlier, the appellant may file with the Board at any time after 120 days. The complex analysis leading to that result was explained and should be reviewed in Miranne v. Dept. of Navy, 121 MSPR 235, 242 ¶ 13, 2014 MSPB 42 (2014) ("Our review of this process leads us to conclude that an appellant is precluded from filing a Board appeal challenging the same matter that is already the subject of a timely-filed discrimination complaint with an agency prior to either the issuance of the agency's FAD or the passage of 120 days.").

AJs are informed that the legislative history of the Reform Act makes it clear that the Board is to be liberal in receiving discrimination allegations. (44 Fed. Reg. 38348 (1979)). An appellant may raise an allegation of discrimination at any time before the Board if the appellant did not know of the existence of a basis for the allegation of discrimination when the appeal was filed. Perez v. Dept. of Army, 32 MSPR 575, 577 (1987). But if an appellant does not raise a contention of racial discrimination in his appeal, does not argue it before the AJ, does not adduce evidence in support of it, and in a PFR does not assert that he had no knowledge of the basis for the contention prior to filing his appeal, discrimination issues will not be considered in the PFR. Vires v. Dept. of Navy, 38 MSPR 569, 573 (1988) (allegation was rejected that was raised after pleadings were closed and after the appellant filed a PFR; there was no demonstration that the appellant did not know of the existence of the basis for the allegation when he filed his appeal); Carter v. FAA, 18 MSPR 345, 346 (1983) ("In this case, appellant's contention of racial discrimination concerns an incident occurring on July 31, 1981. However, appellant did not raise the contention in his petition for appeal, did not argue it before the presiding official, nor adduce any evidence in support thereof, and has not asserted that he had no knowledge of the basis for the contention prior to filing his appeal. Therefore, the Board will not consider this issue for the first time on review. See Brown v. Department of Transportation, 15 M.S.P.R. 617 (1983); Thomas v. Department of Commerce, 10 MSPB 335 (1982); Shepherd v. U.S. Postal Service, 5 MSPB 55 (1981)."). [Refer to Chapter 5, "Review of New Evidence," concerning petitions for review.] Raising discrimination is not difficult, for the appellant need only check the box on the Board's appeal form that asks if he has been discriminated against. See O'Reilly v. CSA, 8 MSPR 68, 69 (1981). The Board's regulation on the processing of mixed cases was upheld, and its application was subjected to the usual "arbitrary and capricious" judicial review standard in Ballentine v. MSPB, 738 F.2d 1244 (Fed. Cir. 1984).

Mixed cases may proceed to an EEOC administrative judge who, recognizing that the hearing is within the province of MSPB, dismisses the EEOC hearing request and sends the case back to the agency for issuance of an EEO final agency decision and notice of Board appeal rights. The procedure was alluded to in Goodwin v. Dept. of Transp., 106 MSPR 520, 524 n.5, 2007 MSPB 207 (2007):

It appears from this record that the agency deviated from the EEOC regulations by issuing a final agency decision that adopted the EEOC AJ's dismissal of the complaint without issuing a final agency decision analyzing the merits of the appellant's claims in accordance with 29 C.F.R. § 1614.110. The EEOC AJ dismissed the complaint from the EEOC federal sector process because EEOC AJ's have no jurisdiction to conduct hearings regarding mixed-case complaints, but such a dismissal does not relieve the agency of its obligation to issue a final agency decision with respect to the complainant's discrimination claims, see, e.g., Lang v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 219 F.3d 1345, 1347 (Fed. Cir. 2000) (noting that mixed-case EEO complaints do not receive a hearing before an EEOC AJ); Tyler v. Gonzalez, EEOC Petition No. 0320070027 (June 1, 2007) (noting that an EEOC AJ dismissed the matter as a mixed-case complaint and remanded the case back to the agency for a final decision with appeal rights to the Board).
