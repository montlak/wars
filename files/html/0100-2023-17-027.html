d. Detail

An individual does not have RIF appeal rights involving reduction in grade if he is detailed to a lower-graded position in a different competitive level. There is no reduction in pay and no change in the official position assignment. Chance v. FAA, 5 MSPR 277 (1981); see Yerke v. USPS, 70 Fed. Appx. 536, 538 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP) (temporary assignment to a less desirable position does not qualify as a demotion under 5 CFR 210.102(b)(4) and a RIF within the Board's jurisdiction since it does not involve the change of an employee to a lower grade or to a position with a lower rate of pay); Scorcia v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 95-3059) ("An agency detail of an employee, without reduction in grade or pay, is not by itself sufficient to trigger the RIF protections of 5 CFR Part 351. An agency is only required to use RIF procedures 'when it releases a competing employee from his or her competitive level by furlough for more than 30 days, [or upon] separation, demotion, or reassignment requiring displacement' or when the release is required due to a reorganization. 5 CFR 351.201(a)(2)."); Brown v. Dept. of Navy, 68 MSPR 490, 491 (1995) (RIF was not created by a detail that resulted in no reduction in grade or pay); Facer v. Dept. of Energy, 24 MSPR 404, 406 (1984) (the employee only has rights incident to the position held by official appointment, whether or not the employee is performing another job in an acting capacity); cf. Paul v. Dept. of Navy, 80 MSPR 174, 178 ¶ 8 (1998) ("An appealable RIF demotion occurs when an employee is released from his or her competitive level during a reorganization and assigned to a position carrying a lower grade or rate of pay."); Chleapas v. DHEW, 1 MSPR 479, 480, 1 MSPB 464 (1980) (detail involving no reduction in pay did not change the appellant's official position assignment; it did not provide an appeal based on loss of grade).

Details occurring as a result of or during a postal reorganization did not constitute RIFs, Dixon v. USPS, 64 MSPR 445, 450-51 (1994):

An agency is only required to use RIF procedures, however, when it releases a competing employee from his competitive level by furlough of more than 30 days, separation, demotion, or reassignment requiring displacement, when the release is required due to a reorganization. 5 CFR 351.201(a)(2); Thomas v. United States, 709 F.2d 48, 50 (Fed. Cir. 1983). A detail by its very nature is temporary and involves no formal appointment since the employee continues to be the incumbent of the position from which he was detailed. See Frankel v. Dept. of Education, 17 MSPR 453, 455 (1983). Therefore, since the agency only detailed the appellants, they were not entitled to the substantive protections of Part 351.

Furthermore, the appellant in Brown was permanently reassigned to his position following the agency's restructuring. See Brown, 58 MSPR at 347. Thus, the Board based its finding that his assignment constituted a demotion on the fact that the appellant's new assignment was permanent. Here, the appellants have been only detailed, i.e., temporarily assigned to either agency Placement Centers or other positions within the agency. Moreover, since the record indicates that the agency still intends to permanently reassign the appellants, there is no basis to conclude that the appellants' details constituted constructive permanent reassignments.

The appellants next argue that they were constructively--if not in fact--separated from their permanent positions, and that such separations are appealable as RIF actions under 5 CFR 351.901. In support of their argument, they rely on Jones v. Department of the Army, 42 MSPR 680 (1990).

The appellants' reliance on Jones, however, is misplaced. In Jones, the Board found that an agency conducting a RIF is required to grant a preference eligible employee any assignment rights that he had to permanent positions, even if such positions were classified at a lower grade, before offering him a temporary vacant position, notwithstanding that the employee could retain his tenure status as a career employee for the duration of his temporary position. See Jones, 42 MSPR at 687-690. The Board further found that delaying the determination of the appellant's assignment rights until the temporary position expired had the significant potential to denigrate or extinguish the retention rights to which he was entitled, as a preference eligible, under the Veterans Preference Act. Id. Thus, the Board concluded that the appellant would be at a disadvantage compared to those agency employees subjected to the RIF who were not assigned to temporary positions.

In this case, however, the agency did not implement the RIF regulations of Part 351. The appellants' retention rights, therefore, have not been denigrated compared to those of other employees because the agency has not determined the retention rights of anyone affected by the restructuring. Consequently, Jones is inapposite to the facts in this appeal.
