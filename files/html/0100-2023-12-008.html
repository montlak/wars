broida mspb Chapter 05: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 05: Initial Decision and Petition for Review, Subchapter 01: Initial Decision, Section B: Required Analysis, Subsection 01: Incomplete Analysis; Failure to Mention All Evidence

1. Incomplete Analysis; Failure to Mention All Evidence

Addison v. Dept. of Treasury, 30 MSPR 615, 617-18 (1986), reiterated that the job of an attorney-examiner employed as an AJ is to analyze issues and facts and write in a fashion that reflects a cogent and complete analysis of each charge and of the determinative evidence:

An initial decision must identify all material issues of fact and law, summarize the evidence, resolve issues of credibility, and include the presiding official's conclusions of law. Spithaler.... The presiding official relegated his discussion of the substantial evidence issue to a single footnote in his initial decision. Moreover, even though he stated that "the notice of proposed removal identifies numerous specifications of alleged performance deficiencies under each critical element," the presiding official failed to specifically address the manner in which those alleged deficiencies were established by the agency or how he ultimately came to conclude that they were sufficient to meet the agency's substantial evidence burden of proof. The presiding official also failed to state whether he found the testimony of appellant's former supervisor...to be credible with regard to the alleged performance deficiencies even though he found her testimony, with regard to personal animus and reprisal, to be incredible.... While the Board must necessarily defer to the presiding official's credibility determinations, especially where those findings are based on the demeanor of the witnesses, the presiding official must nevertheless fully set forth the reasons for his findings in the initial decision in order for the Board to defer to such findings....

Dodson v. Dept. of Navy, 33 MSPR 386, 391 (1987), admonished as to the essential qualities of initial decisions, as it remanded an initial decision as a result of inadequate analysis:

The agency's notice of proposed removal consisted of four charges comprised of eleven separate specifications, each based upon a distinct factual predicate. In her initial decision, however, the administrative judge does not state the specifications, identify all material issues of fact, or summarize the evidence on each issue sufficiently to disclose the evidentiary basis for her conclusion that the agency's charges were sustained. She also fails to state the legal reasons and authorities upon which she bases her conclusions of law.

In her petition for review, the appellant identifies numerous alleged inconsistencies in the agency's evidence and the testimony of its witnesses on several of the charges and specifications, with citations to the record. While we do not here express any opinion on the merits of those allegations, the administrative judge failed to address them and thus to afford us an explained and reasoned adjudication to review....

Only a stipulation between the parties will satisfy a party's burden of proof on a matter; otherwise, the AJ must issue a decision containing findings of facts and the reasons or bases for those findings on all material issues. Hamilton v. VA, 9 MSPR 476, 478 (1982). It is within the discretion of the AJ, as the trier of fact, to make determinations on the materiality of evidence and to weigh the probative value of evidentiary submissions. See Eikenberry v. Dept. of Interior, 37 MSPR 438, 445 (1988). Those determinations must be explained. The initial decision should not be conclusory; it should summarize the evidence submitted by the parties and explain the reasoning used in arriving at legal findings. Condaxis v. Dept. of Commerce, 7 MSPR 467, 469, 7 MSPB 331 (1981); see Colbert v. USPS, 93 MSPR 467, 470 ¶ 6 (2003) ("The AJ reduced more than a box of documents and over 350 pages of testimony to four double-spaced pages of facts and analysis. Although brevity can be good, the ID left us asking how the agency could have failed to prove that the appellant's undisputed conduct...was actionable misconduct."); Special Counsel v. Eubanks, 76 MSPR 405, 413-14, 418 (1997) (faulting an ALJ decision because it failed to summarize and consider all relevant evidence under each specification; the Board must have the "benefit of the ALJ's determinations of fact, reasoning and assessment of the evidence"; requirements governing initial (or recommended) decisions apply whether the case is brought under the Board's original jurisdiction (OSC complaint) or under its appellate jurisdiction); George v. Dept. of Air Force, 24 MSPR 269, 271 (1984) (inappropriate for an AJ to omit the findings as to whether the charged conduct occurred, to assume the charges to be true, and to determine that the charged conduct would not support a removal); Pledger v. Dept. of Navy, 34 MSPR 456, 458 (1987) (noting lack of adequate findings); Pittman v. OPM, 2 MSPR 546, 550, 3 MSPB 68 (1980) (inadequate analysis by the AJ); cf. Aqua Products, Inc. v. Matal, 872 F.3d 1290, 1325 (Fed. Cir. 2017) ("an agency's refusal to consider evidence bearing on the issue before it is, by definition, arbitrary and capricious").

The necessity for reasoned analysis by the AJ on dispositive points of the appeal, including the propriety or mitigation of a penalty, was emphasized in Fox v. USPS, 81 MSPR 522, 530-31 ¶¶ 19-21 (1999):

As set forth above, an initial decision must identify all material issues of fact and law, summarize the evidence, resolve issues of credibility, and include the administrative judge's conclusions of law and legal reasoning, as well as the authorities on which that reasoning rests. Spithaler, 1 MSPR at 589; see 5 CFR § 1201.111(b)(1) (each initial decision shall contain "[f]indings of fact and conclusions of law upon all the material issues of fact and law presented on the record," as well as "[t]he reasons or bases for those findings and conclusions."). Here, after addressing the appellant's disparate penalty claim, the initial decision merely finds that "the agency considered the relevant factors and imposed a penalty that is within the realm of reasonableness." See Douglas v. Veterans Administration, 5 MSPR 280, 306 (1981). There is no summary of the evidence relating to the reasonableness of the penalty, nor any discussion of the relevant Douglas factors and why the agency should be deemed to have exercised its discretion within the tolerable limits of reasonableness by demoting the appellant to a PS-3 position.

An initial decision becomes in many cases the final decision of the Board. Consequently, such a decision is required to constitute an "adjudication," 5 USC § 1204(a)(1), which must be articulated in a reasoned opinion providing an adequate basis for review by a Court of Appeals. This is essential to enable the parties and any reviewing court to determine the factual basis for the Board's decision and to ascertain whether the Board considered all relevant factors or made any error of judgment. Spithaler, 1 MSPR at 588.

Here, we cannot determine from the initial decision whether the administrative judge considered all relevant factors or made an error of judgment with regard to the agency's choice of penalty. The initial decision, therefore, does not meet the Spithaler standard, and must be remanded. See Lassiter v. Department of Justice, 60 MSPR 138, 148 (1993) (where the appellant argued that the agency should have mitigated its penalty by reassigning him to an administrative position, and the administrative judge did not address this contention, the Board found error under Spithaler); Wellman v. Department of the Navy, 49 MSPR 149, 152-53 (1991) (the administrative judge's failure to consider the appellant's medical condition in determining the appropriateness of the penalty was error under Spithaler); Angelo v. U.S. Postal Service, 52 MSPR 664, 668 (1992) (remanding the appeal where the administrative judge failed to address all relevant mitigating factors).

See Smith v. VA, 93 MSPR 424, 426 ¶ 4 (2003) ("However, because the administrative judge's findings here were abbreviated (limited to about a page), incomplete, inconsistent with the great weight of the evidence, and lead to an inherent improbable result, they are not entitled to deference."); Angelo v. USPS, 52 MSPR 664, 668 (1992) (remanded because the AJ failed to consider and address alleged mitigating factors of disparate treatment and bias of the agency against the appellant because of "a vendetta against his attorney"); cf. Campbell v. Dept. of Air Force, 72 MSPR 480, 485 (1996) ("[A] genuine factual dispute may arise where, as here, only one party offers evidence on factual issues in question, and where a reasonable factfinder could draw more than one factual inference from the evidence.").

The appellant is not the only party entitled to a complete decision. The Board will set aside a decision favoring an appellant if the AJ did not completely analyze all the charges against him. Burton v. VA, 35 MSPR 52, 54 (1987). Failure to treat issues and identify the decisional standard resulted in remand of a AJ's decision in Edgeston v. USPS, 1 MSPR 300, 301, 1 MSPB 289 (1980). But the Board did not fault an AJ on the basis that his decision contained language similar to that of other decisions considering the dismissals of air traffic controllers following their strike against the government. There was no demonstration that the AJ failed to consider the appeal on its own merits. See Bailey v. Dept. of Transp., 18 MSPR 266, 269 n.6 (1983).

Although initial decisions should not be conclusory, AJs are not required to mention all of the evidence of record. Pifer v. USPS, 31 MSPR 395, 396 n.* (1986). That an AJ does not develop every facet of the record does not render the initial decision incomplete. Colbert v. USPS, 93 MSPR 467, 471 ¶ 11 (2003) (noting that "[a]lthough a remand might result in a stronger ID, it would not change the outcome, because the record so strongly supports the AJ's conclusion."). Under Smith v. OPM, 31 MSPR 406, 410 (1986), following Marques v. DHHS, 22 MSPR 129, 132 (1984), that an AJ did not mention in her initial decision all of the extensive evidence adduced in testimony, or respond to every theory and speculation presented by appellant, did not mean that she failed to consider the evidence in reaching her decision, or that she was biased against the appellant. See Harris v. SEC, 972 F.3d 1307, 1321 (Fed. Cir. 2020) (Chapter 43 performance case: "just because the Administrative Judge did not discuss all of these pre-PIP events does not mean he did not consider them in reaching his decision"); Schiller v. DOD, PH-0432-13-0143-I-1 (NP 1/28/2015) (Chapter 43 performance case: "The appellant offers no authority for the proposition that an initial decision must address each individual alleged fact in the voluminous written and hearing record, and ignores the instruction of Spithaler to 'summarize' the evidence. See Spithaler, 1 M.S.P.R. at 589."); Fouquet v. USDA, 82 MSPR 548, 553 ¶ 13 (1999) ("[T]he fact that the administrative judge found Frost not to be a credible witness, but did not specifically explain her finding, does not establish that she did not consider evidence bearing on Frost's credibility."); Kirkpatrick v. USPS, 74 MSPR 583, 589 (1997) (the AJ was not required to specifically address every point raised by the appellant); Spates v. USPS, 68 MSPR 9, 12 (1995) ("[T]he administrative judge was not required to mention all of the testimony, and the fact that he did not do so does not mean that he did not consider all of it.... Moreover, the appellant has pointed to no testimony that he believes that the administrative judge failed to consider that would indicate prejudice to his substantive rights."); Taylor v. Dept. of Army, 44 MSPR 471, 475 (1990) ("that the administrative judge did not specifically mention those documents does not mean that he did not consider them"); Stephenson v. Dept. of Air Force, 40 MSPR 431, 434 (1989) ("That she did not mention this specific testimony of the appellant does not mean she did not consider it."); Johnson v. Dept. of Air Force, 37 MSPR 659, 664 n.6 (1988) (initial decision need not respond "to each theory or speculation advanced by the employee"); Gallo v. USPS, 35 MSPR 507, 514 (1987), following Marques, supra; Diaz v. Dept. of Navy, 25 MSPR 265, 266 (1984) (failure of the AJ to cite the brief did not mean he failed to consider it; if the AJ did err by not citing the brief, the error was harmless because the appellant made no argument in the brief warranting a different result); O'Kane v. Dept. of Air Force, 23 MSPR 25, 28 (1984) (AJ may fairly characterize the evidence rather than provide a precise transcript quotation); Evans v. GSA, 20 MSPR 401, 402 (1984) (no prejudicial error when the AJ failed to make a finding of whether the appellant grabbed a security guard once or twice; the charge of bad judgment and behavior was sustained).

The AJ need not discuss every piece of evidence in order to show that it was considered in the litigation of the entire issue before the AJ. Imbrogno v. DLA (Fed. Cir. 1989 NP No. 88-3280); see Vanda Pharm. v. Teva Pharm., ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2023 NP) ("However, '[t]he fact that the district court did not in its opinion recite every piece of evidence does not mean that the evidence was not considered.' Plant Genetic Sys., N.V. v. DeKalb Genetics Corp., 315 F.3d 1335, 1343 (Fed. Cir. 2003) (citation omitted)."); Bennett v. Dept. 88-3280).of Interior, DE-0752-12-0183-I-3 (NP 1/20/2023) ("An administrative judge's failure to mention all of the evidence of record does not mean that she did not consider it in reaching her decision. See Mithen v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 122 M.S.P.R. 489, ¶ 14 (2015), aff'd, 652 F. App'x 971 (Fed. Cir. 2016) (Table). This is especially true in this case, given the extensive written record and 10-day hearing."). Minor errors are not going to require a remand. Typographical errors in a decision would not ordinarily demonstrate harmful error or bias. Polonchak v. USPS, 52 MSPR 405, 408 (1992). But a clear mistake on a material factual point that may influence the outcome of the case may require remand for clarification of the disputed point and any necessary redetermination of the outcome. See Essman v. Dept. of Transp., 71 MSPR 107, 109-10 (1996) (AJ misconstrued the date of a letter that conveyed information relied on in the disposition of a constructive adverse action appeal); cf. Rodriguez v. Dept. of Treasury, AT-0752-12-0078-I-1 (NP 6/12/2013) ("The Board has held that an administrative judge's failure to mention all of the evidence of record does not mean that she did not consider it in reaching her decision. Marques v. Department of Health & Human Services, 22 M.S.P.R. 129, 132 (1984), aff'd, 776 F.2d 1062 (Fed. Cir. 1985) (Table), cert. denied, 476 U.S. 1141 (1986). Here, however, the administrative judge's failure to mention Mr. Berkowitz's sworn statement strongly indicates that she did not take it into consideration in making her credibility determinations as to whether he told the appellant that she would be terminated if she did not accept the reassignment offer.").

The Federal Circuit provided a glimpse of the type of factfinding it expects from the Board in a performance case, Mortensen v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1990 NP No. 89-3213):

In her brief, Mortensen generally argues that the Board "failed to consider" the testimony of her witnesses and her own testimony. As we have said previously, merely because the fact finder does not specifically mention a particular matter in its opinion forms no basis for an assumption that it did not consider those elements. Perkin-Elmer Corp. v. Computervision Corp., 732 F.2d 888, 901 (Fed. Cir....). There is more than ample evidence in the record to support the Board's conclusion; clearly the Board's findings were not "unsupported by substantial evidence."

Mount v. USPS (Fed. Cir. 1995 NP No. 94-3387), added:

It is true that the administrative judge's opinion did not explicitly recite all the evidence favorable to Mr. Mount. That fact, however, does not mean that the fact finder failed to consider such evidence. Smith v. OPM, 31 MSPR 406, 410 (1986); Brown v. Dept. of the Navy, 25 MSPR 300, 303 (1984); see Perkin-Elmer Corp. v. Computervision Corp., 732 F.2d 888, 901 (Fed. Cir.), cert. denied, 469 U.S. 857 (1984). There is no need to refer to specific testimony in the initial decision, Coombs v. USPS, 41 MSPR 604, 610 (1989), nor to catalog all the evidence of record. Pifer v. USPS, 31 MSPR 395, 396 n.* (1986); see Marques v. DHHS, 22 MSPR 129 (1984), aff'd mem., 776 F.2d 1062 (Fed. Cir. 1985), cert. denied, 476 U.S. 1141 (1986).

The AJ's failure to specifically mention all of the evidence of record, or all of the credibility factors enumerated in Hillen v. Dept. of Army, 35 MSPR 453 (1987), discussed below, does not mean that she did not consider relevant evidence or ignored pertinent Hillen factors resolving credibility issues in reaching her decision. Cohen v. GSA, 53 MSPR 492, 497 (1992);See Howard v. Dept. of Transp., 511 Fed. Appx. 984, 987 n.5 (Fed. Cir. 2013 NP) ("Additionally, merely because a decision does not mention a particular point 'forms no basis for an assumption that it did not consider those elements.' Windsurfing Int'l Inc. v. AMF, Inc., 782 F.2d 995, 998-99 (Fed. Cir. 1986); see also Perkin-Elmer Corp. v. Computervision Corp., 732 F.2d 888, 901 (Fed. Cir. 1984) (holding that when 'a court does not discuss certain propositions,' that 'does not make the decision inadequate or suggest the court failed to understand them')."); Christensen v. USPS, 51 MSPR 681, 685 (1991) ("Even though the administrative judge did not assess the credibility of all of the witnesses, that fact alone does not establish that she did not consider their testimony in reaching her decision, and the appellant has not shown reversible error in this regard."); Catalano v. USPS, 23 MSPR 432, 436 (1984) (no requirement that AJ refer to specific testimony in an initial decision).

Yet standards must be maintained. Although the failure of an AJ to mention a particular piece of evidence does not mean that it was not considered, an AJ's finding contrary to a significant piece of evidence that has a direct bearing on the case, without mentioning it, raises a question as to its consideration. See Barrett v. Dept. of Interior, 54 MSPR 356, 362 (1992); Blyther v. USPS, 112 MSPR 537, 541-43 ¶¶ 8, 12, 2009 MSPB 215 (2009) (although the initial decision need not mention all of the evidence of record concerning EEO and reprisal issues, the Board remanded for further analysis when the initial decision included "little explanation of the bases for the AJ's conclusion that the appellant had not substantiated her discrimination claims," and the decision also contained no specific description of the appellant's alleged performance deficiencies); Wallace and Martin v. Dept. of Commerce, 106 MSPR 23, 35 ¶ 16, 2007 MSPB 141 (2007) ("However, the ID completely ignored Wellington's testimony that she was present at the time of the conversation in question and that her testimony was both consistent with Wallace's version and contradicted Grace's version of the conversation. While it is possible that the AJ considered the conflicting evidence in concluding that Grace was more credible than Wallace with respect to their conversation, "[his] finding contrary to a significant piece of evidence that has a direct bearing on the case, without mentioning it, raises a question as to its consideration."").

The Federal Circuit remanded a whistleblower's case because of incomplete factual analysis by the AJ, Potter v. VA, ___Fed. Appx.___ (Fed. Cir. 2021 NP):

In Potter I, Tiffany Potter petitioned this court for review of the Board's December 13, 2018 decision denying corrective action in her claim filed under the Whistleblower Protection Act. The Board determined that Ms. Potter failed to show a prima facie case that her July 10, 2014 email was a contributing factor to her nonselection by the Department of Veterans Affairs ("agency") for Chief Nurse IV in November 2015. See Potter I, 949 F.3d at 1379. We vacated that portion of the 2018 decision because it relied on a finding that the parties agreed was erroneous--i.e., that Dr. Deering (who canceled the Chief Nurse IV vacancy in November 2015) did not know of Ms. Potter's July 10, 2014 email. Id. at 1379-81. We remanded with instructions to the Board to "consider whether, in view of Dr. Deering's knowledge of Ms. Potter's July 10, 2014 email, Ms. Potter presented evidence sufficient to satisfy the knowledge-timing test [of 5 U.S.C. § 1221(e)(1)], or if Ms. Potter otherwise presented evidence sufficient to demonstrate a prima facie case of whistleblower reprisal," and if so, to "consider whether the [agency] [could] meet its burden of showing that it would have taken the same November 2015 personnel action regardless of Ms. Potter's protected disclosure." Id. at 1380.

On remand, the AJ found that a preponderance of the evidence established that the July 10, 2014 email was a protected disclosure and that it was a contributing factor to Ms. Potter's November 2015 nonselection. The AJ then considered whether the agency demonstrated by clear and convincing evidence that it would have taken the same November 2015 personnel action in the absence of the July 10, 2014 protected disclosure. The AJ's consideration in this regard was guided by the factors articulated in Carr v. Social Security Administration, 185 F.3d 1318, 1323 (Fed. Cir. 1999)--namely, (1) the strength of the agency's evidence in support of its personnel action; (2) the existence and strength of any motive to retaliate on the part of the agency officials who were involved in the decision; and (3) any evidence that the agency takes similar actions against employees who are not whistleblowers but who are otherwise similarly situated. J.A. 8-16 (assessing these three Carr factors). The AJ concluded that the first Carr factor weighed "heavily" in the agency's favor, that the second Carr factor weighed "modestly" in Ms. Potter's favor, and that the third Carr factor was neutral. As a result of its Carr analysis, the Board found that the agency demonstrated by clear and convincing evidence that it would have taken the same November 2015 personnel action in the absence of the July 10, 2014 protected disclosure. It therefore denied corrective action.

...

When addressing whether the government has shown that it would have taken the same personnel action absent a protected disclosure, "[i]f considerable countervailing evidence is manifestly ignored or disregarded in finding [the] matter clearly and convincingly proven, the decision must be vacated and remanded for further consideration where all the pertinent evidence is weighed." Whitmore v. Dep't of Labor, 680 F.3d 1353, 1368 (Fed. Cir. 2012). Ms. Potter argues that in conducting its Carr analysis, the Board's decision failed to consider certain evidence that may have detracted from its overall conclusion. We agree.

Ms. Potter observes that Dr. Deering had to testify before Congress about the patient-care crisis at the Phoenix VA Health Care System, whereupon he was asked why he hadn't been fired. She notes that he had received death threats. And she cites his testimony that he was "feeling frustrated because people were going to the [Inspector General]," and that he "just wished they would come to [him] and say here's what it is so we could tackle it together." According to Ms. Potter, this and other evidence not addressed in the Board's decision detract from the reasons the agency gave for its November 2015 nonselection and indicate the presence of a retaliatory motive.

The agency disputes the import of the evidence that Ms. Potter cites. But the extent to which such evidence affects a Carr analysis involves factual questions for the Board--not this court in the first instance. And, as to evidence that the Board's decision failed to evaluate (including that set forth above), we cannot be assured that it was given the consideration and weight appropriate in a Carr analysis. See Whitmore, 680 F.3d at 1376 ("While we acknowledge that the [Board] may well have considered the countervailing evidence and rejected or discounted it for various reasons, with no basis in [its] opinion to understand [its] logic, we cannot say that [its] analysis is reasonable or complies with the law for how proof by clear and convincing evidence is to be evaluated."). Remand is therefore appropriate. See id. at 1368. On remand, the Board is not limited to considering evidence bearing on Dr. Deering's motives; rather, it should also consider any record evidence suggesting a retaliatory motive on the part of other agency officials and the extent to which such officials influenced Dr. Deering's decision. See id. at 1371 ("When a whistleblower makes...highly critical accusations of an agency's conduct, an agency official's merely being outside that whistleblower's chain of command, not directly involved in alleged retaliatory actions,...is insufficient to remove the possibility of a retaliatory motive or retaliatory influence on the whistleblower's treatment.").

The Board remanded a decision for failure of the AJ to disclose the evidentiary basis for findings and for failure to explain how she resolved issues of credibility in Dal Santo v. DODDS, 33 MSPR 459, 461 (1987). Another appeal was remanded for failure of the AJ to summarize all of the evidence on the disputed issues of fact and failure to discuss the conflicting evidence offered by witnesses. Arenz v. Dept. of Army, 40 MSPR 307, 310 (1989). Yet another appeal was remanded after the AJ limited the agency to proof of no more than half of 44 specifications supporting five charges comprising rudeness, discourtesy, failure to follow instructions, AWOL, loafing, and failure to learn the job. The AJ should have allowed the agency to prove all the specifications supporting its charges. Chlodnicki v. Dept. of Army, 39 MSPR 376, 378-79 (1989). An unacceptable performance appeal was remanded when the AJ failed to address all of the appellant's relevant challenges to his performance standards. Gruner v. Dept. of Army, 40 MSPR 333, 338-39 (1989); cf. Cranwill v. VA, 52 MSPR 610, 616 (1992) (remanding for a more specific analysis of charged deficiencies in a performance case); Bowling v. Dept. of Army, 40 MSPR 348, 352 (1989) (remand for the AJ to address whether the appellant's performance met an established standard). In a disciplinary case, the Board may find analytical error harmless as to the AJ's analysis of some charges or specifications if the sustained charges analyzed by the AJ justified the penalty imposed by the agency. See Hicks v. Dept. of Treasury, 62 MSPR 71, 74 (1994); Curry v. Dept. of Air Force, 35 MSPR 301, 304 (1987) (no harm done when the AJ did not address some of the 25 specifications discounted by the deciding official; charges were relied upon by the deciding official and sustained by the AJ that supported the adverse action). An AJ's decision may be sustained even if it does not analyze all charges, if the charges it does sustain support the appealed action, Womack v. USPS, 70 MSPR 285, 290 n.* (1996):

Although not raised by the appellant on review, we find that the initial decision does not address all or parts of three of the administrative judge's twenty-one issues, namely issues eleven, twelve, and twenty. Each relates to the charge of failure to perform the essential duties of her position in a timely manner-poor work performance.

An initial decision must identify all material issues of fact and law, summarize the evidence, resolve issues of credibility, and include conclusions of law and legal reasoning, as well as the authorities on which that reasoning rests. Spithaler v. OPM, 2 MSPB 2, 1 MSPR 587, 589 (1980). The administrative judge did not do so here. Nevertheless, the sustained specifications, which correspond to twelve of the twenty issues related to the charge of failure to perform the essential duties of her position in a timely manner-poor work performance, support that charge. Therefore, the administrative judge's failure to address these three issues did not prejudice the appellant's substantive rights. See Panter v. Dept. of the Air Force, 22 MSPR 281, 282 (1984) (an adjudicatory error that is not prejudicial to a party's substantive rights provides no basis for reversal of an initial decision).

The AJ should not advance his views for those not articulated by an agency official. If the AJ is to rely on agency consideration of mitigating factors and agency officials do not testify as to the scope of their review of the penalty, the AJ does not have leave to surmise whether mitigating factors were conscientiously considered by the agency. Banez v. DOD, 69 MSPR 642, 649-50 (1996).
