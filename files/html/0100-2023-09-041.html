broida mspb Chapter 02: Appeal and Discovery Chapter 02: Appeal and Discovery, Subchapter 01: Initiating an Appeal, Section C: Method and Place of Filing, Subsection 02: Irregular and Misdirected Filings, Part c: Non-Mixed Appeal Filed With Employing Agency
c. Non-Mixed Appeal Filed With Employing Agency

Sanford v. DOD, 61 MSPR 207, 209-10 (1994), suggested that leniency would be shown to appellants who mistakenly file an appeal with their employing agency and then direct the appeal to the Board when the error is discovered:

The appellant replied that he misread the instructions and sent the appeal to the agency's regional office by mistake.

The administrative judge found that the agency's decision letter informed the appellant of the time limit for filing the appeal and that the appeal was required to be filed with the Board's San Francisco Regional Office. Thus, the administrative judge found that the appellant failed to show good cause for the untimely filing.

A petition for appeal must be filed within 20 days after the effective date of the action being appealed. This time limit may be waived only upon a showing of good cause for the delay in filing. The Board generally holds that an appellant's failure to follow explicit filing instructions does not constitute good cause for any ensuing delay.

Nonetheless, we have established an exception to this general rule. Specifically, in DeAstrero v. OPM, 39 MSPR 431 (1989), aff'd, 907 F.2d 157 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (Table), the Board found that an appellant established good cause for waiver of the time limit for filing an appeal to the Board where the appeal was timely but mistakenly filed with the Office of Personnel Management (OPM), the agency that issued the decision she sought to appeal. We noted that the delay in filing was caused not only by the appellant's failure to follow the directions in the reconsideration decision, but also by the agency's failure to direct an otherwise timely appeal to the Board. We have declined to apply this rule where the filing was with "neither the agency that issued the decision from which the appeal was taken, nor...the appellant's former employer." [citing St. Pierre v. USPS, 55 MSPR 98, 100 (1992)] . Likewise, we have not applied it where the appellant deliberately and repeatedly filed his appeal with OPM, the agency whose decision he wished to appeal, rather than with the Board.

Here, even though the 12-day delay was occasioned by the appellant's failure to follow the directions in the agency's decision letter, his intention to seek further review of the agency's decision within the time period for filing an appeal is clear. Furthermore, the agency has not established that any prejudice would result from a waiver.

Under these circumstances, we decline to follow the general rule that an appellant's failure to follow filing instructions does not constitute good cause for a filing delay. Rather, we find that the appellant has shown good cause for the brief delay where he filed with the personnel office of his employing agency within the 20-day regulatory time limit and then filed with the Board as soon as he learned of his mistake.

The fact that he was represented does not require a different result as the Board has excused the delay occasioned by representatives' mistaken filing in Casey v. U.S. Soldiers' and Airmen's Home, 5 MSPB 161, 5 MSPR 91 (1981) and Wildeman v. Dept. of the Air Force, 23 MSPR 313 (1984). While the misdirected filing in those cases involved an entity affiliated with the Board, that single factor should not be controlling. All the facts and circumstances surrounding a filing delay must be examined, bearing in mind that good cause is an elastic concept. See Alonzo v. Dept. of the Air Force, 4 MSPB 262, 4 MSPR 180, 183-84 (1980). All that is required to establish good cause is a reasonable excuse and any doubts about whether good cause has been shown should be resolved in favor of an appellant. Id. at 184, 186. As explained above, the appellant here has presented a reasonable excuse sufficient to establish good cause. To the extent that Phipps v. Dept. of the Air Force, 45 MSPR 375 (1990), is inconsistent with this holding, it is overruled.

See Hightower v. USPS, 72 MSPR 567, 569 (1996) (following Sanford); Livingstone v. Dept. of Navy, 66 MSPR 36, 39-40 (1994) (Sanford followed when the Board appeal was sent in error to the agency EEO office; the Board appeal was then properly filed approximately 13 days later); Moore v. Dept. of Army, 65 MSPR 94, 95 (1994) ("The facts in this case are substantially similar to those in Sanford. As noted above, the appellant mistakenly sent his appeal to the agency within the time limit, and then corrected his error by refiling the appeal with the Board's regional office the day after the closing of the filing period. Therefore, we find that the appellant has shown good cause for the one-day delay in filing."); Pidock v. Dept. of Treasury, 64 MSPR 322, 325 (1994) (Sanford was applied to excuse a counsel fee petition, received a day late, that was earlier sent to the respondent agency); Daniel v. Dept. of Treasury, 61 MSPR 249, 251 (1994) (appeal was erroneously faxed to the agency on the last day for filing; the error was corrected by proper filing with the region six days later; applying Sanford: "Board has recently held that it will find good cause for an untimely filed petition for appeal if an appellant, whether represented or not, files the petition with the employing agency within the...regulatory time limit and then files with the Board as soon as he learns of his mistake, and if the agency does not establish that any prejudice would result from a waiver."), followed in Dix v. Dept. of Army, 77 MSPR 414, 417 (1998).

If a dispute arises concerning whether an appeal was actually sent to the agency, the Board will evaluate the evidence. According to Russell v. Dept. of Treasury, 85 MSPR 607, 610-11 ¶¶ 6-9 (2000), dismissing the appeal as untimely, the evidence consisted of appellant's statements, contained in his PFR, that he mailed his appeal to an agency personnel staffing specialist, and the agency's sworn statement by the personnel staffing specialist explaining that her responsibility included probable knowledge of an appeal from appellant and disclaiming knowledge of an appeal. Assuming that the appellant was misinformed by his agency concerning where an appeal should be directed, a late appeal might not be excused if the appellant failed to perfect the Board appeal through proper filing in a reasonable time once he learned of the error in the notice of Board appeal rights. Williams v. MSPB (Fed. Cir. 1993 NP No. 93-3209) (involving an appeal filed with the agency rather than with the MSPB).

Previously, the Board was not likely to excuse an appeal filed with the wrong agency if the appeal did not allege discrimination, and the Federal Circuit declined to set aside a Board decision on the timeliness of an appeal when "[t]he delay which ensued was occupied in part by petitioners' misdirected efforts for relief from other than those exclusively authorized by statute to provide it." Blum v. Dept. of Air Force (Fed. Cir. 1993 NP No. 92-3613) (involving a claim of an improper cancelation of a promotion linked to actions by OPM concerning a classification appeal). But see Makse v. Civil Serv. Comm'n, 634 F.2d 341, 342 (6th Cir. 1980) ("We believe a federal employee told to consult a specific employment supervisor concerning an appellate procedure and who is advised by that person that appeal of a Civil Service Commission decision can be appropriately left with him is entitled to place some reliance upon such assurance. The statement...should be binding on the government, at least to the extent of requiring that the delayed filing in strict accordance with the instructions be excused, as was provided for by resolution. The appeal must be processed as timely filed.").
