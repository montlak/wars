a. Paper Suspension

For a suspension to be appealable to the Board as an adverse action, it must result in a loss in pay. Some agencies--e.g., the Postal Service--occasionally employ a "paper suspension," essentially an entry into the personnel record of a disciplinary record that is counted for purposes of progressive discipline but that does not entail loss of work and pay. The paper suspension could be used, for example, if the employee is a supervisor and the actual imposition of a suspension might undermine the employee's credibility among his subordinates. The suspension still provides a basis for demonstrating progressive discipline and it may hamper an employee's career progression. Cf. Lazard v. USPS, 93 MSPR 337, 341 n.3 (2003); Johnson v. USPS, 85 MSPR 1, 4 (1999) (USERRA claim: "It is undisputed that the suspension was a 'paper suspension' which was recorded in the appellant's personnel file but was never served. Nevertheless, such a suspension involves a 'benefit of employment' because it is disciplinary in nature and affects the employee's status or interest."); Blackiemore v. USPS, 67 MSPR 331, 332 (1995) (noting an arbitrator's mitigation of a removal to "a 14-day paper suspension").

Alaniz v. USPS, 100 MSPR 105, 113-15 ¶¶ 19, 21 (2005), said of a paper suspension:

...These suspensions, some of which were taken for misconduct similar to that presently charged, specifically put the appellant on notice that further misconduct could result in the issuance of even more severe disciplinary action. Id. His continued misconduct in spite of this notice demonstrates a poor potential for rehabilitation and suggests that a lesser penalty would be unlikely to deter future misconduct....

...

According to the Joint Contract Administration Manual between the agency and the appellant's union, an employee suspended for 14 days or less:

will remain on duty during the term of the suspension with no loss of pay. These disciplinary actions shall, however, be considered to be of the same degree of seriousness and satisfy the same corrective steps in the pattern of progressive discipline as the time-off suspensions. Such suspensions are equivalent to time-off suspensions and may be cited as elements of past discipline in subsequent discipline....

Each written notice of a "no time off" suspension informed the appellant that future misconduct could result in more severe discipline, including removal. For instance, the August 2, 2003 Notice of 14-day "No Time Off" Suspension states:

It is hoped that this Fourteen (14) Day "No Time Off" Suspension will serve to impress upon you the seriousness of your actions and that future corrective action will not be necessary.... However, I must warn you that future deficiencies will result in more severe corrective action being taken against you. Such action may include additional suspensions, reduction in grade or pay, or removal from the Postal Service.

While this suspension does not result in a loss of time or pay, it is equivalent to and is of the same degree of seriousness as a time off suspension. It satisfies the same requirement for progressive discipline as a time off suspension, and may be cited as an element of past discipline pursuant to Article 16.10 [of the collective bargaining agreement] .

In light of the content of the notices provided to the appellant here, we disagree with the administrative judge's conclusion that the appellant was not on notice that he had been receiving progressive discipline and that future misconduct could result in removal.

The paper suspension is not appealable if it involves no pay loss. To qualify as a suspension, an employee must incur monetary loss, Labinski v. USPS, 88 MSPR 125, 127-28 ¶ 5 (2001):

Under 5 U.S.C. § 7512(2), appealable adverse actions include "a suspension for more than 14 days," and under 5 U.S.C. § 7511(a)(2), "'suspension' has the same meaning as set forth in section 7501(2) of this title[.] " Section 7501(2) defines "suspension" as "the placing of an employee, for disciplinary reasons, in a temporary status without duties and pay." The appellant received a "15 day suspension, with no time off, and no loss of pay." His suspension was thus not an adverse action appealable to the Board under 5 U.S.C. § 7513(d). See McClure v. U.S. Postal Service, 83 MSPR 605, ¶ 9 (1999) (an appealable suspension does not occur during a period for which the employee receives pay).

Paper suspensions may appear in MSPB settlements.
