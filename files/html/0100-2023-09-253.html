b. Request for Admissions

Another discovery procedure here briefly mentioned is the request for admissions, described in Fed. R. Civ. P. 36. One party serves on another written requests to admit to matters of fact, to the application of law to fact, and to the genuineness of documents. Responses must either state an objection to the request or admit or deny the request. A denial must be fair and encompass only as much of the request as can truthfully be denied. A party cannot say he does not know enough to admit or deny the request unless he states that he has made a reasonable inquiry and still cannot come up with the information. A motion to determine the sufficiency of the response may be filed with the AJ by a requestor who believes that the responses are evasive or incomplete. So far, so good: a lawyer's game of cat and mouse. But under Fed. R. Civ. P. 36, there is a trap for the unwary mouse. If a request is not denied or answered within the time permitted, the rule states that the request is deemed admitted. Board regulations do not limit the number of admission requests that may be served during discovery. The Federal Rules of Civil Procedure outline the basic procedure at Rule 36:

Requests for Admission

(a) Scope and Procedure.

(1) Scope. A party may serve on any other party a written request to admit, for purposes of the pending action only, the truth of any matters within the scope of Rule 26(b)(1) relating to:

(A) facts, the application of law to fact, or opinions about either; and

(B) the genuineness of any described documents.

(2) Form; Copy of a Document. Each matter must be separately stated. A request to admit the genuineness of a document must be accompanied by a copy of the document unless it is, or has been, otherwise furnished or made available for inspection and copying.

(3) Time to Respond; Effect of Not Responding.

A matter is admitted unless, within 30 days after being served, the party to whom the request is directed serves on the requesting party a written answer or objection addressed to the matter and signed by the party or its attorney. A shorter or longer time for responding may be stipulated to under Rule 29 or be ordered by the court. [MSPB regulations call for a 20-day response, 5 CFR 1201.72 (d)(2)]

(4) Answer. If a matter is not admitted, the answer must specifically deny it or state in detail why the answering party cannot truthfully admit or deny it. A denial must fairly respond to the substance of the matter; and when good faith requires that a party qualify an answer or deny only a part of a matter, the answer must specify the part admitted and qualify or deny the rest. The answering party may assert lack of knowledge or information as a reason for failing to admit or deny only if the party states that it has made reasonable inquiry and that the information it knows or can readily obtain is insufficient to enable it to admit or deny.

(5) Objections. The grounds for objecting to a request must be stated. A party must not object solely on the ground that the request presents a genuine issue for trial.

(6) Motion Regarding the Sufficiency of an Answer or Objection. The requesting party may move to determine the sufficiency of an answer or objection. Unless the court finds an objection justified, it must order that an answer be served. On finding that an answer does not comply with this rule, the court may order either that the matter is admitted or that an amended answer be served. The court may defer its final decision until a pretrial conference or a specified time before trial. Rule 37(a)(5) applies to an award of expenses.

(b) Effect of an Admission; Withdrawing or Amending It. A matter admitted under this rule is conclusively established unless the court, on motion, permits the admission to be withdrawn or amended. Subject to Rule 16(e), the court may permit withdrawal or amendment if it would promote the presentation of the merits of the action and if the court is not persuaded that it would prejudice the requesting party in maintaining or defending the action on the merits. An admission under this rule is not an admission for any other purpose and cannot be used against the party in any other proceeding.

The Board's discovery rules approve the use of the request for admissions. 5 CFR 1201.72(c) (2022). Through the end of 1989, the AJ was also given the authority to issue requests for admission under 5 CFR 1201.65. At the end of 1989, this authority was dropped from the Board's rules (perhaps because the AJ retains authority under 5 CFR 1201.41(b)(10) (2022) to order the production of evidence). Through the end of 1989, the Board's rules deviated from the usual litigation practice concerning requests for admission: under 5 CFR 1201.72(c) (1989), "[f]ailure to deny a request for admission shall not be deemed to constitute a binding admission." See Hartman v. OPM, 38 MSPR 337, 338-39 (1988) (failure to respond to a request for admission would not serve as a binding admission, but a negative inference could be drawn if the party failed to comply with an AJ's order to respond). The question of why the Board altered customary practice came to mind. Perhaps it was to avoid trapping the government or an appellant into a default as a result of the neglect of counsel. Board regulatory revisions at the end of 1989 dropped the variation. Ludlum v. DOJ, 87 MSPR 56, 59 ¶ 7 (2000), implied that a request is not admitted if it is not denied--at least if a motion to compel discovery is not filed:

...The agency served its discovery requests, including its request for admissions, on January 11, 1999. A response from the appellant was therefore due twenty days later, or by January 31, 1999. Id.; 5 CFR § 1201.73(d)(2). As the agency correctly notes, the appellant did not submit a response by that date. The time limit for a motion for an order compelling discovery, however, is 10 days after the time limit for the appellant's response has expired. 5 CFR § 1201.73(d)(4); see 5 CFR § 1201.74(a). The agency's motion to compel was filed on February 16, 1999, six days after the February 10, 1999 deadline. Therefore, it was untimely filed. See Golden v. U.S. Postal Service, 60 MSPR 268, 271 n. (1994) (the appellant's motion to compel discovery was untimely filed, and the AJ therefore properly denied it).

Requests for admissions can be useful. An agency may obtain admissions by the appellant about most of the facts, for example, by requesting the appellant to admit each (specified) factual statement in an adverse action proposal or decision, saving considerable time at the hearing if the admissions are specific and focus on essential facts; the same for the appellant. For all parties, there is no point in proving a fact admitted. Admissions might even lead to stipulations, also saving discovery and hearing time. Even though a request may ask an appellant to admit the charged misconduct, that does not justify a refusal to answer the request for admissions, Fuhs v. USPS, 22 MSPR 283, 286 (1984):

[U]nder 5 CFR § 1201.72(b), discovery may extend to "any nonprivileged matter which is relevant to the issues involved in the appeal...." The appellant contends specifically that asking him to answer a written interrogatory, inquiring whether he was absent on the days charged, is unreasonable and unfair because it constitutes the equivalent of a request to admit AWOL. We deem this contention to be meritless on its face. In any event, a request for admissions is an accepted discovery tool. See 5 CFR § 1201.72(c).

The Federal Rules provide for requests for admissions between the parties. Although Board regulations do not proscribe requests for admissions to nonparties, that is not the way they are used, and it does not seem likely that a nonparty's statement about something would be conclusive as to a fact in controversy between the parties.
