broida mspb Chapter 01: MSPB Structure and Jurisdiction Chapter 01: MSPB Structure and Jurisdiction, Subchapter 05: Appeal Rights Based on Military Service and Veterans Preference, Section F: Veterans Employment Opportunities Act, Subsection 06: Mandatory Exhaustion of DOL Remedy; Time for Complaint, Part c: Effect of Settlement Reached by DOL
c. Effect of Settlement Reached by DOL

Gingery v. Dept. of Treasury, 110 MSPR 83, 17-21 ¶¶ 17-21, 2008 MSPB 217 (2008) (rev'd and remanded on other gnds, Gingery v. DOD, 550 F.3d 1347 (Fed. Cir. 2008)), established that adjustment of a VEOA complaint at the level of the Department of Labor does not divest Board jurisdiction to review the appellant's VEOA appeal:

As noted above, 5 U.S.C. § 3330a(d)(1) provides that "[i]f the Secretary of Labor is unable to resolve a [VEOA] complaint...within 60 days after the date on which it is filed, the complainant may elect to appeal the alleged violation to the [Board] ...." While a literal reading of the statute suggests that the Board lacks jurisdiction over a VEOA appeal if the Secretary of Labor has resolved the appellant's VEOA complaint, we decline to adopt that interpretation. The record shows that the appellant did not waive his Board appeal rights by settling his VEOA claim, nor did DOL's resolution provide the appellant with all of the relief that he could have received if his VEOA appeal had been adjudicated and he had prevailed. See Walker v. Department of the Army, 104 M.S.P.R. 96, ¶ 18 (2006) (when an agency violates the appellant's veterans' preference rights by denying him the right to compete for a particular position, the proper remedy is for the agency to reconstruct the selection process for the position and follow the applicable statutes and regulations relating to veterans' preference). In addition, we have no reason to believe that Congress intended for DOL's determination that an appellant's VEOA complaint has been resolved to deprive the Board of jurisdiction over the appellant's VEOA appeal. See Kirkendall v. Department of the Army, 479 F.3d 830, 835 (Fed. Cir.) (en banc) (DOL's rejection of the appellant's VEOA complaint as untimely under 5 U.S.C. § 3330a(a)(2)(A) does not deprive the Board of jurisdiction over his VEOA claim), cert. denied, 128 S. Ct. 375 (2007). Just as the Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit held in Kirkendall that Congress did not intend for DOL to have the final word in determining the timeliness of VEOA complaints, we find that Congress did not intend for DOL to have the final word as to what constitutes an appropriate remedy for a violation of veterans' preference rights.

Further, the Board recently addressed the issue of whether DOL's resolution of an appellant's VEOA complaint divests the Board of jurisdiction over the appellant's VEOA appeal in Heckman v. Department of the Interior, 109 M.S.P.R. 133, ¶¶ 15-16 (2008). The appellant in Heckman filed a VEOA complaint with DOL regarding his nonselection under an agency vacancy announcement. Id., ¶¶ 8, 15. During DOL's investigation of the complaint, the agency informed DOL that it intended to provide the appellant with priority consideration for the next relevant agency opening. Id., ¶ 15. DOL then advised the agency and the appellant that it considered the agency's offer a satisfactory resolution of the complaint, and that the case was closed. Id.

The appellant filed a Board appeal and the AJ found that the Board lacked jurisdiction over the appellant's VEOA complaint because the complaint was resolved by the Secretary of Labor and "there is no right to appeal the alleged violation to the Board unless the Secretary of Labor is unable to resolve the complaint within 60 days of filing." Heckman v. Department of the Interior, MSPB Docket No. SF-3443-05-0484-I-4, slip op. at 10 (Initial Decision, Nov. 28, 2007).

The Board found, however, that DOL's resolution of the appellant's VEOA complaint did not divest the Board of jurisdiction over his VEOA appeal and that the AJ, therefore, erred in finding that the Board lacked jurisdiction on the basis that the appellant's VEOA complaint was resolved by DOL. Heckman, 109 M.S.P.R. 133, ¶ 16.

Here, as in Heckman, the agency violated the appellant's veterans' preference rights by denying him the right to compete for a particular position under a vacancy announcement and the AJ found that the Board lacks jurisdiction over the appellant's VEOA appeal because of DOL's resolution of the appellant's VEOA complaint. See Heckman, 109 M.S.P.R. 133, ¶ 16. We thus find that the AJ erred by dismissing the appellant's VEOA appeal for lack of jurisdiction based on DOL's resolution of the appellant's VEOA complaint. See id. Therefore, we reverse the ID.
