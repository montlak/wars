a. Delay in Payment

One problem commonly encountered concerning back pay is the delay in getting payment to the appellant. The employee may have been out of work, spending what he has to fight his case, and then prevail after years of litigation, but the back pay does not appear because of delays encountered when an agency must write a special payroll check (or electronic transfer equivalent) and determine unusual deductions. The usual process followed by disgruntled appellants involves some waiting, filing an enforcement petition, more waiting, issuance of an order directing payment, more waiting, and, finally, payment. The appellant suffers the brunt of the delay in this process; no reported Board decision docks a federal official's salary for delayed compliance with the Board's decision ordering back pay to an appellant.

Under Freedman v. VA, 30 MSPR 518, 521 (1986), "[w]hile a regional director may accept an agency's representations that it is making a good faith effort and reasonable progress toward compliance with a Board order [see 5 CFR 1201.183(a)(3) (1986)], a regional director also should concurrently require the agency to provide proof of actual compliance within a time certain." Although the time certain was not defined in the opinion, the Board gave the agency 20 days to prove compliance. A Board final decision, Jordan v. Dept. of Army, 30 MSPR 543, 544 (1986), directed reinstatement of the appellant and ordered the agency "to complete computations and issue a check to the appellant for the appropriate amount of back pay within sixty days of the date this decision becomes final." If that type of language is adopted in the regions, the Board will reduce much of the paperwork associated with the confusion between promises to pay and payment. Better yet, in Foster v. Dept. of Labor, 31 MSPR 74, 79 (1986), the Board entered a model for orders in initial decisions:

The agency is ORDERED to complete all computations and issue a check to the appellant for the appropriate amount of back pay within sixty days of the date of this...Order. The appellant is ORDERED to cooperate in good faith with the agency's efforts to compute the amount of back pay due.

If there is a dispute as to the amount of back pay due, the agency shall issue a check to the appellant for the amount not in dispute within the above time frame. The appellant may then file a petition for enforcement concerning the disputed amount.

The Board referenced what it characterized as its standard concerning compliance, Whaley v. USPS, 61 MSPR 340, 345 (1994):

In the normal case where the Board reverses an agency action, the Board's Opinion and Order requires the agency to accomplish three things in compliance: (1) To pay backpay and interest within 60 days; (2) to pay any undisputed amount within 60 days in the event that there is a dispute about the total amount of backpay; and (3) to inform the appellant in writing of all actions taken to comply with the Board's order and of the date on which the agency believes it is in full compliance.

An agency can justify payment of back pay after the date set by the Board if the appellant delays his submission to the agency of information, for example, on interim earnings, required by the agency to calculate his back pay. See Boyd v. VA, 93 MSPR 386, 389 ¶ 5 (2003) ("an agency's delay in awarding back pay may be justified if the appellant delays his submission to the agency of information required by the agency to calculate back pay"); Ciulla v. USPS, 42 MSPR 111, 113 (1989) ("The agency asserted that it had a legitimate concern that the appellant had not attempted to seek employment during the time he was off its rolls, as required by its regulations, that the appellant delayed for almost a month in submitting such information, and that when it received sufficient information it took steps to award back pay. On the basis of the record before her, the administrative judge concluded that the agency had a legitimate concern and did not unduly delay. The administrative judge did not abuse her discretion in finding that, under these circumstances, the agency did not unduly delay or otherwise act unreasonably, and that sanctions are unwarranted."). If the agency must recalculate back pay, it properly requires the appellant to refund back pay erroneously received before the agency issues another check for the adjusted back pay. See Czubinski v. Dept. of Treasury, 85 MSPR 278, 279-80 ¶ 3 (2000) (not explaining whether the agency was attempting to collect the entire amount of back pay first issued to the appellant or only a much smaller amount representing the difference in the two checks), aff'd, Czubinski v. Dept. of Treasury (Fed. Cir. 2000 NP No. 00-3219). When there is some delay in making payment, but the agency establishes that it is acting in "good faith" to try and obtain the payment for the appellant, the Board may dismiss the enforcement petition without prejudice to refiling after a set time allowed for full compliance. See Magee v. USPS, 69 MSPR 193 (1995) (Table).
