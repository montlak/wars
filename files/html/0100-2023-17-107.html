b. Managerial Discretion

The general rule defining management's discretion in a RIF was stated in Holmes v. Dept. of Army, 41 MSPR 612, 614-15 (1989):

The permissible bases for invoking the RIF regulations are set forth at 5 CFR § 351.201(a)(2). An agency must prove by a preponderance of the evidence that its decision to conduct a RIF was based on one or more of these reasons.... Once an agency has made this showing, however, the Board has no authority to review the management considerations that underlie the agency's exercise of its discretion. The agency, not the Board, is responsible for deciding whether to retain or abolish particular positions....

See Fisher v. Dept. of Interior, 2023 MSPB 11, n.2 (2023) ("[A]n agency is accorded a great deal of discretion in making managerial decisions concerning reorganizations, see Armstrong v. International Trade Commission, 74 M.S.P.R. 349, 354 (1997), and we agree with the administrative judge's findings that the agency established that it invoked the RIF regulations for a legitimate management reason and that the record contains no evidence that the reorganization occurred for an improper reason."); Pezdek v. DOD, 80 MSPR 554, 564 ¶ 26 (1999) (despite an internal debate within the agency over proposed staffing patterns, the Board would not second guess the ultimate agency decision on structure following reorganization), vacated and remanded on other grounds, Pezdek v. DOD (Fed. Cir. 1999 NP No. 99-3164), reaff'd following remand, Pezdek v. DOD, 84 MSPR 585 (1999); Cook v. Dept. of Interior, 74 MSPR 454, 459 (1997) ("[t]he term 'shortage of funds' is broad enough to cover a multitude of legitimate management considerations."); Armstrong v. ITC, 74 MSPR 349, 354-55 (1997) ("[a]n agency, facing a deficit situation, has broad discretion to take action to avoid such an event, including conducting a RIF, and...the Board will not second guess an agency's decision to reorganize its work force as it lacks authority to review the management considerations that underlie the exercise of discretion."); Moran v. Dept. of Air Force, 64 MSPR 77, 83 (1994) ("The agency, not the Board, is responsible for deciding whether to retain or abolish particular positions.); Winchester v. TVA, 55 MSPR 485, 489-90 (1992) (since an agency decision to reassign the duties of a position was within management's discretion to run a RIF for a legitimate reason, the Board "lack[ed] jurisdiction to consider the merits of the appellant's allegation of prohibited personnel practice regarding the elimination or reclassification of the PG-8 supervisory position"); Willson v. Dept. of Army, 25 MSPR 167, 171 (1984) ("Once the Board determines that the agency invoked the RIF regulations for a permissible reason, it lacks authority to review the management considerations underlying the exercise of agency discretion."). Holmes was followed in Anderson v. DOD, 48 MSPR 388 (1991); cf. Biter v. ICC, 76 MSPR 82, 94 (1997) (organization of field offices in a transfer of function was discretionary with the agency and not subject to a RIF appeal).

Winchester v. TVA, 55 MSPR 485, 489-90 (1992), declined to credit an allegation of a prohibited personnel practice regarding elimination or reclassification of the position held by the appellant prior to the RIF:

We note that, as part of his prohibited personnel practice allegation, the appellant contends that the agency should have either eliminated entirely the field support supervisory position or officially reclassified it to PG-5. We agree with the administrative judge that the agency's decision to assign the remaining duties of that position to a PG-5 position was within the agency's discretion. Once the Board has determined that an agency has invoked RIF regulations for a permissible reason, the Board lacks the authority to review the management considerations underlying the exercise of the agency's discretion, including the decision whether to preserve or abolish a particular position. See Cox v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 41 MSPR 686, 689 (1989); Willson v. Department of the Army, 25 MSPR 167, 171 (1984). Therefore, we find that the Board lacks jurisdiction to consider the merits of the appellant's allegation of prohibited personnel practice regarding the elimination or reclassification of the PG-8 supervisory position.

Winchester should not be read too broadly. Affirmative defenses of discrimination [discussed below], and whistleblowing reprisal [discussed in Chapter 13 "Whistleblowing Reprisal"] are asserted by appellants and considered by the Board in RIF appeals.

Internal agency regulations should be followed in a RIF, although violation of these regulations does not necessarily lead to reversal of the action. See Moran v. Dept. of Air Force, 64 MSPR 77, 84 (1994) (no error in a decision to abolish the appellant's position rather than another position that could not be performed by military personnel; as to the alleged violation of a regulation precluding the addition of military positions to work centers deleting civilian positions, the Board noted: "We find that the large-scale reorganization engaged in by the agency was not merely a subterfuge to convert the appellant's position into a military authorization"; the duties of the appellant's former position were assumed by residual military personnel, not by new position authorizations.); Gurkin v. Dept. of Air Force, 40 MSPR 95 (1989) (no defense in a regulation prohibiting the creation of military slots during a time that civilian positions were deleted by RIF); Nielson v. Dept. of Navy, 26 MSPR 92, 95 (1985) (no error or violation of regulation when the appellant's duties as head of the Office of Facilities and Services were absorbed into a new position that was filled by a military officer); Brittain v. Dept. of Army, 21 MSPR 402, 403-04 (1984) (recognizing the regulation pertaining to the substitution of military for civilian slots, but the large-scale reorganization undertaken was not a subterfuge to convert the appellant's position into a military slot).

A RIF based on a reorganization is not invalid for lack of official agency function statements; it is sufficient if the basic functions of the organization are known. Feltus v. Dept. of Energy, 4 MSPR 232, 235, 4 MSPB 308 (1980). A reorganization can involve a considerable length of time. In an appeal involving a geographical job shuffle, the agency waited four years to downgrade a position following initiation of the reorganization. Douglas v. Dept. of Interior, 41 MSPR 575, 580-81 (1989) (classification of a position was not the result of a classification error or change in classification standards, but rather of the agency's planned elimination of duties in the organization); Holmes v. Dept. of Army, 41 MSPR 612, 615 (1989) (restructuring occurred over several years).
