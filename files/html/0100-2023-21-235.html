1. Form of Restoration Request

The partially recovered employee must request restoration before the agency can be charged with its denial. See Wright v. USPS, 62 MSPR 122, 126 (1994) ("Here, the agency never denied the appellant restoration because the appellant never directly requested it. There is no evidence in the record showing that the appellant requested restoration. Moreover, on cross examination at the hearing, the appellant stated that she never requested reemployment with the agency, but she believed that her doctors' 'notes' to the agency's injury compensation specialist, Joseph Truss, constituted requests for work."). Larsen v. Dept. of Interior, 36 MSPR 669, 671-72 (1988), suggested that a request for restoration need not be in writing, although the Board did not rule out the possibility that an agency could reasonably require a written request:

...We first address the administrative judge's statement that the appellant failed to make a proper request for restoration because his request was not in writing. Neither the statute, 5 U.S.C. § 8151(b)(2), nor the regulation, see 5 C.F.R. § 353.307, as promulgated by OPM pursuant to its statutory authority, imposes such a requirement, and we are aware of none. The regulation requires only that the individual seeking priority consideration for restoration "appl[y] for reappointment" within thirty days of the cessation of compensation. Id. We note further that, although the agency in this case has asserted that it "informed [the appellant] that he must file an application for employment if he wished to be considered for reemployment," it has provided no justification for its apparent imposition of this additional requirement.

Under these circumstances, we find it appropriate to remand this case. On remand, the administrative judge shall consider whether there is a proper basis for accepting the appellant's appeal despite its apparently untimely filing. If he finds that there is a proper basis for doing so, he shall determine whether the agency had sufficient justification for imposing a requirement that the appellant's application be in writing,[3] and, if so, whether the agency provided the appellant with adequate notice of the requirement. If he finds that the agency did not have sufficient justification for imposing its additional requirement, he shall then determine whether, by his communications with the agency, the appellant made a timely oral request for priority consideration, and if so, the effect of the agency's failure to respond to such request.

[3] Although we do not rule out the possibility that the agency may present sufficient justification for its requirement, we note that the agency previously employed the appellant, and that it therefore apparently was familiar with the appellant's qualifications. In addition, the agency has not asserted any other basis for requiring a written application.

Notice to the agency of a request for restoration need not be elaborate, Garza v. DHS, DE-315H-22-0094-I-1 (NP 2/13/2023):

...In his March 4, 2022 response to the jurisdictional order, the appellant asserted that he had recovered further since the agency terminated his employment, and he submitted medical documentation related to one of his two alleged compensable injuries. In response, the agency asserted that the appellant could contact the agency to invoke his restoration rights as a partially recovered employee, in which case it would then attempt to restore him. We find that the appellant's filing is sufficient to put the agency on notice that he is seeking restoration as a partially recovered employee. To the extent the agency expects the appellant to submit additional filings in order to invoke his restoration rights, we find that this is inconsistent with Board case law. See Gerdes v. Department of the Treasury, 89 M.S.P.R. 500, ¶¶ 12-13 (2001) (cautioning against the imposition of additional notice requirements beyond those set forth in 5 C.F.R. § 353.301 for an individual who seeks restoration)....

See Joves v. USPS, SF-0752-12-0654-I-1 (NP 9/27/2013) ("The form of the appellant's request is immaterial because the regulations do not specify any formal requirements for notifying an agency that restoration is sought; the request may be made either orally or in writing. See Gerdes v. Department of the Treasury, 89 M.S.P.R. 500, ¶ 12 (2001); Larsen v. Department of the Interior, 36 M.S.P.R. 669, 671 (1988)."). If during the course of a restoration appeal before the AJ, the appellant makes a new restoration request, it is within the AJ's discretion to decline to consider the agency's response to the new request; the appellant can start another appeal if the appellate conditions are met. See Knight v. DOJ, AT-0353-13-0330-I-1 (NP 9/30/2013) ("To the extent that the appellant is claiming that the administrative judge failed to adjudicate his other restoration requests and/or did not allow him to amend his appeal to include other restoration requests, we discern no error with the administrative judge's instruction to the appellant that he should file a new appeal regarding these requests. Notably, the appellant raised these other restoration requests after the administrative judge had issued two clarification orders. The administrative judge's decision not to adjudicate the appellant's additional restoration requests raised at this late stage of the proceedings and, in particular, after he issued multiple orders for clarification, is within his discretion, 5 C.F.R. § 1201.41, and does not warrant reversal of the initial decision. If the appellant wishes to pursue an appeal based on any 2013 restoration requests, he should do so with the appropriate regional or field office.").
