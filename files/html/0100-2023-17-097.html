A. BURDEN OF PROOF

The RIF is treated as an adverse action for purposes of allocation of the burdens of proof. The agency has the burden of demonstrating the legitimacy of the RIF. The employee should identify specific defects in the RIF or in the rights he should have secured through the RIF. The employer must respond to the defects alleged by the employee, Aronson v. Dept. of Navy, 4 MSPR 310, 313, 4 MSPB 379 (1980):

Here, the presiding official, anticipating Losure, correctly put this burden on the agency, expressly rejecting any contention that the requirements of 5 CFR Part 351 were merely procedural, which would have placed the burden on appellant under 5 USC § 7701(c)(2). The presiding official may nevertheless exercise his discretion under 5 CFR § 1201.41(b) to regulate the burden of proof or require the appellant to identify the alleged impropriety with sufficient specificity to enable the agency to properly respond. Thus the presiding official correctly said that in this case it was reasonable to require the appellant to identify the positions for which appellant believed himself to qualify under 5 CFR § 351.701. That constitutes a reasonable exercise of the presiding official's discretion in light of the onerous burden which might otherwise be placed on an agency where a large number of other positions exist in the same competitive area. See Losure v. Interstate Commerce Commission, supra, 366 n.6, 2 MSPR 202 n.6. The exercise of that discretion does not affect the burden of persuasion, which remains with the agency.

The Federal Circuit summarized the burdens of proof governing a RIF appeal, Batey v. DMA (Fed. Cir. 1993 NP No. 92-3570):

Whenever a person adversely affected by a RIF challenges the legality of that action, longstanding precedent burdens the agency with the obligation to prove by a preponderance of the evidence that the RIF procedure was properly invoked. Wilburn v. Dept. of Transp., 757 F.2d 260, 262 (Fed. Cir. 1985); Losure v. ICC, 2 MSPR 195, 201 (1980). An agency meets its initial burden of production by making a prima facie showing that it conducted the RIF for any of the five reasons specified in 5 CFR 351.201(a). See Bacon v. DHUD, 757 F.2d 265, 269 (Fed. Cir. 1985). This requirement that the agency establish a proper reason for initiating a RIF merely reflects the unexceptional proposition that an agency must offer a plausible, facially valid basis for taking personnel actions.

Upon meeting its initial burden of production, the agency enjoys a strong presumption that its officers acted in good faith and otherwise in accordance with law in conducting the RIF, and the burden shifts to the aggrieved employee to rebut this presumption with "clear and convincing evidence" that, for example, the agency's reason masks an ulterior, illicit motive for the RIF, or that the official who initiated the RIF lacked the authority to do so. See Dancy v. United States, 668 F.2d 1224, 1226 (Ct. Cl. 1982); George v. United States, 166 Ct. Cl. 527, 531 (Ct. Cl. 1964).

The agency is given some deference in structuring and implementing a RIF, Deweese v. TVA, 35 F.3d 538, 541 (Fed. Cir. 1994):

...Agency action reducing its force for increased efficiency of the service generally involves a certain degree of business judgment. Thus we have said that an agency "is accorded wide discretion in conducting a reduction in force; absent a clear abuse of that discretion, a substantial departure from applicable procedures, a misconstruction of governing statutes, or the like, we do not upset a final agency decision." Cooper v. TVA, 723 F.2d 1560, 1562 (Fed. Cir. 1983) (quoting Dancy v. United States, 668 F.2d 1224, 1226, 229 Ct. Cl. 300 (1982)).

See Garofalo v. DHS, 108 MSPR 169, 2008 MSPB 38 (2008) (identifying a standard of "clear abuse of discretion" or "arbitrary or irrational" to assess actions of an interview panel that led to the appellant's separation by RIF from a TSA screening manager position).

The determination of an employee's entitlements under RIF regulations is a matter of substance rather than one of procedure. Generally speaking, as decisions discussed earlier noted, the determination is not subject to the harmful error standard on review by the Board. What this means in practice is that if there is an error on a retention register, the appellant is entitled to reinstatement even though another employee who had not appealed the RIF had a higher retention standing. Wright v. Dept. of Commerce, 20 MSPR 36, 38 (1984) (the "appellant was entitled to reinstatement irrespective of the position he would have been in absent the agency error"); see Acerno v. DHHS, 815 F.2d 680 (Fed. Cir. 1987) (citing Putnam v. DHHS, 31 MSPR 427 (1986)). Caveat: some cases, e.g., Foster v. Coast Guard, 8 MSPR 240, 244, 7 MSPB 707 (1981), inject a harmful error approach as to improper designation of competitive level.
