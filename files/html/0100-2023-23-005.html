2. Minimum Requirements

It is useful to keep in mind what the Board expects to see in any settlement that it accepts into its record for purposes of enforcement. Before dismissing a matter as settled, the Board (in the person of the AJ, if the case is settled while it is pending before the AJ) must:

1. Decide whether the parties have entered into a settlement agreement;

2. Determine if the parties understand the terms of the agreement;

3. Ascertain if the parties intend to have the agreement entered into the record for Board enforcement;

4. Verify that the agreement is lawful on its face;

5. Confirm that the parties freely entered into the agreement;

6. Find that there is a colorable claim before the Board.

As a practical matter, the agreement itself should contain recitations, signed by the appellant, that she has voluntarily entered into the agreement and that she understands its terms. If the parties agree that the settlement should be subject to Board enforcement, the agreement should so state. These recitations are expected by the assigned AJ. If the recitations are not included in the agreement, an AJ may call the parties to request that the recitations be made in writing or verbally to the AJ. Individual settlements may contain additional recitations; they all include substantive terms; and they may or may not include supplemental enforcement procedures, e.g., that allegations of noncompliance will be presented to the other side for a set time before an enforcement petition is filed with the Board.

If a party claims that a document accepted by the AJ did not constitute a settlement, the Board looks to its language and structure, not to its title, McNeill v. Dept. of Army, SF-0752-12-0177-I-1 (NP 2/7/2013):

In his petition, the appellant asserts that the document reached during the MAP process was not a settlement agreement, but a Memorandum of Understanding and that a fully executed settlement agreement was to follow. The document that the administrative judge entered into the record is titled "Memorandum of Understanding for Negotiated Settlement Agreement." Regardless of the phrase "Memorandum of Understanding" in the title, the document is cast as a settlement agreement. See Kelley v. Department of the Air Force, 50 M.S.P.R. 635, 641-42 (1991) (in construing the terms of a settlement agreement, the Board examines the four corners of the agreement to determine the parties' intent; the parties are bound by the terms of their settlement).

In practice, with the possible exception of last chance agreements waiving Board appeal rights, discussed below, the Board does not judge the equity, i.e., fairness, of a settlement--at least the AJ's initial decision dismissing a case as settled does not ordinarily state that the agreement has been found to be fair. Instead, assuming jurisdiction over the appeal, the settlement will be accepted if the AJ is satisfied that the agreement is lawful on its face and that it was reached freely by the parties. See Mastrogiuseppe v. Dept. of Interior, 66 MSPR 251, 253 (1995) ("An administrative judge must accept a settlement agreement into the record if the Board has jurisdiction over the appeal, both parties understand the agreement, it is lawful on its face, and the parties agree that it will be enforceable by the Board."); Littlejohn v. DHUD, 47 MSPR 331, 332 (1991) (adding the requirement that the parties understand the agreement and agree that it is enforceable by the Board); Sitas v. VA, 41 MSPR 214, 216 (1989) (same holding as Mastrogiuseppe); Lewis v. USPS, 38 MSPR 602, 605 n.3 (1988) ("To be enforceable by the Board, the agreement must be made a part of the record. In admitting such an agreement into the record, the administrative judge must be satisfied that the agreement is lawful on its face and that it was reached freely by the parties"); cf. Ferby v. USPS, 26 MSPR 451, 456 (1985) (a "last chance agreement" [discussed in Chapter 2, "Waiver of Appeal Through Last Chance or Other Preappeal Agreement"] was acceptable if was freely made, fair, and not the product of duress).

Occasionally the Board remands a case just to determine if the parties entered a settlement after it receives an enforcement petition and the Board cannot determine the state of affairs when the AJ closed the case with an initial decision, as apparently occurred in Snowden v. Dept. of Interior, DC-0752-10-0802-I-1 (NP 5/10/2011):

We believe that further adjudication regarding whether the parties, in fact, entered into the settlement agreement is warranted under the circumstances of this case. In reaching this conclusion, we first note that the administrative judge's jurisdictional show-cause orders did not alert the parties to the question of whether they, in fact, entered into the settlement and did not afford them the opportunity to submit evidence and argument on this issue. Despite the absence of evidence on this issue, however, the administrative judge accepted as fact that the parties entered into the settlement, finding it "perplexing" that the appellant would argue otherwise and noting that the appellant did not take any action to ascertain whether the agency had executed the agreement.

Although it was proper for the administrative judge to examine the appellant's actions after the purported settlement, because these actions can be probative regarding whether the parties in fact entered into the agreement, he should have also considered the agency's actions in this regard. See DeLoach v. Department of the Air Force, 108 M.S.P.R. 485, ¶ 11 (2008). Specifically, the agency did not take any action consistent with the belief that it was implementing the terms of the settlement for 2 years. Further, although it finally presented an executed copy of the agreement on appeal before the Board, it has not presented any evidence regarding the circumstances of its signing or why it delayed in demoting the appellant. Moreover, the Notification of Personnel Action that it submitted effecting the appellant's 2010 demotion does not refer to the settlement and instead cites 5 U.S.C. chapter 75 as authority for the action. Because there is insufficient evidence that the parties actually effected the settlement, remand for further adjudication is warranted. See DeLoach, 108 M.S.P.R. 485, ¶ 14.

Before 2017, an AJ could not accept a settlement for enforcement without first determining that the Board had jurisdiction over the appeal. Because some appeals, e.g., constructive adverse actions and whistleblower reprisal (IRA) claims, have complex jurisdictional elements that are fact-dependent, the result was that acceptance of a settlement in one of those appeals could be precluded for lack of at least a preliminary jurisdictional determination. That preliminary jurisdictional determination could depend on discovery and motion practice, perhaps a hearing--steps that a settlement could avoid. In 2017, Delorme v. Dept. of Interior, 124 MSPR 123, §§ 9-21, 2017 MSPB 2 (2017), eliminated the need for a preliminary jurisdictional determination as a condition to acceptance of a settlement for enforcement. Included in the decision, without elaboration, was the conclusion that "public policy objectives...allowing the conservation of resources through settlement in all colorable appeals brought before the Board." Reference to a "colorable claim" may be an oblique suggestion that a settlement must at least entail a claim within the arguable scope of Board jurisdiction.
