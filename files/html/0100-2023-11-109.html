4. Promotion Records, Appraisals--1994 and Before

Under decisions predating TRACON, the agency would respond to a request for information by redacting and coding names to protect the privacy of employees. If there was a need for the names to verify information concerning awards to employees, the redaction was improper if there were not other special factors tilting the balance against disclosure. VA Cent. Office and AFGE Local 1557, 25 FLRA 633, 637 (1987); see Dept. of Commerce, NOAA, Nat'l Weather Serv. and Nat'l Weather Serv. Employees Org., 38 FLRA 120, 135-36 (1990) (directing release of names and duty stations of employees who received commendable or outstanding performance appraisals for rating year), rev'd, FLRA v. Dept. of Commerce, NOAA, 962 F.2d 1055, 1056, 1057, 1059-60 (D.C. Cir. 1992). If a union was willing to take performance evaluation data in a redacted (sanitized) state, there was no need to balance the union's need for information against the individual employee's right to privacy. DHHS, Region IV, HCFA and NTEU, 21 FLRA 431, 441 (1986) (ALJ Decision); see DOJ, INS, Border Patrol, El Paso and AFGE Nat'l Border Patrol Council, 37 FLRA 1310, 1325 (1990) (agency not to "over-sanitize" documents so as to delete information necessary for union to discharge its representational responsibilities).

Earlier decisions demonstrated more flexibility. In a grievance over a performance appraisal, the Privacy Act did not bar release to the union of appraisals of other staffing clerks in the same unit as the grievant. The information was needed for arbitration, to evaluate whether the grievant was treated differently than other employees. OPM and AFGE Local 32, 17 FLRA 685, 690-91 (1985). The disclosure of information pertaining to a promotion package was not precluded as an unwarranted invasion of privacy. The names of individual applicants would not be linked to their promotion evaluation forms. It was unlikely that their identities would become known; the data requested would only be used by the union to process a grievance. There was no indication that the information would become generally known. BATF and NTEU, 18 FLRA 611, 615 (1985). The Authority described its prior balancing test in a case involving a request for various appraisals of an individual who was selected for a position in IRS, Washington and NTEU Chap. 3, 25 FLRA 181, 185-86 (1987). Under FLRA 's earlier, more liberal, policy concerning release of appraisal information, a union could obtain appraisal information about supervisors who the grievant alleged had discriminated against her in a promotion action. The union could obtain various elements of their appraisals relating to EEO enforcement and other matters relating to personnel administration. Dept. of Treasury, IRS, Wash., DC and NTEU Chap. 42, 39 FLRA 241, 251-54 (1991).

Even in more liberal days, FLRA would not approve as negotiable contract proposals for the blanket release of information regarding incentive awards and performance ratings. See DODDS and Overseas Educ. Ass'n, 28 FLRA 871, 881-83 (1987). But see NFFE Local 1482 and DMA, Hydrographic/Topographic Ctr., Louisville, 45 FLRA 1346, 1351-54 (1992) (approving negotiability of proposal to release documents containing reasons why individual employees, rated highly successful, did not receive cash awards when other employees rated at that level received them); DHHS, SSA, Field Operations, Region II and AFGE Local 2608, 43 FLRA 164, 166-69 (1991) (although blanket release might not be required, agency did not compromise privacy interests if it disclosed appraisals for groups of employees for given purpose). Materials that could support appraisals, e.g., employee worksheets, were to be provided to a union that was considering or prosecuting a grievance challenging the appraisals created in partial reliance upon the requested workproduct. See VARO, San Diego and AFGE Local 490, 44 FLRA 312, 313-14 (1992). The VARO result was tenuous, for the D.C. Circuit and other courts started to intercede and find that the Privacy Act precluded release of information pertaining to ratings. See, e.g., FLRA v. Dept. of Commerce, NOAA, 962 F.2d 1055, 1056-60 (D.C. Cir. 1992). VARO was distinguished in Dept. of Air Force, MacDill AFB and NFFE Local 153, 51 FLRA 1144, 1154 (1996), involving a request for disclosure of "Supervisor's Work Folders."
