b. Corroborative Evidence

Conviction of a crime that occurs after an initial decision finding the appellant responsible for the same conduct that is the subject of the administrative proceeding is new but not necessarily material evidence. Harrison v. USPS, 14 MSPR 476, 478 (1983) (court judge and an AJ determined that an assault on a supervisor occurred; they concurred that the assault was not particularly serious). However, evidence submitted with a PFR was new and material when it consisted of charges of rape against an employee who had already been subjected to an emergency suspension for other charges of rape and false imprisonment. The new evidence was material because it substantiated the validity of the supervisors' apprehension about safety of employees, evidence going to nexus. Johnson v. DHHS, 22 MSPR 521, 529-30 (1984). The Johnson approach permitted use of posthearing corroborative evidence. See Priddy, supra.

Special Counsel v. Eubanks, 76 MSPR 405, 418-19 (1997), indirectly accepted as "new" subsequent corroborative evidence in the form of a statement by a witness to sexual harassment. The witness recanted an earlier statement denying harassment by her supervisor. The new testimony of the witness asserted that she was sexually harassed. That evidence corroborated the testimony of others against the respondent in the Special Counsel's disciplinary proceeding:

The Board will not consider evidence as "new" absent a showing that it was unavailable before the record was closed despite the party's due diligence. See Avansino v. USPS, 3 MSPB 308, 3 MSPR 211, 214 (1980). We find that due diligence could not have led the Special Counsel to Ms. Bradley, who has averred that she did not come forward sooner because she was in fear of losing her job. We thus agree with the Special Counsel that this evidence is new because the affidavit was sworn after the close of the record and could not have been obtained, despite OSC's due diligence. See Wilson v. Dept. of Justice, 58 MSPR 96, 99-100 (1993).

In cases where, as here, newly discovered evidence consists of the recantation of a witness's prior testimony, courts have considered such evidence in the course of deciding a party's motion for a new trial based on the new evidence. See United States v. Miller, 987 F.2d 1462, 1466 (10th Cir. 1993); United States v. Chatman, 994 F.2d 1510, 1518 (10th Cir. 1993).

However, in the course of such a proceeding, the trier of fact must first be satisfied that the challenged testimony was actually false, and he or she must ordinarily hold an evidentiary hearing to determine the credibility of the witness and effect of the recantation. Id. The determination of a witness's credibility is a matter for the administrative law judge or trial judge, and on review, the Board or the reviewing court must be able to "discern from the record whether or how the [trier of fact] evaluated the credibility of [a witness's] recantation." Id., citing United States v. Ramsey, 726 F.2d 601, 605 (10th Cir. 1984). See also Gadsden, 27 MSPR at 76 (the ALJ, and not the Board, is best suited to make credibility findings regarding evidence concerning conflicting testimony).

Here, although the parties have not asked for a new trial, the new evidence in the form of Ms. Bradley's affidavit is germane to the charge that the respondent created a hostile working environment. If the allegations in Ms. Bradley's affidavit are true, it impacts on the credibility of other witnesses' testimony, including that of Mr. Eubanks, regarding whether Mr. Eubanks engaged in sexual harassment. Thus, on remand, it is necessary to hold an evidentiary hearing concerning Ms. Bradley's recanted testimony and to make findings of fact and conclusions of law with regard to its credibility and its impact, if any, on the evidence in this case.

Chin v. DOD, 2022 MSPB 34, ¶ 10 (2022) (Member Limon dissenting), rejected a polygraph report, first offered with a PFR by the appellant:

...The appellant should have known that his credibility would be a key issue in this appeal, particularly because his defense to the charge is that he did not intend to steal the food. The appellant apparently believed that the administrative judge would credit his testimony that he did not intend to steal and discovered that he was mistaken when he received the initial decision. However, the Board has held that a party may not correct such an error of judgment after the fact. Mojica-Otero v. Department of the Treasury, 30 M.S.P.R. 46, 48-49 (1986). Because we find that the appellant did not exercise due diligence in submitting to a polygraph examination and providing the results to the administrative judge before the record closed, we deny his motion.

Evidence an appellant received after settling his appeal that would have bolstered his chances on his appeal before to the settlement was new but not material and did not warrant a belated PFR seeking to set aside the settlement. When the appellant settled he effectively decided to resolve his claims without evidence he later determined to be significant, Young v. Dept. of Interior, 76 MSPR 501, 504 (1997):

While the OWCP decision would have been relevant evidence to show that the appellant suffered from a work-related adjustment disorder and post traumatic stress disorder, and would have been relevant to the issue of whether the appellant was disabled and thus entitled to the protections of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 and the Americans With Disabilities Act, it is not dispositive of those issues. Furthermore, the appellant chose not to pursue that affirmative defense when he agreed to settle his appeal, and he waived his rights to complain further about the removal action by entering into the settlement agreement. See, e.g., Smith v. USPS, 9 MSPB 557, 10 MSPR 320, 321 (1982).

The appellant alleges on petition for review that the settlement agreement was coercive because the OWCP decision was "clearly an important part of my defense." A party challenging the validity of a settlement agreement carries a heavy burden and must show that the agreement was unlawful, involuntary, or the result of mutual mistake before it will be set aside. See Bowie v. USPS, 72 MSPR 42, 44 (1996). Nothing in the record or in the new evidence of the OWCP decision shows that the settlement agreement was invalid because of duress, fraud, or misinformation, or mutual mistake. The fact that the appellant agreed to settle his appeal while awaiting a decision from OWCP does not make the settlement agreement the result of coercion and the issuance of the OWCP decision does not provide good cause to excuse the untimeliness of his petition for review.
