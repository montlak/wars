c. Minimal Impact of Change

The de minimis effect of a bargainable change is not avoid a status quo order, USIA, Voice of Am. and NFFE Local 1418, 33 FLRA 549, 562 (1988):

The Respondent alleges that a status quo ante remedy should be denied because the change in preparation and cleanup periods had a "de minimis" impact.... In Department of Health and Human Services and Social Security Administration, 30 FLRA 922, 926 (1988), we held that where the decision to make the change is itself negotiable, the extent of the impact of a change in conditions of employment on unit employees is irrelevant in determining whether the agency had a duty to bargain. Therefore, Respondent's assertion provides no basis for denying a status quo ante remedy because the Respondent failed to fulfill its obligation to bargain over the decision to change conditions of employment.

But cf. Ass'n of ALJs v. FLRA, 397 F.3d 957, 959-64 (D.C. Cir. 2005) (FLRA could adopt rule that would apply de minimis approach to violations of substantive bargaining obligations).

Results vary. A decision to remove certain auditing duties from payroll clerks to establish a new position required impact bargaining, but the failure to bargain did not warrant an SQA remedy. The impact was minimal--the changes in job duties did not result in and were not intended by the agency to create a loss of grade or pay for any affected employee. DOD, Dept. of Army Hq., Ft. Sam Houston and AFGE Local 2154, 8 FLRA 623, 625 (1982). Yet in DHHS, SSA, Baltimore and AFGE Local 1164, 37 FLRA 278, 287 (1990), reassignment of one employee from a field to a claims representative position warranted an SQA remedy. The agency failed to show that its operations would be unduly disrupted by undoing the reassignment of one employee.

FLRA issued a General Statement of Policy or Guidance in Dept. of Education and USDA, 71 FLRA 968, 971 n.31 (2020) (Member DuBester dissenting), vacated, AFGE v. FLRA, 25 F.4th 1 (D.C. Cir. 2022), primarily directed to impact and implementation bargaining, but extending to substantive bargaining the rule that negotiations are limited to a change to that has a substantial impact on a condition of employment.
