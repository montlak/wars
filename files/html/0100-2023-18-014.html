3. Collective Bargaining Contract Violation

The Board enforces provisions of union agreements, equating them to agency regulations. Violations of those provisions are treated under the harmful error doctrine. Warren v. DOD, 87 MSPR 426, 431 ¶ 10 (2001) (equating a provision of a negotiated agreement to an agency policy or regulation for the purpose of an analysis of RIF assignment rights); Hall v. Dept. of Navy, 73 MSPR 251, 255 (1997) ("The Board will enforce binding provisions of a collective bargaining agreement in the same manner as it enforces agency regulations and will determine whether harmful error occurred."); Williams v. DHHS, 29 MSPR 525, 527 (1985) ("The Board will treat provisions of a union agreement in the same manner as agency regulations[.] "); Hicks v. Dept. of Treasury, 9 MSPR 506, 508-09 (1982) (Board enforces provisions of a negotiated agreement in the "same manner as agency regulations when provisions represent guiding principles and establish nondiscretionary policy under which the agency operates and have the effect of regulatory requirements"); Leazenby v. USPS, 8 MSPR 384, 390 (1981) (harmful error warranted reversal of removal when the agency failed to follow its collective bargaining contract by providing favorable consideration to an employee's efforts to rehabilitate himself from alcoholism); Giesler v. Dept. of Transp., 3 MSPR 277, 280, 3 MSPB 367 (1980) (provision of the union agreement is treated in the same manner as a provision of an agency regulation); cf. Gutkowski v. USPS, 505 F.3d 1324, 1328 (Fed. Cir. 2007) (suggesting but not deciding whether the Board could direct mitigation of a penalty by imposing demotion to a position when the Board's order would conflict with provisions of a negotiated agreement that regulated how the position would be filled).

Contract provisions are treated as internal, nondiscretionary agency policies. Wright v. USPS, 5 MSPR 242, 245 n.2, 5 MSPB 285 (1981); see Rowland-Clum v. Dept. of Army (Fed. Cir. 1996 NP No. 96-3159) (RIF procedural rights did not accrue to the petitioner through a contract section that required the union to be notified of functions scheduled for review under an agency program shifting work to contractors); Deweese v. TVA, 35 F.3d 538 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (reversing a RIF when the agency failed to follow contractual provisions governing both transfers and reassignment during the period of a RIF and the dissemination of information within the agency that would permit employees affected by a RIF to receive prior consideration for vacant positions being filled at other locations of the agency); Harvey v. USPS, 85 MSPR 43, 47 ¶ 8 (1999) ("The Board treats the provisions of a collective bargaining agreement as having the weight of regulations and requires agencies to comply with them."); Reed v. Dept. of Transp., 76 MSPR 126, 135 (1997) (record did not demonstrate that the failure of the agency to negotiate staffing levels with the union affected the outcome of a Board appeal); Coleman v. TVA, 23 MSPR 670, 675 (1984) (negotiated agreement provided justification for separate competitive levels); Jones v. District of Columbia Gov't, 13 MSPR 210, 211-12 (1982) (collective bargaining provisions calling for advance consultations before RIF and requiring the employer to make every effort to minimize the impact of RIFs did not establish harmful error when the provisions were not followed by the employer; the appellant did not establish that consultation would have affected RIF implementation); Craddock v. DHHS, 6 MSPR 231, 233, 6 MSPB 200 (1981) ("[T]he Board notes that in Giesler v. Department of Transportation, 3 MSPB 367, 368 (1980), we held that rights derived from collective bargaining agreements would be enforced by this Board in the same manner as provisions of an agency's regulations"); cf. Latham v. USPS, 117 MSPR 400, 406 n.5, 2012 MSPB 20 (2012) (USPS Employee and Labor Relations Manual: "The ELM has been incorporated by reference into the applicable collective bargaining agreements. National Agreement between the National Association of Letter Carriers and the U.S. Postal Service, Article 19; National Agreement Between the American Postal Workers Union and the U.S. Postal Service, Article 19. It has also been incorporated into the regulations governing the Postal Service. 39 C.F.R. § 211.2.") (Latham overruled on other grounds, Cronin v. USPS, 2022 MSPB 13 (2022)).

The AJ may consider a contract error, sua sponte, if the error implicates a basic procedural right when "cognizance of the error is necessary to prevent manifest injustice." See Luger v. VA, 29 MSPR 261, 262 n.* (1985). Whether it is the interpretation of the Board or an arbitrator that is sought, if a contract is relied upon, it must be placed into the record if the Board is to consider its effect. Parsons v. Dept. of Air Force, 6 MSPR 672, 673, 6 MSPB 564 (1981); see Hernandez v. Dept. of Air Force, 9 MSPR 12, 13 (1981) ("[T]he appellant's failure to introduce into evidence the union agreement or significant portions of it leads us to conclude that he has failed to meet his burden of proof as to this affirmative defense[.] ").

An example of Board interpretation of a negotiated agreement is Pleasant v. DHUD, 98 MSPR 602, 608-09 ¶¶ 8-11 (2005), reviewing an arbitrator's award:

In her request for review of the arbitrator's decision, the appellant challenges the arbitrator's findings regarding her claims that the agency's third charge constituted an infringement of her right to engage in union activities in violation of 5 U.S.C. § 7102 ("Employees' rights") and harmful procedural error in violation of the parties' collective bargaining agreement. The Board treats the provisions of a collective bargaining agreement to which the agency is a party in the same manner that it treats provisions of agency regulations, and it will enforce employee rights derived from such agreements. See Randle v. Department of the Navy, 50 M.S.P.R. 574, 581 (1991). In assessing an agency's violation of a collective bargaining agreement, the Board will determine whether harmful procedural error occurred. See Hall v. Department of the Navy, 73 M.S.P.R. 251, 255 (1997); see also Borden v. Department of Justice, 59 M.S.P.R. 353, 356 (1993) (a violation of the parties' collective bargaining agreement may constitute harmful procedural error warranting reversal of the agency's action). Harmful procedural error cannot be presumed; an agency error is harmful only where the record shows that the error was likely to have caused the agency to reach a conclusion different from the one it would have reached in the absence or cure of the error. Hidalgo v. Department of Justice, 93 M.S.P.R. 645 ¶ 13 (2003). The employee bears the burden of proving that the agency committed the harmful error. Id. ¶ 15.

The agency's third charge, which was upheld by the arbitrator, concerned a memorandum that was sent to the appellant dated August 27, 2002 concerning her use of official time. The memorandum advised the appellant that she must obtain prior approval before utilizing official time, pursuant to Article 7, Section 7.07 of the parties' CBA. The appellant first challenged the agency's instructions set forth in that memorandum in an unfair labor practice charge filed with the Federal Labor Relations Authority (FLRA). However, upon reviewing the evidence submitted in support of the charge, the FLRA found that the issuance of a complaint was not warranted.

Like the FLRA, the arbitrator in this case rejected the appellant's claim that the agency's instruction that she obtain approval before utilizing official time was contrary to past practice and, therefore, a violation of the parties' CBA and 5 U.S.C. § 7102. The arbitrator observed that at the time of the incidents giving rise to this case, the appellant was only authorized to spend 25% of her time on union business, whereas she had previously been authorized to spend 100% of her time on union business and, consequently, had not been required to seek prior approval before engaging in representational activities. The arbitrator also rejected the appellant's claim that the agency improperly instructed her to divulge the specific nature of her union business, in violation of 5 U.S.C. § 7102. According to the arbitrator, the agency only required that she provide general accounting codes related to her representational activities so that it would be able to accurately account for her official time use.

Based on our review of the limited material presented, as well as our review of the arbitration decision, we find that the appellant has failed to establish that the arbitrator erred in rejecting the foregoing claims. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.154(d)(4); Holly v. Department of Health & Human Services, 92 M.S.P.R. 601 ¶ 10 (2002), review dismissed, 69 Fed. Appx. 467 (Fed. Cir. 2003) (Table) (an appellant seeking review of an arbitration decision has the burden of providing the Board with the materials necessary to support matters raised on review, including a transcript or tape recording of the arbitration hearing). Accordingly, we find that they fail to provide a basis for setting aside or modifying the arbitrator's decision.

Contract interpretation having the effect of expanding the plain language of the agreement is appropriate when the provisions are ambiguous or the plain meaning serves to compel an irrational result. Powell v. Dept. of Army, 7 MSPR 199, 202, 7 MSPB 111 (1981). Poirier v. Dept. of Navy, 31 MSPR 214, 217 n.3 and 4 (1986), added as to contract interpretation:

[3] Appellant also alleges that the Board has no jurisdiction to interpret the terms of a collective bargaining agreement. We disagree. It is well settled that the Board has authority to interpret and enforce provisions of an applicable collective bargaining agreement that relates to an issue brought before it. See generally Letenyei v. Department of Transportation, FAA, 735 F.2d 528, 531 (Fed. Cir. 1984) (authority to interpret provision of collective bargaining agreement); see also Geisler v. Department of Transportation, 3 MSPB 367, 3 MSPR 277, 280 (1980) (Board will treat provisions of collective bargaining agreement to which agency is a party in the same manner as provisions of the agency's regulations). Thus, appellant's allegation that the Board has no authority to interpret the terms of a collective bargaining agreement is misplaced.

[4] We make this finding with the recognition that the collective bargaining agreement is a distinct type of contract not susceptible to interpretation exclusively under common-law concepts governing private contracts. Instead, it is necessary to consider the entire employment relationship reflecting the common law of the particular industry. See United Steelworkers of America v. Warrior & Gulf Nav. Co., 363 U.S. 574, 578-579, 80 S. Ct. 1347, 1351, 4 L. Ed.2d 1409 (1960).

The arguable obligation to negotiate expressed in Executive Order 12871 (reprinted after 5 USC 7101 and requiring agencies to negotiate over certain permissive subjects of bargaining under 5 USC 7106(b)(1)), created no enforceable right of administrative or judicial review. Monk v. Dept. of Navy, 68 MSPR 560, 562-63 (1995) (still enforcing a memorandum of understanding with the union that called for the employer to use OPM authority permitting waiver of qualification standards when it had the opportunity to fill a vacant position during a RIF), followed in Reed v. Dept. of Transp., 76 MSPR 126, 135 (1997) ("[A] provision requiring an agency and a union to negotiate does not create an enforceable right of administrative or judicial review unless it explicitly affords such a right."). Yet negotiability law developed by the FLRA and reviewing courts may be superimposed on contract requirements. See Devine v. Brisco, 733 F.2d 867 (Fed. Cir. 1984) (applying negotiability law to an arbitrator's decision to reduce a penalty after the arbitrator concluded that the contract barred the discipline as stale). [Refer to Chapter 17, "Arbitration Award" for Federal Circuit decisions reviewing labor contracts and awards that involve issues of harmful error.]

The Board considers arguments that labor contract provisions are illegal. The Board rejected an argument that a transfer provision of an agreement (pertaining to the transfer that appellant refused to accept) violated the Veterans Preference Act. Champion v. TVA, 21 MSPR 561, 562 (1984). A provision of a collective bargaining agreement that is contrary to a government-wide regulation will not be enforced by the Board, according to Doe v. PBGC, 117 MSPR 579, 591 n.9, 2012 MSPB 42 (2012), involving a provision permitting an agency-directed fitness for duty examination that was prohibited by OPM regulations:

The Board treats provisions of a collective bargaining agreement in the same manner as agency regulations. See Giesler v. Department of Transportation, 3 M.S.P.R. 277, 280 (1980), aff'd sub nom. Giesler v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 686 F.2d 844 (10th Cir. 1982). While this long-standing rule typically results in the enforcement of a contractual obligation assumed by the agency, see id., where, as here, the collective bargaining provision conflicts with a government-wide regulation, the Board will not give it effect. Cf. Dodson v. Department of the Navy, 111 M.S.P.R. 504, 508 (2009) (declining to give effect to agency regulations under the National Security Personnel System that appeared to be in conflict with government-wide regulations and statute).

If an agency commits harmful error administering a negotiated agreement, the removal of an appellant may not be sustained. Stalkfleet v. USPS, 6 MSPR 637, 641, 6 MSPB 536 (1981). Harmful error occurred when the contract required the agency to reassign disabled employees across crafts if the reassignment would permit continued employment. By failing to reassign the employee, the agency may have denied him an opportunity for employment. The discharge was reversed. Lunkin v. USPS, 20 MSPR 220, 223 (1984). An agency committed harmful error by placing an injured employee on enforced leave instead of following a contract provision requiring placement of the employee on light duty. Borden v. DOJ, 59 MSPR 353, 356 (1993). [Refer to light duty, enforced leave, and related contractual issues in Chapters 6 and 12.]

In Crumpton v. SSA, 6 MSPR 479, 482, 6 MSPB 405 (1981), the contract provided that the employee could have a union representative present during an interview with management involving conduct that might lead to discipline. The employee was not provided that right, but he was allowed representation at a later meeting with management. Although the contract was violated, the error was not harmful; there was no demonstration that the outcome of the appeal might have been different had a representative been present. See Robinson v. USPS, 28 MSPR 681, 687 (1985) [this and other decisions are discussed later in this chapter, "Right to Counsel; Union Representative"]. The same was true as to the alleged failure of an agency to permit union representation at training or instruction sessions and at performance discussions. Munisteri v. Dept. of Army, 14 MSPR 317, 318-19 (1983). The failure of an agency to advise an employee of his grievance rights as to an adverse action was not harmful error if the employee brought his case to the Board. Any possible error concerning grievance rights was beyond the scope of the Board's authority. Mascol v. Dept. of Navy, 7 MSPR 565, 568, 7 MSPB 408 (1981). Bogumill v. VA, DE-0752-09-0507-I-1 (NP 1/26/2011), provides an example of the Board's treatment of an assertion of denial of union representational rights:

...Further, the record contains sufficient evidence to uphold the agency's charges even if we exclude the evidence that the appellant asserts is tainted by the agency's alleged violation of her rights pursuant to Miranda not to self-incriminate or of her representation rights under her union agreement. See, e.g., Miguel v. Department of the Army, 727 F.2d 1081, 1086 (Fed. Cir. 1984); see also McLaughlin v. U.S. Postal Service, 55 M.S.P.R. 192, 203 (1992) (the Board need not reach whether an appellant's constitutional rights were violated if the record contains sufficient untainted evidence to support the charge).

Reversible error was not committed by an agency's reliance on supervisors' notes, prohibited by a negotiated agreement, without a showing of harmful error. Brown v. VA, 32 MSPR 696, 697-98 (1987). An agency's reliance on past discipline, in violation of a contract precluding use of aged incidents of past discipline to support a current action, was improper, but harmless, error. The severity of the current offense--striking a supervisor--alone supported removal. Deutsch v. USPS, 39 MSPR 386, 389-90 (1989) (contract prohibited reliance on prior discipline over two years old). Violation of a contract provision requiring the initiation of disciplinary action within 60 days from the agency's knowledge of the infraction was subject to the harmful error rule; lacking a demonstration of harm, the error was of no consequence to the initiation of the adverse action. Clark v. EEOC, 42 MSPR 467, 470 n.1 (1989); cf. Parker v. Dept. of Navy, 50 MSPR 343, 347 n.3 (1991) (no harmful error when the agency failed to give a 60-day, contractually required, advance notice of adverse action; the appellant did not show that the error likely had a harmful effect upon the outcome of the case before the agency); Pope v. Dept. of Navy, 44 MSPR 289, 295-96 (1990) (60-day limit was permissibly avoided because of additional offenses committed by the appellant prior to initiation of the adverse action). [Refer later in this chapter to "Stale Charges; Laches."] No harmful error occurred when an agency suspended an employee for failing to respond to an emergency immediately. The contract required the agency to notify the union that an emergency existed, but agency regulations required the employee to respond. Callery v. DOJ, 50 MSPR 158, 163 (1991).

Arguably untimely processing of an adverse action under the provisions of a labor agreement was not harmful error, McInnis v. Dept. of Educ., 679 Fed. Appx. 998, 1001 (Fed. Cir. 2017 NP):

See 5 U.S.C. § 7701(c)(2)(A) (providing that the board cannot sustain an agency decision if the employee "shows harmful error in the application of the agency's procedures in arriving at such decision"); Ward v. USPS, 634 F.3d 1274, 1281 (Fed. Cir. 2011) (emphasizing that the board is "required to run a harmless error analysis to determine whether [a] procedural error require[s] reversal"): Diaz v. Dep't of the Air Force, 63 F.3d 1107, 1109 (Fed. Newman v. Dept. of Air Force, 842 Fed. Appx. 563 (Fed. Cir. 2021 NP), also considered the timeliness provision of a labor agreement:

Because § 5.01.b of the MLA states that the "Employer will strive to effect disciplinary action within...45 days of the offense," it "articulate[s] a goal, rather than a mandatory time limit." Bross v. Dept. of Commerce, 94 MSPR 662, 666-67 ¶¶ 8-13 (2003), aff'd, Bross v. Dept. of Commerce, 389 F.3d 1212 (Fed. Cir. 2004), post, considered the impact of a clause of a memorandum of understanding negotiated with the union that set the level of the deciding official to normally be at the level of a division chief, except in the situation of precedent setting cases, allowing for the deciding official to be at a higher level. The appellant was charged with using a government computer to access child pornography. The Board omitted to state why the deciding official was at a level higher than the division chief, but from the nature of the harmful procedural error issue presented to the Board, it can be presumed that there was proof that the result (removal of the employee) could have been different had the division chief decided the case. Without examining or commenting on any evidence that may have been offered concerning the negotiating history of the MOU, the Board declared the case to be precedential, justifying the agency's elevation of the level of the deciding official:

The administrative judge found that the agency violated an MOU with the American Federation of Government Employees (AFGE) when it assigned Dr. Clark to serve, in place of Dr. Wright, as the proposing and deciding official, (This opinion will generally refer only to the "deciding official" since it is uncontested that whomever the agency designated would act as both the proposing and the deciding official.)

In September 1985, the agency and the AFGE entered into an MOU establishing a procedure for designating "proposing/deciding officials." Paragraph 1 of the MOU provides as follows: "Division Chiefs or their equivalents will normally serve as both Proposing and Deciding Official on conduct-based actions." Paragraph 3(b) of the MOU states, in relevant part, that a "higher level management official will be the Proposing and Deciding Official for...conduct-based actions in precedent-setting cases." Thus, under the terms of the MOU, Division Chiefs or their equivalents would "normally" act as deciding officials, but the agency reserved the right to name a higher-ranking official as the deciding official in "precedent-setting cases" involving misconduct.

Dr. Wright was the Chief of the Statistical Research Division where the appellant was assigned. Under the MOU, he or his equivalent would "normally" be designated as the deciding official when the charged misconduct concerned an employee in the Statistical Research Division. The agency has shown, however, that this was a "precedent-setting" case which allowed it under the MOU to designate Dr. Clark as the deciding official.

The MOU's provision that division chiefs or equivalent officials will "normally" serve as proposing and deciding officials indicates that the agency and the union regarded most disciplinary decisions as other than "precedent-setting," as they used that term. Indeed, the record shows that the agency has rarely invoked this provision.[2] Under these circumstances, we find that the provision at issue here covers cases that differ significantly from those the agency has handled previously, i.e., that it covers cases that will set a precedent in an area where there previously was none.

[2] The assistant chief of the agency's human relations division testified that there had been only one other case, since the CBA went into effect in 1985, in which an official higher than the division chief served as the deciding official in an action against a member of the bargaining unit. She also indicated that a higher-level official was designated because the division chief "had not been to work in a long time," because he "was afraid that the employee might decide to go after him," and because "he just didn't want to do anything." Evidently, therefore, the present case is the only one in which the agency has invoked the "precedent-setting" provision. We do not conclude from this, however, that the agency must distinguish this case from all other cases since 1985. The MOU provision at issue here does not require that all "precedent-setting" cases must be decided by an official above the division director level. Instead, it only provides that they "may be" decided by such an official.

The present case meets this criterion. The agency submitted unrebutted evidence that no Census Bureau employee had ever been accused of or charged with the type of misconduct in which the appellant had engaged. A search of the agency's records revealed that although prior cases involved misuse of agency computers, they dealt with matters such as using the computers for homeowners' association business or personal tax matters. At the union's request, the agency looked for any other case in which a Census Bureau employee may have used agency Internet access and computer equipment to download and possess images of children engaged in sexually graphic activities. It found no such case. Moreover, we find that the criminal nature of the appellant's use of the computer distinguishes this case significantly from the other unauthorized uses mentioned above. Thus, on its facts, this case was precedent-setting.

For the reasons stated above, we find that the agency was authorized to designate, as the deciding official in this case, an official above the division director level, that the appellant accordingly has not shown that the agency violated the MOU, and that he therefore has not substantiated his claim of harmful procedural error.

On review, the Board's decision was affirmed, although for reasons different than those expressed by the Board, Bross v. Dept. of Commerce, 389 F.3d 1212, 1216-17 (Fed. Cir. 2004):

We think the agreement is clear that the question of discipline must be addressed by the Division Chief or equivalent, unless (1) a higher level of management makes a determination to serve as the Proposing and Deciding Official; (2) that decision is based on the precedent-setting nature of the case; and (3) that the determination of the precedent-setting nature of the case is reasonable. The appellant contends that none of these requirements was satisfied here.

Bross first contends that the decision to elevate a case under Paragraph 3(a) of the MOU must be made explicitly by a high-level management official and that no such determination was made in this case. Rather, he contends that the decision to displace Wright as the Proposing and Deciding Official was made by personnel staff. Commerce contends that the decision to elevate was made in this case by Clark. The undisputed record supports Commerce's view that Clark made this determination. Clark indicated to Wright during their first meeting that she "would likely be the...proposing and deciding official in the case." While the record demonstrates that staff from personnel did encourage Clark to take the case, as Bross himself acknowledged, there is no evidence in the record that "the MOU placed the personnel shop in other than an advisory role." The fact that other members of the agency at the time also decided that the matter should be elevated to Clark's level does not establish that Clark herself did not make the decision.

Second, the appellant claims that Clark did not herself make the determination that the case was precedent-setting. While Clark did not explicitly state that she made herself the decision-maker because of the precedent-setting nature of the case, she was advised that "there had not been similar offenses [and] ...it was one of the most serious offenses that the Census Bureau [had] dealt with." She also made it clear that she believed her authority to displace Wright rested in the MOU. Although it would have been preferable for Clark's determination of the precedent-setting nature of the case to have been made in a more explicit fashion, the plain language of the MOU does not so require; Paragraph 3(a) simply states that a "higher level management official may be the Proposing and Deciding Official for...conduct-based actions in precedent-setting cases."

Finally, Bross argues that even if a determination was made as to the precedent-setting nature of Bross's case, it was unreasonable. We disagree. We have no difficulty concluding on this record that substantial evidence supports the Board's conclusion that the determination that Bross's case was precedent-setting was reasonable.

While Bross's case was not the first case to involve unauthorized use of an agency computer, the parties agree that it was the first case involving the unauthorized use of a computer to access child pornography. The Board also found that the criminal nature of Bross's use of the computer distinguished this case significantly from other Census cases, and that "on its facts, [Bross's] case was precedent-setting." 94 M.S.P.R. at 667. We agree. The reasonableness of this determination is also demonstrated by the validity of the agency's concerns that unlimited access by the probation officer to Bross's work computer under the terms of his parole might negatively affect public trust in the Census Bureau's ability to protect the confidential and private nature of individual data.

Bross also argues that it is unreasonable to elevate a precedent-setting case under Paragraph 3(a) of the MOU for the purpose of providing a harsher penalty. We disagree. The MOU does not bar the agency from factoring in the likelihood that a Division Chief's potential decision will be overly lenient (or overly harsh) in relation to the underlying conduct, and the impact that the imposition of a particular penalty would have on the functioning of the agency in the future. Clark was plainly concerned that Wright might not impose sufficient discipline, and that the imposition of insufficient discipline would create an undesirable precedent for the agency. She specifically noted "the serious nature of the incident, the misdemeanor and because [Wright] and his staff seemed--appeared to be so closely involved that they weren't recognizing the potential for adverse implications on the Census Bureau." Personnel officials advising Clark and responsible for assessing the impact of individual adverse action determinations upon the rest of the agency found that "it would have created a very bad precedent to have had a very light penalty or a light penalty in this kind of situation." We hold that it is reasonable to consider the impact that the penalty imposed in a precedential case will have on the rest of the agency when deciding to elevate the case to a higher-level official.
