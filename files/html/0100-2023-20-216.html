d. Preliminary Reprisal Leading to Personnel Action

The Board demonstrated its willingness to review collateral events, e.g., past discipline and performance appraisals, used by agencies to justify actions attacked as retaliatory, e.g., nonselection for promotion. The Board sustained an AJ's finding of reprisal in Cosgrove v. Dept. of Navy, 59 MSPR 618, 624-26 (1993), as it examined past events relied on by the agency to justify its nonselection of the appellant for a competitive promotion:

The agency asserts that the Board's review of a prior disciplinary action is limited to determining whether that action is clearly erroneous, if the employee was informed of the action in writing, was given an opportunity to dispute it to a different authority, and the action was made a matter of record, citing Bolling v. Department of Air Force, 9 MSPR 335, 339-40 (1981), and it correctly notes that those criteria were satisfied with regard to the letter of reprimand.

The rule set forth in Bolling is applicable in adverse action appeals, where the issue is whether the appellant's prior disciplinary record shows that a stiffer penalty is warranted for his misconduct than might otherwise be the case in the absence of such a prior disciplinary record, given the established preference for progressive discipline in Federal employment. Bolling, 9 MSPR at 340. With regard to an IRA appeal, however, where the agency bears the burden of proof by "clear and convincing evidence" that it would have taken the same personnel action in the absence of an appellant's whistleblowing, the case of Thompson v. Farm Credit Administration, 51 MSPR 569 (1991), is more pertinent and instructive.

In Thompson, the Board found that the agency's establishment of one of the appellant's performance elements as "critical" was based on a retaliatory motive, Thompson, 51 MSPR at 583-84, although the general rule with regard to the Board's review of an agency's performance standards in performance appeals under 5 USC chapter 43 is "limited to determining whether the agency has abused its discretion in establishing the [performance] standards at issue so as to cause harm to the employee to whom the standards were applied." Siegelman v. Department of Housing and Urban Development, 14 MSPR 326, 330 (1983). Further, the Board in Thompson noted, and considered retaliatory, the fact that the appellant there had been issued an official reprimand for the unauthorized distribution of information to persons outside of the agency "despite the appellant's belief that he acted properly." Thompson, 51 MSPR at 581.

Thus, we hold that, in an IRA appeal, where the appellant has met his burden of proof of showing that retaliation for his whistleblowing was a contributing factor to the personnel action at issue, it is not error for the administrative judge to closely scrutinize the appellant's past disciplinary record for further evidence of possible retaliatory animus by the agency. Moreover, it behooves an agency, when faced with a "clear and convincing evidence" standard of proof, to fully explain all of its potentially questionable actions in order to meet that burden.

In this case, the administrative judge correctly found that the appellant's letter of reprimand, in and of itself and unaccompanied by its referenced documentation, failed to assist the agency in meeting its strict burden of proof.

Also, the Board adopts the administrative judge's finding that the agency did not show that the appellant was not selected for the GS-12 Supervisory Auditor position because the person selected was better qualified, had a better performance record, and the appellant refused to do "audits" and did "reviews" instead. The fact that the selectee had been a CE Officer for 4 months and the appellant had not is not persuasive evidence that she was more qualified than the appellant because she was not a GS-511 Auditor while she held the position of CE Officer. Also, there cannot be a fair comparison of the appellant's ratings and the selectee's because the appellant's performance standards were written, albeit not intentionally, in such a way that he could not receive a higher rating than "fully satisfactory." Further, the negative comments on the appellant's annual ratings were in the main not related to his performance standards or critical elements, and could reasonably be inferred to be a reference to either his whistleblowing activities, or the controversy being generated by his unpopular audits, or both. Finally, the appellant continued to perform all of the tasks necessary to an audit, but he called his actions "reviews" in order not to be a participant in the command's violations of the regulations with respect to appointments to assure proper audit standards.

Reprisal may occur as a result of a preliminary action, taken in reprisal against an individual, that leads to another action that may not be directly motivated by reprisal. Bonggat v. Dept. of Navy, 56 MSPR 402, 413 (1993), having determined that the reassignment was a reprisal, reversed a subsequent disability separation:

Concerning final relief in this appeal, the Board has held that once a personnel action has been reversed, an agency's obligation is to place the appellant as nearly as possible in the status quo ante; the agency must return the appellant as nearly as possible to the situation he would have been in if the action in question had never occurred.... Reversal of the reassignment here thus appears to require the appellant's return to his old position as Housing Manager and reversal of his separation, after reassignment, for disability in the position of Budget Analyst. If the appellant's medical problems continue after reinstatement and prevent him from performing the duties of Housing Manager, the agency can again propose separation for disability. But restoration to the status quo ante first requires the appellant's return to his old position. See Davis, 49 MSPR at 449.

Cosgrove was not mentioned, as the Board declined to examined past personnel actions as evidence of reprisal under the whistleblowing or EEO laws in Webster v. Dept. of Energy, DC-0752-13-0280-I-2 (NP 7/6/2015):

Regarding the appellant's contention that she was unable to prove this affirmative defense because the administrative judge "dismissed nine of [her] other related issues," the appellant is apparently referring to the nine actions that she raised in her January 2012 EEO complaint, as amended, in addition to her removal. The appellant seems to contend that she should have been allowed to argue the merits of these actions because they are "inextricably intertwined" with her removal and her whistleblowing activities.

The Board's jurisdiction is limited to those matters over which it has been given jurisdiction by law, rule or regulation. Maddox v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 759 F.2d 9, 10 (Fed. Cir. 1985). With the exception of the removal, none of the actions raised in the appellant's January 2012 EEO complaint, as amended, are within the Board's jurisdiction. See 5 C.F.R. § 1201.3(a)(1)-(11). The Board has previously held that nonappealable actions, even when intertwined with otherwise appealable actions, may be excluded from a hearing because they fall outside the Board's jurisdiction. See, e.g., Wilson v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 102 M.S.P.R. 70, ¶ 8 (2006) (excluding all issues other than an appealable 30-day suspension and the appellant's affirmative defenses related solely thereto); Lethridge v. U.S. Postal Service, 99 M.S.P.R. 675, ¶ 9 (2005) (nothing suggests that the Board has jurisdiction over nonappealable actions even when they are allegedly "inextricably intertwined" with appealable actions). Thus, the scope of this adverse action appeal is limited to the appellant's removal and the affirmative defenses she raised in connection with her removal.
