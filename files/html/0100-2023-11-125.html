f. Suitability Determination

Sheehan v. Dept. of Army, 43 MSPR 204, 207 (1990), declined to apply collateral estoppel to a negative suitability determination that the agency sought to use against the appellant in a removal case:

The Board recognizes three threshold requirements for the application of collateral estoppel: (1) The issue to be resolved must be identical to that involved in the prior action; (2) the issue must have been actually litigated in the prior action; and (3) the determination of the issue in the prior action must have been necessary and essential to the resulting judgment. Even if these three requirements have been met, however, the Board may refrain from applying collateral estoppel if its application would result in injustice or compromise public policy. See, e.g., Lehman v. OPM, 40 MSPR 286, 288-89 (1989). With respect to the identity-of-issues requirement, the Board has found that differences precluding the application of collateral estoppel may be in a range of matters including the facts, subject, periods of time, case law, statutes, procedural protections, notions of public interest, or qualifications of tribunals. See Siegert v. Dept. of Army, 38 MSPR 684, 688 (1988).

After considering these standards, we find that the collateral estoppel requirements have not been met in the present case. In Sheehan v. OPM, [43 MSPR 204 (1990),] the administrative judge did not decide whether the penalty imposed by the agency--removal--was reasonable. The Board has found that such a decision is separate from that of whether the agency proved the merits of the charge and showed that discipline promoted the efficiency of the service, and necessary in any adverse action appeal.... Cf. Hurlbut v. OPM, 36 MSPR 250, 254-56 (1988) (in an appeal of a negative suitability determination, the administrative judge did not err in failing to apply the doctrine of collateral estoppel to prevent the appellant from relitigating a past misconduct charge for which the Board had sustained the appellant's removal).

Hurlbut v. OPM, 36 MSPR 250, 255-56 (1988), abstained from applying collateral estoppel in an appeal from a negative suitability determination after the appellant was removed for poor workmanship:

The record in this appeal discloses that all of the prerequisites for the application of the doctrine of collateral estoppel have not been met. The issues involved in this action simply are not identical to the issues involved in the prior action. The issues involved in the removal action were: (1) Whether the appellant committed the acts of misconduct for which he was removed; (2) whether disciplinary action for those charges would promote the efficiency of the service; and (3) whether the penalty of removal for the sustained misconduct charges was within the tolerable bounds of reasonableness. The issues involved in this appeal are framed by the agency's own regulations. Section 731.202(a) of Title 5 of the Code of Federal Regulations provides that the agency shall make its determination that an individual is unsuitable for appointment on the basis of: (1) Whether the conduct of the individual may reasonably be expected to interfere with or prevent the effective performance in the position applied for; or (2) whether the conduct of the individual may reasonably be expected to interfere with or prevent effective performance by the employing agency of its duties and responsibilities.

Contrary to the agency's contention in its petition for review, the issues involved in this action were not litigated in the removal action. The Board's decision sustaining the appellant's removal from Federal employment made no findings concerning whether the appellant's conduct may reasonably be expected to interfere with or prevent performance in a position he subsequently applied for. Similarly, the removal decision made no findings concerning whether the appellant's conduct may reasonably be expected to interfere with, or prevent the effective performance by an employing agency of its duties or responsibilities. The Board's decision sustaining the appellant's removal only concerned whether he should have been removed from the position of Engineering Technician and did not consider his suitability for other positions.

Since the removal decision did not consider the issues or factors involved in a suitability determination, we agree with the administrative judge's determination that the appellant's removal from a prior position did not preclude her from finding that the appellant was eligible for future Federal employment....
