d. Disability Accommodation

The need for a disability accommodation does not preclude adverse action for failure to properly request leave through required documentation. Oates v. Dept. of Labor, CB-7121-12-0022-V-1 (NP 5/2/2013), reviewing an arbitrator's award, explained:

The appellant argues that the agency's action was based on disability discrimination and should be overturned for that reason.[7] Specifically, he argues that the agency was aware of his disability, and that he made several oral requests for accommodation in the form of a modified work schedule. He alleges that he turned in the accommodation forms that the agency requested, but, even without those forms, the agency was still required to engage in the interactive process with him. Finally, the appellant argues that the agency had previously accommodated him with a modified schedule, and his attendance problems began only when the agency took that accommodation away.

[7] The arbitrator made no finding on whether the appellant was "disabled" under the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990. We note that the broader definition of "disabled" under the Americans with Disabilities Amendments Act of 2008 does not apply in this case because the cause of action arose prior to the effective date of that statute. See Auston v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 01-2011-0219, 2012 EEOPUB LEXIS 2119, at *1 n.1 (July 19, 2012). For purposes of analysis, we assume without deciding that the appellant was, in fact, "disabled" under the law due to his back pain, depression, and sleep apnea.

As an initial matter, we find that the appellant's disability was only indirectly related to his removal. We find it likely that many of the appellant's unscheduled absences were occasioned by his medical and psychological conditions. However, as explained above, the appellant was not removed for excessive absences. Cf. Martinez v. General Services Administration, EEOC Appeal No. 0120122326, 2012 EEOPUB LEXIS 3347, at *23 (Nov. 15, 2012). Rather, he was removed for failing to get his absences approved by providing proper documentation to the agency. The appellant does not allege that his disability prevented him from providing the required documents, and he has not explained how a modified work schedule would have facilitated his ability to do so. See Thom v. Department of the Army, 114 M.S.P.R. 169, ¶ 6 (2010). We are unaware of any civil service law, rule, or regulation to prevent the agency from requiring documentation for disability-related absences.

We acknowledge that the accommodation issue is arguably material to this appeal because a modified work schedule may have enabled the appellant to avoid the unscheduled absences in the first place, thereby relieving him of the need to document them. We also acknowledge that the appellant may have been accommodated with a modified work schedule in the past.[8] However, we find no basis in civil service law to disturb the arbitrator's reasoned and explained finding that the appellant was at fault for failing to engage in an interactive process that might have resulted in an accommodation during the time period at issue. The arbitrator found that the agency responded to the appellant's oral accommodation request and participated in the interactive process by meeting with him, discussing the matter, and providing him forms to complete. Although we agree with the appellant that he was not required to use these forms to make his initial accommodation request, there was nothing wrong with the agency's requiring him to fill out those forms and return them to management as the next step in the interactive process. See Equal Employment Opportunity Commission Notice 915.002, Enforcement Guidance: Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship under the Americans with Disabilities Act, Question 3 (Oct. 17, 2002), available at http://www.eeoc.gov/policy/docs/accommodation.html [now at https://www.eeoc.gov/laws/guidance/enforcement-guidance-reasonable-accommodation-and-undue-hardship-under-ada]. The interactive process is a two-way street, and both parties have an obligation to participate in good faith. Collins v. U.S. Postal Service, 100 M.S.P.R. 332, ¶ 11 (2005). Under the circumstances, we see no basis to disturb the arbitrator's finding that the appellant was responsible for the breakdown of the process when he failed to reciprocate with the agency. To the extent that the appellant is challenging the arbitrator's finding that he failed to fill out and return the forms as instructed, this is beyond the scope of our review. See Kirkland, 119 M.S.P.R. 74, ¶ 12 (absent legal error, the Board will defer to an arbitrator's findings of fact).
