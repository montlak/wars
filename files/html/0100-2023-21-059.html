d. Content of Medical Report

"The persuasiveness of a medical report stems from its explanation of how certain aspects of a particular condition render the employee unable to perform specific work requirements. Wagoner v. OPM, 7 M.S.P.R. 277, 279 (1981). Further, objective clinical findings, medical diagnoses, subjective evidence of pain and other evidence showing work restrictions are the factors to be assessed in determining one's entitlement to disability retirement benefits." Tebay v. OPM, 24 MSPR 92, 94 (1984). Wagoner v. OPM, 7 MSPR 277, 279 (1981), explained the importance of medical reports that provide detailed analysis of a medical condition and its effects in a disability retirement appeal:

As is often the case, the medical reports supporting the appellant's application diagnose the appellant's problem, mention that pain is an attendant symptom, and then conclude that the appellant is totally disabled. Reports of this nature are not persuasive because they do not explain exactly how the appellant's particular condition renders him totally disabled, that is, how certain aspects of the condition render the appellant unable to perform specific requirements of the grade or class of his position. Such reports fail to answer the logical questions of whether it is the pain alone which is disabling, whether the pain can be controlled to the extent that the appellant can continue working, or whether the appellant may be disabled by pain on some occasions but able to work on others. The only straightforward testimony in this regard is that of the appellant, but even his testimony does not establish how his constant pain totally disables him.

See Grewe v. OPM, 12 MSPR 560, 563 (1982) ("The record does not show, however, the amount of time spent on these functions as compared to the many others in his position, or whether reasonable alternative methods of accomplishing these tasks could be used. Cf. McClain v. OPM, MSPB Docket No. SF831L8010296 (November 19, 1981); Bruno v. OPM, [11 MSPR 3] 10 MSPB 1 (1982). Further, the medical reports do not prove that appellant's pain is disabling or whether it may be controlled. Wagoner v. OPM, 7 MSPB 174 [7 MSPR 277] (1981)."); cf. Perreira v. DHHS, 33 F.3d 1375, 1377 n.6 (Fed. Cir. 1994) (National Childhood Vaccine Injury Act case: "An expert opinion is no better than the soundness of the reasons supporting it. Fehrs v. United States, 620 F.2d 255, 265 (Ct. Cl. 1980); cf. Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals, Inc., 113 S. Ct. 2786, 125 L. Ed.2d 469 (1993)."). [Refer in Chapter 4 to "Evidence in Disability Annuity Appeal; Chavez; Post-Separation Evidence."]

Medical reports are evaluated on their completeness; but not all medical reports or documentation are necessary to support an application, c (NP 4/26/2023):

...We disagree with OPM's reading of Christopherson and find that it does not create an affirmative obligation for the appellant to provide all underlying medical documentation in order for a medical opinion or record to be deemed competent. In Christopherson, 119 M.S.P.R. 635, ¶ 10, there appeared to be no medical reports in the record concerning the appellant's medical conditions; instead, there were only documents that referenced medical reports concerning such medical conditions. The Board in Christopherson found that OPM rebutted the presumption of disability by showing a lack of medical evidence explaining how the appellant was unable to perform her specific work requirements, prevented her from being regular in attendance, caused her to act inappropriately or, alternatively, that her medical conditions were inconsistent with work in general, in a particular line or work, or in a particular type of work setting. Id. Here, however, the appellant provided medical reports from two separate providers that described his PTSD diagnosis, detailed the significant impact of the 2016 active shooter incident on his PTSD, and supported his burden for proving his entitlement to disability retirement benefits....

See Mathias v. OPM, AT-844E-20-0743-I-1 (NP 4/27/2023) ("In reaching her conclusion, the administrative judge properly considered the December 2018 physical examination and audiogram from before the appellant's resignation, his post-resignation functional capacity evaluation, and testimony from the appellant and his wife. See Henderson, 117 M.S.P.R. 313, ¶ 19 (noting that the Board should consider objective clinical findings, diagnosis and medical opinions, and subjective evidence of pain and disability in determining entitlement to disability retirement). Indeed, nothing in the law mandates that a single provider tie all of this evidence together. Henderson, 117 M.S.P.R. 313, ¶ 19.").
