a. OPM

The OPM Director intervenes of right under 5 USC 7701(d)(1) (involving any case interpreting or applying any civil service law, rule, or regulation under the jurisdiction of OPM). 5 CFR 1201.34(b)(1) (2022). OPM's intervention motion must be filed at the earliest practicable time. 5 CFR 1201.34(b)(1) (2022). See Uhlig v. DOJ, 86 MSPR 660, 664 ¶ 6 (2000) ("In any case under 5 USC § 7701 before the Board in which the interpretation or application of any civil service law, rule, or regulation under the jurisdiction of OPM is at issue, and in which the Director of OPM is of the opinion that an erroneous decision would have a substantial impact on any civil service law, rule, or regulation under the jurisdiction of OPM, the Director may, as a matter of right, intervene. 5 USC § 7701(d)."); Martin v. Dept. of Air Force, 79 MSPR 380, 382 n.2 (1998) (OPM was permitted to intervene concerning the Board's interpretation of the Back Pay Act, a statute within OPM's administrative responsibility); Harris v. USDA, 50 MSPR 686, 689-90 & n.1 (1991) ("Because the Director...challenges the Board's interpretation of the provisions of the Back Pay Act and Chapter 83 of Title 5, which OPM is responsible for administering under 5 USC 5596(c) (1982) and 5 USC 8347 (1982) respectively, we find that the requirements for OPM intervention are met."; noting that OPM intervened as early in the proceeding as practicable, 13 working days after the appellant's reply to the agency response in a compliance proceeding); Former CSA Employees v. DHHS, 14 MSPR 371, 372 (1983) (restating statutory and regulatory requirements governing OPM Director's intervention). [Refer to Chapter 17, "Review Request by OPM," relative to OPM requests for judicial review; refer to Chapter 5, "OPM Reconsideration Requests."]

Parties should recall the distinction between service and receipt when they respond to OPM's intervention brief, Scott v. DOJ, 69 MSPR 211, 220 n.1 (1995):

A party may file a response to the Director's brief on intervention within 15 days of the "date of service" of the brief. 5 CFR § 1201.114(g)(1). The record on review closes on expiration of the period for filing the response to the brief on intervention. 5 CFR § 1201.114(i). The Board's regulations define "date of service" as the date on which documents are served on other parties, and "service" as "the process of furnishing a copy of any pleading to Board officials or other parties." 5 CFR § 1201.4(i), (j). A party completes the process of furnishing a copy of a pleading to another party when he or she deposits a copy of the pleading in the mail. See Riley v. Dept. of the Army, 53 MSPR 683, 687 (1992). Accordingly, a response to a brief on intervention must be filed within 15 days after the date on which the envelope containing the service copy of the petition has been postmarked. See id. In the absence of evidence to the contrary, the Board will presume that a document served by mail was mailed on the date given in the certificate of service. See Sargent v. Dept. of the Air Force, 55 MSPR 387, 397 n.8 (1992).

Here, OPM's brief was served upon the parties by mail on August 24, 1994, as indicated by the certificate of service accompanying the brief. Thus, the parties should have filed their responses within 15 days of August 24, 1994, or by September 8, 1994. Because the responses to OPM's brief were filed after the deadline and were not accompanied by motions showing good cause for the untimely filings, we have not considered them. See 5 CFR § 1201.114(f).

OPM may intervene when a case is heard through PFR following a remand decision, Joyce v. Dept. of Air Force, 83 MSPR 666, 669 ¶¶ 4-5 (1999):

In any case under 5 USC § 7701 before the Board in which the interpretation or application of any civil service law, rule, or regulation, under the jurisdiction of OPM is at issue, and in which the Director of OPM is of the opinion that an erroneous decision would have a substantial impact on any civil service law, rule, or regulation under the jurisdiction of OPM, the Director may, as a matter of right, intervene. 5 USC § 7701(d). If the Director exercises the right to participate in a proceeding before the Board, the Director shall do so as early in the proceeding as practicable. Id.

Despite the appellant's arguments to the contrary, OPM may intervene in this proceeding even though it is challenging the Board's determinations in Joyce, and not the AJ's findings on remand. See, e.g., Martin v. Department of the Air Force, 79 MSPR 380, 382-83 (1998) (granting the Director's request to intervene and challenge the full Board's remand decision after a petition for review had been filed challenging the remand initial decision), reversed on other grounds, Martin v. Department of the Air Force, 184 F.3d 1366 (Fed. Cir. 1999); Abbott v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 67 MSPR 124, 127 (1995) (the Board considered the Director's brief on intervention that requested that the Board reconsider its holding in Dennis v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 62 MSPR 462 (1994)). The law of the case doctrine does not bind OPM, which was not a party in the prior proceeding and did not have the opportunity to submit argument to the full Board on the Joyce rulings until the agency petitioned for review from the remand initial decision. See Griffin v. Office of Personnel Management, 75 MSPR 263, 270-71 (1997). The notice of intervention was filed within the regulatory time limits. See 5 CFR § 1201.114(g)(1). The Director could not have filed a petition for reconsideration from the Board's decision in Joyce because that decision was not a final decision. See 5 CFR § 1201.119(a)-(b).

See Uhlig v. DOJ, 86 MSPR 660, 664-65 ¶ 9 (2000) (OPM may intervene at the PFR stage in a counsel fee dispute: "It was not until the administrative judge awarded attorney fees and the agency filed its petition for review that the issues argued in OPM's brief on intervention were presented to the Board.").

The intervention process is used in support of OPM's right to secure judicial review of Board decisions. Under the statute providing the judicial review right at OPM's initiative, OPM must first seek to intervene in the Board proceedings so that the Board may review OPM's arguments before they are considered by the reviewing court, 5 USC 7703:

(d)(1) Except as provided under paragraph (2), this paragraph shall apply to any review obtained by the Director of the Office of Personnel Management. The Director may obtain review of any final order or decision of the Board by filing, within 60 days after the Board issues notice of the final order or decision of the Board, a petition for judicial review in the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit if the Director determines, in the discretion of the Director, that the Board erred in interpreting a civil service law, rule, or regulation affecting personnel management and that the Board's decision will have a substantial impact on a civil service law, rule, regulation, or policy directive. If the Director did not intervene in a matter before the Board, the Director may not petition for review of a Board decision under this section unless the Director first petitions the Board for a reconsideration of its decision, and such petition is denied. In addition to the named respondent, the Board and all other parties to the proceedings before the Board shall have the right to appear in the proceeding before the Court of Appeals. The granting of the petition for judicial review shall be at the discretion of the Court of Appeals.

(2) This paragraph shall apply to any review obtained by the Director of the Office of Personnel Management that raises no challenge to the Board's disposition of allegations of a prohibited personnel practice described in section 2302(b) other than practices described in section 2302(b)(8), or 2302(b)(9)(A)(i), (B), (C), or (D). The Director may obtain review of any final order or decision of the Board by filing, within 60 days after the Board issues notice of the final order or decision of the Board, a petition for judicial review in the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit or any court of appeals of competent jurisdiction if the Director determines, in the discretion of the Director, that the Board erred in interpreting a civil service law, rule, or regulation affecting personnel management and that the Board's decision will have a substantial impact on a civil service law, rule, regulation, or policy directive. If the Director did not intervene in a matter before the Board, the Director may not petition for review of a Board decision under this section unless the Director first petitions the Board for a reconsideration of its decision, and such petition is denied. In addition to the named respondent, the Board and all other parties to the proceedings before the Board shall have the right to appear in the proceeding before the court of appeals. The granting of the petition for judicial review shall be at the discretion of the court of appeals.

Unless OPM raises its issues before the Board, it is precluded from raising them in court. See Horner v. Burns, 783 F.2d 196 (Fed. Cir. 1986) (declining to review issues raised by OPM if they were not raised on the OPM's request for Board reconsideration).

If OPM did not intervene before issuance of a final decision, OPM's concerns about a Board order should be raised through a petition for reconsideration, not in a subsequent motion to intervene as a party. Hicks v. Dept. of Treasury, 25 MSPR 444, 445 n.* (1984). If, however, there is no final decision and the case is still being processed regionally, OPM's participation should be through intervention and not through a reconsideration petition. Van Duzer v. Dept. of Navy, 39 MSPR 202, 204 (1988); see Davis v. USPS, 64 MSPR 652, 654 n.2 (1994) (noting that the consolidation will be granted for specific cases when OPM seeks to intervene on a particular issue common to more than one appeal, but the request for consolidation must encompass only existing cases, i.e., a general request for consolidation will not be granted).

The Board will not ordinarily question OPM's right to intervene, Uhlig v. DOJ, 86 MSPR 660, 664 ¶ 8 (2000):

The Board has limited authority to question the Director of OPM's opinion that an erroneous decision would have a substantial impact on any civil service law, rule, or regulation under the jurisdiction of OPM. Joyce, 83 MSPR 666 ¶ 6; Hammond v. Department of the Navy, 50 MSPR 174, 180 (1991) (with the exception of petitions for reconsideration of nonprecedential decisions, the Board may not question the authority of the Director of OPM to seek reconsideration of a Board decision); see also Newman v. Lynch, 897 F.2d 1144, 1146-47 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (finding that the Board did not have the authority to review OPM's determination of substantial impact under 5 USC § 7703(d), even though the Board stated that the Postal Reorganization Act was not a civil service law under the jurisdiction of OPM).

See Patrick v. USDA, 72 MSPR 509, 518-19 (1996) (citing Newman v. Lynch, 897 F.2d 1144, 1147 (Fed. Cir. 1990): the Board may not judge the propriety of OPM's exercise of discretion in seeking reconsideration under 5 USC 7703(d); the Board will not become involved in determining whether an OPM reconsideration request involves interpretation or application of a civil service law, rule, or regulation under OPM's jurisdiction).

If OPM is to intervene after the initial decision, OPM must file its request for intervention within 45 days of the date that the PFR was filed. 5 CFR 1201.114(i) (2022). An OPM intervention request was untimely when it was filed within the 20-day period following the agency's response to a cross-PFR. Engdahl v. Dept. of Navy, 40 MSPR 660, 661 n.2 (1989). The Board is disinclined to apply the 45-day time limit if a case is technically before the Board, e.g., through a remand to the agency for a penalty redetermination, when OPM files its motion to intervene. Guzman-Muelling v. SSA, 91 MSPR 601, 605 ¶ 10 (2002) (also noting the unpredictable course of appellate litigation, through the Supreme Court, involving agency authority to rely on past discipline in a pending adverse action appeal).
