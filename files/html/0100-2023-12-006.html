IV. FRAMEWORK FOR NEGOTIABILITY APPEALS

Negotiability determinations are principally made through the processes of negotiability appeals, secondarily through unfair labor practice proceedings. We address first the negotiability appeal process, and include in that discussion mention of the ULP scheme. [Unfair labor practice procedures are treated in Chapter 8.]

Negotiability determinations by FLRA resolve negotiability disputes, defined by AFGE Local 1770 and DOD Domestic Dependent Elementary and Secondary Sch., 64 FLRA 953, 953 n.1 (2010):

Negotiability disputes exist either when there is a claim that "a proposal is outside the duty to bargain" or, as here, that there has been "an agency head's disapproval of a provision as contrary to law." 5 C.F.R. § 2424.2(c).

Under 5 CFR 2424.2(c) (2022):

Negotiability dispute means a disagreement between an exclusive representative and an agency concerning the legality of a proposal or provision. A negotiability dispute exists when an exclusive representative disagrees with an agency contention that (without regard to any bargaining obligation dispute) a proposal is outside the duty to bargain, including disagreement with an agency contention that a proposal is bargainable only at its election. A negotiability dispute also exists when an exclusive representative disagrees with an agency head's disapproval of a provision as contrary to law. A negotiability dispute may exist where there is no bargaining obligation dispute. Examples of negotiability disputes include disagreements between an exclusive representative and an agency concerning whether a proposal or provision:

(1) Affects a management right under 5 U.S.C. 7106(a);

(2) Constitutes a procedure or appropriate arrangement, within the meaning of 5 U.S.C. 7106(b)(2) and (3), respectively; and

(3) Is consistent with a Government-wide regulation.

DOD, AAFES v. FLRA, 659 F.2d 1140, 1147 (D.C. Cir. 1981), describes the evolution of a negotiability appeal:

Negotiability disputes characteristically arise when the union submits a proposal for inclusion in a collective bargaining agreement. If the agency believes that the proposal is contrary to law or applicable regulation, or is otherwise nonnegotiable under the statute, it may inform the union of its refusal to negotiate. Section 7117 thereupon provides a right to appeal the agency's determination of nonnegotiability to the FLRA.[35]

[35] See 5 U.S.C. § 7117. If the union wishes to appeal, it first obtains a written allegation from the agency explaining its reasons for holding the proposal nonnegotiable. 5 C.F.R. § 2424.3 (1980). The union then files a petition seeking FLRA determination of the negotiability issue. 5 C.F.R. § 2424.4 (1980). The agency is served with a copy of this petition, to which it may respond with a statement setting forth reasons supporting its position. 5 C.F.R. § 2424.6(a)(2) (1980). The union then has a final right of rebuttal. 5 C.F.R. § 2424.7 (1980).

The FLRA is required to decide the negotiability issue at the earliest possible date, issuing a written decision containing specific reasons for sustaining or setting aside the agency's allegation of nonnegotiability. The FLRA's decision addresses only the negotiability of the union proposal under the Reform Act. It neither considers the merits of the proposal nor orders the agency to agree to it. If the proposal is found negotiable, the Authority holds only that the agency must bargain in good faith upon the union's request.

The negotiability appeal structure is also summarized, with commentary on the complementary ULP procedure, FLRA v. OPM, 778 F.2d 844, 845 (D.C. Cir. 1985):

If an agency asserts that a union proposal is non-negotiable, the employees' bargaining agent may file either an unfair labor practice charge against the agency for failure to negotiate in good faith, or appeal the non-negotiability declaration in an expedited negotiability appeal. The negotiability appeal procedure is clearly intended to provide a speedy alternative to the traditional unfair labor practice procedures. The appeal procedure was conceived of as a simple process, designed to resolve mostly straight-forward legal questions focused on the negotiability of specific bargaining proposals. Because it is unnecessary to have a full blown "trial" in a negotiability appeal, it was assumed that the FLRA would give bargaining parties prompt answers to bargaining disputes. Once a particular proposal is found to be negotiable, the FLRA is authorized to seek enforcement in court to compel compliance with its bargaining orders. Ideally, the expedited procedure is supposed to resolve bargaining disputes in a timely and efficient manner, so as to minimize disruptions in collective bargaining....

There are distinctions, discussed below, in the circumstances justifying use of the ULP procedure (generally used when backpay may be involved as a remedy for improper unilateral changes in employment conditions coupled with a refusal to bargain over related proposals) and negotiability appeal procedures (prospective in effect and generally used when the agency declines to negotiate a proposal but there is not a unilateral change involving potential backpay).

FLRA considered but declined a stay of negotiability proceedings during the pendency of a related case in the court of appeals. By the time FLRA issued its negotiability decision, the judicial case was decided. See NTEU and Dept. of Treasury, IRS Office of Chief Counsel, 66 FLRA 809, 809 (2012) (recon. den., 66 FLRA 1030 (2012)).

In the early years of FLRA operations, complex negotiability determinations, often involving a dozen or more proposals in a single decision, dominated FLRA's docket and resources. More recently the number of decided negotiability cases has dropped to a trickle because of FLRA's use of alternative dispute resolution early in the negotiability appeals process to educate the parties on the law and to assist them in refashioning proposals (or abandoning proposals or opposition to proposals) to conform with the negotiability law developed since 1979 by FLRA and its reviewing courts.
