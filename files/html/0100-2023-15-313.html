5. Mishandling or Improper Possession of Weapon

Discharge of a weapon at a fleeing suspect warranted removal of a supervisory police officer when agency regulations did not permit use of the weapon under the circumstances. Hylton v. FAA, 13 MSPR 335, 340 (1982); see Flood v. Dept. of Army, 394 Fed. Appx. 693 (Fed. Cir. 2010 NP) (inappropriate storage of weapons in an abandoned vehicle at the agency facility warranted adverse action); Mahan v. Dept. of Treasury, 89 MSPR 140, 145-46 ¶¶ 10-12 (2001) (rejecting an argument of self-defense and sustaining removal of a criminal investigator who discharged a weapon during the course of a domestic dispute at her home); Boatman v. DOJ, 66 MSPR 58, 62 (1994) (suspension of a correctional officer who violated post orders by leaving a vehicle with a weapon in it unattended: although there was no harm, "[t]hat more dire consequences did not occur is a matter of chance, but the agency need not await such events before taking action"); Johnson v. Dept. of Army, 22 MSPR 137, 138 (1984) (reducing to suspension a removal for the principal charge of the loss of a weapon when there were mitigating circumstances: the holster was possibly defective; the weapon was unloaded; the loss was immediately reported; and the weapon was found); Limbert v. VA, 13 MSPR 341, 344 (1982) (mitigating removal to suspension of a supervisory police officer who neglected to secure an unloaded revolver: although the appellant failed to make proper journal entries concerning the weapon, he acted prudently in attempting to secure the gun when he was unable to open the safe usually used for weapons storage). Discharge of a privately-owned weapon in the office by an employee, with an injury to that employee, resulted in his removal, action justified by the AJ as a result of the danger created by the employee and lack of counterbalancing mitigating factors. Shepel v. Dept. of Commerce, 68 Fed. Appx. 200, 201-02 (Fed. Cir. 2003 NP). Failure to properly report an incident involving an agency weapon led to removal of a criminal investigator, affirmed by a reviewing court in Love v. DOJ, 184 Fed. Appx. 962 (Fed. Cir. 2006 NP). [T]he Board has found removal to be a reasonable penalty when an appellant has violated an agency's security regulations by his unauthorized possession of a firearm on agency premises. MacCormac v. Department of the Air Force, 26 M.S.P.R. 611, 612, 614 (1985) (the Board upheld the appellant's removal for intimidating conduct toward a subordinate and violating agency security regulations concerning possession of weapons); Majette v. U.S. Postal Service, 16 M.S.P.R. 419, 425-26 (1983) (removal was reasonable for a security police officer who was arrested for unauthorized possession of a firearm while off duty, despite his clean disciplinary record and satisfactory performance history); MacDonald v. Department of the Navy, 4 M.S.P.R. 403, 404-05 (1980) (removal of a police officer was sustained for carrying loaded firearm in paper bag in an agency installation while off-duty).

Rodriguez v. DOJ, NY-0752-10-0081-I-1 (NP 10/7/2011), concluded that removal was appropriate for charges against a law enforcement officer, including inappropriate display of a weapon during an off-duty traffic confrontation:

[I]n his testimony, the deciding official made it clear that while he did not consider the violation of the code of professional responsibility alone to be a removable offense, the off-duty misconduct was "egregious" and "heavily damaged" his manager's confidence in the appellant to the extent that the manager did not feel he could bring the appellant back to an "operational status" because he "would always worry" about the appellant. The Board's role is not to displace the judgment of senior agency managers who must have confidence that employees will act appropriately at all times. Ellis v. Department of Defense, 114 M.S.P.R. 407, ¶ 11 (2010); see Batts v. Department of the Interior, 102 M.S.P.R. 27, ¶ 11 (2006); Ingram v. Department of the Air Force, 53 M.S.P.R. 101, 106, aff'd, 980 F.2d 742 (Fed. Cir. 1992) (Table). Rather, the Board must ensure that managerial judgment has been properly exercised. Ingram, 53 M.S.P.R. at 106. In this case, the deciding official clearly gave extensive and thoughtful consideration to the aggravating and mitigating Douglas factors. Because the deciding official made it clear that management could no longer trust the appellant in his law enforcement position, the penalty of removal did not exceed the tolerable limits of reasonableness for the off-duty conduct combined with the violation of the code of professional responsibility. See Devall, 178 F.3d at 1260; see also Watson v. Department of Justice, 64 F.3d 1524, 1530 (Fed. Cir. 1995) (holding that "[l]aw enforcement officers are held to a higher standard of conduct than are other federal employees"); Jones v. Department of the Army, 52 M.S.P.R. 501, 506 (1992), aff'd, 912 F. Supp. 1397 (D. Kan. 1995).
