b. Prior Law

Before 1985, the Board was not inclined to award fees under the "substantial innocence test" when adverse actions were mitigated on appeal. The Board's unwritten rule was, with exceptions, that if some penalty was sustained, the appellant was not substantially innocent, the charges were not wholly unfounded, and no fees would be allowed. The Board's policy was to deny fees if some charges or some portion of the penalty was sustained following mitigation. Smith v. VA, 3 MSPR 350, 352, 3 MSPB 432 (1980), an extreme example of the Board's policy, reversed a removal: only one of six charges of misconduct was sustained. The Board denied counsel fees, stating that the employee was not substantially innocent of the charges. Although only one of the agency charges was established by a preponderance of the evidence, there was substantial evidence in the record supporting all the charges. Despite reversal of the penalty, if one charge was sustained in a suspension case, it could not be said that the agency should have known that it would not prevail; further, the employee was not substantially innocent. Pattison v. USDA, 8 MSPR 401, 403-04 (1981). If some evidence of a threat was shown, the action against the employee was not wholly unfounded even though removal was reversed; the employee was not substantially innocent. Langenbach v. USPS, 12 MSPR 411, 414-15 (1982).

In Sterner v. Dept. of Army, 711 F.2d 1563 (Fed. Cir. 1983), the court did not jump at the chance to award fees to an individual whose penalty was mitigated. In Sims v. Dept. of Navy, 711 F.2d 1578 (Fed. Cir. 1983), decided under the Back Pay Act, 5 USC 5596, the court considered the claim for fees arising from a reduction in penalty from removal to a 10-day suspension. The employee claimed that the agency knew or should have known that it would not prevail on the merits of the case. The court observed that the mitigation argument did not strike it as a circumstance in itself warranting fees under the statutory standard, particularly for an employee who admitted the wrongdoing giving rise to the penalty. The court determined that the agency action was not clearly without merit; the agency could not have known that the Board would retroactively apply the Douglas mitigation factors. Lacking any evidence of bad faith, harassment, or intentional delay of the proceedings, there was no reason to disturb the Board's decision that the appellant was not entitled to fees.

In the mid-1980s, the Board followed Sims in Smith v. DOJ, 29 MSPR 150, 151 n.* (1985), citing Sims for the proposition that "[t]he Board's mitigation of a penalty, where [the] employee admits wrongdoing which gave rise to the mitigation penalty, does not warrant an award of attorney fees in the interest of justice." Attorney fees were not considered merited for an appellant found to have falsified a medical certificate, even though less than all the charges were sustained and the removal was mitigated to a 30-day suspension. Deery v. Dept. of Navy, 22 MSPR 651 (1984) (Table); see Mitchum v. TVA, 29 MSPR 682, 684-85 (1986) (insubordination was mitigated to a suspension by exculpating factors, including the illness of appellant; fees were denied). Mitigation of a penalty, if the employee admitted the wrongdoing that gave rise to the mitigated penalty, did not to warrant a fee award. Smith v. DOJ, 29 MSPR 150, 151 n.* (1985). Removal was mitigated to a 60-day suspension for unauthorized absence during the labor strike of the air traffic controllers. Fees were held properly denied by the AJ. Werts v. FAA, 26 MSPR 74, 75-76 (1985). The Board determined, under Yorkshire, that to be substantially innocent, the employee must prevail on substantially all the charges. If one charge, unauthorized absence, was sustained, and another, striking, was not, and if the underlying conduct involved in the sustained charge and the unsustained charge were the same, the employee was not substantially innocent. Pagington v. Dept. of Transp., 26 MSPR 70, 73 (1985).

Although fees were not generally warranted for an appellant guilty of some misconduct, Ybarra v. DLA, 25 MSPR 419, 421-22 (1984), exceptions occurred. A removal was reduced to a reprimand in Filip v. VA, 22 MSPR 642, 643 (1984). Five of six specifications were not sustained. The appellant was found guilty of only the technical violation of an unenforced agency regulation. Examining the degree of fault on the part of the employee and the existence of any reasonable basis for the agency action, the Board concluded that the employee was substantially innocent. The fee award was in the interest of justice. The agency had no reasonable basis for removal because of its inaction toward others who violated the same regulation and because the agency failed in its proof on the remaining charges. Fees were also awarded to an appellant whose removal was reduced to a 14-day suspension. The employee and his counsel tried to focus the agency's attention on information that should have changed the outcome of the investigation leading to the adverse action. Fees were apparently awarded on the basis that the agency knew or should have known that it would not prevail; however, the significance of the holding was that the imposition of some penalty, even a suspension, although indicative of some misconduct, would not bar a fee on the theory that the employee was not wholly innocent. Crumbaker v. Dept. of Labor, 24 MSPR 627, 629-30 (1984). Fees were awarded under the substantial innocence standard after a demotion was reversed under Chapter 75. The Board sustained only 13 of 82 examples of unsatisfactory performance charged by the agency. Brown v. Coast Guard, 28 MSPR 539, 554 (1985).
