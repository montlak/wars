9. Notice

In order for sanctions to be imposed, the complainant must be on clear notice that her conduct violates an order of the administrative judge. In Menchaca v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120112970 (2011), the Commission reversed the issuance of a sanction by the administrative judge of dismissing her hearing request for failure to attend a settlement conference where it found that the complainant was not given a clear order to personally attend the conference. The Commission, in Menchaca, found:

In this case, AJ1 dismissed the hearing requests due to Complainant's failure to appear at the February 2, 2011 settlement conference. All supports the dismissal with an Acknowledgment Order dated June 29, 2009, which warns that "failure to follow this Order or other orders of the Administrative Judge may result in sanctions pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109(f)(3)." AJ1 found that Complainant and the Attorney's behavior constituted a violation of her orders and dismissed the hearing.

Complainant and the Attorney argue that there was no clear order issued by the AJ requiring Complainant to attend the settlement conference. Complainant supports her assertion by providing an affidavit. In addition, the Attorney provided the Commission with a copy of his letter to the Supervisory AJ providing a contemporaneous recounting of the events that transpired on February 2, 2011. The Attorney also noted that. Complainant was available via telephone and that there was no obvious reason for having Complainant physically present for the settlement conference.

Upon review of the record, the Commission finds that there was no written order issued by either AJ specifically requiring Complainant to physically attend the settlement conference. Furthermore, the Attorney offered to reach Complainant by telephone, but AJ2 declined this offer. It is undisputed that the Attorney had permission to attend the conference telephonically and did so. The Attorney indicated that he had the authority to negotiate for his client, the Complainant. Based on the record as a whole, we find that it has not been established that Complainant's physical presence at the EEOC's District Office was ordered or required for the parties to engage in meaningful settlement discussions. Therefore, we determine that the dismissal of the hearing in this matter was not an appropriate sanction for this case. Because we determine that the dismissal was inappropriate and are remanding the case back for a hearing, it is unnecessary for us to address the Agency's final decision on the merits of Complainant's claims of discrimination.

No sanction was appropriate where the complainant failed to appear for a prehearing conference and was given insufficient time by the administrative judge to respond to the notice to show cause. In Price-Burnett v. Postmaster General, 01A35341 (2004), the Commission found that the administrative judge abused his discretion because the complainant had previously traveled to Georgia for a hearing that the judge postponed after the agency contended it never received the prehearing conference notice. The complainant contended that she did not receive the notice of the rescheduled prehearing conference and was out of town when the notice to cause was delivered. Under those circumstances, the Commission remanded the case for a hearing.

In Brent v. Attorney General, 01A30035 (2004), the Commission also remanded the case for a hearing where the complaint contended that the reason for not submitting prehearing information was that she never received a scheduling order. In remanding the case, the Commission noted that there was no proof the complainant had received the scheduling order and that the administrative judge never issued a notice to show cause prior to canceling the hearing and remanding the case to the agency.
