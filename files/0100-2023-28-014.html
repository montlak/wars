II. CERTIFICATION STAGE

Once the formal complaint has been filed, the complaint moves forward to the certification stage. There is no agency investigation in class complaints. Rather, it is the administrative judge who develops the record and has the responsibility for deciding whether to accept or dismiss the class complaint. Specifically, 29 CFR 1614.204(d)(1) provides:

Within 30 days of an agency's receipt of a complaint, the agency shall: Designate an agency representative who shall be any of the individuals referenced in § 1614.102(b)(3), and forward the complaint, along with a copy of the Counselor's report and any other information pertaining to timeliness or other relevant circumstances related to the complaint, to the Commission. The Commission shall assign the complaint to an administrative judge or complaints examiner with a proper security clearance when necessary. The administrative judge may require the complainant or agency to submit additional information relevant to the complaint.

It is error for the agency to dismiss a class complaint without referring it to the EEOC for assignment of an administrative judge. Such a decision is not within the agency's jurisdiction. Kwok v. USPS, 01871083 (1987).

Because there is no agency investigation in a class complaint, the record may contain insufficient information for the administrative judge to make a determination on the certification of the class. The administrative judge has the discretion to permit precertification discovery. See Judges' Handbook at 10-4.

The administrative judge, at the certification stage of processing, issues a decision on whether or not the class complaint can be certified and move forward, or whether it should be dismissed. The administrative judge may dismiss the complaint, or any portion of the complaint, for any of the reasons listed in 29 CFR 1614.107(a). See 29 CFR 1614.204(d)(2). In addition, the administrative judge may dismiss a complaint, or any portion of a complaint, if the claims lack specificity and detail under 29 CFR 1614.204(d)(4), or if there was undue delay in moving for class certification under 29 CFR 1614.204(b). See MD-110 at 8-5. Each of these bases for dismissal is discussed below.

The administrative judge may also dismiss a class complaint because it does not meet the prerequisites for a class complaint under 29 CFR 1614.204(a)(2). See 29 CFR 1614.204(d)(2); MD-110 at 8-5. The prerequisites--numerosity, commonality, typicality, and adequacy of representation--are discussed below.

If it is unclear whether an allegation was brought to the attention of the counselor, 29 CFR 1614.204(d)(3) provides:

If the allegation is not included in the Counselor's report, the administrative judge shall afford the agent 15 days to state whether the matter was discussed with the Counselor and, if not, explain why it was not discussed. If the explanation is not satisfactory, the administrative judge shall dismiss the allegation. If the explanation is satisfactory, the administrative judge shall refer the allegation to the agency for further counseling of the agent. After counseling, the allegation shall be consolidated with the class complaint.

Prior to the 1999 amendments to the regulations, the certification decision was only a recommended decision. The agency issued the final decision on whether to accept or dismiss a class complaint, and the burden was on the complainant to appeal any final agency decision. Much of the case law, therefore, involves certification or dismissal under the old regulations. The case law is still relevant, however, and the reader should simply be aware that any pending or future cases will involve a decision subject to final agency action rather than merely a recommended decision by the administrative judge.
