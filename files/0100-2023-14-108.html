6. Promises to Grant Something to Which an Employee Is Already Entitled

A promise by an agency to treat the complainant "fairly and equally," as it does all other employees, does not constitute consideration for withdrawal of a complaint. In Cockrell v. Postmaster General, 01956101 (1996), the Commission declared such a settlement agreement as a "nudum pactum" and set it aside because the agency did not confer any benefit upon the complainant but only agreed to provide something to which she was already entitled. See also Rowe v. Postmaster General, 01963176 (1998). The same result is obtained when the agency and the complainant agree to treat each other with mutual respect and use common courtesies. See Maldonado v. Postmaster General, 01A03983 (2002).

However, such a promise, if accompanied by another provision conferring something of value, may constitute adequate consideration. The provision requiring mutual respect, dignity or fair treatment is void in such agreements, but the remainder of the agreement remains in force. See, e.g., Stanberry v. Postmaster General, 01A04855 (2002).

The agency and the complainant in DuBois v. Social Security Administration, 05950808 (1997), entered into a settlement agreement with the following pertinent provisions:

The agency agrees that it will rate the complainant fairly for the rating period ending September 30, 1993, in a manner which is consistent with the discussions held between the complainant and his first-line supervisor and the input of the reviewing official.

With respect to the discussions between appellant and his first-line supervisor, the settlement agreement in Dubois indicated that:

The agency agrees that during the remainder of the rating period which ends September 30, 1993, if the complainant so requests, the complainant's first-line supervisor will provide him with counseling as to the level of his performance, on a monthly basis, if the complainant requests it. During these sessions, the complainant's first-line supervisor will inform him of the level at which he is performing as of the time of that discussion, and will inform him of ways in which he might improve the level of his performance.

The Commission in DuBois found that the settlement agreement conferred no benefit upon the complainant and was too vague to be enforced, and therefore the Commission found the agreement was void:

In the present case, the terms of the settlement agreement provided appellant with no more benefits than he was already entitled to as an employee of the agency. We note in this regard that the agency was already obligated to rate appellant and other employees "fairly." Therefore, the agency incurred no legal detriment.

Furthermore, we find that the provision requiring appellant to be rated "fairly" is too vague to permit enforcement. The settlement agreement provided no reliable method for determining whether appellant was rated fairly. The only means mentioned in the settlement agreement is that the rating had to be in a manner which was consistent with discussions held between appellant and his first-line supervisor and the input of the reviewing official. We note, however, that the discussions were discretionary. (Footnote omitted.) The Commission has previously held that a binding settlement agreement requires a contemporaneous meeting of the minds. Kenneth N. Brown v. Department of Defense (DLA), EEOC Request No. 05940628 (November 3, 1994); Mullen v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05890349 (May 18, 1989). Based on our review of this matter in its totality, we find that there was no meeting of the minds between the parties when the settlement agreement was signed. At the time the parties entered into the settlement agreement, there was no certainty that the discussions, a major element in determining appellant's rating, would ever occur. Therefore, for the foregoing reasons, we find that the settlement agreement is void.

See also Complainant v. Secretary of Army, 0120122836 (2014) (agreement that allowed complainant to submit new information to the rater for consideration of a higher rating but left decision on rating solely within province of the agency was an illusory promise); Denson v. Postmaster General, 01A44845 (2005) (agreement providing that agency treat complainant with respect and dignity and complainant follow agency rules and regulations void for lack of consideration); Estock v. Postmaster General, 01A15048 (2002) (an agreement providing that the agency would make a "concerted effort" to improve the relationship and the day-to-day dealings and would not "assume the worst" in dealing with the complainant was void for a lack of consideration because agency incurred no legal detriment). Similarly, in Reid v. Postmaster General, 01A20309 (2002), the Commission found that an agreement requiring the agency "to fully explore every opportunity" to change the complainant's shift was void for lack of consideration.

However, in Bishop v. Postmaster General, 01A11756 (2002), the Commission found that a settlement requiring that the agency not allow coworkers to verbally abuse the complainant and utilize counselors to inform employees of the agency's zero tolerance policy for inappropriate behavior was not void for lack of consideration. The Commission, in agreeing to the terms, found that the agency incurred a legal detriment. Similarly, in Williams v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0114453 (2002), the Commission found that a settlement agreement that required the supervisor to maintain an "open door" policy was not void for lack of consideration and that the agency incurred a legal detriment. The Commission found it was not significant that the policy also benefited the complainant's coworkers "with the critical elements being complainant's benefit from the policy." Id.

In Wells v. Secretary of Air Force, 01970058 (1997), the Commission found that the agency's promise to warn a supervisor to stop using foul language in exchange for withdrawal of a complaint was illusory, and therefore no settlement agreement existed:

In the present case, the agency agreed to inform the Machinist Foreman of the impropriety of language used in appellant's presence and to warn her not to use such language in appellant's presence in the future. The agency attempted to unilaterally change the meaning of the term "warn" by interpreting the warning that was to be issued to the Machinist Foreman as not requiring the issuance of an "oral admonishment." As the terms "warning" and "admonishment" are synonymous, the agency's promise to "warn" the Machinist Foreman became illusory; the agency did not agree to give appellant any benefit, nor did the agency suffer any detriment in exchange for appellant's withdrawal of his EEO complaint. While the propriety of the agency's unilateral action in attempting to alter the plain meaning of the term "warn" was questionable, we find that regardless the June 14, 1996 settlement agreement was a nudum pactum. The agency's final decision finding no breach of the settlement agreement is REVERSED. Appellant's complaint is REMANDED to the agency for further processing from the point processing ceased in accordance with the Order below.

Similarly, an agreement in which the complainant withdraws her complaint and is permitted to apply for other jobs at the agency is void for lack on consideration. Jackson v. Postmaster General, 01A53202 (2005) ("We find that the agency, in merely agreeing to allow complainant to apply for positions, provided complainant nothing more than that which she was already entitled to as an employee....").

A promise to "hold an in-person meeting...to discuss working conditions" and to provide an iPad to assist the hearing-impaired complainant with "communication at work and in the service of customers" was illusory according to the Commission in Erika H. v. Postmaster General, 2021001901 (2021), because the "Agency was simply providing a deaf employee with a reasonable accommodation."
