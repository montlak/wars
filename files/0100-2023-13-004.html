B. TIMELINESS

As set forth in the regulations above, the investigation must be completed within 180 days of the date the formal complaint was filed. 29 CFR 1614.108(e). If there is an amendment or another complaint is consolidated with the oriGINAl complaint, the investigation must be completed within 180 days of the most recent amendment or consolidated complaint, or within 360 days of the oriGINAl complaint, whichever comes sooner. 29 CFR 1614.108(f).

Commission regulations at 29 CFR 1614.606 address consolidated complaints, and state in relevant part:

When a complaint has been consolidated with one or more earlier filed complaints, the agency shall complete its investigation within the earlier of 180 days after the filing of the last complaint or 360 days after the filing of the oriGINAl complaint, except that the complainant may request a hearing from an administrative judge on the consolidated complaints any time after 180 days from the date of the first filed complaint.

See also MD-110 at 5-13 to 5-14. The regulations, at 29 CFR 1614.108(f) further provide that within the allotted time frame, the agency must provide the complainant with a copy of the investigative file. Not only must the investigation be completed within 180 days, the report of investigation must be written and compiled within this time frame as well. MD-110 at 5-32 sets forth this requirement more explicitly:

A timely completed investigation means that within the applicable time period the agency must complete several actions.

1. The complaint must be appropriately investigated in a manner consistent with Chapter 6 of this Management Directive. An appropriate factual record is one that allows a reasonable fact finder to draw conclusions as to whether discrimination occurred.

2. Copies of the investigative file, including a summary of the investigation must be provided to the complainant(s); and

3. The agency must give complainant a notice of his/her right to request a hearing (if it is not a mixed case), within 30 days from receipt of the investigative file, or of the right to request a final action by the agency pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.110.

(Footnote omitted). This means that the investigation itself must be sufficiently complete before the 180-day deadline to allow the investigator enough time to write and assemble an appropriate report.

Even if there was an amendment or consolidation of a complaint, the complainant may request a hearing before an EEOC administrative judge any time after 180 days from the date the oriGINAl complaint was filed. 29 CFR 1614.108(g). MD-110 at 5-31 provides:

A complainant has the right to request a hearing, even in the case of consolidated complaints, after 180 days have passed since the filing of the oriGINAl complaint, even if the agency's investigation has not been completed.

When a hearing has been requested, responsibility for developing the record shifts from the agency to the Commission. See 29 CFR 1614.109(a). At this point, the investigation ceases and it is the administrative judge who is responsible for overseeing development of the factual record. Id. There will be situations where the timing is such that an amended or consolidated complaint will spend virtually no time in the investigative stage. If, for example, an amendment is requested on the 170th day of the investigation, the claim giving rise to the amendment will spend only 10 days in the investigative stage if the complainant requests a hearing after the close of the 180-day investigation period. "Amendment and Consolidation of Complaints" are both discussed in Chapter 3.

If the investigation cannot be completed within the relevant time period, the parties may agree to an extension of up to 90 days in accordance with 29 CFR 1614.109(e):

By written agreement within those time periods, the complainant and the respondent agency may voluntarily extend the time period for not more than an additional 90 days.

The extension must be in writing and must be voluntarily agreed upon by the parties. A complainant is not required to agree to an extension, and is well within his rights to cease participating in an investigation when the agency seeks an extension that the complainant declines. The Commission faulted the agency in Koch v. Securities and Exchange Commission, 01965642 (1997), for attempting to require the complainant to attend an investigative interview after the 180-day time period where the complainant opposed an extension.

The agency may unilaterally extend the time period only under very limited circumstances. Under 29 CFR 1614.108(e):

The agency may unilaterally extend the time period or any period of extension for not more than 30 days where it must sanitize a complaint file that may contain information classified pursuant to Exec. Order No. 12356, or successor orders, as secret in the interest of national defense or foreign policy, provided the investigating agency notifies the parties of the extension.

The limited authority the agency has to unilaterally extend the investigation for up to 30 days comes from Executive Order 12356, which is codified in relevant part at 47 CFR 14874 and 15557. The provisions deal with matters of national security. Investigators need not generally be concerned about whether questionable information is classified as secret. This is because the Executive Order requires such information to be clearly marked in the investigative file.

When an agency exceeds the 180-day period for completing an investigation, the complainant's remedy is to request a hearing. The failure of the agency to meet the regulatory period for completing an investigation does not provide a basis for reversing a finding of no discrimination. Henderson v. Attorney General, 01991767 (2001).
