21. Failure to Discipline

An employee is not aggrieved within the meaning of Title VII when the agency fails to discipline a supervisor for a breach of agency regulations. Plummer v. USPS, 01842746 (1985). The employee does state a claim cognizable under Title VII if he alleges that he is aggrieved by the breach of regulations vis-a-vis another employee. However, the purpose of Title VII is compensatory and not to impose punishment on offending employers.

The general rule is that failure to discipline a coworker does not state a claim for relief. Kuo v. Secretary of Navy, 01983560 (1999). An exception to that general rule is in cases involving sexual harassment, where an employee can be aggrieved by the agency's failure to discipline the offender. An agency is required to take prompt and effective remedial action in response to allegations of sexual harassment. In appropriate cases, the failure to discipline the harasser can aggrieve a victim of sexual harassment. See Chapter 12 discussing sexual harassment.

However, in Kramer v. Postmaster General, 01962027 (1996), the Commission held that the fact that coworkers may have engaged in conduct for which the complainant previously had been disciplined does not state a claim. Similarly, a complaint that another employee has been appointed to act in a position does not state a claim. See Smith v. Secretary of Army, 01974029 (1998). The Commission further held in Shaunda D. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120172776 (2017), that management's failure to take any corrective action against a coworker when he and a patient used sexually explicit language, which the complainant overheard and took offense to, did not state a viable claim of a discriminatory hostile work environment. By contrast, though, an allegation that the complainant was disciplined for conduct for which others were not disciplined does state a claim. For example, in Wedderburn v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A60112 (2006), the complainant alleged that he was suspended for an altercation with a white employee that involved a knife. He further alleged that a similar incident had occurred between two black employees and no discipline was issued. The Commission reversed the agency's dismissal of the complaint finding the complainant was alleging that he was treated differently and that rendered him aggrieved.

The complainant in Broussard-Newman v. Secretary of Defense, 01976529 (1998), did, however, state a claim when she alleged discrimination over an incident in which an employee supervised by the complainant made sexist and racist remarks during a meeting. The employee refused to work for the complainant and asked to be transferred. The agency granted that request. The Commission found that the complainant was aggrieved; she was harmed with respect to a term, condition or privilege of employment, i.e., deprived of the right to take appropriate disciplinary actions against the employee and denied an "equal employment opportunity environment" in which to work. Id. In Broussard-Newman, the Commission stated:

Rather than controlling any negative impact of Employee's allegedly racist and sexist remarks in the workplace, the agency in effect fostered the opinions reflected in Employee's statements when it failed to discipline him for refusing to work under appellant and granted his request to be transferred.

The Commission has previously held that to establish standing as an "aggrieved employee" within the context of 29 CFR § 1614.103, appellant must allege some direct harm which affects a term, condition, or privilege of employment. See Trafficante v. Metropolitan Life Insurance Co., 409 U.S. 205 (1972). The agency suggests that appellant was not harmed because appellant maintained her supervisory position, her duties and responsibilities and her rate of pay. We are not persuaded by the agency's assertions and determine that appellant was aggrieved when the agency allowed Employee's remarks to prevent appellant from performing the duties of her position with respect to an employee under her supervision. See Henry v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05900866 (October 3, 1990). Appellant's complaint therefore stated a valid claim of discrimination which should properly have been accepted and investigated by the agency.
