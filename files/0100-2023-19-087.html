1. Inconsistent Statements

The fact that a supervisor makes inconsistent statements may go a long way towards proving pretext, but inconsistent statements do not require a finding of pretext. The Commission found that the complainant in McCarty v. Secretary of Navy, 01965283 (1998), failed to prove discrimination despite the fact that the selecting official made inconsistent statements regarding her evaluation of the candidates for the position in question:

The Commission finds the Selecting Official's purported reliance on the SF 171s, as espoused in her June 20, 1995 memorandum, to be inconsistent with her statements in her June 8, 1995 affidavit. In response to the EEO Investigator's written interrogatories concerning how the selection determination was made, the Selecting Official stated in the affidavit:

No selection matrix or ranking scheme was utilized in evaluating the applicants. The job interview was a significant factor in the selection determination. The Social Science Analyst position requires highly effective skills in communication, interpersonal relationships (working with and getting along with people), a sincere desire, commitment, and motivation to help people, and the ability to be flexible, adaptable, creative and open-minded. I feel that these critical elements are most [effectively] assessed through the personal interview process.

In her affidavit the Selecting Official also stated:

Prior to the interviews I reviewed each candidate's SF-171 and found them to all be qualified candidates. The SF-171s gave me a general impression, but I did not rank any of them as a result of my review of their SF-171s. I rely heavily upon the personal interview to assess the individual's personal qualities and values that are essential in the successful performance of this position (i.e., commitment, teamwork, flexibility, open-mindedness, positive attitude, etc.). I do not feel that I can adequately assess these personal qualities and values from an SF-171. After reviewing the SF-171s, all of the candidates were pretty much on an equal standing.

. . .

I did not compare [the Selectee's] SF-171 to [appellant's], or any other applicants, during the actual selection process. As I looked at the SF-171s of all the applicants I assessed them to all be capable of performing the basic functions of this position. I initially reviewed the SF-171s when they came in with the referral lists. Those referral lists came in at different times. I usually do not re-review them prior to or during the interview, I focused on the applicants and their responses. I strongly feel that personal interviews are the most important vehicle for assessing personal qualities which are essential to the successful performance of this position.

The Commission finds that the Selecting Official did not rely on the SF-171s when choosing among the interviewees. The Commission finds that the Selecting Official's multiple, consistent statements in her affidavit, some in direct response to the EEO Investigator's questions, show that the Selecting official relied upon the interview to choose among the interviewees. The Commission finds that the Selecting Official's comparison of the SF-171s of appellant and the Selectee in her June 20, 1995 memorandum is an attempt to justify the selection by referencing factors not relied upon in the selection decision.

5. The Interview as the Basis for Selection

The vacancy announcement for the position at issue in this complaint listed the duties as follows:

As the primary program coordinator, provides direction and program staff supervision over day-to-day program operations, including quality assurance and logistics in support of programs. Plans, develops, markets and implements social service program designed to improve quality of life of individuals and families with accurate and empathetic information. Serves as principal instructor for specialized meetings, conferences and briefings with commands or individuals about Family Service Center Services. Develops publicity plan for all programs and creates, generates, and implements publicity through all available media.

The Commission finds that the Selecting Official's use of the interview to make the selection decision is not indicative of discrimination. Because the position at issue involves use of interpersonal skills, the Selecting Official's reliance on an interview to gauge the interviewees' interpersonal skills (including communication skills and ability to get along with others) is believable and has not been rebutted by appellant. Furthermore, subjective criteria are not, of themselves, evidence of pretext. Jenkins, EEOC Request No. 05940284.

The Commission finds that appellant has failed to show by a preponderance of the evidence that his interview or the Selectee's interview were not as described by the Selecting Official or Interviewer A. The Selecting Official referred to various statements in the interview by appellant which led to his nonselection. Appellant has attempted to explain the context of these statements, but has not denied making the vast majority of these statements during the interview. Furthermore, appellant has not shown that the Selecting Official's interpretation of appellant's statements in the interview, which is essentially corroborated by Interviewer A, was motivated by discrimination or is unworthy of belief. In contrast, the evidence shows that the Selectee, in her interview, exhibited none of the negative characteristics (or other negative characteristics) attributed to appellant in his interview.

The Commission finds that appellant has failed to show that the Selecting Official's reliance on the interview for making the selection decision was a pretext for discrimination. The Commission finds that appellant has failed to show that his interview was superior to the Selectee's interview in terms of the qualities sought by the Selecting Official. The Commission concludes that appellant has failed to meet his burden of showing that he was discriminated against on the basis of sex or age.

See also Wilson v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A06001 (2002) (selecting official's change in his explanation for making selection was not deemed sufficient to establish pretext).

Similarly, in Harris v. Postmaster General, 05A10373 (2001), the Commission found that inconsequential discrepancies in the testimony of supervisors did not establish pretext. In that case, there were discrepancies in the testimony of several agency officials over who was responsible for the decision not to rehire the complainant. However, the discrepancy did not establish pretext where all of the supervisors agreed that the complainant was not rehired because he had previously concealed a criminal conviction from the agency.

Without commenting on the practice of a lawyer answering interrogatories, a practice which is dubious at best, in Complainant v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120122820 (2014), the Commission rejected the complainant's argument that he had established pretext because of inconsistent statements in the answers to interrogatories provided by the agency lawyer and responses of the selecting official in his affidavit:

Complainant also contends that that the Agency's explanations for not selecting her are inconsistent, because in an interrogatory, the Agency stated that education was not a factor in its decision; however, in an investigative statement, Si stated that education was a factor in the selection decision. However, the record reveals that an Agency attorney answered the interrogatory in which it was asserted that education was not a factor in the first selection. Si is the selecting official for the position, and as such, has first-hand, pertinent knowledge about the selection process. In contrast, the Agency attorney was not responsible for Complainant's non-selection and has not asserted that she had any first-hand knowledge of the selection process. As such, we cannot find that the inconsistencies between these statements constitute evidence of pretext.

The Commission did not address whether the agency should have been bound by its interrogatory responses.
