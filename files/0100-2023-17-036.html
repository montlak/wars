1. Effect of Other Cases

It is possible, though rare, that a decision of an agency or the Commission in one case can have an effect on another case. Even though two complaints are filed and processed separately, an agency cannot reach conflicting decisions in each case.

For example, in Anderson v. Secretary of Army, 01965817 (1998), the Commission reversed and remanded a finding of no discrimination in a selection case where the agency previously decided, in another complaint, that the selection process was tainted by sex discrimination. The Commission in Anderson explained:

[A]ppellant again raised the issue of the agency's seemingly contradictory conclusion that the only other female candidate was the victim of sex discrimination. In its comments submitted on appeal, the agency again fails to address this issue.

The instant record on appeal contains no information about the EEO complaint of Employee A, or the agency's ultimate finding in that matter. Moreover, on appeal, appellant has failed to provide this information. However, the Commission takes independent note of EEOC Appeal No. 01984024, currently pending before it, which was filed by Employee A. In that case, the agency's statement on appeal confirms that Employee A prevailed in a sex discrimination complaint she filed concerning the same selection decision challenged by appellant. As a result, effective March 17, 1996, Employee A was placed in the position in question, and the oriGINAl selectee was reassigned to the position of Special Projects Officer under the Director of Logistics.

After carefully reviewing all the evidence of record, the Commission finds that the agency erred in issuing its final decision without addressing the issue of the finding of discrimination in Employee A's case. Therefore, the Commission will vacate the agency's June 19, 1996 final decision on appellant's complaint, and order the agency to conduct a supplemental investigation into the impact the finding of sex discrimination in Employee A's case has on the merits of appellant's allegations of sex and race discrimination in the same selection. The Commission advises the agency, however, that due to its finding in Employee A's case that sex discrimination tainted the selection process, the agency cannot now argue that no discrimination occurred or that appellant was not selected because the male selectee was the best qualified of the three candidates.

Use of consolidation would eliminate such results.

Similarly, the Commission can take notice of its own previous factual findings in deciding an appeal. For example, in affirming the agency's decision, which found no discrimination in its termination of the complainant, the Commission took "judicial notice of the findings of fact" it made in a case related to Nelson v. Postmaster General, 01990915 (2000):

The agency submits uncontroverted evidence of its "zero tolerance" policy and that four employees who made confirmed verbal threats were also issued notices of termination, and thereafter terminated. (Report of Investigation p.6). Complainant falls to proffer any evidence which would establish that the agency's reason is a pretext for discrimination. Complainant merely states that she was singled out. In the related case of Nelson v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Petition No. 01993683 (________, 2000), we affirmed the factual findings of an EEOC Administrative Judge who, after a hearing on the merits of another complaint filed by complainant, concluded that complainant made the threats at issue. We take judicial notice of this finding of fact. Moreover, we conclude that complainant failed to meet her burden of establishing that the agency's issuance of the notice of removal pursuant to its zero tolerance policy is a pretext for race, sex or disability discrimination or retaliation.
