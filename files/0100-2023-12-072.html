c. Contract Employment Agreement

Among the factors the Commission will look at in determining if a complainant is an employee of the agency or an employee of an independent contractor is any agreement the complainant has signed with the employing entity. However, as the Commission noted in Angelica P. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120172789 (2018):

The language of the contract between the agency and the staffing firm is not dispositive as to whether a joint-employment situation exists. In determining a worker's status, EEOC looks to what actually occurs in the workplace, even if it contradicts the language in the contract between the staffing firm and the agency. Baker v. Dep't of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01A45313 (Mar. 16, 2006) (while contract between staffing firm and agency provided that contract personnel were employees of staffing firm under its administrative supervision and control, agency actually retained supervisory authority over the contract workers).

In Clark v. Postmaster General, 01A11615 (2002), the Commission found there was little evidence of the actual working relationship between the complainant and the agency because the alleged discrimination occurred after he was on the worksite for only three days. However, in finding that the complainant was not an agency employee in Clark, the Commission noted:

The record shows that complainant signed a "Contract Employee Agreement" with TECH/AID Corporation, a private contractor, to fulfill an order with the agency. The agreement stated that complainant's assignment with the agency was to last approximately three months commencing on August 18, 1998. The agreement specified that the hourly rate for the assignment at issue was $20.16 per hour. According to the agreement, complainant was to submit a TECH/AID timecard at the end of each week, signed by a client supervisor to the company's branch office. The agreement prohibited complainant from accepting employment directly or indirectly from the client agency for ninety business days after completion of the assignment. The agreement stated that complainant was to contact the company's branch office as soon as he is notified that his assignment will be coming to an end. The agreement stated that TECH/AID and/or its clients reserve the right to rescind offers and terminate employees at will.

In a signed statement, complainant states that he was employed as a computer technician for the purpose of installing and testing software to make the agency's computers throughout the state of Maryland were "Y2K" compliant. Complainant states that he reported daily to Person A, the manager of information systems, at the agency's Baltimore office. Complainant claims that Person A gave him his work instructions. According to complainant, on the first day of the assignment Person A spent the day giving an orientation and explaining the software to be installed and tests to be completed. Complainant states that Person A also conducted a orientation on the second day, followed by a practice assignment. Complainant states that on the third day he was stopped at the employee entrance by the agency's police officer and told he was not allowed to enter the building.

In the present case, we recognize that complainant was at the agency for two days prior to the alleged discriminatory action and that therefore there is little evidence available to document the nature of the work done by complainant at the agency. Thus, the Commission finds the "Contract Employee Agreement" signed by complainant and TECH/AID of particular importance. The record reveals per the "Contract Employee Agreement" that TECH/AID hired complainant to complete an assignment at the agency, set the length of the assignment, and fixed the rate of pay complainant was to receive while working on the assignment. In addition, complainant was paid directly by TECH/AID. The record reflects that there was a high degree of skill necessary to complete the assignment and that TECH/AID determined that complainant possessed the relevant skill to complete the assignment. Further, the clause in the contract agreement stating that complainant was not to accept employment directly or indirectly with the agency for a specified period upon completion of the assignment is a strong indicator that at the time complainant signed the contract, he knew that he was not considered an employee with the agency. We note that although complainant stated that he was supervised by Person A and told on a daily basis what assignments to complete, complainant also stated that he attended orientation for the two days that he was at the agency and only participated in a practice drill and a test at one local station. In addition, the Commission notes that the computer work performed by complainant is not the main business of the agency. Thus, the Commission determines that complainant was not an agency employee under the purview of our regulations and that the complaint was properly dismissed for failure to state a claim.

In Smith v. Smithsonian Institution, 0120092412 (2009), the Commission again looked to the underlying terms of the arrangement to determine that the complainant was not an employee for Title VII purposes. In that case, the complainant was hired as a contractor to work on the Latin American Plant Initiative. The work entailed extracting samples, research and cataloging data. Although the work was performed on agency premises and using equipment provided by the agency, the Commission, in finding that the complainant in Smith was not an employee, noted:

The Statement of Work specifies, in full, that:

The contractor will work on the Latin American Plants Initiative (LAPI) Project under the direction of the Collections Manager, Department of Botany (also the COTR). Contractor will be responsible for tasks involving botanical collections, data and image generation, and library research. Following established procedures provided by the COTR, contractor will: a) review herbarium collections to extract possible type specimens, b) conduct library research to locate protologues to verify the type status of extracted specimens, and c) participate in data entry and image generation for specimens. All work will be done at SI location, and all materials and equipment will be provided by SI.

Under Independent Contractor Not an Employee:

It is understood that contractor is undertaking the work hereunder as an independent contractor, not as an employee of the Smithsonian Institution, and neither Contractor nor Contractor's employee are eligible for Smithsonian Institution benefits, including coverage under FECA (workers compensation) and FTCA (Federal Tort Claims Act), or coverage under any Smithsonian Institution workers compensation, medical liability or other insurance policy, or for legal protections afforded to employees under law applicable to employment relationships.

a) Contractor is responsible for providing, at Contractor's own expense and as necessary, disability, unemployment, workers compensation and other insurance, including adequate liability and property insurance, training, permits, and licenses for Contractor and for Contractor's employees,

b) Contractor is responsible for paying all taxes and income taxes, including estimated taxes, incurred as a result of the payments by the Smithsonian Institution to Contractor for performance of this Agreement.

Contractor agrees and acknowledges that the Smithsonian Institution assumes no responsibility whatsoever for the acts, errors and-or omissions of Contractor beyond those which the Smithsonian Institution is responsible for at law.

The Commission, while upholding the agency's dismissal of discrimination claims relating to his employment as an independent contractor with the agency, reversed and remanded the claim of discrimination in the agency's denial to convert the complainant's position to a government position, holding that in the circumstances of Hamilton v. Secretary of Navy, 01996039 (2000), the complainant was an applicant covered by Title VII protection:

The record is clear that complainant was working as an independent contractor at the time during which he was subjected to the alleged discrimination identified above. However, for the purposes of his claim that he was denied a permanent position with the agency because of discriminatory reasons, complainant's status became that of an applicant for employment. As such, for this claim complainant is covered by the EEOC regulations. The Commission notes that the agency failed to address complainant's claim concerning the denial of a permanent position with the agency, and the Commission deems the agency's action to be tantamount to a dismissal of that matter. Complainant's submissions on appeal reveal that complainant clearly referenced the matter in his formal complaint. Accordingly, we find that the agency's dismissal of this claim was improper.

Relying on Ma and Zheng v. Department of Health and Human Services, 01962390, 01962389 (1998), supra, the Commission found the complainant in Brown v. Postmaster General, 01A05797 (2001), was not an employee within the meaning of Title VII where her contract with the agency required her to provide her own vehicles that met the agency's specifications, she was liable for repair and maintenance of the vehicles, and the Postal Service made payments to the complainant without withholding any federal, state, or social security taxes. See also Goodwin v. Secretary of Army, 01A30822 (2003) (holding that complainant was an independent contractor where her pay for cleaning soldiers' rooms was negotiated with the soldiers, she provided her own supplies and equipment, and she did not report to an agency office).
