h. Commuting

In Boozer v. Postmaster General, 0120060283 (2007), the Commission held that the administrative judge erred in granting summary judgment to the agency when he held that, as a matter of law, the agency was not required to accommodate the complainant by switching her to a daytime shift because of difficulties driving at night. The Commission, in Boozer, explained:

After a careful review of the record, we find that the AJ erred when he concluded that, as a matter of law, complainant was not entitled to an accommodation of daytime hours. In making that finding, the AJ determined that complainant needed the requested accommodation because the car lights at night during her commute aggravated her condition. Finding that the requested accommodation only related to complainant's commute, and did not relate to an essential function of the job, the AJ found complainant was not entitled to that accommodation.

A request for a modified schedule such as daytime hours, is a reasonable accommodation. See EEOC Enforcement Guidance on Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under the Americans with Disabilities Act, EEOC Notice No. 915.002, at pp. 34-5 (October 17, 2002); Katrina Baillow v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 07A40111 (June 28, 2005) (finding of discrimination when agency failed to provide employee with daytime hours (June 28, 2005); Darlene Court v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 07A10114 (May 15, 2003) ("Absent another available, effective reasonable accommodation, the agency was required to provide complainant the accommodation she asked for, that of working full-time during the day"). If modifying an employee's schedule poses an undue hardship, an employer must consider reassignment to a vacant position that would enable the employee to work during the hours requested. EEOC Enforcement Guidance on Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship under the Americans with Disabilities Act at p. 34-45.

Furthermore, the Commission has previously held that accommodations related to commuting can be required under the Rehabilitation Act in appropriate circumstances. Eduardo De La Gaza v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01995346 (September 26, 2002) at n.8. See Hackney v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01984048 (August 22, 2001) (citing Hupka v. Department of Defense, EEOC Appeal No. 02960003 (August 13, 1997)); Mitchell v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01A03170 (January 31, 2001), aff'd EEOC Request No. 05A11093 (April 23, 2002). Accordingly, we find the AJ made an error of law on this matter.

Even so, we find there is a factual dispute in the record as to whether complainant desired the accommodation because of her commuting problems, or because the nighttime hours aggravated her condition in and of itself. For instance, on September 23, 2003, complainant's physician reported that she needs the daytime hours because her condition interferes with sleep, and that she is easily fatigued. See ROI Ex 2. Further, she added that complainant's condition interferes with her sleep patterns. See id. On October 20, 2003, she remarked that complainant needs daytime hours because her condition deteriorates at night, and because she encounters car lights. See id. On May 4, 2004, her physician stated that her symptoms exacerbate at night and she needs a daytime shift. Accordingly, there is evidence that complainant's condition is worsened at night due to interference with her sleep patterns, and that she has requested the daytime hours as an accommodation so that she can perform the essential functions of her job.

In a case where the back condition of a complainant made it too difficult for him to commute to and from work, the Commission found that the agency failed to accommodate him by delaying a response to his request to telecommute for two years. In Spence v. Nuclear Regulatory Commission, 0120041082 (2007), the Commission also found that the agency did accommodate the complainant when it subsequently granted his request to telecommute and for a flexible work schedule. The fact that the schedule did not have quite as much flexibility as the complainant would have liked did not constitute a failure to accommodate.
