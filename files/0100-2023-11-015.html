A. TOLLING THE PERIOD FOR COUNSELING

In some instances, the employee will not be aware that an action is based on a discriminatory motive until some later date. In certain circumstances, such unawareness is excusable and will toll the time limitation for initiating precomplaint counseling. The time period is considered analogous to a statute of limitations and is subject to the equitable principle of tolling. See Whitefield v. Department of Energy, 01832292 (1984) (citing Reeb v. Economic Opportunity Atlanta, 516 F.2d 924, 11 FEP 235 (5th Cir. 1975)).

The Commission's basic rule with respect to tolling the statute of limitations for initiating precomplaint counseling is set forth at 29 CFR 1614.105(a)(2):

The agency or the Commission shall extend the 45-day time limit in paragraph (a)(1) of this section when the individual shows that he or she was not notified of the time limits and was not otherwise aware of them, that he or she did not know and reasonably should not have known that the discriminatory matter or personnel action occurred....

In cases where a question arises as to whether the employee should have been aware of the alleged discrimination, a reasonable person standard will be imputed to the employee. Fahlberg v. Department of Air Force, 01842161 (1985). In other words, the employee will be required to show that: (1) he or she was not aware of the alleged discrimination; and (2) in the circumstances, a reasonable person would not have been aware of the alleged discrimination.

As previously noted, the Commission has made a minor distinction between the former Part 1613 and the current Part 1614 in cases where the employee was not reasonably aware at the time an action was taken that it might be discriminatory. Under Part 1613, the 30-day time limit for initiating counseling was not deemed to begin running until the employee knew, or reasonably should have suspected, that the action was discriminatory. Under Part 1614, such circumstances constitute grounds for waiving the 45-day time period for initiating counseling. The effect is the same. The difference, as noted above, is that tolling involves the determination of whether the time limit started to run and waiver involves the determination of whether there exists good cause for setting aside an expired time limit.

Under the doctrine of equitable tolling, the time period for initiating counseling does not begin to run until the occurrence of some event that should put the employee on notice of his or her complaint rights. Waiver goes to whether or not a party has allowed a legal right to lapse, or should be prevented from asserting that right because the party has not exercised due diligence in asserting the right. Even though the Commission blurred that distinction in Part 1614, the distinction is maintained here because it is important to understanding when otherwise untimely counselor contact will be deemed timely.

Also as noted at the outset of this chapter, the Commission's regulation regarding the timeliness of counselor contact, 29 CFR 1614.105(a)(2), combines all three theories for setting aside the time limit. The effect is that the Commission, in its cases involving timeliness, often fails to distinguish or properly label the operative theory being used in a given case. For the reader, though, the important thing to remember is that identifying the operative theory in a given case is not for the purpose of properly labeling that theory, but to properly focus attention on the appropriate factors for consideration.
