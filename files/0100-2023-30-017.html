4. Reinstatement

When retroactive reinstatement is ordered as part of the relief, the employee must be treated as though he or she was never terminated in the first place. This means that the agency cannot subject the returning employee to practices or procedures that are applied to newly hired employees.

For example, in Goins v. Postmaster General, 04920003 (1992), the agency sent a letter to the complainant, who had been ordered to be retroactively reinstated by the Commission, entitled "Dear Applicant." The letter directed the complainant to appear for drug testing, but also advised that current agency employees need not report for such testing. After deciding that the letter was so poorly written that the complainant was reasonable in not reporting for drug screening or even inquiring as to whether he was required to report, the Commission found, in any event, that the complainant could not be subjected to drug testing unless testing was being done for all current employees. "If the policy on which the agency is relying is only applicable to new hires and reinstatement candidates, petitioner should not be required to submit to the drug test, inasmuch as he would not have been required to take the test but for the agency's discrimination." Id.

At least in a case of sexual harassment, a complainant may be justified in failing to accept an agency's offer of reinstatement when the agency makes no specific assurances to prevent future harassment. In Spicer v. Secretary of Interior, 05940880 (1996), after a finding of discrimination, the complainant rejected the agency's offer of reinstatement and the Commission found:

[O]ur review of the record does not indicate that appellant was provided specific assurances that she would be free from future sexual harassment from S1, or further acts of retaliation by S2. We also note that the agency's FAD did not indicate whether appellant would have been under the supervision, direct or indirect, of S1 or S2 upon reinstatement. We could not locate an actual offer to appellant of reinstatement after the issuance of the FAD. Therefore, we are unable to determine what the offer stated. If the FAD was the offer, it was clearly insufficient, as it provided no assurances against being supervised by the harassing official, especially in light of appellant's claim that the Portland Area office had only one such position.

Nor could we find evidence that the offer of reinstatement was made to a position "not in her oriGINAl workplace." We agree with appellant that absent such assurances, it would have been unreasonable to accept such an offer, and therefore, the agency's back pay liability would not end at the time the offer was made. Consequently, we will direct the agency to issue appellant a new offer of reinstatement that provides her with specific assurances that she would not be under the direct or indirect supervision of S1 or S2. We will also direct the agency to award appellant back pay with interest through the date she is reinstated, or the date she declines the agency's offer.

If the agency cannot reinstate the employee to the position he or she would have held but for the discrimination, the agency must place the employee in a substantially equivalent position. The burden of showing that a position is substantially equivalent rests with the agency. See Robertson v. Postmaster General, 04A0008 (2001). Because of the length of time that it takes to adjudicate an EEO complaint, reinstatement of the employee to a substantially equivalent position can be a complicated matter due to changes that have taken place at the agency in the interim. For example, in Robertson, the position the employee was removed from no longer existed at the facility in question at the time of reinstatement:

The Commission's regulations require that, where an employee has been discriminated against, the agency shall offer the employee the position that she would have occupied absent the discrimination or, if justified by the circumstances, a substantially equivalent position. 29 CFR 1614.501(b)(1) A substantially equivalent position is one that is similar in duties, responsibilities, and location to the position for which petitioner applied. Patterson v. Department of Agriculture, EEOC Request No. 05940079 (October 21, 1994). The burden to prove substantial equivalency rests with the agency. Shaw v. Department of the Air Force, EEOC Request No. 05930370 (July 15, 1994).

The remedy phase of an action seeks to turn back the clock to the moment before discrimination occurred and, if possible, to move forward from that point without discrimination. See Michael Miller, et al. v. Department of the Interior, EEOC Request No. 05980293 (September 2, 1999). In the present case, the agency indicated in its Report of Compliance, that the Cleveland Office no longer has transitional letter carrier positions, pursuant to an agreement with the national union, and that petitioner was offered a casual position at the transitional pay rate. The agency issued petitioner a check for back pay to cover the period from September 12, 1993 through November 19, 1994, the date on which all transitional letter carriers were removed.

The Commission finds that the agency's offer of the casual position indicates that it considered the casual position to be substantially equivalent to that of transitional letter carrier. Absent the discrimination, petitioner's continued employment with the agency after November 19, 1994, would have been as a casual employee. Therefore, the Commission finds that to make petitioner whole, she should have been paid as a casual employee from November 19, 1994, to April 23, 1999, the date on which petitioner failed to report for medical assessment prior to reinstatement. See Dorothy Handy v. Department of Transportation, EEOC Petition No. 04950012 (February 23, 1996) (The agency retroactively offered petitioner three Air Traffic Controller positions that were each substantially equivalent to the position she would have occupied absent discrimination.), Constantine Economou v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01901060 (April 16, 1990) (Because of a reorganization, the supervisory GS-14 position complainant was denied due to discrimination was reclassified as non-supervisory GS-14, and the agency retroactively offered complainant back-pay and the non-supervisory GS-14 position.).

A similar scenario unfolded in Monroig v. Commission on Civil Rights, 05A20762 (2003), the Commission found that a reorganization did not absolve the agency of its obligation to place the complainant in her former position or, if it no longer existed, a substantially equivalent position. "Contrary to the agency's assertion that complainant is currently occupying a substantially equivalent position, the record shows that complainant does not have the level of responsibility, independence, status and official recognition that was part of her former position. Maintaining grave level and pay the same as before do not make the complainant whole after discrimination." Id.

A complainant is not entitled to reinstatement if the agency can demonstrate that the position has been eliminated and that the employee or employees who held those positions no longer work for the agency. As the Commission found in Valenza v. Smithsonian Institute, 04A20020 (2003):

The order of relief does not require the agency to create a position or to place complainant in a better posture than he would have been in, had the discrimination not occurred. The record before us on this petition shows that it is not feasible for the agency to offer the petitioner placement in his former position or a substantially equivalent one because his trust fund position no longer exists and there is no comparable position in which to place complainant. In support, we note that the record also shows that the position to which the petitioner was appointed, on December 15, 1992, was a trust fund indefinite position as a Mechanical Engineer, Institution Schedule ("IS") 13. The petitioner was hired specifically for the design and fabrication phase of the High Resolution Camera (HRC) contract with the National Air and Space Administration. That project ended on February 14, 1997, when the HRC was delivered to NASA at the Marshall Space Flight Center. Only scientists and senior engineers carried the HRC project through the integration phase. The record contains an affidavit that states that no mechanical engineers at the grade IS 13 level were assigned to the integration phase and none were still employed by the agency beyond the date of delivery of the HRC. None of the grade IS 13 mechanical engineers were employed by SAO after the delivery of the project. The record substantiates that this trust fund position was funded by a specific contract, like other trust fund positions at the Smithsonian Institution. The position was established as an indefinite position because the actual term of the project could not be determined at the time of appointment. The record contains an excerpt from the Trust Fund Handbook that explains that trust fund employees are paid from trust funds and not funds appropriated by Congress. See Bisson v. Office of Personnel Management, 908 F.2d 947 (Fed. Cir. 1990). For these reasons, we conclude that the position was a project related position, which no longer exists and that there is no comparable position in which to place the petitioner. We conclude that the agency has complied with the Commission's order to the extent feasible with regard to the issue of reinstatement.

If a complainant declines an offer of reinstatement, that declination cuts off entitled to back pay and the complainant is entitled to back pay only from the date of discrimination to the date of declining reinstatement. When a complainant accepts an offer of reinstatement, asks to report on a later date and subsequently changes his mind and declines reinstatement, back pay is terminated as of the date the oriGINAl reinstatement offer expires. As the Commission explained in Complainant v. Social Security Administration, 0420140008 (2014):

Consistent with the AJ's decision, Paragraph 1 of the Commission's Order stated that Petitioner had fifteen days to accept or decline the Agency's offer of reinstatement. Paragraph 1 also stated that, if Petitioner declined the offer, then the date of declination would be the end date for back pay. Because the Agency offered a position to Petitioner on April 4, 2011, he had fifteen days from that date, or until April 19, 2011, within which to accept or decline the offer. It appears that Petitioner accepted the offer, asked to begin working in June 2011, and then informed the Agency on June 17, 2011, that he would not be reporting to work. Petitioner's request to delay his start date for personal reasons does not extend the back pay period. Accordingly, we find that April 19, 2011, is the correct end date for the calculation of back pay.
