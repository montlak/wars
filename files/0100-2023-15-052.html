6. Full Relief Elements

What constitutes an offer of full relief is a matter of law and is unrelated to anything that an employee requests in settlement of the complaint. See Guliford v. Postmaster General, 05880239 (1988). "The test, therefore, in determining what constitutes make-whole relief in a given case is as follows: assuming the truth of appellant's allegations--or, phrased another way--assuming appellant were to prevail fully on his complaint--what relief, as a matter of law, would make him whole for the discrimination practiced against him, regardless of the relief the appellant is or is not seeking." Id. An order of full relief "must be specifically tailored to cure or correct the particular source of the identified discrimination and to minimize the chance of its recurrence." Hamilton v. Secretary of Navy, 05930782 (1994) (citing Allen v. Postmaster General, 05900152 (1990)). Where the complainant alleges that he was denied a promotion on a discriminatory basis, it is not full relief for the agency to merely agree that it would evaluate the merits of his allegation.

Because relief under Title VII involves both legal and equitable elements, determining full relief in a given case is a fact-intensive exercise. The offer of full relief must consist of all relief the employee would be entitled to if he or she prevailed on the complaint. The general parameters of full relief were first set down by the Supreme Court in Albemarle Paper Co. v. Moody, 422 U.S. 405, 95 S. Ct. 2362, 45 L. Ed.2d 280 (1975). The object of relief under Title VII is to, as nearly as possible, place the victim of discrimination in the position he or she would have been in had there been no discrimination.
