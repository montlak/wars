11. Relief Through Other Forums

Primarily, the question of whether resolution of a grievance renders an EEO claim moot only concerns the Postal Service and a few other agencies not covered by the Federal Labor Relations Act. For those employees working for agencies that are covered by the FLRA, there is a prohibition against filing a grievance and an EEO complaint over the same matter. See Chapter 20. Agencies not covered by the FLRA, such as the Postal Service, are not subject to the election of remedy provisions of the Civil Service Reform Act. In those agencies, it is possible for an employee to file both a grievance and an EEO complaint over the same matter.

The resolution of a grievance, whether through settlement or arbitration, does not per se render an EEO complaint involving the same matter moot. The relief provided in that resolution must be examined. If any further relief can be obtained in the EEO arena, then the complaint is not moot. Many of the decisions discussed below must also be considered in the context that compensatory damages were not available to Title VII when those cases were adjudicated. In many circumstances, a claim for compensatory damages will preclude any finding that a complaint is moot.

The remedy granted in the grievance procedure must be the same remedy that would have been granted in a discrimination complaint in order for it to render a complaint moot. For example, in Price v. Postmaster General, 01A54218 (2005), the complainant filed a grievance over denial of two opportunities for overtime. In settlement of the grievance, the agency agreed to provide the complainant with two opportunities for overtime. The Commission in Price found that this relief did not render the EEO complaint moot because the proper remedy based on a finding of discrimination would be to pay the complainant for the lost overtime and not merely provide an opportunity to work the missed overtime.

When an agency purges all letters of warning from a complainant's personnel file as a result of a pre-arbitration settlement, this completely and irrevocably eradicates the effects of any alleged discrimination. Moreover, in the absence of any evidence suggesting there is a reasonable expectation the letters of warning would be used in any future action, the agency properly dismisses the complaint as moot since the employee is no longer aggrieved. See Dawkins v. Postmaster General, 01923945 (1993); see also Robinson v. Postmaster General, 01A05456 (2001) (an EEO complaint is moot where a letter of reprimand was expunged as part of Step 2 grievance settlement). However, when a grievance settlement reduces a 10-day suspension to a documented work discussion, the employee is still aggrieved within the meaning of the EEO regulations. The Commission found that it is reasonable to conclude that such documentation could still be used for future disciplinary purposes. See Noonan v. Postmaster General, 01922267 (1993).

The settlement of a grievance that reduces a letter of warning to a "discussion" does not render a case moot where there is an underlying allegation that the employee is subjected to a hostile working environment as the result of discrimination. "A working environment heavily charged with discrimination may, in and of itself, constitute an unlawful employment practice." Pedro v. Postmaster General, 01910752 (1991) (citing Rogers v. EEOC, 454 F.2d 234, 239 (5th Cir. 1971)). Under such circumstances, it cannot be said that the settlement agreement completely eradicated the effects of the alleged discrimination, or that there was no reasonable expectation that a violation would not reoccur. Similarly, where the complainant has requested compensatory damages the subsequent reduction of a letter of warning to a work discussion through the grievance procedure does not render the claim moot. Jenkins v. Postmaster General, 01A52992 (2005).

The resolution of a grievance which provides that the agency will cease disparate treatment of the complainant, but does not remove all evidence of an official work discussion from agency records, does not completely eradicate the effects of the alleged discrimination. See Miller v. Postmaster General, 01931666 (1993). Similarly, where a performance rating is upgraded from fully satisfactory to highly successful as the result of an administrative grievance, this does not moot a discrimination claim that the employee should have been rated as exceptional. See Perez v. Secretary of Defense, 01990218 (2002).

The Commission in Celine D. v. Postmaster General, 2021003410 (2021), affirmed dismissal for mootness of a complaint of discrimination related to the complainant's indefinite suspension and proposed removal where, through settlement of her grievance, she was reinstated:

Here, the record shows that Complainant filed a union grievance regarding the Agency's refusal to reinstate her in August-September 2018, the very issue also raised in her EEO complaint. On May 31, 2019, the grievance was formally settled at Step 3 of the negotiated grievance procedure. The settlement agreement indicates that the Agency did not schedule Complainant for work after receipt of notification that she was found not guilty of the criminal charges at issue. In the settlement, the Agency agreed to return Complainant to duty and compensate Complainant for its failure to return her to work by paying her a lump sum of $6,673.84. Complainant acknowledges that she was reinstated to work on June 2, 2019. The record also includes evidence showing Complainant was paid the lump sum amount due under the settlement.

Here, we concur with the AJ's determination that the grievance settlement has rendered the EEO complaint moot. The record establishes that although Complainant's discrimination claims were not part of the related grievance, the effects of the alleged discrimination have been eradicated. It is undisputed that Complainant has been returned to work. Moreover, a fair reading of the settlement document indicates that the lump sum payment was to compensate Complainant for the harm she suffered by not being returned to work in 2018 after her criminal acquittal. Complainant now argues that she is entitled to additional relief because she was on suspension since 2014. However, we have already decided in EEOC Appeal No. 2019002842 that this complaint is limited to the issue of the Agency's failure to reinstate Complainant in 2018 following her acquittal and we will not now consider Complainant's application for relief for the earlier indefinite suspension. In the grievance settlement, Complainant agreed to accept the lump sum payment as compensation for the harm caused her in 2018. We conclude that Complainant has accepted her reinstatement and lump sum payment as the measure of the harm caused her in 2018. As such, we conclude that the grievance settlement has rendered the EEO complaint moot. Complainant has been returned to work, so there is no reasonable expectation that the alleged violation will recur, and the settlement eradicated the effects of the failure to reinstate her in 2018 (the alleged discrimination).

When the MSPB entirely reverses a removal based on a violation of the Whistleblower Protection Act and provides all available relief, that action renders moot any claims of discrimination in the removal. However, the EEOC does continue to have jurisdiction over allegations of discrimination in related personnel actions that are not within the jurisdiction of the Board. See Buckles v. Secretary of Army, 01931627 (1994). The continued vitality of the Buckles decision is questionable, since the conduct in that case predated the Civil Rights Act of 1991 providing compensatory damages for complainants. For example, in Christopher v. Postmaster General, 01A12114 (2001), the Commission held that a grievance settlement that reduced a seven-day suspension to a work discussion did not render a complaint moot where the complainant requested compensatory damages.
