E. ANONYMITY FOR COMPLAINANTS

Under 29 CFR 1614.105(g), complainants who contact EEO counselors have a right to anonymity. The counselor may not disclose the identity of a complainant without permission until a formal complaint is filed. Once a formal complaint is filed, the complainant no longer has a right to remain anonymous. Bearce v. Secretary of Army, 01881355 (1988). Nothing in Title VII guarantees confidentiality to complainants. See Renee L. v. Secretary of Commerce, 0120141032 (2017); Sofio v. Secretary of Treasury, 05880633 (1988).

Despite the regulatory guarantee of anonymity in the counseling stage, in Gaines v. Secretary of Navy, 01941789 (1994), the Commission found a complainant was not aggrieved by the failure of the agency to provide a private location for counseling:

The only proper questions in determining whether an allegation is within the purview of the EEO process are whether the complainant is an aggrieved employee or applicant and whether s/he has alleged employment discrimination covered by the EEO statutes. An employee or applicant is aggrieved if s/he has suffered direct and personal deprivation at the hands of the employer. See Hobson v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05891133 (March 2, 1990). In this case, appellant alleged that he was unable to contact an EEO Counselor without this information being revealed because he had to walk into an open office and sit at a desk in a open space. We find, however, that appellant has not shown that he suffered any personal loss or harm associated with the agency's alleged action; there is no evidence that the agency's action adversely affected his ability to pursue his complaints or deterred him from initiating EEO counseling either on his own behalf or on behalf of others. We thus find that appellant was not aggrieved by the agency's actions as set forth in his complaint and AFFIRM the agency's dismissal of issue 2.

The Commission did not explain why the loss of anonymity, standing alone, was not a sufficient personal injury, particularly where its regulations provide for anonymity.

Anonymity in the counseling process is not absolute, however. For example, in Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120123174 (2014), the Commission found that the counselor did not breach the complainant's confidentiality where the complainant revealed he had a gun on agency premises and agency safety policy required the counselor to report that matter.
