broida eeo Chapter 13: Bases of Discrimination Under Title VII Chapter 13: Bases of Discrimination Under Title VII, Subchapter 03: National Origin, Section H: Native Americans

H. NATIVE AMERICANS

By statute, Congress granted employment preferences in certain instances to Native Americans within those agencies of the Department of Interior dealing with Indian affairs. See generally Title 25 of the U.S. Code. Title VII does not interfere with or impede those statutory preference rights.

The Commission has no jurisdiction over allegations of violations of the Indian Preference Act. See Edwards v. Department of Interior, 01851536 (1987). In that case, the Commission assumed, without deciding, however, that a person alleging he was nonselected in violation of the Act had alleged a prima facie case of race discrimination and was asserting a statutory violation as evidence of discrimination. The Commission found no discrimination because all affected tribes preferred the white selectee and had signed a waiver of the Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act, 25 USC Part 450. The Commission treated the complaint as one of race discrimination, though probably it is treated more properly as an allegation of discrimination based on color or national origin.

In Morton v. Mancari, 417 U.S. 535 (1974), the Supreme Court found that Indian preference superseded Title VII and that a non-Indian complainant could not challenge the selection of a Native American under Indian preference on a Title VII basis.

Whether a particular selection is actually based on Indian preference, and thus is a bar to a Title VII action, is an issue that goes the merits of the case. As such, the agency errs in dismissing the complaint as identical to the complaint raised in Morton without an investigation.

In Baldwin v. Secretary of Interior, 01A21546 (2003), the Commission explained Indian preference in the context of a nonselection case:

The Commission notes that Federal policy of according employment preferences to Indians in the Indian service dates as far back as 1834. Act of June 30, 1834, 25 U.S.C. 45. Since that time, Congress has repeatedly enacted various preferences of the type at issue here. Currently, the modern Act provides that qualified Indians shall have preference to appointments to vacancies in the Bureau of Indian Affairs and the Indian Health Service. The purposes of the Indian preference laws are to give Indians a greater participation in their own self-government, to further the government's trust obligation towards the Indian tribes, and to reduce the negative effects of having non-Indians administer matters that affect Indian tribal life. Morton v. Mancari, 417 U.S. 535, 542 (1974).

We have stated that there is no doubt that Indian preference laws result in employment disadvantages within the Department of the Interior for non-Indians. See Eisenbise v. Department of Health and Human Services, EEOC Appeal No. 01933009 (November 24, 1993). However, Congress apparently decided that in this particular employment situation the good to be gained by the preference outweighed the harm caused by it to non-Indians. The Supreme Court has directly ruled that the Act was not repealed or affected by the Equal Employment Opportunity Act of 1972, which amended Title VII to proscribe discrimination in most areas of Federal employment. Morton, supra, 417 U.S. at 546-551. The Court in Morton specifically found that Indian preference is a longstanding and important component of the government's Indian program which does not violate Title VII. Id. This conclusion was found to be mandated by the history and the purposes of the preference and the unique legal relationship between the Federal government and the Indian tribes. Eisenbise, supra. As such, we concur with the FAD's finding that although complainant met the qualifications for the CI position and was recommended to be selected by facility management, the selection of two qualified Native American candidates for the positions at issue was due to the mandate of the Act and not based on discriminatory animus due to race.

See also Hester v. Secretary of Interior, 0120110884 (2012); Freader v. Secretary of Interior, 0120081576 (2011).

However, the Commission has found that the Indian Preference Act only permits racial preferences in hiring decisions and not in decisions to terminate employees. In Cooper v. Secretary of Interior, 07A50098 (2006), the complainant was hired as a facilities manager for three Apache schools. The administrative judge found that a school board resolution calling on the agency not to renew the complainant's contract was racially motivated because the complainant was Caucasian and not Apache. The Commission first rejected an agency argument that the school board was appointed by tribal members and independent of the agency. Because there was racial motivation behind the recommendation, the Commission found the decision not to renew the complainant's contract was also racially motivated. Finally, the Commission held that "[w]hile the Indian Preference Act of 1934, permits racial preferences in hiring decisions of the agency, it does not permit racial motives in terminating existing employees." Id.
