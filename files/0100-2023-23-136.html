B. POSTAPPOINTMENT INQUIRIES

There is nothing discriminatory in conducting postappointment medical examinations and making continued employment contingent upon the successful completion of such an exam, provided the examination is conducted consistent with the Rehabilitation Act, i.e, the inquiry or examination is job-related and consistent with business necessity. An agency may not abrogate an individual's rights under the Rehabilitation Act by requiring him to sign a preemployment statement to the effect that he understands his appointment is subject to a medical examination. Metza v. Postmaster General, 05880181 (1988). While such a statement itself is not inappropriate, to the extent it can be interpreted to limit an employee's rights under the Rehabilitation Act, it is void as against public policy.

The ADA also addresses the use of postappointment medical examinations at 29 USC 12112(d)(4):

Examination and Inquiry.

(A) Prohibited examinations and inquiries. A covered entity shall not require a medical examination and shall not make inquiries of an employee as to whether such employee is an individual with a disability or as to the nature and extent of the disability, unless such examination or inquiry is shown to be job-related and consistent with business necessity.

(B) Acceptable examinations and inquiries. A covered entity may conduct voluntary medical examinations, including voluntary medical histories, which are part of an employee health program available to employees at that work site. A covered entity may make inquiries into the ability of an employee to perform job-related functions.

See also 29 CFR 1630.13-1630.14.

Information on an employee's medical condition or history is subject to the same confidentiality requirements as information obtained during preemployment inquiries. See 42 USC 12112(d)(3).

When an agency disqualifies an employee on the basis of a postappointment medical examination, it must do so on more than the speculation of the examining physician that the complainant's condition may "flare-up" in the future and cause lost time at work. Metza, supra.

However, if an agency conducts a postappointment inquiry on all employees it may ask disability-related questions. An agency does not discriminate on the basis of disability when it subsequently terminates an employee for giving false information in response to a postappointment inquiry. As the Commission explained in Martinez v. Postmaster General, 03A20043 (2002):

Assuming arguendo for purposes of this decision only that petitioner is a qualified individual with a disability within the meaning of the Rehabilitation Act, the Commission agrees with the MSPB Administrative Judge that petitioner failed to prove, by a preponderance of the evidence, that the agency's legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for removing him was a pretext for disability discrimination. The agency stated that after petitioner filed a worker's compensation claim, as is the agency's practice, it submitted his name to a nationwide insurance index system listing information on individuals who have filed claims, including dates of accidents, types of injuries, and settlements. A match returned from the insurance index revealed that petitioner had received $16,519.19 from an insurance company in settlement for an injury sustained in a 1993 motor vehicle accident. In comparing this information to petitioner's Form 2485, his failure to disclose was revealed. The agency stated that as a result of the falsification of Form 2485, the agency lost trust in petitioner's honesty and integrity. The MSPB Administrative Judge found that petitioner failed to present any evidence to corroborate his claim that it was disability discrimination which motivated the agency's action. We agree.

Petitioner argues on appeal that there was something improper about the pre-employment inquiries on Form 2485 and that he should not have had to "disclose any previous medical information on a possible handicapped injury." However, we note that petitioner was asked to complete Form 2485 after a conditional offer of employment was made. Once a conditional offer of employment is made, the agency may ask disability-related questions and require medical examinations as long as it does so for all entering employees in that job category. There is no evidence in the record to suggest that petitioner was treated differently from any other entering employees. Disability-related questions and medical examinations at the post offer stage do not have to be related to the job, but if an applicant is screened out because of disability, the employer must show that the exclusionary criterion is job related and consistent with business necessity. 29 C.F.R. 1630.14(b)(3). There is no evidence in the record to support a finding that petitioner was screened out because of disability. In fact, he was hired and entered into employment with the agency.

As explained in Rose v. Postmaster General, 05A00794 (2002), in order to make a disability-related inquiry or require a medical examination that is not required of all other similarly situated employees, the agency must fulfill one of two conditions:

[W]e remind the agency that the Rehabilitation Act places certain limitations on an employer's ability to make disability-related inquiries or require medical examinations of employees. The Commission has stated that an employer may require a medical examination of an employee only if the examination is job-related and consistent with business necessity. Enforcement Guidance on the ADA and Psychiatric Disabilities (March 25, 1997) (March 1997 Guidance), p. 15. This requirement is met when the employer has a reasonable belief, based on objective evidence, that (1) an employee's ability to perform essential job functions is impaired by a medical condition; or (2) that an employee poses a direct threat due to a medical condition. See July 2000 Guidance, pp. 6-9. Objective evidence is reliable information, either directly observed or provided by a credible third party, that an employee may have or has a medical condition that will interfere with his/her ability to perform essential job functions or will result in direct threat. Id., p. 7. Where the employer forms such a belief, its disability-related inquiries and medical examinations are job-related and consistent with business necessity. Id.

Our prior decision found that the agency did not improperly require complainant to provide the medical documentation before us, because there was objective evidence that complainant's ability to perform the essential job functions was impaired by a medical condition. Specifically, the agency provided evidence that complainant's physician, who diagnosed complainant as having post-traumatic stress disorder, was of the opinion that such medical updates were necessary. This medical opinion, combined with the threatening outburst and emergency suspension which occurred on April 15, 1996, constituted sufficient objective evidence that complainant's ability to perform the essential job functions was impaired by a medical condition.

However, we take the opportunity to again advise the agency that its authority to make medical inquiries is confined to situations where the inquiries are necessary to determine whether the employee currently is unable to perform his/her essential job functions or poses a direct threat due to the condition. Our prior decision addressed only the medical updates in the record before us. Accordingly, if the agency's requests for medical updates has continued even though complainant believes that such updates are not necessary to determine his current ability to perform the essential functions of his job, we advise complainant that he has the right to file a new complaint of discrimination. July 2000 Guidance, p. 31.
