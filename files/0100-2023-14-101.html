3. Impossibility

If the sole provision of a settlement agreement is impossible to perform, the agreement is void for lack of consideration. For example, in Hunter v. Postmaster General, 01A22002 (2002), the agency agreed that it would instruct a customer to erect a new mail box, but then discovered since the existing box was not in violation of Postal Service regulations, it could not do so.

In Boisvert v. Secretary of Air Force, 01A24437 (2003), the Commission found the settlement agreement was void for lack of consideration where the parties agreed that the agency's Human Relations Office, with the input of the complainant and his attorney, would draft a letter that constituted a legally binding admission that the agency had no jobs within his medical restrictions. Thereafter, the agency declared the letter did not constitute such an admission. Finding that was the only benefit the complainant had bargained for, the Commission in Boisvert found the agreement was unenforceable. Similarly in Tang v. Postmaster General, 01A42743 (2005), the Commission found that the settlement agreement was void for lack of consideration where it provided that the complainant's request for reinstatement would be supported by two management officials. However, agency regulations barred the reinstatement of employees removed for cause. Because there was no possibility of the employee being reinstated, the Commission held that the agreement was void.

When the agency agrees to reinstate a complainant under certain conditions, but knows the complainant cannot meet those conditions, the settlement agreement fails for lack of consideration. In Wilson v. Postmaster General, 01973095 (1998), the Commission found:

On September 14, 1992, appellant and the agency entered into a settlement agreement to resolve appellant's formal EEO complaint. The agreement provided:

1. [Appellant] is qualified for reinstatement on the basis of being a former employee and being qualified as a clerk.

2. [Appellant] was not considered for reinstatement because the agency was not in a hiring posture for clerks since [appellant] has filed his complaint.

3. [Appellant] will be considered for future employment as a manual clerk if [a] position becomes available in the SMD [Southern Maryland Division] and subordinate branches.

4. [Appellant] may be considered for transitional employment as a clerk.

5. [Appellant] may also apply to be administered the mail-handlers and labor custodian examination on a non-competitive basis in accordance with Handbook EL 311, Part 261.33 through 261.33, Part B, Sub Item 2.

6. [Appellant] was not considered for reinstatement solely because of the reasons 2 cited in affidavit[s] B and C of the investigative file.

According to appellant, the agency has breached the settlement agreement by failing to reinstate him. In the subject decision dated February 6, 1997, the agency determined that it had not breached the settlement agreement.

EEOC Regulation 29 CFR § 1614.504(a) provides that any settlement agreement knowingly and voluntarily agreed to by the parties shall be binding on both parties. It further provides that if the complainant believes that the agency failed to comply with the terms of a settlement agreement, the complainant shall notify the Director of Equal Employment of the alleged noncompliance with the settlement agreement. Id. The complainant may request that the terms of the settlement agreement be specifically implemented or request that the complaint be reinstated for further processing from the point processing ceased under the terms of the settlement agreement. Id.

In Baker v. Chicago Fire & Burglary Detection, Inc., 489 F.2d 953, 955 (7th Cir, 1973), the court held that a valid contract must be based upon consideration where some right, interest, profit, or benefit accrues to one party or some forbearance, detriment, loss, or responsibility is given, suffered, or undertaken by the other. Where the promisor receives no benefit and the promisee suffers no detriment, the whole transaction is a nudum pactum. See Collins v. USPS, EEOC Request No. 05900082 (Apr. 26, 1990) (a settlement agreement that was not based upon adequate consideration was unenforceable).

Provisions 3, 4, and 5 of the settlement agreement provided three scenarios in which appellant could be considered for employment with the agency: appellant could be considered for future considered for employment as a manual clerk; appellant could be considered for transitional employment as a clerk; or appellant could apply to take the mailhandlers and laborer-custodian examination on a noncompetitive basis. While the agreement merely provided that appellant would be considered for reinstatement, it appears that appellant could not be considered for employment under any of the provisions of the settlement agreement. With respect to provision 3, the record indicates that appellant could not be considered for reinstatement because, as a result of automation, the agency did not hire any manual distribution clerks during the relevant time frame. Indeed, we note that the record indicates that the agency was in the process of implementing automation at the time that the agreement was executed, thereby eliminating clerk positions, and that the agency had not hired a manual distribution clerk since June 1 1991, over a year before the settlement agreement was even executed. With respect to provision 4, the record indicates that appellant could not be considered for transitional employment as a clerk due to his lifting restriction. It appears, however, that appellant was injured on the job in 1987, and that the agency was aware of appellant's lifting restriction when it entered into the settlement agreement. With respect to provision 5, the record indicates that, although appellant passed a noncompetitive mailhandlers examination, he could not be considered for a laborer/custodian position because those positions are restricted to preference eligibles under the Veterans' Preference Act of 1944.

Therefore, based on the foregoing, we find that appellant has accrued no benefit as a result of entering into the settlement agreement. Indeed, the agency stated, in its FAD, that the terms of the settlement agreement cannot be implemented. Moreover, the record reflects that the agency knew or should have known, at the time that it entered into the agreement, that circumstances would prevent it from implementing the terms of the agreement and that appellant would receive no benefit from the agreement. Because appellant has received no consideration in exchange for his agreement to withdraw his EEO complaint, we find that the settlement agreement is unenforceable; thus, in accordance with regulations, the agency should reinstate appellant's complaint for further processing from the point processing ceased.
