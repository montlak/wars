M. NOTICE OF REPRESENTATION

An agency is not obligated to pay attorney fees for work performed before it is on notice that the complainant is represented by counsel. See Hardnick v. Secretary of Air Force, 01971951 (1998). The complainant is not required to give written notice of representation, however, and the appearance of counsel before the agency during the EEO process is sufficient to put the agency on notice that the complainant is being represented. See Safford v. Postmaster General, 01952348 (1997); Tates v. Department of Health and Human Services, 01850647 (1986). The preparation of an appeal document by counsel, where it is signed by the appellant, does not put an agency on notice that the appellant is represented. See Pollan v. Postmaster General, 01882719 (1988). But, a formal complaint signed by the attorney does put the agency on notice. See Koehler v. General Services Administration, 01A02276 (2002).

However, the Commission also has held that written notice of representation is generally required. In Atienza v. Secretary of Navy, 01872727 (1988), the Commission held that notification from the complainant's counsel by telephone was not adequate to meet the notification requirements. Given the later case law, it is doubtful that Atienza remains valid. For example, in Lepree-Williams v. Secretary of State, 01A24154 (2004), the Commission found that the complainant's attorney was not entitled to hours expended prior to notification of representation. In that case, the Commission rejected the complainant's contention that the agency was orally notified of representation not because oral representation was not sufficient but because there was no proof, beyond a mere assertion, that the oral notification actually occurred. Similarly, in Porter v. Secretary of Navy, 01A43720 (2005), the Commission found that the agency was not on notice of counsel's appearance until it was notified in writing of counsel's representation even though it had included counsel in earlier correspondence with the complainant.

However, in Black v. Postmaster General, 0120072590 (2008), the Commission rejected the agency's contention that the complainant was not entitled to fees prior to receiving a formal notice of appearance from his counsel. The Commission, in Black, explained:

The agency's justification for denying the fees for services rendered between February 14, 2002 and March 1, 2004 appears to be that the agency did not have notice of representation until complainant filed his initial appeal, EEOC Appeal No. 0120042589 in April 2004. The Commission is not persuaded by this argument. The relevant regulatory authority reads as follows:

Attorney's fees shall be paid for services performed by an attorney after the filing of a written complaint, provided that the attorney provides reasonable notice of representation to the agency, administrative judge or Commission, except that fees are allowable for a reasonable period of time prior to the notification of representation for any services performed in reaching a determination to represent the complainant.... Written submissions to the agency that are signed by the representative shall be deemed to constitute notice of representation.

29 C.F.R. § 1614 501(e)(iv).

Complainant filed his formal complaint on March 3, 2002. Approximately two weeks prior to filing, complainant and his attorney engaged with the agency in approximately three hours of alternative dispute resolution at the agency facility where complainant worked, and the attorney's fee petition indicates that a letter dated May 21, 2002 was sent by complainant's attorney to complainant's supervisor, who had been in attendance at the alternative dispute resolution session. This letter followed two March 2002 phone calls from complainant's attorney to the same supervisor.

The Commission finds that the agency had reasonable written notice of representation in May 2002 and that fees are available to complainant for services rendered between February and May 2002 as they were performed in reaching a determination to represent complainant. In reaching this conclusion, we acknowledge the agency's argument that complainant did not indicate on his formal complaint that he was represented, nor did he do so in his June 11, 2002 affidavit. However, the relevant regulatory authority contemplates that it is the attorney who is responsible for providing notification, not complainant, and we are not persuaded that the regulations require a formal notice of representation such as the one complainant's attorney submitted to the agency in June 2003.

The Commission also made an exception to the notice provision and the general restriction of two hours review time to determine if counsel will take a case in Complainant v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120121159 (2015), where complainant's oriGINAl counsel died during the pendency of the case:

Regarding the hours expended, the Attorney asserted entitlement to fees for representing Complainant prior to entering an appearance. In essence, the Attorney indicated that Complainant had until September 7, 2011, less than 20 days from the date Complainant first contacted him to respond to the Agency's Motion for Reconsideration. The record indicates that the Attorney did, in fact, respond to the Agency's motion in a timely manner to the AJ. The Attorney had to review the hearing transcript which was well over 300 pages, the report of investigation, and the Agency's motion prior to submission of Complainant's response. The Attorney asserted that, from August 23, 2011 to September 7, 2011, the 14.5 hours excluded by the Agency were expended meeting with Complainant, getting the files from Complainant, reviewing the hearing transcript and investigation file, conducting research and drafting Complainant's reply.

In general, two hours of time is considered reasonable for counsel to consider whether or not to represent a complainant. See Brown v. U.S. Dep't of Justice, EEOC Appeal No. 0120072877 (Feb. 25, 2009); Stance v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 0120080515 (June 24, 2010). In this case, the Agency allowed for 2 hours as reasonably performed in reaching a determination to represent Complainant, and did not object to that work.

However, we find that the circumstances of this case are unique. In cases such as Brown and Stance, supra, representation was considered prior to the filing of the EEO complaint. Here, Complainant had been represented by another individual who passed away while her complaint was pending a decision by the AJ. The record for the Attorney to review to consider representation consisted of hundreds of pages of hearing transcripts and the Agency's investigative file. Furthermore, Complainant received the Agency's Motion for Reconsideration on August 19, 2011. She was given 20 days to respond to the Agency's Motion. Based on the time constraints created by the Agency's filing of the Motion and the extensive volume of documents that needed to be reviewed by the Attorney, we find that the exclusion of 14.5 hours by the Agency for work conducted prior to September 7, 2011, was not appropriate.

Following the adage that it is better to be safe than sorry, the best course is to advise the agency in writing that a representative has been designated.
