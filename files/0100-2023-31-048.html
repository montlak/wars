7. Fee Petitions

Time spent in the preparation of a fee petition is compensable. However, the Commission has been greatly inconsistent on the hourly rate that should be awarded for such work. In several cases, the Commission has held that a lesser hourly rate is appropriate because the work involved in preparing fee petitions is not considered to require great legal skill. See Jackson v. Department of Army, 01831806 (1985); see also Runnels v. Defense Logistics Agency, 01871336 (1987) (holding that the preparation of a fee petition does not require great legal skill and approving one-half of the attorney's customary hourly rate). But see In re Reitan, supra (such hours normally are included in general overhead costs). The better view is that time spent in preparation of fee petitions is compensable, and that has been the trend of recent cases.

Although the costs of general billing are included in a firm's overhead costs, lawyers do not customarily submit bills to clients in the same form and detail as is required to sustain a fee petition. Because the additional time invested in preparation of a fee petition is required by the EEOC, such time should be compensable. In addition, though it is true that preparation of a fee affidavit does not require great legal skill, the time spent by counsel in preparing the affidavit takes away from time that could be spent on other legal matters requiring greater skill.

In more recent years, the Commission continued to drift toward a position where it does not distinguish between the reasonable hourly rate for litigation and fee petitions. See, e.g., Tom v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, 01911245 (1991). That now appears to be its view. In keeping with a recent trend toward compensating attorneys more fully for work spent on fee petitions, the Commission in Curry v. Secretary of Army, 01984576 (2001), found that the attorney was entitled to his full hourly rate for the time spent preparing the fee petition:

The agency also reduced complainant's hourly rate charged for preparation of the fee petition. Without providing any case law in support of the reduction, the agency concluded complainant's attorney was only entitled to two-thirds of his hourly rate for work in conjunction with preparation of the fee petition. We acknowledge that in the past the Commission has held that since the work involved in preparing fee petitions is not considered to require great legal skill, the hourly rate allowed is less than that for actual legal services. See Campau v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 05910293 (May 13, 1991); Runnels v. Defense Logistics Agency, EEOC Appeal No. 01871336 (December 11, 1987). However more recently, the Commission has held that an attorney's work in preparation of the fee petition should be evaluated utilizing a reasonableness standard, which can mean compensating the attorney at his full hourly rate. See Black v. Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05960390 (December 9, 1998). In light of the more recent Commission precedent and the circumstances of this case, we find it reasonable to compensate complainant's attorney at his full hourly rate for the preparation and defense of the fee petition. We conclude that complainant's attorney expended time and research attempting to resolve the fee issue and therefore should be compensated at his full hourly rate for the legal work associated with the petition.

In Layman v. Postmaster General, 01995568 (2002), the Commission found it was not unreasonable for the attorneys involved in the case to spend nearly 16 hours preparing the fee petition. Although the decision does not indicate the number of hours the attorneys spent litigating the case, it was clearly a substantial number since the total fee was over $126,000.

The better practice is to fully document fee requests to an agency. However, there is no requirement that a fee request to an agency be documented, and the Commission will consider evidence submitted for the first time on appeal. In Mathers v. Postmaster General, 01902612 (1990), the Commission held:

[W]e find that the agency's argument that the Commission should not consider documentation which was not submitted with his initial fee request to be misplaced. 29 CFR 1613.271(d)(2)(i) is a directive to the agency, in its role as finder of fact in issuing its FAD on attorney's fees, and to this Commission in our capacity as finders of fact in coming to our decisions on appeals and requests to reopen, on how to calculate attorney's fees for those decisions. It is not a directive to the complainant or complainant's counsel on what is required of them in submitting a verified statement and accompanying affidavit as required under 29 CFR 1613.271(c)(2). Second, decisions made by the agency under 29 CFR 1613.221(d) in accordance with 1613.271(c) are subject to de novo review by this Commission, as is any other decision appealable to the Commission under 29 CFR 1613.231....

But see Childs v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01994951 (2002) (holding that an agency is not required to seek clarification of inadequate time entries).

Previously, the Commission adhered to the rule that time spent in preparation of a fee petition should not exceed 5% of the hours spent litigating a case on the merits, where a hearing is held. See Tom v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, supra. But that rule has now been replaced with a "reasonableness" rule that assesses time spent in preparation of fee requests under the same standard as other time expended in the case. See, e.g., Childs v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01994951 (2002); Lee v. Secretary of Defense, 01982578 (2000). However, as the Commission demonstrated in Lee, the assessment of reasonableness can take into account the overall hours expended on the merits of the case. In Lee, the Commission reduced the award for time spent preparing the fee request where the claimed time amounted to a full one-third of the time spent on the merits of the case.

The Commission reaffirmed its rejection of the three to five percent rule in Poquiz v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0720050095 (2008), finding that 20.6 hours had been reasonably spent on preparing a fee petition. The Commission, in Poquiz, found:

We note that the agency specifically challenges the 20.6 hours billed for the fee petition by Attorney 1 and the 25.3 hours charged for work conducted on the post-hearing brief. With regard to the 20.6 hours billed for time spent on the fee petition, the agency claims this amount is excessive and argues that only 7.3 hours of work should be recoverable for preparing a fee petition since this represents 5% of the total hours spent on a complaint with a hearing which it claims is the cap recognized by the Commission. Alternatively, the agency argues that the number of hours spent on the fee petition were unnecessary.

We note that the agency erroneously relied on outdated case law ruling that hours for preparing an attorney's fee petition should not exceed 3 % of the hours in the main case when the issue is submitted on paper without a hearing, and should not exceed 5% of the hours in the main case when a hearing is necessary. Specifically, in Black v. Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05960390 (Dec. 9, 1998), the Commission found the 3 % and 5% caps represented a "mechanized" and "arbitrary" approach for an award of fees and costs. Instead, the Commission adopted a "reasonableness standard" as the better approach to fee petition issues. Black, EEOC Request No. 05960390. The Commission noted that detailed billing information and extensive documentary and legal support are needed for a fee petition. Id.

Upon review, we find the 20.6 hours claimed by Attorney 1 in preparing the instant fee petition appear to be reasonably expended. Specifically, we note that such time billed included reviewing sample fee petitions, drafting affidavits and declarations in support of the fee petition, organizing and reviewing documents as attachments to the attorney's fee petition, reviewing fees and cost transaction sheets, and calculating the final total for the attorney's fee petition. We find that the hours billed for work on the instant fee petition constitute reasonable time spent working on preparing the fee petition and reviewing and assembling necessary documents to support the petition.
