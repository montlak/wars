C. STANDING: FAILURE TO STATE A CLAIM--DEFINITION OF EMPLOYEE

Title VII, the Rehabilitation Act, the ADEA and the Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act cover employees, applicants for employment and, as discussed below, some cases involving former employees. Most of the law that has developed surrounding the definition of an employee has involved Title VII, but the Commission routinely applies that law to cases under the remaining acts because of the definition of employee is virtually identical in all of the acts.

"A complaint must be filed with the agency that allegedly discriminated against the complainant." 29 CFR 1614.106(a). A complaint of discrimination may be filed against an agency that is not the employing agency if that agency is allegedly the cause of the discrimination. See, e.g., Drew N. v. Postmaster General, 2019003270 (2019); Marvella B. v. Secretary of Defense, 2019004741 (2019); Mares v. Secretary of Army, 01960499 (1998); Velez v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs and Secretary of Air Force, 01961480 (1997). See "Employees of Other Agencies," below for a more fulsome discussion. The employing agency should then be joined as an indispensable party for the purposes of relief. See the section on "Agency Jurisdiction," below. But see "Failure to State a Claim: Investigations and Other Proceedings--Collateral Attacks" below.

An employee who files a complaint against the Merit Systems Protection Board alleging that the initial decision of the Board was discriminatory is not an employee or applicant for employment within the meaning of the Act. See Pletten v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 01810100 (1983). But see Mahdavi v. Office of Personnel Management, 01822299 (1986) (holding that the Title VII procedures apply to "any 'aggrieved employee or applicant for employment'" and there is no requirement that the complaint be filed with the employing agency; in that case, the complainant sought employment with the Navy, but was excluded by OPM from the list of eligibles).
