D. PREVAILING ON PROCEDURAL ISSUES

As much of an injustice as it may seem to complainants forced to go to the EEOC for vindication of their procedural rights to pursue a complaint, attorney fees are not awarded for prevailing on procedural matters. The complainant must prevail on the merits of the complaint to be entitled to such fees. Grossman v. Postmaster General, 05880472 (1988); see also Holt v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 05A50446 (2005) (remand for supplementation of record not entitling complainant to fee award).

Although an agency is not required to compensate a complainant for attorney fees for prevailing on a procedural issue, there is not always a clear demarcation between a procedural and a substantive issue. Weaver v. Secretary of Defense, 01913114 (1991).

In Weaver, the complainant sought EEO counseling regarding a nonselection. However, at the conclusion of counseling, she did not file a formal complaint because the counselor informed her that acceptance of the complaint was contingent on identifying the discriminating official. At that time, the complainant did not specifically know who was responsible for the selection decision and, as a result, did not file a complaint. The complainant attempted to refile her complaint after discovering that it was not necessary to identify an alleged discriminating official as a prerequisite to filing a formal complaint. The agency dismissed the complaint as untimely and the complainant appealed to the EEOC. The Commission vacated the agency's decision and remanded the complaint for processing.

The agency sought reconsideration of the decision, but its petition was untimely filed and denied by the Commission. The agency then informed the complainant that it was accepting and issue for investigation concerning whether or not the complainant was led to believe that she could not file a formal complaint because of her inability to name the alleged discriminating official. The Commission instructed the agency to revise the issue for acceptance because it appeared that the agency was attempting to relitigate the timeliness issue. The agency then revised the issues for acceptance, but still insisted on investigating whether or not the complaint was timely. Ultimately, the Commission determined that there was no discrimination in the selection.

Although in Weaver the Commission found the complainant was not entitled to attorney fees on the selection issue, it rejected the agency's argument that complainant was not entitled to an award of any attorney fees:

However, this appeal involves a second issue which is the agency's attempt to restrain the appellant from filing the instant complaint. EEOC Regulation 29 CFR 1613.261, in part, requires that it is unlawful to restrain, coerce, or discriminate against complainants because of an involvement with a discrimination charge during any stage under 1613.213 et seq. or because an individual filed a charge for discrimination.

The record reflects that appellant attempted to file a complaint but her efforts were thwarted because of the information she received from the EEO Counselor. Appellant appealed the agency's decision to reject her complaint to this Commission. The effect of the Commission's finding was that the agency had restrained the appellant from filing an EEO complaint. The Commission specifically ordered the agency to accept appellant's EEO complaint. This order became the final decision of the Commission on February 5, 1990, when the agency filed an untimely RTR.

The agency argues that the Commission's decision in Michael A. Grossman v. Anthony M. Frank, Postmaster General, United States Postal Service, Western Region, EEOC Request No. 05880472 (September 27, 1988) supports its position that appellant is not a prevailing party and therefore, is not entitled to an award of attorney's fees and costs. In EEOC Request No. 05880472, the Commission stated that "attorney's fees are to be awarded only if a complainant prevails on the merits of his allegations; prevailing on a procedural issue, such as that before us in this decision, does not trigger an entitlement to attorney's fees. See EEOC Regulation 29 CFR 1613.271(c)." The instant matter is factually distinguishable from EEOC Request No. 05880472.

EEOC Request No. 05880472 involved the agency's acceptance of the Commission's authority and accepting designated issues for further processing in accordance with regulation. Had the agency, in the present matter, accepted the Commission's authority on February, 5 1990, and processed the merits of appellant's complaint, appellant, as in Grossman, would not be entitled to an award of attorney's fees and costs.

However, the agency compelled appellant to continue in her efforts to have the merits of her complaint investigated after the final decision of this Commission. The agency's actions constituted interference and restraint and where in contravention of the mandate of 29 CFR 1613.261. Between the time period of February 5, 1990 and October 25, 1990, (when the Commission determined that the agency had complied with the order in EEOC Appeal No. 01892422) appellant's counsel and this Commission continued to solicit the agency to comply with our decision. The agency's delay constituted restraint and interference with appellant's right to pursue an EEO complaint. Further evidence of such interference by the agency is its decision to include the timeliness issue as a part of appellant's complaint accepted for investigation. Having initially prevailed on the issue of restraint and interference, and having to further litigate the issue of the timeliness of her complaint beyond February 5, 1990, we find that appellant is entitled to an award of attorney's fees and costs for services rendered between the time period of February 5, 1990, and October 25, 1990. It is further noted that the agency shall also consider compensating the attorney for time spent prosecuting the present appeal.

A procedural issue in a complaint may be converted into a substantive issue when it rises to the level of interference with the complainant's right to pursue a complaint.

Even though a complainant does not prevail on an underlying claim of discrimination, he will be entitled to an award of attorney fees where he prevails on a claim that the agency improperly denied his claim for official time to prepare for the EEO hearing. Brown v. Postmaster General, 04940004 (1995). Of course, the complainant cannot recover for all attorney fees in his case; only that portion related to the successful appeal of the official time issue.

The Commission's reversal of an agency's improper dismissal of a complaint does not, by itself, render the complainant a prevailing party. In Garrity v. Government Printing Office, 01966741 (1998), the Commission found that the agency's improper determination that the complaint failed to state a claim did not constitute "restraint or interference" with the EEO process so as to trigger an award of fees. Clarifying its earlier decision in Weaver, the Commission in Garrity found:

On appeal appellant requests attorney's fees and cites Weaver v. Department of Defense, EEOC Appeal No. 01913114 (Dec. 5, 1991), in support of her request. In order to obtain attorney's fees appellant must be a prevailing party. Dailey v. Smithsonian Institution, EEOC Request No. 05950225 (July 29, 1996). Appellant is not yet a prevailing party because she has not yet achieved some of the benefit sought in bringing the action. See id. (citations omitted). In Weaver the Commission awarded attorney's fees after finding that the agency restrained and interfered with an appellant's right to pursue an EEO complaint. Weaver, EEOC Appeal No. 01913114. The Commission makes no such finding in the instant matter. The issuance of the incorrect August 12, 1996 decision does not constitute restraint or interference with appellant's right to pursue an EEO complaint.
