1. Genetic Information Generally

The very mention of genetic information conjures up images of DNA testing and lurid scenes from CSI and various spin-offs. However because mere knowledge of a medical condition that is genetic in nature can have an impact in the employment arena, the statutory definition of genetic information extends to:

The term "genetic information" means, with respect to any individual, information about--

(i) such individual's genetic tests,

(ii) the genetic tests of family members of such individual, and

(iii) the manifestation of a disease or disorder in family members of such individual.

(B) Inclusion of genetic services and participation in genetic research--Such term includes, with respect to any individual, any request for, or receipt of, genetic services, or participation in clinical research which includes genetic services, by such individual or any family member of such individual.

(C) Exclusions--The term "genetic information" shall not include information about the sex or age of any individual.

42 USC 2000ff(4).

The definition of genetic information also extends to genetic information about a fetus or embryo of an employee or family member. GINA specifically provides:

Any reference in this chapter to genetic information concerning an individual or family member of an individual shall--

(1) with respect to such an individual or family member of an individual who is a pregnant woman, include genetic information of any fetus carried by such pregnant woman; and

(2) with respect to an individual or family member utilizing an assisted reproductive technology, include genetic information of any embryo legally held by the individual or family member.

42 USC 2000ff-8(b).

The Commissions regulations at 29 CFR 1635.3(c) largely restate the statutory definitions of genetic information, but do include a provision that information on race or ethnicity of an employee or family member that is not derived from a genetic test is not genetic information. Id., 1635.3(c)(v)(2).
