8. Presumption of Harm

There is no presumption that an individual who is the victim of discrimination has suffered emotional distress. The complainant must prove the nature, duration and severity of any emotional harm. In Brown v. Postmaster General, 0720070022 (2007), the Commission, in reducing an award of $75,000 in damages to $15,000, explained:

We find that the $75,000.00 damage award is not supported by the substantial evidence in the record and is excessive in comparison to cases where a complainant suffered similar harm. Complainant has the burden of providing objective evidence to support her claim for damages, yet she has not met that burden here. The AJ awarded her $40,000.00 for her emotional distress, even though the AJ pointed out that she had not presented medical documentation on the issue nor given any significant testimony that she had suffered the harm. See AJ Decision at 86-87. The record indicates that complainant took anti-depressant medication, but her own physician did not testify meaningfully as to the stress or depression that may have been caused by her work situation. See Hearing Tr. at 148-163. Instead of basing his damages decision on objective evidence, the AJ concluded that complainant deserves $40,000.00 because the discrimination occurred at the worst possible moment for complainant--at the time of her surgery and recovery. The AJ further speculates that as there is a correlation between a person's emotional well-being and her ability to recover, complainant's recovery lasted at least three months longer than anticipated because of the work-related stress she endured. See AJ Decision at 87-88. This is, however, a presumption on the AJ's part. Although we are certainly sympathetic to complainant's plight, the law requires that we not presume emotional distress from the simple fact that complainant was the victim of discrimination. The existence, nature and severity of emotional harm must be proven. See EEOC Enforcement Guidance on Compensatory and Punitive Damages Available under Section 102 of the Civil Rights Act of 1991, Notice No. 915.002, Ch. II(A)(2) (July 14, 1992).

With regard to the AJ's $35,000.00 award for the harm to complainant's reputation, we find that it too is not supported by the evidence. Complainant argued that as a result of the discrimination she was not selected to three positions with the agency. She further argues that as she still had at least twenty-three years of service left in her federal career, she is entitled to twenty-three years of pay at the supervisory level. In this latter regard, we agree with the AJ that it would be speculation to try to forecast how complainant's future career would have played out absent the discrimination. The AJ also correctly points out that complainant's position at the time of the discrimination was temporary, not to exceed two years, so she cannot presume the position would have been extended permanently. See AJ Decision at 82-83. Although stating that over time complainant would be able to overcome any stigma by proving herself on the job and rising up the ranks, the AJ appears to base his damages award on the fact that the management officials implicated by complainant's complaint are likely to give negative comments about complainant's work, which, in turn, would impede complainant from regaining a supervisory position in the foreseeable future. See id. at 85. This conclusion, however, is no legal or factual basis for such a high damages award.

After analyzing the evidence and upon consideration of damage awards reached in comparable cases, we find that complainant is entitled to an award of non-pecuniary damages in the amount of $15,000.00. This amount takes into account the severity of the harm and the length of time complainant suffered the harm. See Roppuld v. Small Business Admin., EEOC Appeal No. 07A20141 (Aug. 6, 2003) (awarding $10,000 where complainant was discriminatorily removed from a supervisory position and he experienced depression, weight loss, mental anguish, and loss of professional reputation); Wood v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 01A15274 (Jan. 6, 2003) (awarding $15,000 where complainant experienced, among other things, loss of job opportunities, stress and depression as a result of the discrimination, but where complainant did not submit medical evidence to support a link to physical conditions); Hvizdak v. United States Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 07A20112 (June 30, 2003) (awarding $15,000 where complainant suffered depression, financial stress and loss of reputation, but where complainant did not submit medical documentation).

Although there is no presumption of harm, once harm has been established "[t]he more inherently degrading or humiliating the defendant's action is, the more reasonable it is to infer that a person would suffer humiliation or distress from that action." Taylor v. Postmaster General, 0720090036 (2010).
