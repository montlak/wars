8. Use of "Private" Documents

A somewhat common, though problematic, practice of supervisors is to keep "private" notes on employees who are perceived to present disciplinary or performance problems. The supervisor jots down notes on the employee's alleged misconduct or performance and keeps the notes separate from any agency files. The notes then are used to refresh the recollection of the supervisor for performance appraisals and disciplinary actions.

Complicating this practice is the Privacy Act of 1974, 5 USC 552a. The Act, among other things, requires that individuals have access to agency records that pertain to them, and further requires that agencies have procedures for correcting and amending erroneous records kept by agencies. In order to be subject to the requirements of the Act, a record must be identifiable with the individual and must be maintained within an agency system of records.

Technically, the "private" notes of supervisors do not meet the requisites of the Privacy Act. However, in Chapman v. National Aeronautics and Space Administration, 682 F.2d 526 (5th Cir. 1982), the court found that "private" notes must be treated consistent with the letter and spirit of the Privacy Act when used to make negative determinations about employees. The court essentially was concerned that the employees are given no opportunity to review or rebut the notes and are then "ambushed" when a disciplinary action or a poor performance appraisal is proposed.

In Alvarez v. Department of Air Force, 01850782 (1986), private notes kept by a supervisor on an employee's alleged poor performance were used at the hearing to refresh the supervisor's memory and then entered into evidence. The complainant argued that he previously was unaware of the notes and, relying upon Chapman, contended that he had been "ambushed." In rejecting the argument, the Commission embraced the rationale of Chapman but found the employee had been made aware of the performance deficiencies as they occurred and had not been "misled or lulled into a false sense of security." Id. The Commission noted that in Chapman that the employee remained unaware of the notes for some two and a half years and was never advised of any performance deficiencies.

An agency may not avoid the production of relevant documents by placing them under the control of the RMO as an "individual" and then asserting it has no control over the documents. Riddle v. USPS, 01860645 (1987).
