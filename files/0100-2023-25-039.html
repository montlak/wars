2. Recency of Education

Basing employment decisions on the recency of formal education can be discriminatory when the agency considers those with more recent education to be more highly motivated. In Eldridge v. Defense Logistics Agency, 01883635 (1989), the agency selected a younger candidate for promotion on the basis that his education was more recent and he was, therefore, more highly motivated to pursue further education. The Commission found this to be indicative of discrimination when the selectee was only 24 years old and the complainant had more than twice as many college credits as the selectee. The complainant, who was over 60 years of age, earned her last college credits in 1964. "[W]e find that the 24 year old's more recent education is due to her age. If anything, the fact that appellant received more than twice the number of college credits in the early 1960s, when she was in her late thirties, indicates that she was more motivated than the 24 year old to improve herself through formal education." Id.

Conversely, in Misra v. Office of Personnel Management, 01951753 (1998), the Commission found age discrimination where the complainant recently received a master's degree but was rejected for a fellowship program because he was not "early in his career." The complainant, who was 49 years old, was eliminated from consideration by a three-member screening panel. One of the panelists indicated that the complainant should only be granted a fellowship if there was space in the program because he was not early in his career--a requirement for the fellowship. A second recommended that the complainant not be interviewed and a third gave the complainant an unfavorable rating noting he was "just on the edge with his age as a negative factor...." Id. Under the circumstances, the Commission had little difficulty upholding the recommended decision of the administrative judge granting summary judgment to the complainant.

Regardless of the recency of the education, it can be indicia of age discrimination when the selecting official awards extra points for education beyond high school but can give no logical explanation for doing so. See Curnutte v. Secretary of Navy, 01982813 (1999). Whether it is considered an indicator of pretext will be assessed in light of the totality of the circumstances. For example, in Curnutte, where the Commission considered the education requirement to be pretextual, there also was no logical explanation for giving both the complainant and the selectee the same number of points for experience where the complainant clearly had more relevant experience.

In Gennetten v. Secretary of Navy, 01973098 (1999), applying the disparate impact theory, the Commission found that a policy giving an advantage to recent engineering graduates did constitute age discrimination. See section on "Disparate Impact," below.

However, in Ezell v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 01A55090 (2005), the Commission found that an agency policy giving an edge in entry-level technical and professional positions to candidates with a recent degree was not age discrimination. In Ezell, the Commission found:

Here, we find that the agency articulated legitimate, nondiscriminatory reasons for its actions. The record shows that the agency's college recruitment policy allows the agency to fill certain "entry-level technical/professional positions" with candidates who have recently received a college degree. The policy states that current agency employees, who have received a college degree within the previous four years, will receive first consideration for the relevant vacancies. With respect to the position at issue, the record reflects that the selectee received a masters degree within the prescribed time, but that complainant did not have a college degree. The agency states that because complainant did not meet the eligibility requirement of having a college degree, she was not considered for the position. With respect to complainant's contentions on appeal, we note that the agency's college recruitment policy specifically states that only entry-level positions are eligible to be filled through the program, and complainant has not provided any evidence to show that this program is used as a proxy for unlawful age discrimination. As such, we find that complainant has not shown that the agency's articulated reasons for its actions are pretextual. Accordingly, we affirm the agency's final decision finding no discrimination.

See also Cato v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 01A55479 (2006).

By contrast, in Seago v. Secretary of Agriculture, 07A60030 (2006), the Commission found that the use of academic transcripts in filling attorney positions was age discrimination where it determined that the transcripts and entry level salaries were used as a means of discouraging older applicants. The Commission, in Seago, explained:

The AJ noted that Rl claimed that the reason the last 8 or 9 attorneys hired by the agency were young, was because of the impact of the GS-11 starting salary. The AJ found that Rl's testimony regarding his belief that such a salary would be acceptable to attorneys recently graduating from law school, but not attractive to attorneys with experience already making better salaries with law firms, indicative of age discrimination. The AJ found Rl's testimony indicated that the agency was looking for people just out of law school as opposed to experienced attorneys and that the agency used law school transcripts, which recent graduates would be more inclined to include, but experienced attorneys would not, as a means to screen out older candidates in favor of younger ones. The AJ noted that Rl's recommendations for selection, in addition to the selectee, included two additional female candidates, also less than 40 years old. The AJ found the agency's reasons for not selecting complainant were unsupported by the record. Specifically, the evidence did not indicate that the agency's stated academic achievement criteria was uniformly applied to the applicants and accordingly, an inference arises that discrimination (on the bases of age and/or sex) was the real reason that complainant was not granted an interview and ultimately, not selected for the position.

The Commission also noted that, in the past, the agency had hired attorneys at a level higher than GS-11 when their skills, knowledge and experience warranted a higher entry level.

In Spirer v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01200805519 (2011), the Commission found its administrative judge erred in granting summary judgment on an age discrimination claim where the agency filled positions from an Honor intern program which, an agency official admitted, was designed to bring in "young people." The Commission, in Spirer, found that there were several indications that the agency's actions may constitute age discrimination:

First, we find that the AJ erred when he concluded that there was no genuine issue of material fact with respect to the Agency reasons for not selecting Complainant for any of the 24 positions. The record contains the affidavit that the Agency preferred to hire from its Honors Program, which was an intern program. The record shows that all of the positions were filled by younger candidates or persons who were not within Complainant's protected group. We also find that the statement (that the Agency wanted younger candidates), if true, could serve as evidence of discriminatory motive and age bias. We remind the Agency that the laws prohibit any adverse treatment that is based on age, race or sex discrimination.

The Commission has found selections that are the product of the scholar and intern program can be evidence of discrimination. Kaprive v. National Security Agency, EEOC No. 01972374 (1999). Like in Kaprive, the selecting official here admitted that the Agency prefers to promote young people out of the scholarship and internship positions. Consequently, we find that the record presents a genuine dispute of material fact as to why Complainant was not selected and whether his age was an impermissible factor in the decision not to interview or select him.

The record included evidence regarding the credibility of the Agency's legitimate. nondiscrimination reasons sufficient to defeat summary judgment. There are facts in dispute given the evidence that one of the persons selected did not actually have the experience and qualifications relied on by the Agency and evidence that Complainant was more qualified (in the sense of having significantly more experience than the selectees).

There is an issue as to why the Agency canceled the vacancy announcements on those certificates on which only Complainant's name appeared. The record shows that Complainant was certified by personnel as the best qualified and yet was not interviewed. The record contains statements from the selecting officials that Complainant presented himself well and often was the only candidate certified on the certificates of eligibility. The record shows that management officials made determinations as to which candidates were to be interviewed and modified the area of consideration or cancelled the positions after reviewing the lists.

We find that the AJ failed to draw all inferences from the record in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment, namely Complainant. We note the statement of the selecting official that Complainant was an excellent candidate. Yet, the Agency selected two young women with minimal experience and canceled announcements where Complainant's name was the only name on the Certificate of Eligible Candidates.

In summary, there are simply too many unresolved issues which require an assessment as to the credibility of the various management officials. Therefore, after a careful review of the record, we find that judgment as a matter of law for the Agency should not have been granted.
