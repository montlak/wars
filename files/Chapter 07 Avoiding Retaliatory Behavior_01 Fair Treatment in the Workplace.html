FAIR TREATMENT IN THE WORKPLACE

If you make reasoned decisions and address conflicts fairly, you are much less likely to be the target of complaints and dissatisfaction among employees. Confidence in your ability to make the right decisions and stop inappropriate employee conduct can support harmony and a feeling of shared mission.

Both merit system principles and the laws prohibiting employment discrimination are focused on equal opportunity. Equal opportunity doesn't mean that certain individuals are guaranteed jobs or special breaks. It means that all qualified people, from all types of backgrounds, must be given a fair opportunity to compete for employment and chances for improved employment-related circumstances.

In addition, President Biden has prioritized making sure that federal employment practices go beyond providing equal employment opportunity to ensuring equitable outcomes. So, as a manager, you should be aware of the current administration's push for increased diversity, inclusion, equity, and accessibility in federal employment, as laid out in Executive Order 14035, which presents agencies, OPM, and OMB with a plan, framework, and deadlines. Managers likely won't have specific requirements until OPM and OMB generate a governmentwide plan and agencies develop an agency-specific plan. However, you may have to respond to information requests relating to the status of current DEIA initiatives in agency recruitment, hiring, promotion, retention, professional development, performance evaluations, pay and compensation policies, reasonable accommodations access, and training policies and practices.

Even while these policies go through changes or amendments, you can create an atmosphere of equal opportunity by paying careful attention to some fundamentals:

  • • Treat employees respectfully.
  • • Recognize the value of employees' work.
  • • Make fair decisions.
  • • Ensure accessibility for individuals with disabilities.
  • • Understand that people have different circumstances and may need targeted support or resources to perform, advance, or develop successfully.
  • • Provide resources and support to achieve an equal outcome for employees from underserved communities.
  • • Address harassment or other employee conflict promptly.
  • • Address other claims of discrimination.

Conversely, a manager who plays favorites, or ignores bullying, harassment, and other conflicts, and acts in an unpredictable manner can create the type of resentment that leads to distrust and hostility. These unsettled feelings can lead to complaints, but perhaps more importantly, can keep employees from carrying out the agency's mission.

Respectfulness

Your employees look to you to provide an example of appropriate workplace conduct. Treating employees of all backgrounds with courtesy and respect encourages employees to act in a like manner. It's also consistent with merit system principles.

Managing a respectful workplace means that all of your communications are civil, different opinions are valued, and no individual or group is singled out with offensive jokes or treatment. In such a work environment, employees are much less inclined to assume misunderstandings or adverse actions are the product of discrimination.

In contrast, a manager who chooses certain employees for poor treatment signals that some individuals are not valued. Not only does this invite EEO complaints against the manager, it may also embolden coworkers who are inclined to become bullies. The net effect is a segment of the workforce that is effectively prevented from contributing freely to the agency's mission. These employees also may be inclined to believe that any action by the manager is unfair or even discriminatory.

Example: At one agency, supervisors resentful about an employee's requests for religious accommodation subjected her to harassment based on race, religion, and prior EEO activity. They demoted her, unfairly criticized her, and denied her choice work assignments. They scheduled her to work on her Sabbath and religious holidays. Management resentment turned into hostility, which was adopted by the employee's coworkers.

In the end, the agency had to pay her $200,000 for the severe emotional and physical distress she suffered as a result of her hostile work environment.

Although a manager who targets all employees equally for poor treatment may not be the subject of a successful EEO complaint, the manager is nonetheless likely to be accused of discrimination as employees seek a means of combating the mistreatment. A workplace that is ruled by the fear and uncertainty caused by a disrespectful manager generally is not in a position to flourish.

Recognition

Good managers recognize high-performing employees and provide appropriate incentives for others to excel. Recognition and awards are a positive aspect of your duties as a manager. However, you must be fair in your distribution of praise and other incentives, keeping track of excellence at all types of jobs, not just those at the highest levels.

Keep in mind that recognition can include advancement. Providing advancement opportunities based on superior performance carries out the spirit of merit system principles as long as all qualified employees are allowed an equal chance to compete for those advancements.

Fairness

Making sure your employment-related decisions are fair means giving all who are eligible the opportunity to compete for positions, promotions, opportunities, and awards.

Recruitment. Merit principles require you to make every effort to fairly recruit individuals who represent all segments of society. This includes providing adequate notice that gives those individuals who are interested an equal opportunity to compete for positions. Giving broad notice about available positions also ensures that you are not perceived as acting with a discriminatory motive by encouraging only certain people to apply for positions.

Selection. Base your decisions on the advertised criteria. Merit system principles require you to consider relative ability, knowledge, and skills in an open competition. If you select someone based on factors that are not a stated requirement for the job, you could be accused of acting with a discriminatory motive. Even if you had no desire to discriminate, your inability to explain why you deviated from the selection criteria could get you in EEO trouble. Your decisions should also be documented. Documentation will be helpful if your decision is challenged, and it can help you make sure you made the right decision.

Job opportunities. Selection decisions are not the only time questions of fairness might be raised. You probably are in a position to consider employee requests, make assignments, and grant job opportunities on a daily basis. Be mindful of EEO requirements and merit principles. Make fair and equitable decisions. This means ensuring all eligible individuals are allowed to go on details or serve as acting managers on a rotating basis, making job assignments evenhandedly, and providing proper training for all employees, with attention paid to the type and location of training. Here are a couple examples of discrimination from decisions by the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission:

  • • A manager subjected an employee to disability discrimination when he denied her a promotional reassignment because of her vision impairment. The manager reassigned and trained a coworker in the same position who did not have a disability.
  • • A manager subjected a female employee to sex discrimination when she was denied the overtime pay that came with dock work. The manager felt female employees could not handle the intensity of dock work.

Provide an explanation. When possible, explain your decisions. Briefly outlining the reasons for a decision may help a disappointed employee understand why it was fair. For example, in the case of a nonselection, outline the selectee's superior qualifications. This could help you show that your decision was made based on merit. In the case of a training decision, show how you fairly distribute training opportunities. The lack of an explanation may lead a disappointed employee to draw conclusions that attach a discriminatory or unfair motive to your decision.

Listen to employees. Being fair also means listening to your employees' ideas and concerns without bias. You may have certain "go-to" employees, and you may have others who are not working up to speed or tend to complain too much. Your inclinations may have some support in fact.

However, if you allow yourself to cast employees only in specific roles, you will end up acting in an unfair manner. This could mean favoring ideas based on people rather than content or dismissing valid concerns raised by specific segments of your workforce. Either way, you could be violating merit system principles that require fair and equitable treatment of all employees, and find yourself named in an EEO complaint. Favoritism alone isn't discrimination, but if you can't provide a legitimate explanation for your actions, you could end up in EEO trouble anyway.

Responsiveness

Responding confidently and appropriately to problematic workplace interactions gives you a good chance of resolving situations that could lead to escalating conflict or needlessly develop into EEO claims.

When it comes to bullying or harassment, you are in a unique position to make a difference. Responding appropriately to harassment that you observe or that is reported to you can head off EEO hostile work environment claims and help maintain a peaceful workplace that is focused on work, not drama. Conversely, failing to address harassment can encourage further misconduct and perhaps even compel the victims to file EEO complaints as their only reasonable course of action.

The response must be effective. While the proper reaction depends on the circumstances, your key to success is responding with the action necessary to stop the bullying or harassment at issue. You might respond to simple teasing that seems unwelcome with a relatively mild admonishment, while a racial epithet might require disciplining the perpetrator and separating the employees involved. Remember though, any reaction should not punish the victim.

Contact your HR or EEO office if you an unsure about how to respond to a situation that potentially involves harassment.

You also should respond to claims of unfair treatment that don't involve harassment. If an employee tells you that a certain activity or decision seems discriminatory or unfair, try discussing the situation in a calm and friendly manner. Your willingness to respond to your employee's concerns with an explanation may be all the employee needs to understand your actions. However, you must be careful to respond in a manner that does not intimidate the employee or otherwise discourage EEO concerns from being raised.

Warning: Once an employee seeks EEO counseling, or expresses intent to do so, you need to be very careful not to do anything that could be perceived as discouraging EEO activity. Discouraging an employee from pursuing an EEO claim violates discrimination laws.

USERRA

The Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act prohibits discrimination on the basis of uniformed service with respect to initial employment, reemployment, retention in employment, promotion, or any other benefit of employment. It also clarifies employees' rights with regard to leave that is taken in connection with active-duty service.

Who is covered by USERRA? The following individuals are covered by USERRA:

  • • Members of the uniformed services (the Army, Air Force, Navy, Marine Corps, Coast Guard, any of the National Guard or Reserve components for these services, the commissioned corps of the Public Health Service, and any other category of persons designated as such by the president in time of war or national emergency).
  • • Individuals who have applied to be a member of the uniformed services.
  • • Individuals who perform, have performed, or have an obligation to perform service in a uniformed service.

Individuals with service in the uniformed services are not covered if they are separated from this service with a dishonorable or bad conduct discharge or with a discharge under other than honorable conditions.

Congress has not extended the coverage of USERRA to U.S. Postal Service employees, but the agency has an analogous internal rule. In addition, the Defense Intelligence Agency is not subject to a USERRA claim.

Protections. Based on their uniformed service status, employees who fit into a category of individuals covered by USERRA may not be denied:

  • • Initial employment.
  • • Reemployment.
  • • Retention in employment.
  • • Promotion.
  • • Or any benefit of employment.

Servicemembers are entitled to military leave to cover their absence to perform active or reserve military duties, or to participate in required military training, without charge to pay or annual leave. You can learn more about military leave in the Examining Leave Requests chapter.

Reemployment rights. Under USERRA, if the employee's period of uniformed service was less than 91 days, the agency must reemploy them in the position in which the person would have been employed if their employment had not been interrupted by the uniformed service. The employee must be qualified to perform the duties of that position. If the employee is not qualified to perform those duties after reasonable efforts by the agency to qualify the person, the agency may reemploy them in the position they were employed on the date of the commencement of the uniformed service.

An agency must reemploy employees returning from uniformed service of more than 90 days either in the position they would have held had they not left for uniformed service, or a position of like seniority, status, and pay for which they are qualified. The reemployment position must provide the seniority, status, and rate of pay an employee would ordinarily have attained in that position given their job history, including prospects for future earnings and advancement. The seniority rights, status, and rate of pay must be determined as though the employee had been continuously employed during the period of uniformed service. This is referred to as the "escalator principle."

If an employee is not qualified to perform the duties of such a position after reasonable efforts by the agency to qualify them, they should be reemployed in the position in which they were employed on the date of the commencement of the service in the uniformed services, or a position of like seniority, status, and pay, the duties of which they are qualified to perform.

If an employee's position is abolished while they are performing uniformed service, the agency must reassign them to another position of like status and pay.

Promotions. If an opportunity for promotion, or eligibility for promotion that the employee missed during uniformed service is based on a skills test or examination, the agency should give the employee a reasonable amount of time to adjust to the employment position and then give a skills test or examination.

In determining a reasonable amount of time to permit an employee to adjust to reemployment before scheduling a makeup test or examination, the agency should take into account a variety of factors, including but not limited to:

  • • The length of time the returning employee was absent from work.
  • • The level of difficulty of the test itself.
  • • The typical time necessary to prepare or study for the test.
  • • The duties and responsibilities of the reemployment position and the promotional position.
  • • The nature and responsibilities of the servicemember while serving in the uniformed service.

If the employee is successful on the makeup exam and, based on the results of that exam, there is a reasonable certainty the employee would have been promoted, or made eligible for promotion, during the time that they served in the uniformed service, then the promotion or eligibility for promotion must be made effective as of the date it would have occurred had employment not been interrupted by uniformed service.

Reductions in force. An employee performing active military duty is protected from a RIF and cannot be discharged from employment for one year after her service ends (or for six months if she is a reservist called to active duty under 10 USC Sec. 12304 for more than 30 days but less than 181 days, or was ordered to an initial period of active duty for training of not less than 12 consecutive weeks). However, the employee can still be removed for poor performance or conduct or for suitability reasons.

CREW Act. The Civilian Reservist Emergency Workforce Act of 2021 -- referred to as the CREW Act -- grants USERRA employment and reemployment protections to Federal Emergency Management Agency reservists when they are deployed to disasters and emergencies, or training for such, on behalf of FEMA. It allows these reservists to claim such rights under USERRA even if they do not provide notice of their absence from work due to deployment.

Essentially, under the CREW Act, any FEMA reservist deployed to a Stafford Act event -- major disasters or emergencies declared by the president -- cannot lose their outside employment due to their work with FEMA. They are also able to take time to train for these types of deployments. Additionally, they are protected from employment discrimination for being a reservist.

FEMA reservists should be reinstated with the seniority, status, and rate of pay they would have obtained had they remained continuously employed by their civilian employer.

USERRA claims. Federal employees who believe their USERRA rights have been violated by an agency or the Office of Personnel Management may file a complaint with the Department of Labor or file an appeal directly with the Merit Systems Protection Board. There is no statute of limitations on USERRA claims, which means that they may be filed at any time, no matter how much time has passed since the alleged violation occurred.

Employees making a USERRA claim must first show that their military service was a substantial or motivating factor in the employment action being challenged. If they do this, the agency then has the opportunity to provide evidence showing it would have taken the action anyway, for a valid reason.

Although USPS employees are not covered by USERRA, the MSPB can adjudicate Postal Service employees' improper military leave charge claims based on the agency's analogous internal rule.

Discrimination based on military status

USERRA prohibits discrimination on the basis of uniformed service with respect to initial employment, reemployment, retention in employment, promotion, or any other benefit of employment. It also clarifies the employee's rights with regard to leave that is taken in connection with active-duty service.

Federal employees who believe their USERRA rights have been violated by an agency or OPM may file a complaint with the Department of Labor or file an appeal directly with the Merit Systems Protection Board. Employees making a USERRA claim must first show that their military service was a substantial or motivating factor in the employment action being challenged. If they do this, the agency then has the opportunity to provide evidence showing it would have taken the action anyway, for a valid reason.

Factors that may demonstrate USERRA discrimination. Discriminatory motivation under USERRA can be inferred from a variety of factors, including:

  • • Proximity in time between the employee's military activity and the adverse employment action.
  • • Inconsistencies between the reason provided by the agency and the agency's other actions.
  • • An agency's expressed hostility towards members protected by USERRA together with knowledge of the employee's military activity.
  • • Disparate treatment of certain employees compared to other employees with similar work records or offenses.

Scope of USERRA protection. Be aware that USERRA only prevents discrimination on account of service in the military. It does not prohibit an agency from considering events that occur during an employee's service. If an agency's decision to terminate an employee was based not on their military service but rather on conduct that occurred while they were on a pass during their active military duty status, military service is not a motivating factor in their termination.

The employee must show they were treated more harshly than non-veterans. The fact they were not treated better than non-veterans would not show discrimination.

Case examples. To get a better idea of how claims of discrimination based on military status may arise, it's helpful to review some examples from decisions of the MSPB:

  • • An agency violated an employee's USERRA rights when it terminated his employment four days before he completed his probationary period. Non-veterans in his attorney advisor position were subject to a two-year probation while the employee was subject to a one-year probation due to his preference-eligible veteran status. The employee's supervisors held him to a higher standard of meeting his "fair share," an evaluation element that is applied to attorney advisors only after their first year of employment.
  • • An applicant failed to show that his military service was a motivating factor in his nonselection because the three agency witnesses involved in the decision testified that his military service played no role in their decision to not select him for a second interview, and each candidate selected for a second interview was also a preference-eligible veteran.
  • • An employee who applied for a new position failed to show that his nonselection was motivated by discrimination because there was no evidence that the reviewer was aware he had applied late based on his disabled veteran status.

Mistakes to avoid

Do not:

  • • Make selection decisions without considering all qualified applicants. You may have a favorable employee in mind for a certain job or promotion, but if you fail to consider all those eligible, you will violate merit systems principles and possibly EEO laws.
  • • Ignore teasing, bullying, or other harassment. Mild teasing is a normal part of most workplaces. However, you need to be aware of employee interactions so you can step in quickly and stop any conduct that becomes inappropriate or unwelcome.
  • • Take an adverse employment action without documented support. If an employee files a complaint, you may be called upon to justify your decision. A lack of documentation could prevent you from showing your decision was made on a fair and impartial basis.
  • • Try to discourage EEO activity. Whether it's protesting perceived discrimination, filing an EEO complaint, or acting as a witness, employee involvement in the EEO process is protected by law. If you try to intimidate employees or otherwise dissuade them from engaging in EEO activity, you will be inviting additional EEO complaints.

Ask FMG

Q. What does equal employment opportunity mean for managers?

A. Equal employment opportunity means eliminating barriers to ensure that all employees and applicants are considered for the employment of their choice and have the chance to perform to their maximum potential. It means promoting a work environment that is free of unlawful discrimination and harassment, is accessible to individuals with disabilities, ensures fair and equitable treatment for all employees, values workforce diversity, and fosters mutual respect to achieve the agency mission.

Q. How do I comply with the merit system principle of providing incentives and recognition for excellence if everyone has to be treated the same?

A: The merit system principles and EEO laws require equality of opportunity, not outcome. In addition, Executive Order 14035 requires agencies to prioritize diversity, equity, inclusion, and accessibility. To meet these mandates, you must remove barriers to afford qualified employees an equal chance to compete for jobs, promotions, awards, and perks. Although you don't have to provide every qualified employee the same thing at the same time, you do need to be consistent, fair, open, and impartial. For example, equity might mean recognizing that individuals from underserved communities may not have had the same opportunities and proactively ensuring that all employees are provided the resources they need to reach a more equal outcome regardless of prior circumstances.

Q. Why do federal employees file so many EEO complaints?

A. EEO complaint statistics vary widely with regard to agencies and even specific offices within agencies. However, as a general matter, EEO complaints are fairly easy to file and can seem like a reasonable option to employees who feel they are being treated unfairly, whether because of discrimination or not.

Q. What should I do if I make every effort to be fair, but an employee still names me in an EEO complaint?

A. You should simply cooperate with the EEO process. It can be alarming to be named in an EEO complaint, but supervisors who make well-supported decisions and make sure their workplace is free of harassment should have little to fear. You can contact your HR office if you have any concerns or need advice. You should not try to dissuade an employee from filing a complaint or penalize any employee for involvement in the EEO process. Interfering with the EEO process is against the law.

Q. Why should I have to explain my employment decisions?

A. In many cases, you are not obligated to explain your decisions. Nevertheless, you may choose to explain your decisions in order to help employees understand why they were made instead of drawing their own conclusions. These conclusions could include an assumption that you were acting with a discriminatory or otherwise unfair motive.

Q. Can't I let employees blow off some steam with a little teasing?

A. As a general matter, a little friendly teasing is OK. However, you need to make sure it doesn't go too far. If the teasing is related to race, color, sex, national origin, religion, age, disability, genetic information, or a person's EEO activity, you could be inviting an EEO claim of hostile work environment. Even if the teasing doesn't involve a basis protected by law, allowing unwelcome teasing to persist can lead to escalation and problems down the road.

Q. My style of supervision tends to be a little intense, and I like to hassle some of my employees a bit to encourage them to perform well. Is there anything wrong with that?

A. There are many successful management styles, but if you rely on bullying or other forms of disrespect to achieve your performance goals, you may be violating merit system principles and your employees might feel pressed to file EEO complaints to address perceived unfairness or harassment.
