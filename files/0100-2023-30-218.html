VII. ENFORCEMENT OF REMEDIES

When the EEOC Office of Federal Operations orders remedial or corrective action in conjunction with its final decision, it will also order the affected agency to report on the implementation of that action within a specified amount of time. The action must be taken within 60 days of the date the decision becomes final. See 29 CFR 1614.502(c). The relief ordered by the EEOC is mandatory and binding on the agency and may be subject to judicial enforcement. See 29 CFR 1614.502(a).

Specifically, 29 CFR 1614.502 provides:

(a) Relief ordered in a final Commission decision is mandatory and binding on the agency except as provided in this section. Failure to implement ordered relief shall be subject to judicial enforcement as specified in 1614.503(g).

(b) Notwithstanding paragraph (a) of this section, when the agency requests reconsideration and the case involves removal, separation, or suspension continuing beyond the date of the request for reconsideration, and when the decision orders retroactive restoration, the agency shall comply with the decision to the extent of the temporary or conditional restoration of the employee to duty status in the position specified by the Commission, pending the outcome of the agency request for reconsideration.

(1) Service under the temporary or conditional restoration provisions of this paragraph (b) shall be credited toward the completion of a probationary or trial period, eligibility for a within-grade increase, or the completion of the service requirement for career tenure, if the Commission upholds its decision after reconsideration.

(2) When the agency requests reconsideration, it may delay the payment of any amounts ordered to be paid to the complainant until after the request for reconsideration is resolved. If the agency delays payment of any amount pending the outcome of the request to reconsider and the resolution of the request requires the agency to make the payment, then the agency shall pay interest from the date of the oriGINAl appellate decision until payment is made.

(3) The agency shall notify the Commission and the employee in writing at the same time it requests reconsideration that the relief it provides is temporary or conditional and, if applicable, that it will delay the payment of any amounts owed but will pay interest as specified in paragraph (b)(2) of this section. Failure of the agency to provide notification will result in the dismissal of the agency's request.

(c) When no request for reconsideration is filed or when a request for reconsideration is denied, the agency shall provide the relief ordered and there is no further right to delay implementation of the ordered relief. The relief shall be provided in full not later than 60 days after receipt of the final decision unless otherwise ordered in the decision.

The Commission has held that it has the authority to monitor agency compliance through its orders for relief. In In re USPS, 05850333 (1987), the Commission found that it had not exceeded its authority when it ordered the agency to submit a plan for the regular evaluation and monitoring of the complainant's work assignments to assure that they were commensurate with her position. The Commission rejected the argument that this interfered with the agency's authority to direct its own work assignments. "The Commission, by its Order, has taken no steps to circumvent the agency's authority to establish the means and methods by which it discharges its duties, but has merely undertaken to assure, pursuant to Title VII, that its authority is exercised in a nondiscriminatory manner." Id.

The agency is required to provide a copy of the compliance report to the complainant. However, as the Commission explained in Kessel v. Secretary of Commerce, 0420110014 (2013), the agency may redact information about disciplinary actions taken against specific individuals pursuant to the Privacy Act:

The Agency asserts that it does not have to send Petitioner an un-redacted Report of Compliance because the Report referenced information regarding employees that was protected under the Privacy Act. Petitioner asserts that the Report of Compliance was too redacted and she was unable to determine if compliance was achieved.

Agencies have a duty to protect the privacy of its employees' records under the Privacy Act of 1974, as amended (5 U.S.C. 552a). The Privacy Act requires Agencies to keep confidential any and all information contained in an employee's Official Personnel File and any other files compiled in relationship to the employee covered under the Privacy Act. Id. The Privacy Act safeguards the public from unwarranted collection, maintenance, use and dissemination of personal information contained in agency records. One way the Act "safeguards the public from unwarranted...dissemination of personal information" is by limiting the circumstances under which an agency may disclose, without consent, "records" about an individual maintained within a "system of records." 5 U.S.C. § 552a(b). Doe v. U.S. Dept. of Treasury, 706 F. Supp.2d 1, 6 (D.D.C. 2009). Therefore, in accordance with the Privacy Act, information about another employee contained in a record may not be appropriate for disclosure to a complainant in a Report of Compliance. This includes specific information concerning disciplinary action taken against another employee or management official. As a result, redaction of information protected under the Privacy Act is appropriate in the version of an EEO Report of Compliance that is sent to a complainant.

We note that in the instant case, the Report of Compliance that the Agency submitted to the Commission's Compliance Officer noted that it would not provide Petitioner with the Report, "redacted or not." In the context of an EEO Report of Compliance, the Privacy Act only prohibits the Agency from disclosing specific disciplinary information about another employee. The Agency is still required to disclose non-disciplinary information to Petitioner in the Report of Compliance.

When the appellant files a request for reconsideration, the agency is not obligated to implement any corrective action previously ordered, but it is not prevented from doing so on its own. Jones v. Secretary of Army, 01902315, 01902575 (1990).

Decisions of the Commission on requests to reopen are final and there is no right of further appeal or further request for reconsideration.
