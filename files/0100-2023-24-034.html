12. Agency Knew of Disability and Allowed Employee to Work

When an agency allows an individual to work with a disability for a substantial period of time this can undercut the agency's assertion that the complainant is at risk. This is particularly true where the condition that finally provokes the agency into insisting on medical documentation is not the condition which the agency contends puts the complainant at risk. For example, in Hilton v. Postmaster General, 01971239 (2000), the complainant refused to provide medical information to the agency on her seizure condition and the agency allowed her to continue working for several months. It was not until after the complainant attempted to return to work from approved maternity leave that the agency insisted upon obtaining medical information about the seizure condition. In finding the agency's reasons for seeking the information were pretextual, the Commission in Hilton noted:

First, we note that the agency's failure to take any disciplinary action against complainant during the fifteen-month period between her initial refusal to provide medical information related to her seizure disorder and her return from approved maternity leave without providing such information renders the agency's sudden decision that the failure to provide the information warranted removal inherently suspect. The agency has failed to explain why it was so concerned about the alleged safety risks her condition posed in the workplace yet permitted complainant to continue her duties during this lengthy time period with minimal restrictions. Instead of insisting that complainant produce the requested medical information at the time the alleged concern over safety arose, the agency waited until complainant requested extended sick leave for maternity purposes and used complainant's request for such leave as an opportunity to request medical information regarding a totally unrelated impairment.

Next, the Commission notes that there is no information in the record that suggests that employees without disabilities have ever been required by the agency to produce medical documentation unrelated to the reason for their extended absence as a precondition to returning to duty from such an absence. In fact, the agency's own ELRM provisions indicate that employees on extended sick leave can be required to submit to a fitness for duty examination when the reason for the employee's sick leave is of such nature as to raise "justifiable doubt" concerning the employee's ability to "satisfactorily and safely" perform his or her duties. There is no ELRM provision in the record that permits the agency to require an employee, such as complainant, who is absent on sick leave for one reason to present medical documentation regarding an unrelated medical condition in order to return to duty. Thus, the Commission finds that the agency's own policies undermine its rationale for complainant's removal.

Conversely, when an employee's demonstrated performance establishes that he is a risk because of his disability, he is not a qualified individual with a disability. As the Commission explained in Seymour v. Postmaster General, 0120093459 (2012):

In this case, the evidence of record supports a finding that Agency management had a reasonable belief, based on objective evidence, that Complainant was not qualified for his position as a casual clerk because he posed a direct threat to himself due to his medical condition. See Enforcement Guidance, Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) (March 1, 1999) (Guidance), at question 38 ("if an employee with a disability, with or without reasonable accommodation, cannot perform the essential functions of the position, or poses a direct threat in the absence of medication, treatment, or an assistive device, then s/he is unqualified).

The evidence shows that Complainant had had several seizures at work and was found on the floor by coworkers. On December 4, 2007, Complainant had another seizure and fell near a flat sorter machine, splitting his brow, and had to be taken to the hospital by ambulance where he received six stitches. He was released to return to work with the restriction of not working on the flat-sorter for a six month period. Before the end of the six-month period, on May 24, 2008, Complainant had another seizure at work, was found in an aisle, and paramedics were again called. It is undisputed that Complainant has little or no advance notice of an impending seizure, and a seizure normally causes him to fall to the floor. As a casual clerk, Complainant was required to constantly work around machinery, including the flat sorter machine. Based on his history of having unpredicted seizures at work and falling to the floor and injuring himself, the Agency has supported its conclusion that he was not qualified for his position because he posed a danger to himself. Moreover, as a casual, non-permanent employee, there is no evidence that there was another position to which he could have been transferred where he could have worked more safely.
