1. Electing Both Forums

In Ramsey, supra, the EEOC dealt with the further question of what happens when the employee first initiates the EEO process, then the negotiated grievance procedure, and receives partial relief as a result of her grievance. In other words, the issue is what happens when the employee receives relief in a forum that has not been properly elected and what effect, if any, does that have on the complaint in the proper forum.

The Commission answered the question by ruling that the agency could not dismiss the EEO complaint as containing issues identical to the grievance and that it must process the complaint. First, the Commission noted that appellant had not received all of the relief sought in the EEO complaint because the agency had not admitted that any discrimination occurred and only had promised prospective action instead of retroactive relief. Therefore, the complaint was not moot. Second, the Commission found that the agency had been remiss in not dismissing either the EEO complaint or the grievance immediately after submission. The EEOC reasoned that the employee might not have settled her grievance and withdrawn it, except for her belief that the remaining issues would be addressed via her EEO complaint. Because, through the agency's inaction, the employee was reasonably under this impression, the Commission found it would be unfair to dismiss the complaint. Id.

However, in Younger v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A11389 (2002), the Commission held that the resolution of the grievance was dispositive even where the complainant had first elected the EEO process and the agency was in error in processing the grievance. Although the Younger case does not overrule Ramsey, it probably represents the current view of the EEOC.

In some cases, both forums can be elected when an agency action raises two separate claims and only one of them relates to discrimination. In Perez v. Department of Treasury, 01862358 (1987), the Commission reinstated a complaint which alleged that the keeping of private records on an employee's performance was discriminatory where similar notes were not kept on other employees. The agency dismissed the case as identical to a grievance over a poor performance evaluation based on the notes. The Commission found that two different claims were presented by the complaints and 7121(d) did not bar the complainant from seeking additional relief through the EEO process. Similarly, in Alexander v. Department of Treasury, 01863042 (1987), the employee was allowed to file an EEO complaint over a notice of opportunity to improve performance and a grievance over his removal for poor performance. The Commission noted that the EEO complaint concerned the employee's performance prior to the notice and the removal concerned his performance subsequent to the notice.

However, when an employee requests a transfer and lists two reasons for the request, the actions are not unrelated and he cannot file both a grievance and an EEO complaint. In Begelmahker v. Social Security Administration, 01A53244 (2005), the employee requested both a hardship transfer and a transfer as reasonable accommodation in the same letter. The Commission found that the requests were not unrelated and he could not file a grievance over denial of the hardship transfer and an EEO complaint alleging denial of reasonable accommodation.

Although Ramsey is likely still good case law, there is some question about the continued validity of Perez and Alexander. To the extent that the Commission's regulations at 29 CFR 1614.107(a)(5) no longer allow complaints over proposed personnel actions or preliminary steps to personnel actions, Alexander would be inapplicable. Similarly, to the extent that the 1999 amendments to the Commission's regulations are designed to prevent the fragmentation of claims, the Commission might presently take a different view of the situation presented in Perez. In all likelihood, the outcome would hinge, as it did in Ramsey, upon whether the agency's actions intentionally or inadvertently induced the complainant to forego pursuit of a claim.
