2. Completeness of Record

An agency has an obligation to compile a complete record during the course of its investigation. In part, this obligation is imposed because complainants have the right to request a final agency decision, without a hearing, based on the existing record of investigation. The rule on completeness of investigations must be read in conjunction with the standards for investigations discussed earlier in this chapter under the heading "Investigative Procedures." "Complete" in this context is the same as "appropriate." An appropriate investigation is defined as one that contains sufficient information that a reasonable factfinder can reach a conclusion on the issue of discrimination. In each of the cases above, information critical to the determination was omitted from the report of investigation.

A complete investigative record is one that is sufficiently developed to create an "appropriate factual record." Ollie N. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120180684 (2019). For example, the Commission found in Ollie N. that, together, affidavit testimony from supervisory officials, contemporaneous statements from the complainant and coworkers related to the claims, agency memoranda and records, the complainant's formal complaint, and rebuttal statements established a complete investigative record.

An agency may not fail to provide required information in an investigation and then claim that the record does not support the complainant's claims. For example, in Palmer v. Secretary of Defense, DLA, 01952878 (1997), the complainant alleged that the agency had a pattern and practice of not promoting blacks to the GS-11 level. The investigator requested information from the agency about the race and grade of employees. Rather than respond, the agency instructed the investigator to conclude the investigation without the information due to time constraints. Subsequently, the agency found there was insufficient evidence to support the claim. The Commission remanded the case, finding that "[t]he agency cannot now come before the Commission and credibly argue that the record fails to support appellant's allegations when it is responsible for the insufficiency." Id.

Likewise, when a complainant requests that relevant comparative evidence be included in the investigative record, the agency errs in issuing a final decision without that evidence. Liberato v. Postmaster General, 01954258 (1997). The rule similarly applies to medical evidence of a disability when disability discrimination is claimed, as well as evidence of prior EEO activity when reprisal is claimed. Id.
