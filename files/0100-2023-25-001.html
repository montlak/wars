CHAPTER 16

AGE DISCRIMINATION IN EMPLOYMENT ACT

The Age Discrimination in Employment Act is codified at 29 USC 621 et seq. Just as with Title VII, the federal government was originally exempt from the provisions of the ADEA. The ADEA provides protection to persons who are age 40 or over. 29 USC 631(b). Amended in 1974 to include the federal government, 633a(a) of the Act provides:

All personnel actions affecting employees or applicants for employment who are at least 40 years of age (except personnel actions with regard to aliens employed outside of the United States) in military departments as defined in section 102 of Title 5, in executive agencies as defined in section 105 of Title 5 (including employees and applicants for employment who are paid from nonappropriated funds), in the United States Postal Service and the Postal Rate Commission, in those units in the government of the District of Columbia having positions in the competitive service, and in those units of the legislative and judicial branches of the Federal Government having positions in the competitive service, and in the Library of Congress shall be made free from any discrimination based on age.

Compare the text of 633a(a) to that of 29 USC 623 applicable to the private sector:

(a) Employer practices. It shall be unlawful for an employer--

(1) to fail or refuse to hire or to discharge any individual or otherwise discriminate against any individual with respect to his compensation, terms, conditions, or privileges of employment, because of such individual's age;

(2) to limit, segregate, or classify his employees in any way which would deprive or tend to deprive any individual of employment opportunities or otherwise adversely affect his status as an employee, because of such individual's age; or

(3) to reduce the wage rate of any employee in order to comply with this chapter.

Note that the standard in 633a(a) differs from 623. Title 29 USC 623 dictates that employers may not make employment related decisions "because of" an individual's age. Whereas, 633a(a) reads that employment related decisions "shall be made free from any discrimination based on age." The distinction is important as the Supreme Court noted in Babb v. Wilkie, Secretary of Veterans Affairs, No. 18-882 (Supreme Court April 6, 2020). The Babb Court held that the federal-sector provision of the ADEA does not require proof that age discrimination was the "but-for cause" of a personnel action. To determine whether a personnel action violates 29 USC 633a(a) the proper inquiry, according to the Court, is whether it was "untainted by any consideration of age." Babb rejected the agency's argument that the government should not be held to a higher standard in this context:

It is not anomalous to hold the Federal Government to a stricter standard than private employers or state and local governments. See § 623(a). When Congress expanded the ADEA's scope beyond private employers, it added state and local governments to the definition of employers in the private-sector provision. But it "deliberately prescribed a distinct statutory scheme applicable only to the federal sector," Lehman v. Nakshian, 453 U.S. 156, 166, eschewing the private sector provision language. That Congress would want to hold the Federal Government to a higher standard is not unusual. See, e.g., 5 U.S.C. § 2301(b)(2). Regardless, where the statute's words are unambiguous, the judicial inquiry is complete. Pp. 11-13. (d) But-for causation is nevertheless important in determining the appropriate remedy. Plaintiffs cannot obtain compensatory damages or other forms of relief related to the end result of an employment decision without showing that age discrimination was a but-for cause of the employment outcome. This conclusion is supported by basic principles long employed by this Court, see, e.g., Steel Co. v. Citizens for Better Environment, 523 U.S. 83, 103, and traditional principles of tort and remedies law. Remedies must be tailored to the injury. Plaintiffs who show that age was a but-for cause of differential treatment in an employment decision, but not a but-for cause of the decision itself, can still seek injunctive or other forward-looking relief.

The ADEA, as it applies to federal employees, has several quirks. Section 633a is the only provision of the Act that applies to federal employees, with the exception of 631(b) which sets the basic age parameters of coverage. Section 633a makes no attempt to define what constitutes age discrimination in federal employment. This has created confusion for both the Commission and the courts. The confusion has led to contradictory decisions, particularly involving the correct statute of limitations to apply. Unfortunately, in passing amendments of the ADEA incorporated into the 1991 Civil Rights Act, Congress passed up the chance to correct the statute of limitations problem. The Commission sought to remedy some of this confusion in adopting 29 CFR Part 1614.

In passing the original ADEA, the Congress made the following statements of findings and purpose at 29 USC 621:

(a) The Congress hereby finds and declares that--

(1) in the face of rising productivity and affluence, older workers find themselves disadvantaged in their efforts to retain employment, and especially to regain employment when displaced from jobs;

(2) the setting of arbitrary age limits regardless of potential for job performance has become a common practice, and certain otherwise desirable practices may work to the disadvantage of older persons;

(3) the incidence of unemployment, especially long-term unemployment with resultant deterioration of skill, morale, and employer acceptability is, relative to younger ages, high among older workers; their numbers are great and growing; and their employment problems grave;

...

(b) It is therefore the purpose of this Act to promote employment of older persons based on their ability rather than age; to prohibit arbitrary age discrimination in employment; to help employers and workers find ways of meeting problems arising from the impact of age on employment.

The ADEA originally was passed as Pub. L. No. 90-202 on December 15, 1967. Section 633a, prohibiting age discrimination in federal employment, was passed as an amendment, Pub. L. No. 93-259, in April of 1974 and became effective the following month. Enforcement responsibility was vested in the Civil Service Commission, but transferred to the EEOC under Reorganization Plan No. 1 of 1978.

Particularly as a result of the Supreme Court's decision in Gross v. FBL Financial Services, Inc., 129 S. Ct. 2343 (2009), the burden of proof on a complainant in an ADEA case is higher than under a Title VII case and the mixed motive analysis discussed in Chapter 10 does not apply. The Gross case is discussed fully in the section below on "Age as Determinative Factor." However, the Commission has rejected Gross as applying in federal sector cases. See, e.g. Goblirsch-Erickson v. Postmaster General, 0120110390 (2011); Alotta v. Secretary of Transportation, 0120093865 (2011).

I. STRUCTURE OF THE ACT

In many ways, the ADEA resembles Title VII. Section 633a(a) of the Act reads substantially the same as its counterpart, 42 USC 2000e-16, in that Act. The Act applies to all personnel actions and to the terms and conditions of employment as well.

Unlike the other anti-discrimination statutes, an employee who believes he or she was discriminated against on the basis of age is not required to exhaust administrative remedies before an agency or the Commission. The employee may pursue matters of age discrimination directly in federal court. However, the employee must give the EEOC at least 30 days advance notice of intent to file a complaint in U.S. district court. The notice must be given within 180 days of the alleged discrimination. 29 USC 633a(c) and (d). But a note of caution is in order here. Some courts have taken the position that because the administrative pursuit of an ADEA case is optional, such pursuit does not toll the statute of limitations for filing a civil action. It is possible that in some jurisdictions the time limit for filing a civil action can expire while the complaint is still in the administrative process.

The ADEA does not permit for recovery of attorney fees in the administrative process or in actions in U.S. district court. However, attorney fees may be recoverable under the Equal Access to Justice Act, 28 USC 2412(b). See Boehms v. TVA, 139 F.3d 452 (5th Cir. 1998); Nowd v. Rubin, 76 F.3d 25 (1st Cir. 1996). The EAJA applies only to civil actions and does not permit the recovery of fees in the administrative process.
