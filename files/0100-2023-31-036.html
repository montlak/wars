11. Hybrid Agreements

In Charlie K. v. Secretary of the Air Force, 2022000599 (2022), the complainant and his attorney entered into what was coined a "hybrid" agreement, as follows:

From March 23, 2018, until March 27, 2019, Complainant and his representative entered into a fee agreement of $450 for the initial file review and $250 per hour or 40% of the amount recovered by Complainant, whichever was greater. From March 27, 2019, and thereafter, an agreement was entered into which provided for $100 per hour, regardless of outcome, in addition to 20% of what Complainant recovered.

The AJ found discrimination and awarded $57,725 in attorney fees, finding that $250 per hour was a proper fee based on the agreement. The agency rejected the award, arguing that the appropriate per hour rate was $100. The Commission in Charlie K. disagreed:

From March 23, 2018, until March 27, 2019, Complainant and his representative entered into a fee agreement of $450 for the initial file review and $250 per hour or 40% of the amount recovered by Complainant, whichever was greater. From March 27, 2019, and thereafter, an agreement was entered into which provided for $100 per hour, regardless of outcome, in addition to 20% of what Complainant recovered.

In this case, the AJ found, based upon evidence submitted by Complainant's representative, that $250 per hour was a proper. The AJ considered the agreement a contingency case and relied upon precedent to award attorney's fees accordingly. The AJ found that Complainant's representative provided sufficient evidence to demonstrate total number of hours spent by Complainant's attorney, from the time of the filing of the formal complaint through the hearing, was 230.9 hours. The Agency has not, in its appeal or its briefs, disputed the 230.9 hours expended. Nor have they made any assertion that $250 per hour is an excessive hourly rate for Complainant's attorney's experience and location. As such, these issues shall not be addressed in this decision.

Rather, the Agency argues that $250 is inappropriate because Complainant and his attorney entered into a hybrid agreement for compensation. The Agency argues that $100 per hour is a reasonable hourly rate, because that was the agreed upon rate between Complainant and his representative. The Agency points to case law that indicates that the Agency should not be required to pay that which is beyond the contracted rate.

The Commission cannot agree with the Agency's legal argument in this case. As an initial matter, the contracted rate from March 23, 2018, until March 27, 2019, was $450 for the file review and $250 thereafter. As such, from March 23, 2018, through March 26, 2019, the AJ's assessed $250 was equivalent to the contracted rate and, as the Agency did not dispute either the reasonableness of that rate, nor the number of hours worked by Complainant's attorney, even by the Agency's own reasoning and analysis, $250 per hour is the appropriate for that time.

We now turn to whether the AJ made the proper legal determination of $250 from March 27, 2019, until the hearing was proper. We find that it was. While the Agency argues that $250 is in excess of the contracted rate, the Commission notes that $100 was not the entirety of the contract. Rather, $100 was the amount to be paid in addition to 20% of any amount awarded to Complainant. As such, the amount paid to Complainant's attorney was contingent upon the amount awarded to Complainant, making this contract analogous to a contingency case. Contingency cases are not within the purview of the Commission. Ileana H. v. Dep't of Justice, EEOC App. No. 0720170016 (Sept. 28, 2018).

As such, it is appropriate to provide remedies that are available to the Commission, namely the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by a reasonable hourly rate. Leatherman v. Dep't of the Navy, EEOC App. No. 01A22716 (June 12, 2003) (citing Hensley v. Eckerhart, 461 U.S. 424, 434 (1983)); 29 C.F.R. § 1614.501(e)(2)(ii)(B). As the Agency has not argued, and has submitted no evidence in support of, finding that $250 per hour is not a reasonable rate, and has not debated the number of hours worked by Complainant's attorney, the Commission finds that the AJ did not err as a matter of law in his assigning $250 per hour throughout the case, from review of file to hearing, and there is sufficient evidence to support attorneys' fees in the amount of $57,725.
