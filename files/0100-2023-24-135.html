9. Reassignment to Previous Position

When an agency assigns an employee with a disability to a new position and the employee cannot perform in that position because of her disability, the agency has an obligation to place the employee in her former position. See Frankunas v. Department of Treasury, 01860534 (1987); see also Philbrook and Ignacio, supra. In such cases, the employee is entitled to back pay for any period not worked. There is no merit to the argument that the complainant is not entitled to back pay because she was not willing, ready, and able to work in her new position.

In Mitchell v. Secretary of Defense, 03920186 (1993), the Commission found the MSPB erred in upholding the removal of a disabled employee based on poor performance where the agency assigned the employee to a new position after learning of his disability. The employee was on an extended leave of absence for treatment of a benign brain tumor. During his absence, the agency assigned another employee to his position as a computer analyst and, upon the complainant's return, they assigned him to a systems analyst position. Because of the effects of the tumor, the complainant had difficulty learning new tasks and soon was given an unacceptable performance rating and removed.

In evaluating whether the complainant was a qualified person with a disability, the Commission in Mitchell noted that the requirement that complainant be able to perform the essential functions of the "position in question," with or without reasonable accommodation, referred not only to the complainant's current position, but any position he could have held as the result of reassignment:

[F]urther, petitioner's physician explained that because hearing, vision, and speech all are used in learning, petitioner's loss of hearing, vision problems, and impaired speech would make the learning process more difficult for him. The physician also testified that in most types of neurological disease or injury, it is simpler for an individual to do tasks with which he is acquainted rather than to learn new tasks. The essence of his testimony was that petitioner would find it less stressful, and likely would perform better, if placed in a position which drew upon knowledge and skills that he already possessed rather than placing him in a position which required him to learn new knowledge and skills. In his uncontradicted testimony, petitioner asserted that he was unfamiliar with the EPAS, and that his knowledge of equipment related to that system was outdated, i.e., he needed to learn how to use the system. Record evidence establishes that petitioner experienced difficulty in doing so, e.g., co-workers complained that he was unable to remember the simplest of instructions, even after he had received them for the sixth or seventh time. Based on the foregoing, we find that petitioner's impairments caused the performance deficiencies which led to his removal.

Finally, having established that he is an individual with a disability and that his disability caused the charged performance deficiencies, petitioner must show that he was a "qualified" individual with a disability within the meaning of 29 CFR 1613.702(f). That section defines "qualified" individual with a disability as meaning one who, with or without reasonable accommodation, can perform the essential functions of the position in question. Id. The Commission previously has determined that the phrase "position in question" is not limited to the position occupied or held by the employee, but also includes positions which the employee could have held as the result of reassignment. See Ignacio v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Petition No. 03840005 (September 4, 1984), aff'd, Special Panel No. 1 (February 27, 1986).

The issue, then, is whether the agency met its obligation of considering reassignment as a reasonable accommodation. Although it is clear that petitioner repeatedly requested reassignment to a position involving data administration, the record is incomplete as to the agency's actions in this regard. The agency took the position that it could not reassign petitioner to his prior position because it had filled the position and, in any event, the position had changed substantially during his absence so that it no longer was the same position. The agency further asserted that there were no other vacant positions to which it could reassign petitioner when he returned to work in February 1990. At the hearing, petitioner admitted that the agency had informed him that it could not place him in his prior position because it had filled that position. Petitioner indicated, however, that he was puzzled at the agency's response because "there (were) other data administration positions." Later in his testimony, petitioner stated that he did not necessarily want to replace the incumbent in his former position and noted that there was a requirement for at least two individuals to be in data administration, suggesting that a second unencumbered position might have been available. Finally, we note that the incumbent in petitioner's position left the agency in November 1991, not long after petitioner's removal on October 18, 1991. There is no indication as to when the agency first learned that the incumbent intended to vacate that position. Additionally, there is conflicting evidence in the record as to whether the changes which occurred in petitioner's former position during his absence were such that it no longer was the same position that petitioner had occupied prior to his absence.

Because the evidence of record is insufficient with regard to the reassignment issue, it is the decision of the Commission to refer the matter to the Board for the convening of a supplemental hearing....

In Walker v. Secretary of Air Force, 03960083 (1996), the Commission reversed an MSPB finding of no discrimination, and held that the agency had a duty to accommodate the complainant by trying to place her in her previous job, in which she had not experienced problems with her asthma.
