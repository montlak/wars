2. Subpoenas

Although an administrative judge has the authority to order the attendance of any witness who is an employee of a federal agency, the judge has no subpoena authority over private citizens. This includes private citizens who are former agency employees. See Benner v. Postmaster General, 01881710 (1988).

This inability to subpoena private citizens, particularly those who are former federal employees and may have testimony that is critical to a complainant or an agency, is a serious deficiency in the administrative processing of EEO complaints. Since the burden of proof is on the complainant, more often than not the inability to compel the attendance of a witness works to the advantage of the agency.

But in Crummer v. Secretary of Commerce, 01913933 (1992), the Commission upheld the imposition of an adverse inference by the administrative judge when a witness requested by the complainant retired prior to the hearing. The agency did not notify the complainant's counsel of the retirement of the witness until two days before the hearing. The agency also refused to provide the address or telephone number of the witness to the complainant's attorney or the administrative judge. Nor did the agency make any effort to contact the witness and advise him of the hearing date. The adverse inference drawn in Crummer shows that although administrative judges do not have subpoena authority, they do have some tools at their disposal to compensate in the appropriate circumstances.

In the wake of Crummer it remains unclear as to the circumstances in which the Commission will impose an adverse inference when a retired witness is not produced by the agency. Obviously, the Commission will not impose an adverse inference in every instance where a retired witness does not appear. On the other hand, it is likely the Commission did not mean to suggest that all of the elements present in Crummer are required for an adverse inference to be appropriate. It is clear, however, that the Commission will examine the particular circumstances in every case to determine if an adverse inference is appropriate.

Because the Commission cannot compel testimony from former federal employees, complainants should ask in discovery about the agency's knowledge of witnesses who may be planning to retire or resign, or be separated from the agency prior to hearing. Complainants should also inquire about the agency's knowledge of witnesses with serious illnesses or other circumstances that may prevent their appearance at the hearing. Although, as discussed below under the heading "Depositions," deposition transcripts are not normally accepted in lieu of live testimony, they can be entered into the record when the witness is not available to testify.
