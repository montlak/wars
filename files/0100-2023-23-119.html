B. SCHEDULE A APPOINTMENTS

Reasonable accommodation takes many forms and there is no rote formula that the agency can apply to assure that it has met its burden in any particular case. In addition to particular position accommodations, the Rehabilitation Act requires agencies to engage in affirmative action programs for the hiring and advancement of persons with disabilities. One method of achieving such results is through what are known as Schedule A appointments. Such appointments are temporary, with the idea that the appointment will be converted to permanent status if the performance of the employee is satisfactory.

OPM sets out the basic parameters of Schedule A appointments of individuals with disabilities at 5 CFR 213.3102(u). The regulations were most recently amended in February 2013 to eliminate a previous requirement that Schedule A applicants provide a "certification of job readiness" and to replace a reference to "mental retardation" in the former regulation with "intellectual disability. Section (u) provides, in part:

(u) Appointment of persons with intellectual disabilities, severe physical disabilities, or psychiatric disabilities--

(1) Purpose. An agency may appoint, on a permanent, time-limited, or temporary basis, a person with an intellectual disability, a severe physical disability, or a psychiatric disability according to the provisions described below.

(2) Definition. "Intellectual disabilities" means only those disabilities that would have been encompassed by the term "mental retardation" in previous iterations of this regulation and the associated Executive order, Executive Order 12125, dated March 15, 1979.

(3) Proof of disability.

(i) An agency must require proof of an applicant's intellectual disability, severe physical disability, or psychiatric disability prior to making an appointment under this section.

(ii) An agency may accept, as proof of disability, appropriate documentation (e.g., records, statements, or other appropriate information) issued by a licensed medical professional (e.g., a physician or other medical professional duly certified by a State, the District of Columbia, or a U.S. territory, to practice medicine); a licensed vocational rehabilitation specialist (State or private); or any Federal agency, State agency, or an agency of the District of Columbia or a U.S. territory that issues or provides disability benefits.

The regulation further provides:

(4) Permanent or time-limited employment options. An agency may make permanent or time-limited appointments under this paragraph (u)(4) where an applicant supplies proof of disability as described in paragraph (u)(3) of this section and the agency determines that the individual is likely to succeed in the performance of the duties of the position for which he or she is applying. In determining whether the individual is likely to succeed in performing the duties of the position, the agency may rely upon the applicant's employment, educational, or other relevant experience, including but not limited to service under another type of appointment in the competitive or excepted services.

(5) Temporary employment options. An agency may make a temporary appointment when:

(i) The agency determines that it is necessary to observe the applicant on the job to determine whether the applicant is able or ready to perform the duties of the position. When an agency uses this option to determine an individual's job readiness, the hiring agency may convert the individual to a permanent appointment in the excepted service whenever the agency determines the individual is able to perform the duties of the position; or

(ii) The work is of a temporary nature.

(6) Noncompetitive conversion to the competitive service.

(i) An agency may noncompetitively convert to the competitive service an employee who has completed 2 years of satisfactory service under this authority in accordance with the provisions of Executive Order 12125, as amended by Executive Order 13124, and § 315.709 of this chapter, except as provided in paragraph (u)(6)(ii) of this section.

(ii) Time spent on a temporary appointment specified in paragraph (u)(5)(ii) of this section does not count towards the 2-year requirement.

The Commission has issued guidance on Schedule A appointments provides, which has not currently been amended to reflect the latest changes to OPM regulations:

The Schedule A special appointing authority provides a means by which agencies may prefer applicants with targeted disabilities. The regulations establishing this "excepted" appointing authority include provisions that allow agencies to hire people with severe physical disabilities, mental retardation, and psychiatric disabilities who are certified as likely to succeed in a particular job. Under Schedule A, the individual is appointed as an excepted service employee, and after two years of satisfactory service can then be converted to competitive status. In addition to enabling an agency to obtain a qualified employee and fulfill model employer goals, Schedule A allows agencies to hire qualified applicants with disabilities without posting a position, thereby reducing the time it may take to fill a vacancy.

...

Additional provisions under Schedule A and other authorities that may make it easier for agencies to hire individuals with disabilities include, but are not limited to the following:

student educational employment work-study programs, 5 C.F.R. §§ 213.3202(a) and (b)(10)(ii) (see OPM's information on "Hiring Authorities for Students with Disabilities" at http://www.opm.gov/disability/mngr_3-05.asp); and

veterans preference hiring, including hiring disabled veterans into a Department of Veterans Affairs training program, 5 C.F.R. § 315.604, and preferential hiring of veterans with disability ratings of 30% or more, 5 C.F.R. §§ 316.302(b)(4) and 316.402(b)(4) (see OPM's "VetGuide" at http://www.opm.gov/veterans/html/vetguide.asp).

Appointment under Schedule A can be used even if the applicant with a disability has initially applied by submitting a resumé in response to a vacancy announcement rather than contacting a Selective Placement Coordinator, or even where an announcement has not yet been posted.

Example 2: One of the applicants for an administrative assistant position is blind. He does not have the requisite number of years of experience listed in the job announcement. However, he has extensive volunteer experience performing comparable administrative tasks for a non-profit organization, and is otherwise qualified for the job. To be a model employer, the agency should tell this candidate that he is eligible for appointment under Schedule A. If he is interested in using this process, the agency should use the Schedule A authority to appoint him to the position, even though he does not meet the stated experience requirements for competitive hire. Note that under the revised Schedule A appointment authority, an individual who has submitted proof of disability but not a certificate of job readiness can nevertheless be appointed on a temporary basis with later conversion to the competitive service. The agency may also, at its discretion, accept service under another type of temporary appointment in the competitive or excepted service as proof of job readiness.

Example 3: An agency is hiring an analyst and lists "top 10% of college graduating class" among its selection criteria. An individual with epilepsy who took six years to complete college rather than the usual four applies for the position. Her overall grade point average was not high enough to rank her in the top ten percent of her graduating class. However, during her first two years of college, the applicant experienced uncontrolled seizures which interfered with her ability to attend classes and complete assignments, which caused her to withdraw from several courses before completion. Following a year off during which she had surgery and began using a new medication, she returned to college and had significantly fewer and less severe seizures. Her grades improved dramatically. During her last two years in college, she obtained A's and B's in all of her courses, even while carrying a higher than normal courseload. The agency may take the necessary steps to appoint her to the position pursuant to its Schedule A authority instead of rejecting her application for failure to meet the competitive hiring criteria.

Example 4: An agency intends to fill a vacant GS-15 office director position. Rather than posting the position, the agency may use Schedule A to hire a current employee who is deaf and is qualified for the job. While the employee would become probationary under Schedule A, he would be subject to conversion back to the competitive service after the requisite period of time under Schedule A.

In addition to the provisions that enable agencies to hire individuals with disabilities, Schedule A also includes a special appointing authority for the hiring of readers, interpreters and other personal assistants who some individuals with severe disabilities may need as a reasonable accommodation. See 5 C.F.R. § 213.3102(ll).

Questions and Answers: Promoting Employment, Sec. III, Q. 4.When the agency makes a Schedule A appointment, it has a continuing obligation to assist individuals with disabilities to demonstrate their qualifications for a permanent position. "The goal of affirmative action is not merely employment of a handicapped person but an appropriate placement for such an employee." Nolin v. Department of Army, 01830539 (1984). In that case, the EEOC found that the agency failed to meet its affirmative action obligations when it discouraged the employee from applying for a position on the second floor because the facility was not fully accessible to persons with disabilities. In Nolin, the agency gave no consideration to whether appellant could perform the same duties on the first floor, was slow in implementing even basic building accommodations, and rejected the appellant for seven past positions for which she had been rated highly qualified.

In Hunt v. Secretary of Army, 01851723 (1988), the Commission noted:

[T]he purpose behind the selective placement program is to effectively place a handicapped person in a suitable temporary position, and convert the position to a permanent slot, if the handicapped employee's performance is satisfactory. The Commission stresses that, under the selective placement system, the agency is affirmatively obligated to provide appellant a full opportunity to succeed in the appointed job. This opportunity includes appointment to an appropriate position, appropriate training, and instruction to agency officials on the handicap involved.

In the Hunt case, the Commission found that the agency breached that obligation where it required the employee to perform duties that were not in her position description, required her to operate a word processor instead of a typewriter with little or no training, and also required her to type military correspondence with no training. The agency also made no provision for the complainant to have access to her supervisor, who was located on an upper floor. However, the Commission rejected an argument that she was entitled to five years of back pay and instead found she was entitled to a new temporary appointment with the agency fulfilling its obligations.

The agency demonstrates a legitimate reason for its refusal to convert an employee with a disability to the status of a permanent employee and its approval of such a conversion for an employee without a disability where it shows that a hiring freeze was in effect during the time when the employee with the disability requested conversion, but not at the time the other employee sought conversion. Stacy v. Department of Health and Human Services, 01821693 (1983). Interestingly, the two requests for conversion took place about one month apart, with the hiring freeze being lifted just prior to the other employee's request. Without specifically mentioning it, the Commission apparently found no significance to the fact that the disabled employee's request was not reconsidered at that time.

The creation of temporary appointments was designed to give agencies the flexibility to allow employees with disabilities the opportunity to demonstrate satisfactory performance in the essential functions of the position prior to career probationary appointments. However, the use of temporary appointments does not justify a difference in rates of pay or benefits between employees with disabilities and other probationary employees. Turner v. USPS, 01820528 (1983).

Moreover, an agency may not limit its hiring of employees with disabilities to those positions involving temporary appointments. In Boskovich v. USPS, 01853316 (1987), the Commission rejected the agency's argument that such a program was, in effect, beneficent disparate treatment. Nor was the Commission willing to accept the agency's position that giving the employee a second probationary period was a "gratuitous gesture." Rather, the granting of a new probationary period "arose from the agency's duty to accommodate." The Commission, relying upon Shirley v. Devine, 670 F.2d 1188 (D.C. Cir. 1982), stated "categorical denial of equal status is fundamentally inconsistent with the plain meaning and purpose of Sec. 501, and the fact that a handicapped employee was hired under exception to the normal competitive appointment processes does not allow an agency to afford that employee lesser protection."
