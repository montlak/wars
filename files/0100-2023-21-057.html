24. Sexual Innuendo

Not all conduct is blatantly sexual but that does not preclude a finding that sex is sufficiently implicated to state a claim. For example, in Complainant v. Secretary of Army, 0120142868 (2015), the complainant alleged that she was required to wear a sign reading "ride 'em hard" at a holiday party and was teased about if afterward:

Complainant asserts that at a holiday party, during an event in which her participation was mandatory, she had to wear a sign that said "ride 'em hard." She states that a co-worker stated to the audience at the event that he was introducing a girl "who I know really likes to ride 'em hard." She asserts that Agency management was present at the activity but did not intervene. Complainant asserts that, after the activity, numerous co-workers continued making comments to her such as that Complainant would get promoted because she liked to "ride 'em hard." Finally, Complainant asserts photographs of her wearing the sign "ride 'em hard" were displayed on the Agency's intranet site. When viewing the incidents collectively, in a light most favorable to Complainant, we find that she has set forth an actionable claim of harassment.

Regarding the Agency's assertions that it took immediate and corrective action or that Complainant has not established that the incidents at issue were based on her sex, we find that these assertions again go to the merits of Complainant's complaint and not to the procedural issue of whether she has set forth an actionable claim.

Of course, when the conduct is blatantly sexual one incident may be sufficient to rise to the level of creating a hostile environment. For example, in Twanna C. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120160030 (2016), the complainant alleged that one coworker sent an email from her account to another coworker:

In the instant matter, Complainant alleges that CW-1 sent a sexually explicit email from her government email account to CW-2. The record contains a copy of the email CW-1 sent from Complainant's work email account. The subject of the email is "Don't Go Away." The email sets forth: "I am afraid it has been [too] late for me to actually express myself. But I would him for you to know that I want to hump you really bad. I would like for you to treat my body as your sex toy and abuse it in any way you please. Please tell me that you feel the same about me. If you do, we can get together before you leave so that I can abuse your body as the sex god you are. I LOVE YOU." Complainant's representative further asserts in a letter to the Agency's EEO Office dated July 6, 2015, that "[t]he contents of email has been communicated to a vast number of Border Patrol Agents who work with [Complainant]...[and] she has been placed in a different limelight before her co-workers...."

Upon reviewing the specific facts of this case and the specific nature of the email at issue, we find that Complainant's claim is sufficient to state an actionable claim of harassment. Regarding the Agency's assertion that it took prompt and corrective action with respect to CW-1, this assertion goes to the merits of Complainant's complaint and not to the procedural issue of whether she has stated an actionable claim.
