11. Email Filing

A formal complaint of discrimination may be filed by email. In Hata v. Secretary of Army, 01A24619 (2003), the Commission found that an email complaint was timely filed where the 15th day fell on a Saturday, thereby extending the deadline to Monday and the complainant filed the complaint by email on Sunday. In holding that a complaint could be filed by email, the Commission noted that the agency made no contention that it could not obtain the complainant's signature.

In Clemente M. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120152854 (2016), the Commission excused the late filing of a complaint where:

On appeal, Complainant, through counsel, concedes that he filed his formal complaint one day beyond the requisite fifteen-day period. However, he provides a reason for the delay and requests that his untimely filing be excused. Specifically, Complainant states that due to a transmission error, he had lost the initial draft complaint form that had been sent to him and that he was unable to receive and sign a new complaint form and transmit it by email to the Agency until the day after the formal complaint was due.

Given the specific circumstances of this case, we exercise our discretion in finding that Complainant has provided adequate justification for the equitable excusing of the one-day delay in filing the instant formal complaint. See 29 C.F.R. § 1614.604(c). In reaching this conclusion, we note that there was no prejudice to the Agency due to the late filing because of the closeness in time from the passage of the deadline and the Agency's immediate receipt of the filing.

As electronic means of communication become more the norm than regular mail, new issues related to when the complainant received the notice of final interview triggering the time limit for filing the formal complaint continue to arise, making it incumbent upon the agency to make instructions clear. One such case, Judson v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120172685 (2017), involved the agency's use of "Echo-Sign," an electronic program, as a way to track the complainant's receipt of notices and filings. In Judson, the Commission excused the complainant's late filing because the agency's instructions as to the filing deadline was unclear:

In response to the appeal, the Agency argues it sent the Notice by email on November 21, 2016. According to the Agency, the Notice stated: "you have the right to file a formal EEO complaint of discrimination within 15 calendar days of receipt of this notice," Relying upon an electronic tracking system, the Agency reasoned that Complainant "opened and viewed" the Notice on November 24, 2016, and therefore the formal complaint needed to be filed by December 9, 2016. In support of its determination, the Agency provides a copy of an "Echo Sign" tracking history for Complainant's Notice.

Complainant argues, that based upon information from the EEO Counselor, he understood the time limit to begin when the Notice itself was viewed. In support, Complainant provides an email dated November 21, 2016 from the EEO Counselor which clearly states:

You will receive two emails from our Administrative Team issuing you a [Notice]. One email will be the actual Complaint form (DHS Form 3090-1) which you must print and fill out legibly and completely. You will also receive an email with a password to view the document. Be aware that the moment you key in that password that the 15 day calendar day clock will start ticking... You will have ONLY 15 CALENDAR DAYS to fill out the document and return it back to the [EEO Office]" (italicized emphasis added).

He contends that the Notice, attached to the Agency email, was opened on December 6, 2016, and therefore, his December 21, 2016 filing was within the fifteen-day time limit. Complainant states that this later viewing is further supported by a December 1, 2016 email from the EEO office, reminding him he had a document (i.e. the Notice) waiting for him to access. If he had accessed the document on November 24, 2016, as the Agency claims, Complainant asserts he would not have received such a reminder.

Consequently, we find that the Agency itself contributed to the misunderstanding regarding the start of the filing time limit. The Commission has previously held that an agency may not dismiss a complaint based on a complainant's untimeliness, if that untimeliness is caused by the agency's action in misleading or misinforming the complainant. See Wilkinson v, U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Request No. 05950205 (Mar. 26, 1996). See also, Complainant v. Department of Human Services (TSA), EEOC Appeal No. 0120171555 (July 21, 2017) (Waiver of time limit appropriate when Agency found to have contributed to confusion by instructing that complaint must be filed within fifteen calendar days of "first accessing" this notice.) Therefore, due to the circumstances in this case, we find it appropriate to waive the time limit for tiling a formal complaint. See 29 C.F.R. § 1614.604(c).

See also Devona V. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120171555 (2017) (waiving late filing where use of Echo-Sign and agency instructions were confusing as to what action triggered the 15-day clock for timely filing); Theresia B. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120171839 (2017) (same regarding complainant's understanding that the clock for filing a formal complaint was triggered by opening the email rather than when the email was received).
