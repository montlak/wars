6. Uncertainty of Law

In some instances, the very nature of the case may influence whether tolling of the limitations period is warranted. In areas where the law is rapidly changing, a layperson may be excused from not realizing the possibility that she has been discriminated against by the agency. See Smothers v. USPS, 01842475 (1985). In Smothers, the complainant was denied employment due to her pregnancy in 1982 and filed a complaint in 1984 after reading of a woman who won a similar case. The EEOC found it reasonable that the complainant, as a layperson, had no reason to know of the potential discrimination because the law with respect to pregnant workers was still in the early stages of development.

In Burns v. Postmaster General, 01931061 (1993), the Commission found that the complainant did not have a reasonable suspicion that she was discriminated against at the time of her termination, even though she knew she was an alcoholic at that time. The Commission accepted as reasonable the complainant's assertion that she did not know alcoholism was a disabling condition under the Rehabilitation Act at the time of her removal.

Generally, uncertainty of law is a narrow exception to the requirement that a complaint be timely filed. If the complainant is merely unclear on what the law is, the exception does not apply. It only applies in cases where the law itself is unclear. For that reason, the Burns case is puzzling. Although the law surrounding reasonable accommodation of alcoholic employees has been in a state of flux, it has long been established that alcoholics are protected by the Rehabilitation Act. See, e.g. Delifus v. Secretary of Treasury, 01A40599 (2005) (rejecting complainant's contention that she was not reasonably aware of discrimination because she had no knowledge of Equal Pay Act). In Klein v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, 01A53675 (2005), the Commission waived the time limit for counselor contact for a claim alleging that OPM's denial of a health benefit was discriminatory. The Commission held that the complainant, who was proceeding pro se, did not have a reasonable suspicion that the denial of the claim may have constituted discrimination until nearly a year later when she consulted an attorney.

With the Commission's position on claims based on sexual orientation and gender identify in a state of evolution over the last few years, see Chapter 12, it is not surprising that it would be somewhat forgiving of complainants bringing such claims. In Complainant v. Secretary of Army, 0120150820 (2015), the complainant did timely initiate contact with an EEO counselor after his termination, but because he was alleging sexual orientation discrimination, was advised to contact the Office of Special Counsel. Even though four years passed before he again contacted a counselor, the Commission found the contact timely "[g]iven the unique circumstances of this case."

Uncertainty as to the state of the law, however, is not the same as ignorance of the law. As the Commission expressed in Lara G. v. Attorney General, 0120162585 (2016), the reasonable suspicion standard applies to a complainant's unawareness of relevant facts, not ignorance of the law. Lara G. involved an employee of a Department of Justice contractor, who served as an agency senior program advisor in Bosnia. The complainant argued that her untimely counselor contact should be waived because she was unaware of the law regarding joint employer liability. The Commission in Lara G. rejected that argument, reasoning:

Here, Complainant contacted the EEO Counselor on March 11, 2016, regarding allegedly discriminatory events that occurred in August and November 2013, well beyond the forty-five day time limitation. Complainant argues, below and on appeal, that as a contractor, she did not believe she had the ability to file an EEO complaint against the Agency. Complainant argued that she only developed "reasonable suspicion" of discrimination in February 2016, after she was informed by her former Engility colleagues that she might also be deemed a joint employee of Engility and the Agency for the purposes of filing an EEO complaint.

Notably, Complainant cites no authority to support the contention that her unawareness of her legal rights required the Agency to waive or extend the EEO counseling deadline. She, similarly, cites no instance in which the Commission applied the reasonable suspicion standard in a circumstance involving an asserted ignorance of substantive law rather than an unawareness of relevant facts.

This appeal is governed by our decision in Wood v. Secretary of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05901196 (April 10, 1991), which involved a complainant's untimely filing of a constructive discharge claim. The complainant urged waiver of the filing deadline on the ground that he was unaware he had a possible cause of action. Noting the distinction between ignorance of the Law and ignorance of relevant facts, the Commission did not excuse the untimely filing in Wood, reasoning that complainant had a duty to undertake reasonable efforts to ascertain his legal rights. Here, Complainant's former Engility colleagues timely filed EEO complaints, and there is no reason to believe that Complainant could not have also filed an EEO complaint in 2013, when she was not selected for one position and terminated from her other position.
