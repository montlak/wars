V. TERMS AND CONDITIONS OF EMPLOYMENT

The application of Title VII is extremely broad, covering not only employment itself but the "terms, conditions or privileges of employment." 42 USC 2000e-2(a)(1). The Rehabilitation Act and the ADEA also apply broadly to the terms, conditions, and privileges of employment. "This '[b]an on discrimination in terms, conditions, or privileges of employment'...was intended to reach actual working conditions of employees, no merit being found in [the] contention that this section was intended to be confined to [the] obligation to provide equal employment opportunity for employment apart from physical conditions." Paramo v. USPS, 01832275 (1984) (quoting Harrington v. Vandalia-Butler Board of Education, 585 F.2d 192, 18 FEP 348 (6th Cir. 1978), cert. denied, 441 U.S. 932 (1979)). The employee's allegation that his supervisor disposed of his cup of coffee while not doing the same to a similarly situated white employee stated a claim within the EEO purview.

"Employees' psychological as well as economic fringes are statutorily entitled to protection from employer abuse and the phrase terms, conditions and privileges of employment is an expansive concept which sweeps within the protective ambit of Title VII." Norfleet v. Postmaster General, 01881588 (1988) (citing Rogers v. EEOC, 454 F.2d 234 (5th Cir. 1971)). "The only proper question under the purview issue is whether the complainant alleges employment discrimination on a basis covered by the EEO Statutes." Id.

In recent years, the Commission has found itself at odds with an increasing number of courts over what constitutes a term or condition of employment for Title VII, Rehabilitation Act, and ADEA purposes. The Commission continues to adhere to its view that the purview of these pieces of legislation is "broad and covers most actions by an agency which adversely affect an employee." Newell v. Postmaster General, 05901009 (1990). Courts have used a much more restrictive definition of the scope of the anti-discrimination statutes, requiring that the employee suffer some action that causes a materially significant disadvantage to the employee. See, e.g., Spring v. Sheboygan Area Sch. Dist., 865 F.2d 883 (7th Cir. 1989). The result is that the courts are dismissing actions that the Commission would find to be within its purview. In most cases, if a clear personnel action is at issue, the distinction is of no significance. However, the distinction should be taken into consideration when a complaint involves an allegation of discriminatory treatment without a definitive personnel action. In such a case, a complainant may be able to secure relief in the administrative process that is not available in the courts. For a full discussion of the differences between the federal courts and the Commission on the terms and conditions of employment, see Chapter 3, subsection "Standing: Failure to State a Claim."

Whether or not the Commission will find a particular item is a term, condition, or privilege of employment is determined by the agency's control over that particular item. As the Commission explained in Vaughan v. Secretary of Air Force, 01924661 (1992):

Here, the agency determined that the appellant was not aggrieved with respect to her allegations (2) and (3). It is clear that the appellant has alleged discrimination based upon her sex and disability which are covered by the EEO statutes; however, the agency held that the appellant's base driving privileges are not part of the appellant's employment and, therefore, found that she was not aggrieved. Contrary to the agency's analysis, we find that the appellant's ability to drive to and from work is a privilege of her employment with the agency. This is based on the fact that the agency administers and controls its employees' right to drive on the base, unlike the appellant's ability to drive based upon state licensing rules. Therefore, the privilege to drive on base is a term and condition of an employee's employment much like any other privilege of employment which the agency can restrict (i.e., leave time). Hence, the fact that her privilege to drive to work was revoked because of alleged discriminatory purpose is actionable under the regulations.

The fact that an employment benefit is entirely discretionary and the employee has no entitlement to the benefit does not exempt the agency from a claim of discrimination. For example, in Donelan v. Postmaster General, 05950816 (1996), the Commission held that an employee who alleged that a comparative was offered special terms and conditions to retire and he was not offered such terms constituted discrimination. The Commission reversed the agency's dismissal of the complaint finding, "[u]nder the circumstances, it would be possible for agency officials to deny appellant access to these benefits for reasons other than his eligibility and which would constitute unlawful discrimination, e.g., because of his race, religion, national origin, and so on." Id.
