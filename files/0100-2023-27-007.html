broida eeo Chapter 18: Personnel Actions Chapter 18: Personnel Actions, Subchapter 01: Disciplinary Actions, Section B: Constructive Discharge, Subsection 01: Competitive Service Employees

1. Competitive Service Employees

When an employee in the competitive service or a preference eligible veteran in the excepted service is eligible to file an MSPB appeal over a constructive discharge, that employee must do so prior to filing an appeal of a final agency decision with the EEOC. McWright v. Secretary of Education, 01893025 (1990). An allegation of constructive discharge by such an employee presents a mixed case allegation. See later, subheading "Constructive Discharge." The employee has the right to elect either the MSPB procedure or an expedited agency EEO procedure. Under McWright, if the employee elects the EEO procedure, his first right of appeal lies with the Board and the Commission will not entertain an appeal until that right is exhausted:

We also note that in a constructive discharge case, the issue of MSPB jurisdiction is intertwined with the merits of the action. See Burgess v. Merit Systems Protection Board, 758 F.2d 641, 643 (Fed. Cir. 1985). That is, if the MSPB determines that appellant's resignation was a voluntary act, rather than a constructive discharge, it may deny jurisdiction of her appeal on the grounds that there is no appealable action. In previous cases where the MSPB has denied jurisdiction, this Commission has held that the matter ceases to be a mixed case and may be processed as an ordinary federal sector EEO complaint in order to provide the complainant with a forum for his or her allegation of discrimination. See Larry L. Wilson v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Petition No. 03890084 (June 29, 1989), EEOC Appeal No. 01892774 (November 7, 1989); John R. Connor v. Labor Department, EEOC Petition No. 03880148 (October 1, 1988), EEOC Appeal No. 01890220 (February 21, 1989), request for reopening denied, EEOC Request No. 05890653 (September 22, 1989). In the cited cases, the petitions for Commission review of the MSPB's rulings were administratively closed, and the cases were redocketed as ordinary federal sector appeals. Therefore, we find that if the MSPB should deny jurisdiction of an appeal brought by appellant in the matter herein, it would cease to be a mixed case, and appellant would be entitled to reinstatement of proceedings on her EEO complaint...."

As noted above, many employees, and agencies as well, do not recognize cases as involving an issue of "constructive discharge." As a result, agencies do not inform the employees of their MSPB appeal rights. Complaints often get substantially through the EEO process before the issue is properly identified.

In another recurring scenario, employees initiate precomplaint counseling and then feel compelled to resign. Because the employee had not resigned at the time counseling was initiated, the complaint proceeds through the EEO process instead of being referred to the Board. Due to these recurring situations, the Commission clarified the process for handling constructive discharge cases in Simon v. Postmaster General, 05890464 (1990):

In cases where an appellant specifically articulates a constructive discharge allegation in his formal complaint and, therefore, has resigned from his/her position at the time the complaint is filed, then the complaint should initially be addressed by the MSPB. If, however, an appellant does not specifically allege that he was constructively discharged in his formal complaint, but the issue is raised during the EEO process, then the complaint should remain in the EEO process....

The Commission, disturbed by deferring to the MSPB after a complaint had been in the process for some time, found the specific articulation rule would promote efficiency in the mixed complaint process and avoid "unnecessary procedural complications." See also Nix v. Secretary of Agriculture, 05910505 (1991).

In Harrell v. Secretary of Army, 05940652 (1995), the Commission held it would consider a constructive discharge allegation that has not been brought before the MSPB when "it is so firmly enmeshed in the EEO process that it would unduly delay justice and create unnecessary procedural complications to remand it to the MSPB." In Harrell, the Commission considered such an allegation where the employee had never been given his notice of election rights and the agency did not raise the issue of MSPB jurisdiction prior to issuing its final decision.

It should be noted that in an MSPB case involving constructive discharge, the issues of jurisdiction and the merits are intertwined. To show that the case is properly before the Board from a jurisdictional viewpoint, the complainant must make some showing that his resignation was involuntary. The Board's denial of jurisdiction is based on the fact that the employee has not made a sufficient demonstration of an involuntary resignation. This determination, though dispositive on jurisdiction, is not dispositive before the Commission on the issue of whether a constructive discharge occurred.

For example, in Elshamy v. Secretary of Air Force, 01900757 (1990), the complainant filed an EEO complaint that alleged, among other things, that he was constructively discharged. At the hearing stage, the EEOC administrative judge severed the issue of constructive discharge as a matter raising a mixed case allegation and instructed the agency to forward it to the MSPB as a mixed case. The MSPB subsequently found it lacked jurisdiction over the constructive discharge allegation on the basis that the agency's denial of leave without pay--the event leading up to the resignation--was a matter committed to agency discretion by law. The complainant then filed an appeal with the Commission. The Commission found the allegation of constructive discharge, having been rejected by the Board, was now within its jurisdiction. However, it remanded the case for a hearing and final agency decision because an EEOC hearing on the allegation had never been held and the complainant had not waived his right to a hearing.

As the Commission noted, the mixed case regulations tend to create an anomaly when it comes to processing cases of constructive discharge that also involve allegations of discrimination. Under Cruz v. Department of Navy, 906 F.2d 689 (Fed. Cir. 1990), the Board is required to consider both an appealable action and any discrimination allegations presented in conjunction with the appealable action. The Commission addressed the anomaly in Wood v. Secretary of Navy, 01903253 (1990):

EEOC and MSPB regulations provide the framework for processing "mixed cases." These regulations are entwined because of the processing of mixed cases. The Commission's role in the mixed case process is to determine whether the Board correctly decided the issue of discrimination, as opposed to the merit systems issue. Typically, the Board will issue a decision on both the personnel action involved in the appeal and the discrimination alleged therein. However, when the personnel action is one of constructive discharge, it is apparent that the Board takes a different tack. In these cases, as noted by the MSPB AJ, an appellant is entitled to have his/her allegations of discrimination considered only if he/she raises a nonfrivolous allegation of involuntary resignation. Thus, an appellant must present evidence establishing that his/her resignation was involuntary before he/she is entitled to a hearing of his/her claim of discrimination. The Commission is mindful that this process creates anomalous results which were not contemplated by the regulations of the Board or the Commission. This case typifies the harm that can befall an appellant caught in the mixed case process.

In the present case, the Board ruled in favor of the agency on the jurisdictional issue, and thus determined that appellant's resignation was voluntary. According to its procedures, the Board made this finding without considering appellant's claims of discrimination. Fortunately, the Commission has developed a means of correcting the problem created by the Board's action, which is in line with the reasoning cited in Cruz, supra. This Commission believes that in view of the MSPB's position that the action is no longer an "appealable action," the case is no longer a "mixed case" as defined by EEOC Regulation 29 CFR 1613.402. Thus, we held in the case of Susan Schmitt v. Samuel K. Skinner, Secretary, Department of Transportation, EEOC Appeal No. 01902126 (July 9, 1990), the matter is one which must be addressed by the EEO process, not the "mixed case" process. We make the same finding in this case.

Where the MSPB has held a hearing on the jurisdictional issue, the Commission will review the transcript to determine if the complainant was given an adequate opportunity to present evidence on his allegations of discrimination. If not, the case will be remanded for an EEO hearing. Wood, supra.

The issue of constructive discharges, or involuntary resignations, is finding its way to the Commission more and more often. In Jackson v. Attorney General, 01893346 (1990), the Commission went so far as to apologize to the complainant for the fact that its administrative judge had dismissed the case without a hearing based on an MSPB determination of lack of jurisdiction. The Commission also reminded the administrative judge of his obligation to process cases in accordance with Commission precedent.
