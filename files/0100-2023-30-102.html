1. Damages vs. Equitable Relief

Although compensatory damages have opened up a new area of potential recovery for successful complainants, there still are many cases in process that involve acts of discrimination that occurred before the effective date of the 1991 Act. In some of those cases, it may be necessary to carefully analyze a request for relief to determine if it constitutes a request for compensatory damages or simply a claim for the traditional "make whole" relief. In addition, the distinction is important because the compensatory damage cap does not include monies that constitute equitable or legal relief under the anti-discrimination statutes.

In McGowan-Butler v. Secretary of Treasury, 05940636 (1994), the Commission determined that a claim for reimbursement for medical expenses does not necessarily constitute a claim for compensatory damages. When the complainant seeks reimbursement for medical expenses for treatment of stress or other medical conditions caused by the discrimination, this constitutes a request for compensatory damages. However, if the complainant merely seeks reimbursement for medical expenses that were occasioned by the cancelation of her health benefits due to a discriminatory personnel action, such reimbursement is part of the "make whole" relief. The Commission explained the distinction in McGowan-Butler:

There is no dispute in this case that appellant is not entitled to pursue a claim for compensatory damages pursuant to the Civil Rights Act of 1991, inasmuch as the damages provision of the Act does not apply to pre-Act conduct, such as that involved in this case. The question presented in this request is whether or not the remedy recommended by the AJ is derived from the "make-whole" provisions of Title VII or whether the lost wages and medical expenses ordered by the AJ are tantamount to compensatory damages.

Compensatory damages are awarded to compensate for losses or suffering inflicted due to the discriminatory act. See EEOC Enforcement Guidance: Compensatory and Punitive Damages Available under § 102 of the Civil Rights Act of 1991, EEOC Notice No. 915.002 (July 14, 1992). Medical expenses are generally characterized as compensatory damages, although, as appellant notes, medical expenses have been awarded as part of the "make whole" relief under Title VII in some circumstances. Appellant cites two cases in which courts awarded unreimbursed medical expenses as relief authorized by Title VII. The Commission finds, however, that such circumstances are rare, and that these two cases are easily distinguished from the present case. In EEOC v. Service News Co., 898 F.2d 958 (4th Cir. 1990), the court awarded medical expenses which resulted from the plaintiff's loss of health insurance after she was discriminatorily discharged. The court specifically noted in Service News Co. that the facts in that case warranted a departure from the ordinary rule, given the plaintiff's effort to obtain other insurance and her particular need. (footnote omitted). Similar facts were presented in Weiss v. Parker Hannifan Corp., 747 F. Supp 1118, 1132 (D.N.J. 1990). In both cases the plaintiff was discharged, and lost medical insurance. In this context, medical insurance was viewed as a benefit of the job, and therefore medical expenses were viewed as part of the "make whole" remedy.

In the present case, appellant was not discharged, and did not lose medical insurance. Accordingly, her medical expenses cannot be viewed as a benefit that she would have received absent the discrimination. Under the facts of this case, appellant's medical expenses were an alleged consequence of the discrimination, and therefore are not available as part of the equitable relief of Title VII.

By the same rationale, it would appear that an agency may be liable for insurance premiums paid by a complainant to replace employer-provided health insurance, not as an element of compensatory damages, but as an element of traditional relief.

However, in Scheier v. Secretary of Interior, 01940616 (1994), the Commission muddled the distinction between compensatory damages and "make whole" relief. The decision is unclear as to how it regards a claim that an employee was required to purchase alternative health insurance due to discrimination:

The record indicates that appellant, during EEO counseling, in his complaint and in his letter dated August 30, 1993, to the Commission, detailed the ongoing problems and the hardships he suffered because of the lack of health insurance. He has also stated that he had to spend money for medical treatment because of the agency's failure to provide him with the necessary health coverage. The agency failed to address this claim in its decision. The Commission has previously held that when a complainant shows objective evidence that (s)he has incurred compensatory damages, and that the damages are related to the alleged unlawful discrimination, the agency must address the issue. See Jackson v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01923399 (November 12, 1992), request to reopen denied, EEOC Request No. 05930306 (February 1, 1993). Thus, because appellant made a claim for compensatory damages, the agency should have requested that appellant provide objective evidence of the alleged damages incurred. The agency should have also requested objective evidence linking these damages to the alleged unlawful discrimination. Assuming arguendo that appellant were to prevail in this matter, he might be entitled to compensatory relief; therefore, we do not find that the allegations are moot. See generally Glover v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01930696 (December 9, 1993)....

From the Scheier decision it is not clear whether the Commission regarded both the claim of reimbursement for health insurance and medical expenses as compensatory damages, or whether it was the claim of medical expenses, standing alone, that constituted compensatory damages.

Also in the McGowan-Butler case, the Commission addressed an argument that a complainant's request for back pay for a period when she was on leave without pay constituted a request for compensatory damages where the complainant alleged her medical condition was caused by the agency's discrimination. When the complainant seeks back pay for time lost due to the mental distress caused by the discrimination, the back pay does not constitute compensatory damages. As the Commission in McGowan-Butler explained:

There remains the issue of appellant's claim for back pay resulting from an emotional condition allegedly caused by the discrimination. The agency claims that appellant's claim for back pay is a claim for compensatory damages. Appellant claims that her emotional condition resulted from the race discrimination that she endured, and therefore, she is entitled to reimbursement for the LWOP she took as a result of her inability to work due to her emotional condition. The Commission notes that relief under Title VII has traditionally been limited to equitable relief, and equitable relief usually includes back pay. In addition, we have held that a complainant is entitled to reimbursement for any leave that he/she was compelled to take in direct response to discrimination. See Joyce M. Bouchell v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01932122 (June 23, 1994); Pamela Olson v. Department of Housing and Urban Development, EEOC Request No. 05930413 (June 16, 1994). Accordingly, the Commission disagrees with the agency that the portion of relief involving a claim for back pay is a claim for compensatory damages.

Nonetheless, the Commission finds that the record contains insufficient information on the issue of whether or not appellant's emotional condition resulted from the race discrimination that she endured. Accordingly, we deny the agency request to reconsider this portion of the prior decision, and remand the matter of appellant's claim for back pay for a supplemental investigation as outlined below.
