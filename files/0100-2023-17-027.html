D. SUBMISSION OF EVIDENCE

Review by the Office of Federal Operations is an appellate review and, generally, new evidence will not be accepted. For example, an agency may not support a final agency decision with evidence submitted after the close of the hearing record. Munno v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01A01734 (2001) (evidence was submitted on appeal that the complainant would not have received an SES position but for prohibited retaliation was rejected).

The rule is flexible, however. In Thomas v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01891407 (1989), the Commission accepted an affidavit from a witness who had not testified at the hearing. The Commission did so because the RMO testified about complaints received by appellant from an AMVET representative. On appeal, the appellant filed an affidavit from the representative stating that he had never complained to the RMO. The Commission found that the appellant could not have anticipated the testimony of the RMO. Therefore, the notarized affidavit was accepted into evidence and fully credited.

The most common test for the acceptance of new evidence on appeal is whether the proffered evidence was not previously available despite the exercise of due diligence. For example in Estate of Petersen v. Secretary of Transportation, 07A50016 (2005), the Commission rejected the agency's attempt to impeach the testimony of a witness on appeal observing:

After a careful review of the record, we are not persuaded by the agency's arguments and we see no reason to disturb the AJ's finding of discrimination. The findings of fact are supported by substantial evidence which demonstrated that S exhibited bias against complainant because of her gender. There was testimonial evidence from other air traffic controllers, which supported the AJ's finding that S made derogatory comments about complainant stating that "women controllers did not move traffic well" and "that's just what the tower needs, another woman controller who can't do the job." Another controller confirmed that S referred to complainant as "bitch" and "c**t" and that S never referred to complainant by her first name but instead called her "that woman."

Regarding this evidence, the agency argues on appeal that the testimony is unreliable because at least one of the air traffic controllers who testified, later, after the closing of the record in this matter, made an inconsistent statement. However, the evidence on which the agency relies is not persuasive. Moreover, we conclude that the agency failed to demonstrate that the claimed impeachment evidence was not available during the hearing process. As to the agency's "new" evidence of a witness' lack of credibility, which it did not present at the hearing, the Commission will normally not consider new evidence unless there is a showing that it was not reasonably available at that time. EEOC Management Directive 110 Chapt. 9-15 (Rev. November 1999). Here, the agency has made no showing that the evidence of a witness' so-called inconsistent statement was not available during the hearing process. Even if the agency did make such a showing, the evidence the agency submitted on appeal is that of another employee stating what this witness told him. This is not evidence of the witness' inconsistent statement.

While normally, a party may not submit new evidence or raise a new issue on appeal, in Chen v. Postmaster General, 01A34839 (2005), the Commission allowed an exception due to the incapacitation of the complainant's representative. The complainant's representative submitted evidence that for a several month period he had a serious illness that incapacitated him. During that time he underwent four operations and ultimately had a leg amputated. The Commission in Chen reversed the grant of summary judgment by the administrative judge noting that evidence was submitted on appeal establishing a genuine dispute of fact.

Another exception to the general rule that new evidence will not be accepted on appeal is when a complaint has been dismissed for failure to state a claim. In Eckert v. Postmaster General, 01A54605 (2005), the Commission noted, "[a]lthough it is true that we do not accept new evidence on appeal when the evidence could have been made available during the investigation, that rule does not apply here as complainant's complaint never reached the investigation stage."

The Commission also will accept new evidence on appeal when an agency investigation is inadequate. For example, in Enemas v. Postmaster General, 01A54665 (2005), the Commission accepted statements on appeal that called into question the credibility of management witnesses where the agency failed to include the statements of several potential witnesses in its investigation.

Note that with most of the exceptions, the new evidence is considered in determining whether the case should be remanded to supplement the record through additional investigation or a resumption of the hearing.

The Commission may, on appeal, decide to remand a case for the taking of additional evidence where the record is incomplete. In such cases, it is no response to a Commission remand for further investigation that the information sought is within the control of another component of the agency. Robinson v. Postmaster General, 01931313 (1993). However, the Commission remanded the case for a second time for further investigation. It gave no explanation for why it did not at least consider drawing an adverse inference, given the clarity of the additional information sought on the oriGINAl remand.

In Thompson-Truitt v. Secretary of Treasury, 05940870 (1996), the Commission explained the criteria for ordering a supplemental investigation. The basic standard is whether the parties had a full and fair opportunity to explain and supplement the existing record. In Thompson-Truitt, the Commission found the parties did have such an opportunity where the administrative judge allowed the complainant to amend her complaint to include national origin discrimination and gave both parties the opportunity to develop the record fully on the allegation:

A year before the previous decision was issued, the Commission had held that it is appropriate for an AJ to consider an additional basis for an issue that is already being addressed at a hearing. Mary Patania v. Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05920913 (April 1, 1993). This ruling accords with Chapter 6 of EEO MD-110 which, as the agency points out, states that a hearing "completes the investigation of a complaint by ensuring that the parties have a fair and reasonable opportunity to explain and supplement the record. . ." Applying Patania to this case, we find that the AJ properly permitted testimony and questioning on the allegation of national origin discrimination, and on the allegation of reprisal as well.

It follows that the AJ's action obviated the agency's failure to investigate appellant's allegation of national origin discrimination. Thus, the agency's failure was not an adequate reason for the previous decision to order a supplemental investigation. Indeed, in MD-110 we alert parties to a different standard for ordering supplemental investigations:

It is the obligation of the parties to obtain and enter into the record any evidence necessary to [an AJ's] adjudication. The Commission will be less inclined to remand for further supplementation of the record where parties had the opportunity to develop the record below.

Therefore, the previous decision should not have directed a supplemental investigation until determining whether the parties were given a "fair and reasonable opportunity" to submit evidence on the national origin allegation. We have decided to reconsider appellant's complaint under that standard.

After reviewing the hearing transcript and record of this case, the Commission concludes that the parties were given just such an opportunity at the hearing. With respect to her national origin allegation, appellant was permitted to enter evidence, to testify, and to question witnesses. The agency was permitted to examine and cross-examine on the subject. Similarly, the parties were given the opportunity to submit evidence on the reprisal claim.

Because the hearing afforded the parties a fair opportunity to submit evidence, the AJ had a sufficient record on which to adjudicate the national origin and reprisal claims. The AJ erred in failing to do so; however, the cure for the failure should not have been to order the agency to conduct a supplemental investigation. The agency acted properly in ruling on the claims in its FAD.
