g. Supervisors/Chain of Command

Whether employees are similarly situated is an issue of fact. When facts over the chain-of-supervision of the complainant and alleged comparative employees are in dispute, summary judgment is inappropriate. For example, the Commission in Pignatello v. Secretary of Navy, 01956769 (1997), determined that the AJ's granting of a motion for summary judgment was improper because a material issue remained regarding whether the complainant was treated differently than similarly situated employees:

The AJ found that appellant did not establish a prima facie case because his comparative employees were not similarly situated. The Commission's decisions have held that to be considered similarly situated, the employees named as comparatives must have reported to the same supervisor; must have been subject to the same standards governing discipline; and must have engaged in conduct similar to petitioner's without differentiating or mitigating circumstances distinguishing their misconduct or the appropriate discipline for it. See Martin v. U.S.P.S. Petition No. 03930101, September 30, 1993, Woods v. Milner, 760 F. Supp. 623, 645 (E.D. Mich. 1991).

It is an undisputed fact that appellant and the comparatives worked in the same facility, the agency's Defense Printing Service, branch office. In addition, appellant alleges that the agency Director and Deputy Director (the fourth and third-line supervisors) were the same during the time of his altercation and the comparatives' situation. He further asserts that the Director was consulted by the Deputy Director regarding the recommended penalty in his case and the same Director was consulted by the supervisor in the comparative employees' case. The Director, in fact, recalled recommending the minimum penalty, a letter of caution, in the comparatives' case.

The record is unclear regarding the Deputy Director's position in the chain of command at the time of the comparative's discipline. The Deputy's response to the investigator's question on whether the comparatives' supervisor was under her in the management chain is ambiguous. She replies, "No, I was not in a non-supervisory role when this incident happened."

Having carefully considered the record as a whole, we find that, contrary to the AJ's decision, whether appellant has established a prima facie case, by showing that similarly-situated employees were treated more favorably, remains a material issue in dispute. For this reason, we find that it was improper for appellant to be denied a hearing regarding the matters in question. Accordingly, the agency's final decision should be VACATED, and the complaint REMANDED in accordance with the ORDER set forth below.

Supervisors are not required to be equal in their treatment of subordinates, as long as each supervisor treats his or her own subordinate employees in the same fashion. An employee who alleges that her supervisor treated her less favorably than other supervisors treated younger employees has failed to state a prima facie case of age discrimination. See Lance v. Department of Energy, 01842871 (1986). The relevant comparison group was other employees under the same supervisor. Unfortunately for the complainant, the supervisor had no other subordinate employees and there the case ended. Also, a supervisor who is new to the job may not be similarly situated to an experienced supervisor who is fully trained. More can be expected of the latter. Mack v. Secretary of Navy, 01910094 (1991).

Even where the supervisors are not the same, employees may be deemed similarly situated if a common link is provided by the agency official giving advice to the respective supervisors. In Oates v. Postmaster General, 01951975 (1997), the complainant was able to show discrimination by demonstrating that employees not in her protected classes engaged in the same behavior as she did (fighting), but were disciplined less severely, even though the other employees were not under the same supervisor. In Oates, the Commission found:

We find the evidence supports a finding that the appellant successfully established a prima facie case of disparate treatment because of her race, color and sex. Appellant testified that she was aware of four employees (two white males, one black male, one white female) who had engaged in fighting on the postal property in late 1990 but were not discharged or disciplined. Although the record does not contain specific information regarding the fighting incidents in late 1990, it includes specific information about two incidents in 1991 involving two of the white male comparators who were accused of engaging in verbal altercations and threats toward their supervisors. One of the white males was found to have verbally abused postal supervisors and declined to leave the agency premises after being told to do so. He was charged with improper conduct and suspended for one day in August 1991. For another incident in May 1991 involving yelling at a supervisor and showing a bad temper, the same employee was given a letter of warning. The other white male comparator was charged with engaging in verbal abuse and threatening behavior toward a supervisor in February 1991. The record contains an unsigned copy of a notice of 7-day suspension which apparently was never issued.

The SA [Supervisor] acknowledged that he knew about the fighting incidents involving the four comparators and had served in an advisory capacity in all those instances. He also testified that he advised the ATS [Acting Tour Supervisor] regarding the January 1992 incident involving the appellant, and that he prepared the Emergency Procedure Letter that was issued to the appellant. And, the ATS acknowledged that he placed the appellant on an emergency procedure, followed by a letter of removal, on the basis of the SA's recommendation.

Thus, it is clear that the SA played a pivotal role in advising and recommending appropriate discipline with regard to not only the January 1992 incident involving the appellant but also with regard to the 1990 fighting incidents involving the other four white or male comparators. The ATS admitted that he relied on the SA's recommendation in placing the appellant in a non-duty, non-pay, status in January 1992 and later discharging her in May 1992 for that incident. Moreover, evidence regarding at least some 1991 altercations involving two of the four comparators shows that the ATS was in fact a responsible management official for taking (or failing to take) disciplinary action.

The SA's testimony also acknowledged that none of the four comparators involved in the 1990 fights was fired. Further, there is no evidence disputing the appellant's testimony that none of them was even put off the clock for those fighting incidents. We, therefore, find that the appellant established a prima facie case of race, color and sex discrimination in that the responsible management officials placed her in a non-duty, non-pay status, and discharged her, but that they took no disciplinary action against the four white or male comparators who engaged in fighting.

The FAD implies that the agency's action against the appellant was nevertheless taken for a legitimate non-discriminatory reason. The FAD states that the responsible management officials relied on two memoranda issued to the employees in 1991, warning them that physical altercations and assaults on other employees may be punished by dismissal, and that foul language and loud boisterous disturbances would not be tolerated.

We, however, find that the agency's reliance on these memoranda is pretextual for the following reason. The record contains several other memoranda, mostly pre-dating the incidents involving the four comparators, which similarly warned the employees against violence. For instance, an August 1988 memorandum to all employees warned them that "[f]ighting and/or threats of violence shall be considered offenses warranting removal from the Postal Service." Another May 1990 memorandum to all employees warned them that even a verbal threat would be considered an assault, and that threats of physical violence or references to past incidents of violence would warrant severe disciplinary action and removal. Thus, although the 1990 fighting incidents involving the four comparators occurred prior to the issuance of the two 1991 memoranda relied upon by the agency, they definitely occurred subsequent to the issuances of the 1988 and 1990 memoranda on the same subject. Therefore, we reject the agency's contention that the taking of the disciplinary action against the appellant was a legitimate non-discriminatory response to the agency's announced policies on violence.
