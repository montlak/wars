C. RELIGION-BASED HARASSMENT

As noted above, the Commission withdrew its proposed regulations on harassment that would have included religious harassment, but as of July 2008 had significantly revised Section 12 of its Compliance Manual to address issues involving religious discrimination, including harassment based on religion. The reason for withdrawing the proposed regulations was that substantial questions were raised about the possible First Amendment consequences of harassment based on religion. When an employee is subjected to derogatory comments because of his or her religion, no particular problem arises. But in a situation where an employee or supervisor is accused of creating a hostile environment by attempting to persuade others of the accuracy of his or her particular religious beliefs, the First Amendment can be implicated. Commission guidance was further revised in 2021. EEOC Compliance Manual on Religious Discrimination, No. 915.063 (2021).

Except in rare cases, the analysis of cases alleging a hostile environment based on religious harassment is the same as for other bases of harassment. The harassment must be based on conduct that has either the purpose or effect of creating a hostile, abusive, or offensive work environment. When an employee cannot show that the agency acted with intent to harass him, he must, in the alternative, show that the conduct was sufficiently pervasive to have the effect of creating a hostile work environment. As the Commission explained in Moore v. Secretary of Defense, Army and Air Force Exchange, 01933575 (1994):

The record indicates that appellant succeeds in establishing a prima facie case of discrimination on the basis of religion. This is because he is a member of a protected group, non-Christians, and suffered adverse treatment by being subjected to music which was against his religious beliefs. The agency is able to articulate legitimate, nondiscriminatory reasons for playing such music, however. These are that in early December religious music was played one morning by mistake and only for a very short time, while religious music was played on December 24, 1991, from 8:00 A.M. onwards to accommodate Christians (the vast majority of the work force) who enjoyed such music and would be leaving early that day. Based on these reasons, the commission finds that the agency has met its burden of going forward with the evidence.

Appellant must now establish by a preponderance of the evidence that the agency's articulated reasons are but a pretext for discrimination. Appellant cannot do this, however, because he cannot show that the Christmas music was deliberately played to harass him on the basis of his religious beliefs, rather than by mistake or to accommodate the vast majority of his fellow workers who liked such music. Even if appellant could prove that there was a deliberate intent on the part of the agency to harass him by playing such music, it still would not rise to the level necessary to prove discrimination. This because the harassing music complained of was played only on two days in December 1991, and for relatively brief periods of time. Yet such harassment must generally be ongoing and continuous in order to constitute unlawful discrimination. The two such isolated incidents complained of here, separated as they were by several weeks, are not sufficient in themselves to rise to the level of discriminatory harassment. See Hicks v. Gates Rubber Co., 833 F.2d 1406, 1412-13 (10th Cir. 1987). Accordingly, we find that appellant has failed to show by the necessary preponderance of the evidence required under the guidelines of McDonnell Douglas, supra, that he was discriminated against by the agency.

Though it was less than clear from the Commission's proposed regulations, it appeared that conduct constituted harassment when it had the intent to create a hostile working environment, even when such conduct did not in fact create a hostile working environment. This does not appear to be the view adopted by the Supreme Court in Harris. In Harris, the Supreme Court specifically enunciated a two-pronged test that requires that a reasonable person would have found the work environment hostile, and that the plaintiff actually did find the work environment hostile. This appears to preclude a finding of harassment where the conduct is intended to create a hostile environment but does not, in fact, do so. This is the view embraced by the Moore decision when the Commission noted that, even if the complainant had demonstrated intent, he had not shown the conduct rose to the level of creating a hostile work environment.
