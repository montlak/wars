10. Training

When the selectee for a position has been given more training than the complainant, the refusal to provide training may be probative of pretext. But where the selectee receives the training as the result of settlement of a grievance and the complainant turns down a similar opportunity for additional training, there is no showing of pretext. Estok v. USPS, 01842637 (1986).

The failure to provide training after a promotion can be indicative of pretext. For example, in Kelly v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01956660 (1998), the Commission found that the complainant proved pretext for discrimination, based in part on the agency's failure to provide the complainant adequate training to perform in her position:

We find that the AJ properly concluded that appellant established that the agency's explanations for her removal were pretext for prohibited discrimination. The AJ noted that although RO 1 and RO 2 testified that they offered appellant a lateral move to another GS-10 position, the Associate Director testified that they had never discussed this move with him, We too find this failure peculiar, given the fact that appellant's eventual demotion had to be cleared by the Associate Director. We also note that the agency's EEO Officer testified that he found it "odd" that with all the documents that appellant received regarding her performance, she never received an oral counseling.

Furthermore, contrary to the agency's finding, there is evidence to support the AJ's finding that RO 1 was upset about having his recommendation ignored concerning the supervisory position in which appellant was placed. Appellant testified that RO 1 told her that he would never promote her into the position and that if she ever said anything, he would deny it. We also note that RO 2 testified that RO 1 told him that appellant was not his first choice for the position. The agency also found that appellant's lack of training and insufficient time to learn the job had little to do with her demotion, and that her poor organizational skills were the primary reason behind her removal. We disagree, and note that proper training and sufficient time to learn the job might have contributed to better organization on appellant's part. Appellant may have found it very difficult to organize contracts of which she had little or no knowledge. As for the agency's contention that appellant received sufficient training, we note that she did not receive Levels 1 and 2 of the technical training she was supposed to receive until December 1993, six months after she started the job; that she did not receive Level 3 training until March 1994, one month prior to her demotion; and that she never received Level 4 of the required training. Accordingly, we find that appellant established that she was discriminated against with regard to her removal.
