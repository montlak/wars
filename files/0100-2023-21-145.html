11. Conduct Directed Toward Others

A finding of hostile environment harassment can be based in part on conduct specifically directed toward the complainant and category-based conduct not directed at the complainant. A good example is provided by Broomfield v. Secretary of Treasury, 0720110038 (2013):

Regarding the claim of hostile work environment harassment, there is substantial evidence to support the AJ's finding. There is testimony about jokes being made about "Barack Osama." After Hurricane Katrina, several of the officers who are hunters said they should get hunting tags and go hunt down the looters who were Black. Officers would make rude remarks about a female police officer's biracial child and would comment that Complainant was the father. Complainant testified that a former Chief had told other police officers that he would never promote another "nigger" and he (Complainant) had heard about the comment. Complainant also testified that folks were resistant to diversity training and disturbed about attending. One officer made the comment about the only way to be hired on to the Mint was if you were "a minority or a homosexual." The record also shows that during the events at issue, the Denver Mint employed 53 employees of whom only four were Black. The number of Black employees decreased by nearly 54 percent, from 10 of 54 in 1997 to 4 of 53 in 2008. The Mint had not hired one Black police officer since about 2000.

The AJ could properly find and conclude that Complainant was subjected to a racially hostile work environment. Even if Complainant may not have heard all remarks or remarks were not specifically directed toward Complainant, this fact is not dispositive. See Spriggs v. Diamond Auto Glass, 242 F.3d 179, 185-86 (4th Cir. 2001) (racial harassment not directed specifically at plaintiff, but part of plaintiff's work environment, could be actionable). Moreover, the sheer number and gravity of the incidents cited and the fact that EEO posters were openly defaced in the work environment significantly erodes the credibility of the agency's contention that its officials were unaware of the racially charged atmosphere. In addition, as cited by Complainant from Penry v. Federal Home Loan Bank, 155 F.3d 1257 (10th Cir. 1998):

A play cannot be understood on the basis of some of its scenes but only on its entire performance, and similarly, a discrimination analysis must concentrate not on individual incidents, but on the overall scenario. As the Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit noted, the factfinder in this type of case should not 'necessarily examine each alleged incident of harassment in a vacuum. What may appear to be a legitimate justification for a single incident of alleged harassment may look pretextual when viewed in the context of several other related incidents.

Id., citing Andrews v. City of Philadelphia, 895 F.2d 1469, 1484 (3rd Cir. 1990) (quoting Vance v. Southern Bell Tel. & Tel. Co., 863 F.2d 1503, 1510 (11th Cir. 1989).

Moreover, there is no requirement that the conduct be witnessed by the complainant firsthand as the Commission observed in Complainant v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120132831 (2015):

We note, as the AJ pointed out, that Complainant heard about these derogatory matters secondhand from other employees. However, merely because Complainant was not present when the derogatory comments were made, does "not render [those] comment[s] irrelevant to [his] hostile work environment claim." Schwapp v. Town of Avon, 18 F.3d 106, 111 (2d. Cir. 1999) (court considered eight additional racially-charged incidents that did not occur in plaintiff's presence in reversing district court's granting of summary judgment to defendants in a hostile work environment claim); Jackson v. Quanex Corp., 191 F.3d 647, 659-661 (6th Cir. 1999); Rodgers v. Western-Southern Life Ins. Co., 12 F.3d 668, 675-676 (7th Cir. 1993); Carter v. Chrysler Corp., 173 F.3d 693, 701 fn.7 (8th Cir. 1999). Therefore, second-hand evidence of discriminatory comments is relevant to whether a hostile work environment existed, because these comments can also impact the work environment. Schwapp, 18 F.3d at 111. As such, we find that there is a dispute as to whether these comments rise to the level of a hostile work environment.

See also Adams v. Secretary of Army, 0120073776 (2007) and Lofton, et al v. Secretary of Army, 0120073757 (2007) (Commission reversed agency's dismissal of racial harassment claims involving some 20 complainants where not all of the comments and actions had been directed at each complainant).

However, the complained of conduct must still be sufficiently severe or pervasive to rise to the level of actionable harassment as the Commission pointed out in Drew N. v. Postmaster General, 0120171410 (2017). In that case, the complainant alleged race-based harassment when he witnessed a coworker confront another coworker, and when he reported it the agency took no action. The Commission upheld the agency's dismissal for failure to state a claim in Drew N., explaining:

Following a review of the record, we find that in the instant case, Complainant fails to state a viable claim of a discriminatory hostile work environment. The actions alleged, without more, concern a single confrontation between two co-workers in. Complainant's work area, and are simply insufficiently severe or pervasive to state a valid claim. Complainant does not allege that he was confronted or that any actions were addressed towards him. Nor does he indicate he suffered any harm or loss.
