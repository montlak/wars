1. Election of MSPB

Once the employee elects to pursue his or her case before the Board, it is governed by the procedures of the Board set out at 5 CFR Part 1201. The Board will process the case in the same manner it processes cases not involving allegations of discrimination. The Board is required to process cases involving allegations of discrimination through to an initial decision within 120 days of the filing of the appeal. See 5 CFR 1201.156. The Board must hear and decide the issues of discrimination whether raised in the initial appeal, or at a later stage of the proceeding.

Once an initial decision is received from the Board, the employee has several options: (1) The employee may file a petition for review with the full MSPB prior to the date the initial decision becomes final. The Board must review the decision on the merits with regard to its own statutory jurisdiction and also review the decision on the allegations of discrimination. (2) The employee may allow the initial decision of the Board to become final through expiration of the appeal period, then file an appeal in the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit. The court must review the Board's actions under its own appellate standards and can only review the Board's treatment of matters related to civil service law. The court has no jurisdiction over the Board's determinations on discrimination. (3) The employee may allow the initial decision to become final and then appeal only the issues of discrimination to the EEOC. (4) The employee may file a petition for review with the MSPB and then appeal the Board's final decision on issues of discrimination to the EEOC. (5) The employee may file a civil action in U.S. district court on the issues of discrimination. The district court will hear the issues of discrimination de novo and review the Board's decision on civil service law under appellate standards. (Of course, the employee has a sixth option which is to accept the decision of the Board and do nothing. The author presumes that anyone seriously considering that option has abandoned reading long before now.)

Under MD-110 at 4-4, if an employee files an appeal with the MSPB on a mixed case before filing a formal complaint of discrimination with the respondent agency the agency, must dismiss any complaint on the same subject matter, regardless of whether the allegations of discrimination are raised in the appeal to the MSPB. Upon dismissal, the agency must advise the employee to raise the allegations of discrimination in connection with his/her appeal to the MSPB. See also Lamb v. Department of Navy, 01861430 (1986).

As is the case with grievances, the agency must develop a system for logging both EEO complaints and MSPB appeals.

Once an employee has elected to proceed before the MSPB, the withdrawal of his appeal does not mean he can then file an EEO complaint. As the Commission explained in Cox v. Postmaster General, 01943638 (1995):

[T]he fact that appellant subsequently withdrew his MSPB appeal did not negate his prior election. See Hammond v. General Services Administration, EEOC Request No. 05940428 (August 25, 1994); Dean v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01942928 (June 29, 1994). Therefore, we find that appellant's prior appeal to the MSPB constituted an election of forum and the agency's dismissal on that ground was proper....

Where in the MSPB process the appeal was when withdrawn is irrelevant. The MSPB in Margeret M. v. Veterans Affairs, 2022003019 (2022), dismissed the complainant's appeal for failure to prosecute. Before the Commission, the complainant argued that because she did not respond to any of the MSPB's orders, the Commission could hear her claim without running afoul of the forum election rules. The Commission disagreed in Margeret M.:

EEOC Regulation 29 C.F.R. © 1614.107(d) provides that an agency shall dismiss a complaint or a portion of a complaint where the complainant has raised the matter in an appeal to the MSPB and 29 C.F.R. © 1614.302 indicates that the complainant has elected to pursue the non-EEO process. An agency may dismiss a prior EEO complaint where a subsequent MSPB appeal considered or could have considered the merits of the contentions raised in the EEO complaint. See Aho v. Dep't of Agric., EEOC Request No. 05860085 (May 22, 1987). Complainant essentially argues that because the MSPB dismissed her appeal for failure to prosecute, Complainant would not be getting a second bite of the apple by being permitted to pursue her constructive discharge claim before the Commission. We reject Complainant's argument. We find that by filing her MSPB appeal, Complainant elected to pursue her constructive discharge claim in the MSPB forum. See Traylor v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01955677 (July 29, 1996). The mere fact that Complainant failed to respond to any of the MSPB's orders does not invalidate her decision and we decline to essentially reward Complainant for her failure to prosecute one appeal by giving her a second opportunity to pursue the same claim in another forum. We therefore affirm the AJ's dismissal of Complainant's constructive discharge claim.

It is only the filing of an appeal with the MSPB that constitutes an election of remedy. Filing a compliance petition seeking enforcement of a prior Board decision is not an election of remedy. As the Commission explained in Jones v. Secretary of Navy, 01955370 (1996):

On November 29, 1994, the Merit Systems Protection Board (MSPB) issued a decision reversing the agency's decision to remove appellant and ordered that the agency reinstate him. The record indicates that on January 17, 1995, appellant's first day back to work with the agency after his reinstatement, appellant got into a fight with Person A and that appellant and Person A each received a letter of reprimand pertaining to that incident. By letter dated March 30, 1995, appellant informed the MSPB that the agency had failed to comply with the Board's order and had retaliated against him. Appellant stated that the agency had failed in its investigation of the incident involving him and Person A and that the agency had reprimanded him for the incident. On March 31, 1995, the MSPB issued an acknowledgement order and directed the agency to file a written response to appellant's petition for enforcement within 15 days.

In the instant complaint, dated April 25, 1995, appellant alleged that he was subjected to discrimination in retaliation for prior EEO activity, when, on January 17, 1995, the agency allowed Person A to assault appellant and make racial remarks towards him, and arrested appellant for allegedly fighting in the workplace.

In its FAD, the agency dismissed appellant's complaint for raising a matter that appellant had elected to pursue before the MSPB. EEOC Regulation 29 CFR § 1614.107(d) provides, in pertinent part, that an agency shall dismiss a complaint or portion of a complaint where the complainant has raised the matter in an appeal to the MSPB and § 1614.302 indicates that the complainant has elected to pursue the non-EEO process. A mixed case complaint is a complaint of employment discrimination filed with a federal agency, related to or stemming from an action that can be appealed to the MSPB. 29 CFR § 1614.302(a)(1). An aggrieved person may initially file a mixed case complaint with an agency or may file a mixed case appeal directly with the MSPB, pursuant to 5 CFR § 1201.151, but not both. 29 CFR § 1614.302(b). Further, the agency is required to inform every employee who is the subject of an action which is appealable to the MSPB and who has raised an allegation of discrimination concerning that action of his or her right to file a mixed case complaint or to file a mixed case appeal with the MSPB. The employee must be informed that he or she may not file both a complaint and an appeal, and that whichever is filed first shall be considered an election to proceed in that forum.

We find that the agency improperly dismissed appellant's complaint. The agency determined that appellant's complaint should be dismissed because appellant had elected to pursue the matter with the MSPB. The record indicates that appellant did not file an appeal with the MSPB but a petition for enforcement of the Board's decision of November 29, 1994. Under such circumstances, appellant has not made an election to proceed in the MSPB forum, which would foreclose his pursuit of the matter through the EEO process. Therefore, the agency improperly dismissed appellant's complaint.

In order to preserve Commission review, the employee must not only raise the issues of discrimination with the Board, he also must pursue them. See Chaires v. Department of Housing and Urban Development, 0340192 (1986) (employee withdrew allegations of discrimination in the Board proceeding).
