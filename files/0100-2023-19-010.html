a. Similarly Situated

"The fact that one or more comparators outside complainant's protected class were treated the same as complainant will not defeat complainant's ability to establish a prima facie case, where there are comparators outside of complainant's protected class, who were treated more favorably than complainant." Mellerson v. Social Security Administration, 01981404 (2001). See also Bailey v. Postmaster General, 01A40437 (2005) (evidence that three white males not allowed to sign overtime list did not prevent establishing prima facie case where there was evidence that one white male worked large amounts of overtime). Cf. Eubanks v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, 01A40070 (2005) (no prima facie case of race discrimination where all of the selectees were black).

Whether the Commission will deem a comparison employee to be similarly situated in any given case is a function of several factors. Although not all of these factors are relevant in any given case, the factors include such things as the personnel action involved, the background circumstances, supervisory personnel involved and the time frame involved. Other factors also may be relevant given the specific case. In Powell v. Postmaster General, 01911979 (1991), the Commission gave an articulation of the test for determining whether an employee is similarly situated:

In order for appellant to show other employees outside his protected group are similarly situated to him for the purpose of creating an inference of disparate treatment, he must show that all of the relevant aspects of his employment situation are nearly identical to those of the employees whom he alleges were treated more favorably...The Commission recently held in a case involving discipline that employees are similarly situated when they: (1) are subject to the same supervisor; (2) perform the same job functions; (3) are on the same tour of duty and (4) are disciplined during approximately the same period....

See also Jones v. Postmaster General, 01A03750 (2001).

"In order to properly assess who is similarly situated, however, it is necessary to first isolate the precise employment decision in [the] case." Lacy v. Secretary of Treasury, 01930795 (1993). In that case, the complainant alleged that her position was reclassified and downgraded through a classification audit and that she was not granted priority placement via a special agency program. The Commission found similarly situated persons were limited to employees who also had their positions reclassified and downgraded by classification audit. It further found she would not be similarly situated to employees who had obtained higher-level positions outside the special agency program. Likewise, in Radcliffe v. Postmaster General, 01954521 (1997), the Commission found it was error for the administrative judge to grant a motion for summary judgment on the basis that all employees had been treated the same in that their positions were abolished by a reduction-in-force. The Commission found the focus on the abolition of the jobs without regard to the placement of the employees after the RIF was improper. It was not until the focus was properly placed on the treatment of employees subsequent to the RIF that it became apparent the complainant had demonstrated he was treated differently than similarly situated employees.

Usually, complainants will want to define the category of similarly situated persons as broadly as possible, and the agency will want to define the category as narrowly as possible. Often, the proper definition lies somewhere in the middle. For example, in Rodgers v. Postmaster General, 01930515 (1993), the complainant was denied reinstatement after his conviction and incarceration on charges of distributing narcotics. The complainant argued that the category of similarly situated persons included all employees who violated agency regulations against criminal activity and dishonesty and who continued to be employed. The Commission found such a category was overly inclusive because it contained persons who have never requested reinstatement. The agency argued the category of similarly situated persons should include only those who had been convicted of distribution of narcotics and incarcerated and who subsequently requested and were granted reinstatement. The Commission found such a category was under inclusive because it essentially required an identically situated person. The proper category, the Commission found, consisted of those persons who violated agency regulations against criminal activity, applied for reinstatement with the agency and were granted reinstatement.

In the same case, the Commission held that the agency drew a legitimate distinction between the complainant and a similarly situated employee who was reinstated in that the reinstated employee was convicted of possession of narcotics and not distribution, but the reinstated employee was never incarcerated for his offense. Such distinctions were legitimate reasons for treating the complainant differently than the reinstated employee and were not indicia of discriminatory motive.

Not every distinction in the status of an employee is significant in determining if two employees are similarly situated. For example, in Peterson v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 07A40070 (2005), the Commission rejected the agency's position that the complainant, a probationary employee, was not similarly situated to a temporary employee hired for a term of employment when both employees performed the same function. By contrast, in Christmon v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 07A50006 (2005), the Commission held that a contract dental assistant was not similarly situated to a dental assistant who was a career civil servant.

A complainant is not required to prove that his class as a whole has been discriminated against in order to establish an individual prima facie case of discrimination. Conversely, proof that complainant's class as a whole has not been discriminated against is not proof that the complainant has not been subjected to discrimination. Hill v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 01920537 (1992).
