D. PUNITIVE RELIEF

Until recently, the discrimination statutes did not provide for punitive relief. The issue of "punishment" does come into play in a fashion when a personnel action, such as a selection or promotion, is found to be discriminatory and such an action is canceled. The victim of the "punishment" in this case is the original selectee who, in most instances, is an unknowing beneficiary of the discrimination. As discussed more fully below, in the past the Commission eschewed the displacement of the so-called innocent beneficiaries of discrimination. The idea behind this reluctance to displace innocent beneficiaries was that they should not suffer for the wrongs of others. However, in recent years, the Commission reversed that policy and now requires agencies to demonstrate that such displacement would be inappropriate.

As discussed more fully below in the section on "Disciplinary and Corrective Action," the Commission has taken an aggressive posture in reviewing agency decisions to discipline or not discipline offending management officials.

Although the 1991 Civil Rights Act does authorize awards of punitive damages in cases of intentional discrimination, the federal government is exempt from the provision. As discussed more fully below, there is one district court decision holding that the U.S. Postal Service can be liable for punitive damages, but most cases have held to the contrary.

The Commission, even in a case where it finds discrimination, can order training as corrective relief, but not discipline of offenders. It can, if it so chooses, recommend discipline. See, e.g., Sandoval v. Secretary of Army, 07A10102 (2002).

Several proposals have been brought before Congress that would require discipline of managers and supervisors who have been found to have engaged in discrimination. However, the proposals raise serious due process questions for those managers and supervisors because they are not parties to the underlying complaints of discrimination brought against the agency. The Office of Special Counsel does have the authority to bring actions against officials who engage in discrimination on the basis that such discrimination constitutes a prohibited personnel practice. See 5 USC 2302(b). For a detailed discussion of the Office of Special Counsel, see Peter Broida, A Guide To Merit Systems Protection Board Law & Practice (Dewey Publications, Inc. 2023).
