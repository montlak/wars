A. BASIS OF DISCRIMINATION

Under 29 CFR 1614.106(d), a complainant can amend a complaint as a matter of right at any time before the request for a hearing. After the request for a hearing, a motion seeking leave to amend the complaint must be filed with the administrative judge assigned to the case.

The Commission has long considered an amendment to be proper procedure when it relates to the basis of discrimination and the amendment occurred prior to the investigation of the complaint. Wade v. Department of Labor, 01830303 (1984).

The complainant must allege some prohibited basis for believing she has been subject to discrimination. Failure to allege a basis is adequate grounds for dismissing the complaint as not within the EEO purview. See Fields v. USPS, 01831818 (1983). However, it is not required that the employee actually designate a basis delineated in the Statute. The factual statement concerning the complaint is the "crucial element." An employee who alleges that his performance evaluations declined after an on-the-job injury has alleged a sufficient basis for a claim of disability discrimination, and his failure to allege the legal conclusion is not grounds for dismissal of the complaint. See Teske v. USPS, 01831651 (1985).

The Commission regards the charging party's failure to allege the proper bases of discrimination as placing an incorrect legal conclusion on the factual allegations of the complaint. As such, the failure is a technical defect and not grounds for disallowing the amendment. See Burt v. Department of Air Force, 01831457 (1983). But the Commission did not allow amendment of a complaint on appeal where the employee was represented by counsel and made no effort to amend the complaint prior to the close of the hearing. See McMenimen v. USPS, 01841721 (1986). The Commission found that amendment of the complaint to include the basis of disability discrimination would have deprived the agency of any opportunity to demonstrate reasonable accommodation efforts. The continued viability of McMenimen is undermined by some of the cases discussed below.

Conversely, the Commission will allow an amendment of the basis to include reprisal on appeal where the appellant, who was unrepresented, continually referred to reprisal during the administrative processing of his complaint. The agency never specifically dismissed the reprisal allegation and, in failing to do so, did not advise appellant of his right to appeal its implicit dismissal of the allegation. See Thornhill v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01892899 (1989). Similarly, in Schmaltz v. Secretary of Interior, 01970883 (1997), the Commission remanded the complaint to allow the complainant to add age as a basis after an appeal of the dismissal of his reprisal allegations. See also Complainant v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120151481 (2015) (allowing amendment of age discrimination claim at appeal stage).

There are cases in which the Commission will essentially amend a complaint, sua sponte. When the record suggests a basis of discrimination that the complainant did not allege, it is appropriate for the Commission to remand the case for a supplemental investigation. For example, in Willingham v. Postmaster General, 01954651 (1997), the Commission found that, although the complaint only alleged race discrimination, the record indicated that the Commission should have also investigated discrimination based on disability and the combined bases of race and disability. The Commission in Willingham remanded the case to the agency to conduct an investigation including those bases:

The Commission finds that although the appellant alleged discrimination based on his race/color, the record discloses that the agency may have removed the appellant from agency employment based on his having a record of disability or on the combination of the appellant's race and record of disability. Both the proposing official and the deciding official indicated in their memoranda of March 3, and March 11, 1994, that their removal recommendations were based on the results of the fitness-for-duty examination. The only documentation of that examination which is included in the record indicates that the fitness-for-duty physician found the appellant medically unqualified to perform essential functions of his position based on clinical evidence of ongoing psychosis and history of drug abuse. The fitness-for-duty physician specifically indicated in his note of July 22, 1994, that he found no evidence on February 16, 1994, that the appellant was using drugs or that he had used drugs after he was discharged from the hospital program in November 1993. The only indication of possible psychosis mentioned in the physician's note was the appellant's history of hearing voices.

Given this evidence, the agency should have investigated and issued a determination in its final agency decision as to whether the appellant was removed from agency employment because of his disability (record of drug addiction and record of hearing voices). See 29 CFR § 1630.2(g)(2). The agency also should have placed in the record all the evidence upon which it relied in determining that the appellant posed a direct threat to the health or safety of himself or others in the workplace. See 29 CFR § 1630.2(r) and § 1630.15(b)(2). Therefore, the Commission vacates the final agency decision and remands the complaint for a supplemental investigation to determine whether the agency removed the appellant from agency employment based on his record of disability or on the combination of his race and his record of disability.

See also Morgan v. Postmaster General, 01963083 (1997).

In Owens v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A10081 (2002), the Commission sua sponte amended a complaint under Title VII to include a claim under the Equal Pay Act. The Commission affirmed the agency's final decision that the complainant had not demonstrated sex discrimination under Title VII in keeping her in a lower-graded position than employees performing similar duties. However, noting that "the agency may very well have committed a form of sex discrimination by violating the EPA...," the Commission remanded the case for investigation of that claim. Id.

But the Commission has held that a complaint alleging disparate treatment on a prohibited basis may not be amended to include the allegation that the action was taken in reprisal for protected activity. See Quirindongo v. USPS, 01841750 (1986). "The fundamental question in such a case is whether the agency has treated the appellant less favorably than similarly situated employees.... The focus of a reprisal complaint is not on disparity of treatment, but rather on whether adverse action was taken against the appellant because of his participation in protected activity." Id.

On the other hand, the Commission has found that the agency was not prejudiced by a last minute decision of the administrative judge to include a reprisal allegation where it was the EEO investigator who first discovered evidence of reprisal during the course of his investigation. The Commission found the agency was not placed at a disadvantage because evidence of the incident was presented entirely by available witnesses and the allegation did not require the presentation of any documentary evidence. See Robinson v. Secretary of Treasury, 05910633 (1991).

In Whitacre v. Secretary of Navy, 01995803 (2001), the Commission made the broad proclamation that "a complainant may allege discrimination on all applicable bases, including sex, race, national origin, color, religion, age, disability, and reprisal, and may amend his or her complaint at any time to add or delete bases without changing the identity of the claim." (Emphasis added.) As a result, the complainant was allowed to amend a complaint of sex-based hostile environment harassment to include a claim of reprisal.

Still, the practice remains that the Commission will exercise some discretion in permitting amendments. For example, in Rubiola v. Attorney General, 0120073986 (2010), the Commission declined to permit the complainant to amend her pregnancy discrimination complaint to include race as a basis where she "previously had been afforded notice on several occasions that he complaint was considered to be strictly a claim of sex discrimination" and made no attempt to amend the complaint.
