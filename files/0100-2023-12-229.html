6. Precomplaint Counseling

The agency need not give the employee lengthy extensions prior to dismissing a complaint for failure to prosecute. In Bond v. USPS, 01831872 (1983), the appellant requested a meeting with the counselor be postponed one week due the unavailability of his representative. The meeting was scheduled for one week later and the appellant appeared and refused to proceed because his representative was still unavailable. The EEOC found the agency's dismissal of the complaint was justified where it had notified the representative of the rescheduled meeting by certified mail and the representative had not contacted the agency to request further postponement or explain his absence. An agency may not dismiss a complaint when its attempts to contact the appellant are unsuccessful it knows the complainant is represented, but makes no attempt to contact the representative. See Williams v. USPS, 01830194 (1983).

The agency erred in dismissing a complaint where the employee stated on several occasions that sought-after documentation had been provided to the EEO counselor. Although the counselor made reference to these documents, they were not included in the counselor's report. See Cansler v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 01832526 (1984). When the requested clarifying information is included in the counselor's report, it is error for the agency to dismiss the complaint because the employee fails to submit a personal statement containing the same information. See Polivka v. Department of Navy, 01830263 (1983).

In a decision that would appear to make little sense, the Commission held that a failure to cooperate in the counseling stage is "cured" by the subsequent issuance of a notice of final interview and the filing of a formal complaint. In Potter v. Postmaster General, 01953761 (1996), the Commission noted:

As the agency's final decision correctly recognizes, appellant failed to be cooperative with the agency's EEO counseling proceedings concerning her discrimination claim. However, we note that after its unsuccessful attempts to resolve the matter and/or to conduct EEO counseling by mail on several occasions, the agency voluntarily decided to conclude EEO counseling, and to issue a notice of final interview to appellant, providing her rights to file a formal complaint. We find that the agency's issuance of the notice of final interview and appellant's subsequent timely filing of her formal complaint have adequately cured appellant's previous improper conduct during her EEO counseling. See Connolly v. Papachristid Shipping Ltd., et al., 504 F.2d 917 (5th Cir. 1974). It is noted that appellant's timely filing of a formal complaint has triggered the agency's duty to initiate an investigation of the matter, rather than dismissing it for failure to cooperate....

Calling into question earlier decisions concerning dismissal of a complaint for failure to cooperate during the precomplaint stage, in Del Sol v. Postmaster General, 01964943 (1997), the Commission held that the reference in 1614.107(a)(7) to a complainant's failure to respond to a request for information only arises after a complaint is filed. In Del Sol, the Commission held:

Any dismissal...must concern the action of a "complainant." At the time of the March 7 and 25, 1996 letters appellant had not yet filed a complaint and was therefore not yet a complainant. The Commission finds that the agency's dismissal...was improper.

The Commission recognizes that the complaint is vague and can not be investigated without clarification. The agency should contact appellant to clarify this complaint. If appellant then refuses to cooperate in the clarification of his complaint, then the agency should dismiss the complaint on the grounds that appellant failed to cooperate.
