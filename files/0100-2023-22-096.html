5. Practices Requiring Accommodation

An agency is not required to accommodate all of an employee's religious practices. An agency is required only to accommodate those practices that are "compelled by a person's belief in the tenets of a religion." Scott v. Postmaster General, 01A43637 (2005). In Scott, the Commission found that the agency did not fail to accommodate the employee's religious practices where it denied leave to attend a religious conference. The Commission based its decision on the fact that attendance at such a conference is an "optional" religious practice and, therefore, the agency was not required to accommodate it.

The fact that a complainant's religious belief is contrary to the practices of an established religion that he practices does not preclude it from being a bona fide religious belief. In Bullock v. Postmaster General, 07A40101 (2005), the Commission found that the complainant had a bona fide religious belief that the Sabbath was on Saturday, even though his avowed religion recognized Sunday as the Sabbath. The Commission in Bullock found:

According to complainant, sometime in May 2000, he became convinced that Saturday was the proper day on which the Sabbath should be observed. Complainant testified he came to this conclusion after reading the bible and praying. Specifically, complainant testifies it was Chapters 23 and 24 in the book of Luke which complainant testified as saying:

It was preparation day. And the Sabbath was about to begin. The women who had come with Jesus from Galilee followed Joseph and found the tomb how his body was laid in it. Then they went home and prepared spices and perfumes, but they rested on the Sabbath Day in obedience to the Commandment...On the first day of the week, very early in the morning, the women took the spices they had prepared and went to the tomb...Why do you look for the living among the dead? He is not here. He has risen. Remember how he told you why he was still with you in Galilee, the son of man must be delivered into the hands of sinful men, but be crucified on the third day and live again. Then they remember his word.

Complainant reasons that since Easter is on Sunday, and Easter is the day Jesus rose, and the bible indicates that Jesus rose the first day of the week, the day after Sabbath, then Saturday, is the Sabbath.

....

First, we find complainant has established a prima facie case. There is substantial evidence to support the AJ's finding that complainant has a bona fide religious belief that Saturday is the Sabbath after reading the bible, praying, and talking to others with similar beliefs....

The Commission also rejected the agency's argument that it would be an undue hardship to accommodate the complainant by allowing him to take off Saturdays.

In terms of the religious practices that an agency must accommodate, the Commission has moved away from earlier case law holding that an agency is required to accommodate only those practices and beliefs that represent the fundamental tenets of the religion. However, the standard that replaces that used in earlier case law is less than clear. In contrast to the Scott case, in Brown v. Postmaster General, 01A50280 (2005), the Commission found that the agency denied the complainant religious accommodation by not allowing him to wear a kufi because of an agency policy that prohibited employees from using headwear, such as caps, and "do-rags." The complainant, a Muslim, conceded that wearing the kufi was not required by his religion, but testified that he had a personal bona fide belief that doing so was following the ways of the prophet Mohammad. The Commission specifically found that the agency was incorrect in asserting it was required to accommodate only those religious beliefs and practices that constituted a fundamental tenet of the religion.

Also, in contrast to Scott, in Yau v. Postmaster General, 07A50063 (2006), the Commission found that the agency failed to accommodate the complainant's beliefs when it denied his requests for leave to attend a two--week training conference for Buddhist Temple service men. The Commission found that the training was required for the complainant to maintain his status as a temple service man. See also section on "Optional Religious Practices," below.

Title VII did not require the agency to allow the complainant to leave work three and a half hours prior to her religious meeting in Shellie T. v. Veterans Affairs, 2020000771 (2021). Complainant alleged that a change in her work hours prevented her from attending her weekly religious meetings because she could no longer go home between work and church. Finding no legitimate reason why she would need to go home, the Commission concluded that the complainant did not establish a prima facie case:

The Supreme Court has found that accommodations which create more than de minimis monetary or efficiency costs cause undue hardship. Trans World Airlines, Inc. v. Hardison, 432 U.S. 63 (1977). A showing of undue hardship cannot be merely hypothetical, but must instead include evidence of an actual imposition on coworkers or disruption of work schedules or routines. Tooley v. Martin Marietta, 648 F.2d 519, 521 (4th Cir. 1987).

Applying this analysis to the instant case, we find that the AJ properly found that Complainant did not establish a prima facie case of religion discrimination. Complainant has not shown that the Agency wrongfully failed to reasonably accommodate her religious beliefs. In so doing, we note the salient undisputed facts addressed by the AJ. To wit, Complainant failed to adequately explain why she had to leave work by 3:30 P.M. to join the congregation three and one-half hours later, at 7:00 P.M. Moreover, we note that Complainant's minister stated in a statement that, "our meeting[s] are on Thursday at 7:30 P.M." The AJ was unpersuaded by Complainant's assertion that she had to go home prior to the religious meeting, instead of going directly to the meeting from the work site. Finally, like the AJ, we note that Complainant conceded that the Agency was approving leave when she requested it to depart one half-hour early to attend her religious meetings, which already constituted an accommodation to her religious practices. After careful consideration of the evidence of record, we concur that Complainant has not established that she was denied religious accommodation in violation of Title VII.
