2. Drivers

In Holmes v. Postmaster General, 01977073 (2000), the Commission applied the rule in Selix to a case involving a driver with sleep apnea. The Commission rejected the agency's subjective evaluation that the complainant's disability posed a safety threat because there was no individualized assessment of that threat. The Commission in Holmes found:

A determination as to whether an individual poses such a risk cannot be based on an employer's subjective evaluation or, except in cases of most apparent nature, merely on medical reports. See Selix v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01970153 (March 16, 2000). Rather, it must be based on an individualized assessment of the individual that takes into account: (1) the duration of the risk; (2) the nature and severity of the potential harm; (3) the likelihood that the potential harm will occur; and (4) the imminence of the potential harm. 29 CFR § 1630.2(r). In other words, the employer must gather and base its decision on substantial information regarding the individual's work and medical history. See Selix, supra.

The agency did not establish that complainant poses a direct threat in the case at hand. Simply on the ground that complainant was diagnosed with sleep apnea, the agency's doctor placed a medical hold on complainant, noting that sleep apnea "was known to be associated with an increased risk of motor vehicle accidents due to impaired alertness or to falling asleep at the wheel." The agency did not observe complainant acting in an unsafe manner at any point during his employment. Moreover, the agency did not have any documentation indicating complainant had problems at prior jobs due to sleep apnea, nor any medical evidence to establish that complainant should be restricted from performing any kind of activity. Instead, the agency doctor, relying solely on his general knowledge of sleep apnea and medical reports which indicated that complainant, at times, suffered from day time sleepiness, inferred that complainant would be a danger if driving. While the agency told complainant that he would be placed on medical hold pending further medical information, it subsequently removed him without waiting to receive this evidence or conducting any individualized assessment. Furthermore, medical evidence in the record indicates that complainant's apnea was under control through the use of hormones and a machine to help him breathe at night and that a sleep evaluation in December 1996 showed no evidence of significant apneas. In November of 1995 and again in April of 1996, doctors indicated that complainant could work with no restrictions, as long as he continued to keep the doctors updated on his condition. Given this evidence, we find that the agency failed to prove that complainant was a direct threat as defined by Commission regulations.

Where the agency twice observed the complainant in a trancelike state, it was justified in asking for medical documentation that he was able to drive without being a risk to himself or others. Ison v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01A60235 (2006). Similarly, in Cashdollar v. Postmaster General, 01A50828 (2006), the Commission found that the agency was justified in suspending the complainant's duties as a driver pending a fitness--for--duty examination where the complainant told his supervisor that he suffered from anxiety and panic attacks, heard voices and that his medications had adverse side effects. The Commission found that it was reasonable for the agency to suspend the complainant's driving privileges even before examination was conducted since, under the circumstances, allowing him to continue driving posed a direct threat to the complainant or others. Similarly, the Commission upheld an agency's direct threat defense where the agency removed the complainant, a mail carrier, from the workplace and required her to obtain a medical clearance from her psychiatrist after she admitted to attempting to collide with another car while driving a Postal Service vehicle, but "did not get there in time." Kesha Y. v. Postmaster General, 0120140713 (2016).

By contrast, in Padron v. Postmaster General, 01A53132 (2006), the Commission found that the agency did not have a reasonable belief that the complainant was a direct threat to herself or others where it denied her a driving assignment based solely on her hearing impairment without conducting an individualized assessment of her condition.

In Durboraw v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01970594 (1999), the Commission found there was a substantial risk of harm for an appraiser who was required to inspect properties in northern Texas where his condition left him susceptible to spasms that were unpredictable and had caused one accident in the past. Driving comprised some four to five hours of the complainant's time per day and, thus, was an essential function of his position.
