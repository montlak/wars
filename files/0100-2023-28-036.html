2. Conditional Certification and Modification of Certification

The administrative judge may make his or her certification of a class conditional, and/or may modify a certification once it has issued. MD-110 at 8-6 explains:

When appropriate, the Administrative Judge may decide to certify a class conditionally, for a reasonable period of time, until a complainant finds representation. For example, if the record on a class complaint satisfies the numerosity, typicality, and commonality requirements for class certification, the Administrative Judge may "conditionally" certify the class for a reasonable period of time so that the class agent may secure adequate representation. Administrative Judges should refer complainants to any attorney referral systems that may be operating in EEOC district offices or other attorney referral services for assistance in obtaining adequate legal representation.

Even after a class is certified, the Administrative Judge remains free to modify the certification order or dismiss the class complaint in light of subsequent developments. See General Telephone Co. v. Falcon, 457 U.S. 147, 160 (1982). The Administrative Judge has the authority, in response to a party's motion or on his/her own motion, to redefine a class, subdivide it, or dismiss it if the Administrative Judge determines that there is no longer a basis for the complaint to proceed as a class complaint. Hines v. Department of the Air Force, EEOC Request No. 05940917 (January 29, 1996).

See also 29 CFR 1614.204(d)(6). This is consistent with Fed. R. Civ. P. 23(c)(1), which provides that a district court may alter its certification order any time before a decision on the merits. See, e.g., Forehand, et al. v. Florida State Hospital, 89 F.3d 1562 (11th Cir. 1996).

In Geter v. Federal Energy Regulatory Commission, No. 01903145 (1990), the Commission found it was proper to decertify the class, at the request of the class agent, where the class had dwindled over years of processing from a potential 150 members to nine members:

In light of appellant's explicit consent, we concur with the AJ's recommendation for decertification of the class in this case. Caselaw makes it clear that we have the authority, even after a certification order has been entered in a class complaint, to modify it in light of subsequent developments in the litigation of the complaint. General Telephone Co. v. Falcon, 457 U.S. 147, 160 (1982); Nan Hancock v. Treasury, EEOC Request No. 05880996 (October 18, 1988). In this case, it is clear that consolidation of the nine individual complaints remaining will not be impractical, and we will grant appellant's request, as class agent, to decertify the class and allow the nine remaining class members to proceed with the processing of their individual complaints.

As noted above, certification of the class is a decision that is subject to alteration as the case proceeds and more facts are revealed. As the Commission explained in Dunbar v. Social Security Administration, 01975435 (1998):

Regarding the class requirements set forth in § 1614.204(a)(2), we note that our decision takes into account the class agents' limited access to precertification discovery. Hines, Jr., et al., EEOC Request No. 05940917.

Furthermore, the following explanation provided in Hines is appropriate in the instant matter:

Our regulations covering the period after acceptance, however at 29 CFR Section 1614.204(f) not only provide for discovery, but give the [administrative judge] discretion to "direct that an investigation of facts relevant to the complaint or any portion be conducted by an agency..." The [administrative judge] has the latitude to redefine a class, subdivide it or recommend dismissal if it is discovered that there is no longer a basis to proceed as a class complaint.

In Ponce v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120073347 (2010), the Commission found that outright denial of certification was improper where only failure of the class was to establish adequacy of representation. The Commission found that denial of certification was premature and the class agent should be given the opportunity to secure adequate representation. But, the conditional certification is only for a reasonable period of time to secure adequate representation. See Clayton v. Postmaster General, 0720120022 (2013) (admonishing complainant for not responding to appeal and securing adequate representation after conditional certification).
