g. Filing Request With the Incorrect Entity or Office

Where the complainant mistakenly sent her request for a hearing to the agency instead of the EEOC, the Commission held the complainant was entitled to a hearing. In Williams v. Postmaster General, 01A30078 (2003), the complainant's attorney sent the request for a hearing to the agency. Though there was no indication the request was ever filed with the proper EEOC district office, the agency was on notice of the hearing request and the case was remanded for a hearing. A similar result was reached in Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120142709 (2015):

Regarding the issue of whether Complainant properly requested a hearing, Complainant, on appeal, makes repeated reference to various purported errors by two named employees from the Agency's Missouri and Colorado facilities not processing his request for a hearing. In support of his claim, Complainant has submitted a copy of an April 7, 2014 request for a hearing purportedly submitted by him and a "Designation of Agency Representative" form, dated July 16, 2014, and filed by the Agency with the Hearings Unit of the St. Louis District Office and served on Complainant's address of record. Complainant argues; "this is proof that the United States Postal Service knew that I had requested a hearing. They have representation with their agency representation. Why else would they want additional notices etc?"

We find that the record in this case contains sufficient evidence for us to determine that it is more likely than not that Complainant timely requested a hearing and that the request was somehow misplaced by the Agency. Complainant has provided detailed names and telephone numbers of Agency officials who purportedly assured him that a hearing request was being processed. In response to the appeal, the Agency has not provided any statements from these named Agency officials identified by Complainant denying his contentions. Moreover, the Agency has provided no explanation for why it filed a designation of representative form with the EEOC's hearing unit in St. Louis in this matter. Given the specific circumstances of this case, we find that the Agency's final decision should be vacated and the complaint remanded for a hearing as directed below.

The Commission in Lashell S. v. Postmaster General, 0520160517 (2016), reopened a case on its own motion, vacating an administrative judge's dismissal for the complainant's failure to comply with the requirement under 29 CFR. 1614.108(h) that hearing requests be filed with the EEOC office. The complainant in Lashell S. filed her request for a hearing with the agency, and the agency forwarded the timely request to the appropriate EEOC office. Under those circumstances, the Commission held:

[W]e disagree with the AJ and find that Complainant's hearing request was valid and should have been docketed and processed by the Hearings Unit.

EEOC Regulation 29 C.F.R. § 1614.108(h) provides that "the complainant may request a hearing by submitting a written request for a hearing directly to the EEOC office indicated in the agency's acknowledgment letter. The complainant shall send a copy of the request for a hearing to the agency EEO office." We note that prior to changes to EEOC regulations in 1999, hearing requests were sent only to the agency, which then forwarded them to the appropriate EEOC hearings unit. When publishing the final rule change in 1999, the Commission explained that the purpose of the change to having the requests sent directly to the EEOC hearings units was "to eliminate delays." Federal Register, Vol. 64, No. 132 (July 12, 1999), 37649. The Commission added, "If any agency receives a request for a hearing that has not also been submitted to EEOC, the agency should forward the request along with the file to EEOC...." Id.

Here, Complainant timely filed her hearing request with the Agency, which promptly forwarded it to the appropriate hearings unit, as anticipated by our regulations. In fact, the record establishes that the hearing request was forwarded to the New Orleans Hearing Unit well within the 30-day period for requesting a hearing. Therefore, as delay here was not an issue, no regulatory purpose was served by the AJ denying the hearing request.

In Jennifer K. v. Secretary of Navy, 0120171630 (2017), the Commission reversed an administrative judge's hearing request dismissal as a sanction against the complainant for "untimely filing and blatant disregard for proper jurisdictional filing." The complainant in that case worked in San Diego, CA, and, through her attorney, filed a request for a hearing with the EEOC's Los Angeles District Office despite the agency's instructions that the request should be filed with the EEOC's Charlotte, NC District Office. Along with the request for hearing, the attorney indicated that the complainant's duty office was in San Diego and that she received all of her assignments from the San Diego office, making Los Angeles the EEOC office with jurisdiction over the geographic location in which the discrimination occurred. The agency objected, and the complaint was transferred to Charlotte. Almost a year and a half after the complainant filed her hearing request, the agency moved for dismissal of her hearing request as a sanction for failure to file her request for a hearing with the proper EEO office, which the administrative judge granted. The Commission in Jennifer K. reversed the sanction, noting that she made a colorable claim for why Los Angeles was the proper office and emphasizing that the agency suffered no harm:

First, we find no evidence that Complainant or her legal counsel violated any order or directive of an EEOC AJ. Rather, Complainant timely filed her hearing request with the Los Angeles hearings unit and presented a colorable argument for why it was the appropriate location for the hearing as Complainant worked in San Diego when the alleged discrimination occurred and asserted that Agency officials in San Diego were, at least in part, responsible for the discrimination. Second, we find no evidence that the Agency was harmed by Complainant's actions. Complainant served the Agency with her request for a hearing and her arguments for why it should be in Los Angeles. The Agency was able to file a timely response to her arguments and present its views that Charlotte was the more appropriate venue. The Los Angeles hearings unit was persuaded by the Agency's arguments and transferred the case to Charlotte. Third, if the ensuing delays resulted in any harm in this case, it was to Complainant and not the Agency. We note that the Agency waited about five months after the case had been transferred to Charlotte to file its motion for sanctions. While Complainant's counsel continued to protest the transfer to Charlotte during some of this period, there is no indication that the Agency incurred any harm as a result or that there has been any demonstrated negative effect on the hearings process.
