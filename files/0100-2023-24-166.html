HH. WORK HOUR MODIFICATIONS

The agency's first obligation is to provide an accommodation that allows an employee to put in a full work day. In Grabon v. USPS, 01841542 (1986), the agency responded to an employee's request for accommodation of a knee condition by reassignment to a position that required standing no more than six hours per day by placing the employee on a six-hour work day. The Commission found that the employee was not restricted to a six-hour work day and had identified several positions that would not require him to stand for the majority of the day. The agency offered no explanation for the failure to accommodate the employee with a full work day, instead arguing that its reduction in the employee's hours constituted a reasonable accommodation. The Commission ordered the agency to offer the employee reasonable accommodation and to pay the employee for the time lost as a result of the six-hour accommodation.

In explaining modified schedules, the Commission, in its Enforcement Guidance: Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under the ADA, "Types of Reasonable Accommodation Related to Job Performance" at Question 22, notes:

A modified schedule may involve adjusting arrival or departure times, providing periodic breaks, altering when certain functions are performed, allowing an employee to use accrued paid leave, or providing additional unpaid leave. An employer must provide a modified or part-time schedule when required as a reasonable accommodation, absent undue hardship, even if it does not provide such schedules for other employees.

In addition, if work hour modifications impose an undue hardship on the agency, it must consider reassignment to a position that would permit such modifications. Id. at Question 24. Finally:

In some instances, an employer's refusal to modify a workplace policy, such as a leave or attendance policy, could constitute disparate treatment as well as a failure to provide a reasonable accommodation. For example, an employer may have a policy requiring employees to notify supervisors before 9:00 A.M. if they are unable to report to work. If an employer would excuse an employee from complying with this policy because of emergency hospitalization due to a car accident, then the employer must do the same thing when the emergency hospitalization is due to a disability.

Id.

In Yager v. Department of Army, 01873318 (1988), the Commission found that the agency's failure to fill appellant's position created an inference of disability discrimination where the employee sought a return to work on a part-time basis as an accommodation. The Commission noted that "it can readily be inferred that having the benefit of appellant's services part-time would have been better than not having them at all and that providing appellant with such part-time work would not have placed an unreasonable burden on the agency." Id.

A complainant's work hours can be an essential function of a position. For example, in Reilly v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A22449 (2003), the Commission found the complainant's presence during normal business hours was an essential function of her position where she was required to be a mentor to other employees, hold hearings for veterans and other claimants and act as a resource to outside groups. "The agency has shown that the time of day during which complainant performs the essential functions of her position is a critical element of her job, and, therefore, it is not required to provide the reasonable accommodation of a modification of complainant's work schedule." Id.
