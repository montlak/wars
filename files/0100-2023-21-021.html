2. Agency's Burden

Intertwined with the complainant's required showing in a hostile environment case is the agency's burden of proof. It is perhaps here that the attempt to treat hostile environment as a subcategory of the disparate treatment theory of discrimination most goes astray.

In one case, the Commission held that because the employee's burden of establishing a prima facie case of sexual harassment is more difficult than for other forms of discrimination, the agency must rebut the case by "clear and convincing evidence." Ciechon v. Postmaster General, 01871518 (1988) (citing Bundy v. Jackson, 641 F.2d 934 (D.C. Cir. 1981)). Under Ciechon, if the agency successfully rebutted the employee's prima facie case, the burden shifted back to the employee to demonstrate pretext. However, see Butler v. Postmaster General, 01882521 (1988), where the Commission strictly applied the McDonnell Douglas analysis requiring only that the agency carry the burden of articulating a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its action.

A far better statement of the agency's burden was set forth in Quinn v. Postmaster General, 05900546 (1990). In Quinn, the Commission identified five bases for rebutting a prima facie showing of hostile environment harassment:

To avoid liability for hostile environment sexual harassment, an agency must show that: (1) the acts/conduct complained of did not occur; (2) the conduct complained of was not "unwelcome"; (3) the alleged harassment was not "sufficiently severe or pervasive" to alter the condition's of the victim's employment and create an abusive working environment; (4) immediate and appropriate corrective action was taken as soon as the employer was put on notice; and/or (5) there is no basis for imputing liability to the employer under agency principles.

There is one element missing from the Quinn formulation; namely, that the complainant must show that the conduct was based on protected status.

Courts and the Commission have struggled with defining the agency's burden of proof in hostile environment cases. The true burden of proof placed upon an agency, in reality, lies somewhere between the two extremes enunciated in Ciechon and Butler--i.e., between clear and convincing evidence and merely articulating a reason. The problem here is that many courts, as well as the Commission, have tried to analyze cases of hostile environment harassment using the McDonnell Douglas formula. However, McDonnell Douglas only applies to cases involving circumstantial evidence. The vast majority of hostile environment cases involve direct evidence. The employee, and possibly others, present direct evidence of unwelcome sexual comments or conduct. In McDonnell Douglas terms, the issues are not whether the agency can advance a legitimate reason for such comments or conduct and whether the employee can show those reasons are pretextual. There simply is no legitimate reason for engaging in sexual harassment. Rather, the issues in hostile environment cases are whether the employee's testimony is believable--i.e., did the comments or conduct occur and was it unwelcome--and, if so, whether a reasonable person would have found the comments or conduct offensive, hostile, or abusive, and whether the complainant actually found the comments or conduct to be so. It makes no sense to analyze hostile environment cases using McDonnell Douglas. The correct formulation of the agency's burden is whether it can prove any of the five elements set forth in Quinn by a preponderance of the evidence.

Although it makes no sense to attempt to analyze hostile environment cases within the overall framework of McDonnell Douglas, there are two situations where the analysis does come into play in such cases. The McDonnell Douglas framework is useful in the determination of whether the harassing conduct is based on a protected characteristic of the complainant when the conduct itself does not reveal that intent. See below, section on "Nature of Conduct." The McDonnell Douglas analysis is also used in cases where the incidents of alleged harassment include tangible employment actions. In such cases, it is necessary to examine the proffered legitimate reasons for those actions, as well as whether those reasons are pretextual. See below, section on "Use of Tangible Employment Actions."

The first three elements in Quinn actually address the issue of whether a hostile environment was created. The fourth and fifth elements address the issue of whether the agency should be held liable for that environment. The two issues are distinct. Obviously, if the complainant fails to prove a hostile environment existed, there is no reason to further analyze the agency's liability. Merely proving the existence of a hostile environment, however, does not address the question of the agency's liability. For example, where the harassment is engaged in by a coworker and the agency has no knowledge of the harassment, or is not in a position where it should have been aware of the harassment, there is no basis for imputing liability for the hostile environment to the agency.

Although the agency's obligation to provide prompt and appropriate remedial action in response to a complaint of sexual harassment is discussed in detail in the section on Liability for Harassment, below, it is worth stressing here that the Commission has become vigorous in requiring scrutiny of an agency's defense of hostile environment cases on the basis that it took prompt and appropriate action. The appropriate remedial action that must be taken by an agency is dependent upon the facts of the case. The mere transfer of the supervisor on whose watch the harassment occurred may not be sufficient. As the Commission explained in Finlay v. Postmaster General, 01924406 (1992):

What is appropriate remedial action in sexual harassment cases often depends on the particular facts of the case, the severity and persistence of the harassment, and the effectiveness of any initial remedial steps. See Bundy v. Jackson, 641 F.2d 934, 947 (D.C. Cir. 1981). However, generally speaking, when sexual harassment is proven, the resulting remedial action required must include all corrective measures necessary to end the harassment, to make the victims whole by restoring any lost employment benefits or opportunities, and to ensure that harassment does not occur in the future. See Hierholzer v. Department of the Air Force, EEOC Request No. 05910118 (April 19, 1991).

Here, the agency's curative measures included sensitivity training for employees and supervisors and the reassignment of appellant's supervisor, as well as a commitment by the agency to provide a non-hostile working environment for the complainant and other employees. Appellant has asserted that the relief proposed by the agency was inadequate, in part, because the hostile work environment she has endured cannot be alleviated with a one and one-half hour sensitivity training session. Commission policy requires that a remedy for a discriminatory action should "be tailored to cure or correct the particular source of the identified discrimination and minimize the chance of its recurrence." Appendix A to 29 CFR Part 16B-Policy Statement on Remedies and Relief for Individual Cases of Unlawful Discrimination. This is particularly true in cases such as appellant's, which concern an allegation of sexual harassment based on a hostile work environment. Given the particular circumstances of appellant's case, we agree with appellant that the agency's settlement offer did not assure appellant that she would not be subjected to more harassment by her male co-workers when she returns to work for the agency. We note that the agency's initial efforts to curb the harassment of appellant were apparently unsuccessful. The Commission recently held that when co-worker harassment is at issue, an offer of full relief should contain the corrective, curative, or preventative action necessary to ensure that co-workers will not harass the complainant in the future. See Ryan v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 05920484 (July 10, 1992). In this regard, while a supervisor has the utmost responsibility to provide a non-hostile working environment, the transfer of a supervisor who has neglected this responsibility does not always ensure that an individual who has been the victim of co-worker harassment will be protected from such harassment in the future.

Additionally, we advise the agency that an assurance that no future reprisal actions will be taken against an appellant is vital to an offer of full relief. See Harigel v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05910454 (May 16, 1991). There is no indication that appellant was offered such assurance in the agency's offer of full relief. Finally, given the continuing nature of the harassment and its severity, the agency could have offered to periodically review the situation with appellant and the primary offenders, if appellant returned to work in the same office where she suffered the harassment.

Appellant also asserted on appeal that the offer of full relief was inadequate because it did not include "reimbursement" for the three months she was out on leave because of the harassment. The record is unclear regarding what type of leave appellant was referring to or what, if any, monetary reimbursement appellant was alleging that she should have received. Because appellant took leave after she filed her formal complaint and while the harassment was allegedly continuing, we find that the agency should have determined if appellant was entitled to any reimbursement of leave, even though appellant did not make any specific request for such relief. (footnote omitted). See Merriel v. Department of Transportation, EEOC Request No. 05890596 (August 10, 1989); see also Whiting v. ACTION, EEOC Request No. 05900093 (June 27, 1990). With regard to any claim that appellant might have for monetary relief, there is no evidence that the agency was aware, when it made its offer of full relief to appellant on July 27, 1992, that appellant sought the remedy of compensatory damages or that the agency had been provided with any evidence that appellant had incurred such damages (i.e., monetary reimbursement for leave taken or doctor's bills incurred because of discrimination). (footnote omitted). Consequently, we do not find that when the agency made its initial offer of full relief to appellant on July 27, 1991, that it should have considered the issue of compensatory damages.

There is no doubt that, as set forth in Hicks, supra, it is the complainant who bears the ultimate burden of persuasion in any discrimination case, including a hostile environment case. But it is a mistake to discuss the agency's burden in terms of a mere burden of producing a legitimate reason. In a hostile environment case, the burden is more squarely on the agency to rebut the complainant's proof rather than simply to deny it.
