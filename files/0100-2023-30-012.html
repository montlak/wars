A. RELIEF FOR APPLICANTS

When a complainant prevails on an allegation that he or she was denied a position with the agency due to unlawful discrimination, the remedial relief must include an offer of the job the applicant was denied, or a "substantially equivalent position," if circumstances warrant. 29 CFR 1614.501(b)(1)(i). The offer of relief must be made to the applicant in writing and the applicant must be notified that he or she has 15 calendar days from receipt of the offer to accept or reject it. Failure to notify the agency of a decision will be considered an election not to accept the offered position, unless the applicant can show that circumstances beyond his or her control prohibited a reply within the prescribed period. Id. The requirement that the complainant respond in 15 days is an important one. For example in Nielsen v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 012013492 (2012), the complainant failed to respond to within the 15 days and argued on appeal that the agency's offer was not unconditional because it was made in an email. The Commission found that the complainant by failing to timely respond was not entitled to the position and would receive back pay only from the time of the discriminatory nonselection up until the agency's offer.

If the offer of employment is accepted, back pay is computed in accordance with 5 CFR 550.805. Back pay accrues from the date the applicant would have started if originally selected until the date the applicant actually starts work for the agency. The applicant also is entitled to credit for the service time that would have accrued if he or she initially was selected. This can be important with respect to further promotions, because often such opportunities are dependent upon a minimum length of service in a lower level position. The time credit does not extend to negate any probationary period the employee otherwise would serve. This means an employee who obtains this type of relief may be entitled to be considered as having served, for example, a year in the position for promotional purposes, but still be on probation for the first year of actual employment if others in the same position are required to serve a similar probationary period. The rationale is simple. The employee is entitled to be placed in the position he or she would have been in but for the discrimination not a better position. Waiving the probationary period would place the employee in a better position by giving him or her more protection under civil service laws than other employees in their first year of actual service.

If the applicant declines the position, he or she still is entitled to back pay for the time period extending from the date of the original selection to the date of the agency's employment offer. The agency must inform the complainant of this right in its offer of employment.

Back pay is limited to two years prior to the date the complaint was filed for complaints involving discrimination prohibited by Title VII or the Rehabilitation Act. 29 CFR 1614.501(b)(3).

An applicant for employment who is found to have been subject to discrimination may be denied the relief outlined above if the agency also finds clear and convincing evidence that the person would not have been selected for the position even absent prohibited discrimination. 29 CFR 1614.501(b)(1)(i)-(ii). The agency still is required to take affirmative steps to ensure the discriminatory practice does not recur in the future. The question immediately arises as to how an agency can be found to have discriminated against a person, yet not discriminated against them in a way that entitles him or her to relief. The easiest explanation is through example. The agency has two applicants for employment--A, who is white, and B, who is black. The selecting official unfairly rates applicant B because of her color and appoints applicant A to the position. Applicant B still can be denied relief if the agency, by clear and convincing evidence, can demonstrate that applicant A was better qualified and would have been selected for the position even if B had been fairly rated. Note, however, that the burden for this defense rests on the agency and that the agency must meet this burden through clear and convincing evidence. Clear and convincing evidence is a recognized burden of proof in civil cases that requires a showing substantially above the preponderance of the evidence standard used to issue a finding of discrimination. The Commission distinguishes these cases from the contemporaneous mixed-motive cases discussed above.

The basic parameters of relief for applicants were summarized by the Commission in Greesman v. Secretary of Army, 01993872 (2002):

As for the relief, we note a job applicant has the right to be free from discrimination throughout the selection process. If the process is discriminatory at any phase, the applicant must be awarded full relief, i.e., the position retroactively, unless the employer shows by clear and convincing evidence that even in the absence of discrimination, the applicant would not have been selected. See Pryor v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05980405 (August 6, 1999). Placing such an onerous burden on the employer is proper, inasmuch as the employer's unlawful acts caused the difficulty in determining what would have resulted if there had been no discrimination. See id. Thus, in a case such as this one, complainant is entitled to retroactive promotion, "unless clear and convincing evidence contained in the record" demonstrates that, even absent discrimination, complainant would not have been selected for the position she desired. See id; see also 29 C.F.R. 1614.501(c)(3); see Day v. Mathews, 530 F.2d 1083 (D.C. Cir. 1976).

An agency may not escape hiring an applicant for employment who has been the victim of discrimination by arguing that it was the ranking process and not the selection process that was at fault. See Gray v. Department of Navy, 01872102 (1987). The Commission rejected the agency's argument that because the rating process discriminated against the appellant, relief only could be granted with respect to the rating of the candidates and not the appellant's nonselection. "The interrelation between the discriminatory rating process and the nonselection of the appellant requires that the agency retroactively hire the appellant and award her back pay unless it can produce clear and convincing evidence that it would not have selected her even absent discrimination." Id.

The obligation to make a complainant whole for discrimination is more than just the placement in a position denied. The complainant must actually be allowed to work in that position. See Cotter v. Attorney General, 01934276 (1993). In that case, the agency placed the complainant into a special agent position but refused to issue him a badge, let him qualify to carry a firearm, or work as a special agent. The Commission noted that inherent in a remedy for discrimination is the assumption that the complainant will actually be employed in the position and perform the duties of that position.
