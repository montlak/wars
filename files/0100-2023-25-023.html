4. Rehired Annuitants

Although a rehired annuitant is an employee at will and may be terminated for no reason at all, she may not be terminated for reasons in contravention of the ADEA. Randall v. Department of Health and Human Services, 01821935 (1984). The appellant adequately demonstrated that she was treated differently from younger employees in her work assignments, that the agency had pressured her to retire, and that it had used her rehired annuitant status as an easy means of terminating her employment:

In order to prevail on her claims of disparate treatment, the appellant must show that it is more likely than not that, but for the appellant's age, the agency would not have forced her into retirement. In McDonnell Douglas, supra, the Supreme Court set forth a model for establishing a prima facie case of disparate treatment. Although the elements of a prima facie case outlined in McDonnell Douglas referred to a hiring case, the Court made clear that the elements set forth do not establish an inflexible standard, as "[t]he facts necessarily will vary in [employment discrimination] cases, and specification...of the prima facie proof required from the [appellant] is not necessarily applicable in every respect in differing factual situations." McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, supra, note 31. Therefore, in applying the McDonnell Douglas standards of a prima facie case to the instant fact situation, the appellant, in order to meet this threshold requirement, would have to show that: (1) she was a member of a protected group; (2) she was qualified for the position that she held in her rehired status; (3) she was terminated despite her qualifications; (4) and the position was filled by an individual not of the appellant's class. The Commission, after a review of the record, finds that the appellant has established a prima facie case of age discrimination, i.e., (1) her membership in a protected class (age 69); (2) her qualification for the position (favorable appraisals and awards); (3) her termination; (4) her replacement in the same position by one outside of her protected class.

The Commission does not dispute the contention that as a reemployed annuitant the appellant served at the will of the appointing official. However, this condition is not a license to discriminate against such employees. The authority to terminate at will does not insulate an agency from the prohibition of the Age Discrimination in Employment Act. Therefore, the agency's argument to the effect that they had unquestioned authority to terminate the appellant is misplaced. The agency stressed that the Complaints Examiner did not give any given reason for his determination that the appellant's rehired annuitant status was used as a pretext to terminate the appellant for discriminatory reasons. However, the record shows, to the contrary, that the entire thrust of that portion of the recommended decision devoted to the age discrimination claim was in support of the conclusion that the appellant had in fact suffered discrimination because she was sixty-nine years of age, was viewed as being ripe for retirement, and utilizing her rehired annuitant status was a convenient way of terminating her.
