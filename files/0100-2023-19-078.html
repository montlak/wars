E. STATISTICAL EVIDENCE

Statistical evidence of disparate treatment can be useful in establishing either a prima facie case of discrimination or pretext. The statistics used must not only reflect a low percentage of the protected group in the positions at issue, but must give some indication of the pool of applicants. However, a complainant in a disparate treatment case cannot establish pretext solely through the use of statistics. Roemer v. Attorney General, 01A13955 (2002).

"In employment discrimination cases, the Commission notes that the composition of the work force, rather than the population as a whole, is the standard to be employed and qualifications may be essential factors when upper level management positions are at issue." Morris v. Defense Logistics Agency, 01880582 (1988) (citing Hazelwood School District v. United States, 433 U.S. 299 (1977),; Metrocare v. Washington Metropolitan Transit Authority, 679 F.2d 922 (D.C. Cir. 1982)). The Commission further explained that "in determining the comparison pool, only the minimum objective qualifications, not subjective qualifications, are relevant." Morris, supra.

In Coppea v. Defense Mapping Agency, 01842674 (1986), the Commission acknowledged that the statistics presented by the complainant reflected a low number of over-40 females in supervisory positions. Although this low number in Coppea would appear to support the appellant's contentions that the agency discriminated against her on the basis of age and sex, the Commission noted:

We do not know, however, whether the under-representation of older women in agency management is due to discrimination or caused by a fewer number of qualified older females applying for these positions. This distinction is critical. In establishing proof of discrimination from statistical evidence, the "required comparison [is] to a qualified pool of employees presumptively eligible for promotion." Pouncy v. Prudential Insurance Co., 668 F.2d 795, 803 (5th Cir. 1982).

The Commission also noted that the agency's entire supervisory work force was not the applicable comparative group, but that the "statistics provided by appellant fail[ed] to be meaningful because they did not compare those who were qualified and sought agency promotions or new positions." Id.

In order for statistical evidence to be relevant to show pretext, it must evaluate similarly situated employees. Although statistical evidence may be combined with other circumstantial evidence to prove pretext, the other evidence does not make the statistics relevant if they do not involve similarly situated employees. In Schultz v. McDonnell Douglas, 105 F.3d 1258, 1259-60 (8th Cir. 1997), the court found that where the statistical evidence did not concern similarly situated employees, other evidence of age animus did not bolster the relevance of the statistics:

At trial [the plaintiff] attempted to introduce statistical evidence demonstrating a pattern of age discrimination by McDonnell Douglas. The statistics were based on data taken from the company's Production Operations Division, which consisted of 3,731 employees.

The district court excluded the statistical evidence from the divisional level because it was not based on comparable employees, citing Nitschke v. McDonnell Douglas Corp., 68 F.3d 249, 252 (8th Cir. 1995), and Hutson v. McDonnell Douglas Corp., 63 F.3d 771, 777 (8th Cir. 1995). The statistics dealt with a much larger group of employees than the district court found relevant; they were not comparable in its view because they did not include performance evaluations.

Schultz contends that he had offered additional evidence of age animus at the departmental level to show the reasons offered for discharge were pretextual. He also claims that there was evidence demonstrating the involvement of managers at the higher divisional level in his termination. This would have made the divisional level statistical evidence relevant, and it should have been admitted. He seeks a new trial so that he can present the statistics.

. . .

The district court did not abuse its discretion in excluding the statistical evidence. While it is true that MacDissi v. Valmont Indus. Inc., 856 F.2d 1054 (8th Cir. 1988), indicated that other evidence of discrimination may add to the relevance of statistical evidence, it did not contradict the rule that the statistics must evaluate comparable employees to be meaningful indicators of pretext. 856 F.2d at 1058-59; Hutson, 63 F.3d at 778 (discussing MacDissi). Even if the decision to discharge Schultz had been made with the involvement of division level managers, he has not shown that the 3,731 division employees reflected in the statistics were comparable to him. McDonnell Douglas claimed Schultz was selected for discharge because of his performance ratings, and the statistical evidence did not incorporate the performance ratings of other employees. The probative value of statistical evidence that does not reflect significant differences among employers would be prejudicial and misleading. See Fed. R. Evid. 403.

Even when the comparative group is too small to be statistically significant, it still may provide probative evidence on the issue of discrimination. In Steele v. Department of Treasury, 01822343 (1983), the Commission found that, while the "sample universe" of the work group was too small to be statistically significant, combined with the other evidence of record, it provided an ample demonstration of discrimination on the basis of race and age where all of the work unit supervisors were white and all other accountants in the work unit were under 40. However, the use of statistics failed to show pretext where the comparisons did not support the appellant's allegation that the agency's promotional policies favored black employees, and the oriGINAl selectee was a white male who declined the position. See Stember v. USPS, 01822265 (1983). There are occasions where the work force is so small that no inferences can be drawn from statistical disparities. See Wagner v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01930590 (1993) (no inference was justified in the fact that white representation in the work force declined from five to three).

The probative value of statistics is dependent directly upon the degree of disparity they show and the size of the sample. In order to be persuasive on the issue of pretext, the disparity must be gross. See Wright v. Veterans Administration, 01872285 (1988) (complainant demonstrated that blacks were twice as likely to be reassigned as their white counterparts); Albright v. USPS, 01831549 (1985).

Statistical evidence in disparate treatment cases can be used to provide a background against which evidence of pretext is evaluated. As the Commission demonstrated in Dyson v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 01945757 (1996):

The SO admitted that at the time of the selection there were no black or female managers in the unit and the record demonstrates the lack of females and blacks in management positions in the past. It is against this backdrop that the Commission analyzes the record and the issue of pretext.

The SO's reason for waiting from June 1992 until January 1993 before making the selection, that he wanted to make sure that the unit was in line with the Agency's strategic plan, is inconsistent with his testimony that changes and modifications continued to occur before and after the selection. Record testimony shows that the functions of the position needed to be performed no matter what changes were occasioned by the Agency's strategic plan. The Agency presented no evidence of the specifics of the strategic plan, when it was implemented or what changes it made in the essential functions of the position once implemented. The fact that the selectee was not performing the duties of the position even after his selection as he was continuing his work in quality control, and that the functions of the position were being performed as they were prior to the selection, supports a finding of pretext.

The Agency argues that Appellant was an external candidate at the time of the selection. Therefore, she could not have been selected, due to its policy of selecting external candidates only if there were no qualified internal candidates available. At the time of the announcement and application, Appellant was a qualified internal candidate. By virtue of the Agency's RIF, the termination of her participation in the ETP and the SO's delay in making the selection, Appellant became an external candidate with little, if any, chance of being selected.

The Commission, thus, finds the SO's reason for the delay not worthy of belief, as the Agency gives no plausible reason why the selection could not have been made while Appellant was still a qualified internal candidate. The delay served no purpose except to change Appellant's status from internal to external candidate. The Commission also finds that the SO's incorrect evaluation of Appellant's qualifications supports a finding of discrimination. The Agency admits that Appellant successfully contradicted the SO's evaluation of her qualifications and that she did perform many of the duties expected of the candidate, while the selectee performed none of the specific duties of the position. Considering Appellant's academic credentials and her other qualifications, it is clear that Appellant's qualifications are plainly superior to those of the selectee. In addition, we find the SO's testimony not to be credible as his denial of preselection is substantially outweighed by the testimony of members of his staff and the Agency's own admission that there is evidence of preselection.

Considering the inconsistencies and implausibility of the SO's reasons and the Agency's admissions, against a backdrop of an underrepresentation of blacks and women in management positions in the unit, the Commission concludes that the Agency's proffered reasons for Appellant's non-selection are a pretext for discrimination based on race and sex.

Whether used to establish a prima facie case or pretext, a statistical disparity must be shown to be related to, or a function of, the selection process. Fish v. Department of Housing and Urban Development, 01830745 (1983). The disparity itself is significant only insofar as it can be related causally to a discriminatory motive.

A complainant may not rely solely on statistical evidence to establish that the agency's action was motivated by prohibited discrimination. See Williams v. Veterans Administration, 01850370 (1986). Statistical evidence may be considered with evidence of a general atmosphere of discrimination when proving pretext. See Scott v. Secretary of Defense, 01902727 (1990).
