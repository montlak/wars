3. Behavior Towards the Complainant

Attitudes toward complainants expressed by supervisors also can be indicative of retaliatory motive. In Wiggins v. Postmaster General, 01953715 (1997), the Commission found that the appellant's supervisor's negative attitude about appellant's protected activity was sufficient evidence of retaliatory animus upon which to base a finding of discrimination:

It is undisputed that appellant's supervisor was aware of appellant's prior EEO complaint, which had resulted in a finding of discrimination, at the time he considered appellant's request for advanced leave. Appellant, himself, referred to this previous complaint, and his entitlement to restored leave, in his written request.

Significantly, the supervisor, in his affidavit, responded to a question about his awareness of appellant's prior EEO activity with a description of appellant "making numerous accusations as a constant reminder at St. Pauls that he had filed an EEO and had made money off of it. It was at times a kick in the face to other employees." The supervisor went on to further describe his negative feelings towards appellant by indicating that he "has had a problem working with any manager. He has filed numerous EEO's, grievances, and he has received money for numerous things." In addition, it appears that after the supervisor denied appellant's requests for overtime (allegation no. 1), appellant was instrumental in organizing a picket line on November 30, 1992, outside the post office protesting alleged mistreatment by management.

The supervisor averted that "[t]hrough these actions I was publicly humiliated, and I was pulled out of the St. Pauls Post Office for which I was the potential postmaster."

Considering these expressions of bitterness towards appellant over his prior EEO activity, the Commission finds support for the AJ's finding that the supervisor's retaliatory animus more likely than not motivated him to reject appellant's request for advanced leave out of hand, rather than attempting first to resolve the discrepancy with appellant's doctor's note and the confusion about appellant's leave balance restored to him through his previous EEO complaint. In this case, the Commission finds no significant evidence of record to contradict the AJ's finding of reprisal.

A similar example is provided in Lee v. Postmaster General, 01A51982 (2006), recon. denied, 05A60971 (2006), where the Commission upheld a sua sponte finding of reprisal by its administrative judge:

The AJ found sua sponte, however, that complainant was subjected to per se discrimination under Title VII and the Commission's regulations by interfering with complainant's right to pursue EEO activity when S1 did not allow complainant to see her union representative to report alleged discrimination. The AJ found that in violating complainant's rights to pursue remedies for possible discrimination, the agency's actions constituted retaliatory harassment. Specifically, the AJ found that on September 27, 2000, complainant requested to see her union representative regarding a safety issue and not getting the proper accommodation for her medical condition. The AJ found that after having made two requests, complainant was finally allowed to see a union representative after 3:00 P.M. The AJ found that from September 27, 2000 until June 27, 2001, complainant requested to see her union representative on seven (7) different occasions, only to have S1 deny her requests outright, or purposely delay the meeting until later in the day around 3:00 P.M., knowing that complainant had to leave by 3:30 P.M. for home because of her infant child and would only have about 30 minutes to meet with her union representative. The AJ noted that at the hearing, S1 conceded that she would often put complainant off to see her union representative to discuss EEO violations, or as S1 herself stated "If I'm not in the right frame of mind, I have to push her to the side to keep her from aggravating me." The AJ further noted that S1 exaggerated how often complainant requested to see her union representative, and she concluded that S1 harbored a retaliatory animus toward complainant for seeking redress through the EEO process. The AJ also noted the following statement of S1 at the hearing: "And sometimes it was like [complainant] provoked me. I read those write-ups [that complainant filed with the union]. How can she be doing her work? It looked like every two hours she was going to see what I was doing. Because I read some of her statements. Anybody that can sit and precisely tell what a supervisor is doing every hour on the hour, that makes me wonder when you are doing your work if you're watching me." The AJ concluded that "[i]t is evident through [S1's] testimony that she resented and felt threatened by Complainant's filing grievances against her alleging her discriminatory and harassing conduct." AJ Decision, p. 280 of Hearing Transcript.

The AJ further found that based upon complainant's EEO activity in meeting with her union representative, S1 harassed complainant as follows: by asking her when she would "bid out of the unit"; by publicly reprimanding her for going to Workers' Compensation without authorization; for telling complainant that she was "full of sh-t" and that if complainant reported her using obscenity that S1 threatened that it was going to be "her word against [complainant's]"; by informing other employees that complainant is going to get herself fired; by singling complainant out when she and other employees were three minutes late in returning from lunch; by placing complainant on an off-duty status on May 23, 2001; by pushing a mail cart at complainant; and by rushing complainant in the bathroom while she was in a stall. The AJ found that S1's retaliatory animus was apparent through her testimony. Finally, the AJ found that this conduct interfered with complainant's work performance, and it interfered with her sleeping and her home life as well. The AJ noted that S1 denies that most of the alleged incidents occurred, however, the AJ did not find S1 to be a credible witness. The AJ noted that two unbiased, uninterested parties, confirmed that two of the incidents which S1 denied, occurred as alleged. In sum, the AJ found that complainant proved a claim of retaliatory harassment.

Where a supervisor disclosed the complainant's EEO activity in an email to several employees and actively solicited negative information about the complainant and threatened a "counterclaim," the Commission found this was sufficient to demonstrate a causal connection between the protected activity and an action reasonably likely to dissuade someone from filing or pursuing a complaint. In Schofield v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120082521 (2010), the Commission also found it was retaliation when the supervisor demanded notes that the complainant gave to EEO officials about his complaint and then shared that information with an RMO.

An agency can rebut a showing of pretext by demonstrating that the supervisor treats all employees the same, including those who have not filed EEO complaints. Giles v. Postmaster General, 01A50997 (2006). In that case, the Commission found that although the complainant established she had engaged in protected activity, the supervisor knew of that activity, and treated her harshly, the agency presented testimony from coworkers that the supervisor treated all employees that way.

A case of reprisal is rebutted when the agency produces the testimony of coworkers, stating that the complainant, however, was disgruntled after her nonselection for a position and that her attitude, not her previous EEO complaint, was responsible for creating strained relations between her and her supervisors. See Tartamella v. Department of Health and Human Services, 01830651 (1983). The employee has a right to dispute her nonselection through the EEO process, but does not have a right to allow her own dissatisfaction with her nonselection to affect work performance or relations with her supervisors.
