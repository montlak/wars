B. BEST QUALIFIED LISTS

For competitive positions in the federal government, the general process is to screen all applicants to determine which applicants meet the minimum qualifications for the job. Often, applicants are screened further and separated into those who are "qualified" and those who are "highly qualified" or "best qualified." In many cases, it is only those who make the "best qualified" category that then proceed to the interview stage. There are many individual variations on this theme. In some instances, the official or officials who select the successful candidate from the best qualified list are not the officials who initially screened candidates for placement on the list. In some instances, they are the same. When dealing with promotion or appointment cases, it is important to obtain the applicable regulations of the involved agency to determine precisely the process that was used and that the agency was required to use. The general rule in promotion and appointment cases is that the agency is free to choose among equally qualified applicants absent some evidence showing a discriminatory motive. Beasley v. Department of Transportation, 01873049 (1988).

That the appellant did not make the best qualified list for the position does not prevent him from establishing a prima facie case of discrimination. It is not necessary that the appellant establish he was as well or better qualified than the selectee in order to state a prima facie case. See McCreight v. Department of Navy, 01831420 (1983). Conversely, the fact that appellant made the best qualified list for similar positions in the past is not relevant to his failure to make a best qualified list for the disputed position. "[T]he range of best qualified candidates for a particular position is dependent upon what candidates make application." Weinstein v. USPS, supra.

The mere fact that all candidates met the basic qualifications for the position does not lead inexorably to a conclusion that prohibited discrimination did not motivate the selection. When the agency asserts that it selected the best qualified candidate for a position, the qualifications of the candidates are at issue and should be reviewed. However, as the Commission noted in Bowser v. USPS, 01830780 (1983):

[I]t is not the function of [the] Commission to substitute its judgment for that of a selecting official familiar with the present and future needs of his facility and therefore in a better position to judge the respective merits of each candidate. This is so unless other facts suggest that proscribed consideration entered into the decision making process.

The precaution about deferring to the agency's judgment in the first instance also applies to the administrative judge. See Jackson v. Department of Navy, 01831311 (1983) (evidence showing that one member of the screening panel was friendly with a white male candidate who made the best qualified list was not a sufficient demonstration of pretext in a sexual discrimination case where the panel referred candidates to the selecting official in alphabetical order and there was no further contact between members of the selection panel and the selecting official). If the record does not contain sufficient evidence to determine if the highest ranked candidate was better qualified than the complainant, the Commission will remand for the taking of additional evidence. See Hill v. USPS, 01842640 (1986). In that case, the ranking of the candidates was included in the record, but the actual qualifications of the candidates were not included.

Once an applicant has been rated as highly qualified, the assertion of the selecting official that he cannot perform the functions of the position is not sufficient to establish a legitimate reason for nonselection where no interviews were held and no other attempts were made to verify the selecting official's assessment. Hardin v. Department of Navy, 01801636 (1981).

When two candidates are equally qualified for the position in question, the agency is not required to select the minority candidate. But "a finding that two candidates are equally well qualified cannot serve as a mask to hide a selection between those candidates based on discriminatory motive." In re Department of Energy, 05870248 (1987) (employee demonstrated that a position vacancy was tailored specifically for the ultimate selectee and that she was denied the training given to the selectee that impacted upon her ability to compete for the position).

When the agency asserts as its legitimate reason for not selecting the complainant that he was not deemed "highly qualified," it must introduce evidence of the ranking criteria and qualifications of the applicants rated as highly qualified. In the absence of such evidence, the agency has not sustained its burden of production. Walker v. Secretary of Army, 01932200 (1993).
