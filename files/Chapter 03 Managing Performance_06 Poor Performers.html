POOR PERFORMERS

Effectively managing employee performance is the essence of your job as a manager. Your goal is to train, mentor, develop, and lead your employees to perform at their highest potential.

Unfortunately, a significant part of managing performance involves dealing with poor, or even unacceptable, performance. Managers are entitled to expect employees to work to the level of performance standards that are clear, measurable, and achievable.

Even more unfortunate, surveys show that managers could be doing a better job of dealing with poor performers.

To effectively handle poor performance, you must:

  • • Identify employees whose performance is not meeting standards.
  • • Give them a genuine chance to improve.
  • • Offer them appropriate assistance.
  • • Take action to reassign, demote, or remove them if they cannot or will not perform acceptably.

When dealing with problem employees, you need to identify whether the difficulties have to do with performance or conduct. Performance issues involve how well employees are accomplishing the tasks, functions, or duties of their positions. Conduct issues concern whether an employee has failed to or refused to comply with a law, rule, regulation, policy, or lawful order of a superior. Sometimes the distinction between performance and conduct is unclear. HR can help you figure it out.

You also need to be aware that poor performance may result from many different causes. While most are attributable to the individual employee, some can be attributed to the organization -- even directly to you. Before dealing with the employee, you must rule out these other possible causes.

Managers have a variety of tools available for dealing with substandard performance. They include:

  • • Counseling.
  • • Performance improvement plans.
  • • Reassignment.
  • • Demotion.
  • • Removal.

If you must take formal action against an employee who has not responded sufficiently to counseling, consult with the employee relations specialists in your HR office before you take any concrete steps. If an employee fails to respond to counseling or training, you have four options:

  • • Reassignment.
  • • A performance-based action (demotion or removal).
  • • Progressive discipline.
  • • Negotiated settlement.

HR will advise you on which option best fits agency policy and your specific situation and exactly how to proceed.

Causes of poor performance

There are many causes of substandard or unacceptable performance. Some of them are attributable to the organization, and some to the individual employee.

It's important to carefully consider the cause or causes of a performance problem before attempting to deal with it. The reason: Different actions are likely to be effective in different situations.

For example, if the root cause of a performance problem is an employee's lack of effort, then counseling, a direct warning, and then either discipline or a performance-based adverse action would make sense.

But if the root cause of the problem is a lack of resources necessary to do the job properly, counseling, warning, and then disciplining the employee won't help at all -- and may make things worse.

Similarly, if the root cause of the performance problem is a disability that could be reasonably accommodated, taking the traditional counseling/warning/discipline route would not only be ineffective, but would violate laws and regulations.

Chapter 43 vs. Chapter 75

Most federal agencies are authorized under 5 USC Chapter 43 to demote or remove employees for unacceptable performance, commonly referred to as performance-based actions. Chapter 43 can be used only to address poor performance. Actions based on unacceptable performance may also be taken under 5 USC Chapter 75, which is written in a manner that permits it to be used for either conduct or performance or a combination of both. Both chapters allow for an employee to be demoted or removed, provided that the agency has followed the procedures spelled out in that chapter.

Agencies have the authority to choose which chapter to use when dealing with poor performers and, therefore, which set of rules to follow. Most agencies allow supervisors and managers the flexibility to use either chapter. Having both options available can increase the willingness of a manager to take an appropriate action by choosing the authority that best suits the particular situation.

Notable differences. These chapters have similar sets of rules, but they also differ in substantive ways. You should be familiar with these differences before you take action. Here are a few of the more notable differences:

  • • Chapter 75 is dedicated entirely to the taking of an adverse action against a federal employee. In contrast, Chapter 43 is about performance management for all employees with only one portion of the chapter dedicated to taking an action against an employee whose performance is unacceptable.
  • • Chapter 43 makes the supervisor get more involved with the employee and the work.
  • • Chapter 43 has a provision that the penalty cannot be mitigated on appeal whereas the MSPB may reduce the agency's penalty in a Chapter 75 action.
  • • Chapter 75 action must be supported by a preponderance of the evidence, but Chapter 43 action must be supported by substantial evidence, which is a lower burden of proof.
  • • Chapter 43 action does not require the Douglas factors, but they must be considered by the deciding official in a Chapter 75 action when determining the appropriate penalty.

Performance counseling

Performance counseling is the name generally given to the process by which you, as manager, counsel an employee whose performance you have identified as poor or unacceptable in an effort to get that employee to improve.

The counseling process is not a matter of law or regulation, leaving the details up to agencies. If you do have to take a performance-based or disciplinary action against an employee with unacceptable performance, documentation of your prior counseling efforts may provide crucial information to HR and assure that any employee appeal will be unsuccessful. Consult with HR as soon as you realize serious counseling will be required.

Some managers avoid counseling or less formal forms of feedback on poor performance in the hopes that the poor performance will become so clearly unacceptable they can spring an unacceptable rating on the employee during the annual performance appraisal and then justify removal. This "gotcha" strategy is not only unfair, it gives the employee a good opening to overturn a removal on appeal since you will not be able to show you gave the employee clear notice of unacceptable performance and an opportunity to improve.

The bottom line is that it is your responsibility to deal promptly and effectively with performance problems, not let them get worse through inaction.

Counseling has several objectives:

  • • Advise an employee that performance is inadequate.
  • • Ascertain the reasons why performance is inadequate.
  • • Specify precisely what is unacceptable in the employee's performance if it is so poor as to be unacceptable.
  • • Specify precisely what the employee is expected to do in the future.
  • • Provide clear warning that a failure to correct unacceptable performance will result in adverse consequences.

Performance counseling is not easy. You may be uncomfortable when you have to tell employees that their behavior is not up to your expectations. You may be angry about the poor performance itself and about the difficult position it has put you in. You may want to limit your criticism to the odd hint that things could be better, perhaps in the middle of a more positive conversation. Or you may want to be more forthright with your criticism: "That report was two days late and badly done at that. You'll have to do better."

If the only problem you have is an occasional lapse by an otherwise good employee, that may be all you need. But if the problem is more serious, you will have to do more counseling.

Effective performance counseling doesn't happen by accident. Like most things you do as a manager, it requires a solid plan and good execution. To get it right, you should:

  • • Send a counseling memorandum to the employee which states precisely why the counseling session will be held with details of the performance deficiencies that make it necessary.
  • • Get the right place. Meet in private. No one wants to have this sort of conversation within hearing distance of coworkers.
  • • Eliminate distractions by silencing notifications on your phone and computer and closing the door of the meeting place.
  • • Get the right attitude. Don't be hostile, but don't be apologetic either. There is a clear business purpose to performance counseling sessions, so adopt a businesslike tone.
  • • Get the right facts. Always have your facts straight before going into a counseling session. Use any available documentation.
  • • Explain the purpose of the meeting. It is to notify the employee of performance deficiencies, and spell out precisely what must be done to correct them.
  • • Pinpoint the deficiencies. Highlight exactly where performance is not up to the requirements of the employee's performance standards. Provide concrete examples and explain why it's important the employee meet the required standards.
  • • Nail down the cause. If you're not sure why the employee is underperforming, ask.
  • • Specify performance expectations. Explain exactly what the employee must do to attain a satisfactory level of performance in the deficient areas. As necessary, clarify or supplement written performance standards with verbal and written directions. Try to obtain the employee's buy-in on concrete steps toward performance improvement.
  • • Arrange for mutual feedback. Let the employee know you will be watching the performance issues you have identified, and explain how you will give the employee an opportunity to demonstrate improvement.
  • • Offer appropriate help. If the employee indicates an underlying cause for the performance deficiency that you can reasonably help to fix -- for example, lack of knowledge of new software -- arrange for assistance. And then follow through.
  • • Do not offer inappropriate help. If the employee lacks the basic knowledge or skills necessary to perform the essential duties of the job at the current grade level, it's not your place to provide emergency basic training. Nor should you offer to reduce the workload below regular requirements simply because the employee supposedly can't handle it (but be aware of possible disability issues).
  • • Point out the consequences. Clearly state to the employee that failure to bring poor performance in line with performance standards will lead to adverse consequences. Do not fudge this point or soften it by saying that action might result. Make it definite.
  • • End the session cordially but make it clear that performance improvement is required.
  • • Document the meeting. It doesn't have to be extensive, but the documentation should reflect the time, date, and place of the meeting, as well as the main points made to the employee, any points brought up by the employee, and a statement of what is to happen going forward. Together with the original counseling memorandum, this documentation can be crucial if an adverse action ultimately becomes necessary. The employee should be sent a version of the meeting documentation, and this too should be preserved.

It is easy to make mistakes in a counseling session because of the inherent tension of the situation. Remember not to:

  • • Show anger or hostility.
  • • Theorize as to the employee's thought processes or motives.
  • • Compare the employee to other employees by name.
  • • Apologize for bringing the matter up.
  • • Trivialize the seriousness of the session.
  • • Rely on generalities, rather than on specific facts and examples.

Don't try to soften the blow by "sandwiching" the negative between positive statements like "Overall, it's been great having you here," or "You've done a great job for us and I know we can overcome these little problems." This is only likely to confuse the employee, who may take away from it the notion that things aren't really all that serious. They are serious or you wouldn't need to have a counseling session. Both you and your employee need to focus on what is wrong.

However, if you can agree on realistic steps to improve the situation and both of you wind up on the same page, use a bit of positive reinforcement of that situation and end things on a more upbeat, if still serious note. Try something like: "It seems as if we both know what needs to be done. If you do what you've agreed to do, I'm sure things will improve."

Opportunity to improve

Hopefully, the informal steps you take with employees, like performance counseling, will resolve the issue and place your employee back on the track of acceptable performance. However, if the employee is still working at an unacceptable level in one or more critical elements, under Chapter 43 you will need to give them a formal opportunity to improve their performance before you take a performance-based action. This opportunity period is designed to give the employee a chance to bring their performance up to an acceptable level. It's also your opportunity to clearly express your expectations and the consequences of not meeting those expectations.

Your agency may have its own mechanism for providing employees with a formal opportunity to improve, but most agencies have adopted the use of a performance improvement plan. If the employee fails to improve to an acceptable level by the end of the opportunity period, further action is warranted.

For actions taken pursuant to Chapter 43, you can only remove or demote an employee for unacceptable performance in a critical performance element. You cannot take these actions against an employee who fails to meet noncritical or additional performance elements.

Offer assistance. Because you want to ensure the success of your employee, be prepared to offer assistance. Depending on the nature of the job and the employee's experience, try the following:

  • • Give the employee a checklist. A simple checklist that organizes and prioritizes tasks can spark a change.
  • • Pair them with another employee. Have the employee work in conjunction or shadow someone who does the same or similar job well. The poorly performing employee may discover new methods of doing their job or gain inspiration to perform at a higher level.
  • • Offer training. Think about training as it pertains to elevating the performance in the critical element(s) at issue. Perhaps training that focuses on organizational skills, writing skills, time management, or data analysis can help raise the employee's performance.
  • • Supervise closer. Your employee will understand the seriousness of their need to improve if you make a genuine effort to supervise them more closely. Do this by monitoring the employee's progress, offering regular feedback, physically observing how they do their job, and meeting routinely.

Note: Not every employee will require the same type of assistance. However, once you determine the type of assistance to offer, be sure to follow through with it during the opportunity period.

Process of formal opportunity to improve

Once you determine that your employee's performance is unacceptable in one or more critical elements, follow the process below for providing a formal opportunity to improve:

  1. 1. Issue an "opportunity period" notice -- Inform the employee in writing of the critical element(s) in which they are failing, what is needed to bring performance up to an acceptable level, what assistance will be provided, and the consequences of failing to improve during the opportunity period.
  2. 2. Provide a period to improve -- During a specified time period, your employee must bring performance up to an acceptable level in the failed critical element(s). The duration of this opportunity period may vary. When determining the length of the period, you need to take into account the nature of the employee's duties and responsibilities and the performance deficiencies involved. Be sure to document the employee's progress and to provide any appropriate assistance.
  3. 3. Determine if performance has improved -- Examine the employee's performance and compare it with standards and expectations outlined in the opportunity period notice.

The goal is for your employee to improve and maintain an acceptable performance. However, if the employee fails to perform acceptably by the end of the opportunity period, or improves but then fails again in the same critical element within one year from the start of the opportunity period, you may reassign, demote, or remove the employee without going through another opportunity period.

When poor performance does not improve

Whether an employee cannot perform acceptably or just doesn't want to, you not only should take action to correct the situation, you have a legal obligation to do so as the employee's manager. Employees, who perform unacceptably after being given a chance to improve must, by law, be demoted, reassigned, or removed from federal service. You can't just leave them there and hope they get better. They won't.

How should you take action? That will be depend in good part on the advice and support you get from your HR office, which can make sure that your action fits in with agency policy and guide you through the regulatory aspects. There are three basic choices:

  • • Performance-based actions. The agency uses the procedures set up in law specifically to deal with performance, rather than conduct problems. They include demotions, reassignments, and removals.
  • • Progressive discipline. Letters of reprimand, suspensions, demotions, reassignments, and removals. These actions are processed using the procedures set up for dealing with misconduct problems. Keep in mind that agencies are not required to use progressive discipline. Check with your HR office for guidance.
  • • Negotiated settlements. Sometimes managers are able to avoid the time and effort involved in taking adverse action by working out a settlement agreement with an underperforming employee. Often such agreements provide the employee with a specified time period in which to retire, resign, or find another job before action is initiated.

Why would your agency use progressive discipline, a process designed primarily for dealing with conduct problems, in a performance situation? Usually, this happens because the procedures involved in a disciplinary action are substantially simpler (and less burdensome on the manager). An adverse action requires a higher standard of proof, and there is a possibility the penalty may be modified on appeal. Action can be taken more quickly than under performance-based procedures. Your HR office will explain this in more detail.

What about negotiated settlements? When you take this option, you effectively present the employee with the choice of removal or resignation/retirement. If the employee accepts, this saves everyone a lot of time and trouble. But the employee may later try to renege on a resignation or retirement on the grounds that it was coerced. For this reason, we strongly recommend that you not initiate such discussions on your own. Check with HR and agency counsel.

You cannot use a threatened removal to encourage an employee you simply don't like to seek another job or retire earlier than planned. Make sure that you really do intend to remove the employee without a settlement, and have the documentation in hand to show a removal would have been justified by unacceptable performance.

All three of these options assume the employee is actually performing unacceptably, that is, failing to meet at least one performance standard linked to a critical performance element and that you can prove it. Documentation may be in the form of an annual performance appraisal, but you don't have to wait until the conclusion of a rating cycle.

Talk with HR and make sure no employee who is performing unacceptably receives a within-grade increase. WIGIs for unacceptably performing employees are in any case forbidden by law and would make it difficult to sustain any action against the employee on appeal.

Mistakes to avoid

Improving employee performance is your job every day. Keep these things in mind to avoid common mistakes.

Do not:

  • • Take sudden action against employees whose work has been rated satisfactory even when it hasn't been up to that level. If deficient performance has been rated satisfactory in the past, it's necessary to put the employee on clear notice of what increased or improved level of performance will be required in the current rating period. Make any required changes to performance standards, clearly notify the employee of what is required, and provide a reasonable period for adjustment -- before you propose or take action for poor performance.
  • • Give an employee a within-grade increase just before taking action for poor performance. Since a WIGI can only be authorized when an employee is performing at a fully acceptable level, if you give one immediately before you propose an adverse action for unacceptable performance you will undercut your case from the beginning.
  • • Overlook the responsibility to provide a positive atmosphere where excellence and employee development are encouraged and rewarded and poor performance is not tolerated.
  • • Fail to give poor performers a genuine chance to improve. Many will. If they don't, and you can document what you did to help them, you will make it easier to remove them.

Ask FMG

Q. I have an employee who seems to be doing their best, but they keep missing deadlines. Is the employee just not good enough to do the job?

A. It is possible your employee isn't really qualified to do it (a poor hiring or promotion decision, perhaps) and should be reassigned, demoted, or removed. But there are other possibilities you should look into and do something about as appropriate. To name some, your employee may:

  • • Need more training, particularly in time management.
  • • Not understand your expectations and priorities.
  • • Be overloaded.
  • • Be caught up in poor work-flow design.
  • • Suffer from poor coworker cooperation.
  • • Be a victim of harassment or other workplace difficulties.
  • • Have a medical impairment and perhaps require reasonable accommodation of a disability.
  • • Be distracted by personal problems.

Q. Can I take a performance-based action, including withholding a WIGI, against an employee who is barely satisfactory and could do much better?

A. No. Performance-based actions (demotion, removal) can only be taken against employees who are working at an unacceptable level. WIGIs must be withheld for performance below the fully successful level.

Q. Am I required to provide training to everyone who performs poorly?

A. If the training would help the employee master a new or changed process, technology, or situation, yes. But you are not obligated to provide training in a basic skill or knowledge the employee should already have to work in the job at the existing grade level.

Q. When is it appropriate to use progressive discipline for a performance problem?

A. It is particularly appropriate in situations where the employee is capable of performing the work but doesn't do so out of laziness, inattention, or unwillingness to follow your orders. Agencies are not required to use progressive discipline, but it is always an option in an appropriate situation.

Q. Can I downgrade or reassign an employee who is not performing acceptably, or is removal my only option?

A. You have the alternatives of demotion to a lower grade or reassignment to a different position available. They may be appropriate if an employee is trying hard but simply lacks the qualifications to succeed in the current position. However, reassigning an employee with performance problems is not normally recommended because it rarely solves the problem. It just moves it around. Make sure you work closely with the HR department (and your superiors) if you are considering reassigning an employee.

Q: After my employee received notice that a performance-based action was being pursued for their unacceptable performance, they threatened to call their lawyer. Is the employee allowed to bring their legal counsel into a performance matter?

A: Yes. Under Chapter 43, an employee whose reduction in grade or removal is proposed is entitled to be represented by an attorney or other representative. The same holds true for an employee against whom an action is proposed under Chapter 75.

Q. Can I offer an employee a chance to retire, resign, or seek another job in advance of an intended removal?

A. Yes, if you intend to go through with the action and have the documentation to back it up, you can offer this chance as a settlement of the issue. But be careful. Work with HR and agency counsel to ensure that you don't create a "coerced resignation" situation.

Q. A seasoned employee was transferred to my unit with no change in duties. The employee's former manager consistently rated them at an acceptable level, but their performance is clearly unacceptable. What should I do?

A. Perhaps you and the other manager are not interpreting performance standards the same. Before taking action against the employee, consult with your superior to gain a clear understanding of standards application. Then counsel the employee to ensure they have a clear understanding of expectations. From there, monitor and act on performance issues. Previous unrealistic ratings are not binding on you.
