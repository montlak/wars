6. Medical Inquiries and Giglio

This is not a primer on criminal law and, indeed, the author does not have the requisite experience to write such a tomb. However, there are times when some knowledge outside of the parameters of employment law is necessary. One of those situations involves law enforcement officers who could potentially testify in a criminal case and any circumstances that might reflect on the credibility of that testimony.

Under Giglio v. United States, 405 U.S. 150 (1972), prosecutors are required to turn over to defense counsel information that could reflect on the credibility of law enforcement officers who will testify at trial. In Percy K. v. Attorney General, 0120091967 (2015), the Commission did not give agencies carte blanche to make medical inquiries in such situations, but did find, in the facts of that particular case, that no violation of the ADA had occurred. The Commission, in Percy K., explained:

After a review of the record in this case, we find that the Agency's medical inquiry was not discriminatory, because the AUSA had a reasonable belief, based on objective evidence that Complainant's medical history might need to be reviewed and that he was unable to perform the essential functions of his position, which involved testifying persuasively in federal court proceedings.

Specifically, the AUSA averred that he was aware, from having worked with Complainant previously, that Complainant had been hospitalized due to stress issues. In a March 2008 letter to Complainant's attorney, the Acting General Counsel of the EOUSA recalled that in February 2007, Complainant had informed another federal prosecutor with whom he was working at the time about his personal health problems and medications. Complainant denies this, but did admit to telling another Special Agent that he had depression. Moreover, the Chief Division Counsel averred that during the March 2007 meeting between herself and the AUSA, the AUSA reported having observed Complainant's problems first hand, and questioned whether Complainant was still on medication.

While information relating to Complainant's prior psychiatric history might or might not be considered exculpatory evidence pursuant to the Giglio policy, the AUSA's prior history with Complainant provided a reasonable basis for the AUSA to request the information so it could be reviewed as potential impeachment information dealing with the accuracy of Complainant's testimony. The Commission finds that the AUSA held a reasonable belief that Complainant's prior history of depression implicated the need to review his personnel records as the information contained therein could impact the performance of his essential job functions; testifying in court without running afoul of the Agency's Giglio policy.

This is not to say that all situations which present circumstances similar to the instant case will result in a similar outcome. The Commission is concerned about the potential disclosure of medical information where no reasonable basis for inquiries exists. Rather, we advise that such future requests for medical documentation be based on reasonable belief and objective evidence, and reliable information. In the absence of such belief, once the responding Agency,--in this case the FBI--denied there was any Giglio information, we would expect the matter to be closed. In this way, routine requests for medical information will not be condoned.
