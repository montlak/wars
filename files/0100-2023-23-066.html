9. Essential Job Functions and Position Descriptions

The ADA provides that consideration must be given to the employer's judgment as to what functions are essential to a given position. The Act further provides that a written position description that exists prior to advertising or interviewing for the position is evidence of the essential functions. 42 USC 12111(8). However, the provision specifies that the position description must be in existence before the complainant was hired. See also 29 CFR 1630.2(n)(3)(i)-(ii). The importance of a preexisting position description in determining essential functions was underscored by the Commission in Orosz v. Secretary of Commerce, 01970955 (2000):

We are not convinced by the agency's argument that rotating between three work sites is an essential function of the GS-11 position. We note that the agency states in its FAD that the rotation to the Washington, D.C. location was eliminated. Therefore, complainant need only receive training on the equipment located at the Rockville and Riverdale, Maryland sites. In support of our finding on the essential functions, we note that the amendment to the Position Description for the GS-11 position adding the rotational requirement was made subsequent to complainant's recommendation for promotion to the position. Furthermore, the amendment only added the rotational requirement while the remaining essential functions for the position remained the same. There appears to be no dispute that complainant is qualified for the position. We note that the rotational assignment was added in order to provide incumbents training and experience on various equipment and networks located at each site. We find that the agency has failed to show that complainant could not receive the essential training and experience by some other means while remaining at the Silver Spring location. We note that complainant asserts that the Silver Spring site as well as the Riverdale site is equipped for networking. Therefore, after examining the duties involved and the training provided at each site, we find that the rotational training and experience are not essential duties or functions of the position. Thus, the Commission finds that complainant is fully capable of performing the essential functions of the GS-11 position without accommodation and is a qualified individual with a disability.

The agency's position description is not dispositive, however, of what constitutes the essential functions of a position. The Commission will look to the actual duties performed. For example, in Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120130537 (2014), the Commission found that the position description could not be reconciled with duty status report. The position description related to customer service duties and the status report referenced operating heavy machinery and standing for seven hours a day.
