A. FAILURE TO MENTION FEE ISSUE

The failure to make any mention of attorney fees in a settlement agreement has, at times, resulted in agency liabilities for fees and, at other times, exonerated the agency from payment of fees. While the Commission has vacillated in its position on the failure to mention fees in a settlement agreement, the Supreme Court resolved the issued in Buckhannon Board & Care Home, Inc. v. West Virginia Department of Health and Human Services, 532 U.S. 598 (2001). In Buckhannon, the Supreme Court rejected the so-called "catalyst" theory as the basis for determining prevailing party status under a fee-recovery statute. The Court found that such statutes did not permit an award of fees in the absence of court-ordered relief or a settlement agreement that specifically provided for fees. As a result, the Commission now holds that fees are not recoverable unless the settlement agreement provides for fees. See Warren v. Secretary of Army, 01A04898 (2003). See also Guitierrez v. Postmaster General, 0520080682 (2008), holding that "we find that the settlement agreement provided no provisions for the payment of attorney's fees and costs. As such, we find that the complainant waived the ability to collect fees and costs with regard to the creation and adoption of the settlement agreement." Buckhannon is discussed in more detail in Chapter 22 under the subheading "Prevailing Parties."

Similarly, in Kittrell v. Secretary of Navy, 0120100331 (2012), the Commission found that clause provided for reasonable attorney fees in accordance with 29 CFR 1614.501(e)(iv) was not an agreement to pay fees for precomplaint counseling services in the absence of any mention of precomplaint counseling fees in the settlement agreement. The Commission noted that agencies are not required to pay precomplaint fees in the absence of a finding of discrimination that is appealed by the agency, but are not prohibited from agreeing to do so. However, mere reference of the regulation did not constitute such an agreement.
