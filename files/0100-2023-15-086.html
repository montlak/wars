4. Failure to Respond to Motion

When an agency makes a motion for summary judgment, it is not dispositive that the complainant fails to respond. The administrative judge improperly grants such a motion when he fails to make an examination of the record to determine independently whether any material issues of fact exist. In Bracey v. Postmaster General, 01912620 (1991), the Commission held:

The Commission finds that appellant's failure to respond to the agency's Motion for Summary Judgment does not necessarily mean that she failed to establish the existence of a genuine issue of material fact. The agency had already conducted an investigation, appellant had completed an affidavit, and she had provided evidence in support of her allegations; therefore, in response to the agency's motion, the AJ was required to evaluate the evidence of record and determine whether there were any material facts still in dispute.... [T]he fact-finder's function is not to weigh evidence and render a determination as to the truth of the matter, but only to determine whether there exists a genuine factual dispute....

Failure to respond to a motion for summary judgment or a notice of intent to issue a summary judgment decision by the administrative judge does not warrant dismissal of the case as a sanction. See Hooker v. Secretary of Defense, 01A05543 (2002); Gladden v. Secretary of Commerce, 01A03968 (2002). Indeed, failure to respond does not warrant any sanction. See Rogers v. Secretary of Air Force, 01A33323 (2003). Instead, the administrative judge may issue a summary judgment decision based on the existing record if there are no material facts in dispute. See Casarez v. Secretary of Treasury, 01A10673 (2001). While summary judgment cannot be issued as a sanction, in certain cases it is appropriate for an administrative judge to draw an adverse inference based on a complainant's failure to respond to discovery and, based on that inference, determine there are no genuine disputes of material fact and the case is appropriate for summary judgment. See Dombrowski v. Secretary of Navy, 01A24924 (2003).

Likewise, an administrative judge should not solely rely on the representations of agency counsel in finding that there is no genuine issue of material fact. See Daley v. Secretary of Treasury, 01960094 (1997). In that case, the Commission noted that the administrative judge paid little attention to questions in the factual record and appellant's contrary representations. See also Pedersen v. Department of Justice, Federal Bureau of Prisons, 05940339 (1995).

However, if a complainant represented by counsel fails to respond to an agency's motion for summary judgment, the Commission will likely reject the complainant's assertion of being denied an opportunity to submit evidence at a hearing, unless the complainant can at least point to some specific evidence already in the record that raises a genuine issue of fact. As the Commission held in Hanley v. Postmaster General, 01960603 (1998):

We reject the appellant's counsel's bare assertion on appeal that the appellant was "denied an opportunity to present evidence, through a hearing, to support his claim." As noted earlier, the counsel did not file any opposition to the agency's motion, nor did she submit any evidence to the AJ demonstrating the existence of a genuine issue on any of the material facts which would have warranted a hearing. Likewise, on appeal, the counsel fails to point to any evidence of record demonstrating the existence of any such genuine issue which would warrant a reversal of the AJ's decision. We, therefore, find that the AJ properly granted the agency's motion to issue a decision in its favor without an evidentiary hearing.

In addition, failure to respond to an administrative judge's notice of intent to issue a summary decision is not a basis for imposing sanctions against the offending party. Judges' Handbook at 5-3.

If an agency files a motion for summary judgment and later does not oppose the issuance of the judge's findings without a hearing, it will not be heard to argue on appeal that it was prevented from fully presenting its case at the hearing. Ames v. Secretary of Army, 01944172 (1996). In Ames, the Commission rejected the agency's argument that it could have avoided liability for discrimination by showing, at a hearing, that it would have taken the same action even absent discrimination.

In MacAllister v. Secretary of Navy, 01972305 (1998), the Commission passed up a chance to clarify the procedure to be followed when an agency comes to believe that an administrative judge improperly granted its motion for summary judgment. Granted, there are not many cases that are likely to fall into this procedural quagmire, but the Commission remanded the case for a hearing without clarifying the proper procedure. In MacAllister, the agency moved for summary judgment. In spite of a response from the complainant, supported by evidence, that there were material facts in genuine dispute, the administrative judge issued a recommended decision granting the motion. In a rare move, the agency then determined there were factual disputes and sent the case back to the judge for a hearing. The judge, however, returned the case to the agency indicating the agency's only option at that stage was to issue a final decision or conduct a supplemental investigation.

Running out of time on the 60-day clock, the agency in MacAllister issued a final decision adopting the judge's decision. The Commission remanded the case for a hearing. However, it is unclear from the Commission's decision in MacAllister whether it believed the agency was correct in sending the case back to the judge:

After the agency finished its investigation of the appellant's complaint, she requested a hearing before an EEOC administrative judge (AJ). The agency then made a motion pursuant to our regulation at 29 CFR 1614.109(e)(1) requesting the AJ to issue a recommended decision (RD) in the agency's favor without a hearing. Appellant filed an opposition. The appellant disputed the agency's contention that the undisputed evidence failed to establish a prima facie case of discrimination. She submitted documentary evidence disputing the agency's assertions that her position was not supervisory before May 1994, that she did not have any prior EEO activity, and that she was not treated differently from other similarly situated employees not of her protected class. She also pointed out that the investigation was incomplete because some of her witnesses were not interviewed by the investigator.

On August 13, 1996, the AJ granted the agency's motion. The AJ found that there was no genuine dispute as to any material facts, and that the evidence of record failed to establish a prima facie case of alleged color, sex, age or reprisal discrimination.

On October 17, 1996, the agency returned the case-file to the AJ for a hearing along with an acknowledgment that the appellant's opposition to the agency's motion disclosed that material facts were in genuine dispute, the disputed evidence presented credibility issues, and the investigation was incomplete. For instance, the agency acknowledged that the appellant maintains that her supervisor was aware of her intention to file an EEO complaint at least two weeks before the counseling session, and that she viewed the counseling session as a form of harassment where a possible disciplinary action was discussed. Further, appellant maintained that the supervisor falsely accused her of harassing her co-workers, and that a similarly situated black female employee was not counseled. The agency acknowledged that the investigator did not take statements from the two employees who were allegedly harassed by the appellant. Further, the agency acknowledged that the supervisor's credibility was called into question by statements from five co-workers who supported the appellant's position. Lastly, the agency acknowledged that the appellant's SF-50, her position description and the organizational chart since February 1993 showed that she was in a supervisory position. She had assigned work to employees, approved their leave requests, and signed their performance appraisals as the first-line supervisor. The agency acknowledged that management had failed to articulate a reason for suddenly informing the appellant, after her having been on the job for fifteen months and only two weeks after she threatened to file an EEO complaint, that her position was not supervisory.

On October 30, 1996, the AJ informed the agency that it did not have the authority to return the case file to her for a hearing, and that the agency's only option under 29 CFR 1614.110 was to either issue a FAD with appeal rights or to complete the additional investigation addressing the defects the agency found in its oriGINAl investigation before issuing the FAD.

On December 18, 1996, the agency issued the instant FAD. Noting that it did not have the time to conduct a supplemental investigation because of the 60-day limitation in 29 CFR 1614.109(g) for issuing a FAD, the FAD held:

There appear to be material facts in dispute. Although attempts to remedy that problem by remanding the case were made, the [AJ] has now held that there is no such authority. Therefore, the Department of the Navy is bound by 29 CFR 1614.109(g) which provides that an agency can only reject, modify, or adopt an AJ's findings of fact and conclusions of law on the merits of the complaint. The AJ's decision in this case is a finding of no discrimination. Given the insufficiency of the investigative file, it is not possible to reject the AJ's decision. Consequently, the Department of the Navy must adopt the AJ's Recommended Decision without a hearing.

It is from this FAD that the appellant now appeals.

...

This appeal comes to us in an unusual posture where the agency, after having persuaded the AJ successfully that there was no genuine dispute as to material facts and therefore a decision was warranted in its favor on the record, acknowledges that there are gaps in the investigative record on material issues and that the evidence on material issues is in fact in dispute. The FAD acknowledges that the evidence of record raises credibility questions that must be resolved at a hearing. The FAD also acknowledges that the only reason for the agency's inability to conduct the needed supplemental investigation was the fact that the AJ's recommended decision was about to become its own final decision by operation of our regulation by the time the agency discovered the gaps in its investigation after receiving the RD.

A final decision on the merits of the appellant's allegations of discrimination cannot be issued in the absence of a complete and impartial factual record. See 29 CFR 1614.108(b). Further, the Commission has stated that "[t]he hearing is an adjudicatory proceeding which completes the investigation of a complaint by ensuring that the parties have a fair and reasonable opportunity to explain and supplement the record and to examine and cross-examine witnesses." EEOC Federal Sector Complaint Processing Manual, EEO MD-110, Chapter 6, Section I, at page 6-1 (October 1992). Here, the appellant's complaint has not been fully investigated, nor has she had an opportunity to examine and cross-examine the witnesses on the disputed testimony at a hearing.

Therefore, the FAD is VACATED and the complaint is REMANDED for processing pursuant to the ORDER below.
