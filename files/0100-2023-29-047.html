2. Election of EEO Procedure

According to 29 CFR 1614.302(d), the agency is required to process a mixed case complaint just as it would any other complaint of discrimination following the procedures set out at 29 CFR 1614.102 through 1614.109. It is not nearly that simple, however, as the regulation at 29 CFR 1614.302(d) goes on to note the following exceptions:

(1) At the time the agency advises a complainant of the acceptance of a mixed case complaint, it shall also advise the complainant that:

(i) If a final decision is not issued within 120 days of the date of filing of the mixed case complaint, the complainant may appeal the matter to the MSPB at any time thereafter as specified at 5 CFR 1201.154(a) or may file a civil action as specified at § 1614.310(g), but not both; and

(ii) If the complainant is dissatisfied with the agency's final decision on the mixed case complaint, the complainant may appeal the matter to the MSPB (not EEOC) within 20 days of receipt of the agency's final decision;

(2) Upon completion of the investigation, the notice provided the complainant in accordance with § 1614.108(f) will advise the complainant that a final decision will be issued within 45 days without a hearing; and

(3) At the time that the agency issues its final decision on a mixed case complaint, the agency shall advise the complainant of the right to appeal the matter to the MSPB (not EEOC) within 20 days of receipt and of the right to file a civil action as provided at § 1614.310(a).

Reduced to its essential parts, the regulation states that the agency shall investigate the complaint and issue a final decision. The employee does not have the right to an EEO hearing. The agency must issue a final decision within 120 days of the filing of the complaint, or the employee may elect to appeal the matter to the MSPB. At the MSPB, the employee is entitled to an evidentiary hearing. The employee also has the right to file an action in U.S. district court. Note that when the complainant elects the EEO procedure, he must appeal a final agency decision to the MSPB prior to appealing to the Commission. McWright v. Secretary of Education, 01893025 (1990).

The purpose of the regulations regarding mixed cases is to avoid duplication of complaints. However, dismissal of an EEO complaint by the agency is warranted only where the agency can demonstrate that the MSPB fully considered all of the discrimination allegations involved in the EEO complaint. See Rolfes v. Department of Treasury, 01821724 (1983). The Board's review of the denial of a within-grade increase was a distinct matter from appellant's allegations that her work was criticized unduly because of her sex, even though testimony at the Board hearing tangentially had touched upon the issue of undue criticism. See also Araujo v. Department of Army, 01821989 (1983) (complainant's allegations that her removal and the agency's refusal to place her in a lower grade in lieu of removal were identical to the issues raised before the MSPB, but her allegation that she was denied placement into a management training program was dismissed improperly by the agency because that matter was not within the jurisdiction of the Board to review).

If the Board mistakenly adjudicates a case where the EEO complaint was filed first, the adjudication constitutes an election of remedy. "The Commission has also held that upholding the unequivocal legislative interest to provide only one forum in which to challenge the propriety of an agency action alleged to have been based, in whole or in part, on discriminatory factors, takes precedence of complainant's equitable entitlement to have an adjudication of that issue in one forum or the other." Boreli v. Secretary of Navy, 01991851 (2001).

Conversely, if the complainant does not file a Board appeal but instead requests an EEOC hearing, the Commission will assume jurisdiction over the case where it has been in the EEO process to become "firmly enmeshed" in the process long enough. See, e.g., Haralson v. Secretary of Defense, 01991612 (2002) (holding that even though the case was within the Jurisdiction of The MSPB, the EEOC would assume jurisdiction because the agency failed to advise the complainant of his MSPB appeal rights and the case had been in the EEO process for six years).
