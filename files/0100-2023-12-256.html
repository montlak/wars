broida eeo Chapter 03: Formal Complaint Process--Acceptance and Dismissal Chapter 03: Formal Complaint Process--Acceptance and Dismissal, Subchapter 03: Bases for Dismissal of Complaints, Section P: Election of Remedies, Subsection 01: Negotiated Grievance Procedure

1. Negotiated Grievance Procedure

When the complainant is in a collective bargaining unit that has a negotiated grievance procedure under Chapter 71 of Title V, and that procedure permits the processing of allegations of discrimination, the complainant may elect to pursue a complaint of discrimination through the grievance or through the EEO process, but not both. The relationship of the EEO process to the negotiated grievance process is set forth at 29 CFR 1614.301:

(a) When a person is employed by an agency subject to 5 USC 7121(d) and is covered by a collective bargaining agreement that permits allegations of discrimination to be raised in a negotiated grievance procedure, a person wishing to file a complaint or a grievance on a matter of alleged employment discrimination must elect to raise the matter under either part 1614 or the negotiated grievance procedure, but not both. An election to proceed under this part is indicated only by the filing of a written complaint; use of the pre-complaint process as described in § 1614.105 does not constitute an election for purposes of this section. An aggrieved employee who files a complaint under this part may not thereafter file a grievance on the same matter. An election to proceed under a negotiated grievance procedure is indicated by the filing of a timely written grievance. An aggrieved employee who files a grievance with an agency whose negotiated agreement permits the acceptance of grievances which allege discrimination may not thereafter file a complaint on the same matter under this part 1614 irrespective of whether the agency has informed the individual of the need to elect or of whether the grievance has raised an issue of discrimination. Any such complaint filed after a grievance has been filed on the same matter shall be dismissed without prejudice to the complainant's right to proceed through the negotiated grievance procedure including the right to appeal to the Commission from a final decision as provided in subpart D of this part. The dismissal of such a complaint shall advise the complainant of the obligation to raise discrimination in the grievance process and of the right to appeal the final grievance decision to the Commission.

(b) When a person is not covered by a collective bargaining agreement that permits allegations of discrimination to be raised in a negotiated grievance procedure, allegations of discrimination shall be processed as complaints under this part.

(c) When a person is employed by an agency not subject to 5 U.S.C. 7121(d) and is covered by a negotiated grievance procedure, allegations of discrimination shall be processed as complaints under this part, except that the time limits for processing the complaint contained in § 1614.106 and for appeal to the Commission contained in § 1614.402 may be held in abeyance during processing of a grievance covering the same matter as the complaint if the agency notifies the complainant in writing that the complaint will be held in abeyance pursuant to this section.

Section 1614.301 applies only to grievance procedures that are the result of collective bargaining. It does not apply to administrative grievance procedures established by the agency. Also, the section applies only to agencies subject to 5 USC 7121(d). The U.S. Postal Service is not subject to 7121(d) and, thus, the prohibition against dual processing does not apply to that agency.

Withdrawal of a grievance does not negate the election of remedies and the complainant may not subsequently proceed with an EEO complaint. See Bracket v. Secretary of Air Force, 05910383 (1991). However, where the grievance is filed under a contract provision that allows the union to file on the employee's behalf, this is not an election and withdrawal of the grievance by the union does not effect the employee's ability to pursue an EEO complaint. See Hicks v. Secretary of Air Force, 01980342 (2000).

When the agency dismisses a complaint on the basis of election of remedy, it must submit evidence that the collective bargaining agreement actually permits the processing of discrimination allegations. If the evidence does not support the conclusion that the agreement allows grievances charging discrimination, the agency improperly dismisses an EEO complaint even where a grievance has been filed. See Williams v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01A05052 (2001); see also Gadsden v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01997081 (2001) (agency submitted some evidence of grievance procedure, but none related to whether it covered allegations of discrimination); Salisbury v. Secretary of Army, 01996324 (2001); Batts v. Secretary of Army, 01A02972 (2000). Compare Weeks v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A14577 (2002) (agency submitted grievance and relevant provision of collective bargaining agreement); Hurst v. Secretary of Transportation, 019931789 (2001) (dismissal upheld where agency submitted copy of grievance and collective bargaining agreement). On appeal from a dismissal on the grounds that the matters were already raised in a grievance proceeding, the agency must submit a copy of the collective bargaining agreement itself to the Commission for review. Failure to do so will result in reversal of the dismissal and reinstatement of the complaint. Irwin R. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120180492 (2018). As the Commission in Irwin R. explained:

In this case however, the record does not contain a copy of the CBA which would confirm that the negotiated grievance procedure permits claims of discrimination. While the Agency's final decision did cite Complainant's grievances, it nevertheless failed to include in the record a copy of the CBA for the Commission's review. Therefore, we cannot sufficiently determine whether the CBA permits election of a grievance procedure for claims of discrimination instead of the EEO complaint process. Based upon the record before this Commission, the Agency's decision dismissing Complainant's complaint under 29 C.F.R. § 1614.107(a)(4) was improper. See Spellman v. Dep't of Homeland Sec., EEOC Appeal No. 0120103173 (Jan. 4, 2013) citing Marshall v. Dep't of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05910685 (Sep. 6, 1991).

It is the agency's burden to prove that the complainant has elected to pursue his or her discrimination complaint through the negotiated grievance process. In Mkrtchyan v. Broadcasting Board of Governors International Broadcasting Bureau, 01991800 (2001), the Commission found that the agency did not prove that the matter was raised in a grievance because the agency failed to provide "a copy of a union agreement and/or any relevant documentation indicating that the agency's negotiated grievance procedure permits claims of discrimination to be raised therein." Id. The Commission reinstated the complaint for processing.

When the complainant and the agency disagree over the scope of the grievance, the Commission will not rely on the parties' characterizations of the grievance but will examine the actual grievance to determine if identical claims are raised by the grievance and the EEO complaint. The complainant cannot change the nature of the grievance simply by elaborating on the matters covered in the EEO complaint. For example, in Labrador v. Secretary of Energy, 01996457 (2001), the Commission found the grievance and the EEO complaint were identical and that the complainant elected to proceed through the negotiated grievance procedure:

On appeal, complainant argues that the issues raised in the grievance and the EEO formal complaint are distinct, as the grievance involves the agency determination to disallow his travel reimbursement. Specifically, complainant argues that the grievance involves the agency's changing the method used to determine his allowable travel and moving expenses from the mutually agreed "fixed rate" method to the "actual expense" method. Complainant states that in contrast, the EEO formal complaint contains matters relating to how the agency treated complainant while still in North Dakota planning his move to California, as well as the disallowance of his travel reimbursement.

EEOC Regulation 29 CFR 1614.301(a) states that when a person is employed by an agency subject to 5 USC 7121(d) and is covered by a collective bargaining agreement that permits claims of discrimination to be raised in a negotiated grievance procedure, a person wishing to file a complaint or grievance on a matter of alleged employment discrimination must elect to raise the matter under either part 1614 or the negotiated grievance procedure, but not both. An aggrieved employee who files a grievance with an agency whose negotiated agreement permits the acceptance of grievances which allege discrimination may not thereafter file a complaint on the same matter under this part 1614 irrespective of whether the agency has informed the individual of the need to elect or whether the grievance has raised an issue of discrimination.

The record contains a copy of the negotiated agreement that provides in pertinent part that employees have the right to raise matters of alleged discrimination under the statutory procedure or the negotiated grievance procedure, but not both. The Commission finds that complainant filed a grievance pursuant to a negotiated grievance procedure on February 11, 1999, and thereafter filed a formal EEO complaint on May 20, 1999. We are not persuaded by complainant's assertions on appeal that the subject of his May 20, 1999 EEO formal complaint is different than the subject raised in his February 11, 1999 negotiated grievance. We determine that the matters raised in the instant EEO complaint are an elaboration of the matters set forth in his grievance.

In Colon v. Social Security Administration, 01991815 (2002), the Commission found the complainant's grievance over denial of a hardship transfer did not preclude her EEO complaint for failure to make reasonable accommodation. In finding that the grievance and the EEO complaint raised different matters, the Commission observed that hardship transfers were based on criteria enumerated in the collective bargaining agreement without regard to whether the employee was a qualified individual with a disability. Because a request for reasonable accommodation is analyzed under EEOC guidance, the Commission found the grievance was not an election of remedy with respect to the reasonable accommodation complaint.

The election of remedies and its relationship to the negotiated grievance procedure is covered in detail in Chapter 20, subheading "Negotiated Grievance Procedure."
