broida eeo Chapter 21: Forms of Relief Chapter 21: Forms of Relief, Subchapter 05: Compensatory Damages, Section L: Calculating Nonpecuniary Damages, Subsection 02: Evidence, Part a: Evidence Required
a. Evidence Required

Since its holding in Jackson I, supra, the Commission has required "objective evidence" of compensatory damages. Initially, it appeared that the Commission was excluding subjective evidence of damages such as the complainant's own testimony about the effects of the discrimination. Subsequent decisions have clarified that the Commission's use of the term "objective evidence," was not intended to exclude subjective evidence. Rather, the Commission has used the term to require a certain degree of specificity and detail in the evidence submitted in support of a damage claim.

In Szabo v. Secretary of Navy, 01942593 (1994), the Commission moved further toward defining the required "objective evidence," linking the claimed emotional distress to the discriminatory actions:

Nevertheless, appellant also stated that he was seeking compensation for pain and suffering, and mental anguish. The Commission has held that, where a complainant has requested compensatory damages for emotional distress, the agency must request information concerning the non-pecuniary damages prior to issuing an offer of full relief. Mitchell v. Department of Commerce, EEOC Appeal No. 01934120 (March 4, 1994). In addition, the Commission has noted that evidence of emotional distress should include detailed information on physical or behavioral manifestations of the distress, if any; any other information on the intensity of the distress, and the duration of the distress; and examples of how the distress affected appellant both on and off of the job. See Carle v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 01922369 (January 5, 1993). Therefore, because appellant in this case made a claim for non-pecuniary damages for emotional distress, the agency must request that appellant provide some objective evidence linking those damages to the adverse actions at issue herein.

In a series of cases in 1994, the Commission began to articulate with some specificity the types of evidence that it considers "objective." For example, in Adesanya v. Postmaster General, 01933395 (1994), the Commission noted:

[E]vidence of pecuniary losses could take the form, if appellant obtained medical care, of receipts and/or bills for medical care, medication, and transportation to the doctor. Other evidence could take the form of a statement by appellant describing her emotional distress, and statements from witnesses, both on and off the job, describing the distress. Such statements might include information on physical or behavioral manifestations of the distress, if any, and any other information on the intensity of the distress, the duration of the distress, and examples of how the distress affected appellant both on and off the job. In addition, the agency should ask appellant to provide objective and other evidence linking the pecuniary (if any) and nonpecuniary losses to the unlawful discrimination. See Carle v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 01922369 (January 5, 1993) (setting forth some of the types of evidence that an agency should seek when considering a request for compensatory damages).

Agencies have been cautioned by the Commission about giving a narrow construction to the objective evidence requirement. See, e.g., Bergholtz v. Secretary of Navy, 01964961 (1998) ("We find the agency erred in unduly limiting the definition of 'objective evidence' to 'medical proof' of harm caused by alleged discrimination."); see also Gonzalez v. Postmaster General, 01962418 (1998) (damage award based solely on credible testimony of complainant and his wife). An agency is not free to reject medical evidence because it is not provided by a "certified" physician. See Cooper v. Postmaster General, 01972655 (1998) (accepting evidence from a licensed acupuncturist who prescribed herbal remedies).

A generalized claim for $300,000 for emotional distress is not evidence of compensatory damages. The complainant bears the burden of proof on the issue of compensatory damages and must present specific evidence. Rivera v. Secretary of Navy, 01934157 (1994). In Rivera the Commission also expanded on the types of evidence that may be submitted to support a claim for compensatory damages:

[I]n addition to a detailed statement by the individual claiming emotional distress damages, other evidence of such damages could include statements by health care professionals, such as physicians, psychologists, psychiatrists, therapists or counselors, as well as friends, family or coworkers who could attest to the existence, nature and severity of appellant's distress, its duration and causation.

...

Objective evidence could include statements from the complainant concerning his or her emotional pain and suffering, inconvenience, mental anguish, loss of enjoyment of life, injury to professional standing, injury to character and reputation, injury to credit standing, loss of health, and any other nonpecuniary losses that are incurred as a result of the discriminatory conduct. Statements from others, including family members, friends, health care providers, other counselors (including clergy) could address the outward manifestations or physical consequences of emotional distress, including sleeplessness, anxiety, stress, depression, marital strain, humiliation, emotional distress, loss of self-esteem, excessive fatigue, or a nervous breakdown. Objective evidence also may include documents indicating a complainant's actual out-of-pocket expenses related to medical treatment, counseling, and so forth, related to the injury allegedly caused by discrimination. As noted above, the agency should advise the complainant that she or he must establish a connection between the alleged discriminatory action and the resulting injury. In determining damages, the agency is only required to consider objective evidence of damages shown to be a result of the alleged discrimination; the agency is not responsible for remedying pre-existing conditions or for rectifying an individual's health problems in general. We further note that a complainant, in order to establish that compensatory damages were incurred for emotional pain and suffering, may need to present personal and sensitive information to the agency to show that the injury is linked solely or partially to the alleged discriminatory conduct.

In Yancey v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01940945 (1994), the Commission held that the agency must address the issue of compensatory damages where the complainant alleged the discrimination affected his medical and psychological condition, as well as his marriage.

In Carpenter v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01945652 (1995), the Commission held that under-oath testimony is not required to establish a claim for damages. In that case, the complainant submitted a lengthy signed statement detailing her damages. However, an unsigned, unsworn statement submitted by the complainant, addressing the effects of the alleged discrimination, is insufficient to prove compensatory damages. See Verble v. Secretary of State, 01950260 (1996). Although the agency's request could have been more specific regarding the evidence that should have been submitted, it was clear from the complainant's response, which included copies of checks and receipts, that she was aware of the type of objective evidence that was required to support her claim. The Commission in Verble distinguished between testimony and unsworn statements in support of a compensatory damages claim:

Courts have awarded compensatory damages based solely upon the testimony of the complainant. However, in the instant case, the Commission declines to find that the unsigned statement from appellant is equivalent to her testimony. Specifically, had [the complainant] testified as to the alleged damages, the agency would have had the opportunity to question her, and the testimony would have been subject to credibility determinations by a trier of fact.

The agency has an obligation to advise a complainant on the types of evidence required to establish a claim for damages, but it's not clear that a failure on the agency's part to discharge its obligation is harmful error. For example, in Hairston v. Secretary of Education, 0120103308 (2013), the Commission noted that the agency had provided only minimal information to the complainant on damages evidence, also noted that the complainant made only minimal effort to obtain representation. The Commission in Hairston did consider further evidence of damages submitted on appeal explaining:

As a preliminary matter, we agree with Complainant in concluding that the Agency provided a strikingly bare minimal amount of guidance to Complainant in its Compensatory Damages Notice. The Agency never explained to Complainant what type of relief is included in a compensatory damages claim and what form of evidence was needed to meet his burden of proof. For example, we note that the Agency failed to obtain any affidavits from Complainant or advise him to produce affidavits in support of his claim. Of course, Complainant had his own responsibility to obtain the requisite information and the best way for him to do that was to obtain legal representation. Although, Complainant indicates that he attempted to obtain legal representation, it appears as though his efforts were minimal. In addition, while the Agency failed to provide a thorough form of notice to Complainant, we cannot say that it failed to meet the literal terms of our previous order. Accordingly, given the fact that the Agency minimally complied with our previous order and that Complainant had an opportunity to rectify previous misunderstandings on appeal, we conclude that justice is better served by determining the amount of compensatory damages incurred based upon the totality of the record, including Complainant's statement on appeal, rather than remanding this matter back to the Agency for a supplemental investigation.

The evidence submitted must establish both the harm to the complainant and the causal connection between the harm and the discrimination suffered. See, e.g., Davis v. Secretary of Navy, 01952890 (1997) (the submission of insurance statements showing treatment is insufficient evidence because the statements do not address the issue of causation). Where the agency issues a damage award in a final decision and the complainant challenges the amount of that award, the agency may not argue the issue of causation on appeal. The agency's decision that the complainant was entitled to damages establishes the requisite nexus between the discrimination and the harm alleged. See Harris v. Postmaster General, 01A12447 (2002).

Medical evidence is not required to support a claim of damages, though the Commission has noted in several cases that the failure to produce medical evidence can affect the amount of an award. However, the presentation of medical evidence does not guarantee of an award of compensatory damages. See, e.g., Simmons v. Secretary of Air Force, 01A61211 (2006) (medical evidence failed to establish that any harm was caused by agency's discrimination).

In Miller v. Postmaster General, 01956109 (1998), the Commission drew an adverse inference against an agency that refused to provide a copy of the complainant's evidence of damages submitted during agency processing of the case. Although the Commission did not base its award of damages solely on the adverse inference, it clearly held the complainant to a lesser standard in establishing damages in light of the adverse inference.
