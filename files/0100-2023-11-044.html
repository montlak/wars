15. Procedures Other Than EEO, Use of

Sometimes employees lodge complaints of discrimination through the use of dispute resolution procedures other than the EEO process, or through informal channels. When those avenues prove fruitless, the employee then seeks to initiate the EEO process.

In general, the statute of limitations is not waived because an employee has pursued means other than the EEO process for resolving his complaint in the interim. See Placher v. USPS, 01840535 (1985) (contacting a congressional representative does not toll the time limitation); also Tsubota v. Department of Navy, 01832606 (1984). Contacting an agency inspector general with a complaint of disability discrimination likewise does not excuse a failure to timely contact a counselor. See Salinas v. Department of Air Force, 01830658 (1983); also McLoughlin v. Secretary of Treasury, 05A01093 (2003) (report of harassment to Internal Affairs Office did not toll time period for initiating counseling, particularly where report did not occur until two months after the complainant's last day on the job). But see Whipple v. Secretary of Army, 05910586 (1991) (the agency was estopped from asserting untimely counselor contact because the EEO counselor told the complainant she could not initiate counseling since the matter had been raised with the inspector general).

When the complaint is from an applicant for employment, the Commission may be more lenient in deciding to waive the time limit for contacting a counselor. For example, in Bennett v. Secretary of Defense, 01966329 (1997), the Commission found that appellant, an applicant for a position, had presented adequate justification for extending the time limit for seeking EEO counseling where he had attempted to lodge complaints in other forums. Although there was evidence that the appellant suspected discrimination early on, the Commission found that the circumstances in Bennett warranted extending the time period:

The Commission finds that appellant had a reasonable suspicion of discrimination at the time he wrote to the Personnel Office one week after the agency's offer was withdrawn, and he stated:

This letter exists to inform you and the office you represent, I hereby protest the decision involved in this matter due to discrimination, wrongful termination, and civil rights violations. Due to these infringes, I hereby request a hearing on this matter.

Appellant wrote again to that office and also to his Congressman at the end of March. He specifically requested a hearing and other information on expressing his rights. Appellant had also written to the official who interviewed him. The response from the agency was telephone contact by an EEO Counselor in early April. The Counselor states that he explained EEO procedure, but there is no evidence of written material being sent to the appellant. In response, the appellant tells the Counselor that he wants to consult with someone. At that time, appellant was waiting for some type of communication from his Congressman. The response from the Congressman and the agency in late April 1996 was that the agency is continuing to explore means of obtaining authority to hire appellant. The Commission finds that these communications imply that the process is not over, and as a consequence, appellant does not decide to contact the EEO Counselor until July. He files his formal complaint on August 8, 1996.

Note that in Bennett, in addition to contacting his congressman, the complainant on several occasions clearly indicated his intent to challenge the agency's decision on the basis of discrimination. The Commission likely was swayed not only by the complainant's lack of familiarity with the EEO process, but also by the fact that he did contact officials logically connected with the EEO or selection process, and indicated a desire to contest the decision to withdraw his employment offer.

The fact that the employee also uses other, informal methods to attempt to resolve her complaint is not necessarily an indication that she has no intent of pursuing an EEO case. In Golden v. Secretary of Navy, 01982658 (1999), the Commission found that the complainant timely contacted a counselor and exhibited sufficient intent to pursue an EEO complaint, while she simultaneously engaged in extensive discussions with management to resolve the matter informally. The Golden case is instructive because the complainant and the counselor gave affidavits that were directly at odds. The complainant contended that she told the counselor she wished to start the complaint process. The counselor contended that complainant specifically stated that she did not want to initiate counseling. Confronted with such a direct conflict in testimony, the Commission examined the overall conduct of the complainant during the relevant time period and concluded that she had exhibited sufficient intent to initiate the EEO process. The Commission in Golden found:

On appeal, both appellant and the EEO counselor in question provide sworn statements that are completely at odds with respect to appellant's intent when she made contact prior to September 2, 1997. According to the EEO counselor, appellant stated that she did not wish to initiate the EEO process unless her own attempts to resolve the dispute failed. According to appellant, she was under the impression that she initiated the EEO process during the June 18, 1997, meeting, and that she indicated September 3, 1997, as the date of initial contact on the formal complaint in error.

Because this ambiguity cannot be fairly addressed based on these statements alone, we must evaluate the appellant's intent based on her actions during the period in question.

On appeal, appellant states that she contacted the EEO counselor by telephone to arrange a meeting after her supervisor berated her and shouted at her on June 11, 1997. She had a two hour meeting with the EEO counselor on June 18, 1997. Over a two month period, she met several times with management officials about the problems she was having with her supervisor, reporting back each time to the EEO counselor. On or about July 16, 1997, the EEO counselor called appellant to inquire about her progress. At that point, appellant informed the EEO counselor that she would contact her again when she returned from two weeks of leave. According to appellant, she contacted the EEO counselor again in August to check on the status of her case, and was given the impression that it was proceeding in the normal course. The EEO counselor states that this conversation did not occur. However, it appears that appellant's attempts to resolve this matter on her own ceased at this time. After receiving a negative performance evaluation on September 2, 1997, appellant called the EEO counselor to arrange a meeting on September 3, 1997, when she was given a pre-complaint form and Notice of Rights and Responsibilities.

A review of these facts clearly shows that appellant's intent was to preserve her EEO rights by making timely contact with the EEO counselor, but that she also desired to resolve the dispute herself, apparently trying to avoid filing an EEO complaint if possible. She was quite conscientious about maintaining contact with the EEO counselor to report her progress throughout her attempts to settle the dispute, thus demonstrating diligence in pursuing her rights. We find that this continuous and repeated contact between the appellant and the EEO counselor is strong evidence of appellant's intent to pursue the EEO process, especially in conjunction with the two hour meeting which took place on June 18, 1997. The meeting on September 3, 1997, merely formalized the process which had been on-going. We also find that it is certainly plausible that September 3, 1997, was erroneously reported as the initial contact date because it is the same date the pre-complaint form was received by the appellant.

An attempt to use another forum to pursue an EEO complaint can undermine a complainant's contention that he or she did not have a reasonable suspicion of discrimination. For example, in Price v. National Aeronautics and Space Administration, 01994622 (2001), the complainant contended that he was not reasonably aware that reprisal was a basis of discrimination. The Commission found that the agency's EEO poster did not include reprisal as a potential basis for a complaint. However, the Commission rejected the complainant's explanation for his untimely counselor contact where, about a year before that contact, he brought a complaint to the Office of Special Counsel alleging that reprisal for filing an EEO complaint was a prohibited personnel practice.
