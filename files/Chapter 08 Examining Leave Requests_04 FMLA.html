FMLA

Leave under the Family and Medical Leave Act is particularly complex. The leave provided by the FMLA is LWOP, but employees may substitute annual leave, sick leave, and paid parental leave for unpaid leave. Most executive branch federal employees are covered under Title II of the FMLA, but quite a few are covered under Title I. You may even manage both Title I and Title II employees in your unit.

Basic entitlement. The FMLA allows employees to request and receive up to 12 weeks of unpaid leave every year:

  • • For the birth and care of a child.
  • • For the placement of a child with the employee for adoption or foster care.
  • • To care for a parent, spouse, son, or daughter with a serious health condition.
  • • For a serious health condition that makes the employee unable to perform the essential functions of the job.
  • • For any qualifying exigency arising out of the fact that the spouse, son, daughter, or parent of the employee is on covered active duty in the armed forces.
  • • For an employee who is the spouse, son, daughter, parent, or next of kin of a covered servicemember who suffers a serious injury or illness on active military duty. Such an employee is entitled to 26 weeks of leave during a single 12-month period to care for the servicemember.

Annual leave or sick leave may be substituted for LWOP under some conditions.

Leave may be taken:

  • • All at once.
  • • In several blocks of time.
  • • Intermittently -- in hourly or daily increments.

Although this basic entitlement applies to all eligible federal employees, there are some important differences in eligibility and in the details of the entitlement. These depend on which agency an employee works for and on the nature of the employee's appointment.

Serious health conditions

The serious health conditions for which FMLA leave may be used are defined much more restrictively than illness and injury under sick leave regulations. Your HR office is responsible for properly applying the two sets of definitions.

Under the FMLA, a serious health condition is an illness, injury, impairment, or physical or mental condition that includes any one of the following:

  • • Inpatient care involving at least an overnight stay in a hospital or similar facility.
  • • Continuing treatment by a health care provider.
  • • Any period of incapacity due to pregnancy or childbirth, or for prenatal care.
  • • Any period of incapacity or treatment for such incapacity due to a chronic serious health condition that requires periodic visits for treatment and continues over an extended period of time. Includes conditions that cause episodic incapacity (e.g., asthma, diabetes, epilepsy).
  • • A period of incapacity that is permanent or long-term due to a condition for which treatment may not be effective. The individual must be under the continuing supervision of, but need not be receiving active treatment by, a health care provider (e.g., Alzheimer's, severe stroke, or terminal stages of a disease).
  • • Any period of absence to receive multiple treatments (including any period of recovery) by a health care provider for restorative surgery after an accident or other injury or for a condition that would likely result in a period of incapacity in the absence of treatment (e.g., chemotherapy and radiation for cancer, physical therapy for severe arthritis, dialysis for kidney disease).

"Incapacity" refers to the inability of an employee to perform the essential functions of their position. Agencies have the option of providing a list of the essential functions of the position and any specific requirements to the certifying health care provider.

Warning: Although the definition of essential functions comes from disability law, a serious health condition under the FMLA is much broader than a disability under the Rehabilitation Act and the Americans with Disabilities Act. Disability definitions do not apply to FMLA situations. However, if a disability does exist, FMLA leave may be used as a reasonable accommodation.

If FMLA leave is denied to an employee with a disability, this may be considered discriminatory under EEO law.

Conditions not covered. Serious health conditions do not include:

  • • Routine physical, eye, or dental examinations.
  • • Continuing treatment that includes the taking of over-the-counter medications, bed rest, exercise, and similar activities that do not require a visit to a health care provider.
  • • A condition for which cosmetic treatments are administered, unless inpatient hospital care is required or unless complications develop.
  • • An absence because of an employee's use of an illegal substance, unless the employee is receiving treatment for substance abuse by a health care provider or on referral by a health care provider.

Unless there are complications, the definition also excludes:

  • • The common cold.
  • • The flu.
  • • Earaches.
  • • Upset stomach.
  • • Minor ulcers.
  • • Headaches (other than migraines).
  • • Routine dental or orthodontic problems.
  • • Periodontal disease.

Allergies, restorative dental or plastic surgery after an injury, removal of cancerous growth, or mental illness resulting from stress may be serious health conditions only if they require inpatient care or continuing treatment by a health care provider.

Care of family members. If the serious health condition is suffered by a family member, HR must determine whether the employee "is needed to care for" the family member. This is the case if the family member:

  • • Is unable to care for basic medical, hygienic, or nutritional needs or safety.
  • • Requires transport to medical treatment.
  • • Requires psychological comfort and reassurance.
  • • Cannot be cared for by the person normally responsible or requires changes in care that must be arranged by the employee.

Medical certification

How does your HR office determine whether a serious health condition meets the FMLA definition or if an employee is needed to care for a family member with a serious health condition? The regulations provide for medical certification by a health care provider. Under most circumstances, the employee is given a form by HR, has it filled out by the health care provider, and returns it to HR within a specified deadline.

Neither you nor your HR office may contact an employee's health care provider directly for information or clarification if there are any questions about FMLA certification. This is never permitted, even with the employee's permission. The regulations provide for clarification through an agency physician, with the employee's permission, and for second and third medical opinions, if necessary.

And remember, certification under the FMLA, like all employee medical information, is subject to the Privacy Act and must be treated in absolute confidence.

Substitution of annual or sick leave

If your employees fall under Title II, you may not place an employee on FMLA leave unless you confirm that the employee has invoked or intends to invoke the entitlement to this leave. FMLA leave is leave without pay. However, Title II employees may substitute the following types of paid leave for LWOP, as part of their 12-week FMLA entitlement:

  • • Annual leave or sick leave (if applicable to the specific situation).
  • • Advanced annual or sick leave, in accordance with the applicable regulations.
  • • Leave made available under voluntary leave transfer or leave bank programs.

You may not deny the employee's right to substitute this leave. That is, if an employee requests available annual leave for a purpose that qualifies under the FMLA, this leave must be approved. If the employee requests available sick leave for a purpose that qualifies under both FMLA and sick leave regulations, this leave must be approved.

The only exception would be if medical treatment for the employee or family members could be rescheduled to better suit your agency's work requirements -- with the approval of the employee's health care provider.

From an agency's point of view, decisions to substitute paid leave should actually be encouraged, since they reduce the agency's total annual leave obligation. However, if the employee does not wish to substitute paid leave for LWOP under the FMLA, you may not require it under Title II. Note that the employee is required to indicate the intent to substitute paid leave before the leave commences and may not retroactively make the substitution.

The practical result of this situation is sometimes called "leave stacking." Your employee with a serious health condition or with a family member with such a condition could use 12 weeks of unpaid FMLA leave and sick leave as accrued up to certain limits -- as much as an additional 12 weeks of sick leave to care for a family member with a serious health condition -- and you would have to approve it. Furthermore, the employee would still have the full complement of annual leave available.

The use of leave stacking in the context of substitution of paid parental leave is discussed in more detail in the Paid parental leave section.

Use of sick leave and annual leave under Title I. The rules are different for the USPS and other Title I employees. In this case, the agency may designate any qualifying leave, paid or unpaid, as FMLA leave, as long as the employee receives proper notification. Normally, an employee requesting annual leave will not provide the information necessary to determine whether it qualifies as FMLA leave, but if the leave request is denied, the employee may present this information as a justification of the need to take leave.

In this case the agency must determine whether the annual leave request is FMLA-qualifying. If it is, the agency must grant it and may count the annual leave against the 12-week FMLA total. If a sick leave request falls under the FMLA, the agency may determine that it counts against the 12-week total. This has the effect of reducing the agency's total yearly leave obligation.

These decisions must be made by HR, not by you. You should contact HR with any relevant information.

Paid parental leave

The Federal Employee Paid Leave Act, signed into law on Dec. 20, 2019, provides employees with 12 administrative workweeks of paid parental leave under the FMLA for the birth of a child or the placement of a child for adoption or foster care. Paid parental leave can be used for births or placements occurring on or after Oct. 1, 2020.

Employees may substitute paid parental leave for unpaid FMLA leave, just as may be done with sick or annual leave. It's important to understand, however, that paid parental leave only arises under the FMLA. It's not a stand-alone leave entitlement. Therefore, when an employee uses paid parental leave, it counts against the 12-week FMLA leave entitlement. An employee cannot use 12 weeks of paid parental leave, and then an additional 12 weeks of unpaid FMLA leave.

Here are some other important features of paid parental leave:

  • • PPL entitlement is triggered by the occurrence of the birth or placement. PPL cannot be used prior to the birth or placement, even for birth- or placement-related purposes.
  • • The employee does not have to use accrued annual or sick leave before being allowed to use paid parental leave.
  • • Paid parental leave must be used within 12 months of the date of the birth or placement of the child. It does not accumulate for any subsequent use.
  • • The act did not change the FMLA requirement of completion of at least 12 months of service. So employees who have not completed 12 months of federal service by the time they want to start leave are not entitled to paid parental leave -- or any form of FMLA leave.
  • • If two covered federal employees are parents of the same newly born or placed child, each employee would have a separate entitlement to substitute 12 weeks of PPL for their FMLA unpaid leave based on the birth or placement.

Eligibility may occur after birth or placement. Watch for the situation where an employee isn't eligible for PPL at the time of the birth or placement, but becomes eligible later on before the one-year PPL eligibility period ends. To use PPL, an employee must satisfy the general requirements for FMLA eligibility, including more than one year of federal service. So, if an employee has not completed at least a year of service at the time of a qualifying birth or placement, they would not immediately qualify for FMLA -- and, in turn, PPL substitution. However, once the employee completes one year of service, they would become eligible for FMLA and would be able to substitute PPL during the remainder of the year following the birth or placement.

Leave stacking. You also should be aware of leave stacking with PPL usage. Leave stacking occurs when employees use sick leave or annual leave for purposes that would qualify for FMLA leave, and then take their FMLA entitlement as unpaid leave. Employees covered by Title II of FMLA have always been able to stack leave, but the PPL regulations specifically state that an employee may request to use annual leave or sick leave without invoking FMLA. In other words, leave stacking is explicitly authorized. If an employee does this, the agency would exercise its normal authority to approve or disapprove the timing of when the leave may be used. Note that if the employee wants to use sick leave for an authorized reason in connection with the birth or placement of a child (for example, recovery from the delivery or court-ordered bonding for adoption), you generally should approve the sick leave request.

Documentation. The agency may require the employee to provide appropriate documentation it deems necessary to establish that the use of PPL is directly connected to a birth or placement. Appropriate documentation includes:

Childbirth

  • • Birth certificate.
  • • Document naming employee as second parent, such as declaration of paternity or court order of filiation.
  • • Appropriate court documents.
  • • Consular report of birth abroad.
  • • Documentation provided by the child's health care provider.
  • • Hospital admission form associated with the delivery.
  • • Other documentation approved by the agency.

Adoption

  • • Documentation provided by the adoption agency confirming the placement and date of placement.
  • • Letter signed by the parent's/parents' attorney confirming the placement and date of placement.
  • • Immigrant visa for the child issued by U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services.
  • • Adoptive placement agreement.
  • • Independent adoption placement agreement.
  • • Other documentation approved by the agency.

Foster Care

  • • Foster care placement record.
  • • Other documentation from the foster agency confirming the placement and date of placement.
  • • Foster care placement letter issued by the relevant local department of social services or authorized voluntary foster care agency.
  • • Other documentation approved by the agency.

Agreement to return to work. Before starting paid parental leave, the employee must agree in writing to return to work for the agency for at least 12 weeks beginning on the date the leave ends. This requirement may be waived if the employee is unable to return to work because of the continuation, recurrence, or onset of a serious health condition (including mental health) of the employee or the child, as long as it is related to the birth or placement of that child.

If the employee claims to be unable to return to work for these reasons, the agency may require the employee to provide medical certification supporting the claim.

Recovery of cost of health insurance. If the employee fails to return from paid parental leave, the agency may recover from the employee its cost for maintaining the employee's health insurance coverage during the period of the leave. However, this provision does not apply to employees who fail to return due to the continuation, recurrence, or onset of a serious health condition of the employee or the child, or due to any other circumstances beyond the control of the employee.

GAO, Library of Congress, and congressional employees. The act also provides the same entitlement to paid parental leave to the Government Accountability Office, Library of Congress, and congressional employees, who are not covered by Title II of the FMLA. The leave is in addition to other paid vacation, personal, family, medical, or sick leave provided by the employing office.

Monitoring FMLA leave

Successful FMLA administration requires that your agency have a system in place to monitor FMLA use. This is an HR responsibility, and you should work with HR in any FMLA situations that require close monitoring.

In order to plan your unit's work efficiently, you should be aware of the leave situation of all your employees, including remaining FMLA entitlements, the status of those away on FMLA leave (and other forms of leave), and when they are expected to return. Coordinate updating of information with HR and, when necessary, make sure your employees understand their current leave status.

Intermittent leave. FMLA leave may be taken intermittently due to a serious health condition of the employee or a family member if it is medically necessary for planned medical treatment or recovery from the condition. Intermittent leave also may be taken for new-child purposes if both the employee and the agency agree to it.

Managing the use of intermittent FMLA leave can be particularly difficult since the details may be unforeseeable. Once intermittent FMLA leave has been approved for a specific health care reason, it can only be used for that reason. An employee approved for intermittent FMLA leave for cancer treatments or asthma cannot use it for unrelated medical matters.

Discuss with HR what is required to make sure employees receive their correct leave entitlement.

Medical recertification. At agency expense, HR generally may require medical recertification on a periodic basis -- but not more than once every 30 calendar days -- for intermittent leave, leave taken for purposes relating to pregnancy, chronic conditions, or long-term conditions. However, recertification usually may not be requested until the period of absence noted in the original medical certification has passed.

Recertification may be requested more frequently than every 30 calendar days, or before expiration of the minimum duration of the period of incapacity specified on the medical certification, if:

  • • The employee requests that the original leave period be extended.
  • • The circumstances described in the original medical certification have changed significantly.
  • • The agency receives information that casts doubt upon the continuing validity of the medical certification. If you receive this information, let HR know.

Employee benefits and return to work. Agencies are generally required to continue employee benefits, especially health insurance, during a period of FMLA leave as if the employee were not on leave. On completion of leave, the agency must return the employee to the same position or one with equivalent pay, benefits, and other terms. However, employees cannot use their FMLA leave to claim better treatment than employees who were not on leave, for example, in a reduction in force.

The agency may require medical certification of an employee's fitness for duty before allowing an employee to return to work from FMLA leave after the employee's incapacitation (including a period of intermittent leave), if:

  • • Certifications are required for similarly situated employees in similar situations.
  • • The certification applies only to the incapacitation for which the employee was granted FMLA leave.
  • • The incapacitation relates directly to the essential functions of the employee's position.

The certification requirement must clearly be job-related and consistent with business necessity. For example, an attorney could not be required to provide certification after recovering from the amputation of a leg. This might well be required of a warehouse employee with specific physical job requirements. The agency may not seek a second opinion.

Watch for abuse

Whether FMLA leave is paid or unpaid, it permits extensive absence from the workplace, to the point where productivity may be affected. For this reason, it is particularly important to prevent abuse of the system.

Recertification within the permissible limits is one way to discourage abuse. Obtaining second and even third medical opinions is another.

Finally, any indication that FMLA leave is not being used for a legitimate purpose should be investigated, just as with sick leave or workers' compensation abuse. Employees who use FMLA leave for purposes not intended may be disciplined.

In one real-life example, the USPS approved FMLA leave to permit an employee to care for his incapacitated wife. However, surveillance video showed that one day the employee and his wife left their house, went shopping, and performed other activities. Another day, the wife left the house, and the employee remained home. The employee failed either to go to work or to call in and change the type of leave he was on. The MSPB upheld the agency's removal action.

Mistakes to avoid

Do not:

  • • Confuse coverage under Title I and Title II of the FMLA. Title II covers most executive branch federal employees. Title I covers primarily the USPS and some federal employees on temporary or intermittent appointments.
  • • Confuse FMLA and sick leave. Definitions of family members and purposes for which the leave may be used differ considerably.
  • • Insist that employees use available sick or annual leave before using LWOP. This is an employee choice under the FMLA. However, under Title I, the agency may determine that certain sick or annual leave requests fall under the FMLA and substitute them for LWOP as part of the annual 12-week limit.
  • • Insist on too much medical information. You are entitled to know very little. Leave it to your HR office.
  • • Directly contact the employee's health care provider. This is never permitted under the FMLA, even with the employee's permission.

Ask FMG

Q. Can an employee take the 12 weeks of FMLA leave on an installment basis?

A. Yes, it can be used in blocks of time or intermittently, if that is appropriate to the situation.

Q. Do employees have to provide notice of an intention to use FMLA leave?

A. Yes, under Title II, which applies to most federal employees. However, in some cases the incapacity of the employee and the employee's representative may excuse the employee. Agencies may not place employees on FMLA leave if they do not request it. Check with HR before you deny leave or take action for AWOL where FMLA leave might be involved.

Under Title I, no. The U.S. Postal Service or another Title I employer must determine whether any form of leave is FMLA leave, whether or not the employee requests it. The agency also has to give the employee proper notice that it has designated the leave as FMLA leave.

Q. Can I ask for medical certification under the FMLA?

A. Not directly. The employee is subject to detailed medical certification requirements to demonstrate the existence of a "serious health condition" and, in the case of a family member, to demonstrate that the employee "is needed to care for" the family member with the serious health condition. This certification is obtained from the employee's health care provider by HR through a standard procedure and form.

Q. Can I contact the employee's health care provider if I have any questions?

A. No, under no circumstances may you contact an employee's health care provider. Raise any questions with HR. The regulations provide for clarification through an agency physician, with the employee's permission, and for second and third medical opinions, if necessary.

Q. Can I designate an employee's sick leave or annual leave as part of the 12-week FMLA entitlement?

A. No. Under Title II this is the employee's choice. In the case of Title I employees, the HR office will determine when other forms of leave count toward the 12 weeks.

Q. Can a denial of FMLA leave violate EEO law?

A. Yes, if it can be shown to be discriminatory. In particular, although the definition of "serious health condition" is much broader than the definition of disability, if an individual with a disability requests FMLA leave this may be considered a request for reasonable accommodation. A denial of that request could be considered discriminatory.

Q. Do employees on FMLA leave lose benefits?

A. No. Agencies are generally required to continue employee benefits, especially health insurance, during a period of FMLA leave as if the employee were not on leave.

Q. Can employees on FMLA leave lose their jobs?

A. Yes, but only if they otherwise would have lost them, as through a reduction in force. Proceed with caution and check with HR.

Q. Are the FMLA rules different for employees who care for family members suffering from an injury or illness while on active military duty?

A. Yes. Employees are entitled to 26 weeks of leave during a 12-month period to care for a covered family member who suffers a serious injury or illness on active military duty. This includes the 12 weeks of regular FMLA leave and is not in addition to it. This applies to both Title I and Title II employees.

Q. Can agencies require documentation from employees to confirm a family relationship?

A. Yes. Employers are allowed to require employees to provide reasonable documentation of the family relationship. This may take the form of either a simple statement from the employee or documentation such as a birth certificate or court document. The Department of Labor has advised employers that it is the employee's choice whether to provide a simple statement or another type of documentation.

Q. Can grandparents use paid parental leave?

A. Maybe. First, if an employee who is a grandparent can establish an in loco parentis relationship with a grandchild, the employee may be entitled to use FMLA unpaid leave for the birth or placement for adoption or foster care of that grandchild. To qualify for this use of FMLA unpaid leave, an in loco parentis relationship can be established based on day-to-day care alone or financial support alone (as well as on both).

Then, if the employee satisfies all the other requirements for using FMLA unpaid leave, HR will need to determine whether the employee meets the specific requirements for being able to substitute PPL, including that the employee has a continuing parental role with respect to the child. Use of PPL is reserved for periods when an employee is acting in a parental role and engaged in activities directly related to the care of the child, to increase parent-child bonding. So, if the FMLA unpaid leave entitlement was established based solely on financial support, the employee would not qualify for use of PPL. The employee needs to show engagement in the day-to-day care of the child.
