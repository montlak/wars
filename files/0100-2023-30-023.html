III. TYPES OF EQUITABLE RELIEF

Because the EEOC has jurisdiction over all kinds of personnel actions, as well as the terms, conditions, and privileges of employment, the types of relief ordered in EEO cases encompass an exceptionally wide range of actions. Generally, the Commission has the authority to tailor relief to the specific situation.

"Full relief under Title VII is that which makes the complaining party whole." Hamane v. USPS, 01841834 (1986) (citing County of Los Angeles v. Davis, 440 U.S. 625, 99 S. Ct. 1379, 59 L. Ed.2d 642 (1979)). An agency that reduces the grade of an employee and reassigns him cannot claim that full relief has been granted when it restores his former pay grade, but not his former position. In Hamane, the agency dismissed the complaint on the grounds that full relief had been granted. In such instances, the Commission treats the dismissal as a final agency decision issued without a finding of discrimination.

Because Title VII is a "make whole" statute, the Commission has broad authority to fashion relief to the specifics of the case. For example, in Kandarian v. Department of Health and Human Services, 01862104 (1987), the Commission found that discriminatory harassment led to an on-the-job injury requiring that the appellant take sick leave. The Commission determined that the leave could not be charged to the appellant and that he must be recredited with the leave, because that would place him in the same position he would have been in had no discrimination occurred.

Relief should be "designed to identify the source of discrimination and minimize the chance of its recurrence." June v. USPS, 01870663 (1987). In that case, the Commission found the possible remedies for sexual harassment included insulating the appellant from the RMO for a period of time, or educating or disciplining the supervisor. Because of the posture of the June case--the agency had dismissed it for failure to prosecute when the appellant rejected the agency's proposed settlement that she be treated with dignity--the Commission did not order any specific relief, but remanded it to the agency for further processing.

That the Commission has broad authority to fashion relief to the individual case was made amply clear in In re USPS, 05850282 (1987):

EEOC Regulations provide that when the Commission finds that an employee has been discriminated against, the agency shall include one or more of, but need not be limited to the actions listed at 29 CFR 1613.271(b)(1) through (5) (emphasis added). The regulations do not expressly state what other remedies are available; however, the Commission is empowered by law and regulation to fill in the interstices of the regulations through policy, rule, or regulation. To that end, on September 11, 1984, the Commission announced its intent to achieve certainty and predictability of enforcement in those situations where the agency has reason to believe that a law it enforces has been violated. The Commission recognizes that the basic effectiveness of its law enforcement program is dependent upon securing prompt, comprehensive and complete relief for all persons directly affected by violations of the statutes enforced by the Commission. Therefore, the Commission set forth the policy that in negotiating settlements, in drafting prayers for relief in litigation pleadings, and in issuing federal sector Commission decisions or Orders, obtaining full remedial, corrective and preventive relief is the standard by which the agency is to be guided. The Commission has also stated that this type of relief is not designed for punitive purposes. Rather, this relief is to be tailored to cure or correct the particular source of the identified discrimination and to minimize the chance of its recurrence. See "EEOC Policy Statement on Remedies and Relief of Individual Cases of Unlawful Discrimination." (February 5, 1985)....
