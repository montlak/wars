7. Disability Discrimination Claims

In disability discrimination claims, the burden is on the complainant to establish that he or she has a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits a major life activity. However, it is the agency's responsibility to investigate claims of discrimination. In Bartuseck v. Postmaster General, 01A45204 (2006), the Commission reversed the agency's finding of no discrimination on the basis that the complainant had not established he was a qualified individual with a disability and remanded the case for a supplemental investigation. The Commission found that although the evidence did not establish that the complainant was substantially limited in a major life activity, it also found that the agency had not investigated the severity, frequency or duration of the complainant's symptoms. Finding the investigation was inadequate, the Commission in Bartuseck, noted:

While it is clear that complainant's anxiety disorder and depression with resulting panic attacks affect his ability to sleep, think, concentrate and work, it is unclear to what extent his condition impacts these major life activities. We note that while the record does contain information on the type of symptoms complainant experiences during panic attack episodes, it does not provide details on the frequency and severity of complainant's symptoms. Although there is evidence that complainant's condition affected his ability to sleep, we note that there is insufficient evidence regarding the degree to which his sleep is affected or the frequency of his sleep problems as they relate to his depression and anxiety disorder. Thus, we find the record is inadequately developed to determine the effect of complainant's anxiety disorder and depression on his major life activities.

There is no question that complainant bears the burden of proof in demonstrating that he is substantially limited in a major life activity because of his anxiety and depressive disorders. See Murphy v. United Parcel Service, 527 U.S. 516, 521-523 (1999). On the other hand, the agency is charged with the obligation to develop an adequate investigative record.

Specifically, the requirement that an agency investigate complaints of discrimination is codified at 29 C.F.R. § 1614.108. The agency has a duty to develop an impartial and appropriate factual record upon which to make findings on the claims raised by the written complaint. An appropriate factual record is one that allows a reasonable fact finding to draw conclusions as to whether discrimination occurred. See 29 C.F.R. § 1614.108(b). The investigator is required to conduct a thorough investigation--identifying and obtaining all relevant evidence from all sources regardless of how it may affect the outcome. EEOC Management Directive (MD)-11O, p. 6-8 (Nov. 9, 1999). Therefore, an investigator must exhaust those sources of information likely to support the positions of complainant and the agency. Id.

In particular, in investigating a claim of disability discrimination, the agency must ensure that the investigator "asks the right questions" of complainant, ones designed to elicit pertinent evidence on the threshold issues of: whether complainant has an impairment; whether it affects a major life activity; and whether it substantially limits a major life activity. Carr v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01A43665 (May 18, 2006).

In the present case, we find the agency failed to develop an adequate evidentiary record because it contains insufficient information upon which to determine whether complainant is substantially limited in a major life activity because of his anxiety and depression disorders. Accordingly, the case is remanded for a supplemental investigation.
