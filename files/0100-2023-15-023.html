E. VENUE

Although they often are the same in a given case, the venue of the complaint and the site of the hearing are two distinct matters. Venue addresses the geographic office of the EEOC that is assigned to the complaint. The site of the hearing is simply the location where the hearing will take place. Particularly where the complaint oriGINAtes from an agency located in a large city, venue and the site of the hearing likely will be the same. In EEOC administrative proceedings, the terms venue and hearing site tend to be used interchangeably.

The EEOC has several district and field offices throughout the country. EEOC administrative judges prefer to hold hearings in those EEOC offices that have hearing facilities, but routinely travel to other facilities for hearings. In those cases where the EEOC district office does not have an available hearing facility, the judge will set the locale of hearing and order the agency to provide a suitable hearing site at that locale. The hearing site can be an agency facility, a facility provided by another government agency, or a private facility.

Pursuant to EEOC Order 965 (January 4, 1982), an appellant had three choices of where to pursue an EEO complaint: (1) the location where the alleged discriminatory act was committed; (2) the location where the relevant employment records are maintained; or (3) the location where the appellant would have worked but for the alleged discrimination.

Under MD-110, the administrative judge is responsible for setting the hearing site. If either party seeks to change the hearing site, it must submit such requests to the administrative judge in writing and must explain, in detail, its reasons for seeking the change of hearing situs. The administrative judge can require further information before making a ruling. MD-110, at 7-4 to 7-6, provides:

E. The Site of the Hearing

Appendix N of the Management Directive is a list of the addresses of the Commission district and field offices, their geographic jurisdictions, and where federal employees and applicants should send hearing requests. Hearing requests are sent to the district office having jurisdiction over the agency facility where the complaint arose. In an agency's written acknowledgment of a complaint or an amendment to a complaint, the agency must advise the complainant of the Commission office and its address where a request for a hearing shall be sent. Where two or more complaints have been consolidated and the Commission district or field offices identified in the agency's complaint acknowledgment letter differ, the office identified in the last filed complaint will govern the location of the office to which the hearing request shall be made. Should the agency's organizational component where the complaint arose not fall within one of the geographical jurisdictions shown in Appendix N, the agency should contact the following office for guidance:

Equal Employment Opportunity Commission

Office of Field Programs

Attention: Hearings Coordinator

131 M Street, NE.

Washington, D.C. 20507

Email at: info@eeoc.gov

Upon receipt of a hearing request, the Administrative Judge assigned to hear the complaint will determine the site of the hearing. Within his/her discretion, the Administrative Judge is authorized to conduct the hearing in the Commission district or field office, in a Commission area or local office, at the agency's organizational component where the complaint arose or at such other location or by virtual conference as s/he may determine appropriate within a local commuting distance from the agency's component unless otherwise agreed to by the parties. In determining the hearing site, the Administrative Judge should consider factors such as the location of the parties; the location of the Commission district, area, and local offices; the number and location of witnesses; the location of records; travel distances for the Administrative Judge, the parties, and witnesses; travel costs; the availability of sources of transportation; and other factors as may be appropriate including the availability of appropriate virtual conferencing equipment.

Similarly, where an Administrative Judge is considering whether the hearing should be held by video conferencing, there are a number of factors that should be considered before electing to proceed. These factors include the availability and proximity to the participants of the video-conferencing facilities; the adequacy of the available video-conferencing facilities, including any technological issues; the cost to the respondent agency (if any) balanced against the savings in travel time for all parties and the Administrative Judge; the number of expected participants; and the objections of the parties, if any. Should a party object to conducting the hearing by video conference, the Administrative Judge will document for the record both the nature of the objection and his/her ruling on the objection, including the reasons therefore. See Allen v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01A51259 (Aug. 21, 2006).

If the Administrative Judge sets a hearing by video conference or a hearing site that is outside the local commuting area of the agency's organizational component where the complaint arose, the agency must bear all reasonable video-conferencing costs if any, or travel expenses of complainants, their authorized representatives, agency representatives, and all witnesses approved by the Administrative Judge, except that an agency does not have the authority to pay the travel expenses of the complainant or the complainant's witnesses or representatives if they are not federal employees.

F. Request for Change in Venue

Should either party desire that a hearing be held within the jurisdictional area of another EEOC district office, it must submit a request, in writing, to the Administrative Judge assigned to the case in the appropriate EEOC district or field office having jurisdiction over the agency's organizational component where the complaint arose. In its request, the party must set out, in detail, its reasons and justification for the requested change. The Administrative Judge will rule on the request only after the directors of the concerned EEOC district offices, or their designees, have conferred on the matter.

The Judges' Handbook at 7-2 specifically directs administrative judges to consider the factors listed in MD-110 in determining the hearing location.

If the administrative judge approves the change of venue request by an agency, the agency may be required to pay the complainant's costs associated with the change in venue, in advance. The costs may include travel fees for the complainant, his representative and witnesses. In cases involving applicants for employment, costs are not payable in advance but may be recovered if the complainant prevails. In Gessner v. Secretary of Transportation, FAA, 01902081 (1990), the Commission explained:

The payability of such costs must be determined by the agency on a case-by-case basis, consistent with applicable laws, travel regulations, and decisions of the U.S. Comptroller General with regard to an agency's authority to make advance payment of such costs. See, e.g., Matter Of: Expenses of Outside Applicant/Complainant to Travel to Agency EEO Hearing B-202845, September 29, 1982, in which the Comptroller General decided that in the absence of specific authority, an agency may not pay in advance the travel expenses of an outside applicant/complainant to attend an equal employment opportunity hearing requested by the complainant. Such expenses, however, may be recovered by the complainant should he/she prevail on the complaint. The management directive provides further:

It is not the intent of this section to attempt a delineation of the many and varied circumstances which might arise with respect to the payability of such costs which may depend upon the nature of the cost involved and the status of the individual involved. Since, in the vast majority of cases, complaints will be heard in the agency organizational component where the complaint arose, it is anticipated that such questions will arise relatively infrequently. When such questions do arise, however, agencies should seek advice from their appropriate legal counsel's office and obtain a legal opinion as to the legality of the advance payment issue at hand....

(Emphasis in oriGINAl.)

In In re Department of Housing and Urban Development, 05850012 (1987), the Commission gave the following explanation of the difference between requests for change of venue by complainants and agencies:

In the discussion of the provisions of 42 USC 2000e(f)(3), EEOC Order 965 provides that a hearing "will normally be held at the agency facilities where the complaint arose."...EEOC Order 965 specifically provides that a complainant may request that the hearing be held at the other venues set forth above only when "the complainant is unable to maintain or support the action if the hearing were to be held at the agency facility where the complaint arose." In such case, EEOC Order 965 further provides that:

The Complaints Examiner should set the hearing at one of those other locations, unless the agency agrees to reimburse the complainant for any additional travel cost incurred by him/her or his/her representative by attending the hearing at the agency facility where the complaint arose....

An agency's request to hold the hearing at the other venues is treated differently than a complainant's request. EEOC Order 965 specifically provides that:

If the agency requests that the hearing be held at a location other than the agency facility where the complaint arose, the Complaints Examiner will accede to the request, even if the case, due to a change in geographic jurisdiction, must be transferred to another Commission office. In this instance, the agency must first be advised that, in accordance with EEOC Management Directive 501, the agency will be held liable for the costs of transporting all parties to the hearing to the new location (to include the Complaints Examiner, regardless of their location and federal employment status.)

...

There could be situations where an agency's request is so unreasonable that the Examiner should not accede to the request even if the agency affirms its obligations under MD 501....

(Emphasis in oriGINAl.)

The In re Department of Housing and Urban Development case was based on EEOC Order 965. That order has now been superseded by MD-110, which grants the administrative judge greater discretion in deciding on requests for a change in hearing site. Still, the general rules for complainants and agencies set down in that case likely continue to hold true. A complainant's request for a change in hearing site should be granted if the complainant cannot maintain the action in the chosen hearing location, unless the agency agrees to reimburse the complainant for additional travel costs involved. On the other hand, an agency's request for a change in hearing site should be granted as long as it agrees to defray any extra costs to the complainant.

The Commission upheld the administrative judge's grant of a change of venue to the agency when there was some question as to where the actual nonselection occurred, all of the agency witnesses were at the hearing location sought by the agency, the agency's witnesses had been approved to testify, and the complainant's witnesses had yet to be approved. In re Department of Housing and Urban Development, supra. The Commission also rejected an argument by appellant that the agency was in violation of MD 501 because it only agreed to pay "reasonable" travel costs and not "all" travel costs, noting it "declines to hold that a complainant may not be held to any standard of reasonableness." Id.

An examiner's order granting the request of the agency for a change of venue for the hearing was arbitrary when it was submitted only 30 days prior to the hearing, the appellant opposed the change of venue, and the reasons for his opposition were legitimate, i.e., most of the witnesses were located at the site where the hearing oriGINAlly was set and the location was centrally convenient to witnesses who would be required to travel. Stith v. Department of Health and Human Services, 01830432 (1984).
