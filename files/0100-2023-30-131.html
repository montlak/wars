L. CALCULATING NONPECUNIARY DAMAGES

Agencies and complainants alike awaited some definitive rulings on the part of the EEOC that would begin to take nonpecuniary compensatory damages out of the realm of theory and into the realm of actual dollars and cents. Obviously, Landgraf substantially delayed those rulings while the Commission waited to see in which cases it would be possible to recover compensatory damages. Several factors have emerged in the past few years as determinative of the amount of nonpecuniary damage awards. The Commission will look at the degree to which the harm was caused by the agency's discrimination and the degree to which it was caused by other factors. It will also look at the nature, severity, and duration of the harm. Particularly determinative in large damage awards issued by the Commission has been the fact that, in those cases, the duration of the harm is such that its effects continue well after the litigation is concluded.

It is worth noting that the Commission does not consider the type of discrimination perpetrated against the complainant, nor does it directly consider the egregiousness of any agency actions. To focus on those issues would run the risk of converting a compensatory damage award into a punitive damage award--i.e., an award that punishes the agency for its conduct rather than compensating the complainant for the harm suffered. The type of discrimination and the egregiousness of the agency's actions factor into a damage award only indirectly, insofar as those issues may impact on the credibility of the complainant's evidence on the extent of damages. For example, in a given case, a factfinder may be less likely to credit a complainant's testimony that the discrimination had a wide-ranging psychological impact if the complaint involves a failure to promote as opposed to a hostile environment claim. As a general rule, one would expect to see more harm in the latter situation. In Taber v. Postmaster General, 01983780 (2001), the Commission, relying on United States v. Balistrieri, 981 F.2d 916, 932 (7th Cir. 1992), cert. denied, 114 S. Ct. 58 (1993), noted:

The more inherently degrading or humiliating the defendant's action is, the more reasonable it is to infer that a person would suffer humiliation or distress from that action; consequently, somewhat more conclusory evidence of emotional distress will be acceptable to support an award for emotional damages.

See also Bustamante v. Postmaster General, 0120120185 (2013).

When an agency makes a damage award on the premise that the amount is what the complainant requested and the agency is mistaken, the amount of the award must be reassessed. See Campbell v. Postmaster General, 01961474 (1999) (agency awarded $3,000 in damages, but counsel had requested $300,000).

The Commission requires that its administrative judges explain compensatory damages awards. However, it appears that the Office of Federal Operations is not required to give such an explanation. See, e.g., Spencer v. Secretary of Treasury, 07A10035 (2003) (judge's award for $40,000 for failing to provide a rationale and then awarding $5,000 with virtually no explanation of its own).

The purpose of compensatory damages is to compensate the victim of discrimination for his or her suffering and not to punish the employer for its discrimination. For example in Nannie D. v. Secretary of Army, 0720150021 (2016), the Commission found:

Non-pecuniary damages are available to compensate the injured party for actual harm, even where the harm is intangible. Carter v. Duncan-Higgins, Ltd., 727 F.2d 1225 (D.C. Cir. 1984). Emotional harm will not be presumed simply because the complainant is a victim of discrimination. The existence, nature, and severity of emotional harm must be proved. The method for computing non-pecuniary damages should typically be based on a consideration of the severity and duration of harm. Carpenter v. Department of Agriculture, EEOC Appeal No. 01945652 (July 17, 1995). We note that for a proper award of non-pecuniary damages, the amount of the award should not be 'monstrously excessive' standing alone, should not be the product of passion or prejudice, and should be consistent with the amount awarded in similar cases. See Ward-Jenkcins v. Department of the Interior, EEOC Appeal No. 01961483 (March 4, 1999) (citing Cygaar v. City of Chicago, 865 F.2d 827, 848 (7th Cir. 1989)).

As a result, the egregiousness of the employer's conduct does not factor directly into the calculation of damages. It does, however, come into the equation indirectly in that the "more inherently degrading or humiliating the agency action, the more reasonable it is to infer that complainants would suffer humiliation or distress from the agency action." Heffley v. Postmaster General, 07A40138 (2005).
