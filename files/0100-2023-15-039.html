K. BIFURCATED DAMAGES HEARINGS

While the administrative judge has the authority to decide whether to bifurcate a case, he or she must advise the parties of the decision in advance of the hearing. The Judges' Handbook at 7-10 provides:

1. Taking Evidence on Relief at the Hearing on Liability

The Administrative Judge has the sole discretion to order the parties to present evidence on relief as part of the hearing on liability but must advise the parties prior to the hearing as to whether they should be prepared to do so. Factors the Administrative Judge may consider in exercising this discretion include: impact on the length of the hearing; number of witnesses to testify about relief; amount and kind of relief sought; and the effect on docket management. If attorney's fees and costs are requested as part of the relief, the Administrative Judge should set a schedule for submission of the petition for attorney's fees and costs and the agency's opposition. The Administrative Judge should make clear to the parties that the agency cannot take final action and appeal rights do not attach until the Administrative Judge has issued a decision that encompasses liability, relief, attorney's fees, and costs, if appropriate.

2. Bifurcating the Hearing

An Administrative Judge may choose to bifurcate the hearing between the liability phase and the relief phase. If the Administrative Judge bifurcates the hearing, the Administrative Judge should inform the parties as early as possible and ensure that they understand the process. Factors to be considered when deciding whether to bifurcate the hearing include: expected length and complexity of a bifurcated hearing; the interests of the parties in minimizing expenses prior to a decision on liability; and docket management considerations of the Administrative Judge and the District Office. The same procedures described above should be followed for the hearing on relief.

3. Determining Relief Based on a Written Record

If the circumstances warrant, the Administrative Judge may determine relief based on written submissions. The Administrative Judge should not make this determination on a written record if the credibility of the complainant or key witnesses is material to the determination of relief.

But the Commission has also held that the judge has the discretion to change his or her mind at the hearing. In Hardman v. Postmaster General, 05990591 (2001), the administrative judge initially determined the hearing would be bifurcated. However, at the hearing, the judge changed his mind and decided to take evidence of damages. The complainant's representative did not object and put on the complainant's testimony as well as documentary evidence on the issue of damages. On reconsideration, the complainant argued that he was not given an opportunity to present evidence of damages. The Commission rejected that argument without lengthy analysis. Although not specifically mentioned in the Hardman decision, the persuasive factors were likely the failure of the complainant's representative to object at the hearing or raise the issue during the initial appeal.

The last provision of MD-110 is worth noting. The administrative judge is not required to hold an actual hearing on the issue of damages but can limit evidence to written submissions. See, e.g., West v. Postmaster General, 07A30129 92004) (rejecting the complainant's argument that she was deprived of a hearing on damages and noting "the record contains sufficient evidence pertaining to complainant's alleged damages...for a determination to made in this regard").
