D. SEXUAL ORIENTATION; TRANSGENDER STATUS/GENDER IDENTIFICATION

Claims of transgender and/or sexual orientation discrimination are covered under Title VII as sex-based discrimination. Bostock v. Clayton County, Georgia, 590 US ___, Nos. 17-1618, 17-1623, 18-107 (June 2020) ("[a]n employer who fires an individual merely for being gay or transgender violates Title VII.") That is the current state of the law.

For decades, however, the EEOC and the courts held that "sex" as a protected basis under Title VII did not extend protection on the basis of sexual orientation and transgender status. See Smith v. Department of Navy, 01841205 (1986) (holding that, based on a plain language reading of the Statute, Title VII did not extend to a transsexual employee the right to have her true sexual identity protected); Smith v. Department of Navy, 01841210, (1986) (affirming its view that Title VII did not cover claims of discrimination based on sexual orientation). Historically, the word "sex" was recognized only as extending to an individual's gender assigned at birth. Several unsuccessful attempts had even been made to amend Title VII to include sexual orientation as a basis of discrimination. See, e.g., Employment Nondiscrimination Act, S. 811, H.R. 1397.

This thinking began to change when the Supreme Court held that discrimination based on sex stereotypes or failure to conform to those stereotypes is unlawful sex-based discrimination. Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins, 490 U.S. 228 (1989). To bring sexual orientation and transgender status within the ambit of Title VII's protection against sex-based discrimination, the Commission and some courts began to rely on the Price Waterhouse gender stereotyping analysis.

Then, in 2012, the Commission decidedly held in Macy v. Attorney General, 0120120821 (2012), and Day v. Postmaster General, 0120122376 (2013), "gender identity, change of sex, and/or transgender status" are protected sex based statuses under Title VII. A few years later, the Commission recognized sexual orientation as a protected basis in Baldwin v. Secretary of Transportation, 0120133080 (2015).

Some circuit courts also began to dispense with the stereotyping analysis, finding simply that sexual orientation and gender identification are covered bases under Title VII. See Zarda v. Altitude Express, Inc., 883 F.3d 100 (2nd Cir. 2018) (sexual orientation); EEOC v. R.G. & G.R. Harris Funeral Homes, Inc., 884 F.3d 560 (6th Cir. 2018) (transgender and transitioning status); Hively v. Ivy Tech Community College of Indiana, 853 F.3d 339 (7th Cir. 2017) (sexual orientation). Other courts continued to hold that sexual orientation is not covered under Title VII as sex-based discrimination. Bostock v. Clayton County, Georgia, No. 17-13801 (11th Cir. 2018) (sexual orientation); Evans v. Georgia Regional Hospital, et al., 2017 WL 943925 (11th Cir. 2017) (sexual orientation). Still others continued to rely on the stereotyping analysis to bring these types of claims within Title VII protections. Prowel v. Wise Business Forms, Inc., 579 F.3d 285 (10th Cir. 2009) (sexual orientation).
