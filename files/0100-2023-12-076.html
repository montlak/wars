g. Discriminatory Actions Committed by Independent Contractors or Consultants

An agency is liable for the actions of a third party, such as an independent consultant or contractor, when it knows or should have known of the consultant's or contractor's conduct and fails to take immediate and appropriate corrective action. For example, in Pullano v. Postmaster General, 01A22070 (2003), the complainant entered a last chance settlement agreement with the agency that required that she meet with an EAP counselor, who was an independent contractor with the agency. The complainant alleged that the counselor required her to provide him with medical records and see a psychiatrist at her own expense. In upholding the agency's dismissal of the complaint for failure to state a claim, the Commission in Pullano noted:

The agency is not relieved of liability merely because the EAP Counselor is a contractor, not an agency official. See Nicholson v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05901141 (Dec. 6, 1990) (failure to notify claimant of group therapy sessions with EAP Counselor states a claim, even though EAP Counselors are not agency employees). To the contrary, "an employer is liable for harassment by a co-worker or non-employe[e] if management knew of should have known of the misconduct, unless the employer can show that it took immediate and appropriate corrective action." EEOC Enforcement Guidance, Vicarious Employer Liability for Unlawful Harassment by Supervisors, at 18, n.58 (June 18, 1999) (citing 29 C.F.R. 1604.11(d)). Where the employer knows or should have known about the contractor or non-employee's conduct, but fails to take immediate and corrective action, dismissal for failure to state a claim is inappropriate. Bautista v. Department of Defense, EEOC Appeal No. 01A12388 (June 12, 2002). In such cases, the Commission considers the extent of the employer's control, and any other legal responsibility the employer may have with respect to the non-employee's conduct. Id.; Fauntroy v. Department of the Treasury, EEOC Appeal No. 01995207 (Aug. 18, 2000) (citing 29 C.F.R. 1604.11(e)).

In the present case, unlike Fauntroy and Bautista, complainant has not alleged that the agency had control over the contractor's actions, nor that complainant made management aware of the employer's actions. Accordingly, she fails to state a claim. Accordingly, the agency's dismissal is AFFIRMED.
