H. BAR STATUS OF COUNSEL

The fact that a member of the bar was in "inactive" status at the time he represented the complainant is not a bar to an award of attorney fees. In Wynn v. Secretary of Treasury, 01200636271 (2007), the complainant's attorney was a member of the Mississippi bar, but was in "inactive" status. The agency argued that status precluded an award of attorney fees. The Commission rejected the agency's argument noting that it had provided no support for the proposition "that bar status (active or inactive) has an effect on entitlement to attorney's fees." The Commission also noted that the agency's argument was disingenuous considering that, prior to representing the complainant, the representative had spent 30 years as an estate tax attorney for the agency and had been in "inactive" bar status the entire time.

Citing to Wynn, the Commission in Bryant F. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 2020002864 (2021), reiterated that challenges to attorney fees awards based technicalities like bar admission and resumes will not prevail:

Though the Agency denied Complainant's request for attorney's fees based on technical grounds, such as Attorney-2's failure to provide a resume and show that she is a member in good standing with the state bar, we find such technical denials to be inconsistent with both our regulations and case law, which impose a strong presumption of entitlement to attorney's fees for the prevailing party in an EEO case. In Wynn v. Dep't of the Treasury, EEOC Appeal No. 0120063627 (Feb. 8, 2007), we addressed a similar factual pattern, where the agency disallowed attorney's fees on the grounds that the attorney of record in that case was an inactive member of the bar, and not a member in good standing. Though the Commission ultimately found the attorney to be in good standing with the state bar, the Commission was unpersuaded by the agency's reliance on minor technical grounds as the basis for the denial.

In finding the Agency's denial to be unwarranted, the Commission emphasized that the agency adduced no evidence showing that the attorney's bar status had any effect on Complainant's underlying entitlement to attorney fees. Given the strong presumption of entitlement, we too are disinclined to deny Complainant's request to recoup attorney fees based on minor technical grounds. We note that the Agency itself acknowledged in its response to Complainant's request for attorney fees that Complainant was indeed entitled to attorney fees; however, the Agency's final decision unilaterally denied Complainant's request for attorney fees. We ultimately agree with both Complainant and the Agency's oriGINAl position that Complainant, as the prevailing party, is entitled to attorney fees and costs. See also Orlando O. v. Attorney General, 2021003074 (2021) (rejecting agency's modification of attorney fee award because the "complainant failed to provide "sufficient information about the skills and qualifications" of her attorneys; also noting the agency's failure to raise the issue at the hearing on damages).

However, in Conrad v. Attorney General, 0120111519 (2011), the Commission found it was proper for the administrative judge to reduce counsel's contractual rate of $150 per hour to $90 per hour during a several month period when his license to practice law was suspended and he was acting as a paralegal.

Normally, $150 per hour for the services of someone who is not a lawyer, such as a law clerk or paralegal, would be quite high. However, in Page-Sanchez v. Postmaster General, 0720050080 (2007), the Commission approved that award for a law school graduate who's bar application was pending and who assisted counsel.
