e. Job Tasks

In an EPA case, the focus is not on job descriptions or titles but on job requirements and performance. Simkins Finucan v. Postal Rate Commission, 01914057, E13 (1993). In that case, the Commission found that a female attorney's job was not comparable to those of higher paid male attorneys. Noting that comparison of skills and responsibilities required for a position is most problematic when dealing with professional or executive employees, the Commission found that even though some job tasks were the same, there was a difference in the level of difficulty of assignments and in the supervision required. "The difficulty appellant experiences in handling more complex matters, and the increased supervision she received, is relevant, therefore, to a determination of whether her job was substantially equal to the work of the male comparisons." Id.

In the same case, the Commission also found that it was not dispositive that a U.S. district court found a Title VII violation regarding another female employee's complaint. Although the court generally described the work of the female employee as "comparable" to that of the male, "this finding does not rise to the level necessary to establish a prima facie case under the EPA." Id.

The Commission came to a similar result in Complainant v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 0120130592 (2015), by comparing actual job duties:

Complainant, a Performance Improvement Analyst, compared herself to a male Program Manager (Candidate 3) and asserted that they performed the same duties yet he was paid more. A review of the record reveals that the coworker had a different job title than Complainant. Additionally, the male coworker and Complainant had different duties. The record reveals that while both Complainant and the male Program Manager shared responsibility for executing the five key elements of the performance improvement plan (assisting in the Program Evaluation Report (PER) screening committee; reducing human errors; managing and ensuring the self-assessment program; benchmarking; and operating experience), the male Program Manager performed other duties and responsibilities Complainant did not perform. Specifically, the record reveals that Complainant only analyzed data within departments and presented her results to her manager for approval. In contrast, the record reveals that the male Program Manager not only analyzed data from his subordinates, but drew conclusions from that data, put actions in place based on his conclusions, and directed, managed, and supervised his subordinates who provided the data to him. The record confirms Complainant did not have the supervisory or managerial responsibility that the male Program Analyst had in his position. As a result, we find that Complainant failed to establish that she performed "equal" work to the male Program Manager.

See also Complainant v. Secretary of Agriculture, 0120140620 (2015), for a comparison of specific duties. In Stefan C. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120132211 (2017), discussed supra, the Commission also considered the complexity of the work performed.

Overlaps between or among jobs is also not dispositive evidence of substantially equal work. Caitlyn H. v. Secretary of Defense, 0120160654 (2018), provides a good example:

According to Complainant, she was subjected to discrimination based on sex and age when she was paid less than her colleagues for performing the same work. Complainant alleged that there was no clear delineation in the work performed by GS-11 and GS-12 IT Specialists between 2011 and 2014. Complainant averred that all Service Desk employees answered calls for assistance at random and that there was only one GS-12 IT Specialist who performed duties that she did not perform. According to S3, he was Complainant's second-level supervisor until April 2012. S3 averred that while he supervised the Service Desk GS-12 employees were expected to provide a faster response and resolution of trouble tickets than GS-11 employees. S2 stated that he became Complainant's supervisor in April 2012. According to S2, in October 2012 he gave GS-12 IT Specialists additional responsibilities, and in October 2013 he implemented higher performance standards for GS-12 IT Specialists. Complainant stated that in late 2014 S2 attempted to create more of a division in the work performed by the different grade levels by designating GS-12 IT Specialists as subject matter experts (SMEs) in various areas.

The record contains the GS-11 and GS-12 IT Specialist position descriptions, and the duties and supervisory control levels are different for the positions. Complainant averred that sometimes on weekend shifts she works without a GS-12 IT Specialist present and that she is therefore responsible for additional responsibilities that would ordinarily be handled by a GS-12 employee. According to S2, Complainant received a performance award in 2014 for taking on more responsibility such as when she worked without a GS-12 IT Specialist present and prepared the daily brief at the end of her shift.

...

In the present case, we find that Complainant failed to establish a prima facie case because Complainant and her male GS-12 coworkers did not perform substantially equal work. Although there was some overlap between the work performed by Complainant and the work performed by her coworkers, the record reflects that the GS-12 IT Specialists had higher standards in their performance plans, performed additional duties, and had additional responsibilities.

Assuming arguendo that Complainant established a prima facie case, we find that the difference in pay was justified by a factor other than sex, as there were three GS-12 IT Specialists who worked at the Service Desk, and a fourth, C3, was promoted in 2014. Accordingly, we find that Complainant failed to establish a violation of the Equal Pay Act.
