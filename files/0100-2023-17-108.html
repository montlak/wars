X. REQUESTS FOR RECONSIDERATION

There is no further right of appeal once the Office of Federal Operations issues its final decision. But under 29 CFR 1614.405(b), within 30 days of receipt of a final decision by the OFO, a party may request reconsideration on the basis that:

(1) The appellate decision involved a clearly erroneous interpretation of material fact or law; or

(2) The decision will have a substantial impact on the policies, practices or operations of the agency.

The Commission also has the authority to reopen and reconsider its decisions without motion. See 29 CFR 1614.405(b). The Commission still retains the right to deny a request for reconsideration for failure to meet the criteria, but reopen the case on its own motion. See Morita v. Secretary of Air Force, 05960450 (1998). The Commission's decision on a request for reconsideration is final and the parties have no further administrative recourse, but the Commission does retain the inherent authority to reopen a reconsideration decision on its own motion to correct manifest error. See, e.g., Kleinman v. Postmaster General, 05930493 (1993).

Decisions from OFO must be read carefully, particularly those sections of the decision which describe any further rights of the parties. For example, in Fox v. General Services Administrator, 01942731 (1995), the Commission held that the complainant was barred from challenging a decision to remand his complaint for further investigation after the supplemental investigation had taken place and a new final agency decision had issued:

Regarding appellant's contentions on appeal, the Commission notes that appellant was properly informed of his right to request reconsideration of the Commission's decision in Appeal No. 01932838. Neither appellant nor the agency requested reconsideration; accordingly, that decision became final on October 21, 1993. Under the "law of the case" doctrine, legal or factual determinations once made are generally binding in subsequent proceedings in the same case. Plunkett v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05920288 (May 14, 1992). Having failed to contest in a timely fashion the Commission's decision to remand the case for only a supplemental investigation and a new FAD, appellant may not now assert error in that decision. See id.

The problem is that a decision remanding a case for further processing at the agency level might not be understood to be a "final" decision of OFO subject to reconsideration. The key, it would seem in Fox, was that the parties were put on notice that they could seek reconsideration of the decision to remand.

Consistent with the tighter standards for reconsideration, the Commission has also initiated a practice of issuing summary decisions in requests for reconsideration. For example, in Jennings v. Secretary of Defense, DLA, 05971079 (1998), the Commission wrote:

On September 15, 1997, Rosa L. Jennings (hereinafter known as appellant), timely initiated a request to the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) to reconsider the decision in Jennings v. Dept. of Defense (DLA), EEOC Appeal No. 01970926 (August 19, 1997). EEOC Regulations provide that the Commissioners may, in their discretion, reconsider any previous Commission decision. 29 CFR § 1614.407(a). The party requesting reconsideration must submit written argument or evidence which tends to establish one or more of the following three criteria: new and material evidence is available that was not readily available when the previous decision was issued, 29 CFR § 1614.407(c)(1); the previous decision involved an erroneous interpretation of law, regulation or material fact, or misapplication of established policy, 29 CFR § 1614.407(c)(2); and the previous decision is of such exceptional nature as to have substantial precedential implications, 29 CFR § 1614.407(c)(3). Appellant's request is denied.

After a review of appellant's request for reconsideration, the previous decision, and the entire record, the Commission finds appellant's request does not meet the criteria of 29 CFR § 1614.407(c), and it is the decision of the Commission to deny appellant's request. The decision of the Commission in Appeal No. 01970926 remains the Commission's final decision. There is no further right of administrative appeal from the decision of the Commission on this request for reconsideration.

Different from past decisions is the absence of any discussion of the facts or the reasons why the request does not meet the Commission's reconsideration criteria. See also Faconti v. Postmaster General, 05960614 (1998); Pullum v. Postmaster General, 05960325 (1998); King v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 05971062 (1998).

All decisions of the Commission on requests for reconsideration are final and further reconsideration may not be sought by either party.
