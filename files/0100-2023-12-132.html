6. Change in Position Description--Work Duties

A change in position description unaccompanied by any other action is not a sufficient injury to state a claim. "[A] change in position description would ordinarily fall under an employer's discretion, and could thus be based on virtually any reason that did not violate federal EEO law." Hunter v. Secretary of Defense, 01A23577 (2003).

However, a change in work duties is sufficient injury to state a claim for relief. For example, in Greenstein v. Postmaster General, 0520110467 (2011), the Commission granted the complainant's petition for reconsideration and reversed an earlier decision holding that changing the complainant's route so that he had to carry a 35-pound mail satchel and extra two to three hours a day was not a sufficient injury to state a claim for relief. In Greenstein, the Commission explained:

In his request to reconsider, Complainant argues that the previous decision erred in determining that he did not suffer harm or loss with respect to a term, condition, or privilege of employment for which there is a remedy. Complainant maintains that the Agency changed his route by requiring him to carry a 35-pound mail satchel on his shoulder for an additional 2 to 3 hours. As a result of the change, he has developed back injuries and is seeking treatment.

After reconsidering the previous decision, the entire record, and Commission precedent, the Commission finds that the request meets the criteria of 29 C.F.R. § 1614.405(b), and it is the decision of the Commission to grant the request. We find that the previous appellate decision involved a clearly erroneous interpretation of material fact or law.

The Commission has long held that route adjustments can be sufficient to constitute a harm or loss with respect to a term, condition, or privilege of employment. See, e.g., Caison v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01881975 (July 19, 1988) ("[R]oute adjustments concern terms, conditions or privileges of employment which do state a claim...."); Hislop v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01975066 (Mar. 17, 1998) (finding that the complainant alleged harm to a term, condition, or privilege of employment when the agency removed S-999 mail from his route, "i.e., his route was adjusted."); Weeks v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01996880 (Dec. 7, 2000) (finding that the complainant stated a claim by alleging that his route was improperly adjusted for 40 minutes of office assistance).

Most recently, in Bailey v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 0120092806 (Mar. 31, 2011), the Commission reversed the Agency's dismissal of a complaint, in which a letter carrier alleged discrimination on the basis of age when the agency's route adjustments completely changed her assignment. The complainant in Bailey alleged that the Agency changed her route from business buildings to two-story buildings without elevators. She alleged that the route adjustment harmed her by changing the physical requirements of the position to such an extent that it made it physically difficult for her to perform. The Commission in Bailey found that she successfully stated a claim, alleging that the terms of her position had changed and that her route had been altered to the point that it became difficult for the employee to perform.

We find Bailey to be very similar to the instant case, in that Complainant alleged that the Agency's route adjustments changed the physical requirements of his job to such an extent that it became physically difficult for him to perform. Before the adjustment, Complainant drove a postal vehicle to deliver mail; after the adjustment, Complainant has to physically walk on foot and carry the mail on his shoulders with a 35-pound mail satchel. Complainant alleges that it is physically difficult for him to perform his adjusted route, and has suffered back injuries as a result.

Because of the similarities between the instant case and Bailey, and previous decisions upon which Bailey relied, the Commission finds that Complainant sufficiently alleged a harm or loss with respect to a term, condition, or privilege of employment for which there is a remedy, when he claimed age discrimination for his route adjustment.

Similarly, in Libby K. v. Secretary of Agriculture, 0120160736 (2017), the complainant's allegation that management removed a collateral duty from her and transferred that responsibility to a coworker, thereby depriving her of a benefit of employment and limiting further career advancement, was a sufficient loss of purposes of stating a cognizable claim of discrimination.
