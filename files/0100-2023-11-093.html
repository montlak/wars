K. PHONE CONTACT

There is nothing which prohibits contacting an EEO counselor by phone to initiate precomplaint counseling. Not every contact made constitutes contact for the purpose of precomplaint counseling. Just as the counselor has an obligation to engage in certain activities during precomplaint counseling, so too must the employee.

In LaBrie v. Postmaster General, 05920268 (1992), the Commission found that the complainant presented evidence of timely contact with a counselor by telephone where she presented a phone bill showing four telephone calls to the agency facility within the time period for initiating counseling. The Commission noted that a review of the remaining calls on the phone bill indicated that the complainant was not in the habit of calling the agency facility and that such evidence corroborated the complainant's assertion that she had contacted a counselor by telephone.

In Elomina v. Postmaster General, 01966177 (1997), the Commission found that the agency failed to determine whether the appellant made timely EEO contact. The appellant in Elomina asserted that her EEO contact was timely:

On appeal, appellant argues that her initial EEO Counselor contact did not occur on February 26, 1996, as determined by the agency in its final decision. Appellant argues that the initial EEO contact occurred, instead, on January 10, 1996, via telephone to an agency EEO office in Hawaii. In support of this argument, appellant submits copies of her phone bill which show two telephone calls made to Hawaii on that date, as well as other calls made to Hawaii in February 1996, identified by appellant as "EEO Hawaii." If appellant's initial EEO Counselor contact with regard to her December 22, 1995 separation from agency employment occurred on January 10, 1996, it would be within the forty-five day limitation period for initiating timely contact with an EEO Counselor. However, we are unable to ascertain from the present record whether appellant's initial EEO contact occurred on January 10, 1996, as she has alleged on appeal, or on February 26, 1996, as determined by the agency. Accordingly, the agency's decision to dismiss appellant's complaint for failure to timely contact an EEO Counselor is VACATED.

The Commission in Elomina ordered the agency to conduct a supplemental investigation to determine if the appellant's EEO contact was timely:

Specifically, the agency shall indicate whether the telephone numbers identified by appellant belong to the agency's EEO Office; obtain statements from employees of the EEO Office indicating whether they were contacted by appellant; and obtain any other relevant information regarding appellant's EEO contact. Thereafter, the agency shall issue a final agency decision or notify appellant that the agency is processing her complaint. The supplemental investigation and issuance of the final decision or notice of processing must be completed within thirty (30) calendar days of the date this decision becomes final. A copy of the agency's new final decision or notice of processing must be sent to the Compliance Officer as referenced below.

In Ghazzawi v. Postmaster General, 05930022 (1993), the Commission found the complainant had timely contacted an EEO counselor where he contended that he telephoned the counselor, gave his name, discussed his nonselection and was discouraged from filing a complaint. The agency's records contained a notation regarding anonymous contact of a counselor with regard to national origin discrimination (Egyptian) and the complainant contended he was the only Egyptian employee who could have contacted a counselor on that date.
