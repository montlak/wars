10. Contingency Agreements

Counsel fee awards in Title VII cases are not governed by any contingency agreement entered between the attorney and client. The EEOC will not award fees on the basis of a contingency contract that calls for the attorney to recover 40% of the employee's back pay award. See Toliver v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01890107 (1989); Jackson v. Department of Air Force, 01831163 (1983). A contingency fee agreement is a matter between the complainant and counsel. The agency, which is not party to the agreement, is under no obligation to honor it. See Williams v. Department of Health and Human Services, 01840395, 1313 (1985). If counsel believes that a fee enhancement is in order, the appropriate method is to request an upward adjustment of the "lodestar" amount.

As the Commission explained in Sinnott v. Secretary of Air Force, 01973683 (2000):

The fact that counsel has entered into a contingent fee contract with his client is irrelevant for the purposes of determining what the reasonable attorney fees should be under the relevant regulatory scheme. Nowhere does the applicable case law speak of the reasonableness of the fees in terms of a percentage of the underlying award. Rather, the reasonable fee is comprised of two elements hourly rate and hours expended. Consequently, counsel's contingent fee agreement with complainant is a matter solely between complainant and counsel. The agency, not being a party to the agreement, is under no obligation to honor its terms. See Toliver v. Department of Health and Human Services, EEOC Appeal No. 01890107 (March 22, 1989).

There is, in fact, no reason to enter into a true contingency agreement with a client in an EEO case. Contingency agreements find their application in cases where fees are not permitted as part of the recovery. In those cases, contingency agreements permit representation on the basis that the lawyer, if successful, will share in the actual recovery. Because fees may be awarded under Title VII and the Rehabilitation Act, agreements between counsel and the employee can be reached whereby the employee will retain his or her right to full recovery for the injury and counsel retains his or her right to be compensated for the services rendered. Contingency agreements may be useful in cases under the ADEA because fees are not recoverable in the administrative process.

While it may be unnecessary, because of the fee recovery provision in Title VII and the Rehabilitation Act, to enter into a contingency agreement that provides counsel will receive a portion of any award, it may be desirable to make the actual hourly rate contingent upon success. The complainant and counsel can agree to a stipulated hourly rate that will increase if the complainant prevails. Such an agreement is a matter between the complainant and counsel. Complainant is then legally responsible for paying the difference, unless the Commission approves the contingency rate. See Tom v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, 01911245 (1991) (approving an award of counsel fees in the amount of $200 per hour where an agreement provided that counsel's rate would be $75 per hour, or $200 per hour if the complainant prevailed). Counsel presented evidence that his customary billing rate was $200 per hour for noncontingency cases.
