C. PROCESSING COMPLAINTS

The Commission indicated that processing complaints, often referred to as "spin-off" complaints, will be reviewed under the harmful error standard. The mere demonstration that the agency committed error in the processing of an EEO case does not entitle the employee to a favorable decision on the merits. In addition to showing error, the employee must show that "it is likely that an alleged error affected the result [otherwise] its occurrence cannot have been prejudicial." Cross v. Department of Air Force, 01840120 (1985). In that case, the Commission held that it was error for the agency to dismiss a complaint during informal counseling, but that the error was not harmful since the record showed the allegations were untimely.

In Willis v. Secretary of Treasury, 05900589 (1990), the Commission issued its definition of harmful error with a slightly different twist:

Since Commission regulations do not define what constitutes harmful or prejudicial error, we must refer to the rules governing the federal courts for guidance. Exclusions of evidence shall not be considered harmful error unless such exclusions adversely affect a substantial right of the parties. See DeMars v. Equitable Life Assurance Society of the United States, 610 F.2d 55, 61-62 (1st Cir. 1979) citing Fed. R. Civ. P. 61 and Fed. R. Evid. 103(a). Therefore, in the evidentiary context, harmful error means that the erroneously excluded evidence would have caused the reviewing authority to reach a different result than that reached as a result of the exclusion of said evidence. See Sheryl L. Loveland v. Edward C. Aldridge Jr., Secretary, Department of the Air Force, EEOC Petition No. 03880157 (December 1, 1988).

However, the Commission is not hampered by this rule, since it also found it has the authority to consider any evidence it deems relevant, even if previously excluded by the administrative judge. Id.

Failure to provide the complainant with a copy of the hearing transcript is harmful error in that it deprives the complainant of the opportunity to present specific argument on appeal regarding the agency's final decision. Navis v. Attorney General, 05930324 (1993). The proper remedy is to vacate the final agency decision with instructions to provide the complainant with a copy of the transcript and thereafter issue a new final decision.

Since the Commission has the option of remanding the complaint to the agency, the harmful error doctrine does not often play a factor in EEOC decisions. For example, the fact that the agency committed an error in its refusal to allow an employee to amend his complaint at the formal stage to include reprisal as an additional basis of discrimination is harmless where the complaint was dismissed because it was untimely. Parsons v. Department of Interior, 01861778 (1986).
