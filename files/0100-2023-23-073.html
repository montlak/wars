16. Effect of Disability Retirement

From a legal perspective, there is nothing inherently inconsistent about an employee requesting reasonable accommodation, on the one hand, and contending that he or she is qualified for disability retirement on the other hand. Nor does OPM's approval of a disability retirement application per se require a finding that an employee is not a qualified individual with a disability.

The Commission, in Sandberg v. Postmaster General, 01952301 (1997), rejected an agency argument that the complainant was not a qualified individual with a disability because her application for disability retirement was approved by OPM:

Finally, the agency determined that appellant was not qualified to work as a carrier because her application for disability retirement had been approved. We disagree, and note that OPM's determination that appellant was disabled is not determinative of whether appellant is a "qualified individual with a disability" under the ADA. See EEOC Enforcement Guidance on the Effect of Representations Made in Applications for Benefits on the Determination of Whether a Person is a "Qualified Individual with a Disability" Under the Americans With Disabilities Act of 1990 (ADA), No. 915.002, February 12, 1997. In so finding, we note that the standards of the ADA are fundamentally different from the standards of other statutory schemes.

A finding by the Office of Personnel Management that an employee does not qualify for disability retirement does not preclude a finding that the employee is a person with a disability under the Rehabilitation Act. The retirement statutes and the Rehabilitation Act use different standards to determine disability. Hupp v. Secretary of Air Force, 01934405 (1994) ("For example, in making its finding, the MSPB considered that appellant did not enroll in a pain clinic, a factor which need not be considered under the Rehabilitation Act since the Act generally does not take into account potential improvement with treatment.").

Foreshadowing a later Supreme Court decision in 1999, in Faconti v. Postmaster General, 01976463 (1999), the Commission held that representations made by an employee in a disability retirement application may be relevant to the question of whether or not the employee is a qualified individual with a disability, but they are not dispositive of that issue. In Faconti, the Commission held:

Under the Commission's regulations, an agency is required to make reasonable accommodation to the known physical and mental limitations of a qualified individual with a disability unless the agency can show that accommodation would cause an undue hardship on its operations. 29 CFR § 1614.203(c). As an initial matter, the Commission finds that appellant is an individual with a disability as defined by the Rehabilitation Act. This fact is not disputed by the agency. Appellant must also show that he is a "qualified" individual with a disability. The agency has argued that appellant cannot be found to be "qualified" because he represented in his application for disability retirement that he was physically unable to perform the duties of his position with the postal service. However, it is the Commission's policy that because of fundamental differences between the Rehabilitation Act and other disability benefits programs, representations made in connection with an application for disability benefits may be relevant to--but are never determinative of--whether a person is a "qualified" individual with a disability. See EEOC's "Enforcement Guidance on the Effect of Representations Made in Applications for Benefits on the Determination of Whether a Person Is a "Qualified Individual with a Disability" Under the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 (ADA)," Notice No. 915.002 (February 12, 1997). In this case, it is undisputed that appellant, when he was able to work, was qualified to perform the functions of his position. It is critical to remember that the Rehabilitation Act's definition of "qualified" requires that an employee be able to perform the essential functions of the position at issue with or without reasonable accommodation. In this case, appellant asserts he would have been able to continue performing the essential functions of his position if the agency had accommodated his request to work at home or at a work site within walking distance of his home. Therefore, a premature finding that appellant was not qualified would unfairly cut off consideration of his claim that the agency failed to reasonably accommodate his disability which would have enabled him to perform the essential functions of his position. See Southerland v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05930714 (July 15, 1994).

In Cleveland v. Policy Management Systems Corp., 526 U.S. 795, 119 S. Ct. 1597, 143 L. Ed.2d 966 (1999), the Supreme Court struck down a negative assumption that an employee who contended she was disabled for purposes of Social Security Disability Insurance was not a qualified individual with a disability entitled to reasonable accommodation. As with the Commission, the Supreme Court focused on the difference in the purpose of the ADA and the disability benefits statutes. The Court, in Cleveland, 119 S. Ct. at 1602, explained:

In our view, however, despite the appearance of conflict that arises from the language of the two statutes, the two claims do not inherently conflict to the point where courts should apply a special negative presumption like the one applied by the Court of Appeals here. That is because there are too many situations in which an SSDI claim and an ADA claim can comfortably exist side by side.

For one thing, as we have noted, the ADA defines a "qualified individual" to include a disabled person "who...can perform the essential functions" of her job "with reasonable accommodation." Reasonable accommodations may include:

"job restructuring, part-time or modified work schedules, reassignment to a vacant position, acquisition or modification of equipment or devices, appropriate adjustment or modifications of examinations, training materials or policies, the provision of qualified readers or interpreters, and other similar accommodations." 42 USC § 12111(9)(B).

By way of contrast, when the SSA determines whether an individual is disabled for SSDI purposes, it does not take the possibility of "reasonable accommodation" into account, nor need an applicant refer to the possibility of reasonable accommodation when she applies for SSDI. The omission reflects the facts that the SSA receives more than 2.5 million claims for disability benefits each year; its administrative resources are limited; the matter of "reasonable accommodation" may turn on highly disputed workplace-specific matters; and an SSA misjudgment about that detailed, and often fact-specific matter would deprive a seriously disabled person of the critical financial support the statute seeks to provide. The result is that an ADA suit claiming that the plaintiff can perform her job with reasonable accommodation may well prove consistent with an SSDI claim that the plaintiff could not perform her own job (or other jobs) without it.

(Citations omitted).

Although an application for disability retirement and a request for accommodation are not mutually exclusive, as a matter of law, the individual who files both is responsible for the factual representations made in each. To the extent that the factual representations may be inconsistent, the individual can be called upon to explain those apparent inconsistencies and any real inconsistencies may be taken into account in deciding whether the individual is entitled to accommodation.

There is nothing inconsistent in a complainant contending that her resignation from the agency was coerced and in contending that she presently is no longer able to work. In Scott v. Secretary of Navy, 01984359 (1999), the Commission rejected an agency argument to the effect that such contentions were incompatible by noting simply that "[a]ppellant contends that her situation at work was so bad she had to leave and cannot return.... Appellant has alleged that the agency's actions forced her to seek disability retirement."
