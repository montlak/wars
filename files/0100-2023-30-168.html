5. Collateral Source Rule

Medical expenses are recoverable as part of a compensatory damage award. See 42 USC 1981a(b). Depending on the source of such payments, an agency may be entitled to an offset against a medical expense damage award if the complainant has otherwise received payment of medical expenses. Originally, in Carpenter v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01945652 (1995), the Commission found that a complainant only could recover for actual out-of-pocket medical expenses. However, in Wallis v. Postmaster General, 01950510 (1995), the Commission, noting that the issue had not been specifically argued in Carpenter, held that agencies may not deduct from a medical expense damage award payments from "collateral sources" such as health insurance.

The Wallis decision was based on two rationales. First, the Commission relied on prior precedent, Berg v. United States, 806 F.2d 978 (10th Cir. 1986), to find that a collateral source cannot be used as an offset to a damage award where the source of the funds is separate and distinct from the general treasury. Second, the Commission relied on a line of cases holding that a defendant is not entitled to an offset against damages even where it has contributed to the collateral source, unless the benefit provided to the plaintiff was in response to some legal liability of the defendant. See, e.g., Phillips v. Western Company of North America, 953 F.2d 923 (5th Cir. 1992).

The Commission reasoned that medical benefits paid by a health insurance plan could not be used as an offset against a medical expense damage award because the source of funds was separate and distinct from the general treasury, and because health insurance is provided as a fringe benefit of employment and not as a substitute for legal liability.

The Commission cited to Wallis in Johnson v. Secretary of Interior, 01961812 (1998), and found that the agency miscalculated the complainant's pecuniary damages by accounting only for her out-of-pocket medical expenses. The Commission held that the complainant was entitled to reimbursement for the entire amount of her medical expenses.

The Commission has, however, held that a complainant must request application of the collateral source rule in order to be reimbursed for the full amount of medical expenses. In Kelly v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01951729 (1998), the Commission found that the administrative judge properly awarded the complainant only the costs of her copay for prescriptions. In a footnote, the Commission noted the Wallis decision, but held that the collateral source rule only applies if the complainant requests it:

Although the Commission has applied the collateral source rule to health insurance benefits, Wallis v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01950510 (November 13, 1995), appellant has not requested the application of the collateral source rule nor has she provided any information concerning the prescription expenses paid by her health insurer. See Carpenter v. Department of Agriculture, EEOC Appeal No. 01945652 (July 17, 1995) (collateral source rule not raised--past pecuniary damages based on amount of money complainant personally paid).

In effect, the Kelly decision requires complainants to tell the agency and/or the Commission to follow the law as set forth in Wallis. The Commission exacerbates this burden on complainants by routinely advising them to produce evidence of "out-of-pocket" medical expenses. For example, in McGill v. Secretary of Navy, 01955718 (1998), the Commission found that some of the complainant's medical expenses were the result of discrimination. The Commission advised the complainant to submit "any documentation of his out-of-pocket expenses. If appellant has any documentation concerning these costs, he should submit it to the agency so that he may be reimbursed." Id. Such a statement is highly likely to mislead a complainant into thinking that he is only entitled to be reimbursed for the money he actually expended for medical treatment.

In Finlay v. Postmaster General, 01942985 (1997), the Commission found that medical benefits paid under FECA can be offset against medical expenses:

The Commission now turns to the calculation of damages payable to appellant. The Commission notes the agency's argument that appellant is not entitled to recover past or future pecuniary damages in connection with her psychotherapy, since such expenses are payable, and have been paid, under FECA. This argument pertains to the "collateral source" rule, which holds that "benefits received by the plaintiff from a source collateral to the defendant may not be used to reduce that defendant's liability for damages." 1 Dobbs, Law of Remedies § 3.8(1), at 372-73 (2d ed. 1993). In Berg v. United States, the Tenth Circuit held that collateral source funds cannot be used to offset damages payable by the Federal government where the source of funds is "separate and distinct" from general government revenues. 806 F.2d 978, 984-86 (10th Cir. 1986).

The Commission notes that under FECA, OWCP disburses funds to the injured employee, and is reimbursed by the agency. Accordingly, the source of funds used to pay appellant's past and future medical expenses is not collateral to the agency, and may be used to offset the pecuniary damages which might otherwise be recoverable. Cf. Wallis v. U.S. Postal Service (health insurance benefits funded by employer-paid premiums are collateral source funds, and not subject to offset).

In Finlay, the Commission did not mention the second line of cases it relied upon in Wallis, holding that a defendant is entitled to an offset where the defendant has provided a benefit in exchange for absolution from legal liability. That is probably because that line of cases has no applicability to the federal government. Certainly, it is true that in the private sector employers provide workers' compensation, partially, in exchange for insulation from tort liability for work-related injuries. But because of sovereign immunity, the federal government has never had exposure to tort liability for work-related injuries. The Phillips line of cases has no real applicability to the federal government.

Where, as a result of discrimination, the complainant was required to pay her own health insurance premiums, the cost of those premiums can be recovered as pecuniary damages. Wooten v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01960727 (1997).

But a complainant may not recover for the cost of increased health insurance premiums and additional child care due to a high-risk pregnancy where there is no evidence linking the risk of the pregnancy to the discrimination. Leperi v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01964107 (1998).

The Commission has noted that application of the collateral source rule does not result in double recovery for the complainant because the health care provider may recover from the complainant the monies it expended. Rivers v. Secretary of Treasury, 01992843 (2002).

Procedurally, it is not necessary that the complainant raise the collateral source rule before an Administrative Judge. Per the Commission in Jeffry R. v. Postmaster General, 2020002824 (2021), it may be raised for the first time on appeal.
