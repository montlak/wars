THE EEO PROCESS

The federal EEO process was designed as a model of complaint resolution. Each step provides the opportunity for claims of employment discrimination to be resolved in a manner that can be satisfactory to all parties.

While it sounds like a good idea to work toward early settlement of disputes, you may have questions about where you fit in the EEO process. An employee may accuse a manager of discrimination, but the complaint is filed against the agency head, not the manager. Where does this leave the manager in terms of responsibilities and obligations?

It's important to understand that a manager, even one accused of discrimination, is considered a witness, not a defendant. As a witness, a manager's obligation is to cooperate with the officials who process EEO claims and to tell the truth. While being accused of discrimination can be alarming, it's crucial to remain calm and professional at all times.

The employee might believe unlawful discrimination occurred. Or the employee might feel your decision was wrong and choose to contest it through a readily available means -- the EEO process.

Either way, it's a signal that the employee has concerns that are serious enough for them to take action. If you keep your cool, listen with an open mind, and take advantage of the help provided by the EEO process, you may be able to resolve the issue early on.

Understanding EEO laws

Managing workforce diversity and maintaining a discrimination-free workplace are high on the list of critical functions performed by federal managers and supervisors, so it's a good idea to get to know the laws against discrimination. Federal equal employment opportunity laws include the following:

  • • Title VII of the Civil Rights Act, which prohibits discrimination on the bases of race, color, sex, religion, and national origin.
  • • Rehabilitation Act, which protects individuals with disabilities from employment discrimination by the federal government. Although the Rehabilitation Act applies to federal employees and applicants, the Americans with Disabilities Act provides the standards applied to claims under the Rehabilitation Act.
  • • Age Discrimination in Employment Act, which prohibits discrimination against individuals over 40.
  • • Equal Pay Act, which mandates the same pay for individuals who do the same work without regard to sex.
  • • Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act, which prohibits discrimination on the basis of genetic information.

Equal employment opportunity means that agencies must provide employees and applicants a fair opportunity to compete for positions and a full opportunity to function and progress in their careers based on their abilities and performance, rather than on non-merit factors, such as race, sex, national origin, age, or disability.

The objective of the EEO program is not to provide special or unfair advantage to any groups or individuals, but to ensure that employees are not unfairly disadvantaged based on non-merit factors.

Don't retaliate. One more thing to keep in mind: In EEO matters, retaliation occurs when a manager, supervisor, or sometimes even a coworker takes action against an employee or applicant who raised an EEO complaint or was somehow involved in EEO matters.

Getting angry and taking action against an employee in retaliation for filing a complaint or otherwise participating in the EEO process is against the law and will only make things worse. So will saying anything that indicates to the employee that you are hurt or feel betrayed by the complaint. Although this may seem like a non-threatening expression of your feelings, it could "chill" an employee's desire to engage in EEO activity. Chilling conduct is a type of prohibited retaliation.

Process overview

Managers are involved in most of the day-to-day decisions that become the subject of EEO disputes. You assign work, promote and select employees, issue ratings and reward performance, and when necessary, take corrective disciplinary action.

Accordingly, managers are also directly involved in most EEO disputes. If you must respond to suspicions or allegations that your actions or decisions may not fully comply with EEO requirements, it is essential to take a positive approach:

  • • Recognize that the EEO complaint process is the law.
  • • Refrain from perceiving complaints as a personal attack.
  • • Remain calm and professional at all times.
  • •Make sure you understand the EEO complaint and alternative dispute resolution processes, as well as your role in them.
  • • Work closely with your EEO and HR offices in an effort to resolve issues.
  • • Be cooperative throughout the EEO process.
  • • Provide evidence requested during the investigation and discovery periods.
  • • Seek guidance if you need it.

The purpose of the process. Most people have a general idea about how the court system works, but federal employees who want to make claims of discrimination usually have to work through a process that is somewhat different. This process, called the administrative process, is designed to encourage the resolution of complaints as early as possible. If complaints can be resolved quickly, the people involved will get back to doing the agency's work without being distracted and workplace harmony can be restored.

Every agency has an EEO complaint process that has to be carried out under rules set by the EEOC. If the rules are followed by everyone involved, employees will be able to trust the outcomes of the process and may be more willing to explore options for resolving their issues. Likewise, if you as a manager understand the process, you may be more comfortable allowing it to work.

The EEO process is most effective if you keep an open mind and have a sincere desire to resolve the dispute. As you work toward resolving a dispute, keep in mind that settlement agreements can involve complicated legal, financial, and personnel issues, so it is essential that you work closely with agency experts in those areas before agreeing to or signing a settlement.

Stages of the EEO process

The EEO process can be long if a complaint proceeds through all the stages. With a positive attitude, you may be able to contribute to an early settlement that allows everyone to move on more quickly. If that doesn't happen, you may feel uncomfortable at times, but it's crucial that you keep your cool and let the process work. If you have any questions or concerns at any point in the process, contact your HR or EEO office. Here is a brief overview of the progressive stages:

1. Employee seeks EEO counseling. This is sometimes referred to as filing an informal complaint. At this point, an employee may be offered alternative dispute resolution, or ADR, as a means for reaching a resolution. If ADR isn't offered, or the employee chooses not to pursue ADR, counseling proceeds. During counseling, the EEO counselor works to define which type of discrimination is being raised and what actions are being contested. In addition to framing the complaint, the EEO counselor is also charged with trying to resolve the matter at issue by working with the employee and the agency. Whether the employee chooses ADR or counseling, there will be opportunities for settling the complaint if both parties are willing to keep their minds open to realistic options.

2. Formal EEO complaint. If a resolution isn't reached during ADR or counseling, the employee has the right to file a formal EEO complaint. After a formal complaint is filed, the agency must conduct an investigation of the issues raised in the complaint. Managers are required to cooperate with EEO investigators.

3. Final decision or administrative hearing. Once the investigation is complete, the employee can request either a final decision by the agency or an administrative hearing.

4. Final decision. If the employee requests a final decision by the agency, the agency considers the record of the investigation and issues a decision as to whether discrimination occurred as claimed by the employee.

5. Administrative hearing. If the employee requests an administrative hearing, an administrative judge is responsible for considering the claim. The judge usually tries to help the parties settle the case. If those efforts are unsuccessful, the judge may decide the evidence is sufficient to issue a decision without holding a hearing. More likely, an administrative hearing will be held with the administrative judge presiding. The hearing will resemble a trial in a civil court but may be somewhat less formal. After the hearing, the administrative judge will issue a decision. If discrimination is found, the judge will determine what is necessary to remedy the discrimination.

6. Appeal to the EEOC. An employee may appeal an agency's final decision or an administrative judge's decision to the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission. An agency may appeal an administrative judge's decision to the EEOC. The EEOC will not hold a hearing but will consider the evidence in the record to determine if the decision by the agency or the administrative judge was proper.

Starting the EEO process

The federal EEO complaint process begins when employees or applicants who feel they have been discriminated against contact an EEO counselor with the intent of discussing the perceived discrimination. This is usually referred to as the pre-complaint or informal stage of the EEO process.

EEO counselors. Depending on how your agency is structured, there may be staff members in the EEO office who work solely as EEO counselors and have that title. However, at some agencies employees who have unrelated responsibilities may take on EEO counseling as a collateral duty, or there may be contract workers who carry out EEO counseling. Your agency should have signs posted in common areas that provide the information necessary to start an EEO complaint. However, if you're not sure how to seek EEO counseling, contact your EEO office for guidance.

An employee or applicant's contact with an EEO counselor doesn't necessarily have to follow a certain formula or occur in a specific place, but it must show the person's intention to begin the EEO process. For example, if an employee stops an EEO counselor in the hallway and brings up an EEO issue, that could be interpreted as seeking EEO counseling even though the conversation took place in a common area rather than a counselor's office. However, a casual social conversation with an EEO counselor that doesn't include any mention of an EEO problem would not be enough to show an employee's intention to start the EEO process by seeking EEO counseling.

EEO officials. Contacting an EEO counselor is the crucial first step in pursuing an EEO claim against a federal agency. However, sometimes even imperfect counselor contact is adequate to start the EEO process. Under certain circumstances an employee or applicant may fulfill the requirement of seeking EEO counseling by contacting an agency official "logically connected to the EEO process" and showing an interest in pursuing an EEO claim. This can adequately indicate a desire to move forward regardless of whether counseling begins on that day. Accordingly, expressing an interest in pursuing the EEO process with an EEO officer or an EEO manager may be enough to properly start the process.

Other agency officials. Under some circumstances, if an employee talks with an agency official about an EEO matter, it may count as EEO contact and the official may have a duty to direct the employee to EEO counseling. For example, if an employee tells a manager the employee is being sexually harassed, even if the manager is not connected to the EEO office, that contact may be enough to make the agency aware that the employee wishes to start the EEO process. Under these circumstances, the manager may be obligated to tell the employee who to contact in order to start the EEO process.

If an applicant or employee attempts to raise an EEO claim with you, direct them to the EEO office to request counseling.

EEO counseling

Federal employees and applicants who feel they've been subjected to unlawful discrimination must discuss the matter with an EEO counselor before taking further action. They must seek EEO counseling within 45 days of the act that was discriminatory or, if the claim involves a personnel action, within 45 days of the effective date of the action. If the employee does not choose ADR, the agency has 30 days to complete counseling.

An EEO counselor represents neither the employee nor the agency in the discrimination claim. The counselor does not decide if discrimination occurred. Their job is essentially to give the employee information about the EEO process, to define the employee's claims, to conduct a limited inquiry, and to try to work out a resolution.

Once the counselor understands the employee's complaint, they gather facts surrounding the matter, which usually includes meeting with the managers or supervisors involved.

If an EEO counselor seeks an appointment with you, act in a professional, business-like manner. You are obligated to cooperate, but if you adopt a positive attitude, you may be more likely to aid in reaching a positive outcome. The EEO counseling process gives you an opportunity to listen and understand your employee's concerns. It also helps to maintain open communication between you and your employee.

Many issues raised in EEO counseling stem from misunderstandings, a lack of awareness, or simply an inadequate exchange of information. EEO counselors can help improve communication and provide an ideal opportunity for the parties to reach an understanding before lines are drawn and positions taken.

Formal complaints

If a matter is not resolved during the counseling or ADR stage, the employee may file a formal complaint. The complaint must be filed within 15 days of an employee receiving notice from the agency that they have a right to file the complaint.

After a formal complaint is filed, a more thorough investigation is conducted in connection with the complaint, but settlement efforts can still continue.

The purpose of an investigation is:

  • • To gather facts that will allow a determination as to whether discrimination occurred.
  • • To gather information that would help determine an appropriate remedy if discrimination is found.

The agency is responsible for conducting the investigation. Some agencies use their own employees to investigate EEO claims. Others use outside contractors. In either case, the investigators must be objective and unbiased because even the appearance of a conflict of interest can impact employee faith in the integrity of the investigation.

The investigator collects relevant factual information relating to the claims in the complaint and prepares an investigative summary. The investigative process is non-adversarial. As a neutral party, the investigator is obligated to collect relevant evidence from all parties.

An investigator can use numerous methods for gathering information. These include interviews, fact-finding conferences, and exchanges of letters or memoranda, requests for information, interrogatories, and affidavits.

If you are named in a formal discrimination complaint, you are entitled to:

  • • Review a copy of the complaint.
  • • A representative of your choosing at interviews with an investigator or counselor.
  • • A review of any transcript, statement, or affidavit reflecting your interview.

Generally, you will not be entitled to see the statements of other witnesses.

An investigator could seek you out in person, asking for an interview, documents, or other relevant materials. The investigator could also contact you by telephone or other means. If the investigator doesn't appear in person, you could be sent a list of questions.

No matter how the investigation proceeds, you are always obligated to cooperate. Try to relax and answer the investigator's questions honestly. If you have properly documented your employment decisions, you should be able to refresh your memory with your notes. You might also be asked to provide copies of your documentation to be included in the investigation materials.

The agency must complete the investigation within 180 days of the date the complaint was filed unless the parties enter into an agreement extending the investigation period for not more than 90 days. The agency can also unilaterally extend the time period for up to 30 days if the file must be sanitized for specific national defense or foreign policy reasons.

After the investigation is complete, the agency provides the employee with a copy of the investigative file and notifies them that they can request an administrative hearing within 30 days of receiving the file or request an immediate agency decision.

Administrative hearings

An administrative hearing is run by an administrative judge who has full responsibility for deciding the case. The hearing provides the parties with a fair and reasonable opportunity to explain and supplement the record that was developed during the investigation. In some cases, it gives the parties an opportunity to examine and cross-examine witnesses.

Before a hearing takes place, the administrative judge usually will try to get the parties to settle the case. Again, this is a time to work with an open mind. Although previous settlement efforts might have failed, the administrative judge can sometimes bring a unique perspective or provide different ideas for settlement that may provide what's needed to resolve the matters at issue.

The administrative judge also presides over discovery, which is a process by which one party gains information that is in the possession of the other party. The purpose of discovery is to allow each party to know as far as it may properly be known the exact position held by the opposing party and the nature of the evidence likely to strengthen or weaken that position.

Methods for discovery include the following:

  • • Interrogatories. Written questions served on the adversary who must provide written replies under oath.
  • • Deposition. A recorded session during which both parties are given the opportunity to present and cross-examine a witness.
  • • Stipulation. An agreement or concession made by the parties.
  • • Request for admissions. A device by which one party asks the other to affirm or deny an issue of material fact.
  • • Request for the production of documents. A request that the opposing party produce specified documents.

If you are asked to provide a statement or materials during discovery, comply to the best of your ability. Likewise, if you are asked to testify at a hearing, answer the questions honestly and to the best of your ability. If you have any concerns about your role in this part of the process, consult with your HR or EEO office.

Once the hearing is complete, the administrative judge will issue a decision as to whether discrimination occurred. The decision is not always immediate. While you await a decision, remember to remain calm and professional at all times.

Appeals

Both the employee and the agency can appeal the decision of an administrative judge.

The agency must take final action within 40 days of receiving the hearing file and the AJ's decision. In its final order, the agency will tell the complainant if it will fully implement the AJ's decision. If the agency decides not to fully implement the AJ's decision, it must file an appeal with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission. These steps will be handled by your agency's attorney.

A complainant may appeal after the agency has issued a final action or decision on a complaint. The appeal must be filed within 30 days of receiving the agency's final action.

The EEOC will look at the record of evidence to determine if the decisions made were appropriate. It does not conduct a hearing. If the EEOC decides the agency did not conduct an adequate investigation or the administrative judge did not properly consider the matter, it may send the complaint back for an additional investigation. Be prepared for this possibility by retaining any notes or documentation you have until the case is finally decided.

Official time

Official time in the context of the EEO process is "on-the-job" time that is used by an employee to work on an EEO complaint or to take actions in connection with the complaint. Some agencies use the term "administrative leave" instead of official time. A manager must allow an employee with an EEO complaint a "reasonable" amount of official time, if that person is otherwise on duty, to prepare a complaint and to respond to requests for information from the agency and the EEOC.

Work on an EEO complaint can include meetings with the employee's representative or attorney. The time spent on these meetings is also subject to the reasonableness standard. However, if the meetings are scheduled outside of the employee's duty hours, the employee is not entitled to official time.

As a manager, you have to be careful not to react to a request for official time in a manner that could be viewed as retaliation. However, you are allowed to require that the time taken be reasonable. A reasonable amount of time is whatever is appropriate under the particular circumstances of the complaint in order to allow the employee to complete the tasks necessary to pursue the complaint. A complex complaint might require more work, but this must be balanced against the agency's legitimate need to have employees perform their assigned duties on a regular basis.

Ideally, you and the employee can reach an agreement on a reasonable amount of official time. However, if there is a disagreement, make sure the reasons for your decision are fair and documented, not simply the product of frustration with the employee. Better yet, if you have any questions about how much time is appropriate for an employee, seek guidance from your HR or EEO office.

Mistakes to avoid

The EEO process can be daunting, but you can go a long way by following a few simple principles.

Do not:

  • • Make negative comments about the EEO process.
  • • Attempt to resolve EEO matters on your own. Always seek help from HR or EEO if you have a concern.
  • • Destroy documentation concerning an employment decision raised in an EEO complaint. Not only is this against EEO regulations, but it will deprive you of the opportunity to refresh your memory.
  • • Deny an employee official time in a summary manner. Make sure you have a specific reason for limiting the time allowed.
  • • Jump to conclusions. Although your instinct may be to act defensively, the process is designed to end conflict as early as possible. A positive attitude will aid that goal.
  • • Be unprofessional. Act in a professional, business-like manner throughout the EEO process. Not only will this help the process run more smoothly, it will also help you avoid making statements that reflect poorly on your decisions or lead to claims of reprisal.

Ask FMG

Q. "Resolving a complaint" usually means that managers must compromise in some fashion. Doesn't that embolden or encourage staff to file complaints, even frivolously, knowing that management will concede?

A. Yes, this is certainly the downside of the EEO process. If you run a workplace that is fair and professional, you decrease the chances that employees will file EEO complaints. If they do, you just have to remember that the EEO process is protected by law, so even if it is painful sometimes, you have to act within its parameters. If there is some merit to the complaint or a misunderstanding that can be cleared up, it's more likely it will be resolved early if you are open to resolution rather than instantly writing it off as frivolous. If the complaint is frivolous, it should come out eventually. In the meantime, just keep on being professional.

Q. When should a complaint be settled?

A. When it makes sense to do so. That's usually when there is merit to the complaint or when settling an uncertain issue makes sense from a morale or financial viewpoint. You may have some input into whether a claim should be settled. Or your agency's representative or attorney may make the decisions regarding settlement. If you are not included in the settlement decision, it's important for you to comply with the decision, regardless of your private thoughts about the matter. Disputing a settlement may invite a claim of retaliation.

Q. Who determines whether a dispute is handled through ADR?

A. That's usually up to the employee since the employee has the right to raise the matter through the statutory process. However, the agency may decide certain types of cases are inappropriate for ADR.

Q. What if I don't want to be part of ADR?

A. You can express your concerns to your agency's HR or EEO office, but if ADR goes forward, it's your obligation to cooperate by engaging in the process with goodwill.

Q. I'm careful not to discriminate against anyone but have still been named in EEO complaints. How come?

A. You may have made an error you were unaware of, or someone may incorrectly have perceived that an action was discriminatory. Just remember not to take it personally.

Q. May an employee remain anonymous during EEO counseling?

A. Yes. An employee may choose to remain anonymous during EEO counseling. An EEO counselor may not reveal the identity of the employee unless authorized to do so by that individual. However, as the counselor works to find a solution, you may be able to determine the employee's identity. Even so, you should not try to initiate a direct discussion about the matter at issue. Allow the counseling process to proceed unhindered.

Q. Am I entitled to see the documents related to an EEO investigation if I'm the manager who is claimed to have committed discrimination?

A. Once a formal complaint is filed, you are entitled to see a copy of the complaint. You also are entitled to review any transcript that contains statements made by you during the EEO investigative interview.

Q. Am I entitled to have a representative present when answering questions by an EEO investigator?

A. Yes, though you will not be permitted to designate a representative who would have a conflict of interest or position, for example, an agency EEO or HR official.

Q. What happens if an employee has a history of filing EEO complaints whenever they receive a negative performance report?

A. Understandably, this can be frustrating. However, an employee is not prohibited from filing multiple EEO complaints, except in extremely rare circumstances that don't apply to this situation. If the performance reports are fair and well-documented, the complaints should be processed with a relative minimum of fuss, and unless the employee can rebut your legitimate explanations, they will probably be unsuccessful.
