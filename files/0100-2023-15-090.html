2. Inferences

An important aspect of determining whether there is a genuine dispute of material fact is the proposition that all possible inferences must be drawn in favor of the nonmoving party. Those inferences can create a genuine dispute of fact. For example, in Levingston v. Secretary of Treasury, 0120091224 (2009), the Commission reversed a summary judgment decision in favor of the agency where the administrative judge drew an inference that was favorable to the agency that had moved for summary judgment. In Levingston, the undisputed facts demonstrated that the selecting official had made a mathematical error in calculating the scores of the candidates. The administrative judge concluded that the complainant would still have been the second-ranked candidate even if the scores had been correctly calculated. In reversing the summary judgment decision in Levingston, the Commission noted:

In this regard, we note that the AJ has not elaborated on precisely how complainant's score remained lower than that of the selectee following this correction. Moreover, complainant argues that in his reply to opposition for a summary judgment, the agency stated that the SO may not be "the world's greatest mathematician, but that certainly does not mean that he discriminated against the complainant based on his race." Complainant properly argues that the agency assertion is based upon an inference favorable to the agency, namely, that the selecting official's incorrect ratings were merely an innocent mistake. However, in the summary judgment determination, it was complainant, not the agency, as the non-moving party who was entitled to all favorable inferences...

As the court explained in Williams v. Secretary of Defense, No. 01-4016 (3rd Cir. 2002):

[T]o defeat summary judgment when the defendant answers the plaintiff's prima facie case with legitimate, non-discriminatory reasons for its action, the plaintiff must point to some evidence, direct or circumstantial, from which a fact finder could reasonably either (1) disbelieve the employer's articulated legitimate reasons; or (2) believe that an invidious discriminatory reason was more likely than not a motivating or determinative cause of the employer's action. In other words...a plaintiff who has made out a prima facie case may defeat a motion for summary judgment by either (i) discrediting the proffered reasons, either circumstantially or directly, or (ii) adducing evidence, whether circumstantial or direct, that discrimination was more likely than not a motivating or determinative cause of the adverse employment action.
