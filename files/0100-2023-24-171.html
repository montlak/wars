5. Bidding for Schedules

Allowing a complainant to bid for a particular schedule is not a form of reasonable accommodation. Demarcus I. v. Postmaster General, 0120171336 (2018). Demarcus I. involved a mail carrier complainant who suffered a traumatic brain injury, which limited his ability to walk for long periods of time. The agency had successfully accommodated the complainant for four years by assigning him to a specific mail route. A new supervisor began rotating routes, and the complainant requested to have the same route that served as an accommodation. The supervisor asked for updated medical documentation, which the complainant provided. Based on language in the medical notes, the supervisor assigned the complainant to a light-duty position that did not amount to full-time work. Complainant was later able to resume full-time work because he began taking "mini-bids" to ensure a 40-hour week. The complainant claimed a failure to reasonably accommodate his disability. The agency countered that it accommodated him with a light-duty assignment and allowed him to take mini-bids. The Commission found the agency's remedial actions inadequate in Demarcus I., noting that allowing an employee to take mini-bids is just "routine business," not a reasonable accommodation:

In this case, the record shows that Complainant is an individual with a disability. The Agency noted in its July 23, 2012 letter that it found him to "have a physical impairment that substantially impacts a major life activity." Additionally, Complainant's updated medical documentation shows that he is substantially limited in standing, walking, lifting, bending squatting, climbing, kneeling and twisting. As such, we find that Complainant has shown that he is an individual with a disability.

We further find that Complainant is a qualified individual with a disability. A qualified individual with a disability is an "individual with a disability who satisfies the requisite skill, experience, education, and other job-related requirements of the employment positions such individual holds or desires, and who, with or without reasonable accommodation, can perform the essential functions of such position." 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(m). Complainant was performing his job with an accommodation since 2012, and there is no evidence that Complainant failed to perform the essential functions of his position. Additionally, PM2 stated that if Complainant was assigned a route with "curbside then his current medicals dated April 19 would allow him to perform the work assignment." ROI at pg. 166. Accordingly, we find that Complainant is a qualified individual with a disability, who is entitled to a reasonable accommodation.

We find that the Agency failed to provide Complainant with a reasonable accommodation since April 4, 2016. The Agency stated that it accommodated Complainant when it placed him in a light duty assignment. However, we do not find this to be an effective accommodation. Despite being a full-time carrier, Complainant's light duty assignment did not provide him with full-time employment, and he had to take leave to make up the difference. PM2 stated that she placed Complainant on a light duty assignment because his documentation stated that he could only walk for ".25 min and stand 1.5 hrs. in a 8 hour day." However, Complainant argues that PM2 did not correctly understand his walking limitation. PM2 stated that Complainant informed her that he could walk for 15 minutes, take a break, and then walk again for 15 minutes. While we note that PM2 may have misunderstood Complainant's walking limitation, PM2 stated that a route with "curbside" could have accommodated Complainant's limitations, but there is no indication that this was considered, let alone granted. Additionally, there is no explanation why Complainant was not placed back into the C-32 route, or a similar route, which he had successfully worked for years as an accommodation.

Further, while Complainant obtained full-time work when he requested the mini-bids, we find that his bidding for specific assignments is routine business, and not an accommodation granted by the Agency. A reasonable accommodation is an adjustment or change at work for a reason related to a medical condition. See Bryan R. v. United States Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 0120130020 (Mar. 20, 2015), citing EEOC Enforcement Guidance on Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship under the Americans with Disabilities Act, No. 915.002 (Oct. 17, 2002). Therefore, we find that the Agency failed to provide Complainant with a reasonable accommodation since April 4, 2016.

Additionally, the Agency has not shown any evidence that providing Complainant with an effective accommodation would have posed an undue hardship. Accordingly, we find that the Agency discriminated against Complainant based on his disability because: He was a qualified individual with a disability; the Agency failed to provide him with an effective accommodation since April 4, 2016; and the Agency did not show that doing so would have caused undue hardship.

In addition to the Agency's actions discussed above, we are troubled by the Agency's handling of Complainant's request for a reasonable accommodation. Complainant stated that he attempted to discuss his limitations with PM2 and CC2, and that they did not listen to him. Additionally, Complainant had to mitigate the situation by requesting mini-bids, while he continued in the reasonable accommodation process. The Agency then stated it did not need to accommodate him because he was already accommodated with the mini-bids. Further, CC2 asserted that Complainant "refused accommodation" even though the Agency had not granted him an accommodation. Moreover, the Agency asked if Complainant were willing to relocate or work outside his local commuting area. When Complainant declined, the Agency accused Complainant of failing to engage in the interactive process in its final decision, despite his sending a letter to the DRAC clearly showing his participation in the interactive process and expressing his interest in working with the Agency to find an effective accommodation. We remind the Agency of its ongoing duty to engage in the interactive process once an employee requests a reasonable accommodation.
