1. Partial Dismissals

When a formal complaint is filed, the first decision the agency must make is whether to accept or dismiss the complaint. It is also possible that portions of the complaint will be accepted for further processing and other portions of the complaint will be dismissed. The Commission's 1999 regulation amendments substantially revised the process for appealing those portions that are dismissed. Under the 1992 version of Part 1614, the complainant had an immediate right to appeal dismissed portions of a complaint. The agency was required to notify the complainant that any dismissal of claims was a final agency decision subject to immediate review by the EEOC Office of Federal Operations, and to further notify the complainant of the applicable time limits for filing an appeal. See Schenker v. Postmaster General, 05880108 (1988).

In essence, the Commission's approach to the appeal of partial dismissals prior to the 1999 amendments was to create a mandatory interlocutory appeal. Most appellate bodies follow the rule observed in the federal court system--an appeal on a dismissed claim may not be filed until the remaining case is adjudicated. An exception lies in the case of an interlocutory appeal, but the judge whose ruling is challenged must "certify" the matter for interlocutory appeal. Appeals are certified only when the challenged ruling is likely to affect the outcome of the case, or is precedential and there is substantial public interest in having the matter immediately resolved. An interlocutory appeal allows an immediate review of the ruling. Such an appeal is rarely granted because the appellate courts disfavor piecemeal litigation.

Under the 1999 version of Part 1614, the mandatory interlocutory appeal is eliminated and an agency's decision to dismiss portions of a complaint is reviewable by OFO only after the agency takes final action on the accepted portions. 29 CFR 1614.107(b). The agency must explicitly notify the complainant of what claims are being accepted and what claims are being dismissed prior to investigation of the complaint. Section 1614.107(b) currently provides that the agency must take the following action when dismissing a portion of a complaint:

Where the agency believes that some but not all of the claims in a complaint should be dismissed for the reasons contained in paragraphs (a)(1) through (9) of this section, the agency shall notify the complainant in writing of its determination, the rationale for that determination and that those claims will not be investigated, and shall place a copy of the notice in the investigative file. A determination under this paragraph is reviewable by an administrative judge if a hearing is requested on the remainder of the complaint, but is not appealable until final action is taken on the remainder of the complaint.

The EEO investigator is not the proper official to accept or dismiss issues. This must be done by the agency head or his designee.

MD-110 further elaborates on the current procedures to be followed in cases involving partial dismissal, at 5-27 to 5-28:

1. Where a Hearing is Requested

If the complainant requests a hearing from an Administrative Judge, the Administrative Judge will evaluate the agency's reasons for believing that a portion of the complaint met the standards for dismissal before holding the hearing. If the Administrative Judge believes that all or a part of the agency's reasons are not well taken, the entire complaint or all of the portions not meeting the standards for dismissal will continue in the hearing process. The parties may conduct discovery to develop the record for all portions of the complaint continuing in the hearing process. The Administrative Judge's decision on the partial dismissal will become part of the Administrative Judge's final decision on the complaint and may be appealed by either party after final action is taken on the complaint.

2. Where a Final Decision By the Agency Is Requested

Where a complainant requests a final decision by the agency without a hearing, the agency will issue a decision addressing all claims in the complaint, including its rationale for dismissing claims, if any, and its findings on the merits of the remainder of the complaint. The complainant may appeal the agency's decision, including any partial dismissals, to the Commission.

Agency decisions shall include the following:

(a) findings of fact and conclusions of law on the merits of each issue in the complaint;

(b) appropriate remedies and relief in accordance with subpart E of part 1614 when discrimination is found;

(c) notice of right to appeal to the Commission (EEOC Form 573, Notice of Appeal/Petition, to be attached), unless the complaint involves a mixed case, where the agency should provide notice of right to appeal to the MSPB (not the EEOC) within thirty (30) days of receipt of the agency final decision;

(d) notice of right to file a civil action in federal district court;

(e) the name of the proper defendant in any such lawsuit; and

(f) the applicable time limits for appeals and lawsuits.

The primary reason for the revision in processing partial dismissals was to avoid fragmentation of complaints, as discussed above, subheading "Partial Dismissals." Immediate appeal rights of partial dismissals resulted in a great deal of fragmentation, as the Commission explained at 64 Fed. Reg. 37,647 (1999):

In the NPRM, the Commission proposed changes to the regulations to eliminate interlocutory appeals of partial dismissals of complaints. Currently, where an agency dismisses part of a complaint, but not the entire complaint, the complainant has the right to immediately appeal the partial dismissal to EEOC. The Commission provided for interlocutory appeals of partial dismissals in Part 1614, hoping to streamline the process and avoid holding two or more hearings on the same complaint. Multiple hearings could have occurred absent an interlocutory appeal when EEOC reversed an agency's partial dismissal after a hearing was held on the rest of the complaint. The Commission believes that this result can be accomplished without the unintended delays or fragmentation of complaints that may have resulted from implementation of the current provision. The Commission proposed to amend section 1614.401 to remove the right to immediately appeal the dismissal of a portion of a complaint. In addition, the Commission proposed to add a paragraph to the dismissals section, section 1614.107, explaining how to process complaints where a portion of the complaint, but not the entire complaint, meets one or more of the standards for dismissal contained in that section.

...

The Commission believes that eliminating interlocutory appeals of partial dismissals will result in a more efficient complaint process and will help avoid fragmentation of complaints. The Commission has decided, therefore, to finalize the proposals without change. The concerns raised by some of the commenters are addressed by the procedure contained in new section 1614.107(b).

As is apparent from the above excerpts of Part 1614 and MD-110, the Commission has done more than simply eliminate what were essentially interlocutory appeals from the partial dismissal of complaints. In cases where a hearing request is made, the administrative judge reviews the dismissal. In this manner, an administrative judge can correct an errant dismissal before the case has gone to hearing and include the dismissed claims with the claim proceeding to hearing.

Prior to the 1999 regulation amendments, "[t]he Commission generally has held that an agency will not be permitted to hide behind an acceptance letter to reject allegations by failing to plainly reject the allegations and to give the appellant proper appeal rights." Jayme v. Attorney General, 01941376 (1995) (citing Sumter v. Department of Interior, 05940500 (1994)); see also Jozlin v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01920040 (1992); Mosby v. United States Postal Service, 01913992 (1991). This meant that an agency could not advise a complainant only of the claims accepted for investigation and then later argue that all other claims were dismissed. If an issue was dismissed, the agency was required to explicitly tell the complainant of its dismissal. However, where a claim was repeatedly omitted from the agency's acceptance of claims and, over the course of several years, the complainant failed to contest the definition of the claims, implied dismissal was proper. See, e.g., Mole v. Secretary of Treasury, 01966182 (1998). Likewise, in Couvalt v. Postmaster General, 01993346 (2002), the Commission found the complainant could not raise on appeal claims that the agency failed to address in its final decision, where the agency defined the accepted claims and gave the complainant a chance to respond. Not only did the complainant fail to respond, she failed to raise the narrowing of her claims with the investigator. Under those circumstances, the Commission found the complainant had waived her right to pursue those claims.

In Alexander v. Secretary of Defense, 01956509 (1997), the Commission pointed out that proper dismissal of a claim on one basis does not allow the agency to dismiss the claim in its entirety where other bases are also alleged. The Commission in Alexander stated:

Although the Commission finds that the agency properly dismissed reprisal as a basis, we note, as discussed above, that appellant alleged discrimination on the bases of reprisal and disability. We therefore find that the agency's decision to dismiss allegations (b)(1)-(4) in their entirety was improper, as appellant's allegations of discrimination on the basis of disability with respect to these allegations remains alive. Accordingly, the agency's decision to dismiss allegations (b)(1)-(4) for failure to state a claim was improper and is REVERSED.

Nothing in the 1999 amendments, the notice of final rulemaking or the 1999 revisions to MD-110 indicate that the Commission, in eliminating the immediate appeal of dismissed complaint claims, intended to change its previous case law requiring explicit dismissal of claims. The bottom-line is that agencies are required to construe complaints based on the factual allegations and not on the legal conclusions that complainants attach to the factual allegations.
