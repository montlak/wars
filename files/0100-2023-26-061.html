f. Defamation Actions--Threatened Civil Actions

Being ever litigious, sometimes one federal employee meets a complaint by another employee with a complaint of his or her own. In some cases, supervisors have filed civil actions against employees who filed EEO complaints, charging that the allegations of discrimination amount to defamation.

Of course, this leads to counterclaims. When an employee alleges that his supervisor has filed a state court claim of defamation against him in retaliation for an EEO complaint, this states a claim for relief. As the Commission explained in Quick v. Secretary of Air Force, 01975552 (1998):

Courts have recognized that the filing of a state court action for defamation in retaliation for an employee's exercise of Title VII rights violates the anti-retaliatory provision of Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, as amended. See EEOC v. Levi Strauss & Co., 515 F. Supp 640, 623, 644 (N.D. Illinois 1981); and EEOC v. Virginia Carolina Veneer Corp., 495 F. Supp. 775, 777-778 (W. D. Virginia 1980); see also Berry v. Stevinson Chevrolet, 74 F.3d 980, 986 (10th Cir. 1996) (malicious prosecution in retaliation for filing an EEO complaint violates § 704 of Title VII). The courts have so held because retaliation, whether in the form of a subsequent discharge or a court proceeding, places an added cost on the exercise of statutory rights and, as such, has a chilling effect on the exercise of those rights. EEOC v. Levi Strauss & Co., 515 F. Supp 640, 642-643 (N.D. Illinois 1981); accord Garcia v. Lawn, 805 F.2d 1400, 1405 (9th Cir. 1986) (retaliation is likely to cause irreparable harm to the public interest in enforcing the law by deterring others from filing charges); see also, Robinson v. Shell Oil Co., 117 S. Ct. 843, 848 (1997) (a primary purpose of anti-retaliation provisions is maintaining unfettered access to statutory remedial mechanisms) Marr v. Department of the Air Force, EEOC Appeal No. 01941344 (June 27, 1996) (a supervisor's attempt to dissuade an employee from participating as a witness constituted unlawful interference with the EEO process).

In most cases the question of motivation is a question of fact to be determined after an investigation and/or a hearing. Boyd v. Department of Transportation, EEOC Appeal No. 01955276 (October 10, 1997); see also, EEOC v. Levi Strauss & Co., 515 F. Supp at 644 (to establish the propriety of an injunction, the Commission must demonstrate that the defamation action was filed for a retaliatory purpose); but see EEOC v. Virginia Carolina Veneer Corp., 495 F. Supp. at 777 (summary judgment granted based on a finding that the allegations contained in a state court action constituted an admission of a retaliatory motive where the alleged defamatory writings, statements or actions were made only to the EEOC, and to the Department of Labor, Employment Standards Administration).

The Commission also found that the agency in Quick prematurely decided that, because the court complaint was filed by the supervisor as an individual, the agency was not liable:

The agency dismissed the appellant's complaint on the ground that the civil action was filed by the supervisor individually and that the agency did not have control over the matter. Thereby, the agency expressed its view of the legal and factual merit of the appellant's complaint.

The Commission repeatedly has reversed agencies' dismissals for failure to state a claim where the agency based the dismissal on its view of the ultimate merit of the complaint allegations. See, e.g., Franz v. Treasury, EEOC Request No. 05950734 (April 29, 1996) (agency's argument that the complainant was not disparately treated went to the merit of his claim); Ernst v. Treasury, EEOC Request No. 05950131 (March 7, 1996) (the agency's argument that the denied detail was to a lower-grade position with fewer responsibilities and less opportunities for promotion went to the merits of the complaint); Hagan v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 05920709 (January 7, 1993) (agency wrongfully dismissed allegation of disparate treatment in the payment of licensing fees based on the ground that the agency could not pay an employee's licensing fees); Cann v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05920861 (December 31, 1992) (agency wrongfully dismissed allegation that the agency failed to upgrade an employee to full time regular status on the ground that there were no full time positions available to him under the terms of the collective bargaining agreement); Ferns v. Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05920597 (September 10, 1992) (agency's argument that the complainant was ineligible for position goes to the merits of the complaint).

In the instant case, the agency has not provided a factual record upon which the question of employer liability can be fully addressed, including whether the supervisor should be deemed to have been an agent of the agency. See Meritor Savings Bank v. Vinson, 477 U.S. 57, 72 (1986). In Meritor, the Supreme Court declined to issue a definitive rule on employer liability, given the insufficiency of the record in that case. However, the Court noted that Congress wanted courts to look to agency principles for guidance on the question of employer liability. Id.; see also, Berry v. Stevinson Chevrolet, 74 F.3d at 987-88 (employer liable because, more likely than not, management officials at Toyota West caused the employee to initiate the criminal complaint). The agency also has not investigated the appellant's complaint to determine the supervisor's motive for filing the civil action.

Given these circumstances, the Commission finds that it was premature for the agency to conclude, in effect, that it had no responsibility for the supervisor's action.

The Commission reminds the agency that, pursuant to 29 CFR § 1614.102(a)(5), it has a duty to provide training and advice to managers and supervisors to assure their understanding and implementation of the EEO policy and program, including the anti-retaliation provision set forth in 29 CFR § 1614.101(b). In addition, the agency is required to take appropriate disciplinary action against employees who engage in discriminatory practices, including retaliatory practices. 29 CFR § 1614.102(a)(6). See "Section-by-Section Analysis," 29 CFR Part 1614, 57 Federal Register 12634, 12642 (1992) (whenever any regulation in Part 1614 speaks of a discrimination complaint, the reference should be read to include a retaliation complaint).

Similarly, a supervisor's threat to file a civil action against an employee for his EEO activity is sufficient to set forth a claim of retaliation. In Reed v. Secretary of Transportation, 01A05085 (2003), the Commission entered a finding of discrimination where a supervisor threatened to file a civil action against an employee who testified before Congress about a policy he believed was discriminatory and specifically named the supervisor in his testimony. The Commission in Reed found:

Applying the facts herein, we find that RMO retaliated against complainant. Complainant clearly engaged in protected activity when he testified before Congress about an agency policy or practice that is made unlawful by one of the employment discrimination statutes. We find that RMO did threaten to file a civil suit after being named by complainant during this testimony. In so finding, we conclude that CW1's statements were credible and corroborated. Moreover, the RMO made the threat against complainant one week after complainant testified before Congress, making the threat sufficiently close in time to permit an inference of retaliatory motive. We find the threat to be adverse treatment that is reasonably likely to deter complainant or others, like CW1, from engaging in protected activity, as contemplated by the statute.

The determination that RMO's threat of a civil action was sufficient to constitute unlawful interference is consistent with our previous decisions wherein we have held that it is unlawful retaliation for a manager to threaten to file a civil action in response to an EEO complaint. See Boyd v. Department of Transportation, EEOC Appeal No. 01955276 (October 10, 1997). Even where, as here, complainant is not alleging that an agency manager actually followed through on the threat and filed a civil action, unlawful reprisal is still present. We stated in Boyd that:

...[I]in the instant case, the appellant is not alleging that the agency's managers filed a civil action against her in retaliation for her informal complaint of discrimination. Instead, the appellant is alleging that manager 2 told her in the presence of the facility manager that he would bring a legal action against her if she pursued an EEO complaint against him, and manager 1's attorney wrote her that she would face legal action for slander if she pursued her EEO complaint.

If proven true, these acts would constitute unlawful interference with the exercise of the appellant's EEO rights because they would have placed an undue and potentially chilling burden on the exercise of those rights: the need to weigh the risk of incurring the cost of defending against a slander action against the desirability of obtaining a remedy for the alleged discrimination. See Robinson v. Shell Oil Co., 117 S. Ct. 843, 848 (1997) (a primary purpose of anti-retaliation provisions is maintaining unfettered access to statutory remedial mechanisms); see also Marr v. Department of the Air Force, EEOC Appeal No. 01941344 (June 27, 1996) (a supervisor's attempt to dissuade an employee from participating as a witness constituted unlawful interference with the EEO process).

Moreover, it is not necessary for complainant to demonstrate that he himself was actually deterred from filing a complaint. We stated in Boyd that, "[t]he mere fact that the appellant filed the instant formal complaint does not defeat her claim of unlawful interference with the EEO process. See Johnson v. Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05921027 (March 18, 1993) (merely because the complainant filed an EEO complaint despite the EEO Office's lack of assistance does not mean that the complainant is no longer aggrieved)."

For these above-stated reasons, the Commission finds that complainant has successfully proven his claim of retaliation, through unlawful interference with the EEO process, in violation of 29 C.F.R. 1614.101(b) and the anti-retaliation provisions of Title VII. In finding unlawful retaliation, we conclude that the agency offered no legitimate, non-discriminatory reason to negate the action of RMO. We note that, pursuant to 29 C.F.R. 1614.102(a) (5), agencies have a duty to provide training and advice to managers and supervisors to assure their understanding and implementation of the EEO policy and program including the anti-retaliation provision set forth in 29 C.F.R. 1614.101(b). In addition, federal agencies are required to take appropriate disciplinary action against employees who engage in discriminatory practices, including retaliatory practices. 29 C.F.R. 1614.102(a) (6). See "Section-by-Section Analysis," 29 C.F.R. Part 1614, 57 Federal Register 12634, 12642 (1992) (whenever any regulation in Part 1614 speaks of a discrimination complaint, the reference should be read to include a retaliation complaint). Accordingly, the agency is directed to take action consistent with the order below.
