7. Failure to Meet Class Prerequisites

Under 29 CFR 1614.204(a)(2), an administrative judge is required to dismiss any class complaint that does not meet the four prerequisites for class certification--numerosity, commonality, typicality and adequacy of representation. The class prerequisites are discussed in detail below, but a general description of the prerequisites are described in the Judges' Handbook at 10-2 to 10-3 as:

(a) Numerosity--The class is so numerous that a consolidated complaint of the members of the class is impractical. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.204(a)(2)(i). See Wood, Sr., et al. v. Department of Energy, EEOC Request No. 05950985 (October 5, 1998);

(b) Commonality--There are questions of fact common to the class. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.204(a)(2)(ii). Factors to determine commonality include: whether the practice at issue affects the whole class or only a few individuals; the degree of local autonomy or centralized administration involved; and the uniformity of the membership of the class, in terms of the likelihood that the class members' treatment will involve common questions of fact. See Woods, et al. v. Department of Housing and Urban Development, EEOC Appeal No. 01961033 (February 13, 1998).

(c) Typicality--The claims of the agent of the class are typical of the claims of the class. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.204(a)(2)(iii). The overriding principle with regard to typicality is that class agents must possess the same interest and suffer the same injury as the members of the proposed class. General Telephone Co. v. Falcon, 457 U.S. 147, 156 (1982); Johnson-Feldman, et al. v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 01953168 (August 7, 1997), request for reconsideration denied, EEOC Request No. 05971098 (September 10, 1999).

(d) Adequacy of Representation--The agent of the class, or, if represented, the representative, will fairly and adequately protect the interests of the class. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.204(a)(2)(iv). If the record on a class complaint satisfies the numerosity, typicality, and commonality requirements for class certification, the Administrative Judge may conditionally certify the class for a reasonable period of time so that the class agent may secure adequate representation. EEO MD-110, 8-5.

Although the class agents bear the burden of proof in demonstrating they have meet the prerequisites for class certification, the record must be adequately developed to permit a reasoned decision on certification. See, e.g., Jones v. Postmaster General, 0120060367 (2008) (finding that administrative judge did not err in failing to order agency to comply with discovery requests on underlying claims, but remanding case for further evidentiary development on certification issues).

When an administrative judge dismisses a class action complaint on the grounds that the class does not meet the requirements of numerosity, commonality, typicality, and adequacy of representation, the agency may not cancel a subsequent individual complaint filed by a class member over the same allegations on the grounds that the complaint is identical to the rejected class complaint. Wahlquist v. Defense Logistics Agency, 01842461 (1986). A dismissal of a class complaint on the grounds of failure to fulfill the requirements of a class is not a decision on the merits, so the complaints are not identical.

An administrative judge properly dismisses a class complaint where the putative class is a subclass of a pending class action. See Hickey v. Secretary of Army, 01892024 (1989) (holding that black females alleging discrimination in promotional practices are a subclass of all black employees complaining of discriminatory employment practices).

A confusing situation arises where a second class action is filed on behalf of employees who allege they were subject to the same discriminatory practices as those alleged in the original class action, but further allege the discrimination did not occur until after the filing of the original complaint. In Watson v. National Aeronautics and Space Administration, 01832878 (1985), the EEOC followed the lead of the Sixth Circuit in Bronson v. Board of Education, 525 F.2d 344 (1975), and intimated that it would not find such class actions to be identical to the original class action. However, the Commission also found that the complaint had a fatal factual flaw in that the named plaintiff was a member of the original class and the action she complained of was covered by the original class action. The Commission also noted that there was a strong dissent in the Bronson case and the right to file a subsequent class action was by no means clear.
