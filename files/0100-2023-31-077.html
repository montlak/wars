3. Paralegals

In the Finch case, the Commission noted that the definition of a paralegal is "broad-based" and not restricted to those who have a formal education as a paralegal. The question is whether the attorney employing the paralegal believes the person is qualified to perform the duties assigned. The Commission further noted that without the use of paralegals, it is likely that attorney fees in a given case would be higher.

Finch was revisited by the Commission on the agency's request for reconsideration. 05880051 (1988). The agency advanced the argument that the complainant was not entitled to fees for a "paralegal" because the attorney referred to that person as a legal assistant. The Commission rejected the argument, finding that the work the assistant performed--discussions with the client and legal research--was consistent with services provided by a paralegal. The Commission also rejected the agency's move to have the assistant's hourly rate reduced from $30 to $15 where, in its final decision, the agency had agreed to pay $30.

The Commission has awarded compensation for time spent by a law clerk making photocopies of cases. The Commission found such an activity was within the "typical research duties performed by a paralegal" and that it was reasonable for the law clerk to copy materials while researching for an attorney. See Castillo v. Secretary of Transportation, 01970175 (2000) (awarding fees for a law clerk); Bryant v. Secretary of Army, 01912990 (1992). However, in Castillo the Commission held that the attorney could not increase, in his request for fees, the hourly amount he paid the clerk. The Commission rejected the argument of complainant's counsel that the increase was justified because of his supervision of the clerk.

Not all paralegals are equal. In Cain v. Secretary of Commerce, 07A40022 (2005), the Commission found that it was appropriate to award an hourly rate of $100 to a paralegal who was also a law school student. The Commission noted the finding of the administrative judge that because the paralegal was a law school student, he had "an enhanced set of skills." Id.

Because fees cannot be awarded to federal employees who represent other employees, 29 CFR 1614.501(e)(1)(iii), fees are not allowable for paralegals who work under the direction of an attorney who is a federal employee. As the Commission found in Canady v. Secretary of Army, 01882686 (1988):

In instances where paralegals work on a case under the supervision of an attorney, the attorney may normally bill his client for these fees as part of his services. However, the professional relationship exists between the attorney and the client, not between the paralegals and the client. If the attorney is not eligible to recover fees because he is a Federal employee, then he cannot recover as part of his services the fees billed for the paralegals' work.

Though hours spent by a paralegal are compensable, the Commission will review such claims to ensure they are not duplicative of work performed by the attorney. See Lee v. Secretary of Defense, 01962241 (1997) (finding the hours a paralegal spent at a conference with the attorney and client were redundant).
