1. Past Statements and Actions

Past statements and actions that indicate a discriminatory bias are not only relevant, they can lead to a finding of pretext. Past statements or actions, standing alone, may be insufficient but the Commission uses a totality of the circumstances approach in evaluating pretext. A good example is provided by Barnette v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120100558 (2012):

On closer review of the record, given the totality of the circumstances, we find that the Agency's reasons are a pretext for race discrimination and that the Agency's finding of no race discrimination is not supported by substantial evidence in the record.

Discrimination often is subtle, and there rarely is a "smoking gun." EEOC Compliance Manual Section 15, "Race and Color Discrimination," No. 915.003 at 15-V.A.2 (May 20, 1998) (Compliance Manual). As such, determining whether race played a role in decision-making requires a careful examination of all of the relevant facts and circumstances. Id. Potential evidence of racial disparate treatment includes race-related statements made by a decision-maker, relevant background facts, relevant personnel policies, the decision-maker's race, and statistical evidence. Id.

As set forth below, we find that the evidence shows, more likely than not, that Complainant's non-selection was motivated by race.

First, the record contains an admission by C1, the Caucasian management selecting official for the position, that he made negative race-related statements about African-Americans. Specifically, when asked if he had made disparaging or derogatory statements about African-Americans, C1 testified, "I probably have at some point in time, but certainly not in a public forum."

While the Agency argues that this testimony is irrelevant, we find otherwise. We find that his own admission of having made "disparaging" statements about African-Americans constitutes relevant evidence of racial bias or animus on his part against African-Americans. Moreover, we find that the AJ's failure to factor this was an error.

The record contains unrebutted evidence of the lack of African-Americans in Supervisory Social Worker positions and in GS-13 or higher positions within the organizational unit where the Social Worker Executive position is located. Specifically, Complainant asserted, unrebutted by the Agency, that there has never been an African-American Supervisory Social Worker at the facility in the 30 years she has worked there.

In addition, the demographic breakdown of the employees in the Office of the Chief of Staff reflects that all four of the GS-13 or higher employees are Caucasian. While the Agency argues that these facts are irrelevant, we find otherwise. Specific employment decisions must be considered in a larger context and should not be viewed as isolated incidents. See Compliance Manual, at 15-V.A.2. Relevant information that can shed light on whether the employer's adverse employment decision was motivated by race includes the employer's treatment of other employees and the work environment generally. Id. Moreover, statistics reflecting the employer's general policy or practice can be helpful in determining whether race was a factor in a particular selection decision. Id.

The record contains evidence that SE1's three-year appointment as Acting Social Worker Executive was an unusual personnel practice. Specifically, C1 testified that it was "[p]robably not" a normal practice to keep someone in an acting position for three years.

Notwithstanding the Agency's argument that engagement in an abnormal or prohibited personnel practice alone is not evidence of unlawful discrimination; an employer's deviation from an applicable personnel policy, or a past practice, can support an inference of a discriminatory motive. See Compliance Manual, at 15-V.A.2. Here, we find C1 failed to provide an explanation for why he appointed SE1 to the Acting position instead of following through with R1's recommendation to appoint Complainant.

After examining all the facts and circumstances, we find that the Agency's explanations for Complainant's non-selection are a pretext for race discrimination. These include: C1's admission that he made disparaging statements about the African-American race; the absence of African-Americans in Supervisory Social Worker positions; the absence of GS-13 or higher African-Americans in the Office of the Chief of Staff--the office where the position was located and the office that C1 was the head of; C1's admission that SE1's three-year appointment in the Acting position was not a normal practice; and C1's failure to explain why he appointed SE1 to the Acting position when R1 had recommended the appointment of Complainant.

In Balderas v. Attorney General, 0120110222 (2012), the Commission found out-of-country work assignments based on friendships with the assigning official was evidence of sex discrimination where the official "normally picked her friends for out-of-country assignments, and her friends were male."
