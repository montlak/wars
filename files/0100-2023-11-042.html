13. Negotiated Grievance Procedure, Use of

The relationship between the EEO process and an available negotiated grievance procedure has long been a source of confusion for agencies and employees alike. The confusion is exacerbated by the fact that a distinction needs to be made between negotiated grievance procedures, i.e., grievance procedures that are the result of a collective bargaining agreement, and agency administrative grievance procedures.

Agencies that are subject to 5 USC 7121(d) may allow allegations of discrimination to be pursued via the grievance procedures of the applicable collective bargaining unit if the complainant is covered by the procedure. The employee must elect one procedure or the other and cannot pursue the matter in both forums. 29 CFR 1614.301(a). An agency should dismiss an EEO complaint when a grievance is pending on the same matter if the grievance is filed first, whether or not the employee has raised allegations of discrimination, as long as the grievance procedure permits consideration of discrimination allegations. Only the filing of a formal discrimination complaint or a written grievance constitutes an election of procedures. An employee who seeks precomplaint counseling has not elected the EEO procedure. Under 7121(d), the employee may request the EEOC to review the final agency decision on the allegations of discrimination presented by the grievant.

The counselor must give written notice of election of remedy rights. 29 CFR 1614.105(b). An agency is also required to inform any employee who initiates an EEO complaint after filing a grievance of his or her obligation to raise the issue of discrimination in the negotiated grievance procedure. 29 CFR 1614.301(a).

Especially in cases that involve the interpretation or application of a provision of the collective bargaining agreement, serious consideration should be given to pursuing the matter through the grievance procedure. Failure to adhere to the contract may be evidence of discrimination. But, the Commission is concerned only with collective bargaining provisions to the extent they tend to demonstrate the existence of a discriminatory motive. The Commission has no authority to enforce a provision in the absence of discrimination. Actions to enforce a contract provision are appropriately pursued through the grievance procedure.

When an agency misleads an employee on whether the appropriate route to pursue allegations is through the EEO process or the grievance procedure, the time limit for initiating counseling will be waived. Mosley v. Department of Air Force, 01840930 (1984). Even though the agency did not mislead the employee deliberately, she pursued the EEO complaint immediately after her grievance was rejected on the basis that discrimination was not within the purview of that process. But, the mere filing of a grievance alleging discrimination does not toll the statute of limitations when it is determined that the allegations are not within the purview of the grievance procedure. Williams v. Department of Navy, 01842322 (1984). In Williams, there was no evidence that the agency misled the complainant about available remedies, the grievance procedure clearly stated that allegations of discrimination were exempted, and the complainant had previously filed EEO complaints.

The United States Postal Service is not subject to 5 USC 7121(d) and, therefore, the statutory prohibition against pursuing both a grievance and an EEO complaint over the same personnel action does not apply to that agency. There are also a few other agencies that are not covered by 7121(d). In those agencies, processing of an EEO complaint may be held in abeyance during the processing of the grievance. The decision to hold the EEO complaint in abeyance is within the discretion of the agency, but it must notify the complainant, in writing, of its decision to do so. 29 CFR 1614.301(c). For more on the relationship between the EEO process and the negotiated grievance procedure, see Chapter 20, subheading "Negotiated Grievance Procedure."

When the employee elects to pursue a matter through the negotiated grievance procedure, the employee may not use the date of the denial of that grievance as the start of the period for initiating counselor contact. Schafer v. USPS, 01831179 (1983). The fact that an employee does not realize an incident is discriminatory because she was not aware of her rights under the collective bargaining agreement is not grounds for tolling the limitation period. Thomas v. USPS, 01831304 (1983).
