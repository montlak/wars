2. Mixed Cases

When a class action alleges that the action that affects the class is within the jurisdiction of the Merit Systems Protection Board, then any hearing on the class action must be held by the MSPB. In Thockmorton v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01A20994 (2003), the Commission found that although its regulations on mixed-case complaints do not specifically address the issue that a class action complaint alleging discrimination in the context of a reduction-in-force could be appealed only to the MSPB and not the EEOC.

However, in Rosengarten v. Secretary of Transportation, 0720070073 (2007), the Commission found a class action complaint alleging that a reorganization and the resulting reassignments had a disparate impact based on age did not present a mixed case issue. The Commission found that the administrative judge improperly defined the potential class claim by focusing on the fact that the reorganization and reassignments resulted in some complainants resigning or retiring rather than transferring to Oklahoma. Instead of viewing the allegations as relating to constructive discharges, which would have put the matter within Board jurisdiction, the Commission, in Rosengarten, found:

As an initial matter, we find that the AJ's decision did not accurately capture the claim in the class complaint. Complainant alleged in the class complaint that the agency discriminated against employees based on their age when it directed the consolidation of all FAA accounting operations in six regions into a single finance center located in Oklahoma, informing them that they could only keep their jobs if they transferred to Oklahoma. The class complaint alleged that the consolidation order adversely impacted employees over age 40. We find that the class complaint regards the consolidation and transfer of jobs to Oklahoma. While this resulted in some separations and retirements, such effects constitute alleged harm to some of the individual members of the class caused by the consolidation and transfer, not the definition of the class claim.

We need not address whether the AJ misapplied the procedural requisites of Throckmorton because it does not apply in this case. As the class complaint regards consolidation of operations and transfer of jobs, it is not a mixed case complaint appealable to the MSPB. Accordingly, the routine processing procedures of 29 C.F.R. § 1614.204 apply; not 29 C.F.R. § 1614.302.

The regulation set forth at 29 C.F.R. § 1614.204(d)(2) & 107(a)(1) provides, in relevant part, that an AJ may dismiss a class complaint that fails to state a claim. If an employee or applicant for employment is not aggrieved, their claim does not state a claim. The Commission's federal sector case precedent has long defined an "aggrieved employee" as one who suffers a present harm or loss with respect to a term, condition, or privilege of employment for which there is a remedy. Diaz v. Department of the Air Force, EEOC Request No. 05931049 (April 21, 1994). We disagree with the agency's finding that the class complaint does not state a claim. Having your job moved to another part of the country states a claim of harm, and it is more likely than not that a number of purported class members sustained harm. While some purported class members may not be harmed because they were satisfied with reassignments to other jobs, enjoyed the move to Oklahoma, would have retired anyway, and so forth, this does not mean the class complaint as a whole fails to state a claim. Purported class members who were not harmed can be denied individual relief at the remedy stage. See 29 C.F.R. § 1614.204(l)(3).
