2. Restoration of Time

When an employee is improperly denied official time to prepare for a hearing, the precise remedy will depend on whether the employee prepared for the hearing on his own time or used some form of leave to prepare on agency time. As the Commission explained in Graham v. Postmaster General, 05910978 (1992):

The Commission notes that a request to reopen is only appropriate when one or more of the above criteria are satisfied; however, the agency herein does not dispute the Commission's ultimate finding awarding appellant six hours of official time, but requests clarification as to the manner in which such time shall be provided to appellant. Therefore, as the Commission concludes that the agency has failed to allege or to show that our previous decision was erroneous, the Commission denies the agency's request to reopen. See 29 CFR 1613.235(b). In response to the agency's request for clarification, the Commission offers the following guidance on the manner in which our previous decision should be interpreted.

The Commission's Order in our previous decision provides, in pertinent part, the following:

The agency shall determine whether appellant was required to take annual leave or leave without pay in order to prepare for the hearing. If appellant used annual leave, then the agency is directed to restore six hours of such leave to him. If appellant prepared for the hearing during his own time, then the agency shall provide him with six hours of official time to be used as he deems suitable. If, however, appellant was required to take leave without pay in order to prepare for the hearing, then the agency is ORDERED to issue appellant a check for the six hours of official time not provided to him in this matter.

The agency stated in its request to reopen that appellant prepared for the hearing during his own time; therefore, the Commission's decision directs the agency to "provide (appellant) with six hours of official time to be used as he deems suitable." As a threshold matter, the Commission advises the agency that the Commission did not intend that appellant be required to report to work and perform no tasks for a six hour period. Rather, the Commission's intent was that the agency allow appellant to take six hours of time away form his workplace, during his regularly scheduled tour of duty, because he was required to use six hours of his own time to do something (i.e., prepare for his hearing) which appellant should have been allowed to do on official time.

As to when and how appellant should be allowed to utilize the six hours of official time due him, the Commission notes that reasonableness should be the guide, and that the parties should jointly determine when and under what circumstances the time shall be used. In this regard, the Commission notes that the parties have a duty to cooperate during attempts to comply with the Commission's decision. For example, while appellant should be allowed to request to use this time when it is convenient for him, he should be required to notify the agency in advance for purposes of obtaining consent, and to coordinate his requests for time with the operational needs of the agency. Furthermore, it would be reasonable to allow appellant to use the time in increments rather than in one six hour block, if he wishes; however, increments of less than one hour each would not be practical or reasonable. Finally, it would not be reasonable to require appellant to utilize the time at issue immediately; however, for administrative reasons it would be practical to advise appellant to use the time he is due within a six month period following receipt of this decision, if appellant has not already used the six hours of official time.

When a complainant is improperly denied official time for hearing preparation and is forced to use annual leave, the proper remedy is restoration of that leave time. Brown v. Postmaster General, 04940004 (1995).
