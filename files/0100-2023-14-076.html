6. Impossibility of Compliance

Though rare, there are occasions where it becomes impossible for an agency to comply with a settlement. In such cases, the Commission will not find the agency in breach of the agreement. For example, in Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120123120 (2014), the Commission found:

In its August 2, 2012 final decision, the Agency concluded it was not in breach of the agreement. The Agency stated that less than three weeks after entering into the settlement agreement, on May 15, 2012, Complainant engaged in inappropriate conduct and was placed in emergency off-duty status. Subsequently, he was removed from his position by letter dated June 11, 2012. Thus, compliance with the agreement became impossible because of the intervening events.

...

In the instant case, we agree with the Agency that the intervening events made it impossible for it to meet with Complainant. Complainant was charged with engaging in inappropriate conduct that included lewd conduct, aggressive behavior, inappropriate comments, and lunging at a supervisor.' As a result, Complainant was barred from being at the postal facility. As such, we find that the Agency properly determined that it could not comply with the agreement because of these intervening events.

Eliz T. v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, 0120160918 (2018). See also discussion of Executive Order 13839 in "Expungement of Records--'Clean Paper' Agreements" in this chapter.

Impossibility, however, does not entirely relieve the performer of its obligations under the contract. In Cathy V. v. Secretary of Defense, 2021000639 (2021), for instance, the Commission found the doctrine of impossibility applicable where the agency agreed to place the complainant in "the next" first line supervisor position "that become available" to be "completed no later than 12 months from the effective date of this agreement," but due to budget cuts no such position became available during that time period. The Commission agreed with the agency that performance was impossible, but reminded that it was still obligated to comply with the terms of the contract:

In the instant case we are unpersuaded by the Agency's argument that the Agreement only required it to perform should a vacancy become available within twelve months of the signing of the Agreement. On the contrary we find that the clause stating that the action "will be completed no later than 12 months from the effective date of this agreement" commits the Agency to comply with the terms of the Agreement within twelve months. The Agency's argument that unforeseen external events, in the form of budget cuts, prevented the Agency from complying within the agreed-upon time frame, raises the issue of "impossibility of performance." Under this doctrine, where certain circumstances arise after execution of the contract, making it "impossible" to perform a provision in a settlement agreement, the non-performance may be excused. See Flemon v. U.S. Department of Agriculture, EEOC Request No. 05880291 (August 22, 1988) see also East Capitol View Cmty. Dev., Inc. v. Robinson, 941 A.2d. 1036, 1040 (D.C. 2008). We find that the doctrine of impossibility of performance is applicable in the instant case, and that the Agency's failure to comply with the terms of the Agreement within the twelve-month period mandated by the Agreement does not constitute a breach.

However, we further find that the doctrine does not mean that the Agency is relieved of its obligation to place Complainant in the next WS-2610-12 first line supervisor position that becomes available within the 402 EMXG. On the contrary, that obligation is still in effect and has not been complied with. Complainant has produced a copy of a job announcement for a WS 2610-12 Electronic Integrated Systems Mechanic Supervisor position at 402 EMXG with a posting date of November 2, 2020. On appeal, the Agency admits it did not place Complainant in this position because it believed that the Agreement only required it to act if a position became available during the twelve-month period following the signing of the Agreement, and the WS 2610-12 Electronic Integrated Systems Mechanic Supervisor position only became available after the twelve-month period was over. As noted above, we find that the Agency's interpretation is erroneous and by failing to place Complainant in the WS 2610-12 Electronic Integrated Systems Mechanic Supervisor position, we find that the Agency breached the Agreement, notwithstanding the fact that the position only became available after the end of the twelve-month period referenced in the Agreement.

Where we find a breach, the Commission has two options to remedy the situation: 1) reinstate the underlying complaint or 2) order specific performance. In this case, Complainant has requested specific performance. Therefore, on remand, we direct the Agency to specifically enforce the terms of the Agreement by placing Complainant in the next available WS-2610-12 first line supervisor position that becomes available within the 402 EMXG. We further find that due to the Agency's breach of its obligations under the settlement agreement, Complainant is entitled to back pay to cover the difference in salary between her current position and a supervisory position because had the Agency complied with the Agreement, Complainant would have been receiving supervisory level pay. We therefore order that Complainant receive back pay from the date the selectee for the WS 2610-12 Electronic Integrated Systems Mechanic Supervisor position first began working in that position to the date Complainant receives her promotion to a supervisory position.
