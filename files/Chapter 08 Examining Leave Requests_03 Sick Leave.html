SICK LEAVE

Sick leave, like annual leave, is an entitlement of federal employment. Most managers and employees understand the basics. Sick leave is not nearly as complex as leave under the Family and Medical Leave Act. But FMLA leave is in good part administered by the specialists in your HR office, with your cooperation. When it comes to sick leave (as with annual leave), you are the primary administrator, with HR available for advice.

Most employees are very familiar with sick leave, but there are tricky issues and a considerable potential for abuse. Sick leave abuse is a recurrent problem in the federal civil service, as it is in the private sector. It damages employee morale and hurts productivity. If you tolerate it, you signal to your employees that rule-breaking is OK or that you're afraid to enforce the rules. Workers who don't abuse sick leave are stuck doing the work of those who do, creating resentment and inviting workplace disputes.

As a manager, you have a legal obligation to enforce sick leave rules. If you fail to do so, this may be reflected in your own performance evaluation. But rule enforcement has its pitfalls. To avoid them, consult with your HR office as soon as you suspect a problem.

Sick leave accrual. All full-time federal employees accrue sick leave at the rate of 13 days per year with no limit on the total accumulation. Part-time employees earn one hour of sick leave for every 20 hours worked in a pay status. Sick leave may be used as soon as it is credited.

Sick leave annuity credit

The service of employees is increased by their unused sick leave if the employee retires on an immediate annuity or dies in service leaving a widow or widower entitled to a survivor's annuity. Sick leave credit cannot be used to meet the minimum length of service required for retirement. Employees covered under both the Civil Service Retirement System and the Federal Employees Retirement System are entitled to 100 percent of their sick leave balance being credited toward their annuity computation.

Sick leave abuse

Regardless of retirement credit, many employees -- especially those who do not plan to work for the government long enough to earn a retirement annuity -- may feel they have "earned" their sick leave and should take it all whether or not they have a legitimate use for it. Regardless of their justification, use of sick leave for other than authorized purposes is a violation of the law.

To help you balance the need to enforce the rules with the rights of your employees, keep the following rules of thumb in mind. Also, make sure your employees understand these guidelines and follow them:

  • • When employees want to use available sick leave for an authorized purpose, they should. They must make every effort to follow proper request procedures, but they should not hesitate to use the sick leave they have accrued.
  • • When employees don't have an authorized reason to use sick leave, they must not. Regardless of how much sick leave they may have accrued, they have no right to use it for unauthorized purposes. If they need leave for a purpose not authorized by sick leave regulations, they should use annual leave. If they have no annual leave available, they may request leave without pay. Both annual leave and LWOP requests are subject to your approval, based on your staffing needs.

Warning: Sick leave is an entitlement of federal service. Your attempts to combat abuse should never be directed against employees who use their sick leave for legitimate purposes. Nor should employees be recognized or rewarded for not using sick leave. You do not want to suggest that those who have the good luck not to become ill are somehow better employees than those who do become ill, or who need to take care of sick children. And you certainly do not want sick (and possibly infectious) employees coming to work because they believe it will make you think more highly of them.

Authorized use of sick leave

Employees are entitled to use sick leave when they:

  • • Are incapacitated for duty by physical or mental illness, injury, pregnancy, or childbirth.
  • • Care for a family member who is incapacitated as a result of physical or mental illness, injury, pregnancy, or childbirth.
  • • Make arrangements for or attend the funeral of a family member.
  • • Receive or attend to a family member receiving optical, dental, or medical treatment, or examination.
  • • May have been exposed to a contagious disease.
  • • Need to be absent for purposes related to adopting a child.

An employee is considered incapacitated and should take sick leave if they are unable to perform any of the work they normally do, not just limited in a range of duties. Employees who can perform some of their duties should come to work.

Travel time. Sick leave may be used to cover necessary travel time to medical examinations or treatments. Generally, this travel will be short distances -- for example, to and from a local doctor's office or hospital. Sick leave may also be used when employees need to travel longer distances, including out of state, to obtain medical care. Sick leave may also be used for travel time when employees are accompanying a family member who is receiving medical examination or treatment.

Special purposes. There are certain circumstances where you might think an employee has to use sick leave for an absence, but they are actually entitled to time off without a charge to leave or a loss of pay. Employees may use up to seven days of paid leave in any calendar year, in addition to regular annual and sick leave, to serve as a bone marrow donor. Up to 30 days of paid leave may be used each year to serve as an organ donor.

Law enforcement officers and firefighters may attend the funeral of a fellow officer or firefighter without a charge to leave or loss of pay.

Family care. Up to 104 hours (13 workdays) of available sick leave may be used per year to provide care for a family member incapacitated by illness or injury, or for purposes connected with the death of a family member.

A total of 480 hours (12 workweeks) per year of available sick leave may be used to care for a family member with a serious health condition. If some of the 104 hours available for bereavement or other care of family members have been used, the amount must be deducted from the 480 hours. These entitlements are distinct from the leave without pay available under the FMLA.

Available sick leave may be used without limit in connection with adopting a child. Employees may use sick leave for meetings with adoption agencies, social workers, attorneys, judges, or others directly involved in an adoption proceeding.

For the purposes of sick leave administration, "family members" include:

  1. 1. Spouse and spouse's parents.
  2. 2. Sons and daughters, and their spouses.
  3. 3. Parents and their spouses.
  4. 4. Brothers and sisters, and their spouses.
  5. 5. Step and foster parents and children.
  6. 6. Grandparents and grandchildren, and their spouses.
  7. 7. Same sex and opposite sex domestic partners and domestic partner's parents, including domestic partners of any individual named in 2-6 above.
  8. 8. Any individual related by blood or affinity whose close association with the employee is the equivalent of a family relationship.

Domestic partners. For sick leave purposes, a domestic partner is an adult living in a committed relationship with another adult. This includes domestic partners of the same sex and of the opposite sex, as well as those living in relationships acknowledged by states as marriages or equivalent to marriage.

The children of domestic partners are considered the equivalent of the employee's own children.

In loco parentis relationships are included under "parents" and "sons and daughters."

Sick leave and FMLA leave. Sick leave is sometimes confused with FMLA leave, not least because sick leave may sometimes be substituted for LWOP under the FMLA.

However, they are not the same. They differ in their time-period entitlements, authorized purposes, and definitions of "family member." Sick leave may be substituted for unpaid FMLA leave, but only if the regulatory conditions of both sick leave and FMLA leave are met. Employees who need FMLA leave for purposes authorized for sick leave use, and have the sick leave available, may use it instead of LWOP. Otherwise, they must use LWOP or available annual leave.

Request, approval, and documentation

You and your agency may establish procedures for requesting sick leave and require your employees to follow them. Check with HR to find out your agency's request procedure policy, and to determine how much leeway you have in your own unit.

Although your employees are entitled to use sick leave for legitimate purposes, their requests are still subject to your approval. You may require employees to request advance approval of sick leave for routine medical, dental, or optical examination or treatment. Otherwise, if normal request procedures are followed, you usually should approve the request.

Employees must notify you if they must stay out on sick leave longer than originally expected.

Employees may request that sick leave be substituted for annual leave for illnesses that occur during a period of approved annual leave, and these requests should be routinely approved unless there is some reason to suspect abuse. Annual leave cannot be substituted retroactively for sick leave applied for and granted.

You may take disciplinary action if request procedures are not followed. Check with HR. But remember, an employee who is actually ill or injured has a statutory right to use sick leave, even if correct request procedures are not followed.

Advanced sick leave. At the request of the employee and in appropriate circumstances, an agency may advance a maximum of 240 hours of sick leave to a full-time employee when:

  • • An employee is incapacitated for the performance of their duties by physical or mental illness, injury, pregnancy, or childbirth.
  • • The employee or a family member has a serious health condition.
  • • The employee would jeopardize the health of others by their presence on the job because of exposure to a communicable disease.
  • • The purposes relate to the adoption of a child.
  • • The employee needs to care for a covered servicemember with a serious injury or illness.

Up to 104 hours may be advanced to a full-time employee:

  • • When the employee receives medical, dental, or optical examination or treatment.
  • • To provide care for a family member who is incapacitated by a medical or mental condition or to attend to a family member receiving medical, dental, or optical examination or treatment.
  • • To provide care for a family member who would jeopardize the health of others by their presence in the community because of exposure to a communicable disease.
  • • To make arrangements necessitated by the death of a family member or to attend the funeral of a family member.

240 hours is the maximum amount of advanced sick leave that may be credited to a full-time employee at any one time. Sick leave should not be advanced if the employee will not be returning to duty.

Repayment of advanced sick leave: An employee is required to repay advanced sick leave, except in very limited circumstances like disability retirement or death. Therefore, an employee requesting advanced sick leave should think carefully about whether they want to incur this debt and how the debt will be repaid. Generally, advanced sick leave is repaid by subsequently earned sick leave, so if an employee were to use 13 days of advanced sick leave, it would take a full year without using another hour of sick leave to repay the sick leave debt. Because advanced sick leave must be repaid, an agency should not advance sick leave when it is known (or reasonably expected) that the employee will not return to duty.

Donated annual leave under the Voluntary Leave Transfer Program or Voluntary Leave Bank Program may be used to liquidate an indebtedness incurred by the leave recipient for advanced sick leave used because of a medical emergency, such as an elderly family member's medical emergency.

Documentation. By governmentwide regulation, employees must provide documentation certifying illness within 15 calendar days after the date you request it. Some exceptions are permitted if certification cannot be obtained within the time limit despite the employee's best efforts, but the limit may not be extended beyond 30 calendar days after your request. Consult HR on enforcement of this employee obligation.

You have a right to require employees to produce administratively acceptable evidence of illness, injury, treatment, or examination (usually a doctor's certificate) in order to support a request for sick leave. In most cases of short sick leave absences of three days or less, the employee's word suffices.

Employees may be placed under a requirement to produce written administratively acceptable evidence in support of all sick leave requests. Documents placing employees under such requirements are usually referred to as "sick leave restriction" or "requirement" letters. Labor agreements often specify the circumstances under which an agency can require an employee to produce documentation in support of a sick leave request.

Common challenges

You may find sick leave administration challenging if you have employees who insist on pushing the envelope on taking sick leave. Consult HR before you take any action so that you know what your options are. Here are the most frequent challenges, together with suggested responses:

General problem with sick leave abuse or disregard of request procedures. You may have just come on board as a manager and discover a preexisting problem, or you realize you've been lax about enforcing leave request rules for too long and need to tighten up.

  • • Response: Don't take any action until you consult HR, and make sure any action you take complies with agency policy and regulations and the provisions of any applicable labor agreement. It's unreasonable to expect employees to suddenly begin observing procedures they've been allowed to ignore for some time without consequences, and may not have even realized existed. Give fair warning that things are changing, provide your employees with precise information about proper request procedures and your expectations, and then enforce the procedures fairly.

Fraudulent, suspicious, or pattern use of sick leave. You suspect that an employee may not be using sick leave for an authorized purpose. The employee suddenly requests sick leave in an apparent response to a work event, such as a denial of annual leave, an unpopular work assignment, or an unwanted overtime assignment. Also suspicious is the use of sick leave in a discernible pattern; for example, before or after weekends or holidays.

  • • Response: Counsel the employee, explaining that sick leave is only available for legitimate purposes and that any abuse in the future will result in disciplinary action. If the employee indicates personal problems or appears to be suffering from personal problems (including substance abuse) that may be causing sick leave abuse, refer the employee to the Employee Assistance Program. You cannot order an employee to contact the EAP, and consultations are confidential. Document your counseling. Follow up with progressive discipline if the sick leave abuse continues.

Failure to properly request sick leave. An employee disregards established sick leave request procedures. She neither contacts you nor has someone else call in. Or the employee remains absent longer than expected but doesn't notify you of how things are going or when a return to work is likely.

  • • Response: Advise the employee that failure to follow the required sick leave request procedures creates operational problems and, if repeated, can result in disciplinary action. Document the discussion. Take disciplinary action if the problem continues.

Failure to provide acceptable documentation in support of sick leave requests. An employee disregards your instructions to provide a doctor's certificate or other administratively acceptable evidence in support of sick leave. Or the documentation appears fraudulent or too vague to permit you to determine whether the employee was incapacitated by illness or injury.

  • • Response: Advise the employee that sick leave cannot be approved without the production of administratively acceptable evidence; for example, a doctor's certificate or note. Explain that refusal to provide such documentation within established deadlines (within 15 calendar days, extendable to 30 calendar days if the agency finds extenuating circumstances) will result in the absence being charged to absence without leave and may be followed by disciplinary action. Document the discussion.

Refusal to report for work when not incapacitated for light duty. An employee is not completely incapacitated for all work, but refuses to follow your instructions to report for assignment of duties that could be performed despite the injury.

  • • Response: Advise the employee that sick leave is not available if he is not "incapacitated" -- unable to do any of his normal work -- but merely unable to perform certain duties. This particularly refers to employees who are recovering from injuries that only partially impair their ability to work.

Sick leave restriction

Agencies may request administratively acceptable evidence of injury or illness for any period of sick leave, no matter how short. This is referred to as sick leave restriction.

If an employee continues an apparent pattern of abuse over a period of time (six to eight weeks is usually sufficient) after receiving a written warning, it's time to contact HR about issuing the employee a sick leave restriction letter. This requires medical certification after every absence on sick leave, no matter how short, for a specific period of time, usually three to six months.

The restriction letter is intended to make employees think twice about using sick leave because they must prove incapacity for duty by going to a health care provider each time. Employees who do not follow the requirements of their leave restriction letters are subject to having their absences carried as absence without leave, and to disciplinary action for that AWOL and for failure to follow the requirements of their leave restriction letter.

You may consult the sample sick leave restriction letter in Manager Advantage Resources, but remember that any leave restriction letters must conform to guidelines established in agency regulations or labor agreements.

Monitoring restriction. After a restriction letter is issued, you should closely scrutinize the employee's attendance and sick leave usage and ensure the employee follows the letter's requirements. This means not approving sick leave unless the employee has complied with the medical certification and other requirements of the letter. Employees may not be disciplined for taking approved leave, unless there is direct evidence of abuse.

Deadline for providing documentation. By governmentwide OPM regulation, employees must provide documentation within 15 calendar days after the date the agency requests it. You may not impose a shorter limit. Some extension is permitted -- at the agency's discretion and for good reason -- but the limit may not be extended beyond 30 calendar days.

Enforce sick leave restriction. If the employee fails to comply with any requirements of the restriction letter, seek assistance from HR to take appropriate disciplinary action against the employee. It doesn't do you or the employee any good to issue a restriction letter and then not follow through if the employee fails to comply.

Treat employees individually. In all cases of abuse of sick leave or failure to follow request procedures, treat individual employees according to their record. If you have one sick leave abuser, for example, you may not and should not require all employees to produce doctor's notes for every instance of sick leave. Employees with good attendance and conduct records should be trusted not to abuse sick leave.

Warning: Do not be surprised if employees unhappy with your response to their sick leave abuse file grievances or EEO complaints. Don't let fear of complaints prevent you from enforcing sick leave rules. Just make sure you consult HR, steer clear of any rule or labor agreement violations, and document all your actions.

Mistakes to avoid

Most supervisors are familiar with sick leave administration, but it is easy for problems to accumulate if you make these common mistakes.

Do not:

  • • Ignore sick leave abuse. Rarely does the situation correct itself. Managers often name absenteeism as their major problem. If you fail to take steps to correct sick leave abuse, your unit will not be as productive as it could be.
  • • Accept insufficient or suspicious documentation. If an employee's absence was lengthy -- more than three days -- or occurred under suspicious circumstances, you can request certification from a health care provider that presents sufficient information to judge whether the employee was actually incapacitated for duty.
  • • Confuse sick leave to care for family members with FMLA leave. It's easy to confuse the two, especially since sick leave is sometimes used in taking FMLA leave. But administration, definitions, and conditions are very different. Get the details from HR.
  • • Fail to apply the labor agreement. The provisions of a labor agreement may vary considerably from general agency policy and may override it.
  • • Allow employees to disregard request procedures. Sooner or later, this will cause problems. If you are too lax, you will find it difficult to enforce procedures when a case of serious abuse comes along.
  • • Fail to refer an employee to the EAP when appropriate. Supervisors are expected to be reasonably alert to signs of possible substance abuse and other personal problems and to refer employees to the Employee Assistance Program when personal problems may be contributing to sick leave abuse or other difficulties at work. Note that you cannot order an employee to consult the EAP.

Ask FMG

Q. I have an employee who always calls in sick the day after a holiday. How should I handle it?

A. Confront pattern abusers. Employees who abuse sick leave often call in sick according to specific patterns, such as just before and after holidays and weekends. Discuss these patterns with HR. When you have documented the problem, point it out to the employee. Hopefully, this will stop abuse -- at least for a time. If not, seek further advice from HR.

Q. When can I require an employee to provide medical certification?

A. Usually whenever an employee is absent more than three days or there are suspicious circumstances. Check your agency's policies and the labor agreement to be certain and ask HR what you can do. You may also require medical documentation for every instance of sick leave, regardless of the length, when an employee is under sick leave restriction.

Q. How long does the employee have to provide medical certification once I request it?

A. By governmentwide regulation, employees must provide documentation within 15 calendar days after the date the agency requests it. Some exceptions are permitted if certification cannot be obtained within the time limit despite the employee's best efforts, but the limit may not be extended beyond 30 calendar days after the agency's request.

Q. What if medical certification is vague or merely says the employee was "unable to work"?

A. Employees must provide "administratively acceptable" evidence. You can insist on documentation sufficiently detailed to determine whether and to what extent the employee was incapacitated. If documentation appears suspect or is too vague, you may request further information. However, do this only after coordinating with HR and familiarizing yourself with the provisions of agency policy and any labor agreement.

Q. Can I require an employee to work if the employee is able to perform some duties?

A. You certainly shouldn't try to drag a sick employee into work. However, you can deny sick leave if the employee is capable of performing available -- presumably lighter -- duties. This particularly refers to employees who are recovering from injuries that only partially impair their ability to work.

Q. What is a "sick leave restriction" or "requirement" letter?

A. A sick leave restriction letter is issued by a manager and requires an employee to support all sick leave requests, no matter how short, with medical documentation. You can issue a sick leave restriction letter when an employee is suspected of sick leave abuse.

Q. Can I deny sick leave if an employee fails to follow the established request procedure?

A. Not if an employee is actually ill or injured or is using sick leave for another statutory purpose. You can discipline an employee for failure to follow procedures in some cases, but check with your HR experts first. An employee who is absent for an authorized sick leave purpose is not absent without leave.
