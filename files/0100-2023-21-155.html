G. AGE-BASED HARASSMENT

The Commission's treatment of harassment based on age is largely the same as its treatment of harassment under Title VII. The general rule is that the agency is responsible for harassment by supervisory employees whether or not the agency, as an entity, knew or should have known of the harassment. The agency is responsible for conduct by coworkers that amounts to harassment if someone in the supervisory chain-of-command knew or should have known of the conduct and took no immediate action to stop it.

The Commission takes a totality of the circumstances approach in assessing whether individual acts rise to the level of harassment. In Von Zimmer v. Department of Army, 01821300 (1983), the Commission held that it was error for the agency to review each allegation of harassment as an individual episode, wholly unrelated to the other allegations:

Considered in isolation, each of the referenced events may seem insignificant. However, appellant has alleged that these acts constituted a pattern of harassment designed to adversely affect appellant's employment because of his age and/or national origin. Appellant brought these actions to the attention of management and management either failed to act or prevented appropriate action. In this regard, an employer may not stand by and allow an employee to be subjected to a course of harassment by co-workers, thus the agency must accept responsibility for their supervisor's derelictions, if such existed. See Fire Fighters Institute for Racial Equality v. City of St. Louis, 549 F.2d 506 (8th Cir.), cert. denied 434 U.S. 819 (1977). The employer may also be responsible for employee's harassment of other employees merely by condoning such conduct. Friend v. Leidinger, 588 F.2d 61 (4th Cir. 1978).

The Commission determined that a series of incidents, while perhaps trivial as individual incidents, amounted to a pattern of harassment that the agency had allowed to exist.

In Robertshaw v. Secretary of Air Force, 01992287 (1999), the Commission also found that one comment was sufficiently severe or pervasive to state a hostile environment claim:

In the present case, complainant contends that she was subjected to harassment when the Lieutenant Commander made derogatory remarks about her age and when he requested that she transfer to another position. With regard to the comment where the Lieutenant Commander supposedly told complainant that if she was 30 or 35 he would not even consider the job transfer, but since she is 54 and her career is basically over, it does not matter where she work as long as she gets a pay check, we find that this comment by itself is sufficiently severe to state a claim. Viewing this incident and the other identified incidents in the light most favorable to complainant, we find that complainant has stated a cognizable claim under the EEOC Regulations. See Cervantes v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05930303 (November 12, 1993).

But, in Jan M. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120171142 (2018), a single incident of the complainant being called an "oldtimer" was not sufficiently severe or pervasive to state a hostile environment claim.

The passage of time between allegedly harassing incidents may weaken the complainant's argument as to the severity or pervasiveness of overall hostile environment claim. In Telfer v. Secretary of Transportation, 01A12265 (2001), the Commission found that three incidents of comments about older workers over a three-year period were insufficient to state a hostile environment claim.

As with other forms of harassment, the complainant does not need to show that the conduct directed at him or her was explicitly age-based. All the complainant needs to show is that the conduct, whatever its nature, was based on age. For example, in Posey v. Secretary of Treasury, 0720050077 (2007), the complainant prevailed in showing a hostile environment based on age, sex and color. The complainant, an older white female, was able to demonstrate that she was subjected to treatment more harsh than similarly situated younger black females. As the Commission, in Posey, explained:

We find that substantial evidence in the record supports the AJ's finding that complainant satisfied all of these criteria. The testimony of complainant and her co-workers reveals that white older employees were singled out for worse treatment. Specifically, white older employees were being given additional duties that younger black females were not required to do and as a group were rated lower on their performance appraisals than black, young employees. Substantial evidence of the record shows that complainant was under constant scrutiny and criticism regarding her work, and was rated substantially lower than other employees. We concur with the AJ that the agency's nondiscriminatory reasons for its actions, namely, complainant's poor performance, were not supported by the record. The AJ noted that complainant provided documentation that prior to and after being supervised by KM and TM, her supervisors found no problem with her performance. Moreover, we find that KM and TM failed to articulate the nature of complainant's performance problems. As such, we affirm the AJ's finding that complainant was subjected to unlawful harassment by TM and KM.

Similarly, in Capobianco v. Secretary of Treasury, 0720080055 (2008), the Commission rejected the agency's argument that age-based comments made by agency attorneys during conference calls were not directed at the complainant. The Commission also applied the same liability rules discussed above to the case of age harassment finding that the agency was liable for the harassment because the supervisor knew of the comments and took no corrective action only telling the employees to stop swearing during conference calls but not to cease the age-based comments.

Although the Commission, through its proposed regulations and case law, recognized a cause of action for creation of a hostile environment on the basis of age, it is only fairly recently that any court has recognized such a cause of action. In Crawford v. Medina Gen. Hosp., 96 F.3d 830, 834-36 (6th Cir. 1996), the court held that a claim of hostile work environment may be brought under the ADEA:

While, as far as we can discern, no circuit has as yet applied the hostile-environment doctrine in an ADEA action,...we find it a relatively uncontroversial proposition that such a theory is viable under the ADEA. For at least these reasons, in light of the ADEA's employment of the "terms, conditions, or privileges of employment" language, we have no doubt that a hostile work environment claim may be stated. The broad application of the hostile-environment doctrine in the Title VII context; the general similarity of purpose shared by Title VII and the ADEA; and the fact that the Title VII rationale for the doctrine is of equal force in the ADEA context, all counsel this result. We thus hold that a plaintiff may advance a hostile-environment claim under the ADEA.

These are the criteria for a prima facie claim:

1. The employee is 40 years old or older;

2. The employee was subjected to harassment, either through words or actions, based on age;

3. The harassment had the effect of unreasonably interfering with the employee's work performance and creating an objectively intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment; and

4. The existence of some basis for liability on the part of the employer.

Cf. Risinger, 883 F.2d at 484; EEOC Compliance Manual § 615.7.

In this case, the plaintiff's claim failed due to her lack of proof under both the second and third prongs of the prima facie case. First, the plaintiff had virtually no evidence that the "harassment" of which she complained was in any way based on her age. Instead, her theory appears wholly based on a false syllogism: A) My coworkers hate me; B) I am old; C) My coworkers hate me because I'm old. The plaintiff points to only two comments that are objectively indicative of age-based animus: Kermendy's (supervisor) comments that she does not "think women over 55 should be working," and that "[o]ld people should be seen and not heard." Other than those two remarks, however, there is virtually no evidence, apart from Crawford's self-serving conclusions, that the hostility in the Medina workplace was in any way related to age.

The court did not entirely discount the plaintiff's complaints insofar as they focused on coworkers having parties without inviting her, or coworkers being surly or impolite. Even if coworkers failed to invite her to parties because she was over 55, it seems obvious that the ADEA was not intended to remedy minor social slights and the resulting hurt feelings. Pizza parties are simply not a term, condition, or privilege of employment of which Congress has taken cognizance.

Even apart from the fact that only two comments were actually discernibly age-based, there is simply no question that the hostility at Medina, while not insubstantial, was not particularly severe or degrading. Crawford's complaints are of "mere offensive utterance[s]," as opposed to physically threatening or humiliating conduct. Saying "[o]ld people should be seen and not heard" is certainly rude, but it is not enough to create a hostile working environment within the meaning of Harris. Similarly, while a supervisor's opinion that women should retire at age 55 may be unenlightened and logically indefensible, and might be circumstantial evidence of age discrimination if the supervisor were later to have fired Crawford, it hardly rises even to the level of an "offensive utterance," as it is simply one person's opinion, susceptible to retort and dispute.

Finally, Crawford did not produce any evidence tending to show that the harassment interfered with her work performance and/or created an objectively intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment, within the meaning of Harris. We note there is absolutely no suggestion that the environment impeded Crawford's work performance; indeed, she herself claims to like her job, despite Kermendy's asserted abusiveness.
