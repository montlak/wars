B. IDENTIFICATION OF PARTIES

In the fall of 2013, the Commission, without notice and with no formal announcement, adopted a policy of making all complainants in federal sector cases anonymous. Prior to that, the decisions of the Commission reflected the names of the parties, i.e., the name of the complainant and the name and position of the agency head. The Commission has always had an arcane practice of not naming other individuals, such as supervisors and coworkers, involved the complaint. That practice can make Commission decisions somewhat difficult to follow where there are multiple persons involved in complex factual situations. Certainly, the Commission's policy of not naming even those individuals found to have engaged in discrimination is baffling, at best.

The Commission never fully explained its change in policy in making complainants anonymous, though the change has been the subject of much criticism. In a press release, dated October 5, 2015, the Commission announced the following change in policy:

Beginning on Oct. 1, 2015, all federal sector appellate decisions issued for publication will use a randomly generated name as a substitute for the name of the complainant, rather than the generic term "Complainant." This randomly generated name will consist of a first name and last initial, and will be assigned using a computer program that selects names from a list of pseudonyms bearing no relation to the complainant's actual name. A complainant who wishes to be identified by his or her name in a case caption rather than by a randomly generated name may submit a written request to EEOC's Office of Federal Operations.

Starting in October 2013, the Commission had changed its practice of publishing federal sector appellate decisions with the name of the complainant. All decisions published after Oct. 1, 2013, used the term "Complainant" in the caption of the case, rather than the complainant's name.

"EEOC's federal sector appellate decisions are a vital resource for our stakeholders in both the federal and private sectors to better understand the Commission's application of the laws we enforce," said Chair Jenny R. Yang. "The use of randomly generated names strikes a good balance between privacy interests and the ability of our stakeholders to readily reference the important principles in these decisions."

In rare cases, the Commission has always had a practice of protecting the name of the complainant. For example, in Doe, et al. v. Attorney General, 0120070816 (2012), the Commission granted the agency's request to sanitize case records in a class action complaint involving employees of the Witness Security Program. In granting the agency's request in Doe, the Commission explained:

As an initial matter, we find that the parties' request for anonymity is supported by persuasive evidence. The record reflects that Complainant works in the Agency's Witness Security Program (WSP). The Agency has demonstrated that the Attorney General's designee, the Director of the WSP, submitted an affidavit invoking the Agency's determination not to disclose the names of Witness Security Inspectors or duty locations. The affidavit states that the purpose of the WSP is to provide for safe and covert housing of government witnesses whose lives have been placed in danger by virtue of being a witness against persons alleged to have participated in domestic or foreign criminal activity. The Director states that the release of names of Inspectors and their duty stations has been barred because disclosure of the information jeopardizes the safety, work assignments, and careers of employees who deal directly with protected witnesses and their families. Moreover, he states that disclosing the names and locations of employees like Complainant poses a serious danger of extortionate kidnapping, and other crimes to WSP employees, their families, WSP witnesses, and their families, as well as the personnel who currently maintain this information. As a result, the Agency requests that the documents in the instant file be sanitized and redacted, and that Complainant's name and duty station be referred by pseudonyms. Complainant has filed no opposition to this request.

The Commission has, in limited circumstances, allowed complainants to remain anonymous in its federal sector proceedings. See e.g. Doe v. Social Security Administration (SSA), EEOC Appeal No. 01A14791 (February 21, 2003) (pseudonym used to protect Complainant's identity from public disclosure for reason unrelated to the substance of the appeal); and Ryan v. Department of Justice, EEOC Appeal No. 0120062637 (February 12, 2009) (alias used at Agency's request due to interference with certain operations). In light of prior rulings in this regard and upon review of the supporting evidence, we grant the Agency's request to use a fictitious name in this case based on our finding that there is a reasonable likelihood of severe physical harm towards Complainant or innocent third parties if Complainant's name and location were publicized in these proceedings.
