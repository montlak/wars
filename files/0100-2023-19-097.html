E. MIXED MOTIVES IN HOSTILE ENVIRONMENT CASES

The use of the mixed motive analysis can be appropriate in hostile environment cases. In Almon v. Postmaster General, 0720090023 (2011), the Commission used a mixed motive analysis to reverse the termination of a complainant who engaged in a work altercation with two coworkers after being subjected to racial harassment. The complainant in that case, a black male, had been referred to as a "monkey" by two coworkers. The supervisor reported the comment to the postmaster. Although the postmaster held a meeting with the complainant and his coworkers, the supervisor testified that the comments continued and postmaster was aware that the comments continued but did nothing to stop them. Ultimately, complainant had a verbal altercation with the coworkers, which was overheard by customers. Based on a previous disciplinary record, the complainant was terminated and the two coworkers were suspended. The agency contended that the animosity between the complainant and coworkers was not racial but the result of his refusal to join the union. In applying the mixed motive analysis in Almon, the Commission explained:

In this case, the Supervisor testified that in 2002, she witnessed CW1 and CW2 call Complainant a "monkey." The Supervisor further testified that she reported the co-workers' comments to the Postmaster, who held a meeting with Complainant and the co-workers. The Supervisor further stated that after the meeting with the Postmaster, she heard the co-workers make derogatory remarks about Complainant "more than once," but she did not do anything about the co-workers' remarks because she was not Complainant's supervisor at the time. The Supervisor maintained that the Postmaster was aware that the co-workers continued to make such comments about Complainant even after his meeting with them because the Postmaster had to intervene in a "couple" of altercations between Complainant and the two co-workers. Complainant testified that CW1 called him a "jungle monkey" and "snake."

The Postmaster denied that he was aware that the co-workers called Complainant a monkey but contended that he would have probably "thrown the book" at the co-workers if he had known they used the term because it is "almost as bad, if not as bad, as calling a black man a nigger."

The AJ found the Supervisor's claim that she reported the co-workers' use of racially disparaging remarks against Complainant to be credible and the Postmaster's denial to be questionable. We find no basis to disturb the AJ's credibility determinations with respect to the Postmaster's knowledge that Complainant was called a "monkey" by his two co-workers.

The AJ reviewed this case using a hostile work environment and disparate treatment analysis. However, we determine that a mixed motive analysis is more appropriate. Cases in which the employer acts on the basis of both lawful and unlawful reasons are known as "mixed motive cases." Prior to the Cilia Rights Act (CRA) of 1991, 42 U.S.C. § 2000e-2(m), the Supreme Court ruled in Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins, 490 U.S. 228 (1989), that when an employee showed that an impermissible factor played a role in an adverse employment decision, the burden shifted to the employer to show that it would have made the same decision absent the unlawful factor; if the employer met this burden, it could avoid a finding of liability on the discrimination charge. Id. at 237. The CRA effectively overruled that part of the Price Waterhouse decision that allowed an employer to avoid liability by showing that it would have made the same decision even absent the unlawful discrimination. Under the CRA, where liability is found, and the employer showed that it would have made the same decision even absent the discriminatory considerations, the CRA provides that an employee may be entitled to declaratory relief, injunctive relief, and attorney's fees and costs, but she or he would not be entitled to personal relief such as compensatory damages, reinstatement, hiring, promotion, or back pay.

In the instant case, we find that the Agency provided a non-discriminatory reason for terminating Complainant, i.e., Complainant's June 27, 2005 altercation with CW1 and CW2 and history of previous discipline. While we are persuaded that Complainant's June 2005 altercation and disciplinary history were factors in his termination, we are further persuaded that race was a motivating factor in Complainant's termination.

The AJ and Agency maintain that the friction between Complainant and CW1 existed because of Complainant's lack of affiliation with the union of which CW1 was a steward. The AJ further stated that the evidence established that CW1 and CW2's conduct toward Complainant resulted from Complainant's non-union status and not from any protected EEO basis. However, we determine that CW1 and CW2 clearly exhibited racial hostility toward Complainant, as reflected in their use of the incendiary terms "monkey" and "jungle monkey" against Complainant. We note that these terms used by the co-workers about Complainant are derived from a long, history of portraying African-Americans as subhuman or inferior. See EEOC Compliance Manual, Section 15, "Race and Color Discrimination," No. 915.003, 15-38 (April 19, 2006) (A single incident of a racial comparison to an animal may create a hostile work environment); Brooks v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05950484 (June 25, 1996); Williams v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 0120092247 (July 22, 2009) (The Commission found that complainant's allegation that he was referred to as "Monkey Boy" stated a claim of race discrimination); Spriggs v. Diamond Auto Glass, 242 F.3d 179, 185 (4th Cir. 2001) ("To suggest that a human being's physical appearance is essentially a caricature of a jungle beast goes far beyond the merely unflattering; it is degrading and humiliating in the extreme").

The Agency contends that the Postmaster investigated the comments and resolved the matter. However, the Supervisor testified that CW1 and CW2 continued to use derogatory comments about Complainant after the Postmaster met with Complainant, CW1, and CW2. Moreover, the Agency has not shown that it took any appropriate disciplinary action against CW1 and CW2 after learning of the two co-workers' use of racial slurs against Complainant. In fact, the Supervisor stated that she continued to hear the co-workers use slurs against Complainant after the meeting with the Postmaster, but she did not address the matter any further. Most importantly, the continuation of the slurs against Complainant after management was aware that Complainant was subjected to them proves that the Agency failed to fulfill its duty to adequately address and resolve the racially charged atmosphere against Complainant.

As such, we are persuaded that the June 27, 2005 altercation that led to Complainant's termination was the foreseeable result of the Agency's failure to adequately address and stop the racially charged comments directed at Complainant. The June 27, 2005 altercation is the very type of incident that the Agency could have avoided if it had properly responded to the comments. Thus, Complainant's removal is inextricably intertwined with the racial remarks he endured from CWI and CW2. While the Supervisor who initiated and recommended Complainant's removal is African-American, she acknowledged that she was aware that Complainant had endured racial insults from CWI and CW2 at the time she decided to terminate Complainant. Moreover, the Supervisor testified that she did nothing when the insults continued after a meeting with the Postmaster. We determine that the racially charged actions of CW1 and CW2 were essentially ratified by management's indifference to Complainant's plight as the victim of racial slurs and decision to terminate Complainant. See Deirdra Brown v. Department of Justice, Appeal No. 0120045121 (December 20, 2006) (Commission held that responsible management official did not need to have racial animus against complainant to support finding of discrimination when official relied heavily upon the accounts of employees who harbored racial animus against complainant to terminate complainant). Consequently, we find that race was a factor in the Agency's decision to terminate Complainant. We do not find, however, that reprisal was a motivating factor in the Agency's decision to terminate Complainant.

As previously stated, an agency may avoid liability for personal damages by showing that it would have taken the same action absent the unlawful discrimination: Upon careful review of the record, we find that the Agency has not met its burden of proving that it would have terminated Complainant absent its failure to adequately address the racially charged atmosphere that Complainant endured. In so finding, we note that the Postmaster testified that if he had known that CW1 and CW2 referred to Complainant as a "monkey," it would have been an "extenuating circumstance" in his decision to concur with the recommendation to remove Complainant. Therefore, we find that Complainant is entitled to individual relief such as compensatory damages and reinstatement.
