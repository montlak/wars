1. Criminal Convictions

Criminal convictions for felonies or misdemeanors may provide a legitimate basis for rejecting a candidate for a position. The agency may, however, be required to demonstrate that its determination is particular to the individual involved.

In Slade v. USPS, 01841682 (1987), the complainant, a black male, was rejected for positions with the agency because of past convictions. The agency had a policy of removing applicants with recent convictions "involving acts of violence or dishonesty" from the employment register. The Commission had little trouble determining that an across-the-board application of such a policy had a disproportionately high impact on blacks. Relying in part upon Carter v. Gallagher, 452 F.2d 315, 326 (8th Cir. 1971), the EEOC held "that a practice of denying applicants employment because of a prior felony or misdemeanor conviction is discriminatory against Blacks when the rejection is not based on a finding that the applicant's behavior giving rise to the conviction furnished an inference that the applicant could not fulfill the duties of the position." In Slade, the Commission enumerated three criteria the agency must consider:

The employer must examine all relevant factors to determine whether the conviction affects the individual's ability to perform the job. The relevant factors include:

1. the nature, number and circumstances of the offense for which the individual was convicted;

2. the length of time intervening between the conviction of the offense(s) and the employment decision;

3. the individual's employment history.

It is necessary that the employer show that the conviction is job-related and that some factor or factors were sufficiently negative to support the rejection.

In structuring relief in Slade, the Commission did give the agency an opportunity to consider these factors and make an independent determination as to whether appellant's conviction record rendered him unfit for duty.

Similarly, the Commission in Alex W. v. Postmaster General, 0120141016 (2016), upheld a finding of no discrimination in a nonselection case where the agency cited to the complainant's "two felony convictions against property and the government" as particular to the position for which he applied "because he would have access to and handle the public's mail."

Due to the Commission's position that using criminal records as an absolute bar to employment has a disparate impact on blacks and Hispanics, it is not necessary in such a case to present actual statistics demonstrating an adverse impact. However, that policy does not extend to a situation where the agency withholds employment determinations while awaiting the resolution of pending criminal charges. The Commission explained the distinction in Harlston v. Postmaster General, 01913580 (1992), aff'd on reconsideration, Harlston v. Postmaster General, 05920478 (1992):

The AJ found discrimination by generally stating that the agency's erroneous interpretation of the regulations had an disparate impact on black males. The AJ took judicial notice, without citing any statistics that black males are disproportionally arrested in our society. Indeed, an employer's policy or practice of excluding individuals on the basis of arrest records, if not justified by a business necessity, could have a disparate impact upon blacks. Perkins v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05900245 (June 1, 1990) citing Reynolds v. Sheet Metal Workers Local 102, 498 F. Supp 952 (D.D.C. 1980) and Gregory v. Litton Systems, Inc., 316 F. Supp. 401 (C.D. Ca. 1970), aff'd in relevant part 472 F.2d 361 (9th Cir. 1972). The Commission has concluded that the use of arrest records as an absolute bar to employment has a disparate impact on blacks and Hispanics in light of statistics showing that they are convicted at a rate disproportionately greater than their representation in the population. Policy Guidance on the Consideration of Arrest Records in Employment Decision under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, EEOC Policy Statement No. N-915-061 (September 7, 1990) and Policy Statement on the Use of Statistics in Charges Involving the Exclusion of Individuals with Conviction Records From Employment, EEOC Policy Statement No. N-915-008 (July 29, 1987).

If the Commission's policy was applied in the instant matter appellant would not have to proffer statistics if he had been barred from employment with the agency because of his arrest record. However, we find that the AJ erroneously applied the Commission's arrest record policy in the present matter. First, appellant was not barred from employment with the agency. The agency's uncontradicted testimony was that a final determination of his suitability would not be made until the charges were resolved in some manner. However, the agency's failure to comply with its regulations did not bar appellant from employment. Even if the agency had reserved a position for appellant, he would not have been considered until the charges were resolved. Second, appellant's application was halted due to pending charges and not because of any arrest record.

Therefore, in order for appellant to prevail on a disparate impact analysis, appropriate statistics would have to be proffered to substantiate the claim that the agency's policy on pending charges had a disparate impact on members of appellant's protected class. In the present matter the record does not contain any such statistical information....
