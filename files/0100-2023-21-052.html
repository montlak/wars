19. Repeated Incidents

Incidents which in and of themselves may not seem terribly onerous can combine to create a hostile environment if they occur frequently enough. For example, in Hall v. Secretary of Navy, 05980370 (2001), the Commission found that an actionable claim of hostile environment harassment was set forth where the complainant alleged that she was repeatedly denied essential training, the agency repeatedly permitted her coworkers to improperly perform assignments with the purpose of causing adverse consequences to the complainant, and the agency repeatedly refused to clarify the complainant's job assignments. Similarly, "repeatedly and intentionally referring to an individual by the wrong gender or name may create a hostile work environment based on sex (sexual orientation)." Colleen M. v. Secretary of Agriculture, 0120130552 (2016); see also Cecile W. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120141980 (2016) (reversing dismissal for failure to state a claim where the complainant alleged "that over a span of one month she was repeatedly harassed, including being threatened with a disclosure to the Behavioral Licensing Board").

Where harassment is repeated, the Commission in Debbra R. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120161305 (2016), noted that a supervisor's failure to respond to instances of alleged harassment heightens the severity of the alleged act. Though there was only one incident cited to in the present complaint, the complainant in Debbra R. explained that the incident was one of a "few others" and the "11th time" that she been yelled at by a coworker and her supervisor had failed to take appropriate action. Finding the incident sufficiently severe to alter the conditions of the complainant's work environment, the Commission held:

Although Claim 1 describes one instance of verbal harassment, we find that combined with the alleged frequency of the harassment and management's failure to effectively respond to Complainant's allegation, Claim 1 describes conduct sufficiently severe to state a claim of harassment. We emphasize that the alleged verbal harassment was not "a mere offensive utterance," but lasted approximately thirty minutes. C1 singled Complainant out and threatened physical violence. Moreover, C1's "yelling and screaming" was so loud that S1, S2 and a witness heard him from outside the kitchen; and his conduct was such that all three believed police intervention was necessary. Also with respect to the severity of C1's conduct, we find it telling that S2 called Agency police before she left her office to intervene.

Based on the background information Complainant provided, we find Claim 1 does not represent an isolated incident. Further, S1 and S2 knew that Complainant had an ongoing conflict with Cl and that she had named C1 as her alleged harasser in a pending EEO complaint (Complaint 1), yet they failed to protect her from alleged further harassment. Likewise, the record for the instant complaint reveals that C1 left the kitchen to inform S2, and then S1 that he was angry with Complainant, yet despite their knowledge of the situation, neither S1 nor S2 took the opportunity to keep C1 away from Complainant while he was in an agitated state. There is no indication in the record that S1 intends to take action to prevent further incidents, even though, by his own account, Complainant appeared "very stressed" as a result of C1 's behavior. Despite S1's comment that rest of the kitchen staff went about their business like normal after the police diffused the situation, we find Complainant's account of her own fear and stress supported by the record. Hence we find the alleged conduct in Claim 1 is sufficiently severe or pervasive to alter the conditions of the complainant's employment.

See also Annie F. v. Postmaster General, 0120161201 (2016) (reversing agency's dismissal as "isolated incidents" where coworkers repeatedly hid equipment necessary for the complainant to do her job along with comments such as "fucking bitch" and "fucking cunt rag" and management's failure to address the coworkers' behavior was sufficiently severe to state a claim).

A few "stray remarks" generally will not rise to the level of creating a hostile environment even if they are derogatory. For example, in Mazair v. Secretary of Transportation, 0120091426 (2009), the Commission found that three alleged comments by a manager regarding the complainant's wearing of a kilt--"something must be wrong with you if you spend $200 on a dress," "you don't actually go anywhere while wearing that do you?" and "do you wear that when you're out with your wife?"--did not rise to the level of being sufficiently severe or pervasive to create a hostile environment. But in Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120112975 (2015), the Commission found that references to "Broke Back Lake" made to the complainant over a 10-year period of time were sufficiently severe or pervasive to create a hostile work environment.

In Pollock v. Secretary of Army, 01961497 (1998), three incidents of harassment were sufficient to create a hostile environment where the incidents occurred within 10 days and the complainant was berated loudly enough that coworkers overheard the abuse. Moreover, the Commission noted that the supervisor used profanity, for which she was later reprimanded.

Applying the objective prong of Harris in McKenzie v. DOT, Illinois, 92 F.3d 473 (7th Cir. 1996), the court found that three sexually suggestive remarks over a three-month period were not sufficient, as a matter of law, to unreasonably interfere with the working environment.
