20. Mail Filing

Nothing prohibits the filing of a formal EEO complaint via the mail. However, the prudent employee or representative will do so by return receipt mail. Complaints filed by mail are deemed filed as of the date they are postmarked. If the postmark is not legible, the Commission will presume that any mail filing received within five days of the expiration of the filing period is timely. See 29 CFR 1614.604(b). For example, an appeal received by the agency three days after the filing deadline was presumed timely filed where the filing date was a Friday and the receipt date was the following Monday. See Clark v. Department of Army, 01820237 (1983). Clark, though, is an ample demonstration of the need for proof of mailing. Moreover, the EEOC has adopted certified mail as "the facility to prove the dates of posting and receipt of documents" for all purposes. Hays v. Department of Air Force, 01841913 (1986).

In Ramirez v. Secretary of Navy, 01956527 (1996), the Commission found that the agency presented inadequate evidence that the complaint was not timely filed where it sent the notice of final interview by regular mail instead of certified mail. The mere representation of the agency that it had mailed the notice on a certain day was inadequate. The agency contended that complainant received the notice on June 19 and the complainant contended that she received it on June 28. In the absence of evidence to refute the complainant's representation, the complaint was deemed timely. However, a complainant's bare assertion that he mailed his formal complaint in a timely fashion was adequate to excuse his delay in Sonny M. v. Secretary of Defense, 0120161552 (2016). The agency dismissed Sonny M.'s complaint for failure to timely file his formal complaint. The complainant countered that he mailed his formal complaint well within the time frame for filing, but that there was some confusion concerning the zip codes for the addresses that the agency provided to him. He further explained that the complaint was not returned to him, but he had no proof that he actually mailed the complaint. When he learned months later that the agency did not receive his complaint, he resubmitted it. That was good enough for the Commission, and it reversed the agency's dismissal, noting that the agency bears the burden of supporting its dismissal.

In Thomas v. Secretary of Transportation, 01A00199 (2000), the Commission waived the time limit for filing a formal complaint where the complainant mailed the formal complaint within the 15-day period but inadvertently left the street address off the envelope. The Commission in Thomas explained:

On or about June 11, 1999, the complainant mailed her formal complaint to the Regional Director, Department of Transportation, S.F. Regional Office, Suite 2030, San Francisco, California 94105. The complainant subsequently received the envelope as undeliverable due to her failure to include the street address, that is, 201 Mission St. The complainant then re-addressed the envelope and mailed it to the agency with a July 1, 1999 postmark. The Commission finds that, due to the fact that the complainant omitted one line from a seven-line address, her formal complaint was filed seven days late. The Commission concludes that the complainant attempted to timely file her formal complaint and that her omission of one line of the seven-line address was an inadvertent error. Therefore, the Commission exercises it discretion to deem the complainant's formal complaint to have been timely filed on the date of its initial mailing. See House v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05910352 (May 1991) (untimeliness of a request for reconsideration waived where the complainant initiated a timely request to reopen, but due to her failure to include Washington, D.C. and the zip code on the envelope's address, her request to reopen was returned to her undelivered); and Vann v. Department of the Navy, Request No. 05890406 (August 25, 1989) (Commission exercised its discretion to deem a request for reconsideration to be timely filed where the initial mailing was returned as undeliverable due to an incorrect street address).

In Maximo C. v. Postmaster General, 0120152299 (2015), excused the untimely filing of the formal complaint where the complaint was returned to the complainant due to inadequate postage. He repackaged the complaint, paid the correct postage and wrote "resent" on the envelope. The Commission, noting the record contained the envelope with "resent" on it, waived the time frame.
