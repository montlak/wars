1. Nexus Requirement

The requirement of the ADA and the Rehabilitation is that agencies provide reasonable accommodation to the known physical and mental limitations of a qualified individual with a disability unless doing so would impose an undue hardship on the operations of the agency. Often, the requirement is referred to in a kind of shorthand so that it becomes a requirement to provide reasonable accommodation to a qualified individual with a disability unless it would be an undue hardship. There is nothing inherently wrong with shortened version of the requirement as long as the longer, and more precise, requirement is kept in mind. Because an agency is required to provide reasonable accommodation to the known physical and mental limitations of the individual with a disability and not simply reasonable accommodation of an individual with a disability, there is a requirement that there must be a nexus or connection between the limitations imposed by the disability and the accommodation sought.

For example, in Walker v. Secretary of Agriculture, 0120092573 (2011), the Commission found that the complainant was not entitled to the accommodation of reassignment to another geographic location where he provided no evidence of a connection between that accommodation and his cervical spine disease, peripheral vascular disease and diabetes. In explaining the nexus requirement in Walker, the Commission noted:

Complainant requested an accommodation in the form of a change in his ODS from Fort Lauderdale to Naples. Under the Commission's regulations, an agency is required to make reasonable accommodation to the known physical and mental limitations of an otherwise qualified individual with a disability unless the agency can show that accommodation would cause an undue hardship. 29 C.F.R. § 1630.9. For the purposes of analysis, we assume Complainant is an individual with a disability. 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(g)(1).

An employee is required to show a nexus between the disabling condition and the requested accommodation. See Hampton v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01986308 (July 31, 2002) (citing Wiggins v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01953715 (Apr. 22, 1997)). We find that the undisputed evidence of record supports the finding that Complainant submitted insufficient documentation and has not established the required nexus between his disabling condition and the requested accommodation. Complainant indicated that he experienced stress because of his living situation in Fort Lauderdale. Complainant provided several reasons for the stress such as the lack of safe living conditions in Fort Lauderdale, the socio-economic standing of Fort Lauderdale, the small rental apartment he has compared to his home in Naples, the stress of renting the apartment while owning a house in Naples, his inability to quit smoking due to the stressful environment, and the detest he feels for the Fort Lauderdale area. However, Complainant's physician only suggested that Complainant be allowed to live in an area Complainant considered to be less stressful without providing specific medical reasons. In addition, when the Physician spoke to the RA Official, she merely noted that Complainant could have access to a better diet and exercise with a change in ODS. The record also establishes that Complainant's request for a change in ODS involves issues outside of his medical condition such as his finances and his detest of Fort Lauderdale, Florida. Taking the facts as provided by Complainant and applying them to the law, we find that Complainant has not shown how the request for a modification to the Agency's 50 mile radius policy is connected to his disability (cervical spine disease).
