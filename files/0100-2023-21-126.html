VII. OTHER BASES OF HARASSMENT

Prior to October 1993, the Commission had issued regulatory guidance on two types of harassment: sexual harassment and harassment based on national origin. See 29 CFR 1606.8. In dealing with other forms of harassment, such as racial harassment, the Commission simply referred to its guidelines under national origin. In proposing 29 CFR Part 1609, the Commission attempted to consolidate its guidance, developed by both regulation and case law, on harassment based on race, color, religion, national origin, gender, disability, and age.

The Commission has continued to issue separate guidance on the subject of sexual harassment. As the Commission explained in proposing Part 1609, 58 Fed. Reg. 51,266 (1993):

For several reasons, the Commission has determined that there is a need for new guidelines that emphasize that harassment based upon race, color, religion, gender (footnote omitted), age, or disability is egregious and prohibited by Title VII, the ADEA, the ADA, and the Rehabilitation Act (footnote omitted). First, the Commission has determined that it would be useful to have consistent and consolidated guidelines that set forth the standards for determining whether conduct in the workplace constitutes illegal harassment under the various antidiscrimination statutes. Second, because of all the recent attention on the subject of sexual harassment, the Commission believes it important to reiterate and emphasize that harassment on any of the bases covered by the Federal antidiscrimination statutes is unlawful. Third, doing so at this time is particularly useful because of the recent enactment of the Americans with Disabilities Act. Fourth, these guidelines offer more detailed information about what is prohibited than did the national origin guidelines. Finally, they put in guideline form the rule that sexual harassment is not limited to harassment that is sexual in nature, but also includes harassment due to gender-based animus.

Section 1606.8 of the National Origin Guidelines will be incorporated into and superseded by these proposed Guidelines on Harassment. This does not represent a change in the Commission's position on harassment; rather, it is an effort to combine and clarify.

Sexual harassment continues to be addressed in separate guidelines because it raises issues about human interaction that are to some extent unique in comparison to other harassment and, thus, may warrant separate emphasis. In addition to the guidelines, more extensive guidance on sexual harassment can be found in EEOC Policy Guidance No. N-915-050, "Current Issues of Sexual Harassment." March 19, 1990 (Sexual Harassment Policy Guidance). The Commission's Sex Discrimination Guidelines remain in effect and there is no change in the Commission's policy regarding sexual harassment.

While the legal standards for assessing hostile environment sexual harassment and other types of hostile environment harassment are essentially the same, there still is good reason for maintaining sexual harassment as a distinct category of harassment. From a legal perspective, only sexual harassment presents the opportunity for tangible employment action harassment. Since the legal standards for evaluating tangible employment action harassment differ from the standards for evaluating hostile environment harassment, maintaining the distinction is important. Moreover, conduct that is sexual in nature presents a special problem in evaluating hostile environment cases. Not all sexual conduct is inherently bad. Comments, such as those that invoke a person's race, religion, or ethnic background, serve no useful purpose. But conduct that is sexual in nature can be entirely acceptable and desirable, depending on the circumstances. Of course, there are boundaries and the law surrounding hostile environment sexual harassment is largely concerned with attempting to define those boundaries.

The discussion of hostile environment harassment based on other protected categories starts with a review of the Commission's proposed regulations. The regulations accurately summarize the existing state of the law. Following the discussion of the proposed regulations, there is a review of some of the Commission's case law on the individual bases of harassment. As with other claims of discrimination, a complainant can combine bases of discrimination in a hostile environment claim. See, e.g., Rester v. Secretary of Transportation, 01950139 (1998) (Commission upheld finding of administrative judge that complainant was subjected to hostile environment based on race and national origin).

The reader should be aware that the Commission, on October 11, 1994, withdrew its proposed Part 1609. The Guidelines came under intense scrutiny by Congress in the summer of 1994 with the particular focus being on the proposed regulations as applied to religion. Many religious groups and the American Civil Liberties Union objected to applying the guidelines to religion on the basis that the guidelines were overly broad and, in certain circumstances, could constitute a violation of the First Amendment guarantee of freedom of religion and the Religious Freedom Restoration Act of 1993. As a result, Congress voted to exclude religion from the proposed guidelines and, subsequently, the Commission voted to withdraw the guidelines entirely, opting instead to deal with harassment on other bases case-by-case.

In July 2008, however, the Commission significantly revised Section 12 of the EEOC Compliance Manual dealing with religious discrimination. Much of that guidance is discussed in Chapter 13. However, the Commission's guidance on religious harassment is discussed below.

As noted at the outset of this book, religion is somewhat of an enigma as a Title VII category. All the other categories protected by Title VII--race, color, sex, and national origin--are pure status-related categories. No particular conduct is required. All persons are born with a race, color, sex, and national origin. Religion can be purely status-related. For example, a person can be discriminated against on the basis of his or her religious heritage, regardless of whether he or she practices the religion. But often religion involves specific conduct and beliefs. It is the only category under Title VII where accommodation becomes an issue. No doubt because of its special characteristics, as well as its special place in the history of this country, harassment based on religion should be treated as separate and apart from other forms of harassment. However, this does not undermine the Commission's guidelines as it applies to harassment based on race, color, gender, and national origin. With respect to those categories, the Commission's proposed guidelines represented a reasonably accurate and succinct statement of the law. For that reason, the discussion of harassment, other than sexual harassment, begins with a review of those proposed guidelines.
