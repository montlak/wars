C. SETTLEMENT REMEDIES

Settlements are contracts between the parties. As a result, the parties to a settlement "may contractually agree to a wide variety of measures to settle a complaint." Epstein v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01952695 (1997). The parties are not restricted solely to the remedies that would be provided under Title VII, the Rehabilitation Act, or the ADEA if the complainant is successful. However, the agency cannot provide a remedy that goes beyond the scope of those statutes.

Any provision of a settlement agreement granting the complainant a benefit that is beyond the scope of relief that could be achieved if the complainant was successful is invalid and may not be enforced. In Freeman v. Secretary of Army, 05990031 (2001), the complainant, a GS-12 Provost Marshall, alleged that he was subjected to hostile environment harassment. The complainant subsequently retired. Later, the complainant and the agency entered into a settlement agreement that provided the complainant would receive retirement benefits at the GS-14 level and his personnel records were amended to indicate he had served at that level for the three years prior to his retirement. After OPM expressed concerns about the agreement because it did not provide for contributions to the retirement fund, the complainant claimed breach of the agreement. The Commission, in Freeman, found the agreement was invalid, explaining:

The Commission strongly supports the settlement of complaints at all stages of the EEO process. 29 CFR 1614.603. Nevertheless, an agency may apply only those remedies which a court could order if the case were to go to trial, and the relief provided to settle an EEO dispute cannot be greater than the relief a court could order under such circumstances. See Equal Employment Opportunity Management Directive for 29 CFR Part 1614 (EEO MD-110), 12-2 (May 17, 2000). For example, in a case involving the allegedly discriminatory denial of a promotion, an agency could settle the complaint by providing a retroactive promotion with backpay, that is, the relief which a court could order if there was a finding of discrimination. Specifically, if a GS-9 level employee filed an EEO complaint regarding the denial of a promotion to a GS-11 level position, it would be appropriate, if the employee met the time in grade and other requirements, to offer in settlement a retroactive promotion to the GS-11 level. See EEO MD-110, supra. An agency could not, however, propose a promotion to a GS-12 level position for which the employee had not met the requirements.

A review of the record in the case herein reveals that, even if the underlying complaints are read broadly, complainant raised no claim which would support the relief provided for in Provision 3(c) if the matter were before a court. As noted, the underlying complaints alleged that complainant was subjected to a hostile work environment with regard to the conditions of his employment. Complainant did not assert that he applied for or was denied a promotion to the GS-14 level. Further, there is nothing in the record to suggest that, but for the alleged discrimination, complainant would have achieved that grade level. The Commission notes that the monetary relief available for hostile environment harassment under Title VII and the Rehabilitation Act would instead be an award of compensatory damages.

The remedy for breach of an EEO settlement agreement is prescribed by the Commission's regulations. The complainant has the option of specifically enforcing the agreement or requesting reinstatement of the complaint from the point where processing of the complaint ceased. In some instances, reinstatement of the complaint is not a viable remedy because the parties cannot be returned to the status quo ante.

Under the Commission's initial regulations, the proper remedy for breach of a settlement agreement was reinstatement of the complaint from the point in the process where settlement was reached. Under the 1987 amendments to Part 1613, the complainant could request reinstatement of the complaint, or seek enforcement of the agreement. 29 CFR 1613.217(b). The policy of enforcing settlement agreements is consistent with the practice of other adjudicatory agencies. Once the parties have entered into a knowing and voluntary agreement, there is no reason the agreement should not be enforced, presuming it is legal. The Commission has never adequately explained its policy of preserving a choice between enforcement and reinstatement of the complaint. Through its case law, however, at least a partial reason becomes apparent. There are situations where specific enforcement is simply an inadequate remedy. For example, when an agency fails to pay money to a complainant that it has agreed to pay in a settlement, specific enforcement--i.e., payment of the money with interest--is an adequate remedy. However, when an agency agrees to provide a neutral reference to prospective employers and instead gives a negative reference, there is no adequate enforcement remedy; what has been done cannot be undone.

The policy of allowing a complainant to elect between specific enforcement and reinstatement of the complaint in the event of breach is continued in Part 1614 at 29 CFR 1614.504:

(a) Any settlement agreement knowingly and voluntarily agreed to by the parties, reached at any stage of the complaint process, shall be binding on both parties. A final decision that has not been the subject of an appeal or civil action shall be binding on the agency. If the complainant believes that the agency has failed to comply with the terms of a settlement agreement or final decision, the complainant shall notify the EEO Director, in writing, of the alleged noncompliance within 30 days of when the complainant knew or should have known of the alleged noncompliance. The complainant may request that the terms of settlement agreement be specifically implemented or, alternatively, that the complaint be reinstated for further processing from the point processing ceased.

(b) The agency shall resolve the matter and respond to the complainant, in writing. If the agency has not responded to the complainant, in writing, or if the complainant is not satisfied with the agency's attempt to resolve the matter, the complainant may appeal to the Commission for a determination as to whether the agency has complied with the terms of the settlement agreement or final decision. The complainant may file such an appeal 35 days after he or she has served the agency with the allegations of noncompliance, but must file an appeal within 30 days of his or her receipt of an agency's determination. The complainant must serve a copy of the appeal on the agency and the agency may submit a response to the Commission within 30 days of receiving notice of the appeal.

(c) Prior to rendering its determination, the Commission may request that parties submit whatever additional information or documentation it deems necessary or may direct that an investigation or hearing on the matter be conducted. If the Commission determines that the agency is not in compliance and the noncompliance is not attributable to acts or conduct of the complainant, it may order such compliance or it may order that the complaint be reinstated for further processing from the point processing ceased. Allegations that subsequent acts of discrimination violate a settlement agreement shall be processed as separate complaints under § 1614.106 or § 1614.204, as appropriate, rather than under this section.

Most of the Commission's settlement policies are the same as, or similar to, the settlement policies adopted by most courts and administrative bodies.
