I. EEO EMPLOYEES

The protected activity section of Title VII is construed liberally, and encompassed within its definition are allegations that employees responsible for carrying out the agency's EEO programs have been retaliated against for performing the duties of their positions. Proctor v. USPS, 01810856 (1981). It is important that employees responsible for administering the agency's EEO programs and complaint processes receive insulation from reprisal in order to preserve the integrity of the programs and processes.

EEO counselors, by virtue of their duties, participate in the EEO process. An agency may not adopt policies that would tend to restrain or interfere with a counselor's duties, irrespective of the agency's intent in adopting the policies. As the Commission explained in Simpson v. Secretary of Treasury, IRS, 05930570 (1994):

However, upon further review of the record, the Commission concludes that the previous finding of no Title VII violation in regard to the agency policy pursuant to which appellant was denied acting supervisor assignments was erroneous, for the reasons set forth below. In regard to this allegation, the AJ stated that since the policy prohibiting EEO Counselors and union stewards from serving as acting supervisors had been rescinded since the incident at issue, any argument that it was an unfair burden on the EEO process was moot. The Commission disagrees. The record shows that appellant became an EEO Counselor on July 1, 1989. Appellant had also been recommended for the Management Development Program by the GM, and had served as an acting supervisor on several occasions. However, when the GM was advised of the agency's policy prohibiting EEO Counselors and union stewards from serving as acting supervisors, a policy of which he had not previously been aware, he discontinued appellant's acting supervisor assignments.

EEOC Regulation 29 CFR § 1614.101(b) provides that no person shall be subject to retaliation for opposing any unlawful discriminatory practice or for participating in any stage of the EEO complaint process. (footnote omitted). In this case, appellant had been a participant in the EEO process as an EEO Counselor, and based solely on that participation, was denied the opportunity to serve in an acting supervisor capacity. The agency's policy which prohibited such dual roles was allegedly based upon potential conflicts of interest. However, the agency presented no evidence that in appellant's particular situation, a conflict would have arisen. In fact, there is no evidence in the record to suggest that such a conflict was likely to occur; instead, the agency merely decided, based upon its policy, to discontinue appellant's acting supervisor opportunities. Accordingly, the Commission concludes that the agency's actions constituted, under the facts of this particular situation, a per se violation of the Commission's Regulations prohibiting reprisal.

Note that the Commission did restrict its decision to the particular facts presented in the case, thus leaving open the possibility that an agency may, in certain circumstances, be able to demonstrate that a policy affecting EEO counselors is, in fact, based on a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason.

When an EEO counselor or investigator alleges retaliation in a nonselection for an EEO position, the Commission has given some indication that the counselor will be subjected to a higher standard of proof. In Padin v. Postmaster General, 01931775 (1994), the Commission required more than the complainant's status as a counselor/investigator and a temporal proximity between her protected status and nonselection to support an inference of retaliation:

Under the unique facts of this case, more is needed than appellant's status as a C/I, or closeness in time between that status and the agency's action, to find a causal connection. The position in question was the Manager of EEO Complaint Processing. Therefore, being a C/I was a positive factor; it does not, by itself, support an inference of retaliation. Appellant alleges that she was retaliated against because of her Vigorous and unbiased performance as a C/I. She asserts that she was unfairly criticized for soliciting complaints and for her role in an EEO settlement, and contends that management officials did not cooperate with her EEO efforts. The Commission would view retaliation against an employee for the proper performance of EEO duties as a violation of Title VII. Activities of the employee that are beyond the bounds of proper performance, however, would not be protected activity. As noted above, there are several letters from management officials in the record of complaint number 6-0-0032-90 which give a contrary view. They complain of appellant's professionalism and question her ability to be objective. One, the Moosup Postmaster, reported that two female employees felt threatened when being questioned by appellant; he also stated that appellant first contacted him with the question, "Why have you been discriminating against the female employees in your office?" Appellant denies asking this question. However, these letters show at least that there is a dispute on the question of whether appellant was engaged in protected activity. While these letters were from officials whose facilities were the focus of EEO investigations, they are numerous enough and specific enough to merit consideration as controverting evidence. Moreover, we note an additional letter of criticism from a Labor Relations Representative, who in reviewing one of appellant's investigative reports, found an absence of evidence regarding agency reasons, causing a skewed report. Given the equivocal nature of the evidence regarding appellant's performance of her EEO duties, we conclude that there is insufficient evidence on which to base a presumption that retaliation was operating in the committee's action.

Note that the criticisms of the complainant's work were highly specific. No doubt this influenced the Commission's decision. The absence of such specificity, combined with the proximity in time, might well be sufficient to establish a legal presumption of retaliation.

The fact that an EEO employee has filed her own EEO complaint does not mean there is an inherent conflict of interest with her job duties. Moreover, reassigning the employee to non--EEO duties because she has filed a complaint constitutes retaliation. In Winters v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01A40571 (2006), the complainant was an EEO assistant. She filed an EEO complaint and retained private counsel to represent her. Thereafter, she was reassigned to a secretary's position on the basis that she had a conflict of interest due to her own complaint. The Commission in Winters rejected the agency's argument that it had a legitimate, nonretaliatory reason for reassigning the complainant:

The agency explained that it reassigned the complainant because she filed an EEO complaint, creating a conflict of interest, and noted her attorney was the representative on most of the EEO cases in the EEO office.

In Monroig v. U.S. Commission on Civil Rights, EEOC Appeal No. 07A10012 (April 25, 2002), the employee was a Solicitor, who among other things, served as in-house counsel and represented her employer in matters before the EEOC and the Merit Systems Protection Board. The employee's EEO responsibilities were removed because she filed an informal EEO complaint. Some months later, because of this EEO activity and a memo she wrote concerning the organizational structure of the EEO program which was opposition EEO activity, the employee was reassigned out of her position and into another unit. An EEOC Administrative Judge found reprisal discrimination regarding these employer actions.

In Monroig, the employer argued that the filing of the EEO complaint created a conflict of interest and rendered the employee ineffective in her job. The Commission disagreed. It ruled that anti-discrimination statutes do not limit or condition in any way the protection against retaliation for participating in the charge process, and that the act of filing a complaint without more does not so interfere with the employee's performance of her job that it renders her ineffective in her position. It distinguished federal case law where confidential client confidences were disclosed. The Commission disagreed with the employer's suggestion that the act of filing a complaint by in-house counsel is not protected activity, noting this would remove in-house counsel from Title VII protection.

The agency's reason for reassigning the complainant does not constitute a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason. Like in Monroig, the complainant simply brought an EEO claim. This, by itself, does not create a conflict of interest making the complainant ineffective in her job. While there would be a conflict of interest if the complainant handled her own case for the agency's EEO office, there is none, as in Monroig, in handling the EEO cases of others. Also, while the complainant's attorney was the representative for complainants in most of the cases handled by the EEO office in the NFC, this would not prevent the complainant from maintaining a professional relationship in those cases. The employee in Monroig could professionally represent her employer as a defendant in EEO cases even though she had an EEO case against her employer. Like in Monroig, where the employee did not disclose confidential information, there is no evidence in the record suggesting that the complainant gave her attorney inappropriate information about cases in the EEO office. As in Monroig, the agency's reasoning about a conflict of interest would unfairly remove a category of employees from the protection of anti-discrimination statutes.

The agency discriminated against the complainant based on her prior EEO activity when it reassigned her.
