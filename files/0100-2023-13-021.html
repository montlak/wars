F. PROCEDURAL ISSUES

The Commission has held that an agency may not dismiss a complaint, after investigation, on the basis of a procedural issue without also issuing a ruling on the merits of the case. Holmes v. USPS, 01831551 (1984). In that case, the agency found that the complainant had not stated a claim within the EEO purview when he alleged that a rule that prohibited employees from applying for promotions six grade levels above their present position discriminated against him on the basis of age and disabled status. The agency ruled that, because the complainant was more than six grade levels below the position in question, he was not within the class protected by the rule and had not stated a claim within the purview of EEO regulations. The Commission found that this was simply a way of deciding the underlying merits of the claim under the guise of a procedural issue and remanded the case for further investigation.

However, this is not a bright-line rule and many "procedural" defenses may be raised subsequent to investigation. Although not technically a "procedural" defense, lack of jurisdiction may be raised at any juncture of the complainant process, including on appeal. See Chapters 3, subheading "Standing: Failure to State a Claim--Agency Jurisdiction," and Chapter 8, subheading "Jurisdictional Issues." Also, as a general rule, timeliness may be raised as an issue prior to a decision on the merits of the complaint. See Chapter 3, subheading "Untimely Complaints." An agency is estopped from raising timeliness as grounds for dismissing a complaint after it fully participates in the investigation and hearing and a finding of discrimination has been made. See Richardson v. Postmaster General, 05880040 (1988).
