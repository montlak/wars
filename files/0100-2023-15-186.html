3. Amended Complaints

As discussed in Chapter 3 under the heading "Substantive Amendments," a complainant may amend a complaint as a matter of right at any time before the request for a hearing. Once a hearing is requested, a complainant must file a motion with the administrative judge asking leave to amend a complaint to add a new claim. However, the complainant can amend a complaint to add a basis of discrimination after the request for a hearing and even at the hearing.

In Sachez v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 07A20017 (2003), the Commission found that the administrative judge did not abuse her discretion in adding the basis of color to the complaint based on testimony at the hearing, even though the complainant previously withdrew color as a basis. The Commission in Sanchez explained:

With respect to the AJ's consideration of the basis of color, the Commission has held that a complainant may allege discrimination on all applicable bases, including sex, race, national origin, color, religion, age, disability and reprisal, and may amend his complaint at any time, including at the hearing, to add or delete bases without changing the identity of the claim. See Sanchez v. Standard Brands, Inc., 431 F.2d 455 (5th Cir. 1970); Dragos v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05940563 (January 19, 1995).

Because a "complainant may not be competent to perceive what constituted or motivated the alleged discriminatory act(s), "the administrative judge has a responsibility to conduct the hearing in a manner that will lead to a full development of the record. Fernandez v. Postmaster General, EEOC Request No. 05870562 (March 11, 1988). This means that the AJ may assist the complainant in eliciting further testimony and evidence where necessary. In the instant case, the complainant withdrew the basis of color from his complaint, and the AJ later added the basis of color back to the complaint as a result of the testimony provided at the hearing. The Commission finds that since the complainant had the right to include the basis of color at the hearing, the AJ properly exercised her discretion in adding that basis when she perceived discrimination on that basis from the testimony and the record.

Similarly, in Morrow v. Postmaster General, 0720070058 (2009), the Commission found that the agency was not prejudiced by a finding that it violated the Rehabilitation Act with a fitness-for-duty examination where the only issue identified for hearing was whether the examination constituted sex discrimination. The Commission, in Morrow, found:

Initially, we address the AJ's decision to make a finding of discrimination pursuant to the Rehabilitation Act, when the only basis at issue in the hearing was sex discrimination. In the report of investigation, the only basis of discrimination was identified as sex, however in her affidavit, complainant indicated that it is also possible that she was discriminated against based on a "perceived disability." The investigator declined to include disability as a basis of alleged discrimination. After complainant requested a hearing, an AJ was appointed to the case. On December 15, 2005, at a pre-hearing conference, complainant's representative made an oral motion to amend the complaint to include the basis of perceived physical disability. The AJ denied complainant's motion on the grounds that it was "untimely." On July 6, 2006, the case was subsequently reassigned to a different AJ (the AJ who held the hearing and issued the decision).

When the hearing began, the AJ clearly identified the issue as the agency's requirement on January 10, 2005 that complainant undergo an additional FFD examination after she had already been subjected to an examination, and cleared for duty. At no time, including after the hearing ended or when she issued her decision, did the AJ add any new issues. In addition to disparate treatment on the basis of sex being at issue, the challenge to the FFD examination presented a readily apparent allegation that the examination was itself unlawful regardless of whether complainant is an individual with a disability under the Rehabilitation Act. An agency may only require a medical examination of an employee, or make other medical inquiries, if that examination and/or inquiry is job-related and consistent with business necessity, and protection under the Rehabilitation Act from unlawful medical examinations and/or inquiries does not depend on whether or not the employee is an individual with a disability. See 29 C.F.R. §§ 1630.13, 1630.14; and EEOC Enforcement Guidance: Disability-Related Inquiries and Medical Examinations of Employees Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), No. 915.002 (July 27, 2000).

The Commission finds that the agency was not prejudiced when the AJ made a finding of unlawful medical inquiry/examination under the Rehabilitation Act, because we are not persuaded that the evidence that was presented at the hearing by the agency would have been significantly different had the agency been instructed prior to the hearing that a finding of discrimination under the Rehabilitation Act could result. In addition, the AJ correctly states that amending a complaint to add a basis of discrimination has always been permitted under Commission case law. In Sanchez v. Standard Brands, Inc., 431 F.2d 455 (5th Cir. 1970), the Fifth Circuit stated that "the 'scope' of the judicial complaint is limited to the 'scope' of the EEOC investigation which can reasonably be expected to grow out of the charge of discrimination." Id. at 466. The Commission has interpreted Sanchez to mean that a complainant may amend his or her complaint to add or delete bases without changing the identity of the claim; a complainant may therefore add a new basis to his or her discrimination complaint but not a new or different issue. See also Hummel v. Dept. of Agriculture, EEOC Appeal No. 01A20652 (April 8, 2002) ("adding a new basis does not give rise to an independent claim of discrimination."); Wallace v. Department of the Air Force, EEOC Appeal No. 01951217 (May 27, 1997); Dragos v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05940563 (January 19, 1995); Mendez v. Department of the Treasury, EEOC Request No. 05930641 (February 10, 1994).

Additionally, in Reda v. United States Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01955889 (December 12, 1997), the Commission found no error by the AJ when he added disability as a basis of discrimination, after hearing the testimony of agency officials, wherein they described their perception of complainant's disability. Here, as in Reda, it was incumbent upon the AJ not to turn a blind eye to the evidence in the record that the FFD examination was an unlawful inquiry under the Rehabilitation Act. Briefly, we do not find that the AJ was prevented from making a finding of unlawful medical examination simply because the first AJ ruled that complainant was untimely in raising disparate treatment based on "perceived disability." The AJ who decided this case did not make a finding that complainant was regarded as ("perceived as") disabled. What the AJ found was that there had been an unlawful medical examination, which, as previously stated, does not require a showing that the victim was an individual with a disability pursuant to the Rehabilitation Act.

In sum, we find that the AJ who held the hearing and wrote the decision in this case did not commit an abuse of discretion. Administrative Judges have broad discretion in the conduct of hearings. See 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109(e); Equal Employment Opportunity Management Directive for 29 C.F.R. Part 1614 (EEO-MD-110) at 7-8 to 7-14 (revised November 9, 1999); Bennett v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05980746 (September 19, 2000).

The Commission also reached a similar result in Snyder v. Postmaster General, 0720080050 (2009), relying upon Morrow, but in a different manner finding that the issue of whether the complainant was properly ordered to undergo a fitness-for-duty examination was "inextricably intertwined" with her placement in off-duty status, which was the substance of her complaint. Essentially, in Snyder, the Commission found there was no need to amend the complaint explaining:

We find that the issue of whether the agency properly required complainant to undergo a FFDE is inextricably intertwined with the issue of whether the agency discriminated against complainant when it placed her on off-duty status. The FFDE is the precipitating event that led to the agency's conclusion that complainant was not fit for duty, which culminated in the agency placing complainant on off-duty status. As a natural outgrowth of the examination of the off-duty issue, witnesses provided ample hearing testimony regarding the agency's reasons for sending complainant for a FFDE and determining that she was unfit for duty. Because of the interrelatedness of the FFDE to complainant's off-duty status, the agency had adequate notice that its decision to order complainant to undergo a FFDE was at issue. Consequently, we find that the agency was not prejudiced when the AJ made a finding of unlawful medical inquiry/examination under the Rehabilitation Act, because we are not persuaded that the evidence that was presented at the hearing by the agency would have been significantly different had the agency been instructed prior to the hearing that a finding of discrimination under the Rehabilitation Act could result. See Morrow v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 0720070058 (November 13, 2009) (Commission found that it was incumbent upon the AJ not to turn a blind eye to the evidence in the record that an FFDE was an unlawful inquiry under the Rehabilitation Act although sex discrimination was the only identified issue before the hearing).
