1. Restoration of Employment

The Federal Employees' Compensation Act ("FECA"), 5 USC chapter 81, gives employees who sustain on-the-job injuries certain rights with respect to restoration of former employment when they fully or partially recover from compensable injuries. Restoration of employment under FECA can become rather complicated, in part, because so many agencies become involved in administering the Statute. The Office of Workers' Compensation Claims, Department of Labor, is responsible for determining an employee's initial and continuing eligibility for FECA benefits. The Office of Personnel Management has the authority to promulgate regulations that govern restoration of employment. The Merit Systems Protection Board has jurisdiction over cases alleging that an agency has failed to properly restore a fully or partially recovered employee to duty. The EEOC can become involved when issues relating to disability discrimination arise over the treatment of fully or partially recovered employees.

Additionally, 5 USC 8151 provides:

(a) In the event the individual resumes employment with the Federal Government, the entire time during which the employee was receiving compensation under this chapter shall be credited to the employee for the purposes of within-grade step increases, retention purposes, and other rights and benefits based upon length of service.

(b) Under regulations issued by the Office of Personnel Management--

(1) the department or agency which was the last employer shall immediately and unconditionally accord the employee, if the injury or disability has been overcome within one year after the date of commencement of compensation or from the time compensable disability recurs if the recurrence begins after the injured employee resumes regular full-time employment with the United States, the right to resume his former or an equivalent position, as well as all other attendant rights which the employee would have had, or acquired, in his former position had he not been injured or disabled, including the rights to tenure, promotion, and safeguards in reductions-in-force procedures, and

(2) the department or agency which was the last employer shall, if the injury or disability is overcome within a period of more than one year after the date of commencement of compensation, make all reasonable efforts to place, and accord priority to placing, the employee in his former or equivalent position within such department or agency, or within any other department or agency.

Those pursuing denial of restoration rights before the MSPB should be aware that cases can involve issues of disability discrimination when the agency bases its denial on the compensable condition or when the agency refuses to consider reasonable accommodation of the employee in a vacant position for which he or she is qualified.

In Jorgensen v. USPS, 01872973 (1988), the Commission addressed both the issue of the agency's obligation in restoring a partially recovered employee and whether partially recovered employees were entitled to complete recovery of lost benefits in the same manner as fully recovered employees.

The Commission, citing to Jasany v. USPS, 755 F.2d 1244 (6th Cir. 1985), found that the agency did not have an obligation to restructure a vacant position in such a way as to eliminate essential functions of the position. This finding is consistent with the definition of a qualified individual with a disability under the Rehabilitation Act and its treatment of other cases involving job restructuring. But, if the agency voluntarily undertakes to restructure a position by eliminating essential functions, the employee is entitled to the full array of protection under the Rehabilitation Act.

The Commission in Jorgensen also found that it was discriminatory to deny past within-grade increases to partially recovered employees while granting them to fully recovered employees:

In view of the purpose of the legislation (FECA), OPM's interpretation of 5 USC 8151(a) as applying to partially recovered employees, and the specific reference in 5 USC 8151(a) to within-grade step increases, the Commission finds that the agency erred in interpreting 5 USC 8151(a) as permitting disparate treatment between partially and fully recovered injured employees. In summary, 5 USC 8151 and the Rehabilitation Act are complementary. The minimum restoration rights and benefits due former civil servants who sustain on-the-job injuries are set forth in 5 USC 8151. The Rehabilitation Act provides, in part, that "handicapped" persons (including former federal employees who have partially recovered from on-the-job injuries) are not subjected to discrimination in the form of disparate treatment because of their handicaps.

In Parsons v. USPS, 01862368 (1988), the Commission reached a similar result in finding that it was discriminatory to give full past service credit to fully recovered employees while denying the same to partially recovered employees. Relying upon its decision in Jorgensen, the Commission found that service credit was a benefit covered by 5 USC 8151(a).
