29. Work Performance and the "Hostility" Requirement

In McGinnis v. Secretary of Defense, 01902760 (1990), the Commission appeared to retreat from the requirement that the complainant demonstrate the hostile or offensive environment actually interfered with work performance. It also categorically rejected an agency argument that the sexual comments and innuendo must create an environment that is actually "hostile." It is sufficient under McGinnis if the complainant demonstrates the environment is offensive:

The agency asserted that appellant did not show that she was subjected to a hostile environment. The Commission notes that in order to meet the criteria to establish hostile environment sexual harassment, the environment need not be shown to be hostile but could instead be shown to be offensive. Appellant and Co-worker 1 testified that they found the Deputy Administrator's remarks offensive. Appellant testified she tried to avoid him because of these remarks. The Commission's review of the remarks shows that they could reasonably be considered offensive. The agency asserted that the conduct was not unwelcome as appellant admitted that she had taken part in making such comments. A review of appellant's testimony shows that she admitted making comments to male co-workers but stated that these comments were not as personal as the comments the Deputy Administrator addressed to her. The agency also found significant the fact that no prior complaints had been filed against the Deputy Administrator. Appellant, Co-worker 1, and Supervisor 1 testified that they had not complained earlier about the Deputy Administrator's behavior because they felt due to his high position, it would be useless. The Commission finds that the agency has failed to meet the criteria for avoiding liability for hostile environment sexual harassment.

The Commission further elaborated on the factors to consider in determining whether a work environment rises to the level of being hostile, intimidating, or abusive. In Crane v. Postmaster General, 01924585 (1993), the Commission observed:

In determining whether the conduct at issue rises to the level of a hostile environment, the harasser's conduct should be evaluated from the objective standpoint of a reasonable person. EEOC Policy Guidance at 14. The Commission's Policy Guidance indicates that the following factors are pertinent to the inquiry: (1) whether the conduct was verbal or physical, or both; (2) how frequently the conduct was repeated; (3) whether the conduct was hostile and patently offensive; (4) whether the alleged harasser was a co-worker or a supervisor; (5) whether others joined in perpetrating the harassment; and (6) whether the harassment was directed at more than one individual. Id.

When a complainant does allege that a hostile environment adversely affected her performance, she must not only prove the existence of the hostile environment, but establish a causal connection between that environment and her performance. Maydak v. Secretary of Treasury, 01932574 (1994). In Maydak, the Commission found the complainant could not establish the necessary link where one of the primary incidents upon which the complainant relied occurred prior to the appraisal period at issue. The Commission further noted that the complainant's psychiatrist testified that the complainant probably suffered from moderate depression prior to harassment and did not exhibit signs of severe depression until after the appraisal was issued. Moreover, whether the complainant has received treatment for mental illness is not relevant to the issue of whether a supervisor's actions create a hostile working environment. Since the standard for assessing conduct is an objective standard based on the "reasonable person," the past mental health of the complainant is not taken into account. Id.

The Commission has required minimal interference with complainant's job performance to satisfy a hostile environment claim. For example, in Dennis-Brown v. Postmaster General, 01930016 (1994), the complainant showed that, within a one-month period, she was subjected to an incident of offensive touching by a coworker and comments that suggested sexual intimacy between the two. Based on these incidents, the Commission in Dennis-Brown found that there was an unreasonable interference with the complainant's work performance:

We also find that the conduct unreasonably interfered with her work performance and engendered an offensive working environment.... Appellant on two occasions was interrupted from her work, attempting to fix a jam in the machine and sorting mail, by the co-worker with his sexually offensive activities, which resulted in more lost time from work because of discussions with supervisors about these incidents. Furthermore, there was leave taken by her due to an inability to continue working in the same location as the co-worker. We find that appellant was subjected to an offensive work environment in that the co-worker within a month period touched her in an offensive manner and then suggested some kind of sexual intimacy between appellant and himself. We find both of these incidents to be severe. We find that these events, which appeared to escalate in spite of warnings from appellant and instructions from management, would embarrass and humiliate a reasonable person to the extent that her working conditions would be changed and she would find working in the same environment intolerable. For these reasons we find appellant has established a prima facie case of sexual harassment.

Later in the same decision, the Commission also found that management's failure to make an immediate and effective response to these actions created a hostile environment because the complainant "reasonably feared" there would be a repetition of similar incidents.
