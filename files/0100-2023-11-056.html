C. PRESENT VIABILITY OF CONTINUING VIOLATION CLAIMS

Despite its rejection of the views of the Ninth Circuit and the Commission regarding continuing violations in Morgan, supra, there is no reason to believe that the Supreme Court intended to hold that there can never be a situation where a viable continuing violation claim can be asserted. First, Morgan makes clear that claims involving discrete personnel actions, hostile environment and systemic violations raise different concerns with respect to timeliness that need to be addressed separately, instead of lumping those claims into one continuing violation theory. As a result, under Morgan, a hostile environment claim remains viable, regardless of how long ago some of the individual acts of harassment occurred, as long as at least one act of harassment occurred within the 45 days prior to the initiation of counseling. This is because, in the Supreme Court's view, a hostile environment represents a single claim of an unlawful employment practice without regard to the number of single occurrences that comprise that claim. The question is what becomes of the claim in which the complainant asserts that a series of connected discrete personnel actions, some falling outside the 45-day period, are discriminatory. It would be easy to interpret Morgan as holding that such continuing violation claims no longer exist because each discrete personnel action triggers the 45-day period and, if counseling is not timely initiated, the claim is waived. In all likelihood, it would also be wrong to interpret Morgan in such a way. The most reasonable interpretation of Morgan is that it implicitly adopts the view of continuing violations expressed in Sabree v. United Brotherhood of Carpenters and Joiners Local No. 33, 921 F.2d 369 (1st Cir. 1990) and Berry v. Board of Supervisors, 715 F.2d 971 (5th Cir. 1983), cert denied, 479 U.S. 868 (1986)--the same view held by the Commission prior its decision in Anisman. The reason that Morgan does not preclude assertion of a claim of continuing violation under Sabree and Berry is that the court was not presented with such a claim. The Court in Morgan expressly noted that the plaintiff believed all of the past acts included in his complaint were discriminatory at the time they occurred. 122 S. Ct. at 2068. As a result, the Court in Morgan noted that "[t]here may be circumstances where it will be difficult to determine when the time period should begin to run. One issue that may arise in such circumstances is whether the time begins to run when the injury occurs as opposed to when the injury reasonably should have been discovered. But this case presents no occasion to resolve that issue." Id. at n.7. Essentially, the Court is suggesting an application of the "reasonable suspicion" standard, discussed earlier in this chapter under the subheading "Reasonable Suspicion Standard."

Showing a continuing violation involving discrete personnel actions likely requires very definitive proof that the complainant was not previously aware that the untimely actions were discriminatory and reasonably should not have been aware that they were discriminatory. Morgan specifically notes that discrete personnel actions are "easy to identify" and that the equitable doctrines of waiver, tolling, and estoppel should be applied sparingly.

For example, assume that a 48-year-old black woman who is an auditor applies for four promotions to the position of senior auditor over a two-year period of time. In each instance, the selecting official is her second-level supervisor. On the first three occasions, the woman makes the best qualified list but is not selected. Instead, three auditors from within her division receive the promotions--a 53-year-old white male, a 49-year-old black male, and a 52-year-old Hispanic female. Each of these individuals has significant experience with the agency as an auditor and superior performance evaluations, as does our prospective complainant. On the fourth occasion, the selecting official goes outside the agency to hire a 28-year-old white woman with less than two years of auditing experience. Our prospective complainant now not only believes this last selection was discriminatory, but also that her status as an older black female prevented her from getting the earlier promotions. Assuming she timely initiates counseling on the latest selection, does Morgan preclude her from also challenging the earlier selections simply because they are discrete personnel actions that took place outside the 45-day period for initiating counseling? The likely answer is that Morgan does not require such a result and the complainant can pursue her "continuing violation" claim, provided there are no other facts that did, or should have, put the complainant on notice earlier that the first three nonselections may have been discriminatory. This does not mean that our hero wins her case; it means she has retained the right to challenge all four selections.

Of course, the Commission works slowly even, at times, in recognizing significant Supreme Court cases. As late as November 2002, the OFO issued a decision holding that a claim for a continuing violation was stated because the acts were interrelated and one had occurred within the 45 days preceding initiation of counseling. See Harrell v. Social Security Administration, 01A14917 (2002). The decision makes no mention of Morgan and instead cites to Anisman. Teeters v. Social Security Administration, 01A04724 (2002) (citing Morgan in dismissing continuing violation claim that included discrete personnel actions). Then again, in January 2003, the Commission issued another decision finding a continuing violation claim based on the interrelatedness of the claims with no mention of Morgan. See Murphy v. Secretary of Air Force, 01A24252 (2003).
