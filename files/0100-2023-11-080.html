broida eeo Chapter 02: The Precomplaint Process--Counseling Chapter 02: The Precomplaint Process--Counseling, Subchapter 04: Requests for Counseling, Section E: Contact Not Involving Counselors, Subsection 01: "Logically Connected"/Wrong Agency Officials, Part c: Management Officials
c. Management Officials

In Truelove v. Secretary of Army, 01A30475 (2003), the Commission found that the complainant did not exhibit an intent to pursue the EEO process where he complained to a Colonel about several incidents of alleged discrimination. The Colonel agreed to investigate the incidents and did assign the matter to an investigator. The complainant also contacted an EEO counselor and was informed of the 45-day limit. However, he did not contact the counselor again until several months later. The Commission determined the second contact was untimely and the first contact was not sufficient to preserve his EEO complaint rights because he had not exhibited an intent to initiate the EEO process at that time. The complainant in Bula P. v. Secretary of Treasury, 0120151636 (2016), sent an email to certain unidentified agency officials alleging "discrimination," "refusal of accommodate" and "harassment" as reasons for her resignation. Finding that the complainant's email arguably exhibited an intent to "begin the EEO process," the Commission ordered the agency to conduct a supplemental investigation to determine "the relationship 'with the EEO process' of the officials who received Complainant's email." In Hernandez v. Postmaster General, 01972231 (1998), the Commission chose not to apply the logically-connected standard, but instead found the agency official--a human resources manager--had a "duty" to timely direct the complainant to an EEO counselor:

In this case appellant contacted the HR Manager, an agency official. While there may be some question as to whether this official was "logically" connected to the EEO process, we find that this contact at least gave rise to a duty on the agency's part to direct appellant to an EEO Counselor within the time limitation. There is no evidence in the record that this duty was fulfilled. Therefore, the complaint was improperly dismissed by the agency.

Hernandez suggests that, independent of whether an agency official is logically connected to the EEO process, at least some agency officials have an affirmative duty to advise an employee of how to properly exercise EEO rights.

In Briggs v. Postmaster General, 01965216 (1997), the Commission noted the proper course for agencies to follow where a complainant contacts management officials, rather than an EEO counselor, with an allegation of discrimination:

The record indicates that the appellant contacted management officials on January 31, 1996, the day of the last alleged discriminatory event. The Commission has held that a complainant's contact with management officials to make an allegation of discrimination is, in effect, a request for EEO counseling. Kemer v. General Services Administration, EEOC Request No. 05910779 (Dec. 30, 1991). If the agency officials contacted are not EEO counselors, the agency should respond to appellant's contact by advising her exactly who to contact with her allegations of discrimination. Id. In Guerra v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01944190 (Nov. 9, 1994), the Commission held EEO contact timely for a sexual harassment claim where complainant contacted management but management did not direct her to the EEO counselor even though an internal investigation was conducted. Because the agency in the immediate case failed to so advise appellant of the EEO counselor, the Commission finds that the agency improperly rejected appellant's complaint as untimely.

The Commission has reviewed the agency's untimeliness arguments regarding appellant's mistake of seeking the wrong EEOC authority, her constructive knowledge based on her ten years with the agency along with the agency's compliance with posting regulations. The Commission finds that these arguments are irrelevant to the disposition of this appeal. If the agency officials advised appellant whom to contact when she consulted them on January 31, 1996, it is the Commission's determination that her subsequent EEO contact would be timely.

Briggs, like Hernandez, relies not on the "logically connected" criterion but on the obligation of some management officials not connected with the EEO process to properly advise a prospective complainant of agency complaint procedures.

In Landmesser v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, 05920835 (1993), the Commission set aside the 45-day time limit for contacting a counselor where the complainant brought the sexual harassment to the attention of her immediate supervisor, who had, in fact, witnessed some of the harassment. Without reference to its "logically connected" standard, the Commission determined that the complainant brought the matter to an official "whom appellant thought to be an appropriate recipient of the information." Because the agency has a legal obligation to take prompt and effective remedial action in response to complaints involving a hostile environment, see Chapter 12, subheading "Hostile Environment Sexual Harassment," it is not clear that the Commission would extend the holding in Landmesser to other situations not involving a hostile environment. However, the Landmesser rule may be a narrow exception in cases involving hostile environment harassment. For example, in Delalat v. Secretary of Air Force, 01A54303 (2005), the Commission held that the complainant had not contacted an official logically connected to the EEO office by asking his supervisor to forward correspondence to the agency EEO office in his behalf.

When a complainant contacts another supervisor and complains that she is being sexually harassed by her own supervisor, this constitutes contact with an agency official "logically connected" with the EEO process. Mathershed v. Postmaster General, 01954603 (1996). In that case, the Commission reversed the agency's determination that the contact with the counselor was untimely where it occurred several months after the alleged incidents. Note, however, that this decision seems to be somewhat in conflict with Hash, supra, holding that contact with the head of the agency for the purpose of requesting an end to the harassment does not exhibit an intent to start the complaint process. Still, Mathershed seems to be the predominate view. For example, in Edwards v. Postmaster General, 01A30150 (2003), the Commission found that contact with a supervisor and the agency inspector general with allegations of sexual harassment was contact logically connected to the EEO process for timeliness purposes, even though the complainant was otherwise aware of the EEO process.

A complainant contacting several management officials and complaining that his promotion was denied because of his alcoholism may be sufficient to justify waiver of the time frame for contacting an EEO counselor when none of the management officials referred the employee to a counselor. Noonan v. National Aeronautics and Space Administration, 05950776 (1996). However, it is hard to clearly draw that conclusion from the Noonan decision because the Commission, in reversing the agency's dismissal of the complaint, also relied on the fact that the promotion had been denied for a second time just days before the employee did finally initiate counseling.

In Lucy v. Postmaster General, 01962691 (1996), the Commission held that where the complainant's attorney made contact with a postmaster, it constituted timely EEO contact:

We have [ ] held that contact an official who has the authority to sustain or rescind the event in question "constituted the requisite initiation of contact with an agency official regarding his complaint of discrimination." See Wait v. Department of the Treasury, EEOC Request No. 05891021 (January 30, 1990). In this case appellant's attorney contacted the Postmaster within the 45-day time limit provided by EEOC Regulations for the initial EEO Counselor contact. While there may be some question as to whether this official was "logically" connected to the EEO process, we find that this contact at least gave rise to a duty on the agency's part to direct appellant to an EEO Counselor within the time limitation. There is no evidence in the record that this duty was fulfilled. Therefore, the complaint was improperly dismissed by the agency.

An applicant is given greater leeway than an employee under the "logically connected" standard. This is true even when the applicant is represented by an attorney. In Stephens v. Postmaster General, 01954259 (1996), the complainant, who was an applicant for employment, did not contact a counselor within the 45-day period following her medical disqualification for the position. She did, however, retain an attorney who wrote to the postmaster of the facility offering "to discuss this matter with you prior to the commencement of an administrative charge before the (EEOC)." Because the complainant was an applicant for employment, the postmaster was considered to be a person "logically connected" to the EEO process. The attorney's letter constituted contact within the 45-day period and was deemed by the Commission to trigger the agency's obligation to advise the complainant of her right to seek EEO counseling and the time frame for doing so.

The contact with an official, even if logically connected with the EEO process, must "exhibit, either explicitly or implicitly, an intention by the appellant to begin the EEO process." Hash v. Secretary of Defense, 01933900 (1993). In Hash, the Commission held that contacting the head of the agency to complain of harassment and asking for the agency head's assistance to stop the harassment does not exhibit an intention to start the EEO process. It would seem, however, that this case has now been discredited by the Landmesser case.
