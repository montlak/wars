1. Cancellation of Position

The McDonnell Douglas v. Green formulation of a prima facie case is flexible and is in reality only a requirement that the plaintiff establish enough facts which, if otherwise unexplained, could lead one to conclude that an action is based on prohibited motives. It is not a bar to establishing a prima facie case that the agency abolished the position in question and therefore has not replaced the complainant with someone outside of the protected group. See Rivenbark v. Defense Logistics Agency, 01820559 (1982). In lieu of showing that he was replaced by a younger employee, the complainant in Rivenbark was allowed to adduce other evidence "that might reasonably lead to a conclusion that there was an intent to discriminate." Id. Unfortunately, the Commission gave no indication of what evidence was produced to fulfill the prima facie case requirements.

A prima facie case of discrimination is not made where the agency canceled the vacancies at issue and no selection for the positions was made, absent some evidence the vacancy was canceled for discriminatory reasons. Boyer v. Department of Air Force, 01821406 (1983).

In McDuffie v. Secretary of Navy, 05880134 (1988), the Commission found that cancelation of the vacancy was not a bar to establishing a prima facie case where the black complainant established that a white employee was asked to apply for the position, but declined; established that the complainant was the only qualified applicant on the selection certificate and was rated "highly qualified"; and offered supporting testimony from two coworkers that they believed he was not promoted because of race. The Commission stressed that the elements of a prima facie case are not "immutable" and that the focus is not on a mechanical application of McDonnell Douglas, but on whether the complainant presented sufficient evidence to raise an inference of discrimination.

In Zadkiel v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corp., 01994331 (2002), the Commission found that cancelation of a vacancy announcement where the complainant was the only qualified candidate established a prima facie case of disability discrimination, even though there were no similarly situated employees. As the Commission noted in Zadkiel:

Regarding Position 1, we find that the AJ properly determined that complainant established a prima facie case of disability discrimination. First, we find that the parties agree that complainant is a qualified individual with a disability given that he successfully performed the essential functions of his position with the assistance of a reasonable accommodation. Next, we find that complainant was subjected to an adverse employment action when SO failed to select him for Position 1. Finally, the Commission acknowledges that complainant was the only certified candidate for Position 1, and that there were no "similarly situated" candidates to use as a comparison to determine whether complainant suffered disparate treatment; however, we find that complainant nonetheless produced sufficient evidence to establish an inference of discrimination. See Lawson, supra. Specifically, we find that this inference is established by the evidence showing that complainant was the only eligible and qualified candidate for Position 1, yet instead of taking steps to select him under the downsizing guidelines, SO canceled the vacancy announcement, and re-posted the position. We additionally find that this inference is established by the evidence demonstrating the close proximity between the time complainant disclosed his disability and need for an accommodation to SO, and his subsequent non-selection by SO for Position 1.
