7. Selection and Promotion

A complainant establishes a prima facie case of nonselection or nonpromotion by showing that he is a person with a disability who is qualified for the position in question, that he applied and was not selected, and that a person without a disability was selected for the position. Crawford v. Veterans Administration, 01870235 (1988). In Crawford the Commission found that the complainant demonstrated pretext by showing that his disability was considered by the selecting officials and that it was a factor in his nonselection. However, the Commission went on to find that, given the qualifications of the selectees, the complainant would not have been selected even absent discrimination and ordered the agency to grant him future priority consideration. Although Crawford expresses a traditional formulation of the prima facie case, in reality, there is no specific requirement that a complainant demonstrate that a nondisabled employee was selected. All that is required is that the complainant come forward with some evidence from which an inference of discrimination could be drawn.

Persons with disabilities are not a homogenous group. It is not a legal barrier to establishing a prima facie case that the agency actually selected another person with a disability to fill a disputed position where the persons involved suffer from different disabling conditions. In Fields v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01901487 (1990), the Commission held:

[W]hile the agency argues on appeal that appellant has not presented a prima facie case because another handicapped employee was selected for the position, we do not find this logic persuasive. The Rehabilitation Act does not permit discrimination against one type of handicapped individual simply because another group does not suffer discrimination. Shirley v. Devine, 670 F.2d 1188, 1204 (D.C. Cir. 1982). We note as the D.C. Circuit did in Devine, that the "handicapped" are not a homogenous group and do not share the same traits. Thus, a failure to discriminate against an individual with a physical handicap such as that suffered by the selectee, who is an amputee, does not imply in any fashion that the [agency] did not illegally discriminate against appellant based on his wholly different mental impairment.

Like members of other classes, an employee with a disability must establish that he was qualified and applied for vacant positions. It was proper for the agency to dismiss a complaint of disability discrimination based on nonselection where the employee had excluded himself voluntarily from consideration for the positions in question because they lacked promotion potential and he believed his disability would hinder his performance. Gonzales v. Department of Air Force, 01822066 (1983). To bring the matter within the EEO purview, the employee must allege that he is aggrieved by an act or decision of the agency and not his own voluntary actions.

In disqualifying employees with disabilities from consideration for higher level supervisory positions, an agency must be careful not to have its perceptions of the individual interfere with its assessment of his supervisory abilities. In Cornwell v. Postmaster General, 05890008 (1989), the agency declined to consider an employee who had cerebral palsy and was mildly retarded for supervisory positions even though he met the basic qualifications for those positions. As part of its justification, the agency contended that the complainant sometimes went into trance-like states where he would rock back and forth. Noting that the agency did not contend that the complainant presented a danger to himself or others and that the trance was broken by speaking to him, the Commission found this constituted "social bias" against the complainant.

Moreover, where discriminatory treatment results in the loss of opportunity to enhance promotion potential, a subsequent failure to promote may also be discriminatory. Emphasizing the importance of looking at the whole picture, the Commission found that the agency failed to select complainant based on her disability and the agency's history of refusing to reasonably accommodate her in Husk v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01976712 (1999):

[T]he Commission disagrees with the finding that appellant's nonselection was not tainted by disability discrimination. Both the recommending and selecting officials testified that appellant and the selectee were equally qualified for the position in question. They also both emphasized that the major factor which distinguished the two candidates was the selectee's display of initiative and superior leadership skills as evidenced by her work on the CPEP, and most particularly, her volunteering to serve as the coordinator of one of the CPEP subgroups. While, on its face, this reason appears nondiscriminatory, the evidence clearly establishes that the selectee was given the opportunity to volunteer to serve as a CPEP subgroup coordinator at the January 1994 conference, which, as even the agency concedes, appellant was discriminatorily denied attendance because of her disability. In other words, due to the agency discriminatory actions, appellant was deprived of the opportunity to acquire the one credential which the selecting officials testified made the critical difference in their selection decision. The Commission finds that the agency has failed to prove that in the absence of this tainted motivating factor, it still would not have selected appellant. See Crider v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 05960632 (October 16, 1998). In light of the responsible agency officials' testimony that appellant and the selectee were equally knowledgeable about the position in question, and the fact that appellant had more seniority and superior performance evaluations, the Commission finds that it is more likely than not that, absent the agency's discriminatory actions in depriving appellant of the opportunities provided at the January 1994 conference, appellant would have been selected for the position in question.
