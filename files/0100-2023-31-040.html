3. Experience of Counsel

A major factor in establishing a reasonable hourly rate is the experience of the attorney representing the complainant. The general rule is simple: more experienced attorneys tend to charge a higher hourly rate and the Commission will award higher hourly rates to attorneys with significant experience.

The general rule notwithstanding, some types of experience are better than others. For example, in Abbate v. Secretary of Navy, 01971418 (2000), the Commission awarded $200 per hour to a former agency lawyer with 20 years of experience. The complainant's attorney supported his fee petition with an affidavit from an experienced EEO attorney in the Chicago area where the litigation had taken place stating that she charged $250 per hour and this was consistent with the prevailing market rate in Chicago. Because the experience of the complainant's lawyer in the EEO field was limited, despite his years of experience, the Commission found a reduction of $50 per hour from the prevailing market rate was appropriate. A side note on the case for complainant's counsel to ponder is that the complainant's attorney in Abbate did not seek the $250 per hour charged by experienced EEO lawyers, but sought the $200 per hour awarded.

In Thomas v. Postmaster General, 0720080033 (2008), the Commission upheld the decision of an administrative judge that both reduced the requested hourly rate and cut the number requested hours due to the lack of experience in counsel in the employment law field. In Thomas, the Commission noted:

The AJ determined that based on a review of the record in evidence, complainant's attorney's hourly rate of $240.00 was not reasonable, and that the prevailing rate in similar cases for attorneys specialized in discrimination law in the Dallas-Fort Worth area is $225.00 per hour. The AJ determined that while complainant's attorney did a competent and effective job on behalf of complainant, she is not a specialist in employment law. The AJ concurred with the agency's argument that the number of hours reported was high compared to similar cases which may be reflective of a need to do extra legal research compared to an employment law specialist. Consequently, the AJ determined that an hourly rate of $185.00 would be appropriate. The AJ also determined that a modest reduction of 10% should be applied to the 158.8 hours claimed. Specifically, the AJ determined that while complainant substantially prevailed in claims (2) (3), she did not prevail on the remaining claims (claims (1) and (4)). The AJ stated that while there was significant overlap, there were portions of the hostile work environment claim that were substantively unrelated to claims (2) (3) that complainant prevailed on. Therefore, the AJ reduced complainant's requested attorney fees of $38,112.00 to $26,455.00.
