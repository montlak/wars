b. Eliminated Positions

Significant time can pass from when a position is denied a candidate to the time relief is actually ordered. And, agencies are constantly revisiting organizational structures, eliminating positions, adding positions, promoting, and the like. It is not uncommon for a position to have been eliminated by the time reinstatement or an appointment is ordered. "The Commission has previously found that where a complainant's position has since been abolished, one method for determining the promotions a complainant would likely have received, absent discrimination, is to examine similarly situated employees during the relevant time period. Tuala v. Dep't of the Navy, EEOC Petition No. 04A00026 (Apr. 11, 2002); King v. Dep't of Transport., EEOC Appeal No. 07A40003 (Sept. 29, 2005); Miller v. Dep't of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 0120053382 (Feb. 9, 2007)." Alvaro P. v. Secretary of the Air Force, 2021004984 (2022).

In Mehta v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, 01A32842 (2004), for instance, the agency was found to have discriminated against the complainant when it failed to select him for a GS-14 Enforcement Analyst position. The Commission ordered the agency to reinstate the complainant to his former GS-13 Staff Accountant position, but that position had since been eliminated. Instead, the agency placed the complainant in a GS-13 Operations Specialist position, concluding that placement in a GS-14 position would result in "greater than make-whole relief" and that it was "speculative" whether the complainant would have been promoted. The evidence, however, showed that "nearly all" of the individuals who held the GS-13 Staff Accountant position at the time it was eliminated had since been promoted to the GS-14 or GS-15 level. The Commission in Mehta noted the agency's failure to produce evidence showing otherwise:

[T]he Commission finds no documentary evidence of record to demonstrate how the GS-level qualifications were conducted for the Position. Aside from making assertions about misinformation in complainant's application, the agency sets forth no rationale as to why complainant was qualified at the GS-13 level, or not qualified at the GS-14 level. Furthermore, notwithstanding the agency's contentions to the contrary, the record reflects that most of the selections for the Position, in all locations, were predominately promotions, most of them to the GS-14 and GS-15 levels. Therefore, we conclude that the agency failed to show that despite the discrimination he suffered, complainant would have not have qualified at the GS-14 level for the Position, and would not have received this "promotion" upon selection. Additionally, we conclude that complainant would have been promoted to a GS-15 level upon completing one year at the GS-14, and that complainant is entitled to this remedy, along with any necessary training needed for her to successfully perform at the GS-15 level.

The agency contends that the Position was eliminated in March 2002, and that those who were not "deployed" to other positions were relegated to "single graded" Operation Specialist positions with no promotion potential. However, the agency presents no evidence to show what happened to the selectees in either the Nashville or Columbus Positions, and does not provide a copy of the Operation Specialist position, and does not indicate what is a "single grade" position. If the "single graded position" is a GS-13, then this position would not constitute a suitable remedy for complainant. It is incumbent upon the agency to make provision for the relief mandated by Title VII and the Rehabilitation Act. As the Supreme Court pointed out in Franks v. Bowman Transportation Co., Inc., 424 U.S. 747 (1976), the legislative history reflects that "courts are empowered to fashion such relief as the particular circumstances of a case may require to effect restitution...."

In conclusion, as set forth more fully in the ORDER below, we find that as a remedy in this case, complainant is entitled to a retroactive promotion, back to July 8, 1998, to a suitable GS-14 position, with back pay and interest, and retroactive promotion to a GS-15 level as of July 8, 1999, also with back pay and interest.

Citing to Mehta, the Commission in Alvaro P. v. Secretary of the Air Force, 2021004984 (2022), similarly discussed the agency's burden to establish that it offered the complainant a "substantially equivalent" position where the original position had been eliminated:

[T]he Agency in this case, has not established that Complainant is not entitled to a promotion to NH-IV as make-whole relief, merely because the promotion was not "automatic" for all other NH-III in the 21 CONS unit where he would have worked but for the discriminatory nonselection. Although the newly-created NH-IV positions were advertised agency-wide, the Agency's documentary evidence indicates that all of these positions were filled with personnel from 21 CONS. During the realignment, all of the NH-III Contract Specialists, in the 21 CONS unit where Complainant would have worked, were temporarily placed in the ESD Contract Specialist until the position could be permanently filled. Similar to the agency in Mehta, the Agency in this case offers no evidence to suggest that Complainant's performance as an NH-III, or in the temporary ESD position would have been unsatisfactory, or that at that point in his employment he would lack the necessary training and security requirements to qualify for conversion to NH-IV. Also, despite Complainant's email queries in the record, the Agency never addresses whether Complainant is qualified for the position, and the record lacks a position description for the NH-IV Contract Specialist within ESD (now SAM-D).
