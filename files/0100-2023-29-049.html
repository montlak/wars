4. Breach of Settlement Allegations

Where a breach allegation pursuant to an MSPB settlement agreement involves the same personnel action as an allegation of discrimination, the appellant may still have redress in the EEO arena where the Board's breach decision does not address all possible EEO remedies and/or does not involve discrimination. Britt v. Secretary of Defense, 01961237 (1997), involved a claim that the appellant was discriminatorily not selected for two positions. The appellant also claimed in an appeal to the MSPB that the agency violated a Board settlement agreement by not selecting him for the same two positions at issue in the instant EEO complaint. The Commission in Britt found that dismissal of the appellant's EEO complaint was improper:

The July 18, 1996 MSPB decision did not consider appellant's claims of race discrimination and retaliation. Although the MSPB found that the appellant had received a promotion to a GS-14 position, the MSPB did not consider whether appellant had been discriminated against by being initially denied the promotion to a GS-14 position. Thus, even if appellant were to be collaterally estopped from arguing that he was not promoted to a GS-14 position, appellant may still have been discriminated against and aggrieved if he were initially denied either of the two promotions at issue and then later promoted. In such circumstances appellant may be entitled to remedies, such as backpay or compensatory damages (among other remedies), that were not considered in the MSPB decision which merely examined whether the MSPB settlement agreement was breached.

The Commission finds that the MSPB's finding that a MSPB settlement agreement was not breached does not necessarily lead to the conclusion that appellant was not discriminated against in the selections at issue. Similarly, the MSPB's acceptance of the agency's argument that appellant's 120 day detail into the GS-201-14 Supervisory Personnel Management Specialist position satisfied the MSPB settlement agreement, does not necessarily lead to the conclusion that appellant was or was not discriminated against in the selections at issue.

The Commission finds that the instant matter of discrimination was not considered by the MSPB in its July 18, 1996 decision. Therefore, we find that even if the instant matter was initially a mixed case complaint or a mixed case appeal, it is certainly no longer a mixed complaint or mixed case appeal, and that the agency should process appellant's claim of discriminatory nonselections pursuant to § 1614.108.

In Henry v. Postmaster General, 01984732 (1999), the Commission determined that the complaint was not the subject of a prior Board appeal when the complainant alleged he was denied entry to the agency facility to retrieve personal belongings. The complainant filed a Board appeal over his termination and the case was settled with the complainant agreeing to voluntarily resign. The Commission found the agreement did not prohibit the complainant from entering the premises to retrieve personal belongings, and therefore his claim was distinct from that brought before the MSPB.
