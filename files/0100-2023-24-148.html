Y. REQUESTS FOR ASSISTANCE

An agency may not terminate the employment of a person with a disability because that person requests assistance in performing some job function without evaluating if the request is warranted. Rhodes v. USPS, 01841544 (1986). In that case, the agency was found to have discriminated against the appellant on a theory of disparate treatment because it had not terminated employees without disabilities for requesting assistance in unloading mail trucks. The Commission also found that the agency failed in its obligation to provide accommodation to the employee because providing such assistance, on the few occasions when it was necessary, did not place an undue burden on the agency.

In Raspanti v. Secretary of Army, 01942756 (1997), the Commission found that it did not impose an undue hardship on the agency to provide part-time assistance to a clerical worker for one or two days each quarter. The assistance was needed to help compile a quarterly report which caused the complainant discomfort because of the need for repetitive head movement, which impacted on a neck injury. The Commission found the provision of such assistance would not have imposed an undue hardship on the agency, particularly where the complainant's replacement was provided with such assistance.

When an accommodation involves requesting assistance from other employees as needed, the agency must make it possible for that assistance to be available. In Zobel v. Postmaster General, 07200500351 (2006), the Commission upheld an award of summary judgment where the complainant contended she was required to work outside of her medical restrictions. The Commission found that although the complainant was instructed not to work outside of her lifting restrictions and to get assistance with heavy items, that her placement at the "will call" window was not an effective accommodation. Often, when she needed assistance, other employees were busy and customers waiting for the complainant became irate. See also Heard v. Secretary of Treasury, 0120110751 (2013) (agency failed to accommodate complainant's lifting restriction by telling her to ask others for assistance where others were not always available).

The Rehabilitation Act and the ADA apply to the terms and conditions of employment, as well as personnel actions and reasonable accommodation in performance of essential job functions. As a result, an allegation that the agency has failed to provide reasonable accommodation by providing adequate assistance to an employee confined to a wheelchair in the event of a building evacuation states a claim. Dunwell v. Pension Benefits Guaranty Corporation, 0120081804 (2008). The Commission found that "delaying having and implementing an effective evacuation plan for complainant, who uses a wheelchair, would deny her equal benefits and privileges of employment as are enjoyed by similarly situated employees without disabilities who can be effectively evacuated on their own or using the agency's standard evacuation procedures." Id.
