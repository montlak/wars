HIRING

Finding and hiring employees used to be the job of the human resources office. But that's no longer the case. Hiring managers are much more involved in the process. You will now have to collaborate with HR when it comes to performing a job analysis, identifying needed competencies for the position, writing the position description, and interviewing top candidates.

According to an Office of Personnel Management official, hiring managers do not need to become "HR experts," but they do need "some basic HR knowledge," especially in areas such as conducting a job analysis, structured interviewing, available hiring authorities, and veterans' preference.

Job analysis

The Office of Personnel Management has urged hiring managers to collaborate more closely with HR staffing specialists in just about every area of recruiting and hiring, including the job analysis.

All federal employment practices, including the establishment of position descriptions, the announcement of vacancies, the assessment and rating of applicants, and the final selection must be based on a job analysis for each position (or group of positions), which identifies:

  1. 1. The basic duties and responsibilities.
  2. 2. The competencies or knowledge, skills, and abilities required to perform the duties and responsibilities.
  3. 3. The factors important in evaluating candidates.

A complete job analysis will also include consideration of:

  • • The tools required to do the job.
  • • Formal educational, certification or licensing requirements.
  • • The work environment.
  • • The relationship with other positions.
  • • The requirements needed to achieve the eventual target grade, if the position has one.
  • • The relative weight to be given to assessment factors.

Note: All requirements must be clearly relevant to the actual needs of the position and must not discriminate under EEO law.

So where is all this knowledge found, and how can you, as a hiring manager, contribute to the work of the staffing specialist? As the supervisor of the position, you should already know more about it than anyone else, except perhaps for experienced incumbents. You most likely know more than the staffing specialist about the actual requirements and duties, as opposed to those listed in a position description that may be years out of date.

Some positions, especially entry-level administrative jobs common across government, can be comfortably described with generic job analyses and position descriptions. But if the position you are looking to fill has exceptional requirements that need to be considered in a specialized job analysis, it is up to you to point this out to the staffing specialist.

The need for unique job analyses is more pressing when you are filling higher level and more specialized positions. In such cases, the staffing specialist may even convene a panel to conduct the analysis. As the hiring manager, you should be fully involved. The panel can analyze the way the job has changed in recent years, interview those who hold similar jobs, check out analyses successfully used for similar positions, and even look at predictable future requirements.

The results can be used to write a new and more accurate position description, develop a recruitment strategy, write the job announcement, and conduct a competition that will produce top candidates who are good fits for your real needs in the position. Your collaboration with the staffing specialist at every stage will make this positive result that much more likely.

Appointment types

Employees can be appointed under several different authorities. For the most part, the type of appointment used is determined by the type of agency and the nature of the position. For example, most positions within executive branch agencies are filled using competitive appointments.

Nonetheless, there is some flexibility available to managers in determining which type of appointment to use to fill a particular position.

Here are the types of appointments available:

  • • Competitive appointment. This is the type of appointment used for most positions in the executive branch. Candidates must compete for positions in accordance with requirements established by OPM.
  • • Excepted service appointment. This is the type of appointment used for most positions in legislative and judicial branch agencies, as well as for many executive and policymaking positions within the executive branch.
  • • Senior Executive Service. These are executive positions above the GS-15 level. The majority of appointees are drawn from the career service although some are political or excepted service appointees.
  • • Term appointments. Individuals may receive a term appointment for periods of more than one year, but no more than four years. Typically, these appointments are used to provide staffing for projects, surges in workload, uncertain funding situations, and the phasing out of operations.
  • • Term limited appointments. Individuals may be appointed for up to one year to deal with temporary workload or project situations. These appointments can be extended to a maximum of 24 months.

Selection under category rating

Agencies must provide for selection from among a larger number of qualified applicants by using the 'category rating' approach. Category rating, available to agencies since 2002, is governed by federal law. It replaces the older rule of three, in which candidates were rated numerically with qualified candidates eligible for veterans' preference awarded an additional five or 10 points. The hiring manager could select only from the top three candidates, whose names appeared on the certificate provided by HR. The newer system is designed to provide hiring managers with a greater range of choices while protecting veterans' preference and merit principles.

Under category rating, applicants are first rated by HR using job assessment tools chosen for the position based on the job analysis HR conducts jointly with the hiring manager. The candidates qualified for the position are then grouped in at least two categories under the system adopted by the agency, for example, "qualified" and "highly qualified." Agencies may adopt category rating systems with more than two categories of qualified applicants, for example, "qualified," "well-qualified" and "highly qualified."

A certificate of eligibles is then produced in which the qualified candidates are listed by category. Since eligibles do not receive numeric ratings, points cannot be added for preference eligibles as in numerical rating. Instead, preference eligibles generally are listed above non-preference eligibles in the category to which they were assigned based on the job assessment tools used.

However, all veterans with service-connected disabilities of at least 30 percent and those with at least 10 percent but less than 30 percent must be moved from the category to which they would otherwise be assigned to the top of the highest category with the important exception of scientific and professional positions at GS-9 and above. In those positions, they are assigned to the top of the category in which they are assessed to belong.

If the highest category contains less than three names, it is combined with the next highest to produce a single category.

The certificate should also list, above the categories, any eligibles entitled to career transition assistance or priority consideration.

Selection is the responsibility of the hiring manager (or a management selection panel, if one is used) and is made on the basis of the certificate received from the staffing specialist in HR.

When category rating is used, selecting officials choose from within the highest category of eligibles on the certificate (which should contain at least three names) unless they are required to select a candidate eligible for career transition assistance or entitled to priority consideration.

The hiring manager must select a preference eligible over a non-preference eligible within the highest category unless disqualification procedures are followed.

Disqualification of preference eligibles. If hiring managers pass over preference eligibles in favor of non-preference eligibles, they must follow specific procedures to disqualify them, depending on the circumstances.

  • • Medical disqualification. OPM must approve an agency justification to disqualify and pass over a preference eligible for medical reasons.
  • • Suitability disqualification. Agencies have delegated authority to make a negative suitability determination.
  • • Other reasons. OPM or an agency with delegated examining authority may disqualify a preference eligible for other reasons it considers sufficient.

Special provisions apply when the preference eligible is a veteran with a disability of 30 percent or more.

Hiring managers are not required to consider candidates who have been disqualified and passed over, with proper approval, three times or who have been considered three times for the same position (provided the candidate was in the top category).

Disqualification of veterans with disabilities of at least 30 percent. A veteran certified to have at least a 30 percent compensable, service-connected disability cannot be disqualified and passed over unless these additional provisions are applied:

  • • An agency that proposes to pass over the veteran to select a non-preference eligible or to disqualify the veteran based on the physical requirements of the position must simultaneously notify both OPM and the veteran of the reasons for the determination and of the veteran's right to respond to OPM within 15 days of the date of the notification.
  • • The agency must show OPM that the notice was timely sent to the veteran's last known address.
  • • OPM must make a determination of the veteran's physical ability to perform the duties of the position, taking into account any additional information provided by the veteran.
  • • OPM will notify the agency and the veteran of its decision, with which the agency must comply. If OPM agrees that the veteran cannot fulfill the physical requirements of the position, the agency may select another person from the certificate of eligibles. If it finds the veteran is able to perform the job, the agency may not pass over the veteran.
  • • OPM may not delegate this function.

Hiring veterans

Veterans' preference and special hiring authorities for veterans are important and sensitive areas of the hiring process. They guarantee certain veterans, including those with disabilities, preferred treatment in consideration for many federal job openings. Congress has been more watchful to ensure agencies are complying with the laws and regulations on veterans' preference in hiring. This means that you, as a selecting official, need to be familiar with veterans' preference and related rules and work with your HR office to make sure they are applied properly.

Note: Positions filled by promotion from within your agency, rather than through more general vacancy announcements, are usually not subject to veterans' preference. Be sure to consult your HR staffing specialists. Remember that it is HR's responsibility to establish who is eligible, not yours.

OPM has made it clear that veterans' preference still applies under category rating, but the focus is no longer on points. Instead, veterans automatically go to the top of the category in which they are placed and special rules apply for veterans with disabilities. An OPM spokesperson said HR will, however, still have to determine the type of preference to which a veteran is entitled, as that can be relevant to the category in which the veteran is placed and to the rules an agency must follow if it wants to select a nonveteran instead of a veteran.

Here, in a nutshell, is how veterans' preference works under category rating, according to OPM: Qualified applicants are initially assigned to a quality category (for example, best qualified and highly qualified) based on job-related assessments. Within the particular category, all preference eligibles (disabled, nondisabled, and derived) are listed ahead of non-preference eligibles. Unless the position being filled is a scientific or professional position, GS-9 or above, all disabled veterans are moved to the top quality category regardless of the quality category to which initially assigned. All applicants in the top quality category are referred to the selecting official, with preference eligibles listed before non-preference eligibles. A selecting official may select any preference eligible -- there is no required selection order. The same stringent standard for selecting a non-preference eligible over a preference eligible that applies under the rule of three also applies under category rating.

Certain jobs are reserved for preference eligibles and an agency may not consider or rate an application for the position of elevator operator, messenger, guard or custodian submitted by a non-preference eligible as long as at least three qualified preference eligibles are available for the position.

Special authorities

By law, agencies also required or permitted to use certain special hiring authorities for veterans:

  • • VRA authority. The Veterans Recruitment Appointment authority permits agencies to appoint certain veterans noncompetitively at their discretion to positions at any grade level through GS-11 or equivalent. VRA hires may be made even if the position allows for promotion beyond GS-11.
  • • VEOA authority. The Veterans Employment Opportunity Act of 1998 as amended provides that agencies must allow VEOA-eligible veterans (which include preference eligibles and those who have served for at least three years) to compete for agency merit promotion announcements open to candidates from outside the parent agency. Those selected receive career or career-conditional appointments.
  • • 30 percent disability authority. Agencies have the authority to give a temporary appointment of more than 60 days or a term appointment to most veterans with a disability rating of at least 30 percent. The agency may convert the temporary or term appointment to a career or career-conditional appointment at any time during the appointee's employment.
  • • Appointing from VA training programs for veterans with disabilities. Agencies may make agreements with the Department of Veterans Affairs to host vocational rehabilitation training for veterans with disabilities. These trainees are not considered to be federal employees for most purposes -- the training is a VA benefit. Veterans who successfully complete these programs, with certification, are then eligible for one year for a noncompetitive special tenure appointment by any agency. This may be converted to career conditional with OPM approval.

The rules governing these authorities are complex, so be sure to consult HR.

Appeal rights

Veterans who believe your agency has not properly considered their rights have several avenues of complaint, including the Department of Labor's Veterans Employment and Training Service, the Office of Special Counsel, the Office of Personnel Management, the Merit Systems Protection Board, the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, and the U.S. Court of Appeals, Federal Circuit. The avenue chosen will depend on the nature of the complaint. Generally, veterans may not simultaneously pursue a complaint in more than one venue.

Schedule A hiring authority

Schedule A is an excepted service hiring authority that can allow you to quickly hire people with disabilities and meet the government mandate to increase the representation of people with disabilities in the federal government. You can use this option regardless of whether the competitive process has already begun, and it can save you the time it takes to go through the typical recruitment and hiring process.

An agency may use Schedule A hiring authority to appoint an individual with a disability on a permanent, time-limited, or temporary basis, provided the candidate submits proof of disability. The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission urges agencies to make permanent appointments, unless there is a compelling reason for using time-limited or temporary appointments. Time-limited appointments should be used only when job duties do not require a permanently filled position.

Applicants with disabilities no longer need to supply a certification of job readiness. This is intended to speed the hiring process for agencies by removing an unnecessary burden on applicants with disabilities.

Conversion to competitive service

According to the EEOC, Schedule A is intended to help people with disabilities obtain competitive status in the civil service. An agency may convert the employee to competitive service after the individual completes two years in a permanent or time-limited appointment, with the discretion to credit time served under a temporary appointment toward the two-year requirement. The EEOC urges agencies to convert employees to permanent competitive status once the two years are completed.

Steps for using Schedule A

You should use the following steps recommended by the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission to hire a Schedule A applicant for the position:

  • • Notify your staffing specialist and the selective placement coordinator/disability program manager of the job opening, and explain the position's essential functions and competency requirements.
  • • Review the Schedule A applications that meet the job requirements, and work with HR to check references and conduct interviews. When interviewing candidates, focus on determining whether the candidate can perform the job's essential functions and meet the competency requirements. By law, you cannot ask disability-related questions during the selection process. If none of the Schedule A candidates meet the job requirements, you can still use the traditional competitive process.
  • • Make a selection and notify the appropriate individuals in HR and the SPC/DPM so they can extend a conditional employment offer, contingent upon receiving proof of disability. As the hiring manager, you should not be involved in obtaining this document or discussing medical and disability-related information with the selectee. HR and/or the SPC/DPM should work with the candidate regarding any medical examinations that might be required for the position.
  • • Establish a start date and work with HR and the SPC/DPM to coordinate setting up any accommodations the employee may need. To ensure the accommodation is in place before the start date, HR and/or the SPC/DPM should contact the selectee to determine what, if any, accommodation is needed.

Hiring students

Several programs can be used to hire students and recent graduates. The Pathways Programs are streamlined developmental programs tailored to promote employment opportunities for students and recent graduates in the federal workforce: the Internship Program for current students; the Recent Graduates Program for people who have recently graduated from qualifying educational institutions or programs; and the reinvigorated Presidential Management Fellows Program for people who obtained an advanced degree (e.g., graduate or professional degree) within the preceding two years. . There are also programs for recruiting cybersecurity or IT professionals, which you can learn more about on cybercareers.gov. Check with your HR staff for more information.

Criminal history inquiries

The Fair Chance Act prohibits agencies from making criminal history inquiries prior to making a conditional offer of employment to an applicant. This prohibition applies to executive agencies, the U.S. Postal Service, the Postal Regulatory Commission, and the Executive Office of the President.

The prohibition does not apply if consideration of criminal history record information prior to a conditional offer with respect to the position is otherwise required by law.

The prohibition does not apply with respect to an applicant for an appointment to a position:

  • • That requires a determination of eligibility for access to classified information, assignment to or retention in sensitive national security duties or positions, or acceptance or retention in the armed forces.
  • • As a law enforcement officer.
  • • Identified in OPM's regulations on this prohibition.

Agencies may make inquiries into an applicant's Selective Service registration, military service, citizenship status, or previous work history prior to making a conditional offer of employment to an applicant.

Reference checking

Many hiring managers leave out an essential step in making sure they get the best fit for the job in the employees they hire: They fail to check references by calling the applicants' current and former supervisors.

Reference checking has been shown to be a useful predictor of job performance (based on supervisory ratings), training success, promotion potential, and employee turnover. Adding structure to the reference checking process can greatly enhance its validity and usefulness as an employee selection procedure. Strategies for structuring reference checking include basing questions on a job analysis, asking applicants the same set of questions, and using standardized data collection and rating procedures.

Reference checking is usually conducted near the end of the selection process after the field of applicants has been narrowed to only a few competitors. Most reference checks are conducted by phone which allows the checker to collect reference data immediately and to probe for more detailed information when clarification is needed.

Reference checking is legal

Both the Merit Systems Protection Board and the Office of Personnel Management recommend that you check references and respond to similar calls you may get from hiring supervisors. The MSPB noted specifically in a 2005 report that, "So long as reference checkers focus their inquiries on job-related issues and treat all applicants equitably, reference checking is a legally defensible activity."

Providing accurate information when called as a reference for a former employee is equally important, but many employers refuse to give negative information about former employees, fearing a lawsuit for defamation. This is generally not deemed a serious problem for federal reference providers and reference checkers because of legal protections provided under the Federal Tort Claims Act.

However, you should check with HR to determine the policy in your own agency and to ask for help in determining the limits of what you may ask or answer.

Get a conversation going

The best approach is to call the current and recent former managers of a promising applicant and to ask questions designed to begin a productive conversation that will:

  • • Validate the applicant's claims of experience.
  • • Verify the rating on the latest performance appraisal.
  • • Provide evidence of reliability in attendance and performance.

The best questions are open-ended, rather than yes or no, and allow for follow-up questions. Prepare for a reference-checking call as you would for an applicant interview, reviewing the applicant's resume and writing questions down in advance.

Note: There are privacy issues and matters relating to the Family and Medical Leave Act that make it inadvisable to ask about sick leave usage when checking references. It is better to ask about unplanned annual leave. Employees who have sick leave usage issues are likely to also use a lot of unplanned annual leave, and you can ask about that.

Warning: Some current managers may be tempted to praise problem employees -- to the point of giving over-positive performance appraisals and stretching the truth when they respond to reference checks -- because that seems like the easiest way to make their difficult employees someone else's problem. For this reason and simply to get another point of view, it is worthwhile to go one manager back to get a response from someone who no longer has any reason to be other than objective.

Hiring offenses

The prohibited personnel practices include several hiring practices that violate merit system principles. To avoid a PPP complaint being filed against your agency, you need to understand what actions could trigger them during the hiring process. Make sure you consult with HR before engaging in actions that might improperly influence the selection of a candidate for a position.

Hiring offenses include:

  • • Deceiving or willfully obstructing a candidate's right to compete for employment.
  • • Influencing a candidate to withdraw from competition to improve or injure the employment prospects of another individual.
  • • Granting an unauthorized preference or advantage to improve or injure the prospects of any particular person for employment.
  • • Engaging in nepotism.
  • • Considering improper job references.
  • • Knowingly violating veterans' preference.

Preselection. Note that preselection -- acting upon a preconceived idea that a particular individual may be the best candidate for a particular position -- is not by itself prohibited by law. To violate the law, two elements must exist:

  • • A grant of some illegal advantage, and;
  • • An intentional and purposeful manipulation of the system to ensure that one person is favored and/or another is disadvantaged.

Also be aware that if a preselection is tainted by an unlawful discriminatory intent -- such as Title VII discrimination or military status discrimination prohibited by the Uniformed Services and Reemployment Rights Act -- it is prohibited. If you find yourself in a situation where there is potential for a finding of Title VII or military status discrimination, talk to HR about how to properly proceed.

Appearance of violation. You also need to avoid doing or saying things that make you look like you committed a hiring offense, even when you did not. Negligent or imprudent actions can create the appearance of a violation that can lead to complaints and investigations. For example, it would be irresponsible to broadcast your choice for a position before the competition for the job occurs.

Examples of hiring offenses. Here are some examples of improper hiring practices to give you a better idea of the types of actions you need to avoid:

  • • Deliberately failing to post a job vacancy to stop a particular candidate from applying.
  • • Deliberately misplacing or destroying an application.
  • • Encouraging a subordinate not to compete or to withdraw their application by promising future benefits that the supervisor does not intend to grant.
  • • Reopening a closed vacancy announcement to permit a favored candidate to apply.
  • • Manipulating job qualifications to favor a particular candidate.
  • • Telling a qualified employee not to apply for a job in order to improve another employee's chances of selection.

These more specific situations detail how hiring offenses may play out:

  • • A supervisor falsely rates a subordinate employee who is competing for a promotion as unsatisfactory because the supervisor knows that only employees rated satisfactory and above can obtain the promotion. This is an example of obstructing competition.
  • • A veteran is at the top of the list for hiring, which means they are blocking other candidates from being selected for this particular position. The selecting official, hoping to hire another applicant lower on the list, encourages the veteran to apply for another vacancy instead. This situation demonstrates how a manager may influence an individual to withdraw from competition.
  • • A selecting official hopes to hire an attorney they have known for many years into an investigator position. When the announcement is issued for the investigator position, the attorney does not qualify for the position. The selecting official then has the announcement rewritten to include legal requirements, such as legal analysis and litigation, even though this is not necessary for an investigator position. Ultimately the attorney is hired for the investigator position. This example shows how a candidate may be granted an unauthorized advantage.

Mistakes to avoid

Managers are more involved in the hiring process than ever before. While you don't need to know all facets, you must collaborate with HR for a comprehensive and effective hiring process.

Do not:

  • • Assume the responsibility to determine who is eligible. Determining position eligibility is a function of HR. Let them do their job.
  • • Take a passive approach to structuring job analysis. As the supervisor of the position, you should already know more about it than anyone else. You most likely know more than the staffing specialist about the actual requirements and duties, as opposed to those listed in a position description that may be outdated. Work with HR to accurately determine the requirements that are relevant to the actual needs of the position.
  • • "Wing it" during the interview. Use structured interviews for a fair and consistent process during which all candidates are asked the same questions in the same order, all candidates are evaluated using a common rating scale, and interviewers are in agreement on acceptable answers.
  • • Ask disability-related questions during the selection process. By law, you cannot ask these questions. If none of the Schedule A candidates meet the job requirements, you can still use the traditional competitive process.
  • • Hire without first checking the references of promising candidates. Consult with HR on your agency's policies.

Ask FMG

Q. What is the point of using an interview?

A. Properly used, interviews can serve several useful purposes. They can provide:

Information about the knowledge, skills, abilities, and level of experience of candidates.

An opportunity to ask questions to clarify or further develop items contained in candidates' applications.

An opportunity to pose typical work situations candidates could expect to encounter in the position.

Q. Do all interviews have to be identical?

A. No, that's a common misconception. For the sake of fairness, however, all interviewed candidates should be asked the same or very similar questions on key evaluation areas. Follow-up questions are almost bound to differ.

Q. What does "priority consideration" mean?

A. Priority consideration means you must seriously consider a candidate before other qualified applicants for a position. It does not mean that you must automatically select that candidate.

Q. Is it legal to check references with an applicant's current or former supervisors?

A. It is not only legal, it is recommended by the Merit Systems Protection Board and the Office of Personnel Management. The MSPB noted in a report that, "So long as reference checkers focus their inquiries on job-related issues and treat all applicants equitably, reference checking is a legally defensible activity." Check with HR on your agency's policy.

Q. Can I ask candidates to take a test?

A. Not on your own authority. OPM's regulations give OPM control over civil service competitive examinations. Anything not OPM-approved that requires candidates to write out answers to questions may cause problems. Even interview questions that ask for detailed knowledge of an area may fall under the prohibition. You can avoid this problem by asking candidates how they handled actual work situations. And you may ask candidates to give certain demonstrations of their abilities, such as formal briefings or troubleshooting malfunctioning equipment.

Q. When does it make sense to use a term appointment?

A. When you have a short-term project or a bulge in workload that is likely to be temporary. It can also make sense in special situations, such as phaseouts and shutdowns.

Q. Do I have to consider veterans' preference when filling all positions?

A. No. For example, in-house promotions generally don't require that veterans' preference be considered. HR will advise you.

Q. I've been told I should keep notes on the reasons for my selection decisions. Why should I do that?

A. Because you may have to explain your reasoning if a candidate files a grievance or appeal contesting the decision. Notes can prove quite helpful in refreshing your recollection several months down the road.
