15. Proving Substantial Limitation

In passing the ADAAA, Congress intended to take the focus in disability discrimination cases away from the previous extensive analysis of whether an individual was substantially limited and place the focus on whether an employer has met it is obligations under the ADA. Uncodified Purposes, Sec. 2(b)(5). However, Congress did not eliminate entirely the requirement that an impairment be substantially limiting to be considered a disability. Even with the Commission's new regulations, some degree of proof will be required and, therefore, a brief review of some past decisions by the Commission on that issue is warranted.

The Commission has held that a generalized allegation that a major life activity is affected by a medical condition is not sufficient to establish that the condition substantially limits a major life activity. The complainant must present specific evidence of the effect. For example, in Zeigler v. Postmaster General, 01930854 (1994), the Commission remanded a case for further evidence on the issue of the extent of the medical condition, noting:

The medical documentation fails to establish the impact of appellant's back condition on his major life activities. In describing his self-imposed limitations, appellant stated that he refrained from activities such as running, jumping, and sports. The Commission finds that these activities are not major life activities as contemplated by the regulations. At the present we only know that if appellant walks for an unspecified amount of time his condition will be aggravated. How long he must walk and to what extent his condition is aggravated is not defined in the record presently before the Commission and such information is necessary to the disposition of the present matter. There was also a reference to heel walking, however, heel walking was not defined.

Many complainants overlook the requirement that proving the existence of a medical condition does not equate with a finding that the complainant is a person with a disability. Once again, in Lohr v. Postmaster General, 05930799 (1994), the Commission observed that the complainant failed to submit specific evidence on how his condition substantially limited a major life activity:

[F]ollowing a thorough review of the record, the Commission finds that there is insufficient evidence in the record to support a finding that the symptoms of which appellant complained to her physicians and which have been diagnosed by them as CFS [chronic fatigue syndrome] rise to the level of a substantially limiting physical impairment. Accordingly, the Commission, on its own motion, is remanding this matter for further evidence as outlined below.

Physicians 2 and 3 both diagnosed appellant as having CFS based upon symptoms as reported by appellant which coincided with the published criteria, and a positive blood test for a certain virus which is an indicator for the condition. The physicians listed many symptoms, including significant fatigue, muscle pain and weakness, situational depression, allergic symptoms, short and long term memory defects, calculation impairment and spatial disorganization, chronic headache and sore throat, muscle weakness, symptoms of irritable bowel, and cardiac arrhythmia. However, the physicians did not indicate the extent or degree of these symptoms, other than that appellant reported experiencing them, or the specific manner in which the symptoms impacted or significantly affected appellant in her daily life. In addition, neither in her complaint nor in her affidavit does appellant herself specifically address the nature or severity of the impairment or the extent to which she was affected by this condition, merely stating, in pertinent part, that she had been diagnosed with CFS and needed to be assigned to a daytime shift. Most important is the lack of any evidence, either from appellant or her physicians, as to the degree to which appellant was affected by the reported symptoms, i.e., daily, regularly, or only occasionally. Other than the assertion by appellant and her physicians that she cannot work at night, they have not shown in what manner appellant was impaired; for example, appellant has not provided any examples of the manner in which she was impaired in various life activities at the time of the events at issue. For the above stated reasons, the Commission finds that the record is incomplete with regard to whether appellant's condition constituted a substantially limiting physical impairment. Accordingly, this matter is remanded to the agency to allow the parties to present additional evidence.

See also Jenkins v. Postmaster General, 01954572 (1997) (diagnosis of depression was insufficient where no evidence was submitted to show how the depression limited a major life activity).

In Taylor v. Principal Financial Group, Inc., 93 F.3d 155, 163-64 (5th Cir. 1996), the court explained how the same disability could substantially limit a major life activity for one employee but not another. In finding that the plaintiff in Taylor had articulated an impairment, but not demonstrated that it limited a major life activity, the court observed:

Under the ADA, an actionable disability means, in relevant part, a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more of the major life activities of an individual. 42 USC § 12102(2)(A). To prove discrimination, an employee must show that the employer knew, or should have known, of such employee's substantial physical or mental limitation. After thoroughly reviewing the record, we hold that Taylor has failed to adduce summary judgment evidence which would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find that Principal Mutual knew, or should have known, of any limitations a rising out of Taylor's alleged impairment.

The only relevant evidence offered by Taylor is his own deposition testimony stating that he told Matthews that he was "diagnosed" with bipolar disorder, and that he wanted Matthews to "investigate" the condition. The evidence does not show that Taylor ever told Matthews that he suffered a limitation as a result of his alleged impairment. To the contrary, when Matthews asked Taylor if Taylor was "all right," Taylor told Matthews that he was. For purposes of proving ADA discrimination, it is important to distinguish between an employer's knowledge of an employee's disability versus an employer's knowledge of any limitations experienced by the employee as a result of that disability. This distinction is important because the ADA requires employers to reasonably accommodate limitations, not disabilities. "The determination of whether an individual has a disability is not necessarily based on the name or diagnosis of the impairment the person has, but rather on the effect of that impairment on the life of the individual." 29 CFR 1630.2(j), App. (1995); 42 USC § 12112(a)(5)(A) ("[T]he term 'discriminate' includes...not making reasonable accommodations to the known physical or mental limitations of an otherwise qualified individual with a disability....") (emphasis added); 29 CFR 1630.9, App. (1995) ("Employers are obligated to make reasonable accommodations only to the physical or mental limitations resulting from the disability that is known to the employer.") (emphasis added).

To illustrate the distinction between a disability and a limitation resulting from a disability, consider the following hypothetical of two hearing-impaired employees: One hearing-impaired employee is an assembly worker who suffers no job limitations as a result of her hearing-impairment disability; she is able to perform the essential functions of her job without accommodation. The other hearing-impaired employee, however, is a telephone operator who, because of her inability to hear, is limited in her ability to perform the essential functions of her job; this disabled employee may require a reasonable accommodation as a result of her impairment. Both employees are disabled, but only one employee is limited by her disability.

As noted by the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission ("EEOC") in its interpretive guide to the ADA, "[s]ome impairments may be disabling for particular individuals but not for others, depending on the stage of the disease or the disorder, the presence of other impairments that combine to make the impairment disabling or any number of other factors." 29 CFR 1630.2(j), App. (1995). Thus, while a given disability may limit one employee (and therefore necessitate a reasonable accommodation), it may not limit another. For this reason, the ADA does not require an employer to assume that an employee with a disability suffers from a limitation. In fact, better public policy dictates the opposite presumption: that disabled employees are not limited in their abilities to adequately perform their jobs. Such a policy is supported by the EEOC's interpretive guide: employers "are prohibited from restricting the employment opportunities of qualified individuals with disabilities on the basis of stereo-types and myths about the individual's disability. Rather, the capabilities of qualified individuals must be determined on an individualized, case by case, basis." 29 CFR 1630.5, App. (1995). Accordingly, it is incumbent upon the ADA plaintiff to assert not only a disability, but also any limitation resulting therefrom. In the instant case, Taylor failed to adduce any summary judgment evidence showing that he told Principal Mutual that he was limited as a result of his alleged disability.

There is both an individualized and a generalized test to determine whether an individual is substantially limited in a major life activity. In the individualized part of the test, the complainant must show how his or her impairment limits a major life activity. This test takes into account the medical evidence that is specific to that complainant. In the generalized part of the test, the evidence specific to the complainant's ability to perform a major life activity is compared to the duration, manner or conditions under which the average person in the general population can perform that same activity. The basic method of determining substantial limitation is not likely to change under the ADAAA. What will change is the degree of limitation that an individual needs to show to demonstrate that it is substantial.

Although the complainant has the burden of demonstrating that he is substantially limited in a major life activity, the agency has an obligation to develop a full investigatory record that includes collecting appropriate information on the complainant's specific limitations. See Sweeney v. Postmaster General, 0120060418 (2007) (remanding case to agency for further investigation on issue of substantial limitation).
