B. COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS

When a complainant alleges disparate treatment, as part of its obligation to conduct an investigation, the agency must make a comparative analysis of the treatment afforded the complainant and the treatment of other employees in the same organizational segment. Failure to make such an analysis is grounds for remand to the investigative stage. Foat v. USPS, 01830984 (1983). With the advent of discovery in the hearing stage and the elimination of investigative remands at that stage, the Commission would not remand a complaint for further investigation where the comparative analysis is omitted. However, the Commission would likely continue to follow the Foat case in situations where the complainant elects to proceed to a final agency decision without a hearing. Moreover, the Foat case gives some insight as to when an agency may be subject to sanctions for an incomplete investigation at the hearing stage.

Under Part 1614 and MD-110, an agency still is obligated to collect evidence relating to comparatives as part of its investigation. In Houston v. Postmaster General, 01933377 (1994), the Commission explained this obligation. Note that Houston was issued prior to the 1999 update of MD-110, so the decision's citations correspond to the 1992 version:

EEOC Regulation 29 CFR § 1614.108 requires the agency to develop a complete and impartial factual record upon which to make findings on the matters raised in an EEO complaint in accordance with instructions contained in the EEOC's Management Directives. EEOC Management Directive (MD) 110 indicates that the purposes of an investigation are (1) to gather facts upon which to base a determination as to whether an agency has violated a provision of a law enforced by the EEOC and (2) if a violation is found, to have a sufficient factual basis from which to fashion an appropriate remedy. See EEOC MD-110, Chapter 5, Part IV.B. EEOC MD-110 requires an agency to include in its investigation a thorough review of the circumstances under which the alleged discrimination occurred, the treatment of members of the complaint's group as compared with the treatment of other similarly situated employees, and any policies and/or practices which may constitute or appear to constitute discrimination, even though they have not been expressly cited by the complainant. See EEOC MD-110, Chapter 5, Part IV.C.

EEOC MD-110 specifically requires an agency to seek comparative evidence in every case alleging disparity in treatment, including evidence sufficient to determine whether the persons who are being compared are so similarly situated that it is reasonable to expect that they would receive the same treatment. EEOC MD-110 emphasizes that an agency investigator is obligated to find out whether there were similarly situated individuals whose treatment could be compared to the complainant's treatment, even if such individuals were not named by the complainant. See EEOC MD-110, Chapter 5, Part VI.B.1.

In a nonselection case, the agency must include the qualifications of the complainant and the selectee, as well as the pertinent information regarding the selectee's race, sex, etc. See Williams v. Postmaster General, 01952620 (1997). Finding the report of investigation inadequate to determine whether discrimination occurred, the Commission in Byron E. v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 0120140939 (2016), also a nonselection case, noted that investigators are obligated to include a "thorough review of the circumstances under which the alleged discrimination occurred, the treatment of members of a complainant's group as compared with the treatment of other similarly situated employees, if any." Evidence that should have been in the record, according to the Commission in Byron E., included any profiles or demographic information regarding positions held at that facility; demographic information regarding previous selections by the selecting official; information concerning previous selections by the other panelists; organizational charts providing demographic information concerning employees at the facility; a detailed account of how the scoring system worked and how scores were determined; what interview questions were used and whether those questions were the same or similar to interview questions used for the same or similar positions or whether the selecting official deviated from interview questions which he may have used in the past; and the weight given to each component of consideration (e.g., interviews, screening questions, resumes).
