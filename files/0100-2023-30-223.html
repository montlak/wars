A. DOCTRINE OF LACHES

For those who have endured the EEO process long enough to actually receive an order for relief, it may seem ironic that an EEO complainant must quickly assert his or her enforcement rights if relief does not appear to be forthcoming. Under 29 CFR 1614.504(a), a complainant is required to notify an agency within 30 days of the date he or she knows, or should have known, of any alleged noncompliance. Although under Part 1613 there was no specific time limit for an agency to comply with orders for relief, the doctrine of laches applies to complainants who wish to enforce final agency decisions issued prior to October 1, 1992, when Part 1614 took effect. In Childress v. Secretary of Navy, 01952311 (1997), the Commission observed:

In the instant case, the agency notified the appellant that her promotion would be temporary in May and June of 1988, as indicated by the "cc" on the agency's May 19, 1988 memorandum to the Commanding Officer and by the Form 50s "Notification of Personnel Action." Yet the appellant did not notify the agency in writing of her belief that the agency had not complied with its 1988 final agency decision until April 4, 1994, almost 4 years after she learned that her retroactive promotion would be temporary. The appellant has not offered any explanation for not notifying the agency of her noncompliance claim as soon as she learned that the promotion would be temporary. The Commission also observes that the appellant did not even notify the agency of the alleged noncompliance for more than 2 and 1/2 years after the effective date of EEOC Regulation 29 CFR § 1614.504(a) which requires notice within 30 calendar days of the date the appellant knew or should have known of the alleged noncompliance. Under these circumstances, the Commission finds that the appellant's noncompliance claim is barred from further consideration by the doctrine of laches.
