42. Undisputed Amounts

An agency may not hold up an entire damage award because it still requires evidence on some portion of the award. For example, in DiCamillo v. Secretary of Treasury, 04990008 (2000), the Commission ordered the reinstatement of the complainant provided she had recovered sufficiently to work. The complainant declined, however, to present medical documentation to the agency on her recovery because the agency would not tell her what positions it was considering for reinstatement. The Commission found the complainant could submit documentation on her condition and limitations without knowing the positions. The Commission also noted the agency could not determine any amount of future pecuniary damages until it received this information. But, it found the agency did not need the information to issue an award of emotional distress damages and ordered the agency to pay the undisputed amount of any damage claim within 60 days.

In Taber v. Postmaster General, 0120083624 (2012), the Commission rejected the agency's argument of accord and satisfaction where the administrative judge, without holding a damages hearing, ordered $2,000 in compensatory damages and the agency paid that amount prior to taking final action on the judge's decision. The complainant then filed an appeal when the agency did implement the judge's decision arguing that he should have been entitled to a hearing. The agency responded that under the doctrine of accord and satisfaction the complainant's acceptance of the $2,000 barred his appeal. The Commission rejected that argument in Taber finding:

The Agency argues that Complainant's acceptance of the proceeds of the check constitutes an accord and satisfaction, or some variation of that doctrine, and therefore Complainant must be deemed to have waived his right to appeal. However, an accord and satisfaction arises only where there is a bona fide dispute over the amount at issue and the parties clearly evidence their intention that the dispute will be resolved by the payee's acceptance of the payment. Chesapeake & Potomac Telephone Company of Virginia v. United States, 654 F.2d 711, 716 (Ct. Cl. 1981) ("...merely giving a check for less than the amount claimed is not sufficient to create an accord and satisfaction. There must also be accompanying expressions clearly indicating the debtor's intention that the cashing of the check is to operate as a settlement in full.").

Here there was no dispute that the Agency owed Complainant at least $2,000 in that the Agency declined to appeal and instead fully implemented the AJ's decision. For that reason the $2,000 payment did not constitute consideration for the purported accord. In addition, there is no indication, either explicit or implicit, that both parties intended the payment to fully resolve this case.

The Commission then awarded $10,000 in damages based on written submissions submitted to the administrative judge.
