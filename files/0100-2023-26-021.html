broida eeo Chapter 17: Reprisal Chapter 17: Reprisal, Subchapter 02: Protected Activity--Participation Clause, Section K: Grievances

K. GRIEVANCES

"The prohibition against retaliation does not merely protect activity aimed at setting in motion the Title VII machinery. An employee is engaged in protected activity if he has opposed any practice made unlawful by Title VII." Rice v. Department of Health and Human Services, 01830492 (1984). If a grievance involves matters of discrimination covered by Title VII, retaliation for filing the grievance can be within the EEO complaint purview. See also Forest v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 02920021 (1992) (claiming discrimination under Title VII and Rehabilitation Act in a grievance procedure is a protected activity). But see Warren v. Department of Navy, 01822305 (1983) (filing a grievance is not a protected activity even if it concerns a coworker's use of terms with adverse racial connotations); cf. Hodges-Jenkins v. National Aeronautics and Space Administration, 01970354 (1997) (complaining of a violation of EEO law in a grievance is a protected activity). Properly analyzed, a claim of reprisal for pursuing discriminatory employment practices through a grievance process constitutes participation if the grievance process permits allegations of discrimination. If the grievance process does not permit allegations of discrimination, the filing of a grievance alleging discrimination constitutes opposition to an unlawful employment practice under Title VII.

The protection also extends to union officials who represent coworkers in grievances that raise Title VII allegations. See In re Milhado, 05870174 (1987). When a union steward pursues a grievance on behalf of an employee who alleges discrimination, whether such representation constitutes opposition activity depends on whether the steward reasonably believes that the action is discriminatory. See Hall v. Postmaster General, 07A20136 (2003).

Whether a grievance raises an issue of discrimination and, thus, entitles an employee to pursue administrative EEO remedies for alleged retaliation is a question of fact. As such, the agency cannot dismiss such a claim prior to investigation because the dismissal amounts to a decision on the merits. As the Commission explained in Staley v. Director, Federal Emergency Management Agency, 05920763 (1993):

The Commission notes that it is reopening the previous decision on very narrow grounds, in order to address the finding that the agency's rejection of appellant's reprisal allegations was proper. The Commission finds that the previous decision improperly affirmed the agency's rejection of appellant's allegations of discrimination on the basis of reprisal. The agency's decision determined that appellant's prior protected EEO activity, which was listed on her complaint form as involving the grievance of her rating, did not fall within the definition of prior EEO activity. However, the Commission finds that the agency, in effect, was making a determination on the merits of appellant's reprisal allegations by concluding that she had not engaged in activity constituting protected EEO activity. While appellant may not have previously filed an EEO complaint, the record is insufficient, without the benefit of an investigation, to determine that the grievance mentioned in appellant's complaint, or potentially another grievance or internal administrative appeal, had not raised allegations of discrimination sufficient to permit appellant to allege reprisal. Therefore, the agency's rejection of the basis of reprisal at this stage in the processing of appellant's complaint was improper....

If the negotiated grievance process provides for handling allegations of discrimination, then even the initiation of an informal grievance under the process is protected activity. Anderson v. Secretary of Interior, 01954805 (1996).

If the negotiated grievance process does not provide for processing allegations of discrimination, then a union representative cannot claim reprisal for engaging in protected activity by representing grievants. However, that does not preclude a claim from a female union representative that she is treated more harshly by the agency than her male counterpart. The distinction was explained by the Commission in Mitchell v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01951941 (1996):

[A]n agency shall dismiss a complaint that fails to state a claim under § 1614.103. Appellant has alleged that because of her participation in the EEO process of her coworkers, as their union steward, she (a female union representative) has been subjected to harassment and a hostile work environment. Appellant has further alleged that while her comments and actions were branded by the agency as agitating, the agency identified as acceptable the "pithy" comments of a male union representative. Moreover, on the instant complaint (third complaint) appellant alleged that because she had filed two previous formal discrimination complaints she was subjected to reprisal. The agency claims that appellant's allegations do not concern discrimination against her as an agency employee because these allegations concern her status as a union representative. We disagree. The Commission has consistently held that a reprisal allegation based on union activity, where EEO matters are not part of the union process fails to state a claim under Title VII. See Leary v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Petition No. 03920075 (February 19, 1993).

Nevertheless, if a complainant charges that interference with his or her union activities was based on animus for a protected group under Title VII (for example, females) then a claim has been stated. See Lowery v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05890918 (October 20, 1989)....

The distinction, therefore, is that the basis for discrimination in Mitchell was gender rather than retaliation.
