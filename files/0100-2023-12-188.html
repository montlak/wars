3. Epithets vs. Derogatory Remarks

While, perhaps, a subset of derogatory remarks, the Commission is less tolerant of name-calling, particularly when the language used has historically discriminatory roots. The Commission has demonstrated extreme sensitivity to use of the racial epithet "nigger," because it is a "highly charged epithet" which "dredge[s] up the entire history of racial discrimination in this country." Brooks v. Secretary of Navy, 05950484 (1996). In Brooks the Commission provided the following analysis:

This case rests solely on the words and communicative gestures by Team Leader 1, and does not involve a traditional adverse employment decision, such as a nonselection, discipline, or a poor performance review. However, it is well-established that if sufficiently severe or pervasive, the use of racial epithets or slurs in the workplace may constitute harassment based on race and violate Title VII. The Commission has held that, under certain circumstances, a limited number of highly offensive slurs or comments about a federal employee's race or national origin may in fact state a claim or support a finding of discrimination under Title VII. See Yabuki v. Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05920778 (June 4, 1993).

In Yabuki, the complainant charged discrimination based on race (Asian), color (yellow), and national origin (Japanese), when his supervisor made two highly offensive remarks about the Japanese people in his presence. Specifically, the supervisor in Yabuki stood at the watercooler talking to another employee, remarking disparagingly that soon the Japanese people would own the country. When the supervisor noticed the complainant, he pointed his finger in appellant's face, declaring insultingly that "it was because of [complainant]." According to the initial appeal decision in Yabuki, EEOC Appeal No. 01920172 (May 7, 1992), even if the remark at the watercooler was "anti-Japanese," it was at most a stray comment, unaccompanied by concrete action, and as such did not constitute a direct or personal deprivation for the appellant. In its decision on reconsideration, the Commission expressly overruled the previous appeal decision, and found that the appellant was discriminated against with respect to his race and national origin when he was subjected to verbal abuse and negative comments about Japanese people by his supervisor.

Similarly, in Gamboa v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05890633 (August 31, 1989), the Commission held that a single incident involving conduct and two remarks, taking place in a public forum, stated a claim under the Rehabilitation Act for disability discrimination. In Gamboa, during a group safety meeting with other employees present, complainant's supervisor was speaking. Complainant, who was hearing impaired, requested that her supervisor turn and face her so she could read his lips. Instead of doing so, her supervisor harshly ordered her to move to another area. Humiliated, complainant did so, but once she moved, her supervisor told her to move again.

Complainant claimed disability discrimination, stating that the statement and conduct of her supervisor brought negative attention to her hearing impairment and she suffered distress and humiliation as a result. In response to the agency's argument that hurt feelings are insufficient to invoke the EEO process, the Commission stated that the agency's argument reflected an "inadequate understanding" of the laws prohibiting disability discrimination, for the goal of the Rehabilitation Act is to ensure that disabled persons are not unfairly prevented from entering into the mainstream of society through employment. The Commission stressed that "[d]erogatory remarks that cause [an individual with a disability] injury clearly cannot be tolerated if [disabled] individuals are to be afforded equal opportunity in the workplace."

Likewise, a federal court held that a Black plaintiff-employee had sufficiently alleged race discrimination under Title VII and, in addition, that the plaintiff had adequately alleged a constructive discharge where, during one workday, a supervisor told plaintiff in front of other employees that "all you niggers are alike"; and in private called him a "nigger," and informed him that he was not a human being, he was a "nigger." Bailey v. Binvon, 583 F. Supp. 923, 925 (N.D. Ill. 1984). The plaintiff left work that day and did not return. When the defendant employer made a motion to dismiss the plaintiff's complaint for failure to state a claim under Title VII, the court denied the motion, refusing to characterize the supervisor's alleged remarks as "mere insults" or "even as 'ordinary' discrimination." Id. at 934. According to the Bailey court, such deplorable comments were "qualitatively different" for they "dredge up the entire history of racial discrimination in this country." Id. The court found the case particularly compelling because of the supervisor's comment distinguishing between "human beings" and "niggers." Id.

We find that the previous decision mischaracterized the allegations here as merely "two racially offensive statements over a two month period." Instead, we conclude that the allegations made were that Team Leader 1, who had a known history of making racially derogatory slurs and comments, made at least two such comments to appellant on March 1, 1994, one of which was to attribute negative qualities to Blacks and one of which was to use the highly-charged epithet, "nigger." Just two months later, on May 2, 1994, it is alleged that Team Leader 1 made a third strikingly demeaning racial comment to appellant, in the presence of other employees, by stating that a black rat--a rodent often attributed with unpleasant and unflattering characteristics--looked like appellant's relatives. Appellant's allegations must furthermore be viewed in the context of the particular workplace at issue, where it is alleged (with support from the record) that there is an unseemly recent history of racial abuse, hostility, and discrimination directed at Black civilian employees, including pervasive use of racial slurs, physical attacks, and other blatant demonstrations of bigotry, such as an at-work celebration of "James Earl Ray Day."

In light of the nature of the comments made, viewed in context of the totality of the evidence of the not yet fully developed record, as well as Commission precedent, we conclude that the appellant's allegations are sufficient to state a claim and should not have been dismissed under 29 CFR § 1614.107(a). Accordingly, we must remand the complaint to the agency for further processing.

By contrast, the Commission in Martin v. Attorney General, 01965338 (1998), found that comments regarding the complainant's "white ass" were not sufficiently racially charged to state a claim of harassment. The Commission specifically distinguished Martin from Brooks based on the existence of a supervisor with a known history of making racially derogatory comments:

Although the Commission views with gravity cases such as the one at bar, involving allegations of race-based harassment, especially accompanied by alleged threats of physical assault, we find, for the reasons set forth below, that the agency properly dismissed appellant's complaint.

...

In the present case, we find, from a fair reading of appellant's complaint, that CM harassed him based on race, thereby creating a hostile work environment that caused him humiliation and adversely affected his reputations. However, accepting the truth of appellant's allegations as we must in our analysis of the viability of his claim, we find appellant's allegations are not sufficient to state a claim of a hostile or abusive work environment.

...

In Brooks v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No, 05950484 (June 25, 1996), the Commission stated that "it is well-established that if sufficiently severe or pervasive, the use of racial epithets or slurs in the workplace may constitute harassment based on race and violate Title VII. The Commission has held that, under certain circumstances, a limited number of highly offensive slurs or comments about a federal employee's race or national origin may in fact state a claim or support a finding of discrimination under Title VII. See Yakubi v. Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05920778 (June 4, 1993)." In Yakubi supra, the Commission found in favor of a complainant who had alleged that his supervisor had informed another employee that the Japanese people would soon own the country, and, pointing his finger in the complainant's face, blamed the complainant.

In Brooks supra, the Commission found actionable comments, allegedly uttered two months apart, by an acting team leader ("Team Leader 1") who had called the complainant "a [racial epithet]," and who, in front of other employees, pointed to a dead black rat in the trash, and told the complaint: "That's one of your relatives," We concluded "the allegations made were that Team Leader 1, who had a known history of making racially derogatory slurs and comments, made at least two such comments to appellant on March 1, 1994, one of which was to attribute negative qualities to Blacks and one of which was to use the highly-charged epithet,...." Id. (footnote omitted). We further stated that the allegations of the complainant in Brooks supra "must furthermore be viewed in the context of the particular workplace at issue, where it is alleged (with support from the record) that there is an unseemly recent history of racial abuse, hostility, and discrimination directed at Black civilian employees, including pervasive use of racial slurs, physical attacks, and other blatant demonstrations of bigotry...."

In the present case, given the nature of appellant's allegations and his own statements in support thereof, and our review of the record as a whole, we find appellant has failed to show that he was harmed by an agency action that affected a term, condition, or privilege of employment. We further find, as we indicated above, that appellant's allegations do not rise to the level of an objectively hostile or abusive work environment. We find the conduct and/or remarks complained of, albeit offensive and improper in the workplace, if in fact true, neither sufficiently severe nor pervasive as to alter the conditions of appellant's employment.

Arguably, there is a double standard being enunciated in Brooks and Martin. But, the standard is one that must take into account the totality of the circumstances. Brooks, supra. The "history of racial discrimination in this country" is one of those circumstances. Viewed in that light, it is not the standard that changes depending upon the color or national origin of the victim, but rather the circumstances that make up the whole picture that change.

In Mauricio C. v. Postmaster General, 0120180873 (2018), the following allegations stated a claim of harassment: "I'm being targeted and harassed by someone or people at work with racial slurs. Someone or people are writing Porch monkey by my name on the work schedule posted outside of the MVS office. My life is being threaten[ed] and management is not taking these ongoing threats serious and is doing anything to address my safety concerns."

An allegation that an employee was called a "bitch" several times at a single meeting neither asserted a pattern of conduct showing harassment nor did this incident result in a direct or personal deprivation sufficient to render the employee aggrieved. See Kalm v. Secretary of Navy, 01966107 (1997).
