23. Forced Leave

Placement on administrative leave for a short period of time does not state a claim for relief, but long periods of forced leave may state a viable claim. In Randy G. v. Postmaster General, 0120161627 (2016), the agency placed the complainant on three months administrative leave while the complainant was under investigation for alleged harassment. The complainant believed that the agency's decision to place him on forced leave was discriminatory. The agency dismissed his complaint for failure to state a claim. Reversing the agency's dismissal, the Commission held:

Regarding issue 2, in her report the EEO counselor wrote that an Agency Acting Manager of Labor Relations relayed that Complainant reported that he was harassed by the Maintenance Manager (Lead). The EEO counselor wrote that in response to this the Human Resources Manager sent a team to investigate the allegations, and Complainant was placed on administrative leave pending the outcome of the investigation. He was on administrative leave for over three months. In August 2015, Complainant previously initiated another EEO complaint where he named the Maintenance Manager (Lead) as a responsible management official.

The Commission acknowledges that under certain circumstances, placement on paid administrative leave for a brief period does not state a justiciable claim. But given that this was a long period of forced paid administrative leave, and under the circumstances presented here, issue 2 states a claim and would reasonably likely deter protected EEO activity. Watt v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 0120082308 (June 26, 2008) (complainant placed on extended paid administrative leave for an indefinite period pending an investigation regarding a charge of serious misconduct. This stated a claim of a personal loss or harm regarding a term, condition, or privilege of employment); Davis v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal Nos. 01A60399 & 01A60401 (April 13, 2006) (complainant placed on paid administrative leave for at least two months pending an investigation into her allegation that she had been assaulted by another postal employee this stated a claim). Accordingly, the Agency's dismissal of issue 2 is reversed.

See also Jonathan V. v. Postmaster General, 0120181020 (2018) (finding month-long period of forced paid administrative leave states a claim where the complainant claimed that he was financially harmed during this period because he was deprived of overtime pay for at least 25 days); Allan L. v. Postmaster General, 0120180604 (2018) (placement on paid administrative leave for more than a year rendered the complainant aggrieved).

The Commission in Josephine S. v. Postmaster General, 2020005496 (2020), the complainant alleged that the agency refused her requests to return to work after a fitness-for-duty exam. The agency dismissed the complaint, finding that the complainant was on paid administrative leave and, therefore, not aggrieved. The Commission in Josephine S. disagreed:

The Agency improperly dismissed the complaint for failure to state a claim. In its dismissal decision, the Agency asserts that Complainant is not "aggrieved" because she was on paid administrative leave when not permitted to return to work. However, we have previously found that a complainant is aggrieved even when placed on a paid administrative leave when that leave is of a lengthy indefinite nature such as the circumstances at issue here. See Watt v. Dep't of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 0120082308 (June 26, 2008) (reversing dismissal of claim where placement on paid administrative leave was for an indefinite time period). We further note that one of the bases for Complainant's claim is reprisal and that, for retaliation claims, adverse actions need not qualify as "ultimate employment actions" or materially affect the terms and conditions of employment to state a claim. In accordance with Commission policy, reprisal claims are considered with a "broad view of coverage" so that a complainant is protected from any actions that a reasonable employee would find materially adverse and are reasonably likely to deter protected activity. Burlington Northern & Santa Fe Railroad. Co. v. White, 548 U.S. 53, 126 S. Ct. 2405 (2006). See also EEOC Enforcement Guidance on Retaliation and Related Issues, No. 915.004 (August 25, 2016); Carroll v. Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05970939 (April 4, 2000). Complainant's present claim falls within these parameters and, as such, states a viable claim.

We REVERSE the Agency's final decision dismissing Complainant's formal complaint, defined herein as a harassment claim, and we REMAND this matter to the Agency for further processing in accordance with the ORDER below.
