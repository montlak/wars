C. VIOLATIONS OF AFFIRMATIVE ACTION PLANS

While the existence of an affirmative action plan can provide a defense to agencies against claims of illegal discrimination, the converse is not true. An employee who alleges that he was discriminated against when the agency failed to follow its own affirmative action plan has not stated a claim cognizable under Title VII. In Piper v. Secretary of Treasury, 01892660 (1989), the Commission held:

In its final decision, the agency rejected the portion of appellant's complaint as it related to affirmative action. More specifically, the agency cited Mahoharan v. Columbia College, CA2, 46 FEP Cases 429 (1986) and Liao v. TVA, 49 FEP Cases 441 (1989). These cases held that "even if the affirmative action plan did create an implied obligation to give protection to minorities and women in termination decisions as well as in hiring and promotion, its violation alone is not enough to trigger a Title VII violation." 49 FEP at 443.

In his appeal, appellant contends that Mahoharan and Liao are not applicable to his particular case. He argues that Johnson v. Transportation Agency, 480 U.S. 616, 107 S. Ct. 1442 (1987) and Steel Workers v. Weber, 443 U.S. 193, 99 S. Ct. 2721 (1979) are more applicable to the facts of his case. However, these cases merely hold that an employer may select a member of a protected group over an equally qualified person (not in the selected group) in order to develop a minority-balance within the agency without being inconsistent with the mandates of Title VII.

We find that Liao is applicable to the facts of appellant's case. More specifically, we find that whether the agency's decision not to promote appellant is in violation of the agency's Affirmative Action program is not an issue addressable by Title VII.

The Commission also noted in Harris v. Secretary of Navy, 01892656 (1989):

Concerning this matter, the Commission notes, however, that EEOC Regulation 29 CFR § 1608.1 Statement of Purpose, declares that one of the functions of Affirmative Action Program, as authorized by Title VII, is to provide legal protection against so called "reverse discrimination" suits and that such Affirmative Action Programs on the part of employers are strictly voluntary. Such being the case, the EEO administrative process does not have the authority to provide any enforcement mechanism to ensure that an agency affirmative action program is actually carried out. Absent a showing of individual discrimination, there is no Title VII cause of action for an agency's failure to implement or utilize an affirmative action plan. Ferguson v. Veterans Administration, 723 F.2d 871, 872 (11th Cir. 1984). . . .
