1. Apologies

Apologies, usually lukewarm, sometimes find their way into settlement agreements. Generally speaking, an agency will not agree to apologize for discriminatory conduct, but may agree to apologize for any "inconvenience" caused by "misunderstanding," or similar words to that effect. Whatever value any apology may have often is undermined by lack of sincerity--a sentiment that usually is outside of the powers of the parties negotiating. Occasionally, the Commission finds an agency has breached provisions of a settlement agreement calling for an apology.

For example, in Parlato v. Secretary of Treasury, 01964108 (1996), the complainant alleged breach of a settlement agreement providing for an apology for derogatory statements, including allegations of sexual misconduct. The complainant alleged the agency breached the agreement because the offender did not apologize for all of the conduct specified in the agreement and also because subsequent to the settlement, the offender repeated the allegations of sexual misconduct in an affidavit given in an MSPB proceeding. In part, the Commission based its finding of breach on the first claim on the demonstrated lack of sincerity of the apology. However, the Commission found the agency had not breached the agreement on the latter allegation because the agency had no control over what the offender said to the MSPB. As the Commission in Parlato noted:

Appellant has alleged that the agency breached two provisions of the settlement agreement, provisions 1A and 1E. Appellant stated that provision 1A was breached in that she never received a letter of apology meeting the terms of the agreement. According to appellant, she was assured that in the letter she would receive pursuant to the settlement agreement, Person A would admit that he told "lies" about her and would acknowledge the potential harm of such actions. Appellant stated that, instead, Person A merely stated, in his letter dated August 14, 1992, that he "regretted" the interpretation that was given his remarks. Appellant pointed out that Person A did not address the statements made in his affidavit of June 23, 1992. Appellant also alleged that provision 1E of the settlement agreement was breached when, in preparation for an MSPB hearing, Person A prepared an affidavit stating that he was the victim of whistleblowing and that he was retaliated against for exposing an "affair" between appellant and Person B. We find that the agency breached provision 1A of the settlement agreement. Appellant received a letter from Person A dated August 14, 1992, but that letter did not satisfy the terms of the settlement agreement. Pursuant to provision 1A, Person A was required to write a letter to appellant apologizing for any derogatory remarks he made regarding her in January 1992 and for the statement he made in his affidavit dated June 23, 1992, concerning alleged sexual misconduct between appellant and Person B. The record indicates, however, that the letter that appellant received did not address Person A's affidavit of June 23, 1992.6 We also note that Person A was required to apologize to appellant for remarks he made regarding alleged sexual misconduct between appellant and Person B. The record indicates that after he submitted his letter to appellant, purporting to apologize for his remarks about her, Person A again raised allegations of sexual misconduct between appellant and Person B when he prepared an affidavit for an MSPB hearing. By raising such allegations in his affidavit for an MSPB hearing, we find that the sincerity of Person A's earlier letter to appellant is clearly called into question. Therefore, we find that provision 1A of the settlement agreement was breached.

We find that the agency did not breach provision 1E of the settlement agreement. Pursuant to provision 1E, the agency was required to ensure that Person A refrained from making any derogatory comments to or about appellant. Appellant stated that Person A completed an affidavit for an MSPB hearing and again raised an allegation of sexual misconduct between appellant and Person B. Appellant also indicated that the agency settled its dispute with Person A, and she was never allowed to testify at the hearing and respond to Person A's allegations. Here, the record indicates that Person A made statements about appellant in an affidavit for the MSPB. The agency had no control over statements made by Person A in an affidavit for the MSPB. We find that the agency's obligation to ensure that Person A refrained from making any derogatory comments to or about appellant only applied in the work place. Therefore, the agency did not breach provision 1E of the settlement agreement. Based on the circumstances in this case, we find that the appropriate remedy would be for the agency to implement the terms of the settlement agreement. Therefore, on remand, the agency must implement provision 1A of the settlement agreement. Despite the Commission's application of the plain meaning rule in reviewing settlement agreements, on occasion, parol or extrinsic evidence beyond the four corners of the document will be considered where the terms of the settlement are ambiguous or for equitable reasons.

In Epstein v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01952695 (1997), the Commission found a breach where the agency agreed to issue an apology and subsequently reneged on the agreement. The Commission rejected an argument that the provision for an apology was not valid because it was not part of traditional Title VII relief. In Epstein, the Commission explained that the agency mistakenly equated the requirements of full relief with the parameters of a settlement agreement:

The agency concedes that it has not and will not produce a letter of apology from the Secretary, as stated in the settlement agreement. However, the agency argues that the provision for an apology was not a material element of the settlement agreement. In support of its position, the agency cites two decisions of the Commission holding that an apology is outside the realm of relief contemplated under Title VII, and is therefore not an essential component of an offer of full relief. See Gerencher v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05910947 (January 31, 1992); Chiguito v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05900619 (July 6, 1990). However, this is not a full relief case. Parties to a settlement agreement are not restricted to only those remedies demanded by Title VII; parties may contractually agree to a wide variety of measures to settle a complaint. Therefore, the Commission rejects the agency's argument.

The Commission also found that the agency breached a settlement agreement by not issuing an agreed upon apology in Burke v. Secretary of State, 01A60128 (2006). In that case, the agency agreed to provide the complainant with a letter of apology signed by the Deputy Chief of Mission, U.S. Embassy, Nassau. In addition to finding that the letter was signed by the deputy chief, the Commission also found the letter, which merely acknowledged awareness of the complainant's allegations and promised a discrimination--free workplace, was not an apology.
