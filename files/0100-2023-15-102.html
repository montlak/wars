III. DISCOVERY

Like summary judgment, discussed above, discovery is a prehearing matter. And, like summary judgment, discovery is a sufficiently important topic that it is deserving of a separate discussion.

For those who are newcomers to federal sector EEO complaints, it may be surprising that there was a time when EEOC hearings proceeded without discovery. The Commission's former regulations did not provide for any formal discovery. 29 CFR 1613.218. The absence of discovery was a radical departure from the practices of the MSPB, which have always allowed discovery along the lines provided for in the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure. The investigative stage of the complaint process, to a great extent, was expected to negate the need for discovery.

In the absence of formal discovery, the administrative judge did have the authority to order the production of relevant documents and records. 29 CFR 1613.218(d)(4). Nothing in the Commission's regulations prohibited a party from engaging in informal discovery. If the informal efforts were not successful, the judge could be requested to use the authority to order production of documents.

Since 1992, Part 1614 has specifically provided for the use of prehearing discovery. Title 29 CFR 1614.109(d) states:

The administrative judge shall notify the parties of the right to seek discovery prior to the hearing and may issue such discovery orders as are appropriate. Unless the parties agree in writing concerning the methods and scope of discovery, the party seeking discovery shall request authorization from the administrative judge prior to commencing discovery. Both parties are entitled to reasonable development of evidence on matters relevant to the issues raised in the complaint, but the administrative judge may limit the quantity and timing of discovery. Evidence may be developed through interrogatories, depositions, and requests for admissions, stipulations or production of documents. It shall be grounds for objection to producing evidence that the information sought by either party is irrelevant, overburdensome, repetitious, or privileged.

The right to prehearing discovery is a substantial improvement over Part 1613. However, the regulation itself leaves many questions unanswered. From the wording of the regulation it would appear that the Commission envisions something less encompassing than the traditional scope of discovery. In the first instance, the regulation requires that, unless the parties agree on the methods and scope of discovery, the party seeking discovery must first request authorization from the administrative judge. This is a significant departure from discovery under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure and the discovery procedures of other administrative bodies, such as the MSPB. Courts and the Board prefer that the parties conduct discovery with as little intervention as possible. Each party is left to determine the methods and scope of discovery it wishes to utilize. The regulation, however, was clarified by the 1999 modifications to MD-110 at 7-22 which provides that:

Unless the Administrative Judge requires that a party request authorization to commence discovery, parties may begin discovery upon receipt of the Administrative Judge's acknowledgment order.

If the Administrative Judge requires that a party request authorization to commence discovery, the request must state the method(s) and scope of discovery requested and its relevance to the issue(s) in the complaint.

In other words, the guidance essentially contradicts the regulation by providing that the parties can initiate discovery on their own unless the acknowledgment order provides otherwise. The contradiction is carried forward in the standard Acknowledgment and Order in the Judges' Handbook, at App. B, which authorizes the parties to initiate discovery without seeking the permission of the administrative judge.

The guidance of the Management Directive is further supplemented by the Judges' Handbook at 4-1, which provides:

The authority to conduct pre-hearing discovery is set forth in 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109(b). Chapter 7 of EEO MD-110 provides guidance on the procedures and methods regarding the conduct of discovery in the federal sector complaint process. Neither the Commission's regulations nor EEO MD-110 strictly follows the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure. However, when a discovery question is not answered by the Commission's regulations, guidance, or federal sector decisions, the Administrative Judge may use the Federal Rules as a guide in determining the proper course of action. The Administrative Judge may decide, sua sponte or on motion of a party, to bifurcate the discovery process, restricting initial discovery to liability and then authorizing additional discovery regarding remedies only upon a finding of discrimination.

Discovery permits the parties to obtain and develop evidence regarding any matter relevant to the subject matter of the pending action, whether it relates to the claims or defenses of the party seeking discovery or the claims or defenses of any other party. The Acknowledgment and Order grants the parties the authority to commence discovery. Discovery should proceed with minimal intervention by the Administrative Judge. EEO MD-110, 7-16. While discovery is generally permitted, in appropriate circumstances, the Administrative Judge has the discretion to limit or preclude the parties from engaging in discovery.

(Footnote omitted.)

The regulation also provides that the parties are entitled to "reasonable development of evidence on matters relevant to the issues raised in the complaint." Id. Under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure discovery is permitted on all matters reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. See Fed. R. Civ. P. 26(b)(1). The Commission's regulations are silent on how the standard enunciated in its regulations compares to the standard under the Federal Rules. The Commission's administrative judges do not seem inclined to place any significance on the departure of the language in the Commission's regulation from the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure and generally follow the latter in determining the proper scope of discovery. However, the Judges' Handbook at 4-1, n.5 states:

The written standards for the scope of discovery differ slightly in EEO MD-110 and the Federal Rules. According to EEO MD-110, discovery requests should: (1) be as specific as possible; (2) be reasonably calculated to discover non-repetitive, material evidence; and (3) indicate (if not self-evident) the materiality of the documentary or testimonial evidence sought and the manner in which the information sought will elucidate the accepted claims. EEO MD-110, 7-20. The Federal Rules provide that parties may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, which is relevant to the subject matter involved and that the information sought need not be admissible at trial if it is reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. Fed. R. Civ. P. 26(b)(1).

The Commission's adoption of discovery procedures has generated a host of problems for complainants, agencies and the administrative judges who must oversee the discovery process. Some of the problems lie in the fact that the Commission has included detailed guidance on its discovery procedures, not in 29 CFR Part 1614, which is easily accessible to the public, but in MD-110, which is not so accessible. As a result, many Commission litigants are simply unfamiliar with EEOC discovery procedures. A partial remedy to this situation has been provided by the Commission placing the entirety of MD-110 on its website which is accessible at www.eeoc.gov. Other problems are generated by pro se litigants who are not familiar with discovery methods and procedures. Yet other problems are generated by administrative judges with little or no practical litigation experience. These judges are now being called upon to resolve discovery disputes and rule on motions to compel discovery, as well as motions for sanctions for failure to comply with discovery rulings. Although the use of discovery is, no doubt, of great assistance to both complainants and agencies, the abuse and misuse of discovery continues to be an area of concern for the Commission. As discussed below, however, the Commission, has taken steps to curb discovery abuses.
