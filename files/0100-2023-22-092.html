1. Prima Facie Case

As a result of the Supreme Court's decision in Equal Employment Opportunity Commission v. Abercrombie & Fitch Stores, Inc., 135 S. Ct. 2028 (2015), the requirements of a prima facie case have undergone as transformation. Prior to Abercrombie, the Commission, in Parajan v. Postmaster General, 01901209 (1990), adopted the following criteria for establishing a prima facie case of failure to accommodate a religious belief:

To establish a prima facie case of failure by the agency to accommodate appellant's religion, appellant must show that: (1) he has a bona fide religious belief that conflicts with an agency policy; (2) he informed the agency of this belief, and (3) he was penalized in some way because of the conflict. Protos v. Volkswagen of America, Inc., 797 F.2d 129, 133 (3rd Cir. 1986). Once appellant establishes a prima facie case the burden shifts to the agency to produce evidence showing that it cannot reasonably accommodate appellant without incurring undue hardship or that appellant has been accommodated. Protos, 797 F.2d at 134. In TransWorld Airlines, Inc. v. Hardison, 432 U.S. 63 (1977), the Supreme Court found that accommodations which create more than de minimis monetary or efficiency costs cause undue hardship.

Note that the burden of proof that shifts to the agency in a reasonable accommodation case is not to articulate a legitimate nondiscriminatory reason for its action. Rather, the agency must focus its attention on the possible accommodations and whether those would impose an undue hardship on the agency.

The Court, in Abercrombie, modified the second element of the prima facie case holding that Title VII does not require a complainant to establish that the agency had knowledge of the complainant's protected category; it is sufficient if the agency is motivated by religion in its failure to accommodate. Abercrombie is discussed, in detail, in Chapter 10 in the section of "Knowledge of Protected Group." Briefly, though, the Court rejected the company's argument that it could not have failed to accommodate an applicant by permitting an exception to its dress code and allowing her to wear a headscarf where the applicant did not inform the company that she was Muslim, did not indicate that headscarf was worn for religious purposes, and never requested accommodation. The Court rejected that argument holding that Title VII prohibits certain motives on the part of the employer, irrespective of knowledge.

In order to establish a prima facie case of failure to accommodate, the employee must establish an actual conflict between her religious beliefs and her employment. In Tiano v. Dillard Dept. Stores, 139 F.3d 679 (9th Cir. 1998), the employee failed to establish a prima facie case in charging that her employer failed to accommodate her desire to go on a religious pilgrimage to the former Yugoslavia, where several people had reported sightings of the Virgin Mary. The court found the employee failed to establish that the need to take the pilgrimage at a certain time was part of her bona fide religious belief. As a result, the court found there was no actual conflict between the employee's beliefs and her employment because the pilgrimage could have been taken during periods of approved leave.

In Brown v. Postmaster General, 01A50280 (2005), the Commission signaled a departure from earlier case law suggesting an agency need only accommodate beliefs that were a fundamental tenet of a religion. In Brown, the Commission held that the agency failed to accommodate the complainant's religious practice of wearing a kufi even though the complainant admitted it was not required by his religion. The Commission in Brown held:

Here, the complainant, a Muslim, had a bona fide religious belief, i.e., wearing a kufi was religious wear and followed the ways of the prophet Mohammad. While the complainant testified that he was not obligated to wear the kufi, he did so as part of his bona fide religious belief. The AJ cited Nesbitt v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01996248 (September 19, 2000), to support the conclusion that the agency need only accommodate the fundamental tenets of the religion, not the complainant's optional wearing of the kufi. However, Nesbitt sought only to distinguish religious activity from other types of activity and did not rule that only the fundamental tenets of a religion need be accommodated. The wearing of a kufi conflicted with the agency's employment uniform policy, and the agency enforced this policy against the complainant. The complainant established a prima facie case of religious accommodation discrimination.

After an employee notifies the employer of his need for a religious accommodation, the employer has an obligation to reasonably accommodate the individual's religious practices. A refusal to accommodate is justified only when the employer can demonstrate that an undue hardship would result from accommodating the religious practice. 29 C.F.R. § 1605.2(c)(1). Undue hardship may be shown where the costs of the proffered accommodation are more than de minimis or where such accommodation would deny another employee his job shift preference or conflict with a valid collective bargaining agreement (CBA). Trans World Airlines, Inc. v. Hardison, 432 U.S. 63, 79-85 (1977).

The AJ's decision found that the complainant did not attempt to seek a religious accommodation until after the agency attempted to enforce its uniform policy. We find that the complainant attempted and in fact requested a religious accommodation when the area and station managers sought to enforce the uniform policy. The area manager indicated that when, on April 23, 2002, he instructed the complainant to take off his kufi, the complainant said that he was wearing it for religious purposes. Despite this, the area manager instructed the complainant again to remove his kufi. The complainant reluctantly complied. We find that the complainant's action of informing the area manager that he was wearing the kufi for religious purposes combined with his reluctance under direction to do so constituted a request for religious accommodation, i.e., that he be allowed to wear it. Similarly, the station manager testified that she knew the complainant wore his headwear for religious purposes, and that he probably communicated this to her when she instructed him on May 3, 2002 to remove it. We find that given this and the station manager witnessing the April 23, 2002 incident, the complainant's refusal to remove his kufi constituted a request for religious accommodation, i.e., that he be allowed to wear it.

Both the area and station managers testified that had the complainant requested in writing special consideration to wear his kufi for religious purposes, it would have been granted. Given the circumstances of this case, we find that Title VII did not require the request to be in writing. The agency was in a position to immediately grant the requests without incurring an undue hardship on its operations, and no planning was required. There are no statements or testimony in the record averring that allowing the complainant to wear a kufi would create an undue hardship in any way.

However, in Cole v. Postmaster General, 0120064379 (2008), the Commission appeared to revert to its earlier standard in finding that a complainant who requested a change in work schedule to help park cars, attend a Sunday service, and drive the church bus was not entitled to accommodation because "[t]hese activities are not compelled by a person's belief in the tenets of a religion."
