1. Alternative Means of Testimony

Because the EEO hearing is informal and an extension of the investigation, it is not always necessary that a witness physically appear for testimony. This is especially true if the credibility of the witness is not a real issue and the testimony would be brief. The use of video-conferencing is becoming more common in EEO hearings. While the physical presence of the witness is always preferable, video-conferencing is a considerable improvement over telephonic testimony, because the administrative judge can observe the witness, and can therefore make better credibility determinations.

There are two separate issues that have arisen regarding the appropriateness of taking testimony by an alternative means. The first involves the appropriate circumstances for taking testimony by alternative means. The basic rule is that the administrative judge has broad discretion to allow testimony by alternative means. That first situation is discussed in this section. The second situation, discussed immediately below, involves when, if ever, it is appropriate for an entire hearing to be conducted by alternative means, regardless of the availability of witnesses.

In some cases, there is no substitute for the direct testimony of the requested witness. See, e.g., Harden v. Postmaster General, 01883826 (1989) (where the complainant alleged that he was denied equal overtime, it was error for the judge to accept an affidavit from the agency official who scheduled overtime in lieu of direct testimony,' "...[I]t may be appellant's best opportunity to prove intentional discrimination would be to examine him as a witness."). But an administrative judge properly credits testimony by affidavit where there is no evidence in the record indicating that the affiant has any reason to offer biased testimony. See Desharnais v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01911863 (1991).

An administrative judge does not commit harmful error when he permits an appellant, whose witness is unavailable at the hearing, to submit the witness' testimony in affidavit form. Powell v. USPS, 01850016 (1986). Unfortunately, the appellant in Powell never submitted the affidavit, raised no objections at the hearing, and gave no reasons as to why the testimony could not be adequately solicited in affidavit form. The Commission would not consider the affidavit on appeal.

A party will not be heard to object to testimony by affidavit where it had the opportunity to depose the witness prior to hearing and, on appeal, made no showing that it could have challenged the testimony of the witness. In Poquiz v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0720050095 (2008), the complainant's brother was identified on the complainant's preliminary witness list, the agency chose not to depose him and his testimony related only to the harm suffered by the complainant and not the agency's liability for discrimination. The Commission, in Poquiz, found:

At the outset we address the agency's contention that the AJ erred when she permitted complainant's brother to submit an affidavit in lieu of testifying in person regarding the harm suffered by complainant. We note that in the hearing transcript, the agency acknowledged that it had an opportunity to depose complainant's brother prior to the hearing. Upon review, we find no abuse of discretion by the AJ in permitting complainant's brother to submit an affidavit in support of complainant's claim for compensatory damages. The testimony of complainant's brother did not address the agency's liability for the discriminatory action at issue, but rather, focused solely on the harm suffered by complainant as a result of the agency's discriminatory actions. This is not a case where the agency was denied an opportunity to contest compensatory damages. Specifically, complainant testified at the hearing regarding the harm he suffered as a result of the agency's decision not to hire him for the DEO position and the agency cross-examined complainant regarding the testimony he offered at trial. The record reveals that complainant's brother was identified as a witness who would be providing testimony regarding compensatory damages as early as May 28, 2004, when complainant submitted "Complainant's Preliminary Witness List," while discovery was still ongoing between the parties. We note although the agency had an opportunity to depose complainant's brother, it chose not to do so. The agency also had the opportunity to submit affidavits or other evidence contesting complainant's request for compensatory damages but did not do this.

In the wake of the Commission's decision striking down telephonic hearings discussed immediately below, in Gonzales v. Postmaster General, 0720080027 (2008), the Commission held "that testimony may not be taken by telephone in the absence of exigent circumstances, unless at the joint request of the parties and provided that specified conditions have been met." However, the Commission upheld the use of the telephonic testimony of a witness in that case even though it appeared there were no exigent circumstances where the credibility of the witness was not at issue and neither party objected to the testimony at the time of the hearing.

In a decision that is short on detail, the Commission found that the administrative judge did not abuse his discretion in refusing to admit a statement by the complainant's psychiatrist and then denying a motion to take the psychiatrist's testimony by telephone. In Fletcher v. Postmaster General, 01A50022 (2006), the complainant told the judge that his psychiatrist was unable to attend the hearing because he was attending a conference in Hawaii. The psychiatrist wrote a statement that the judge refused to admit because it was not a business record. Although the decision is unclear, it appears the statement was not under oath. The complainant also alleged that the judge off the record denied his request to have the psychiatrist testify by cell phone. The Commission held that the decision not to admit a non-business record or telephonic testimony was not an abuse of discretion.

The administrative judge did not abuse his discretion in approving the telephonic testimony of a supervisor who physically appeared for the hearing. The supervisor was undergoing cancer treatment and became ill before she could testify that day. The judge took the supervisor's testimony by telephone a few weeks later. The Commission found that the judge properly determined that exigent circumstances existed and neither party objected to the telephonic testimony. Frank v. Postmaster General, 0120110223 (2013).

In the event a witness is unavailable for a hearing and the party believes the witness' testimony cannot be reflected adequately in an affidavit, the safe course is to submit the affidavit and to move, on the record, to have the hearing record kept open until the witness is available. If that request is denied, an objection should be stated for the record. Opposing parties also should be aware that a legitimate objection to submission of testimony through affidavit is the deprivation of an opportunity to cross-examine the witness.

The administrative judge has the authority to make arrangements for testimony by alternative means when witnesses cannot be physically present at the hearing. In Rivera v. Postmaster General, 01883829 (1989), the Commission held that the administrative judge did not abuse his discretion by taking testimony over the telephone from witnesses who were unavailable. The Commission rejected an argument by the appellant that he was not afforded an opportunity to cross-examine the witnesses because he could not see them. The administrative judge gave the complainant an opportunity to cross-examine the witnesses and he declined.
