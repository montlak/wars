4. Legal Detriment Requirement

Each party to an agreement must incur some kind of legal detriment for the agreement to be legally binding. For example, in Ellsworth S. v. General Services Administration, 0120161965 (2017), the Commission set aside the agreement for lack of consideration where the agency's only obligation was to send an email to agency officials informing them of the complainant's title and duties. The agency incurred no legal detriment by that promise, and the agreement was void. The Commission in Ellsworth S. reinstated the complaint.

Consideration involves the mutual promises of each party to accrue a benefit and detriment and not the actual delivery on those promises. An agreement is not void for lack of consideration because the complainant refuses to accept the consideration. For example, in Meghann M. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120141219 (2015), the complainant sought to void the agreement after not cashing the check she received from the agency as per the settlement agreement:

In the instant case, there is no evidence that Complainant actually cashed the check for $15,000.00, which was the only consideration directly due her under the terms of this Agreement. We find, nevertheless, that the Agency met its burden of showing that it attempted to pay the consideration consistent with the terms of the Agreement.

Although we find no breach, but because of the amount of time that has passed, we will require the Agency to reissue a check in the amount of $15,000.00 to Complainant, if she has not yet cashed the oriGINAl check. This will allow us to supplement our records to fully document that there was compliance in this matter.

Complainant is cautioned, however, that should she not cash the check, she is not entitled to any other relief or the reinstatement of the complaint.

Perhaps to be filed under the general heading that an agency cannot have its cake and eat it too, the Commission found that no valid settlement agreement existed where the agency promised to remove the complainant's letter of removal from his personnel file, but maintained an SF-50 in the personnel file that documented the removal. McCloud v. Postmaster General, 01960089 (1998). "By removing the letter, the agency incurred no legal detriment at all; appellant's removal for cause was still documented in his OPF by the SF-50. Accordingly, no contract was formed by the execution of the settlement agreement." Id.

Surprisingly, in McCloud, the Commission ultimately enforced the settlement agreement by ordering the agency to remove all evidence of the complainant's removal from his official personnel file. Invoking the doctrine of detrimental reliance, the Commission found the complainant would not have withdrawn the complaint had he understood that there would still be reference to his removal in his files. The Commission did not explain why the complaint should not simply be reinstated, if there was no consideration and, therefore, no settlement agreement.

Even where the agreement contains consideration on its face, if the agency subsequently takes an action to deprive the complainant of that consideration, the agreement is void. Under a settlement agreement reached in Kaczynska v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01975320 (1999), the complainant was to serve as chief of her department for a one-year rotation following her return from maternity leave. However, the agency chose a permanent chief two weeks after complainant's return. The Commission reinstated the underlying complaint for further processing in Kaczynska because it found that the agency breached the settlement agreement and that implementation of the settlement had become impractical, leaving the complainant without consideration in the agreement:

The Commission finds that the intent of provision 2(b) was to provide appellant with the opportunity to serve as Chief for one year. The agency admits that appellant only served as Chief for approximately one or two weeks. The record indicates that the agency agreed to change the rotational schedule because of appellant's maternity leave. The Commission finds that the agency breached the agreement by making a permanent selection for Chief prior to August 1, 1996 and thereby denying appellant the opportunity to serve as Chief until July 31, 1996.

Though it does not occur often, sometimes agreements are found void because the complainant has given no consideration. For example in Copley v. Postmaster General, 01A6109 (2006), the complainant agreed to decide if he would withdraw his EEO complaint within 60 days of signing the settlement agreement. In response to a breach claim, the Commission found that no agreement existed because it lacked consideration on the complainant's part.

Although a complainant can withdraw a complaint of his own volition without any consideration, where the agency characterizes a document as a settlement agreement it must contain consideration. In Stanton v. Postmaster General, 01950790 (1996), the complainant entered two settlement agreements resolving different complaints. In the first agreement, the complainant received a disputed clothing allowance in exchange for withdrawing a complaint. In the second agreement, which purportedly resolved several complaints, the complainant received nothing and the agreement merely stated his desire to withdraw the complaints to demonstrate that he was a "committed and capable" employee. The Commission voided the second agreement in Stanton, stating:

The record contains two agreements (which we shall refer to as Agreement 1 and Agreement 2), each entitled "EEO Settlement Agreement," entered into by appellant and the agency on September 9, 1994. In Agreement 1 the "formal case no." listed at the top of the agreement is 1C-1954-93. Agreement 1 provided:

I, [appellant], do hereby voluntarily withdraw my EEO complaint based on the stipulation(s) that:

Postal management, as an act of good faith, agrees that because the Complainant is a Cleaner assigned to the Maintenance Craft, he is entitled to a clothing allowance. This clothing allowance will remain in effect as long as...the Complainant continues to perform the duties of a cleaner. This is a full and complete settlement of the above referenced case and there are no other offers of relief either expressed or implied.

In Agreement 2 the "formal case no." listed at the top of the agreement is "1C 1954 93 and all other EEO complaints in the system." Agreement 2 provided:

I, [appellant] do hereby voluntarily withdraw my EEO complaint based on the stipulation(s) that:

On September 9, 1994, through a discussion with [name of person], Senior EEO Specialist, and my chosen representative, [Person A], I of my own free-will-and-accord, has chosen to withdraw all of the EEO Complaints I have in the system at all stages of the Complaints Processing Procedure.

This effort by myself, is an attempt to show the agency that I am a committed, capable employee of the U.S. Postal Service and all I request is an opportunity to share my knowledge and expertise.

By letter dated October 5, 1994 appellant informed the agency that:

Since the receipt of the job offer I received on 9-23-94 did not correspond with the agreement that was told to me I want all EEO's reinstated. It was the understanding of [Person A], APWU representative, and myself that an attempt to acquire a clerical...position, in return for dropping my EEO complaints. Instead I was offered a position on the floor that caused my injury in the beginning...

By letter dated October 26, 1994, the agency found that appellant had not identified how the agency had breached a term of a settlement agreement. The agency also stated that appellant voluntarily withdrew his complaints. The agency denied appellant's request for reinstatement.

On appeal, appellant provides a statement from Person A dated November 26, 1994. Person A stated that on September 9, 1994 the Senior EEO Specialist, appellant, and Person A had a meeting in which the Senior EEO Specialist promised appellant that the Senior EEO Specialist would help place appellant in an office environment job if appellant would "drop all his EEOs." Person A further stated: "[The Senior EEO Specialist] stated as he ended his presentation that if at anytime [appellant] was not satisfied with his results (new job or lack of same) that he would reinstate his EEOs no problem."

EEOC Regulation 29 CFR 1614.504(a) provides that any settlement agreement knowingly and voluntarily agreed to by the parties shall be binding on both parties. That section further provides that if the complainant believes that the agency has failed to comply with the terms of a settlement agreement, the complainant shall notify the Director of Equal Employment Opportunity of the alleged noncompliance with the settlement agreement. 29 CFR 1614.504(a). The complainant may request that the terms of the settlement agreement be specifically implemented or request that the complaint be reinstated for further processing from the point processing ceased under the terms of the settlement agreement.

Settlement agreements are contracts between the appellant and the agency and it is the intent of the parties as expressed in the contract, and not some unexpressed intention, that controls the contract's construction. Eggleston v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 05900795 (Aug. 23, 1990); In re Chicago & E.I. Ry. Co., 94 F.2d 296 (7th Cir. 1938). In addition, the Commission generally follows the rule that if a writing appears to be plain and unambiguous on its face, its meaning must be determined from the four corners of the instrument without resort to extrinsic evidence of any nature. See Montgomery Elevator v. Building Eng'g Servs., 730 F.2d 377 (5th Cir. 1984).

The Commission finds that appellant has not shown how the agency breached Agreement 1 because he has not made any claims regarding the clothing allowance. There is no provision in Agreement 1 concerning appellant's placement into any particular type of position. If appellant wanted such a provision in Agreement 1, then he should have had such a term written into the agreement. See Eggleston, EEOC Request No. 05900795. Therefore, we find that appellant has failed to show that the agency breached Agreement 1. The Commission finds that the agency properly refused to reinstate agency number 1C-1954-93 which was settled in Agreement 1.

The Commission finds that Agreement 2 is not a binding settlement agreement because appellant received no consideration in Agreement 2. The Commission has stated the following concerning the issue of consideration:

Generally the adequacy or fairness of the consideration in a settlement agreement is not at issue, as long some legal detriment is incurred as part of the bargain. When, however, one of the contracting parties incurs no legal detriment, the settlement agreement will be set aside for lack of consideration.

Juhola v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01934032 (June 30, 1994) (citing Terracina v. Department of Health and Human Services, EEOC Request No. 05910888) (Mar. 11, 1992)).

Because we find that the agency incurred no legal detriment as part of the bargain in Agreement 2, we shall set aside Agreement 2 for lack of consideration. See id. All of the complaints purportedly settled in Agreement 2, except for 1C-1954-93, shall be remanded for reinstatement. There is no need to reinstate 1C-1954-93 because we have already determined that it was settled in Agreement 1.

Note that the Commission did not decide the case based on parol evidence by suggesting that the agency tried to take advantage of the complainant by promising him something outside of the agreement. Its decision was based solely on the lack of consideration in Agreement 2.
