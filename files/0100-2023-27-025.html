II. NONSELECTION: PROMOTIONS AND APPOINTMENTS

Nonselection of applicants for positions and nonselection for promotions compose a large percentage of the EEO complaints filed against agencies. Because the appointment and promotion processes employed by agencies have a great deal in common and the law is not substantially different with respect to promotions and appointments, the two areas are treated together.

The first order of business in any nonselection or promotion case is to determine what type of appointment authority is at issue. Agencies have the authority to fill positions through various means, including competitive appointments and promotions, noncompetitive appointments and promotions, temporary details, lateral reassignments, and conversion from temporary to permanent status, to name a few. Determining what type of selection authority the agency has exercised will assist in identifying the procedures the agency was required to follow. In dealing with positions covered by collective bargaining units, it also is important to determine if the applicable bargaining agreement has any provisions regarding selection or promotion.

Determining the authority under which the selection is made further assists in determining the appropriate agency with which to file a complaint of discrimination. The Office of Personnel Management has certain responsibilities in developing selection criteria for competitive service positions and in referring qualified candidates to an agency for competitive positions. See generally 5 CFR Part 300. The employing agency has no jurisdiction over a complaint alleging that OPM selection criteria are discriminatory, even if the complaint alleges that application of the criteria resulted in denial of a position with that agency. See, e.g., Buczynski v. Office of Personnel Management, 01821829 (1983).

In addition, 5 CFR 300.104 provides:

(a) Employment practices. A candidate who believes that an employment practice which was applied to him or her by the Office of Personnel Management violates a basic requirement in § 300.103 (proscribing employment practices that discriminate on the basis of race, color, sex, religion, national origin, handicapped status or age) is entitled to appeal to the Merit Systems Protection Board under the provisions of its regulations.

(b) Examination ratings. A candidate may file an appeal with the Office from his examination rating or rejection of his application, except that, where the Office has delegated examining authority to an agency, the candidate should appeal directly to that agency. The appeal shall be filed and processed in accordance with the instructions in Chapter 337 of the Federal Personnel Manual.

(c) Complaints and grievances to an agency. (1) A candidate may file a complaint with an agency when he believes that an employment practice which was applied to him and which is administered or required by the agency discriminates against him on the basis of race, color, religion, sex or national origin; or age....

A discussion of all of the appointment authorities available to agencies is beyond the scope of this book. But, the importance of determining the nature of the selection at issue should not be overlooked.

As discussed more fully below, many agencies use screening panels in filling vacancies. These panels screen applicants, usually recommending several "best qualified" candidates to the selecting official. The screening panel may submit a list of best qualified candidates to the selecting official in alphabetical order without any indication of their respective rankings. Or, it may submit a list of best qualified candidates along with their respective ranking and scores. It is important in selection cases involving panels to focus on who made the actual selection decision.

The relevant inquiry in a nonselection generally involves the motivation of the selecting official, except in cases where it can be demonstrated that the selecting official merely "rubber-stamped" the recommendation of the screening panel. When the selecting official has exercised his or her own judgment in the selection, the fact that two of the three screening panel members believed the complainant was better qualified is not demonstrative of pretext. In Howley v. Postmaster General, 05940849 (1995), the Commission found that such evidence did not support a finding of pretext where even the complainant admitted that the selecting official was not familiar with her day-to-day performance.

The general rules governing nonselection cases was succinctly set forth in Brown v. Attorney General, 01990536 (2002):

The Commission notes that in non-selection cases, pretext may be found where the complainant's qualifications are demonstrably superior to the selectee's. Bauer v. Bailar, 647 F.2d 1037, 1048 (10th Cir. 1981). However, an employer has the discretion to choose among equally qualified candidates. Canham v. Oberlin College, 666 F.2d 1057, 1061 (6th Cir. 1981). Additionally, an employer has greater discretion when filling management level or specialized positions. Wrenn v. Gould, 808 F.2d 493, 502 (6th Cir. 1987). The Commission also recognizes that while the use of subjective criteria may offer a convenient pretext for unlawful discrimination, subjective criteria are frequently relied upon in promotions to supervisory or management positions, and the use of such criteria is not, in and of itself, an indicator of discriminatory motivation. Podale v. Department of Health and Human Services, EEOC Request No. 05960344 (October 16, 1998). Wilson v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05921062 (August 12, 1993). Subjectivity in the selection of a manager is inevitable because the qualities of a good manager are not readily susceptible to quantification.

The agency cannot, however, sustain its burden with vague reasons such as the selecting official chose the "best qualified candidate." The agency must articulate its reasons with sufficient specificity to give the complainant a full and fair opportunity to demonstrate pretext. See, e.g., Yee v. Secretary of Interior, 0120061381 (2008).
