8. Security Clearances and the Rehabilitation Act

The requirements of the Rehabilitation Act are not diminished where an employee loses his or her security clearance. In Zimmerman v. National Archives, 05920102 (1992), the Commission held that Egan does not preclude it from considering whether an employee with a disability could be reassigned to a nonsensitive position that does not require a security clearance:

The Commission has recognized that it does not have the authority under Title VII to review the merits of an agency's security clearance determination, but that it does have the authority to determine whether the requirement that an individual have a security clearance in order to occupy a particular position was applied in a discriminatory manner. See Thierjung v. Dep't of Defense (Defense Mapping Agency), EEOC Request No. 05880664 (November 2, 1989), and discussion therein. In the instant case, however, the appellant has not alleged that the agency permitted another individual, who did not have an obsessive compulsive disorder, to occupy the position from which he was discharged without first obtaining a top security clearance. Accordingly, we find that the appellant has failed to state a claim of disparate treatment over which the Commission has jurisdiction.

However, the appellant also alleged that the agency failed to accommodate his handicap by reassigning him to a position which did not require a top security clearance. EEOC regulation 29 CFR 1613.704(a) requires that an agency shall make reasonable accommodation to the known physical or mental limitations of a qualified handicapped employee unless the agency can demonstrate that the accommodation would impose an undue hardship on the operation of its program.

EEOC regulation 29 CFR 1613.702(f) defines a qualified handicapped person as a handicapped person who, with or without accommodation, can perform the essential functions of the position in question without endangering the health and safety of the individual or others. The Commission interprets "position in question" to include the complainant's position and all vacant positions for which the complainant qualified. See Ignacio v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Petition No. 03840005 (September 4, 1984), aff'd, Special Panel No. 1 (February 27, 1986). In the instant case, "position in question" apparently would include, but not be limited to, the position(s) which the appellant occupied on detail for almost one year pending the completion of the background check.

A Commission decision on the appellant's failure to accommodate allegation would not involve a review of the merits of the agency's top security clearance determination. See, e.g., Guillot v. Dep't of the Navy, EEOC Petition No. 03890008 (January 5, 1990). Therefore, the Commission's consideration of appellant's failure to accommodate allegation is not precluded by Section 703(g) of Title VII as it applies to Section 501 of the Rehabilitation Act. See generally Dep't of the Navy v. Egan, 484 U.S. at 530 (the Merit Systems Protection Board may consider whether a transfer to a nonsensitive position was feasible for an employee who was removed from his position due to a denial of his security clearance).

In Thomas v. Secretary of Air Force, 03910088 (1991), the Commission authored a lengthy opinion, in which it found that an agency that revokes a disabled employee's security clearance may have an obligation to consider reassignment of that employee to a position which does not require a clearance. In Thomas, the employee argued that the agency's failure to consider reassignment was a failure to make reasonable accommodation. The Commission, reviewing the decision of the MSPB in Thomas, wrote:

[T]he Commission notes that the Supreme Court specifically stated in Egan that as part of the procedural protection afforded an employee, the Board may determine "...whether transfer to a nonsensitive position was feasible." Egan, 108 S. Ct. at 825-826. The AJ noted in her initial decision that subsequent decisions of the Federal Circuit and the MSPB have interpreted Egan to require agencies to consider reassignment, and thus permit Board review of this issue, only if it is mandated by an agency regulation. (citations and footnote omitted) Because no such agency regulation existed in this case, the AJ held that she did not have the authority to review petitioner's allegations regarding the agency's reassignment efforts. Moreover, the AJ stated that the Rehabilitation Act was "not a statute requiring reassignment where the removal action is taken for failure to maintain a security clearance."

The Commission finds, contrary to the AJ's holding, that under certain circumstances an agency may have an obligation as part of its affirmative obligation under Section 501 of the Rehabilitation Act to consider reassignment for handicapped persons who have had a security clearance revoked. See Michael D. Guillot v. H. Lawrence Garrett, III, Secretary, Department of the Navy, EEOC Petition No. 03890008 (January 5, 1990). Section 501(b) of the Rehabilitation Act, codified as Section 791(b), provides that Federal employers, including the agency, are prohibited from denying an individual employment on the basis of that person's handicap when the person "with or without reasonable accommodation, can perform the essential functions of the position in question...and who [m]eets the experience and/or education requirement...of the position...." 29 CFR 1613.702(f). "The legislative history of section 501 illustrates that with respect to the employment of handicapped individuals, Congress expected the Federal Government should be a leader." Statement of Senator Cranston, Cong. Rec. S15591 (Sept. 20, 1978), quoted in Prewitt v. United States Postal Service, 662 F.2d 292, 301-02 (5th Cir. 1981). "In addition, regulations of the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission under the statute emphasize the general policy of the federal government to become a model employer of handicapped individuals." Whitlock v. Donovan, 598 F. Supp. 126, 130 (D.D.C. 1984) aff'd without opinion, 790 F.2d 964 (D.C. Cir. 1986). To reach that goal, "an affirmative action plan is imposed upon the federal employer, § 791(b), which is not imposed on other employers." Crewe v. Office of Personnel Management, 834 F.2d 140, 142 (8th Cir. 1987), citing Southeastern Community College v. Davis, 442 U.S. 397, 407-12, 99 S. Ct. 2361, 2367-70, 60 L. Ed.2d 980 (1979). The agency's affirmative action obligation under the Rehabilitation Act is more than a requirement of nondiscrimination. See Shirley v. Devine, 670 F.2d 1188, 1201 (D.C. Cir. 1982); Davis, 442 U.S. at 410. Federal agencies are required to reasonably accommodate the limitations of a qualified handicapped employee unless the agency shows that such accommodation would impose an "undue hardship" on its operations. 29 CFR § 1613.704.

It has been held that "reassignment is within the scope of reasonable accommodation" and is "consistent with the Rehabilitation Act and regulations promulgated thereto." Ignacio v. United States Postal Service, Special Panel No. 1, at 23 (February 27, 1986). Reassignment is authorized by Section 501 of the Rehabilitation Act's affirmative action requirement and Congressional intent that the federal government be a model employer of persons with disabilities. The Supreme Court has indicated that under the lesser nondiscrimination requirement of Section 504, reassignment may be required in certain circumstances. In School Board of Nassau County v. Arline, 107 S. Ct. 1123, 1131 n.19 (1987), the Supreme Court noted:

Employers have an affirmative obligation to make a reasonable accommodation for a handicapped employee. Although they are not required to find another job for an employee who is not qualified for the job he or she was doing, they cannot deny an employee alternative employment opportunities reasonably available under the employer's existing policies.

Even under the less stringent nondiscrimination requirements of Section 504, Arline indicates that reassignment cannot be denied where "alternative employment opportunities" are "reasonably available under the employer's existing policies."

In the present case, the agency was required to provide petitioner with reasonable accommodation if petitioner established the...elements of a prima facie case of handicapped discrimination....

Because the complainant was precluded from presenting evidence on the nature of her disability, the Commission remanded the case for further factual development.
