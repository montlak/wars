4. Probation

Because the object of relief under Title VII is to put an employee where he or she would have been if not for prohibited discrimination, it is not appropriate to require a reinstated employee to serve a new period of probation. If the employee was a probationary employee when terminated, it is appropriate to credit the employee for time previously served and begin the probationary period from that point in time. See Dorsett v. USPS, 01830120 (1983). But note that applicants for employment are still required to serve a probationary period, even if retroactive credit for time in the position would have placed them beyond the probationary period. See 29 CFR 1614.501(b)(1)(ii).

The Commission's application of this rule has not always been consistent. In White v. USPS, 05840105 (1986), the appellant was terminated during a 60-day probationary period and subsequently reinstated as the result of an informal agreement with a new 90-day probationary period. The employee was removed a second time after serving 60 days. The agency adopted a recommended decision reinstating the employee contingent upon successful completion of a new 90-day probationary period. The Commission upheld the agency decision, rejecting the employee's argument that he was being placed in "double jeopardy" by having to begin his probationary period anew. This is inconsistent with the decision in Dorsett noted above and also is inconsistent with the goal of making an employee whole.

The Dorsett decision is the correct view and has been cited by the Commission approvingly in other cases. See, e.g., Wells v. Postmaster General, 01880979 (1988).

In Hassan v. Postmaster General, 01955052 (1997), the Commission ordered the employee reinstated as a nonprobationary employee where the probation period was nearly completed and there was further evidence the employee would have successfully completed the probation period but for his termination. In Hassan, the employee was within a few days of completing his probationary period and his on-the-job trainer testified the employee would have successfully completed his probation. In addition, a probationary employee with a rating comparable to that of the complainant was given career status.

Similarly, in Hernandez v. Social Security Administration, 05A60218 (2005), the Commission ordered the complainant reinstated in nonprobationary status and promoted from GS-5 to GS-7 where the complainant had only three weeks of probation left when he was terminated and successful completion of the entry position led to promotion.

It is of no consequence that agency policy requires a new probationary period to be served by employees reinstated through other procedures. An agency that is ordered to reinstate an employee pursuant to the EEO process may not require that employee to undergo an additional probationary period. In Roquemore v. Postmaster General, 01951930 (1997), the Commission found that because the complainant had completed his probationary period prior to his discriminatory termination, the agency had to reinstate him as a career employee with the appropriate seniority:

The agency has argued that appellant should serve a new probationary period upon his reinstatement because agency policy requires the same of other employees who return to work with the agency under other circumstances. The Commission does not find this rationale persuasive in view of the plain language of our regulations which require that a victim of discrimination be returned to the position s/he would have been in had the discrimination not occurred. In this case, the record shows that appellant commenced working at the agency on July 26, 1993, and was terminated on September 17, 1993. There is no indication in the record that appellant was having any problems successfully completing his probationary period at the time of his termination. Moreover, the agency has not asserted that had appellant remained in the employ of the agency he would not have become a career employee. Therefore, the Commission finds that the agency should have reinstated appellant to the position he would have been in had he not been the victim of discrimination--a career Mailhandler with a seniority date of July 26, 1993, who has completed the standard probationary period. See Korchnak v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05910483 (November 15, 1991) (Commission orders appellant reinstated as career employee after being wrongfully discharged during probationary period noting the agency failed to show that appellant would not have been retained as a permanent employee even absent discrimination).
