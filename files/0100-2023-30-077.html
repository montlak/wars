8. Medical Expenses

In the past, the Commission held that reimbursement for medical expenses is not recoverable under Title VII. But, as discussed above, medical expenses can be recovered as part of a compensatory damage award under the 1991 Civil Rights Act. However, such expenses are not recoverable under the ADEA.

In Whiting v. ACTION, 05900093 (1990), the Commission explained its former refusal to award medical expenses as follows:

Title VII of the Civil Rights Act expressly authorizes only an award of injunctive relief, reinstatement or hiring with or without backpay, and "any other equitable relief" deemed appropriate. 42 USC 2000e-5(g). Such "equitable relief" has been held to include the restoration of lost benefits, such as pension or fringe benefits, seniority, and other concomitants of employment which would have been earned absent the discrimination. See, e.g., Walker v. Ford Motor Co., 29 FEP Cases 1259, 1266 (11th Cir. 1982). With respect to other damages, the overwhelming weight of authority is that general, compensatory, and punitive damages may not be awarded under Title VII. See Thompson v. Machinists, 35 FEP Cases 845, 851 (D.D.C. 1984); DeGrace v. Rumsfeld, 614 F.2d 796 (1st Cir. 1980); Protos v. Volkswagen of America, 41 FEP Cases 598 (3rd Cir. 1986); Russell v. American Tobacco Co., 528 F.2d 357 (4th Cir. 1986); Hamilton v. Rodgers, 40 FEP Cases 453 (5th Cir. 1986); Farmer v. ARA Services, Inc., 660 F.2d 1096 (6th Cir. 1981); Patterson v. Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co., 475 F. Supp. 344 (N.D. Ill. 1979), aff'd, 659 F.2d 736 (7th Cir. 1981); Shah v. Mount Zion Hospital & Medical Center, 642 F.2d 1150 (10th Cir. 1976).

Generally, courts have cited three reasons for their denial. First, an award of such damages is viewed as a legal, not an equitable, remedy and thus appears not to be authorized by the provisions of 42 USC 2000e-5(g). Second, the courts have noted provisions of the National Labor Relations Act, 29 USC 160(c), which do not allow the award of such damages. Finally, when Congress enacted the Fair Housing laws, 42 USC 3612, et seq., in 1968, it specifically provided for the award of actual and punitive damages; however, when Congress amended Title VII in 1972 to include the award of remedies under 2000e-5(g), it did not include such damages, authorizing only backpay and equitable relief. See, e.g., Protos, supra, 41 FEP Cases at 605; Walker, supra, 29 FEP Cases at 1265, 1266. It has been held that the foregoing considerations "almost compel the conclusion that Congress intentionally left out any provision for either general or punitive damages" under Title VII. Padway v. Palches, 665 F.2d 965, 968 (9th Cir. 1982).

Accordingly, insofar as appellant seeks compensation for medical expenses incurred for a stress condition which allegedly was a direct result of the agency's discrimination, she seeks an award of compensatory damages which cannot be granted under Title VII or the ADEA. Moll v. Parkside Livonia Credit Union, 30 FEP Cases 1236, 1240 (E.D. Mich. 1982) (damages for physical and emotional illnesses which resulted in substantial medical expenses cannot be awarded under Title VII); Stathos v. Bowden, 34 FEP Cases 142 (1st Cir. 1984) (damages for medical expenses are compensatory damages); Hamilton v. Rodgers, supra, 4 FEP Cases at 456 (damages for injuries to emotional and physical health are not awardable under Title VII). Accordingly, we find that appellant is not entitled to be reimbursed for her medical expenses and therefore the agency properly denied her such relief. See Shirley E. Conrad v. Donald Paul Hodel, Secretary, Department of Interior, EEOC Request No. 05880821 (January 6, 1989); Rebecca Truesdell v. Anthony M. Frank, Postmaster General, United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05890405 (March 6, 1990).

However, in McGowan-Butler v. Secretary of Treasury, 05940636 (1994), the Commission suggested that such medical expenses may be recovered as an element of "make whole" relief where the employee, as the result of a discriminatory action, loses employer-provided health benefits. Such cases are distinguishable from those in which the complainant claims the employer's discrimination resulted in medical conditions that required treatment. The McGowan-Butler case is discussed in more detail below in the section on "Compensatory Damages."

In Taber v. Postmaster General, 01983780 (2001), as part of the equitable relief in a failure to hire case, the Commission found that the complainant was entitled to be reimbursed for the cost of health insurance or for his medical expenses from the date he would have been hired through to the date of his actual placement on the agency rolls.
