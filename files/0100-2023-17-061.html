C. DEFAULT JUDGMENT

Although, as discussed below, the Commission does impose default judgments against agencies, lesser sanctions, such as the exclusion of evidence or the imposition of an adverse inference, do not mean the offending party will lose the case. The sanctions can be overcome with other proof. For example, in Newbold-Reese v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120073324 (2009), the Commission upheld the sanction of an adverse inference imposed by the administrative judge where the agency had destroyed notes relating to how candidates were ranked and selected, as well as interview notes. The inference imposed by the judge was that the documents, if preserved, would have rendered the agency's explanation for why the complainant was not granted a second interview as not credible. However, the Commission found that despite that inference, there was no persuasive evidence that the complainant was not selected because of her EEO activity. The Commission, in Newbold-Reese, found that the qualifications of the selectee were "plainly superior" to the complainant.

The procedural posture of the case may also inform the appropriate sanction. In Judson G. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120151916 (2017), the administrative judge dismissed the complainant's hearing request and remanded it back to the agency for its final decision based on the complainant's contumacious conduct. This sanction was unusual because the administrative judge had already granted the complainant default judgment as a sanction for the agency's repeated failure to comply with the administrative judge's orders. According to the administrative judge, after the default judgment but prior to the damages hearing, the complainant "began an intense campaign of personal attacks which openly challenged the AJ's authority" and engaged in disruptive conduct and willful disobedience. The complainant's conduct was "so contumacious" that the administrative judge mooted all previous orders and rulings, including the default judgment in favor of the complainant, and dismissed the complainant's hearing request. The Commission found the sanction to be too harsh, but not because the complainant's conduct did not warrant a sanction. The procedural posture of the case at the time the sanction was imposed and the agency's own poor conduct warranted a more narrowly tailored sanction in Judson G., according to the Commission:

After careful consideration of the arguments of the parties and the record as a whole, the Commission concludes that the AJ erred in imposing overly harsh sanctions in this case. The record is clear that Complainant engaged in a pattern of disruptive and defiant conduct. That conduct included ignoring the AJ's rulings (including default judgment in his favor), abusive personal attacks on the AJ, repeated challenges to the AJ's authority, and willful failure to comply with or to show good cause for non-conformity with the AJ's orders. Complainant's conduct undoubtedly warranted a sanction; however, his actions do not nullify the Agency's prior conduct which resulted in the oriGINAl default judgment sanction. Thus, the Commission concludes that the AJ's sanctions against Complainant, including vacating the November 17, 2014 default judgment, dismissing Complainant's hearing request, and remanding the complaint to the Agency for a FAD were an abuse of discretion. A less harsh, more tailored sanction would have been to dismiss the damages hearing and remand the matter to the Agency with an order to conduct a supplemental investigation into Complainant's entitlement to damages followed by a final decision regarding compensatory damages.

The Commission reaffirms the importance of ensuring compliance with AJ orders and maintaining decorum during the hearing process. When issued, sanctions must be appropriately tailored to match the effects of the conduct at issue. Complainant's noncompliance with the AJ's orders and incessant personal attacks on the AJ were significant and impacted the integrity of the EEO process.

However, the AJ's dismissal of Complainant's hearing request after previously awarding default judgment in his favor as a sanction for the Agency's conduct was an unduly harsh sanction. Accordingly, based on the specific circumstances present, the Commission finds that the AJ abused his discretion when he dismissed Complainant's request for a hearing. Notwithstanding, the Commission finds no basis to disturb the AJ's prior sanction of default judgment in favor of Complainant, and that sanction is reinstated.

Judson G. may have limited application because of the unique circumstances of the case, but it still stands for the proposition that an administrative judge should consider how far along a case is procedurally in choosing among sanctions.
