B. PARTIAL DISMISSAL OF CLAIMS

Another recurring problem for agencies and complainants is what to do when a complaint raises more than one claim of discrimination and the agency accepts one or more claims and rejects others. Under the 1999 amendment to the Commission's regulations at 29 CFR 1614.107(b), the accepted claims are investigated, and any dismissed claims are not. If a claim is dismissed, a notation is made in the investigative file, including the rationale for dismissing the claim. When the agency completes its investigation of the accepted claims, or after 180 days have passed from the time the complaint was filed, a complainant may request a hearing before an administrative judge. The administrative judge assigned to the case will review the agency's dismissal to determine if it is correct. If necessary, the administrative judge will oversee the development of the record for any claims that were improperly dismissed by the agency.

Under the Commission's regulations in effect prior to November 9, 1999, complainants could immediately appeal rejected claims to the EEOC. The question that arose was whether an investigation should proceed on the accepted claims, or await a final decision by the EEOC on the dismissed claims. Agencies could elect to investigate the accepted claims during the time an appeal was being pursued. MD-110, at 4-8 (1992). The result under this procedure was piecemeal processing of discrimination claims if the accepted claims were investigated, or a lengthy delay in the processing of the accepted claims if the investigation was held in abeyance pending appeal. Neither of these results was desirable, and the 1999 regulations represent a compromise between the two extremes that should result in more efficient case processing.

Commission case law under the previous regulations illustrates the inefficiency of how partial dismissals were processed. In Koch v. Securities & Exchange Commission, 01965642 (1997), the agency accepted some claims and dismissed other related claims. The Commission held that the agency did not have unlimited discretion to proceed with investigation of claims pending the outcome of an appeal on related claims:

[W]e find the agency erred by insisting that it must proceed with the investigation of the accepted allegations of Complaints 1 and 3 before the Commission had addressed the dismissal of several allegations on appeal. In the circumstances of this case, the agency's reliance on the following guidance in MD 110 was misplaced. MD 110, Ch. 4, Section III(C), provides that where a complainant has appealed the dismissal of a part of his complaint the agency "may, but [is] not required to investigate the accepted portions of the complaint" during the pendency of the appeal. The appellant gave the agency several reasons for why the agency should not rely upon this discretionary provision of MD 110, namely: the objections based on 29 CFR 1614.108(e), discussed above [the agency had exceeded its time limit for investigation]; the agency's EEO office had a conflict of interest in view of his allegations of impropriety in the processing of his complaints; the agency was denying him the right to effective representation; and, that he did not wish to proceed with the investigation of his allegations on a piecemeal basis while he was appealing the dismissal of several related allegations. Regardless of the merits of all the reasons given by the appellant for not participating in the agency's investigation of his complaints, we certainly do not agree with the agency's determination that he had "engaged in delay or contumacious conduct" by failing to agree to give testimony, warranting a dismissal of his complaints under 29 CFR 1614.107(g). By the time the agency commenced the investigation of Complaint 1 in December 1995, the complaint was already nearly 180 days old. Certainly, the agency cannot blame the appellant for the agency's own delay in this regard. And, as we have already decided, the appellant was well within his rights not to agree to the agency's investigation after the lapse of 180 days. Although the appellant was mistaken in his request that his complaints should be investigated by the Commission's Office of Federal Operations because of the alleged conflicts of interest, it is clear that by the time the agency commenced the investigation the appellant had already acquired the right to have at least one of the complaints heard before an EEOC administrative judge.

Note, however, that the problem with Koch was that an agency decision to proceed with an investigation of the accepted claims was not appealable to the EEOC. The matter became ripe for appeal only after the complainant declined to participate in the investigation and his entire complaint was dismissed for failure to cooperate.
