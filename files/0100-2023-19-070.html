7. Qualifications

Generally speaking, the Commission will not second-guess an agency on the respective qualifications of candidates for a position in assessing whether pretext is present. But, "while an employer's misjudgment of the qualifications of the applicants does not in itself expose the employer to liability, it may be probative of whether the employer's reasons are a pretext for discrimination." Bell v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 05950863 (1997). The Commission will take note of qualifications when there is a wide disparity between the selectee and the complainant through the assessment of "objective standards," such as years, variety, and type of experience. Justice v. USPS, 01850207 (1987). This is especially true where the disparity in qualifications is combined with subjective assessments by the selecting official in making the selection. In the Justice case, the selecting official testified that the purpose of the interviews was to find which applicant he was "most comfortable with" and which one was "on my wavelength."

The Commission will also take particular note of the respective qualifications of the candidates when the selectee did not possess the requisite minimum qualifications of the position. See, e.g., Pendleton v. Environmental Protection Agency, 01853295 (1987).

Although uncommon, it is possible that a disparity in the qualifications of candidates is so great as to require a finding of pretext. See, e.g., Morgan v. Postmaster General, 01883540 (1989). See discussion in Chapter 18, subsection on "Superior Qualifications--Ash v. Tyson Foods."

It is more common, however, for superior qualifications by the complainant to combine with other indicia of pretext to sustain a finding of discrimination. For example, in White v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01960701 (1998), the Commission took into account the prior selection pattern of the alleged discriminating official, along with the complainant's superior qualifications in issuing a finding of discrimination:

At the time of her complaint, appellant was employed by the agency as a Development Clerk, GS-5. Believing that she was a victim of discrimination, appellant sought EEO counseling and later filed a formal EEO complaint dated August 19, 1994. The agency complied with all procedural and regulatory prerequisites, and on July 28, 1995, the EEOC Administrative Judge (AJ) issued a Recommended Decision (RD) finding discrimination based on race. In this regard, he found that appellant's alleged communication deficiency did not exist and that her superior education made her the best qualified candidate. Subsequently, however, the agency did not adopt the AJ's RD for its FAD but instead concluded that appellant was not as qualified as the four STs and that she had failed to prove by a preponderance of the evidence that she had been discriminated against on the basis of race. Appellant now appeals the FAD.

After a careful review of the record in its entirety, the Commission finds, that the AJ's RD summarized the relevant facts and referenced the appropriate regulations, policies, and laws. We therefore discern no basis to disturb the AJ's finding of discrimination.

In this regard, we note that the past practice of the SO was to choose the candidate with the most education if other factors were equal. Appellant received the same perfect rating score, 25, as the STs, yet was not selected even though she had a Master's Degree compared to two STs who had less than one year of college and the third who had only a high school diploma. In addition, although the agency maintains that appellant nonetheless was ranked at the bottom of the Best Qualified Candidates List, when the EEO Counselor asked to look at the list, he was told that it had been thrown away, the first time such a thing had ever happened in the Adjudication Division.

Furthermore, while the SO testified that he did not choose appellant because she had a communication problem, he could not explain why she had such a problem. Her supervisor, on the other hand, testified that appellant had demonstrated skill in oral communication and the two officials who interviewed appellant told the EEO counselor that she had done very well in the job interview. Yet their interview notes, sent to the SO, were thrown away by the SO before the hearing.

Finally, we note that the two incidents involving the black rat and the urine all over appellant's desk were severe enough by themselves to indicate a continuing racial bias, yet no action was taken by the SO, who also managed the division. We concur with the AJ that all the above facts taken together were conclusive evidence that the agency's articulated reasons for appellant's nonselection, among them that she was not ranked in the top ten listing of candidates, was not assertive enough, and had a communication problem, were a pretext for racial discrimination in the light of her plainly superior qualifications. See Bauer v. Bailar, 647 F. 2d 1037, 1048 (10th Cir. 1981).

See also Childs v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01961661 (1998) (internal inconsistencies of the two selecting officials, combined with the qualifications of the complainant, led to a finding of discrimination).
