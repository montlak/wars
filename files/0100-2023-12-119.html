5. Occupational Health and Safety Claims

Complaints related to workplace safety or an agency's failure to report or comply with workplace safety standards are not within the Commission's purview. The Commission confirmed as much in Evan S. v. Veterans Affairs, 2022000172 (2022), where the complainant alleged that the agency subjected him to race discrimination by failing "to provide a safe workplace for all workers, embrac[ing] a policy of negligence which was racist in nature, and refus[ing] to take precautions such as improving air ventilation and sanitation which placed Complainant at greater risk of COVID-19 because of his race." The agency dismissed for failure to state a claim because it was a collateral attack on OSHA proceedings. The Commission in Evan S. agreed:

It is well established that generally allegations of discrimination relating to workplace safety or an agency's failure to report or comply with workplace safety standards are under the jurisdiction of the Department of Labor's Occupational Health and Safety Administration ("OSHA"), and cannot be processed in the EEO complaint process. See Demarcus I. v. U.S. Postal Serv., Appeal No. 0120161563 (Aug. 12, 2016); Complainant v. U.S. Postal Serv., Appeal No. 0120151653 (Aug. 14, 2015); Grant-Garrett v. U.S. Postal Serv., Appeal No. 01A61832 (Jul. 21, 2006). Complainant's formal complaint alleges that all the employees in his workplace, including himself, were subjected to an unsafe work environment. If Complainant wishes to pursue his claims related to the health and safety of his working conditions, including COVID-19 mitigation measures, the proper venue is OSHA at the Department of Labor. The record further reflects that Complainant has already filed two separate complaints with OSHA on this matter.

In addition, based on Complainant's allegations it appears his claim constitutes a generalized grievance and, therefore, also fails to state a claim. Complainant is claiming that all employees, regardless of race, in his workplace are being subjected to the same allegedly unsafe conditions. It is not clear that he is alleging that he has personally suffered some specific injury as a result of the alleged discriminatory practice. See Crandall v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 05970508 (September 11, 1997); Rodriguez v. Department of the Treasury, EEOC Appeal No. 01970736 (August 28, 1997).

In Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120140626 (2014), the Commission found that the agency erred in dismissing as a collateral attack an allegation that he attempted to file a report on hazardous work conditions and it was returned to him. The Commission, in Complainant, explained:

In claim (2), Complainant alleged OSHA's safety violations, and the Agency's improper completion of Form 1767. We do not concur with the Agency's dismissal on the ground that this claim deals with matters under the Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA)'s jurisdiction. We acknowledge that the Commission has held that an employee cannot use the EEO complaint process to lodge a collateral attack on another proceeding. See Wills v. Dep't of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05970596 (July 30, 1998); Kleirunan v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Request No. 05940585 (Sept. 22, 1994).

In the instant claim, however, we discern nothing indicating that Complainant's claim, which has a nexus to the matter identified in claim (1), somehow constitutes a collateral attack on the "OSHA process." Instead, Complainant has claimed that he unsuccessfully attempted to prepare a form relating to a purportedly hazardous work condition, and was returned to him by a safety office as incomplete. We determine that this matter addresses a personal loss or harm to a term, condition, or privilege of employment.
