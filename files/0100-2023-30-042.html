broida eeo Chapter 21: Forms of Relief Chapter 21: Forms of Relief, Subchapter 03: Types of Equitable Relief, Section A: Back Pay, Subsection 05: Deductions From Back Pay, Part f: Health Insurance Premiums
f. Health Insurance Premiums

A complainant is entitled to offset from back pay deductions for health insurance premiums paid due to an agency's discrimination. In Allen v. Secretary of Air Force, 04940006 (1996), the Commission found it was improper for the agency to deduct from back pay that portion of the complainant's wages that went to pay for health care premiums, to the extent those payments exceeded what the complainant would have paid for health care premiums in his agency job. As the Commission noted in Allen:

Finally, petitioner also claims that the agency improperly included wages he was forced to pay out to secure health insurance benefits in the interim earnings deducted from his backpay award. He further asserts that the agency failed to consider excessive mileage he incurred in seeking and holding his interim private sector employment. We find that petitioner's backpay award should have compensated him for the loss of government subsidized health insurance coverage by reimbursing him for the health insurance premiums he paid to secure comparable insurance coverage while employed in the private sector, to the extent such payments exceeded premium payments he would have been required to make if he had secured the woodcrafter position. Therefore, the agency is directed to include any such differential in its recalculation of petitioner's backpay award either by including it in the gross backpay award or by subtracting it from his net interim earnings. The agency shall also deduct additional mileage expenses demonstrated by petitioner to have been incurred, in seeking and holding his interim employment from his net interim earnings.

See also Brockie v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 04A20005 (2002).

In Williams v. Secretary of Navy, 01A01421 (2002), the Commission further explained that the agency has an option in such cases:

With regard to the issue of payment of complainant's health insurance premiums, we find that health insurance premiums are included in a backpay award. The Commission has held that "an award of backpay should compensate a prevailing complainant for loss of health insurance coverage by either: (1) reimbursing him for health insurance premiums paid to continue in an agency-sponsored insurance plan or to secure alternative coverage; or (2) paying him for uninsured medical expenses incurred during the relevant period up to the amount the agency would have contributed to his health insurance premiums." Humiston v. United States Postal Serv., EEOC Petition No. 04980015 (Sept. 24, 1998) (citation omitted). The agency asserts that it paid its share of complainant's health insurance premiums during the relevant period along with a two percent surcharge. However, the agency has not submitted any documentation to support its position. Complainant claims that he paid the agency's share of his health insurance premiums during the relevant period, but he has not provided documentation to establish that he made these payments. Given that the record is incomplete on this issue, we find that this matter should be investigated by the agency in accordance with the Order below. Complainant also claims that he incurred extra prescription drug costs due to an alleged agency error in allowing his health insurance to lapse. We find that the agency should determine whether it failed to provide the appropriate paperwork in a timely fashion to complainant's insurance company, and whether such inaction caused complainant to incur increased prescription drug costs. On remand, the agency shall redetermine whether it has complied with provision 2(b) (regarding the health insurance premium portion of back pay) of the settlement agreement.

See also Yerger v. Postmaster General, 04A50026 (2005); Bergmann v. Attorney General, 04A20001 (2003).

It is possible for a complainant to be reimbursed for health care premiums for part of a back pay period and to be reimbursed for uninsured medical expenses for another part of the back pay period. As the Commission explained in Dropka v. Postmaster General, 01A12087 (2002):

It appears from complainant's arguments on appeal that she is seeking reimbursement for the cost of her health insurance premiums and her uninsured medical expenses during the "back pay period" (i.e., May 28, 1998 through September 29, 2000).5 While complainant may seek reimbursement of her uninsured medical expenses for a portion of the back pay period while also seeking reimbursement for paid health insurance premiums during another portion of her back pay period, we note that she is not entitled to reimbursement of both her uninsured medical costs and her paid health insurance premiums during the same period. Accordingly, during the period when complainant did not have any health insurance coverage, she would be entitled to reimbursement of her uninsured medical costs. In addition, during the period when complainant paid for her own health insurance, she could seek reimbursement for the health insurance premiums.

As part of her claim for unreimbursed medical expenses, complainant is also seeking costs associated with her and her husband's trips to medical centers which include lodging, parking and food. Since these type of expenses are not normally covered under any health insurance plan, absent evidence to show that complainant would have been reimbursed for such expenses if she had been covered under an agency's health insurance plan, we find that complainant is not entitled to reimbursement of such funds.

Lastly, complainant is seeking reimbursement of uninsured medical expenses incurred prior to the date she should have been reinstated to the PTF position (May 28, 1998). We find that complainant is not entitled to any back pay prior to May 28, 1998, including health insurance or medical costs.

The record shows that complainant provided documentation to support out-of-pocket medical expenses incurred in relation to her husband's cancer treatment after May 28, 1998, however, it is unclear, what expenses were insured medical expenses and what expenses were uninsured medical expenses. It appears from the record that complainant may have paid for her own health insurance as of July, 1998. However, the record needs to be clarified on this issue before the agency (or the Commission) can determined the appropriate back pay award. We also find that while the AJ specifically referenced complainant's husband's cancer treatment medical costs in his order, we find that as part of the AJ's back pay order, complainant is entitled to all of her uninsured medical costs during the back pay period. In addition to reimbursing complainant's uninsured medical expenses, we find that the agency is responsible for determining the amount that complainant paid for insurance premiums, deductibles, and co-payments during the back pay period and reimburse complainant for any difference between such costs and what she would have paid had she been using an agency health insurance plan.

Similarly, the agency may be required to account for any deductions it makes for health care in back pay calculations. See Carney v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 04970018 (1998). Although the Carney case is unclear, the implication seemed to be that the agency could not deduct for health care contributions for a period of time when it did not actually provide health care.
