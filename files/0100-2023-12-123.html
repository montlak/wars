9. On-The-Job Injuries and Workers' Compensation

An agency may not rely solely on the OWCP process to discharge its obligations under the Rehabilitation Act. The question as to whether an agency has reasonably accommodated a complaint is a separate inquiry from an OWCP determination. See Tyra F. v. Postmaster General, 0120182247 (2020); Lux v. Postmaster General, 0120092392 (2010).

But, discrimination because of an on-the-job injury is not within the purview of the anti-discrimination statutes. Therefore, a claim that a complainant is being retaliated against based on her on-the-job injury also is not within the EEO purview. Jenkins v. Postmaster General, 01952972 (1996). However, a claim that the complainant is being retaliated against for her EEO complaint, even though the original complaint was premised on a basis not within EEO purview, does state a claim for relief.

By and large, the Commission has been extremely inconsistent when dealing with EEO claims that relate to the processing or denial of workers' compensation claims. After initially holding that workers' compensation was the exclusive province of the Department of Labor and, thus, a workers' compensation claim could not form the basis of a discrimination claim, the Commission has retreated somewhat from that position.

For example, in Chau O. v. Postmaster General, 0120152427 (2015), the Commission explained the difference between a collateral attack on the OWCP process and a claim of discrimination that is related to an OWCP claim:

To the extent Complainant is alleging that Agency officials colluded to include false or fraudulent information on an OWCP form for Complainant, we find that this claim is a collateral attack on the OWCP process. The Commission has held that an employee cannot use the EEO complaint process to lodge a collateral attack on another proceeding. See Wills v. Dep't of Def., EEOC Request No. 05970596 (July 30, 1998); Kleinman v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Request No. 05940585 (Sept. 22, 1994); Lingad v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Request No. 05930106 (June 25, 1993). The proper forum for Complainant to have raised these challenges would be within the OWCP forum.

However. a fair reading of the record reflects that Complainant is also alleging the broader claim that the Agency issued her a modified assignment to return to work and work four hour days which conflicted with her medical restrictions. In her Information for Pre-Complaint Counseling Form, Complainant asserts that she was still under medical treatment and that the Agency was attempting to force her back to work. The record also contains an Offer of Modified Assignment accompanied by a letter from the Agency's Manager, Health and Resource Management informing Complainant that she is to return to work and work four hours per workday. The only questions for an agency to consider in determining whether a complaint states a claim are: (1) whether complainant is an aggrieved employee; and (2) whether complainant raises employment discrimination on a basis covered by EEO statutes. If these questions are answered in the affirmative, an agency must accept the complaint for processing regardless of its judgment of the merits. See Odoski v. Dep't of Energy, EEOC Appeal No. 01901496 (April 6, 1990). In the instant matter, Complainant is alleging, in part, that she was provided with a work assignment that violated her medical restrictions based on her protected classes. Thus, we find that this matter sets forth an actionable claim.

Accordingly, to the extent Complainant is alleging that Agency officials included fraudulent information on OWCP forms, we AFFIRM the Agency's dismissal of this claim. However, to the extent Complainant is alleging that the Agency provided her with a work assignment (four hour workdays) which violated her medical restrictions, we find that the Agency improperly dismissed this matter and we REMAND it to the Agency for further processing in accordance with the Order below.

For a fuller discussion of collateral attacks on the OWCP process, see Chapter 15, and Chapter 18.
