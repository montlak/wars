S. ENFORCEMENT ACTIONS

A complainant may recover attorney fees for a successful enforcement action, if the action is reasonably necessary to obtain compliance with a settlement agreement or final decision. See Landis v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01966903 (1997). However, in order to receive an award of fees for enforcement proceedings, the complainant must be the prevailing party. See Burks v. Postmaster General, 01A11249 (2001); Baker v. Secretary of Air Force, 01963227 (1997). The Commission applies the same prevailing party test in enforcement proceedings that it uses to determine fee awards in decisions on the merits.

The Commission may only award fees for administrative enforcement actions. It has no jurisdiction to award fees for civil court actions seeking enforcement. See Bermudez v. Postmaster General, 01913300 (1991). Conversely, a court may not review an EEOC award of attorney fees. See Chris v. Tenet, 221 F.3d 648 (4th Cir. 2000). In that case, the complainant, dissatisfied with an OFO fee award, filed a civil action in U.S. district court seeking review of the award. The court found it had jurisdiction only over civil actions to enforce the substantive provisions of Title VII.

An attorney will be given a reasonable amount of time to submit a fee petition for enforcement work performed after the Commission finds discrimination. In Handy v. Secretary of Transportation, FAA, 01952032 (1997), the Commission determined that fees incurred in responding to requests for reconsideration and obtaining compliance were necessary and compensable, and were timely submitted:

In its final decision, the agency denied appellant's supplemental fee request as untimely submitted. 29 CFR § 1614.501(e)(2)(i) provides that, upon receipt of a decision from the agency or the Commission awarding attorney's fees, the complainant's attorney must submit his or her itemized request for fees to the agency within thirty days. In this case, appellant's attorney timely filed her oriGINAl request for fees within thirty days of her receipt of the November 12, 1993 Commission decision binding the agency to its previous finding of discrimination and ordering a number of corrective actions, including attorney's fees. That fee request has been paid by the agency.

At issue here are fees for legal services necessitated by events which occurred after the issuance of the Commission's decision. Specifically, appellant's attorney asserts she was required to prepare a response to the agency's request for reconsideration, which was later withdrawn, as well as engage in an effort to gain full compliance with the corrective actions ordered by the Commission. The record reveals, for example, a letter to the agency from the attorney dated May 17, 1994, protesting the fact that the agency still had not provided appellant with a retroactive promotion to an appropriate position, had not given training to the official found responsible for the discrimination, and had not posted the required notice.

The Commission has held that "because a complaint is not fully processed until a complainant has received all the relief due him or her for the agency's prior discrimination, attorney's fees are available for successful compliance efforts when such efforts are necessary." Bermudez v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01913300 (October 2, 1991), request to reopen granted, EEOC Request No. 05920122 (November 30, 1992) (modifying the initial decision, but not altering the holding that attorney's fees are available for successful compliance efforts when such efforts are necessary). Obviously, because compliance efforts may be necessary long after the decision awarding corrective action has been issued, complainant's attorney is no longer bound by the thirty-day time limitation for submitting an initial fee request. In the instant matter, the Commission finds that appellant's attorney's compliance efforts were both necessary and apparently successful, and that her request for supplemental fees for those efforts was submitted within a reasonable period of time.

However, a complainant only is entitled to attorney fees for compliance issues on which he or she is successful. In Peralta v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01961850 (1997), the Commission awarded attorney fees for necessary compliance efforts after the agency did not fully comply with the terms of the settlement agreement. The attorney's efforts, including a prior appeal, were successful in that they secured the agency's compliance with the settlement agreement, including the award of an additional 19 hours of back pay. Accordingly, the Commission found that the attorney was entitled to attorney fees and costs for services rendered in connection with the prior appeal, and for services rendered in connection with the instant appeal, but only insofar as such services related to the issue of attorney fees for compliance. The attorney was not entitled to attorney fees for services rendered in connection with the instant appeal insofar as those services related to appellant's allegations of further breaches of the settlement agreement, or to her new allegations of discrimination, because appellant did not prevail on those matters.

Fees are properly awarded where the agency's delay in complying with a Commission decision results in a petition for enforcement. See Williams v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 04960024 (1997). In that case, the Commission ordered the agency to conduct a supplemental investigation and issue a new final decision within 60 days. When the agency did not comply, the complainant retained counsel and filed a petition for enforcement. Prior to the Commission's enforcement decision, but almost a year late, the agency completed the supplemental investigation and issued a new final decision. The Commission's decision in Williams is exceptionally brief and contains no analysis of whether the petition for enforcement caused the agency's compliance. See also Wiggins v. Postmaster General, 04A00011 (2001) (fees were appropriate where a compliance action resulted in payment of interest); Velasquez v. Attorney General, 04960018 (1997).

It is not necessary for the complainant to file a petition for enforcement with the Commission to be entitled to attorney fees. Even if the agency cures its breaches after notification by the complainant, he is entitled to attorney fees. See Stage v. Secretary of Labor, 01973329 (1998). However, in that case, the Commission rejected most of a claim for over 96 hours in time spent by the attorney because only a portion of those services actually related to the breach allegations. The complainant was not entitled to fees for time the attorney spent on matters related to correction of her official personnel file because the complainant never alleged any breach of the settlement agreement with respect to personnel files.

Many enforcement petitions are filed simply because agencies do not provide complainants with documentation of payments made, such as back pay or damage awards. Money is paid by the agency, but the complainant gets no accounting of how the agency calculated the payment or what the payment represents. In Woodley v. Postmaster General, 0120082337 (2010), the Commission found it could not determine if the complainant was a prevailing party for purposes of a fee award where the agency had paid money to the complainant but failed to explain what it was paying for. The Commission ordered the case remanded to the agency for an accounting with a notation that if agency still owed the complainant money as a result of its previous decision, then he would be a prevailing party for fee purposes.

It should be noted that the Merit Systems Protection Board will similarly award attorney fees in compliance proceedings. However, when the agency complies before a finding of noncompliance, the Board requires some showing that the enforcement petition caused the agency to come into compliance before awarding fees. Generally, when compliance occurs after the filing of the petition, the Board will presume a causal connection between the petition and the agency's compliance unless the agency can demonstrate to the contrary. It could well be the Commission employs a similar standard but found it unnecessary to articulate it in Williams, supra, because the long delay in the agency's compliance, even after the petition was filed, was conclusive evidence of a causal connection between the petition and the agency's eventual compliance actions.
