MERIT PRINCIPLES AND PROHIBITED PRACTICES

While it is obvious that prohibited personnel practices adversely affect those who are directly involved, they also have a negative impact on others in the workplace. Studies have found that employees who merely perceive that others have been subjected to a PPP have a negative reaction even if they are not personally affected. This emphasizes the importance of having a complete understanding of prohibited personnel practices because it is likely that your agency will act swiftly if it finds a PPP may have occurred.

Your status as a manager -- and the influence and control you have over the work lives of your employees -- requires you to uphold merit system principles and to steer clear of committing prohibited personnel practices. Failure to comply with merit system principles, which usually involves the commission of a PPP, can result in an adverse action up to and including removal from the federal service.

It is a prohibited personnel practice to take an action in violation of the merit system principles. For example, one merit system principle states that employees "should be protected against reprisal for the lawful disclosure" of waste, fraud, and abuse; it is a prohibited personnel practice to retaliate against an employee for such disclosures.

Several independent agencies have various jurisdiction over allegations related to prohibited personnel practices.

  • • The Merit Systems Protection Board is empowered to hear and decide complaints for corrective or disciplinary action when an agency is alleged to have committed a prohibited personnel practice.
  • • The Office of Special Counsel investigates allegations that an agency has committed a PPP and may seek disciplinary or corrective action for a prohibited personnel practice before the MSPB.
  • • The Office of Personnel Management is charged with holding managers and HR officials accountable for efficient and effective HR management in support of agency missions in accordance with merit system principles. To carry out this responsibility, OPM has established the Office of Merit System Accountability and Compliance.
  • • The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission makes the final administrative decision on claims that an agency has committed unlawful employment discrimination. Such discrimination is inconsistent with merit system principles.

Merit systems principles

The merit system principles are nine basic standards governing the management of the executive branch workforce. They are designed to ensure that the federal workforce is recruited, managed, and rewarded based on merit, rather than on political affiliation or other non-merit factors.

Merit system principles require managers to:

  1. 1. Recruit qualified persons from all segments of society, and select and advance them on the basis of merit after fair and open competition.
  2. 2. Treat employees and applicants fairly and equitably without regard to political affiliation, race, color, religion, national origin, sex, marital status, age, or disability, and with proper regard for their privacy and constitutional rights.
  3. 3. Provide equal pay for equal work, and recognize and reward excellent performance.
  4. 4. Maintain high standards of integrity, conduct, and concern for the public interest.
  5. 5. Manage employees efficiently and effectively.
  6. 6. Retain, reward, and separate employees on the basis of their performance.
  7. 7. Educate and train employees when it will result in better organizational or individual performance.
  8. 8. Protect employees from arbitrary action, personal favoritism, or coercion for partisan political purposes.
  9. 9. Protect employees against reprisal for "whistleblower" activity, that is, the lawful disclosure of information concerning violations of law, gross mismanagement, gross waste of funds, or abuse of authority.

Prohibited personnel practices

Prohibited personnel practices are just what the term suggests -- actions that individuals with personnel authority over others (i.e., managers and supervisors) are legally prohibited from taking. A personnel action, which includes appointments, promotions, reassignments, disciplinary actions, and other personnel matters, may have to be involved for a prohibited personnel practice to occur.

Prohibited personnel practices are defined by law and provide that managers shall not:

  1.  1. Discriminate on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age, disability, marital status, or political affiliation.
  2.  2. Solicit or consider any personnel recommendation that is not based on a personal knowledge or records of job-related factors such as performance, ability, aptitude, general qualifications, character, loyalty, or suitability.
  3.  3. Coerce an employee to perform or support political activity.
  4.  4. Deceive or deliberately obstruct any person who is attempting to compete for employment.
  5.  5. Influence or attempt to influence any person to withdraw from competition for the purpose of improving or injuring the prospects of another person for employment.
  6.  6. Grant any preference or advantage not authorized by law, regulation, or rule to any employee or applicant to improve or injure the prospects of another applicant.
  7.  7. Hire, promote, or influence the hiring or promotion of a relative.
  8.  8. Retaliate against an employee for whistleblowing activity.
  9.  9. Retaliate against employees or applicants for exercising their appeal rights, for testifying or cooperating with an inspector general or the Office of Special Counsel, or for refusing to comply with an instruction that would require the employee to break the law.
  10. 10. Discriminate based on personal conduct or actions that do not adversely affect an employee's or applicant's job performance.
  11. 11. Violate any law, rule, or regulation implementing or directly concerning the merit principles.
  12. 12. Violate veterans' preference by taking or failing to take a personnel action.
  13. 13. Impose a nondisclosure agreement on an employee if the agreement does not expressly state that the employee's rights under the whistleblower law superseded the terms of the agreement.
  14. 14. Access the medical records of another employee or an applicant for employment in committing or furthering any of the other prohibited personnel practices.

Here's a closer look at some of the more common prohibited personnel practices:

Discrimination. As a manager, you are responsible for treating all your employees fairly. This includes avoiding and preventing all forms of discrimination in the workplace.

The OSC is authorized by law to investigate allegations of discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age, or disability. However, procedures for investigating such complaints have already been established in federal agencies and the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission. Therefore, to avoid duplicating those investigative processes, the OSC follows a general policy of deferring complaints involving discrimination to those agencies' procedures.

The EEOC doesn't have jurisdiction over allegations of discrimination based on marital status and political affiliation. Such allegations, however, may be prohibited personnel practices or other violations of law subject to investigation by the OSC. Further, although the EEOC has stated that sexual orientation is prohibited by Title VII, an employee who believes they have been subjected to sexual orientation discrimination may choose to pursue a prohibited personnel practice rather than filing an EEO complaint.

Granting an illegal advantage. Granting a preference or advantage to an individual for the purpose of improving their prospects of obtaining a promotion or appointment is a prohibited personnel practice.

One situation where this may become a problem is when HR works with hiring managers to reclassify and readvertise jobs multiple times in order to reach a particular candidate. Agencies cannot do anything that interferes with the process of open competition, including:

  • • Rewriting position descriptions and requesting multiple certificates to ensure the referral of a name-requested individual.
  • • Convincing a preference eligible candidate to withdraw their application from consideration.

Managers and selecting officials may find themselves involved in a PPP if they ask HR staff to try to hire a particular individual. For example, HR specialists who announced a position three different times, limited the qualifications for eligibility, and decreased the open period for the vacancy committed a prohibited personnel practice because they engaged in those actions after management attempted to steer them toward hiring a particular candidate.

Legally permissible actions like the ones taken by these HR specialists can be used for an illegal purpose if the intent is to afford preferential treatment to an individual.

Nepotism. Nepotism is the grant of improper preference, assistance, or advancement to an individual related by blood or marriage. Nepotism is forbidden as a prohibited personnel practice and under other federal law.

Public officials may not appoint or promote, or recommend for appointment or promotion, a relative to a position in the agency in which they are serving or over which they exercise control.

Note, however, that an official's mere presence in the chain of command for a position to which a relative is appointed does not constitute impermissible nepotism. The relative of a public official can be appointed by a subordinate of the official if the official is in no way involved in the action and if the agency concerned has no regulations prohibiting such employment.

Veterans' preference rights. Veterans' preference is a benefit granted by law to individuals who meet certain criteria related to military service. Its purpose is to recognize the personal sacrifices veterans have made to society through their military service.

The most visible form of veterans' preference is preference in federal hiring. Veterans' preference augments the rating of a qualified veteran -- a "preference eligible" -- in an employment examination and restricts the circumstances in which an agency may select a non-preference-eligible applicant over a preference-eligible applicant.

A common prohibited personnel practice that seems to cause problems for agencies is passing over veterans without OPM approval. Managers should work closely with the HR department to make sure veterans' preference is provided where required.

Individuals who believe that their veterans' preference rights have been violated may file an appeal to the MSPB under the Veterans Employment Opportunities Act.

Kirkpatrick PPPs. The Dr. Chris Kirkpatrick Whistleblower Protection Act of 2017 is known to enhance accountability for whistleblower retaliation. However, it also imposes obligations on the head of an agency to propose disciplinary action following a determination that a supervisor has committed any of the following three specific PPPs (referred to as the "Kirkpatrick PPPs" by the MSPB):

  1.  8. Retaliating against an employee for whistleblowing activity.
  2.  9. Retaliating against employees or applicants for exercising their appeal rights, for testifying or cooperating with an inspector general or the OSC, or for refusing to comply with an instruction that would require the employee to break the law.
  3. 14. Accessing the medical record of another employee or an applicant for employment as a part of, or otherwise in furtherance of, any conduct described in the other prohibited personnel practices.

If any of these Kirkpatrick PPPs occurs, the head of the agency shall, for a first offense, propose a suspension of no less than three days, although the agency head may propose an additional penalty, such as a reduction in grade. For a second offense, the agency head shall propose a removal.

While many responsibilities assigned to the head of an agency can be delegated (including the authority to act as deciding official in a traditional adverse action), the law explicitly states that this responsibility may not be delegated. Also, although the agency head should exercise judgment when deciding to implement the proposed action, the proposal itself is not discretionary. The law states that the agency head "shall propose" the required action.

The Office of Special Counsel

The Office of Special Counsel is an independent federal investigative and prosecutorial agency. Its basic authorities come from four federal statutes:

  1. 1. The Civil Service Reform Act.
  2. 2. The Whistleblower Protection Act.
  3. 3. The Hatch Act.
  4. 4. The Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act.

The OSC's primary mission is to safeguard the merit system by protecting federal employees and applicants from PPPs, especially reprisal for whistleblowing.

The OSC receives, investigates, and prosecutes allegations of PPPs, with an emphasis on protecting federal government whistleblowers. The OSC seeks corrective action remedies (such as back pay and reinstatement), by negotiation or from the MSPB, for injuries suffered by complainants. The OSC is also authorized to file complaints at the MSPB to seek disciplinary action against individuals who commit PPPs.

The OSC has jurisdiction over PPPs committed against most employees or applicants for employment in executive branch agencies and the Government Printing Office, as well as allegations of whistleblower retaliation for employees of government corporations, the Federal Aviation Administration, and the Transportation Security Administration.

The OSC has no jurisdiction over PPPs committed against employees of:

  • • Central Intelligence Agency, Defense Intelligence Agency, National Security Agency, and certain other intelligence agencies excluded by the president.
  • • Government Accountability Office.
  • • Federal Bureau of Investigation.
  • • U.S. Postal Service (except for cases of nepotism).
  • • Postal Regulatory Commission.
  • • Federal Aviation Administration (except for cases of retaliation).
  • • Transportation Security Administration (except for cases of discrimination and retaliation).

Investigations. The OSC may contact you if it is conducting an investigation into allegations of PPPs that may have occurred in your agency. This means that you have been identified as someone who has or may have information relevant to the matter.

They are also authorized to issue subpoenas for documents or the attendance and testimony of witnesses. During an investigation, the OSC may require employees and others to testify under oath, sign written statements, or respond formally to written questions.

Federal employees are also required to provide to the OSC any information, testimony, documents, and material, the disclosure of which is not otherwise prohibited by law or regulation, in investigations of matters under civil service law, rule, or regulation. The same rule requires federal agencies to make employees available to testify, on official time, and to provide pertinent records to the OSC.

In most PPP cases, the OSC investigator obtains relevant documents and interviews the complainant and agency employees who may have knowledge about events relevant to the investigation. These interviews may be conducted in person at the agency facility, at an OSC office, or by telephone.

Your role in OSC investigations. You may be a witness or a subject of an OSC investigation of an alleged PPP. Witnesses are individuals who have, or may have, factual information about the events upon which the allegations are based. They may be personnel officials, coworkers of the complainant, or other persons who witnessed events related to the complaint.

Federal agencies and employees must provide information to the OSC in connection with its investigations. You are required to answer all of the investigator's questions and to answer them fully and truthfully. If you do not understand the question, you may ask the investigator to restate it or clarify it. The investigator will take notes on what you say and may tape the interview. The investigator may ask you to sign an affidavit or sworn statement.

"Subjects" are agency officials who had authority to take the personnel actions at issue or who may have been responsible for those personnel actions. The OSC routinely interviews agency officials to obtain information about the allegations in the complaint from the agency's perspective.

If the OSC finds that an agency official committed a PPP, that official may be subject to disciplinary action. For this reason, if the OSC designates you a subject, you may want to obtain legal representation and have counsel present during interviews. The OSC will notify you if it considers you to be a subject. If you are not so notified, you can assume that you do not face possible disciplinary action in the matter under investigation.

Stay of personnel action. An employee can ask the Office of Special Counsel to seek to delay, or "stay," an adverse personnel action until after an OSC investigation is completed. If the OSC reasonably believes that the proposed action is the result of a prohibited personnel practice, it may ask the agency involved to delay the personnel action.

If the agency does not agree to delay the action, the OSC can ask the MSPB to stay the action. The OSC doesn't have the authority to stay a personnel action. If the MSPB grants a stay, the head of the agency must give priority to a request for a transfer submitted by the employee.

Remedies

If there is sufficient evidence to prove that a PPP occurred, the Office of Special Counsel can seek corrective action, disciplinary action, or both. Alternatively, some parties may agree to mediate their dispute in order to reach a mutually agreeable resolution of the PPP complaint.

Corrective action. The OSC may enter into discussions with an agency at any stage of a pending matter in pursuit of a resolution acceptable to all parties. The OSC follows a policy of early negotiation to obtain appropriate corrective action (and/or disciplinary action) for apparent violations.

The OSC is required by law to first seek voluntary corrective action from the agency involved. If the agency fails to remedy a PPP upon request of the OSC, corrective action may also be obtained through litigation before the MSPB.

An MSPB action begins with the filing of a petition by the OSC, alleging that there are reasonable grounds to believe that a prohibited personnel practice has occurred, exists, or is about to occur. Corrective actions that can be ordered by the MSPB include job restoration, reversal of suspensions and other adverse actions, reimbursement of attorney's fees, back pay, medical and other costs and damages.

Current or former federal employees and applicants who allege that they were subjected to any personnel action because of whistleblowing may seek corrective action in an individual right of action appeal to the MSPB. By law, the employee or applicant must seek corrective action from the OSC before filing an IRA appeal.

An IRA appeal may be filed:

  • • After the OSC closes a matter in which reprisal for whistleblowing has been alleged; or
  • • If the OSC has not notified the complainant within 120 days of receiving an allegation of whistleblower reprisal that it will seek corrective action.

Disciplinary action. The OSC may also seek disciplinary action against any employee believed to be responsible for committing a prohibited personnel practice.

The OSC begins a disciplinary action case by filing a complaint with the MSPB, charging an employee with the commission of a prohibited personnel practice, and seeking disciplinary action against that person. Individuals found by the MSPB to have committed a PPP are subject to removal, reduction in grade, debarment from federal employment for up to five years, suspension, reprimand, or fine of up to $1,000.

In the alternative, at any time during its investigation of a matter, the OSC may authorize the agency involved to take disciplinary action against an employee believed to be responsible for committing a prohibited personnel practice. During an OSC investigation, an agency may not take disciplinary action against an employee for the alleged prohibited activity under investigation, or for any related activity, without approval from the OSC.

Furthermore, the Office of Special Counsel may intervene as a matter of right, or otherwise participate in most proceedings before the MSPB. The OSC may not intervene in certain proceedings, such as individual right of action appeals or matters otherwise appealable to the MSPB, without the consent of the person initiating the proceeding.

Mistakes to avoid

Because of your status as a manager, you are required to uphold merit system principles and avoid committing prohibited personnel practices.

Do not:

  • • Fail to recognize due process rights. Your employees are entitled to certain rights, including appealing an agency personnel action to the MSPB. Make sure that you do not interfere with those rights.
  • • Make unilateral decisions. You may think that you are doing the right thing when an employee seeks to make a claim of discrimination or reprisal, but you should never make that assumption. Make sure you seek help from legal counsel or HR.
  • • Take a meritless personnel action. This is a red flag for potential prohibited personnel violations. If you have a poor performer or an employee who has committed misconduct that can lead to an adverse action, make sure that you document the act properly and take the appropriate steps.
  • • Fail to utilize the OSC when political activities are at issue. A simple phone call or email to the OSC can clarify the rules when it comes to the Hatch Act and what is allowed when election time rolls around.

Ask FMG

Q. I'm the selecting official for a vacant position in my department and I want a current employee to be promoted to this supervisory position. However, they don't quite meet all the qualifications for the job. Can I ask HR to make some changes to help get the employee promoted?

A. No. Granting a preference or advantage to an individual for the purpose of improving their prospects of obtaining a promotion or appointment is a prohibited personnel practice. Managers and selecting officials may find themselves involved in a PPP if they ask HR staff to try to hire a particular individual.

For example, HR specialists who announced a position three different times, limited the qualifications for eligibility, and decreased the open period for the vacancy committed a prohibited personnel practice because they engaged in those actions after management attempted to steer them toward hiring a particular candidate.

Q. The brother of my second-level manager is a candidate for a position that I manage. My second-level manager has recused himself from any input or involvement in the hiring process for the position. Is this a nepotism problem in the making?

A. No. An official's mere presence in the chain of command for a position to which a relative is appointed does not constitute impermissible nepotism. The relative of a public official can be appointed by a subordinate of the official if the official is in no way involved in the action and if the agency concerned has no regulations prohibiting such employment.

Q. Can I contact the MSPB for advice on whether a particular planned action is consistent with the merit system principles or could be a prohibited personnel practice?

A. The MSPB speaks primarily through its decisions and its studies. The board provides information about its decisions, studies, and procedures to groups through its outreach and education program. The MSPB is not permitted to issue advisory opinions. Contact your HR office for help or your agency's ethics officer.

Q. An OSC investigator wants to interview me about a PPP charge made against another manager in my department. I don't really want to get involved in this matter. Do I have to answer the investigator's questions?

A. Yes. The OSC is authorized to issue subpoenas for documents or the attendance and testimony of witnesses. During an investigation, the OSC may require employees and others to testify under oath, sign written statements, or respond formally to written questions.

Federal employees are also required to provide to the OSC any information, testimony, documents, and material, the disclosure of which is not otherwise prohibited by law or regulation, in investigations of matters under civil service law, rule, or regulation. The same rule requires federal agencies to make employees available to testify, on official time, and to provide pertinent records to the OSC.

Q. I have no intention of committing a PPP, but I know that the employee will claim that I retaliated against him. What can I do?

A. Make sure that you properly document all incidents and evidence of poor performance and misconduct. Through documentation, you should be able to show that your actions are proper so long as you follow through by using the proper channels.
