2. Auxiliary Agencies

When is an organization sponsored by a federal agency part of that agency? That was a question the Commission considered without resolution in Ellis v. U.S. Coast Guard, 01831341 (1983). The appellant, a minister, had applied for membership in the Coast Guard Auxiliary and alleged that denial was because of his ministerial status. The agency dismissed the complaint on the basis that no discrimination occurred. The Commission noted that before any other resolution of the complaint could be considered, a jurisdictional question had to be decided. The Commission ordered the agency to produce detailed information on the nature and functions of the Auxiliary. The information included the duties and responsibilities of Auxiliary members, whether they received compensation, whether membership was a precondition of regular employment with the agency or if members received any preferential consideration for employment, and how many of the agency's regular employees served in the Auxiliary prior to employment with the agency. The inference is that such an organization may come within the purview of the EEOC depending upon the answers to those questions.

In Fenstermacher v. Secretary of State, 05930114 (1994), the Commission further explored the criteria for evaluating whether an entity is an agency of the federal government within the ambit of Title VII. The Commission identified three primary factors to consider in determining whether an auxiliary entity is an agency of the federal government for Title VII purposes: (1) the purpose of the organization; (2) the statutory authority under which the entity was created and operates; and, (3) the extent to which the government controls the entity. In applying these factors in Fenstermacher, the Commission found:

The Commission has, in the past, considered whether an entity is a public or private organization within the meaning of the statutes the Commission enforces. After examining a variety of factors, the Commission concluded that the Kennedy Center and the Smithsonian Institution were part of the federal government and subject to § 717 of Title VII. See Commission Decisions Nos. 89-2, 89-3, CCH Empl. Prac. Guide 6873, 6874. In addition, two circuit courts, albeit in contexts different from that of the complaint here, have also assessed whether entities were public or private under the ADEA.

The D.C. Circuit applied a narrow test to conclude that Radio Free Europe and Radio Liberty were not part of the federal government because the government did not control the day-to-day operations of the radio stations. Ralis v. RFE/RL. Inc., 770 F.2d 1121, 1125-26 (D.C. Cir. 1985). The First Circuit applied a somewhat broader analysis to find that the organization that operated the post exchange and canteen for Puerto Rico's military employees was part of the state government because, among other things, it performed a broad government function and because its operations were authorized under Puerto Rico's military code. Aledo-Garcia v. Puerto Rico Nat'l Guard Fund, Inc., 887 F.2d 354 (1st Cir. 1989).

Under these precedents, the significant factors for evaluating the status of the organization include: (1) the purpose of the organization, i.e., whether it serves an important governmental role; (2) the statutory authority under which the organization was created and operates; and, perhaps most importantly, (3) the extent of government control of the operations of the entity. Other evidence of governmental status or involvement may also be relevant. Each case turns on its own facts. Using the above framework, however, we have analyzed the information made available to the Commission and have concluded that additional information is needed to make a final determination concerning the status of AKRA and the Commissary.

A. Purpose of the Organization

Although the agency's file contained an unsigned copy, the Commission notes that AKRA's Charter (which was subject to the approval of the Assistant Secretary of State for Administration), reads in relevant part:

Pursuant to the authority granted to the Secretary of State under Sec. 31 of the State Department Basic Authorities Act of 1956, as amended, civilian officers and employees of the Government at the American Embassy, Kingston, Jamaica, have formed an Association to operate non-government services and facilities for the benefit of U.S. Government employees and dependents. [AKRA], upon permission of the Assistant Secretary of State for Administration of the Department of State, will operate these services and facilities under the following rules, requirements and guidelines.

AKRA's charter also lists among its purposes, again in relevant part, "[t]o provide support services to the...Embassy as are required to enhance the quality of life of its members and to further the mission of the [U.S.] agencies operating in the locality abroad. In providing such services the Association shall follow all federal regulations and the contracting procedures defined in 6 FAM 531.11." Among the services AKRA stated it would provide its members were a commissary, snack bar and restaurant, and recreational facilities and related activities. AKRA would also provide its members with products for purchase.

The charter, inter alia, further states that "the Association shall: [b]e subject to the oversight of the Chief of Mission who shall be kept informed of Association activities. Annually, the Chief of Mission or his designee shall certify that the activities of the Association meet the requirements established by this charter."

AKRA's charter further provides that it "is subject to revocation for failure to comply with governing laws and regulations promulgated by the United States Government, including operating and reporting requirements set forth by the Department of State. Such revocation shall be determined by the Assistant Secretary of State for Administration upon recommendation of the Commissary Central Fund Board."

The quoted language of the charter is consistent with the language of Sec. 31 of the State Department Basic Authorities Act of 1956, now codified at 22 USC § 2703. That sec. authorizes the creation of "non-Government operated services and facilities at posts abroad." Under that statute, services and facilities are to be made available, if possible, to all federal employees and their dependents stationed at the locality. 22 USC § 2703(a), (c).

Both the statute and the charter appear to envision that services such as the Commissary will advance the goals of the U.S. Government abroad and serve individuals who are themselves federal employees. AKRA and the Commissary thus appear to serve a governmental purpose under Commission precedent cited above. We also note, however, that the statute and the charter explicitly cite the function of AKRA to operate "non-government" services and facilities.

B. Statutory Authority

As noted immediately above, the Foreign Service Act empowers the Secretary of State to "authorize and assist in the establishment, maintenance, and operation" of services like the commissary. 22 USC § 2703(a). Moreover, AKRA's charter must be authorized, and can be revoked, by State Department Officials. The agency's FAM states that without a government-approved charter, an association like AKRA may not "operate as a USG instrumentality." As a result, it appears that AKRA and the commissary were created, and are maintained, under statutory authority. Significantly, furthermore, that statutory authority provides that the authorized services are to be maintained and operated by "civilian officers and employees of the Government." 22 USC § 2703(a) (emphasis added).

C. Control of Operations

The Commission finds that AKRA's By-Laws provide, inter alia, that "Special Membership may be granted at the discretion of the Ambassador...." In addition, AKRA's Chairperson is required to "maintain close liaison with the Chief of Mission and the Administrative Officer of the Post to ensure close cooperation and compliance with official policies...."

Moreover, the Commission finds that an unclassified memorandum, dated November 23, 1991, was written on Embassy stationery to the Deputy Chief of Mission, Kingston, Jamaica, by the agency's EEO Counselor, and stated in relevant part: "It is my recommendation that it would be in the Embassy's best interest if the recent AKRA Board decision were overridden or vetoed as soon as possible."

Further, as we indicated above, the agency conceded its complete control over the Commissary, both from a regulatory standpoint as well as through the authority of the Ambassador and deputy. Such control is exemplified by those portions of the record which we have already discussed, including the fact that one of its purposes was to further the mission of U.S. agencies. This is coupled with evidence in the record, again referred to above, that the agency could override or veto AKRA Board decisions.

The Commission also finds that AKRA job vacancies were posted in the Embassy's newsletter and that an SF-171, the standard form for federal employment, was required as part of the application process. Moreover, although not dispositive of the issue, the Commission finds that the agency and appellant engaged in the EEO process, including the acceptance of her complaint, a resolution entered into with agency personnel, and then reinstatement of her complaint upon her allegation that the settlement agreement had been breached.

Moreover, the agency's FAM clearly indicates its control over the association, from approval of the charter itself, to proposed changes in services, to authorization to operate on federally-owned or leased "property and/or under U.S. Government auspices. Without a charter, associations may not import duty-free products under the name of the ambassador, operate as a USG instrumentality, receive loans or grants from the Central Fund, receive assistance from the Office of Commissary and Recreation Affairs, or receive support (space, utilities, properties, etc.) from the post." 6 FAM 515.1.12.

The Commission also finds persuasive language in the FAM which states that: "Commissary or mess services and recreation facilities shall not be established in localities where another U.S. agency operates similar services or facilities unless the principal officer of the post determines they are necessary." 6 FAM 524.1. (Emphasis added.) Finally, we note that the FAM declares that: "Employee associations are United States Government instrumentalities for federal tax purposes. Therefore, the employee association is subject to FICA employer taxes under 26 USC § 3121, and to withholding requirements under 26 USC § 3404...." 6 FAM 541.13

In light of our analysis in this case, and after carefully reviewing the facts, the Commission has determined that further inquiry is necessary before the issue can ultimately be determined. Accordingly, the Commission is directing the agency, in the Commission's ORDER below, to provide it with certain information and documentation.
