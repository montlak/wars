B. INTERPRETING LANGUAGE IN CONTEXT

One of the primary rules for interpreting settlement agreements is that the terms used in an agreement are given their plain English meaning. This rule, however, is subject to an exception; namely, terms are not given their plain English meaning when to do so would render a provision meaningless or make it impossible to perform.

A good example of the exception was addressed by the Commission in Crutcher v. Postmaster General, 01A06032 (2002). In that case, the parties agreed that an investigation would be conducted by the "EEOC office" and that the complainant's supervisor would not be placed in a supervisory capacity until the investigation was concluded. In rejecting the plain English interpretation of "EEOC office" in Crutcher, the Commission explained:

Ordinarily, the plain meaning of "EEOC" is the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, not the agency's EEO office. The phrase "the EEOC office," in the context of a settlement agreement between the agency and complainant is less clear. In fact, the parties came to opposite conclusions regarding the meaning of the term. Since the Commission was not involved in settlement negotiations, nor a party to the settlement agreement, to find that "the EEOC office" referred to the Commission would render the provision unenforceable--the parties cannot bind the Commission to perform the investigation.

When interpreting express terms in a contract, "an interpretation which gives a reasonable, lawful, and effective meaning to all the terms is preferred to an interpretation which leaves a part unreasonable, unlawful, or of no effect." Restatement (Second) of Contracts 203(a) (1981); see 1 Corbin on Contracts 4.1 Rev. Ed. (Joseph M. Perillo, ed., West, (1993)(1963)) (fn. 4: "A contract will be given that construction which will make it valid and binding instead of a construction which would make it void or unenforceable"). To maintain the enforceability of the settlement agreement, the Commission finds that the term "the EEOC office" refers to the agency's EEO office.

In some cases, it is necessary to consider the context of the case in order to interpret the settlement agreement. For example, in Shade v. Postmaster General, 01A55219 (2006), the parties agreed that the agency would provide a "scooter" to accommodate the complainant's medical condition. The parties fully understood at the time they executed the agreement that the complainant required a sit--down scooter and not a stand--up scooter. The Commission had little difficulty in rejecting the agency's argument that because the type of scooter was not specified, it could provide a stand--up scooter to comply with the agreement. The Commission found that, in the context of the agreement, the agency was obliged to provide the complainant with an appropriate scooter and the stand--up scooter did not meet this requirement.

By contrast, in Ramsey v. Postmaster General, 0120103467 (2010), the Commission declined to interpret a settlement clause that prohibited employees from playing radios without a personal headset to other devices that played music. The Commission, in Ramsey, found:

The settlement terms prohibit "radios." The alleged events presented by Complaint, however, concern music coming from computer and cell phones. The Commission is not persuaded by Complainant's contention that the parties agreed that "personal listening devices" included personal computers, cell phones, MP3 players, ipods, etc. Such alleged shared understanding is not reflected in the executed agreement. If Complainant sought to cease the playing of any music in the work area, irrespective of the device, she should reduced her intentions to writing. Complainant has not established that the Agency breached the February 2009 agreement.

The decision in Ramsey does seem to be an overly restrictive interpretation of the agreement given that the complainant suffered from obsessive-compulsive disorder and was distracted by music in the workplace. However, the decision does demonstrate the necessity of using precise language in settlement agreements.
