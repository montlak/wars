e. Self-Employment Income

There is no bright-line rule as to whether self-employment constitutes a failure to mitigate damages in relation to a back pay award. The general rule is that self-employment does not per se indicate a failure to mitigate damages, but that it will constitute a failure to mitigate when the complainant engages in a self-employment venture that is a change from his or her previous career direction.

In Beaton v. Attorney General, 04A30044 (2004), the Commission found that the complainant's starting of a business after his nonselection did constitute a failure to mitigate damages:

After a careful review of the record, including the compliance file in this case, the Commission finds that the agency has complied with the order. A back-pay claimant under Title VII generally has a duty to mitigate damages. Specifically, Section 706(g) of Title VII, 42 U.S.C. 2000e-5(g) provides that "interim earnings or amounts earnable with reasonable diligence by the person or persons discriminated against shall operate to reduce the back pay otherwise allowable." The burden is on the agency, however, to establish that the employee failed in his duty to mitigate. The Commission has generally held that an agency must satisfy a two-prong test to meet its burden of proof. This test requires the agency to show that: (1) petitioner failed to use reasonable care and diligence in seeking a suitable position, and (2) there were suitable positions available which petitioner could have discovered and for which he or she was qualified. See McNeil v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Petition No. 04990007 (December 9, 1999), citing Simmons v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Petition No. 04930005 (December 10, 1993). Where a petitioner makes no effort to mitigate damages and does not explain the lack of effort, the agency does not have to meet the second prong. Id.

The Third Circuit held that the "threshold question" in determining whether back-pay should be awarded for periods of self-employment, is whether the [petitioner's] "choice to enter into his own business [was] a reasonable method of mitigating damages?" Carden v. Westinghouse Electric Corp., 850 F.2d 996, 1005 (3d Cir. 1988), citing, inter alia, Ford Motor Company v. EEOC, 458 U.S. 219, 232, 102 S. Ct. 3057, 3066, 73 L. Ed.2d 721, 733 (1982). In this case the burden to prove that it was not, falls on the agency. The decision to become self-employed does not, by itself, indicate a lack of reasonable diligence. Id.

The decision to go into business for ones-self does not automatically remove an agency's obligation to provide back-pay. Taylor v. Central Pennsylvania Drug and Alcohol Services Corp., 890 F. Supp. 360, (M.D. Pa. 1995). In Taylor, the court found that the plaintiff was attempting "to mitigate her back pay damages by starting her own businesses when no offers of employment were forthcoming" despite the fact that the venture ultimately failed. Taylor at 373. Further, the court found that the plaintiff had shown that had outside employment become available to her, she would have sold her business and returned to the labor force. However, in Johnson v. Memphis Police Dept., 713 F. Supp. 244, 248 (W.D. Tenn. 1989), the court held that "the plaintiff voluntarily took himself off the type of employment for which he was trained and undertook to learn and develop a different means of livelihood, which the defendant should not be required to finance in retrospect." Here, we find that the record reflects that petitioner voluntarily resigned from a position in the field for which he was trained, and in which he was earning a higher wage than he would have had he been hired by the agency in the position at issue, in order to open a small business. As such, we find that, like the plaintiff in Johnson, petitioner elected to remove himself from the job market at such time as he went into business for himself. The record does not reflect that this was an attempt by petitioner to mitigate his damages. As a result, we find that the agency is not required to award petitioner back-pay for the period during which he was self-employed.

When an agency is allowed to deduct business earnings from back pay, it may not use gross earnings to calculate the deduction. The proper amount to use is gross earning minus business losses and ordinary and necessary business expenses. Bergmann v. Attorney General, 04A60022 (2006). As the Commission explained in Duhn v. Secretary of Transportation, 0420060040 (2008):

In our prior decision, we ordered the agency to pay petitioner back pay and benefits from April 1, 1996 through November 30, 2002 the period of time she was out of work on leave without pay. However, petitioner maintains that the agency miscalculated the amount of back pay owed to her because instead of using her net income as the base point for the back pay calculations, it used the gross receipts from her self-owned businesses. According to Black's Law Dictionary, "gross receipts" are the "total amount of money or the value of other considerations received from selling property or from performing services." Black's Law Dictionary 486 (6th ed. 1991). Like gross earnings, which are an individual's total income before any appropriate adjustments are made, gross receipts are the total sales of a business before any pertinent deductions. During the relevant time period, complainant reported a total $218,978.00 in gross earnings. The agency used this amount to calculate the back pay award. However, petitioner argues that this calculation fails to consider her expenses in generating the earnings. Her gross earnings, or gross receipts, were not her income. She insists that the true indicator of her income during the relevant time period is her "net income," which was $59,375.00. Net business income is defined as "the profit of a business arrived at by deducting operating expenses and taxes from gross receipts." Id. at 525. Petitioner pleads that the agency should recalculate the back pay award based on her net income of $59,375. The agency did not respond to this assertion. We interpret the agency's silence as an acceptance of petitioner's arguments.

We too agree with petitioner's argument. An individual entitled to back pay must receive a sum of money equal to what would have been earned absent the discrimination (gross back pay), minus what the individual actually earned from other employment during the period in question after the normal expenses incurred in seeking and holding the interim employment have been deducted (net interim income). The difference between gross back pay and net interim income is net back pay due. Rogelio Perez v. United States Postal Service, Petition No. 0420040041 (March 3, 2005). Thus, we find, that pursuant to 5 C.F.R. § 550.805(e)(1), the agency should recalculate her back-pay award by subtracting her net interim income, $59,375.00, from her gross back pay.

In Complainant v. Attorney General, 0720150003 (2015), the Commission rejected an agency argument that the complainant actually saved business expenses as a result of his termination:

The Agency argues that the AJ should have deducted Complainant's business expenses from back pay. The Agency made this argument to the AJ, who in not making a business deduction did not explicitly address this matter. While serving the Agency, Complainant set up an S Corporation for tax purposes. For 2005, Complainant's S Corporation reported business expenses of $16,166--taxes and licenses ($8,345), travel to Europe for language proficiency ($4,971), professional fees ($1,000), books, foreign newspapers, and magazines ($500), local transportation expenses ($450), watching foreign movies ($400), meals and entertainment ($250), and office supplies ($250). For 2006, Complainant's S Corporation reported business expenses of $12,910--taxes and license fees ($5,685), rent ($5,000), professional fees (2,000), office supplies ($100), publications ($75), foreign movies ($25), and local transportation ($25). Complainant dissolved his S Corporation, and it did not file tax returns from 2007 onward. The Agency argues that the S Corporation's yearly tax returns for 2005 and 2006 show average yearly business expenses of $14,538, and back pay should be deducted by a similar, proportionate amount.

In legal support of its argument, the Agency cites Sabala v. Western Gillette, Inc., 516 F.2d 1251, 1265 (5th Cir. 1975) (district court did not abuse its discretion in reducing the back pay of city truckers by 10% who were discriminatorily denied transfers to road trucker positions to account for road trucker business related expenses). A similar case is Equal Employment Opportunity Commission v. Lee Way Motor Freight, Inc., 625 F.2d 918, 935 (10th Cir. 1979) (district court did not abuse its discretion in not reducing the back pay of city truckers by 20% who were discriminatorily denied transfers to road trucker positions to account for road trucker meals and lodging expenses because this figure was arbitrary and too hypothetical). In Equal Employment Opportunity Commission v. Kentucky State Police Department, 80 F.3d 1086, 1100 (6th Cir. 1986), the Court ruled that back pay awards should not be reduced by the amount of income and social security taxes which would have been deducted from wages a claimant would have received but for discrimination.

*8 We need not decide as a principle whether business expenses are deductible from back pay. Regardless, the AJ's decision not to do so is supported by substantial evidence. A large portion of Complainant's business expenses were taxes, which he will have to pay in any event when he receives his back pay. The Agency has not shown that Complainant would not have incurred rent in 2006, another large business expense, in the absence of serving it for one month that year--for example, the rent could be for a portion of his living quarters he used in searching for employment. Nor has the Agency shown that Complainant saved another large business expense, foreign travel, as a result of his termination. Complainant continued to engage in foreign travel in 2008, 2009, 2010, and 2012. HT, Complainant, 118-120. The above are the bulk of the business expenses. On the whole, the Agency has not shown Complainant saved expenses as a result of the Agency terminating his services.
