9. Leave

The agency may not require an employee to stay out of work for a predetermined amount of time after childbirth. Nor may an agency require an employee to stay out of work due to pregnancy if the employee is able to perform the essential functions of her job.

Leave for child care after birth is not covered specifically by the Pregnancy Discrimination Act, but, at 29 CFR Part 1604, Appendix, the Commission does note:

[O]rdinary Title VII principles would require that leave for childcare purposes be granted on the same basis as leave which is granted to employees for other non-medical reasons. For example, if an employer allows its employees to take leave without pay or accrued annual leave for travel or education which is not job related, the same type of leave must be granted for those who wish to remain on leave for infant care, even though they are medically able to return to work.

A policy that denies the use of sick leave for "parenting" purposes as opposed to delivery does not violate Title VII because it treats males and females alike. See Battistello v. Department of Agriculture, 01860275 (1987). But an agency that requires a medical certificate for absences of more than three days may not waive that requirement for pregnant females and impose the requirement on males. See Buckley v. Department of Navy, 01871449 (1987).

However, in Cline v. Postmaster General, 01912017 (1991), the Commission found:

Title VII does not require that any particular fringe benefits, such as paternity or maternity leave, be provided. However, if such a benefit is provided, it does require equal treatment of all employees in its provision. See Hishon v. King Ambersan Spaulding, 467 U.S. 69, 75 (1984). In this case the appellant's branch issued a policy granting up to three months of maternity leave to pregnant female employees before and after the birth of their baby. It seems clear that later enunciations of the policy did and do require an affirmative showing of incapacity for any sick leave taken. Earlier versions of similar documents, however do not contain the same explicit language requiring the presentation of medical documentation for the use of sick leave. We note, as did appellant in this current appeal, that the agency had the ability to produce concrete evidence of past policy, either by securing an affidavit from an official who expressly addressed what the policy required in 1985, and/or by reviewing the files of women who were granted maternity leave in that year. In spite of our directive to produce such material in our grant of appellant's request to reopen, the agency has failed to do so: neither affidavit expressly addressed the practice in earlier years, each seems carefully limited to articulating current policy. We therefore find until April 4, 1990, agency policy did not require the provision of medical documentation from pregnant woman seeking to use sick or other leave for periods of incapacitation. In contrast, one extract specifically requires employees seeking other forms of disability leave to submit medical documentation in support of their request.

The Pregnancy Discrimination Act provides that women affected by pregnancy shall be treated the same as women not so affected for all employment-related purposes. See 42 USC 2000(e)(k). Policies involving leave shall be applied to pregnancy-related disabilities on the same terms and conditions as they are applied to other disabilities. 29 CFR 6004.10(b). Under the Act, pregnant women are to be treated the same as other employees on the basis of their ability or inability to work. The Act does not require employers to treat pregnant woman in any particular manner with respect to providing sick leave. Thus, the Act does not prohibit employment practices that favor pregnant women. California Federal Savings and Loan Ass'n v. Guerra, 479 U.S. 272, 286-88 (1987). It merely prohibits the imposition of disparate requirements in cases related to pregnancy or childbirth.

In this case, the agency's policy regarding maternity leave provided that an employee could take leave without having to document her disability, which is presumed. It imposed no special requirements covering childcare, but only created an option regarding use of leave. Childcare-related disabilities lasting more than three months required documentation in the same manner as all other applications for disability leave, including paternity.

The agency's paternity leave policy as implemented by the facility, provided up to two weeks of leave and required that the spouse's disability be documented. Thus, the policy at issue confers a benefit upon pregnant women which it did not confer upon men. In order to show that this policy is discriminatory, appellant must show that the difference in paternity and maternity policies was based on gender rather than condition. This appellant has failed to do. Accordingly, we find that the facility's policy as defined in 1986, did not discriminate against men.

The decision in Cline appeared to directly contradict the Commission's holding in Buckley. On reconsideration, Cline v. Postmaster General, 05920305 (1993), the Commission found that the policy was facially discriminatory and that the agency had not shown its application of the policy was nondiscriminatory. The Commission relied heavily on its own parental leave policy guidance, issued in August 1990, in reaching its reconsideration decision in Cline:

In reaching the present ruling, the Commission relies on its Policy Guidance on Parental Leave, issued on August 27, 1990...which provides ample guidance in considering whether employer policies regarding parental leave are discriminatory in nature. Therein, the Commission concluded that Title VII prohibits employers from establishing policies that treat male and female employees differently when they request time off to care for a newborn child. Policy Guidance on Parental Leave, p.1. The Commission stated that the issue of whether an employer in a particular case is treating male and female employees differently is a question of fact and that when an agency's policy is facially discriminatory, the shifting burdens of proof set forth in McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792 (1973) are inapplicable.

As an example of such a policy, the Commission cited one strikingly similar to the policy herein. In the Commission's example, the employer had a policy that permitted women to take 16 weeks of unpaid maternity leave for the purpose of recovering from pregnancy and for child care, without providing medical documentation, while only allowing male employees to take no more than two weeks of paternity leave for the care of a newborn child. A woman suffering from complications was allowed extended leave with medical documentation but no documentation was required if she returned during the first sixteen weeks. The Commission deemed such a policy to be facially discriminatory. The Policy Guidance stated that if the employer routinely granted 14 weeks (the amount of the male/female gap) of disability leave to pregnant employees without requiring them to document their disability while requiring other employees to do so, it would be difficult to justify such inequality of treatment. Policy Guidance on Parental Leave, pp. 3-4. "In short," the Policy Guidance stated, "the employer must justify any disparity in parental leave by proving that it is attributable to the women's disability."

The Commission's Policy Guidance clearly applies to the present situation, where the agency established unequal leave policies for maternity and paternity leave but has presented no evidence that they were related to actual disability caused by pregnancy. The agency's provision of leave to women for a period of adjustment and in order to make child care arrangements, while denying similar such leave opportunities to men, constitutes exactly the type of discriminatory inequality contemplated by the Commission's Policy.

The previous decision herein cited the decision of the United States Supreme Court in California Federal Savings and Loan Ass'n. v. Guerra for the proposition that the Pregnancy Discrimination Act does not prohibit employment practices that favor pregnant women. However, as appellant correctly argues and as the Court and the Commission's Policy Guidance on Parental Leave note, the law in question was "narrowly drawn to cover only the period of actual physical disability on account of pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions." 479 U.S. at 290; Policy Guidance on Parental Leave, p. 5. Thus, a policy such as the one before us, which has not been proven to be related to disability during pregnancy, would not be within the ambit of the favorable treatment permitted by the Court in California Federal. See also Shafter v. Board of Public Education of Pittsburgh, 903 F.2d 243 (3d Cir. 1990).

Furthermore, while the agency's maternity leave policy has apparently been amended to require provision of medical documentation of disability in order to utilize sick leave, the agency still (at least at the time of the supplemental investigation in 1991) treats women and men differently with regard to the use of annual leave and LWOP for non-disability related reasons following the birth of a child. We also find that our examination of the agency's maternity leave policy, including its later clarifications, discloses that the policy is violative of Title VII in that it establishes a set amount of time (a maximum of four months) that may be taken for maternity leave attributable to medical disability, while the record does not indicate that similar time limitations periods are set by the agency for other temporary disabilities. See Maddox v. Grandview Care Center, Inc., 780 F.2d. at 990-991. (Employer's policy of limiting only leave for maternity related medical conditions to a three month time period found to be facially discriminatory).

In view of the Commission's determination that the maternity and paternity leave policies utilized by the agency in 1985 and up to the present date are discriminatory, the agency is directed to modify these policies in order to afford equal treatment to male and female employees who seek leave for purposes other than medical disability relating to pregnancy. The agency is also directed to modify its current maternity leave policy, if it has not already done so, to require that pregnancy be treated in the same manner as other disabilities with regard to the production of supporting medical documentation and the length of time for which leave will be provided. The agency is advised that the Commission's Policy Guidance on Parental Leave provides an appropriate model for the development of a policy that will satisfy the requirements of Title VII.

The fact that delivery may require leave during a training period is not sufficient reason for failure to hire a pregnant woman if other temporarily disabled male employees are permitted to interrupt training for recovery. LaFountain v. USPS, 01861702 (1987).
