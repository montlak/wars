B. RIGHT TO HEARING

Following the completion of the agency's investigation of a complaint, the employee has the right to request a hearing. The agency is required to notify the complainant that the investigation is complete, provide a copy of the investigative report and further advise the complainant or his of her right to request a hearing. See 29 CFR 1614.108(f). In the absence of completion of the investigation or the required agency notice, the complainant may request a hearing after the expiration of 180 days from the filing of the complaint or, in the case of an amended complaint, within 360 of the filing of the oriGINAl complaint. See 29 CFR 1614.108(g). In any event, the complainant can request a hearing after the expiration of 180 days from the filing of the oriGINAl complaint even if it has been amended.

The request must be made in writing, directly to the EEOC District Office that has jurisdiction over the case. The agency is required to notify the complainant in the acknowledgment letter of the proper EEOC office with which to file a hearing request. Prior to the 1999 amendments to Part 1614, the hearing request was made to the employing agency and not to the EEOC. The agency was required to forward the hearing request to the proper EEOC district office for assignment of an administrative judge.

The process of requiring the complainant to submit a hearing request to the agency and have the agency, in turn, submit the request to the EEOC was simply a waste of time and paper. At best, the process required a needless step. But the problem went well beyond the merely wasteful. At its worst, the process allowed agencies to significantly delay hearing requests simply by not forwarding the request to the EEOC or, in some cases, even deny that the complainant had a right to a hearing.

Anyone who doubts that some agencies used this needless step in the process to delay and discourage complainants from receiving hearings need only review the case law under the former regulations. For example, the Commission was required to issue decisions holding that if the employee requests a hearing, it is not within the agency's discretion to deny that request even if the agency believes the relief requested is beyond the purview of the EEO process. See Busby v. USPS, 01831006 (1983). Similarly, the Commission found that the agency cannot deny the complainant's request for a hearing on the basis that the investigation report already contains sufficient information for the agency to render a final decision. See Schmitt v. Secretary of Transportation, 01882386 (1988). Of course, if an agency believes there are no genuine issues of material fact in dispute, it can move for summary judgment once the case has been assigned to an administrative judge. See 29 CFR 1614.109(g).

In the extreme, the Commission issued decisions like that in Reilly v. Secretary of Interior, 01987085 (1999):

As a final matter, the Commission notes that it has taken the agency almost five years to issue a final decision on appellant's complaint. Appellant filed her complaint in 1993 and a final decision was not issued by the agency until almost five years later. Although the agency apologizes in its final agency decision for its "oversight and delay" in issuing a final agency decision and in the processing of allegation (1), appellant's appeal reflects that she made several requests for a hearing before an EEOC Administrative Judge (AJ). Appellant specifically asserts, through her attorney, that since 1994, she has requested a hearing before the EEOC AJ and the agency has not responded to any of her requests. In this regard, appellant's attorney submitted letters on appeal which reveal that since 1994, she submitted requests to the agency for hearings before an EEOC Administrative Judge and that the agency failed to forward any of her requests to the AJ. The agency's extreme delay in processing appellant's complaint is inexcusable. It has taken the agency almost four years to issue a decision on procedural grounds and it does not appear that a formal investigation, which is to be completed within 180 days of the filing of a formal complaint, has ever been conducted into any of appellant's claims in the present complaint, now over five years from the filing of the formal complaint. Consequently, on remand, the agency shall expedite the further processing of appellant's complaint in accordance with the order below.

Although the agency cannot unilaterally deprive the complainant of his right to a hearing, an EEOC administrative judge can, in limited circumstances, cancel a hearing and remand the complaint to the agency for a final decision based on the investigatory record. One such circumstance is when the complainant fails, without good cause, to attend the hearing. This situation is addressed below and discussed in detail in Chapter 8 in the section on "Sanctions Against Complainants." Another circumstance is when the complainant cannot afford to attend the hearing and the administrative judge determines it would be impossible to hold a telephonic hearing. Such cases are rare.

However, in Neal v. Secretary of Defense, AAFES, 01961348 (1998), the complainant was terminated for a rather bizarre assault on a subordinate employee while serving in Japan. By the time of the hearing, the complainant had returned to the United States and could not afford to travel back to Japan for the hearing. The agency opposed changing the venue of the hearing to the United States because the alleged discrimination occurred in Japan; the agency's principal witness--the subordinate--was a Japanese national and could not be compelled to come to the United States; and the cost of bringing witnesses to the United States was prohibitive. The administrative judge rejected the alternative of allowing the complainant and his representative to participate in the hearing by telephone, finding that it was too difficult to conduct a hearing by telephone given the distance involved. The judge remanded the case to the agency with instructions to issue a final decision on the record.

On appeal, the Commission in Neal upheld the administrative judge:

On appeal, appellant's counsel primarily argues that this matter should be remanded back to the agency. Appellant contends that the AJ erred in refusing to permit appellant and his counsel to participate telephonically in the hearing, In any event, appellant contends that the AJ should have at least issued a recommended decision without a hearing, even though material facts were in dispute and genuine issues of credibility existed, so that appellant "could take consolation in the fact that the record was reviewed in its entirety and impartially."

In the alternative, counsel argues that the agency wrongfully held against appellant his refusal to cooperate with the agency's internal investigation, inasmuch as appellant had been advised that he had the right to remain silent and that the agency's "policies [cannot] usurp the United States Constitution," Counsel asserts that the agency should not have accepted the Subordinate's allegations, noting that the Subordinate "by her own admission" lied to the custodians and the Japanese police, while appellant had a successful and incident-free career prior to her allegations, The brief includes an affidavit from one of appellant's former coworkers (a Black male) who asserts that appellant's immediate supervisor (a Black female) was prejudiced against Blacks. He contended that the supervisor said that appellant was "acting niggerish" in the way he talked when with other Blacks, and that she once said that "if a white crew is going to get the job done then she'd rather have a white crew."

In its comments on the appeal, the agency contends that any problems appellant "may have had with his immediate supervisor are not relevant," inasmuch it was the General Manager who determined to terminate appellant.

As a preliminary matter, the Commission finds that the AJ did not abuse his discretion in declining to hold a telephonic hearing. As apparently conceded by appellant, the AJ properly determined that the hearing should be held in Japan, i.e. the location of "the agency's organizational component where the complaint arose." Chapter 6 of EEOC Management Directive [MD] 110 (October 1992), Further, the agency was not required to pay appellant's expenses to travel to Japan, at least not until (and unless) he prevailed. See, e.g., Sandeen v. Department of Transportation, EEOC Appeal No. 01911768 (August 23, 1991). The Commission has previously found that, when a complainant is unable to attend a hearing due to the inability to pay the costs of attending, the AJ may properly remand the matter to the agency for the issuance of a FAD without a hearing, See, Ray v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01922008 (September 15, 1992); Stroh v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01951645 (April 10, 1997). The Commission's Regulations permitting the issuance of a recommended decision without a hearing are not applicable to this situation, inasmuch as the material facts are disputed and issues of credibility are present. See 29 CFR § 1614.109(e).

Note that the administrative judge does not have the option to use summary judgment powers when material facts are clearly in dispute.
