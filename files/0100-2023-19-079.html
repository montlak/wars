F. PROCEDURAL IRREGULARITIES

Because the agency is required only to articulate a legitimate reason for its actions, a mistaken belief as to proper procedures is generally not sufficient to show pretext. A departure from internal procedures, standing alone, is insufficient to establish a claim of discrimination. See Odle v. Postmaster General, 01A40979 (2005); see also Waterbury v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01A12182 (2002).

In Hill v. Secretary of Air Force, 05950123 (1995), the Commission found that the agency's failure to follow its own regulations was not indicative of pretext. The proper focus, according to the Commission, is not solely on whether the agency has followed its regulations, but its proffered reasons for departing from the regulations. As the Commission in Hill observed:

Because the agency facility complied with its usual practice, if not the letter of the regulations, its reasons were found to be legitimate. It has long been held that some procedural irregularity in itself does not establish prohibited discrimination. Goosetree v. State of Tennessee, 796 F.2d 854, 861 (6th Cir. 1986). Thus the agency's failure to adhere to the requirements of the regulations do not, contrary to appellant's contention, show pretext. Rather, a showing of pretext in a discrimination case requires some showing that the agency's articulated reasons cannot be believed, or some showing that impermissible discrimination was the real reason for the agency's action. Texas Dept. of Community Affairs v. Burdine, 450 U.S. 248 (1981). In this case, appellant must show that the agency deviated from its standard practice and allowed nondisabled employees on light duty to be rated by someone other than his supervisor of record. The record before us does not support such a conclusion. . . .

The fact that there are procedural irregularities in the process can be overcome by a clear and specific explanation of the agency's action supported by evidence. As the Commission noted in Complainant v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120122789 (2014):

In this case we are concerned that there is evidence that strongly suggests that this selection process deviated from the Agency's stated procedures. The Agency protocol required that the selection official document on the certificate the basis for his or her selection. ROI, Exhibit C22, Medical Center Memorandum PM-11. The selecting official has the right to select from a properly constructed merit promotion certificate or from any other appropriate source. This may not have been done. The protocol lists the recognized appointing authorities. The one used does not appear on the list of authorities. However, this alone does not prove age discrimination. The record is undisputed that the panel uniformly rated the selectee as the highest scoring candidate based on the interview. The selecting official stated that she abided by the decision of the rating panel. There is insufficient evidence to show that this is untrue. Accordingly, we find that the record supports the ultimate decision.

But, deviations from standard procedures with no explanation or justification can be evidence of pretext as the Commission noted in Shayna P. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120141506 (2017). A series of mistaken beliefs may also be probative of the agency's true reasons. In Williams v. USPS, 01850031 (1986), the agency's failure to discover several errors in the selection process and its reliance on misstated information in the selectee's job application was a sufficient demonstration of pretext to warrant a finding of discrimination where internal inconsistencies in the application should have alerted the agency to the misstatements:

The inconsistencies of the selectee's stated qualifications, the agency's failure to uncover such inconsistencies prior to final selection, and the reliance in its selection on the misstated information are all admitted by the agency. For its part, the agency has merely asserted that its "series of mistaken beliefs" do not evidence a pretext for intentional discrimination. We disagree and, in this regard, view as controlling the admonition of Burdine, supra to the effect that a mistake or misjudgment as to an applicant's qualifications may be probative of whether the stated reasons for action are pretext for discrimination. Burdine cites two opinions which are at least instructive of the present matter: Lieberman v. Grant, 630 F.2d 60 (2nd Cir. 1980) states that one method of demonstrating pretext is "to show the asserted neutral basis [i.e., the "legitimate, nondiscriminatory" reason for action] was so ridden with error that defendant [employer] could not honestly have relied upon it." Although an employer is given much leeway in the exercise of poor judgment as far as an allegation of discrimination is concerned, its legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason must, ultimately, be reasonably articulated. Loeb v. Textron, Inc., 6000 F.2d 1003 (1st Cir. 1979).

The Commission noted that due diligence would have uncovered the discrepancies in the selectee's qualifications without interfering with the agency's legitimate management prerogative. Presumably, the inference was that the Commission may have upheld the agency's decision, had it discovered the misstatements and still deemed the selectee to be the best qualified applicant. But see Cythia Q. v. Secretary of Defense, 0120141321 (2016) ("A mistake made by the agency is not evidence of pretext unless there is evidence that the mistake was based on a complainant's protected classes.").

Similarly, in Moresi v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0720090049 (2010), the Commission held that the decision of its administrative judge finding discrimination was supported by substantial evidence where there were multiple failures to follow procedures:

Upon review, the Commission finds that substantial evidence in the record supports the AJ's determination that the agency's articulated reason for not selecting complainant is unworthy of belief. Here, the AJ found that the agency's selecting official did not conduct any interviews for the position, nor did he convene a panel to make any recommendations. Rather, the record indicates that the selecting official simply completed rating sheets for each of the three candidates wherein he rated them in the areas of leadership, supervision, accounts payables operational knowledge and experience, knowledge of payments and TSA payable process, knowledge of Lean Six Sigma, accomplishments, awards and education. The record further discloses that the selecting official in this matter made his selection without consulting the Command Staff Advisor in connection with the specific crediting plan he intended to use for the position, in a direct departure from the agency's established merit promotion plan. Moreover, the AJ found the criteria used by the selecting official to be vague, including such things as "leadership" and "supervision." Additionally, the AJ found that the scoring the selecting official employed to rate these vague categories was very difficult for him to explain at the hearing in this matter. The record also indicates that while the selecting official claims that he was looking for a candidate with knowledge of Lean Six Sigma, he ultimately selected the individual who struggled with that process rather than complainant who had become a recognized leader in that process. Finally, the AJ noted that a proposed organization chart for the Accounting Operations Division dated January 31, 2006 shows the selectee in the position of Branch Chief, GS-14, long before the selection ever took place. At the hearing in this matter, the agency failed to provide witness testimony to adequately explain the existence of the chart. Finally, the record indicates that the selection for the position was made on November 26, 2006; long after the expiration of the certificate on August 29, 2006.

Ultimately, the AJ found that the agency's articulated reasons for its non-selection were a pretext to mask unlawful discrimination in that the selecting official wrote the position description for the job, created the criteria for the selection and then made the selection without input from anyone else including a recommending official or panel, and without holding interviews. The AJ also found that the selection was made without consulting the agency's Command Staff Advisor contrary to procedure established by the agency's merit promotion plan. Moreover, the existence of the organizational chart including the selectee in the position at issue herein, well before the selection was made, is enough to support the Al's finding of pretext. For the reasons stated above, the Commission finds that the complainant was a victim of race and sex discrimination as to the non-selection addressed in claim (1).

The Commission has also held that a deviation from standard procedures can, in some cases, be sufficient to justify a finding of pretext. See Monroe v. Secretary of Navy, 01940864 (1994), citing Craig v. Y&Y Snacks, Inc., 721 F.2d 77 (3rd Cir. 1983). This is particularly true where the agency offers no explanation as to its deviation from standard procedures. The Commission reached a similar result in Middleton v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 01940448 (1994), where all previous selections were based solely on seniority and resulted in the promotion of white candidates, but complainant, who was black, was subjected to a competitive selection process when he was the most senior candidate. See also Hagerty v. Secretary of Navy, 0720060084 (2006). In Tyrone D. v. Secretary of Defense, 0720160005 (2016), the Commission affirmed an administrative judge's finding of race discrimination where the complainant's supervisor failed to follow agency protocol for converting the complainant's job back to the GS pay scale by not including the complainant's actual duties and responsibilities in the new position description. The net effect of the agency's actions was that the complainant was paid at a lower rate than the job duties warranted. The supervisor's testimony that the position description should reflect what duties were required for the position rather than the duties actually being performed was discredited by a Human Resources Specialist who testified that the position description "was intended to be a pay document that determined how much an employee should be paid based on what work the employee performed and their level of responsibility." Noting the agency's failure to provide a credible legitimate reason for its deviation from the conversion process in the complainant's case, the Commission found substantial evidence supported the AJ's finding of race discrimination.

However, simply showing that the agency failed to follow its own merit promotion plan "absent an indicia of discrimination constituting a preponderance of the evidence, does not constitute a violation of Title VII." In re McCoy, 01856527 (1987).
