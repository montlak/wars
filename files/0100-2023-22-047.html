N. EQUAL PAY ACT

The Equal Pay Act, codified at 29 USC 206(d), is an amendment to the Fair Labor Standards Act, that prohibits discrimination based on sex in the payment of wages to employees. OriGINAlly, the Department of Labor had enforcement authority under the Equal Pay Act, but Reorganization Plan No. 1, issued by President Carter in 1978, transferred enforcement of the EPA as it applies to federal employees to the Commission as of January 1, 1979.

Previously, the Commission's procedures under the Act were addressed in 29 CFR Part 1620 and the Commission's Complaint Processing Manual at Chapter 14, and were substantially different from those involving complaints of discrimination lodged under the other statutes over which the EEOC has enforcement authority. However, beginning October 1, 1992, with the implementation of Part 1614, complaints under the EPA are processed in the same fashion as all other complaints of discrimination. Pursuant to 29 CFR 1614.202:

(a) In its enforcement of the Equal Pay Act, the Commission has the authority to investigate an agency's employment practices on its own initiative at any time in order to determine compliance with the provisions of the Act. The Commission will provide notice to the agency that it will be initiating an investigation.

(b) Complaints alleging violations of the Equal Pay Act shall be processed under this part.

Formerly, a complainant who alleged that an agency violated the EPA was required to file a complaint with the appropriate district office of the EEOC and not the alleged offending agency. Under the Act, the complaint must be filed within two years of the date the cause of action accrued. The filing deadline was extended to three years in cases where the violation was willful.

After the complaint was filed, the district office was then responsible for conducting an investigation of the complaint. As part of its investigation, the office would request the agency to submit a written presentation defending its action. The employee then had an opportunity to rebut that defense. The rebuttal also was required to be in written form.

Based on its initial investigation and the written presentations of the parties, the office would determine whether an on-site investigation was necessary. The EEOC had the authority to inspect all necessary records, including payroll records, and take testimony in the form of sworn affidavits.

If the office determined that a violation had occurred, the district director would issue an appropriate enforcement order and all affected employees would be notified.

This procedure has now been abolished in favor of processing complaints under the EPA as any other complaint of discrimination under the provisions of Part 1614. Note that although the EPA provides for a two-to-three-year statute of limitations, Part 1614 requires that an EEO counselor be contacted within 45 days of the alleged discriminatory incident or event. Complainants under the EPA are not required to exhaust their administrative remedies, but may file a civil action in U.S. district court without seeking redress through the administrative process. However, if the complainant does file an administrative complaint of discrimination with the employing agency, this does not toll the statute of limitations. Any civil action must still be filed within two years of the violation, or three years if the violation is willful.
