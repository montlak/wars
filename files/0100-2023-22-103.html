4. COVID-19

Executive Order 14043 mandates that all federal employees vaccine against COVID-19. The Fifth Circuit in Feds for Medical Freedom et al., v. Biden, No. 22-40043 at 3 (5th Cir. Feb. 9, 2022, C.J. Higginson dissenting), however, enjoined that mandate and agencies are currently unable to enforce the order. Plenty of discrimination cases have arisen relating to the order. Many relate to religious objections to receiving the vaccine. But, because agencies are precluded from enforcing the order, most are dismissed for failure to state a claim. Roderick P. v. Secretary of Transportation, 2022003526 (2022), is a good example:

Here, Complainant first claims that his request for a religious exemption from the COVID-19 vaccination mandate has not been acted upon. However, it is undisputed that there is a federal court injunction currently in place prohibiting the enforcement of Executive Order 14043, the mandate requiring COVID-19 vaccination of federal employees. As a result, Complainant does not allege that he was actually required get vaccinated which he claims would violate his religious beliefs. See Casie S. v. Dep't of Veteran Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 2022002450 (July 25, 2022). (complainant was not aggrieved on the basis of religion when she did not allege that she was actually required to get vaccinated in violation of her religious beliefs). As such, the Agency correctly dismissed this claim because Complainant has not alleged any present harm or loss with respect to a term, condition, or privilege of his employment.

The Commission further noted in Roderick P. that vaccination status is not a protected class:

Complainant then argues that, as an unvaccinated person, he is being discriminated against by being required to submit to COVID-19 testing under certain circumstances and having his official travel restricted while those who are vaccinated are treated more favorably. However, a fair reading of his complaint reflects that Complainant believes that as an unvaccinated employee he is being treated differently than vaccinated employees. As such, we conclude Complainant is alleging discrimination based on vaccination status rather than a disability or religion, not a basis protected by the statutes enforced by the EEOC. See 29 C.F.R. § 1614.103(a). See also, What You Should Know About COVID-19 and the ADA, the Rehabilitation Act and Other EEO Laws, EEOC Technical Assistance Questions and Answers Updated on July 12, 2022, Question A.6 (if job-related and consistent with business necessity, employers can require mandatory Covid-19 viral testing to evaluate an employee's continued presence in the workplace).
