8. Promotions

Promises to promote an employee should specifically describe the position to which the employee will be promoted. Incorporating a position description into the settlement agreement by reference and attaching it to the agreement is not a bad idea either. In Rafaela B. v. Secretary of Defense, 0120162504 (2017), the settlement agreement simply provided that the agency would "promote Complainant to a GS-13." The complainant expected that she would remain in her GS-12 position, but receive a GS-13 salary. The agency maintained that the agreement was void as a result of the parties mutual misunderstanding regarding how the promotion could be effected, explaining that, after the signing of the agreement, human resources determined that it could not promote the complainant to a GS-13 position while remaining in a GS-12 position description. The Commission, in Rafaela B., rejected both parties' arguments, finding that there was no mutual mistake, and that implied in an agreement to promote is the understanding that it will come with a position description commensurate with the grade to which the employee is promoted:

In the instant case, the Agency contends that by agreeing to "promote Complainant to a GS-13" the parties made a mutual mistake in the formation of the agreement. The Commission disagrees. Following the execution of the agreement, a dispute regarding implementation of provision (c) occurred. Complainant asserts that provision (c) requires the Agency to "promote [Complainant] in her position to a GS-13 salary." Further, Complainant's attorney argues that if the Agency "wanted that position to comply with 'specific regulations, such as those promulgated by OPM' it should have included such language in the settlement agreement.

However, we find that the plain meaning of "promote Complainant to a GS-13" requires her placement in a GS-13, which in turn, results in changes to her salary and position description. In keeping with Agency policies and OPM standards, a promotion to GS-13 included the associated (GS-13) position description. If Complainant intended another meaning for the phrase "promote to GS-13" to ensure she maintained the position description associated with another GS level, then she should have included such language in the agreement. The record reflects that, following Complainant's unwillingness to accept the GS-13 position description, the Agency made several unsuccessful attempts to modify the provision.

Here, it is clear that the Agency has not complied with some of the terms of the settlement agreement as alleged by Complainant. However, due to Complainant's unwillingness to accept the position description associated with a "promotion to GS-13," the Commission finds that the Agency's offer of reinstatement of the underlying EEO complaint is an appropriate resolution.

Where an agency agrees to promote a complainant in exchange for his or her agreement to abandon a claim of discrimination, the Commission will not look favorably upon agency arguments that the promotion turned out to be impossible to implement. As the Commission noted in Virgilio C. v. Secretary of Agriculture, 0120162156 (2018), "when an Agency agrees to do something in a settlement agreement, the Commission presumes that it can do so, except in extraordinary circumstances." In Virgilio C., the agency agreed to promote the complainant to a GS-13 position, but later refused to honor that portion of the agreement because it was "contrary to law." According to the agency, the promotion was impossible because the promised position was a career ladder position that ended at the GS-12 level, precluding the agency from placing the complainant in the position at the GS-13 level. The agency further notified the complainant that, while the promotion provision was rescinded, the remainder of the agreement survived on account of the severability provision of the agreement. The Commission flatly rejected the agency's position in Virgilio C., writing:

Regarding the issue of whether the Agency complied with terms of the settlement agreement, we note that the Agency contends that it cannot provide Complainant with the promotion promised in the agreement because the Hispanic Service Institutions Education Advisor position identified in the agreement is a career ladder position that ends at the GS-12 level. The Agency cites 5 C.F.R. § 335.103 and its Department Personnel Manual as requiring the use of the competitive process for promotions beyond the range of grades in the career ladder. However, these authorities broadly address promotions in general, not the specific promotion at issue in this case. We find that there is simply no evidence in the record that proves that regulations prevent the Agency from promoting Complainant to the promised Hispanic Service Institutions Education Advisor position at the GS-13 level.

Moreover, we note that when an Agency agrees to do something in a settlement agreement, the Commission presumes that it can do so, except in extraordinary circumstances. In this case, at the time of the execution of the agreement, the Agency should have known whether it could promote Complainant to the GS-13 level at the time it entered into the agreement. Further, Complainant reasonably relied upon the Agency's assertion that it would promote him when entering into the agreement. In these circumstances, we conclude that it would be contrary to fundamental fairness to invalidate the promotion provision of the agreement. As such, we find that the Agency must promote Complainant to the GS-13 level, effective April 21, 2013.

Additionally, Complainant broadly maintains that none of the terms of the agreement have been implemented. Despite receiving notice of Complainant's breach claim, the Agency has not addressed its compliance with the agreement beyond the promotion provision. Moreover, the record does not contain any evidence that the Agency has provided Complainant with the promised 80-hour time off award or $10,000 payment. In the absence of any evidence from the Agency regarding its compliance, and Complainant's breach allegation, we are persuaded that the Agency has not provided Complainant with the promised time-off award and lump sum payment.

Therefore, we find that the Agency has breached the terms of the settlement agreement. Complainant requests specific performance of the terms of the agreement, which we find is the appropriate remedy for the agency's breach.
