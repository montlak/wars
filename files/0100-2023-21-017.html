II. HOSTILE ENVIRONMENT/HARASSMENT, GENERALLY

"Hostile environment" is a theory of discrimination and not a basis for discrimination. In Meyer v. Postmaster General, 01955204 (1996), the Commission rejected a claim of discrimination based on "mental disability--hostile work environment." As the Commission explained in Meyer:

[A]lthough it is possible that being subjected to a hostile work environment could contribute to the development of a mental disability, appellant has failed to specify suffering from any mental disability that is medically or legally recognized. Furthermore, with regard to appellant's contention that "hostile work environment is a sub-category of sexual harassment," the Commission notes that there is no evidence of record that appellant alleged that she was subjected to discrimination because of her sex. Because appellant's second stated basis for the alleged discrimination is also not within the purview of Title VII or EEOC Regulations, the agency's decision to dismiss appellant's complaint was proper.

As noted in the introduction to this chapter, there are two distinct questions in hostile environment cases: 1) whether a hostile environment exists; and 2) if so, whether the employer should be held liable for the hostile environment?

As explained more fully in the following sections, in order to have the first question answered in the affirmative, the complainant must establish that he or she was subjected to physical or verbal conduct, the conduct was unwelcome, the conduct was based on a protected category, and the conduct was sufficiently severe or pervasive to create a hostile, offensive or abusive work environment.

In answering the second question, the rules of employer liability for hostile environment harassment are not as simple as those for tangible employment action harassment. There is no strict liability for hostile environment harassment. The basic rules of liability, discussed in detail in the following section, are that an agency is liable for hostile environment harassment by a supervisor unless the agency can prevail on a two-pronged affirmative defense by showing that it exercised reasonable care in preventing and promptly correcting any harassing behavior and that the employee unreasonably failed to take advantage of any corrective or preventive opportunities or otherwise failed to avoid harm. See Burlington Industries, 118 S. Ct. at 2270. When the hostile environment is created by a coworker, the employee must, as part of his or her case-in-chief, show that the agency knew or should have known of the harassing conduct and failed to take prompt and effective corrective action. See 29 CFR 1604.11(d).

The Supreme Court, as well as many other courts, have relied heavily upon the guidance of the EEOC in the area of sexual harassment. The Supreme Court decided two seminal cases involving hostile environment sexual harassment: Meritor Savings Bank, FSB v. Vinson, 477 U.S. 57, 106 S. Ct. 2399, 91 L. Ed.2d 49 (1986), and Harris v. Forklift Systems, Inc., 510 U.S. 17, 114 S. Ct. 367, 126 L. Ed.2d 295 (1993). Before reviewing Commission cases on hostile environment harassment, a review of Meritor and Harris is in order since they set down the basic guidance for hostile environment claims.
