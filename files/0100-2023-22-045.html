L. MEDICAL EXAMS

Most often when dealing with medical examinations cases are brought under the ADA. See Chapter 14. However, in an interesting case, the Commission upheld the finding of the administrative judge that agency insistence on a medical examination prior to returning to duty constituted discrimination on the basis of sex. In Brackett v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0720110026 (2012), the complainant, a police officer, suffered a stroke and was working in a light-duty position. Her physician cleared her to return to full duty, but the agency informed her she would have to take a psychological examination and a physical standard test, including a treadmill test. After a protest by the union, the agency agreed to an examination by neurologist instead of the treadmill test. The agency's physician cleared her for a return to full duty, but then immediately revised her opinion after receiving a new set of job standards from the agency.

The Commission in Brackett rejected the agency's argument that the judge's decision was against the weight of the evidence finding ample evidence of disparate treatment:

On appeal, the Agency argues that the AJ's finding is contrary to overwhelming evidence in the record. First, the Agency argues that while Complainant tried to show that male officers were returned to full duty based solely on releases by their private physicians, this action only occurred when the Office of Occupational Health concurred with the employee's personal doctor. This review was conducted in all cases. The Agency asserts that if the Agency doctor had any significant questions, as in Complainant's case, then the individual would not be released to full duty until those concerns were addressed. The Agency therefore argues that Complainant was not subjected to any disparate treatment.

Second, the Agency asserts that the comparators cited are not similarly situated because they are not taking Coumadin. According to the Agency, Coumadin is what prevented Complainant's return to full duty. The Agency contends that this action is supported by the fact that as soon as Complainant stopped taking Coumadin, she was returned to full duty. Further, the Agency draws a distinction between Coumadin and Plavix, a medication taken by comparator officers following heart attacks. The Agency argues that Plavix is not a blood thinner, but simply an anticoagulant with lower risks of injury. The Agency believes that the AJ's decision erroneously "rests on the unwarranted conclusion that management learned of Complainant's Coumadin therapy through discriminatory means" and improperly relies on comparisons with male employees who are not similarly situated. The Commission disagrees.

We conclude that the AJ's finding of sex discrimination is supported by substantial evidence. As noted above, the AJ concluded that male officers were permitted to resume full duties when released by their private doctors. In contrast, Complainant, who presented releases from two private physicians, was required to submit to additional examinations and tests. These findings are supported by the instant record.

Further, we are not persuaded by the Agency's argument that the Complainant's use of Coumadin is what distinguished Complainant's situation, rather than her sex. The Commission notes that even before Agency officials learned of her medication, Complainant was asked to take a treadmill stress test. A review of the hearing transcript shows that the Agency neurologist acknowledged that it was uncommon for her to review patients (police officers) prior to being returned to full duty. Moreover, the Agency neurologist could not recall seeing any male officers for such review, but she had seen the other female officer.

Accordingly, we find that the AJ's decision, finding that, by a preponderance of the evidence, the Agency's reasons were a pretext for discrimination, is supported by substantial evidence.
