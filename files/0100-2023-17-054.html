E. SHORT-FORM DECISIONS

Instead of writing lengthy decisions on each appeal, OFO often issues decisions that simply indicate its agreement with the decision below. This deference to the administrative judges' decisions is in line with the 1999 regulatory changes that gave EEOC administrative judges the authority to make binding decisions subject to more traditional standards of appellate review. The Commission's short-form decisions started appearing in 1997, perhaps as a harbinger of the 1999 regulatory changes.

For example, in Sloane v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01954358 (1997), the Commission upheld a recommended decision of its administrative judge over the final agency decision, stating:

After careful review of the record in its entirety, including the statements of the parties submitted on appeal, the Commission finds that the AJ's RD sets forth the relevant facts and properly analyzes the appropriate regulations, policies, and laws. The Commission discerns no basis to disturb the AJ's findings and conclusions.

See also Reneau v. Postmaster General, 01960121 (1998) ("We have reviewed the record and discern no basis to disturb the AJ's findings."); Harris v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, SSA, 01954986 (1997) ("[T]he Commission particularly notes that the agency presented no persuasive evidence or argument in its FAD or on appeal which adequately rebuts the AJ's decision."); Kujawa v. Postmaster General, 01953550 (1997); Buizon v. Postmaster General, 01956072 (1997); Hernandez-New v. Postmaster General, 01960287 (1997); Miller v. Postmaster General, 01953542 (1997).

The Commission also may render decisions that merely state a result, perhaps with a regulatory citation. For example, in Welsh v. Postmaster General, 01970678 (1997), the Commission simply wrote:

Based on a review of the record, we find that the agency properly defined the issue in appellant's complaint. Accordingly, the agency's final decision is AFFIRMED.

The Commission was somewhat more expansive in Van Houten v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01970551 (1997):

Based on a review of the record, we find that the agency properly dismissed appellant's allegation of a discriminatory reassignment on May 30, 1995, pursuant to EEOC Regulation 29 CFR 1614.107(d). Accordingly, the agency's final decision dismissing the allegation of a discriminatory reassignment is AFFIRMED.

See also Baumann v. Postmaster General, 01971153 (1998); Powell v. Attorney General, 01972815 (1998); Harter v. Attorney General, 01970618 (1997); Moore v. Postmaster General, 01956242 (1997); Sullivan v. Postmaster General, 01954975 (1997).

Final agency decisions may be summarily reversed, as occurred in Antonik v. Secretary of Defense, 01970169 (1997):

The Commission finds that the agency improperly dismissed appellant's complaint as moot, pursuant to 29 CFR 1614.107(e). The record indicates that the interim relief after the complaint did not render his complaint moot. Accordingly, the agency's final decision dismissing the complaint is hereby REVERSED and the complaint is REMANDED to the agency for further processing in accordance with this decision and applicable regulations.

See also Barrera v. Secretary of Air Force, 01960415 (1997) (reversing agency's decision and reinstating administrative judge's findings).

The Commission also may issue summary decisions in cases involving allegations of breach of settlement agreements. See, e.g., Buggs v. Postmaster General, 01963771 (1997); Manetakis v. Secretary of Army, 01962849 (1997).

The Merit Systems Protection Board has engaged in a similar practice for years, often rejecting petitions for review of initial decisions merely by noting the appellant or the agency has failed to meet the criteria for review.

In addition to its short-form decisions, the Commission, in recent years, has issued summary decisions that give only a cursory indication of the basic facts of the case. For example, in Childs v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01961661 (1998), the Commission upheld the recommended decision of the administrative judge and summarized its basic points:

After a careful review of the record in its entirety, the Commission finds that the AJ's RD summarized the relevant facts and referenced the appropriate regulations, policies, and laws. We therefore discern no basis to disturb the AJ's finding of discrimination.

In this regard, we note that the AJ found the testimony of the two actual selecting officials (SOs) not credible because their testimony was so internally inconsistent and implausible that it could not be credited. For example, So-1, who was the Director of the Loan Guarantee Service in Washington, D.C., stated that it was better for a candidate to start as Assistant LGO before being promoted to LGO, but the ST was promoted to LGO from section chief in the Huntington, West Virginia, RO without first being an Assistant LGO. In addition, while So-1 testified that he reviewed all the candidates' job applications, when he was asked specific questions regarding the work experience of appellant and the ST, his answers revealed that he knew little about their recent work experience. He knew only that ST had been an LGO longer than any of the other candidates.

Similarly So-2, the District Director of the Denver RO, in his testimony attempted to make it appear that he had first-hand knowledge of the ST's work experience when in fact he had not worked with him since the late 1970s. In addition, he told the EEO Counselor that he had not been involved in the selection decision although he had been and had reached a consensus with So-1 to pick the ST. Then, too, neither SO could produce any documentation or written notes showing how he arrived at his decision.

In contrast, uncontroverted evidence showed that appellant was an excellent employee with a proven record as a very good manager. Because of her expertise, she was used to train employees and trouble shoot problems in eight different ROs, gaining a breadth of experience in doing so, on the other hand, there was no evidence that the ST had distinguished himself in any phase of employment. In this regard, while the ST's latest performance appraisal was only "Highly Successful," appellant's latest performance appraisal was "Outstanding."

Thus, we concur with the finding of the AJ that the agency reasons for choosing the ST were a pretext to mask discrimination, as demonstrated by the fact that appellant's qualifications for the position were plainly superior to those of appellant, yet she was not selected. McCleary v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 01933036 (March 7, 1994), citing Bauer v. Bailar, 647 F.2d 1037, 1048 (10th Cir. 1981).

See also Pryor v. Postmaster General, 01961884 (1998) (summarizing administrative judge's findings on a disability discrimination); Amantea-Cabrera v. Postmaster General, 01963801 (1998) (summarizing findings in sex discrimination case); Schultz v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01960075 (1998) (summarizing findings in age discrimination case).
