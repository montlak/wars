X. WORK ENVIRONMENT

An employee's protection from discrimination extends to the work environment. "Agency actions that create 'a working environment heavily charged with...discrimination' are thus impermissible, and an employee who is subjected to such actions is an aggrieved employee" within the meaning of the Act. Cleckner v. USPS, 01850048 (1985) (employee who alleges that the agency employment officer made comments about disabled veterans which offended her has stated a claim within EEO purview).

For a detailed discussion of harassment as creating a hostile work environment, the reader is referred to Chapter 12 under the heading "Hostile Environment Sexual Harassment."

As with all discrimination claims, the employee must allege treatment that is somehow different from similarly situated employees outside the protected group. An employee who alleges that she was not allowed to work in a less dusty environment while suffering from a temporary nasal condition has not stated a prima facie case of discrimination when the record shows that non-black male employees with similar health problems had not been granted the opportunity that appellant sought. See Slade v. USPS, 01842520 (1986). But an employee who alleges that her supervisor interrupted her break as reprisal for filing an EEO complaint has stated a complaint within the purview of Title VII based on the terms and conditions of employment. See Tips v. USPS, 01861794 (1986); see also Tips v. USPS, 01861798 (1986) (same employee stated a claim by alleging that she was allowed less time than other employees to review a vacation roster and select a vacation period); Rhea H. v. Postmaster General, 0120151981 (2016) (the complainant showed an injury to a term, condition or privilege of employment where she alleged that the agency restricted the times at which she could take a break while allowing others to breaks whenever they wanted).

A term and condition of employment can include such things as a locker room for changing. See Danis v. USPS, 01851808 (1987). An allegation that all male lockers were moved to a public area, but not female lockers, states a claim within the purview of Title VII. It also can include such things as not being allowed to take rest and lunch breaks at locations of the employee's choice when permitted by agency policy. See Hoch v. USPS, 01872889 (1987).

But not everything that relates to the physical location of a worksite is a term, condition, or privilege of employment. For example, an order to work in a particular area of the mailroom does not sufficiently aggrieve an employee for purposes of stating a claim. Eatmon v. Postmaster General, 01970097 (1998).

The Commission held that termination of employment moots a complaint regarding working conditions because no relief can be granted when the employment relationship no longer exists. Thompson v. Veterans Administration, 01872228 (1988). However, the Commission also found that the retention of memoranda critical of the complainant's performance constituted a continuing injury because the memoranda could affect his pursuit of future employment. The Commission now has a clear trend in hostile work environment cases of finding that the termination or resignation of the complainant does not moot the complaint. This is in part due to the availability of compensatory damages under the 1991 Civil Rights Act, but it also is due to a shift in Commission policy that requires an agency to make efforts to eradicate a hostile environment with respect to all employees. See discussion on "Mootness" in Chapter 3, Chapter 12 on Harassment, and discussion under "Compensatory Damages" in Chapter 21.

The discriminatory distribution of awards falls within the purview of Title VII where a black employee alleges that three white employees were given awards and he did not receive one despite an excellent attendance record and good performance evaluations. Dillingham v. USPS, 01873332 (1988).

Employees are equally entitled to a safe work environment as a term or condition of employment. When a complainant alleges that she was seriously injured on the basis that management allowed an untrained worker to operate a forklift in her work area and that management's motivation for doing so was discriminatory, she has stated a claim within the EEO purview. Judy v. Postmaster General, 019003020 (1990).
