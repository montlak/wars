6. Filing With Wrong Agency

Despite the fact that agencies use boilerplate language that clearly explains that a notice of appeal must be filed with the EEOC Office of Federal Operations and give the address of that office, some complainants still persist in filing the notices with their employing agency. The Commission remains lenient in such cases and, if the notice was timely filed with the employing agency, it still may be accepted as timely at the Office of Federal Operations.

The Commission relies on Henry v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 05901116 (1990), for the general proposition that "an appeal is untimely filed where the appeal is mailed to the wrong office, even if it would have been timely filed if mailed directly to the Commission." See Puana v. Secretary of Navy, 01996640 (2001), quoting Henry. However, the Commission routinely permits exceptions to the Henry rule where the complainant exhibited an earnest attempt to preserve his or her appeal rights. Of course, it would seem that the mere act of filing an appeal, even if mailed to the wrong office, is an earnest attempt to preserve appeal rights. But in McLaughlin v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, 01994163 (2001), the Commission held that the timely filing of the notice appeal with the Department of Labor instead of the EEOC was not good cause for waiving the time limit. The Commission gave no explanation in McLaughlin as to why filing an appeal with the Department of Labor was not an earnest attempt to preserve appeal rights.

An agency may not hold the notice of appeal, forward it to the Office of Federal Operations after expiration of the appeals period, and then argue that the appeal was not timely filed. In Zwettler v. Secretary of Interior, 05920315 (1992), the Commission, in just such circumstances, accepted the appeal as timely:

Appellant received the August 9, 1991 final agency decision on August 17, 1991. Notwithstanding that the final agency decision included proper appeal rights, appellant's August 18, 1991 notice of appeal was erroneously sent to the agency. The record reveals that the agency's Office for Equal Opportunity received the appeal on August 27, 1991, but waited until September 9 or 10, 1991 before forwarding the appeal to the EEOC and notifying appellant of his mistake. By this time, it was already more than twenty days beyond appellant's August 17, 1991 receipt of the final agency decision.

In Lord v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 05890175 (June 9, 1989), the Commission was confronted with nearly identical circumstances. In that case, the complainant mailed his appeal to the agency's EEO Office within the prescribed filing period even though the final agency decision included explicit, correct instructions on the proper method for filing an appeal to the Commission. Noting that appellant had not engaged in a long and unexplained delay, and that the agency should have promptly forwarded the appeal to the EEOC, the Commission exercised its equitable discretion and extended the prescribed time limit, thereby finding appellant's appeal timely.

Given the foregoing precedent, the Commission finds that the previous decision improperly dismissed appellant's appeal. Appellant's request to reopen is granted. The Commission's previous decision is reversed and appellant's appeal is deemed timely. The agency received the appeal within ten days of appellant's receipt of the final agency decision. However, the agency then waited approximately two weeks before forwarding the appeal to the EEOC and notifying appellant of his mistake. Appellant's mistake resulted in no prejudice to the agency. Indeed, the agency was notified of appellant's appeal earlier than it would have been had appellant correctly routed his appeal. Accordingly, the Commission exercises its equitable discretion to extend the twenty day time limit and finds appellant's appeal timely.

Similarly, in Schweitzer v. Secretary of Air Force, 05920711 (1992), the time limit for filing an appeal was waived where the employee timely filed an appeal with the agency EEO office instead of the Commission and, in the notice of appeal, stated that his representative had been hospitalized and he was filing the appeal on his own behalf. Thereafter, the agency advised a union representative of the error but did not advise the complainant. The Commission found that the complainant would have perfected his appeal with the Commission if timely advised of his error. The Commission also noted the agency simply could have forwarded the appeal to the Commission in a timely fashion.

The Commission has applied the doctrine of laches to appeals filed with the wrong agency. For example, in Polley v. Postmaster General, 0120063407 (2007), the Commission applied laches to determine an appeal was untimely filed with OFO where it had previously been filed with the EEOC's Chicago district office. The complainant's attorney had allowed the case to languish for more than six years before filing an appeal with OFO. The Commission, in Polley, held:

In this case, complainant, through her attorney, filed the appeal with the wrong EEOC office, and allowed the case to languish for 61/2 years before getting it filed with the correct office. While the attorney writes that the EEOC indicated the appeal was pending in response to telephone calls, no specific EEOC staff was identified as saying this. OFO has no record of an appeal by complainant prior to April 5, 2006, and the Chicago District Office does not process federal sector administrative appeals.

The doctrine of laches is an equitable remedy under which an individual's failure to diligently pursue their legal remedies can bar their claims. See O'Dell v. Department of Health and Human Serv., EEOC Request No. 05901130 (December 27, 1990). Here, we have a 61/2 year delay before the appeal was filed with the correct office. The agency should not have to defend itself against such a stale claim. See Motley Jr., et al. v. Tennessee Valley Authority, EEOC Appeal No. 01965792 (September 22, 1998) (class agent delayed over five years prior to filing an appeal regarding an alleged breach of a settlement agreement. Commission found that the agency should not be obligated to defend itself more than five years old simply because class agent did not diligently pursue his rights in filing an appeal). Accordingly, complainant's appeal is dismissed.
