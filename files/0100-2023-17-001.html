CHAPTER 8

EEOC APPEALS

Complainants and agencies each have appeal rights to the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission. Complainants may appeal any final agency action, including a final agency decision, in a case under Title VII, the Rehabilitation Act or the ADEA to the Commission, 29 CFR 1614.401(a), or file a civil action in U.S. district court and request a trial de novo. Any civil action must be filed within 90 days of the final agency decision. See 42 USC 2000e-16(c). Electing to file an administrative appeal does not preclude the complainant from later filing a civil action. However, once the appeal is filed, the complainant either must await a final decision from the EEOC or allow 180 days to pass without a decision prior to filing a civil action.

Agencies may appeal decisions of administrative judges to the EEOC. When an administrative judge issues a decision, the agency must either issue an order fully implementing that decision or issue a final order declining to fully implement the decision. If the agency does not fully implement the administrative judge's decision, it must, simultaneous with issuing its order declining implementation, file an appeal with the Commission. 29 CFR 1614.110(a); 1614.401(b). A complainant also has a right to appeal a final agency order fully implementing the decision of an administrative judge. In most instances, that right would be exercised by a complainant where the agency has determined to fully implement the decision of an administrative judge with a finding of no discrimination. However, a complainant also has the right to appeal a final agency action fully implementing a decision of an administrative judge with a finding of discrimination. Such appeals most frequently occurs where the scope of relief ordered by the judge is in dispute or in a case where multiple claims are made and the complainant only partially prevails on those claims.

Under the ADEA and EPA, a complainant may proceed through the administrative process, including appeal to the EEOC, but is not required to exhaust administrative remedies. Because pursuit of administrative remedies under the ADEA and EPA is optional, a complainant who elects to proceed through the administrative process may waive his or her right to file a civil action if the statute of limitations expires during administrative processing. A few courts have held that the administrative processing of an ADEA and EPA complaint does not toll the statute of limitations for filing a civil action. However, several courts have held that a civil action may be filed within 90 days of a final decision by an agency or the Commission. For a full discussion of the statute of limitation problems in ADEA cases, see Chapter 16 under the heading "Statute of Limitations."

There are two levels of appeal for both agencies and complainants--one appeal as of right and one discretionary. The first level is a right of appeal to the EEOC Office of Federal Operations. After that, either the complainant or the agency may file with the Commission a request to reopen and reconsider any decision of the Office of Federal Operations. A request for reconsideration is purely discretionary and must meet specific criteria set out by the Commission.

The right of agencies to appeal decisions of administrative judges is a direct result of the amendments to Part 1614 that became effective November 9, 1999. These amendments provide that agencies may not reject or modify the decision of an administrative judge and issue their own final decisions. Obviously, under the former system that allowed agencies to ignore the administrative judges' decisions and issue their own, there was no need for agencies to appeal. The appeals process reflects the regulatory changes requiring agencies to implement administrative judges' decisions or justify the failure to implement the decisions on appeal. As a result, the newly created agency right to file appeals with OFO is not so much a right as a requirement when agencies decline to fully implement the decisions of administrative judges.

Another significant change brought about by the 1999 amendments to Part 1614 codifies the standard of review on appeal adopted by OFO through case law over the past few years. As discussed later in this chapter, the 1999 amendments provide that an administrative judge's factual findings will be upheld if supported by substantial evidence. See 29 CFR 1614.405(a). The regulatory change is a reflection of recent Commission case law that has progressively been according more deference to the factual findings of administrative judges. The change shifts the burden of proof on appeal to agencies when they do not fully implement the decision of the administrative judge because of a disagreement with the factual findings. This represents a change in the EEOC appellate process, but is consistent with the appellate process of other administrative adjudicatory bodies. The same standard of review is applied by OFO to appeals initiated by complainants. OFO will defer to the factual findings of administrative judges in appeals filed by complainants and overturn those findings only if they are not supported by substantial evidence on the record taken as a whole. Whether an appeal is filed by an agency or a complainant, OFO conducts a de novo review of all findings of law.

When the agency does appeal a decision of an administrative judge, the appeal effectively stays any relief granted by the judge except in those cases where a removal, separation or suspension continues after the date of appeal. In those cases, the agency is required to grant prospective relief pending appeal but is not required to grant retroactive interim relief unless and until OFO issues a decision. 29 CFR 1614.505.

The new appellate process also eliminates what were effectively interlocutory appeals where multiple claims were raised by the complainant and the agency accepted some claims for investigation while dismissing others. Under the former complaint process, an agency action partially dismissing a complaint was a final agency decision subject to immediate appeal by the complainant. Failure to immediately appeal such decisions in a timely fashion resulted in a waiver of appeal rights. Under the 1999 amendments, an agency may not issue a final decision partially dismissing a complaint. Instead, it must document the reasons it believes dismissal is appropriate in the complaint file and it is not required to investigate those claims. If a hearing is requested, the administrative judge reviews the dismissed claims and may either reinstate them for hearing or uphold the agency's dismissal of the claims. The right to appeal the dismissed claims does not arise until the agency issues a final action on all claims. If the complainant does not request a hearing, the complainant's right to appeal ripens upon the issuance of a final agency decision on all claims.

I. APPEALABLE ACTIONS

Whether an action is appealable and which party has a right of appeal is a function of the type of decision issued. Pursuant to 29 CFR 1614.401:

(a) A complainant may appeal an agency's final action or dismissal of a complaint.

(b) An agency may appeal as provided in 1614.110(a).

(c) A class agent or an agency may appeal an administrative judge's decision accepting or dismissing all or part of a class complaint; a class agent may appeal a final decision on a class complaint; a class member may appeal a final decision on a claim for individual relief under a class complaint; and a class member, a class agent or an agency may appeal a final decision on a petition pursuant to 1614.204(g)(4).

(d) A grievant may appeal the final decision of the agency, the arbitrator or the Federal Labor Relations Authority (FLRA) on the grievance when an issue of employment discrimination was raised in a negotiated grievance procedure that permits such issues to be raised. A grievant may not appeal under this part, however, when the matter initially raised in the negotiated grievance procedure is still ongoing in that process, is in arbitration, is before the FLRA, is appealable to the MSPB or if 5 USC 7121(d) is inapplicable to the involved agency.

(e) A complainant, agent or individual class claimant may appeal to the Commission an agency's alleged noncompliance with a settlement agreement or final decision in accordance with 1614.504.

Appeals involving allegations of noncompliance with settlement agreements or relief granted in final agency decisions are covered in Chapters 5 and 7.

It is important to note that the EEOC also has appellate jurisdiction, in limited circumstances, over final decisions involving grievances where claims of discrimination were raised. The EEOC has no jurisdiction over agency administrative grievance procedures. The grievance procedure must be one that derives from a collective bargaining agreement and the agreement must permit the processing of allegations of discrimination. Also, the Commission's appellate jurisdiction over grievance decisions only applies to those agencies whose collective bargaining rights derive from 5 USC 7121(d). Notably, this excludes the U.S. Postal Service, which is not covered by that statute.

The Commission also has review authority over decisions of the MSPB involving allegations of discrimination. Use of the negotiated grievance procedure and the MSPB as alternatives to the EEO process is covered in Chapter 20 under the subheading "Negotiated Grievance Procedures" and "Mixed Cases Before the Merit Systems Protection Board."
