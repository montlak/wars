HOW TO USE THE EEO GUIDE

Over time, this Guide has adopted conventions of its own. Many of these conventions have been adopted by the author because there is a lack of a formal reporting system for the decisions of the EEOC's Office of Federal Operations, which means there are no accepted conventions for case citations. Most of the citations in this book are to federal sector case decisions issued by OFO. These cases are not available in printed form and are accessible only through electronic databases such as CYBERFEDS, WESTLAW, or LEXIS, or on the EEOC's website at www.eeoc.gov.

Title VII requires that EEO complaints be brought against agency heads. As a result, OFO case captions include the name of the agency head at the time the decision is issued. However, rather than requiring the reader to develop an encyclopedic recall of names of the agency heads, the author has adopted the convention of designating the agency head by title--e.g. "Postmaster General," "Secretary of Defense," "Secretary of Agriculture," etc. This allows the reader to immediately identify the agency involved. In each citation to OFO case law, there is a series of numbers starting with "0." These are the case docketing numbers. Although each of the electronic databases has its own internal citation system, the docket number can be used to retrieve a case in any database.

Block quotations from the decisions of OFO, EEOC Management Directive 110, and the Handbook for Administrative Judges may omit footnotes without a "[Footnote omitted]" signal. Additionally, block quotations may omit references to portions of the record, e.g., "ROI, Ex. 5." Ellipses are not used when record references are dropped. Readers are cautioned to read full opinions of all decisions.

Throughout this book, there are many references made to the Code of Federal Regulations and the United States Code. When references to the CFR and USC are made in the text (as opposed to quotations or parenthetical explanations following citations), an effort is made to keep the citations current, or to show whether the CFR or USC reference was only valid for a past year. The regulations and statutes change, and not all references mentioned in the Guide are checked each year. Readers should check the current edition of the CFR and USC to ensure the validity of the citation.

This Guide reflects the author's interpretation of the importance, organization, and content of decisions from the EEOC Any decision cited in the Guide should be read in its entirety. Any issue in federal sector EEO law entails some complexity. Readers should conduct their own research, using this Guide as a starting but not concluding point for analysis.
