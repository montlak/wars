FITNESS-FOR-DUTY EXAMS

Suppose you manage a unit of dock workers at a naval shipyard and one of your employees hurts their back. Or one of your security officers assigned to transporting currency has a concussion and dizziness from a car accident but wants to come to work anyway. Can you ask them to undergo a medical exam before approving their return to work? The answer is -- it depends on a variety of rules, including agency regulations, disability discrimination considerations, and the procedures for returning to work after taking leave for an injury.

As a manager, you probably already know that anything that involves an employee's medical condition or information can be tricky business. One area that is especially complicated is the fitness-for-duty exam, which evaluates whether the employee can perform the essential functions of the job. Even if you usually defer anything involving FFDs to HR, you still need to have a general knowledge of what to do when certain situations arise.

When agencies need to know whether an employee has a medical limitation that could interfere with the job, they may request the employee to undergo a fitness-for-duty exam.

Fitness-for-duty exams are medical examinations required or requested by an agency to determine an employee's ability to perform assigned duties.

Agencies may need to know about an employee's medical ability to perform when:

  • • The position requires a certain level of physical health such as law enforcement officers.
  • • An employee needs a reasonable accommodation for a disability.
  • • The employee's condition poses a direct threat to themself or others.
  • • An employee wants to return to work after taking leave for a medical condition that could impact their performance.
  • • An employee is injured on the job and makes a workers' compensation claim.

HR will steer the way. Given the complexity of FFDs, your HR office can keep you out of hot water throughout the process. Work closely with HR to:

  • • Determine whether the agency can require the employee to submit to an FFD.
  • • Ensure that ordering the FFD would not be discriminatory.
  • • Ensure you understand the procedures involved in ordering an FFD.
  • • Discuss how to best approach the employee about the FFD.

Knowing how to navigate FFDs is important because violating the procedures for fitness-for-duty exams could result in disability discrimination claims. It could also mean the reversal of any actions based on the employee's medical condition such as removal for medical inability to perform.

Before approaching an employee about a fitness-for-duty exam, contact your HR specialist to ensure you are not violating any of the numerous rules surrounding these examinations.

Before ordering an FFD

If you want to order a fitness-for-duty exam, you must comply with the Office of Personnel Management's regulations on ordering medical examinations and the Americans with Disabilities Act requirements incorporated into the Rehabilitation Act. This means that before approaching an employee about an FFD, you must work with HR to ensure that your agency:

  • • Has the authority to order or offer an FFD exam under OPM's regulations.
  • • Is ordering an exam that is job-related and consistent with business necessity.

Authority to order FFDs. An agency has the right to order FFDs when a statute or regulation authorizes them to do so. Generally, the authority comes from:

  • • Equal Employment Opportunity Commission regulations.
  • • OPM regulations.
  • • An applicable collective bargaining agreement.
  • • Agency regulations on FFD programs for certain positions.

If you do not have the authority to order an FFD examination, you may still offer one. To determine whether you have the authority to order or offer an FFD, discuss the situation with HR.

Agency FFD programs. Certain agencies have FFD programs which require medical exams prior to being hired or while the individual is employed. These programs generally involve positions that require a certain level of physical or mental fitness. Managers must always approach HR if they have evidence suggesting that an employee needs an FFD. HR will then let the manager know if the agency has any specific program requirements or restrictions for the employee's position.

Here are some examples of positions that are likely to have agency-specific FFD programs and rules:

  • • Law enforcement officers.
  • • Firefighters.
  • • Pilots.
  • • High-level security positions such as intelligence analysts and nuclear facilities' employees.

However, agencies may not order an FFD simply because the position requires the employees to be physically fit. Managers need to have some reasonable and objective evidence that ordering an FFD is necessary to successfully fulfill the position requirements. You also need to comply with the specific rules in your agency's FFD program regulations.

Focus on the purpose. To order a FFD exam, you must ensure that the exam is "job-related and consistent with business necessity." By focusing on these elements, your agency can defend itself against a claim of discrimination. Job-related means that the matter at issue must pertain to the position in question. Consistent with business necessity means that a particular test or selection criteria is necessary for a legitimate business goal or aim.

Generally, for a disability-related medical examination to qualify as job-related and consistent with business necessity, you need to have objective evidence that shows:

  • • An employee's ability to perform essential job functions will be impaired by a medical condition.
  • • An employee's medical condition poses a direct threat to himself or others.

When to order an FFD exam

Warning: You must work with HR to determine whether a fitness-for-duty exam should be ordered in any situation.

It's important to tune in to your employees' work habits, performance, productivity, and demeanor at all times. If you observe symptoms or receive reliable information from a credible third party indicating that an employee may have a medical condition that is impairing her ability to perform essential job functions or is posing a safety threat, it may be prudent to order an FFD exam. Keep in mind that any ordered exam must be job-related and consistent with business necessity.

You may show reasonable belief based on objective evidence when you:

  • • Know about a particular employee's medical condition.
  • • Have observed performance problems.
  • • Reasonably can attribute the problems to the medical condition.

Considering information from a third party. When information is learned from another person, you should work with HR to determine whether it's sufficient to justify requiring a medical examination of an employee. For guidance, weigh the following factors:

  • • The relationship of the person providing the information to the employee about whom it is being provided.
  • • The seriousness of the medical condition at issue.
  • • The possible motivation of the person providing the information.
  • • How the person learned the information such as whether directly from the employee whose medical condition is in question or from someone else.
  • • Other evidence that you or your agency has that bears on the reliability of the information provided.

Unsuccessful FFD orders. The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission has found that the following exams were not job-related and consistent with business necessity:

  • • Ordering an employee who suffered job-related physical injuries to undergo a FFD psychological examination, without evidence that the employee's mental state was at issue or that she could not perform the essential functions of her position because of a mental condition.
  • • Ordering a psychiatric fitness-for-duty exam based on disruptive behavior that could have been addressed through discipline, without evidence that the employee could not perform the essential functions of her job or that she posed a safety threat.
  • • Ordering a second fitness-for-duty exam even though the employee had already been examined by an agency medical officer and cleared for duty.
  • • Ordering a series of fitness-for-duty exams, but only providing vague and conclusory reasons for doing so.
  • • Ordering an employee to undergo a fitness-for-duty exam based on the employee's weight when the supervisor made general statements about the employee's appearance and alleged inability to perform his job.

During the hiring process. Generally, agencies may not request an applicant to undergo a medical examination before making a conditional offer of employment. An agency may conduct a job-related medical examination after it has given an applicant a clear conditional job offer, so long as all entering employees in the same job category are subjected to the same examination.

Qualifying as a medical examination

Not all medically-related exams qualify as fitness-for-duty exams. Generally, fitness-for-duty exams are medical examinations required by an agency to determine an employee's ability to perform assigned duties. However, certain medically-related exams are not categorized as medical examinations.

As a manager, you should contact HR to discuss the purpose behind any potential FFDs to determine whether another type of exam would be more appropriate. Regardless of what type of exam you decide to order, ensure that its actions do not discriminate against the employee. This means that you must be consistent in how you administer FFDs with respect to your employees with and without medical conditions.

The following procedures and tests generally are not considered medical examinations:

  • • Tests to determine the current illegal use of drugs.
  • • Physical agility tests, which measure an employee's ability to perform actual or simulated job tasks.
  • • Physical fitness tests, which measure an employee's performance of physical tasks such as running or lifting in the absence of medical examinations.
  • • Tests that evaluate an employee's ability to read labels or distinguish objects to determine the employee's ability to perform actual job functions.
  • • Psychological tests that measure personality traits such as honesty, preferences, and habits.

Remember that even nonmedical examinations may have legal restrictions. Always contact HR before acting so that you can prevent legal problems in the future.

Confidentiality and privacy

You must be careful with any medical information on your employees because mishandling medical documentation can lead to liability under the Rehabilitation Act and Americans with Disabilities Act, regardless of whether the employee actually has a disability.

The law requires agencies and managers to:

  • • Maintain confidentiality, subject to limited exceptions.
  • • Keep medical documentation in a file separate from the personnel file.

Extent of confidentiality. As a general rule, supervisors and managers have the right to know about the specific results of a fitness-for-duty exam when they need to know about necessary work restrictions or accommodations. You do not have a right to more medical information than is necessary and may not disclose it except under the following circumstances:

  1. 1. First aid and safety personnel may be told if the disability might require emergency treatment.
  2. 2. Government officials may be given information necessary to investigate the agency's compliance with the Rehabilitation Act.
  3. 3. The information may, in certain circumstances, be disclosed to workers' compensation offices or insurance carriers.
  4. 4. Agency EEO officials may be given the information to maintain records and evaluate and report on the agency's performance in processing reasonable accommodation requests.
  5. 5. The information may be disclosed to those making the decision whether to grant a reasonable accommodation.

Warning: Even if you think one of these exceptions apply, you must check with HR before disclosing medical information because you may be subject to additional restrictions or procedures.

You do not have a right to access more information than you need to conduct business and manage the employee's duties. Therefore, you may only receive notice that the employee either passed or failed an FFD exam. Generally, you may notify another manager, who needs to know about the employee's status, that a particular individual is not medically qualified because of a failed FFD, but only if the shared information is no more specific than stating that the individual is medically disqualified and does not inadvertently reveal the medical condition. To be safe, always consult HR before taking any such action so that you do not risk revealing anything by mistake.

Remember that you are prohibited from revealing medical information to employees, coworkers, peers, or other individuals who do not qualify for an exception because they do not have a need to know. Here are some mistakes to avoid:

  • • Mailing confidential medical information about an employee's condition to colleagues.
  • • Discussing an EEO complaint and the employee's medical information on a public chat forum or social media website.
  • • Advising an employee -- in front of coworkers -- not to do something because it could exacerbate their medical condition.
  • • Emailing an employee's medical information to staff who are not the employee's supervisors and not involved in the processing of the employee's accommodation request.

Safeguarding and storing medical information. It is also critical that you collect medical information on separate forms and maintain them in separate medical files that are treated as confidential. Medical documentation must be kept separate from other personnel records about the employee and must be stored only in clearly marked and secured envelopes, folders, or filing cabinets. Accidentally revealing an employee's medical information because of a failure to maintain separate, confidential files will expose you to liability.

The following are some examples of violations of the confidentiality and storage requirements:

  • • Placing a medically-related letter in the employee's nonconfidential work file and later giving the file to the employee's new supervisor.
  • • Filling out an employee evaluation that says "due to the employee's work restrictions placed by Department of Labor, she cannot be utilized in new assignments and responsibilities." This language is an unnecessary referral to the employee's medical restrictions without any clear business necessity or relationship to her performance.

GINA prohibitions. The Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act prohibits agencies from discriminating against an employee on the basis of genetic information, which includes individual genetic tests and the family history of a disease or disorder. Many common diseases and conditions have a genetic link, so many more employees with medical conditions may be covered by the law than you'd expect.

When ordering or offering an FFD, agencies should be advising the medical professional not to include any genetic information in the results provided to the agency.

This means that generally you should not be privy to genetic information. However, if for some reason, the medical professional does provide genetic information, your agency is protected from liability if it provided the disclaimer and the acquisition will be deemed inadvertent.

Generally, the results of an FFD first will be provided to the responsible HR representative, who should have removed any genetic information before sharing any medical information with you and others who need know. However, if you do receive genetic information, contact HR to determine how to handle the information and maintain confidentiality.

The following are also prohibited activities:

  • • Conducting an Internet search on an employee in a way that is likely to result in obtaining genetic information.
  • • Actively listening to third-party conversations or searching an employee's personal effects for the purpose of obtaining genetic information.
  • • Making requests for information about an employee's current health status in a way that is likely to result in obtaining genetic information.

Resistant employees and disciplinary action

If an employee refuses to submit to a correctly ordered fitness-for-duty exam, you may be able to take appropriate disciplinary action. Always check with HR before proceeding to the next step of action.

Your next step may depend on:

  • • Whether your agency has the authority to order versus offer the FFD exam. If your agency only has the authority to offer the FFD exam, you generally cannot take disciplinary action if the employee refuses absent extenuating circumstances. For example, if an employee who refuses an FFD is experiencing conduct or performance problems, then you can take a conduct or performance action. However, you cannot base the disciplinary action on the employee's refusal to take an offered FFD.
  • • Whether the employee engaged in any other misconduct. As a manager, you have the right to discipline an employee for misconduct or performance problems, regardless of whether the employee undergoes an ordered or requested FFD. Nonetheless, you still need to be careful with how you handle these situations because their problems could be caused by a medical condition. You have to make sure you do not base your actions on a suspected medical condition, which could trigger protection under the Americans with Disabilities Act. Therefore, any disciplinary actions should focus on the misconduct and performance problems, not on the suspected medical condition or disability. Although you have the right to discipline employees with medical conditions or disabilities, you have to make sure that you would impose the same discipline on an employee without a disability.
  • • Whether the employee is returning to work from leave related to a medical condition. For an employee who wants to return to work after taking leave for a medical condition, you may ask for medical documentation to certify his ability to perform his duties or you can make a legitimate request for an FFD exam. If the employee refuses, you may be able to take appropriate disciplinary action.
  • • Whether you are making a second request for an FFD exam. Once a fitness-for-duty examination has been conducted and the employee is found to be medically qualified, you may not conduct another examination without a reasonable belief that the employee's ability to perform the essential job functions has been subsequently impaired by a medical condition.

Not medically qualified -- manager's options

So what happens when your employee fails to pass the fitness-for-duty exam? The answer is -- it depends on why the FFD was requested or ordered. Here are several situations that may warrant the FFD and plausible outcomes for failed exams.

Warning: Check with HR before taking any action after a failed FFD.

  • • Preemployment. If the position requires a certain level of fitness, then the agency does not have to hire an applicant who fails to pass. However, make sure that the fitness criteria are not arbitrary and actually necessary to performing the position successfully. Also, make sure that all applicants who make it to the same level are treated the same with regards to FFDs.
  • • Offered, not ordered, for job or business necessity. If your employee agrees to undergo a requested FFD but fails, you should contact HR before taking any action because your options will depend on a variety of overlapping factors such as whether: 1) the employee can perform the essential functions of the position with an accommodation; 2) failing the FFD means that the employee poses a direct threat to himself or others due to the nature of his duties; 3) the medical condition is temporary or permanent; 4) taking leave as an accommodation would be suitable to the situation; or 5) the position itself requires a certain level of fitness.
  • • Ordered under an agency program or other regulations. When an employee fails an ordered FFD, your response must comply with the appropriate authority such as the agency FFD program rules or other relevant regulations. You should work with HR to ensure compliance.
  • • Direct threat. If the FFD was based on the possibility of a direct threat to himself or others, the agency can offer an accommodation if possible. For positions that require a certain level of fitness, work with HR to check the agency FFD program rules.
  • • Return to work. An employee who fails an FFD may be kept from the workplace if he fails an FFD after taking leave for a medical condition. However, work with HR to ensure that you are following all the relevant rules.
  • • Positions with security clearance. Some positions require an employee to maintain a certain level of physical or mental fitness. If these employees fail an FFD, it's possible that their security clearance can be suspended or revoked. If this happens, the employee may eventually be suspended or terminated.
  • • Employee's response. An employee who fails an FFD may also want to get a second opinion. This is generally the employee's responsibility, meaning the agency can move forward with whatever action is suitable for this employee's situation. A possible second opinion just means that if the second opinion conflicts with the first opinion, then the agency may have to revisit the FFD to resolve the discrepancy.

Mistakes to avoid

As a manager, you want to be aware of the different issues surrounding fitness-for-duty exams but won't need to know every specific detail. Using HR for guidance must be the priority when navigating the complicated world of FFD exams. Follow these tips to avoid complications.

Do not:

  • • Assume you know how to avoid liability when ordering or offering FFD exams. It's essential for you to talk to HR before approaching an employee or applicant about an FFD. Not doing so could mean big costs in defending against discrimination claims.
  • • Discipline an employee for failing to submit to a requested FFD. Since the FFD was requested and not required, you cannot discipline an employee for refusing.
  • • Conduct research or ask questions about the employee's family history or genetic information. The key here is to be extra careful. You could violate GINA if you actively look or ask for family medical history.
  • • Order an examination without discussing the situation with HR first. If you order an exam and find out later that you only had the authority to make a request, the employee could come back with a discrimination claim. Talking to HR first will make sure you are on the right track.
  • • Request a second examination unless you have reason to believe that a medical condition after the first exam is impairing the employee's performance. Your belief must be based on evidence arising after the first exam took place. You should focus on whether the employee is having problems with essential job functions.
  • • Assume a certain exam does not qualify as an FFD. Remember that certain FFD programs will say what qualifies and what doesn't.
  • • Request an applicant to undergo a medical examination before making a conditional offer of employment. You have to make the conditional offer before you ask for the medical exam. Otherwise, it's possible the applicant could make a hiring discrimination claim.

Ask FMG

Q: What is a fitness-for-duty exam?

A: A medical examination undertaken to determine the ability of an individual to perform the essential duties of a position.

Q: When can I order an FFD exam?

A: An ordered exam may be job-related and consistent with business necessity if a manager receives reliable information from a credible third party or observes symptoms indicating that an employee may have a medical condition that will impair their ability to perform essential job functions or will pose a safety threat. This means that an employer may show that it had a reasonable belief, based on objective evidence when the employer knows about a particular employee's medical condition, has observed performance problems, and can reasonably attribute the problems to the medical condition.

Q: How do the Americans with Disabilities Act, the ADA Amendments Act, and Rehabilitation Act affect FFDs?

A: Managers who want to order an FFD exam must ensure that they are complying with the Office of Personnel Management's regulations on ordering medical examinations and the ADA requirements incorporated into the Rehabilitation Act. The ADAAA amended some of the requirements of the ADA.

Q: Am I allowed to see the result of a FFD exam?

A: It really depends. Generally, the results of an FFD exam will be provided to supervisors and managers who need to know about necessary accommodations or restrictions on the employee's duties. This does not mean that a manager is entitled to see the employee's entire medical evaluation. Instead, managers may see the parts of the results that are necessary for purposes of job accommodations or restrictions.

In some cases, the only thing the manager needs to know is whether the individual is medically qualified or not.

Q: Can I tell others that the employee passed the FFD?

A: Managers may make a general disclosure -- by telling the next level supervisor, for example -- that the employee is medically qualified for the position according to the results of an FFD. However, this disclosure must be general and may not include specific medical information, unless one of the exceptions on disclosures applies.
