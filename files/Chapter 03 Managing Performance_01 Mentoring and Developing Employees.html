MENTORING AND DEVELOPING EMPLOYEES

Have you ever developed a close relationship with a more senior employee or manager who has helped you out, shown you the ropes, given you some valuable advice on how to do your job, get along in your agency, and advance your career?

If you have been in that fortunate position, you already know a good deal about mentoring. You may have returned the favor by "paying it forward" and mentoring junior proteges of your own. Mentor-protege relationships have been a fact of work life for centuries and have always played an important -- if informal -- role in the federal government.

Mentoring is a tool in employee and managerial development. Mentoring is recognized by management and HR experts as an important tool in employee and managerial development. Formal governmentwide mentoring programs have been developed as part of the development of managers and executives, from new managers and management fellows to candidates for the Senior Executive Service.

Your agency may also have formal mentoring programs for managers, for employees in managerial candidacy programs, for new employees, and others. Managers have also developed their own programs for their employees and subordinate managers.

In short, although informal mentoring happens all the time, mentoring is no longer left entirely to chance.

Employee development is your responsibility. Although employee development is your responsibility as a supervisor, you do not have to do it alone. You can and should rely on the advice and support of the HR development specialists in your servicing HR office. HRD is responsible for the provision, administration, tracking, and evaluation of federal employee training. It also administers the principal training budget available for federal civilian employees and managers.

Mentoring employees

It is your responsibility to see that your employees are "mentored" when this is possible and appropriate. But you are not expected to be a mentor to your own employees, that is, to treat them as proteges. You are not their mentor, you are their boss. You may act like a mentor in some ways, in that you share your institutional knowledge, you watch over their professional development, and you may even give their careers direct boosts from time to time. You may come to regard at least some of your employees as your proteges, and they may come to regard you as their mentor.

This can be a very good thing, promoting organizational continuity and productive working relationships, and it happens spontaneously when the supervisor and the employee are a good match. But real care is needed to keep the roles of supervisor and mentor distinct.

Be mindful of ethics. Mentoring relationships can cross the line into cronyism and favoritism, in violation of merit principles and government ethics. The result may be poor morale, workplace disputes, grievances, and EEO claims. Cases of favoritism, in promotion for example, may even be illegal and career-ending for managers. Mentors are expected to advise and assist their proteges, not to rig the game in their favor. This can happen in any mentoring relationship where the mentor has formal or informal institutional power, but it is a particular danger to supervisors and managers who come to regard their subordinates in the chain of command as their proteges.

Mentoring and onboarding. One of the most important purposes of mentoring is to help new employees settle in. Mentoring is part of the process that is also referred to as onboarding, a prolonged orientation of new employees to their duties and their units that can last up to a year or more. Onboarding programs have been shown to enable employees to become fully productive more quickly and to improve retention.

Even if your agency does not have a formal onboarding program, you may develop your own, arranging with HR for the welcoming of your new hire, and then designating a timeline for them to become familiar with their duties. You would generally designate more experienced employees to train the newcomer in aspects of their job, particularly in the ways your unit gets things done, and you might do some of this on-the-job training yourself.

Many onboarding programs have a mentoring component. Typically, more experienced employees volunteer as mentors. As new hires come into the unit, they are paired with mentors, most often by the supervisor.

If you operate a program like this, you need to cultivate a positive relationship with your mentors and make sure they understand the purposes of the program and their role. They are to:

  • • Help new employees settle in and become fully productive.
  • • Share their knowledge of the unit, the agency, and the way things are done.
  • • Develop new employee skills to support the unit's mission.
  • • Give new employees insight on longer-term career prospects.
  • • Improve new employee retention.

A good mentoring relationship is mutually voluntary. That is, it only works well when it is welcome to both mentor and protege, and only so long as it is welcome. Formal mentoring programs, especially those designed for onboarding, require continual monitoring to make sure they are working out for both parties. You may have to help new employees and mentors switch around until they find the right fit.

And remember: No matter how well a mentoring relationship is working, you are still the new employee's supervisor and have your own role to play.

Other mentoring programs. If you find mentoring of new employees valuable, you may want to encourage these relationships to continue, and perhaps to expand a mentoring program to include as proteges all employees at relatively lower grades or with relatively little experience who would benefit. It is usual to draw mentors from the same unit as the proteges, but for more senior proteges, it may be necessary or desirable to establish programs that cross unit lines.

Formal mentoring programs designed specifically for managers also exist. Check with your boss and HR. You may find them valuable as a protege, to give your own career development a boost -- perhaps as far as the Senior Executive Service.

And you can serve your agency by agreeing to serve as a mentor (often after training). Managers who are effective mentors build relationships and reputations of solid value to their careers.

The mentor role

Mentoring is an excellent tool to develop employees. As a federal manager, you have a lot to offer, and serving as a mentor can be very rewarding not only for your protege and the agency, but also for you. Here are some of the benefits:

  • • You get personal satisfaction from helping to develop employees for your agency.
  • • It provides an opportunity to reflect on accomplishments, challenges, and lessons learned.
  • • You build new professional relationships and create networking opportunities.
  • • You get to practice your teaching, guiding, and advisory skills.
  • • It could reenergize you to pursue your personal career goals.
  • • You position yourself for recognition.

Apply. Your agency may have a formal or informal mentoring program. Larger agencies usually have a formal application process. Examine the eligibility requirements, consult your boss, then follow the protocol of your agency to apply as a mentor. The application process usually requires you to provide your background, skills, and special interests so that the agency can match you with an appropriate partner.

Understand your role and responsibilities. Once approved as a mentor, you will likely receive training to understand your role and responsibilities. They primarily consist of:

  • • Advisor. You share your wisdom and past experiences when presented situations or problems by the protege. You offer observations and support their developmental goals. Explore the employee's strong feelings about situations and use effective questions that will enable them to examine their perspective and develop solutions. To be an effective advisor, you must employ active listening.
  • • Guide. Think of yourself as a tour guide escorting a tourist through areas in which you have local knowledge. Teaching your protege about the nuances of functioning successfully within your agency is no different. Utilize your institutional knowledge to provide sound guidance. Your protege will appreciate the inside information and will benefit from it.
  • • Role model. You serve as a positive role model to your protege. As a federal manager, you are already held to a higher standard of behavior. Your professionalism and integrity will earn you respect and set the tone for your role as a mentor.
  • • Supporter. Encouragement goes a long way and giving your protege genuine positive reinforcement will strengthen your relationship. Provide developmental advice and offer constructive feedback on their goals, plans, and ideas. By participating in developmental activities with your protege, you will demonstrate your support of them and your commitment as a mentor.

Set a schedule. You must meet with your protege for the mentorship to work, and face-to-face meetings are the most productive. Agree to and schedule regular meetings that can be held without interruption and are in a convenient location. Some mentoring programs require parties to meet one hour per month. Regardless of the schedule that is agreed upon, stick to it and respect your protege's time.

Set boundaries. It is important to discuss and agree on the appropriate boundaries of your mentoring relationship early on. Since everyone has different boundaries, take the time to have a frank discussion about what each of you expect from the other and how you prefer to interact. Here are pointers to guide you:

Both

  • • Talk about your responsibilities, what you can and can't do.
  • • Agree on frequency, duration, and intervals of meetings/communications and how this will occur.
  • • Beyond agreeing to confidentiality, discuss what confidentiality actually means to each of you in various scenarios.
  • • Agree that the mentor will not advocate on behalf of the protege in terms of promotion, special assignments, or changes in employment.

Mentor

  • • Establish what kind of access the protege will have to you (in-person, email, text, videoconference, telephone, etc.).
  • • Set your limits and determine whether being a mentor means the protege has unlimited access to you for the duration of the relationship.
  • • Let the protege know whether communicating requires an appointment.

Establishing the boundaries at the beginning will alleviate uncomfortable and awkward situations during the course of the mentorship. Only discuss those issues you are comfortable with and if personal problems crop up, refer the protege to the appropriate professionals, such as the Employee Assistance Program.

Work from an action plan. It will be easy to get sidetracked if you don't work from an action plan. In consultation with their manager, have your protege develop a plan that outlines their goals, objectives, and desired outcomes. Be sure that they agree with agency goals and promote your protégé's career development. Include the time by which each goal is to be accomplished and set progress deadlines throughout. Practically all interaction with your protege should be aimed to satisfy the goals of the action plan.

Mentoring activities. Your primary role is to impart counsel and lessons learned, and it is the protege's role to take the lead in the mentoring sessions. Your protege can propose agendas, facilitate the meeting, summarize agreements, review progress toward goals, and follow through with plans. With your busy schedule, you may find yourself in need of ideas for your mentoring meetings. Here are ideas to enhance your sessions:

  • • Spend time learning about each other to build a personal relationship before getting started on mentoring program objectives.
  • • Share career stories. From how you each got started in the government to changes made along the way. Talk about high and low points and what experiences were helpful.
  • • Discuss the protege's strengths and how to enhance their growth.
  • • Introduce the protege to a few key people who could prove helpful to their career.
  • • Look for opportunities for the protege to observe you doing your job. If possible, include him in the activity. Debrief the protege afterward to reinforce learning points.
  • • Attend a presentation your protege is delivering or meeting they are facilitating (with permission from the protege's manager). Have a debriefing afterward.
  • • Review and discuss a letter, proposal, or other document written by the protege.
  • • Meet up with other mentoring pairs for lunch, coffee, or participation in an activity.
  • • If the protege agrees, and there is no conflict, have lunch with the protege and their manager.
  • • Look for assignments where your protege can assist you with your work. Pick something that coincides with the protege's targeted growth areas.
  • • If possible, serve on a committee together on or off the job.

Conclude and reflect. Continuously take stock of the progress your protege makes toward their goals. Stay on track with the mentorship timeline and seek agreement for actions that still need to be accomplished in order to conclude the formal mentoring relationship.

Once concluded, your agency may have you complete an evaluation or questionnaire to examine the success of the mentorship. To gain full benefit of the mentorship, examine the following, make the most of what was valuable, and adjust with your next protege if necessary:

  • • What lessons did you learn?
  • • What did you learn from the protege?
  • • Were you as helpful as you could have been to the protege?
  • • Will the protege's manager see a noticeable change in their development?
  • • What did you discover about yourself during the mentorship?
  • • Did the agency gain by your mentoring skills?
  • • What new knowledge did you gain that can be applied to your work unit?

If you've had a positive mentoring relationship with successful results, you can enjoy the fruits of your labor by maintaining the connection to your protege. Over time, your relationship may transform from mentor-protege to that of colleagues working toward the same mission. This will open networking opportunities and expand your knowledge base beyond that of your work unit.

Develop your employees

Another important tool in employee development is training, formal and informal. Training is the systematic exposure of employees to the skills and knowledge required to perform their duties. Most formal training is administered, tracked, and evaluated -- and often funded -- by HRD.

Development on the job. As a supervisor, it is your job to develop your employees, and that includes arranging for the formal training they need. But there is far more you can and should do. Here are just a few possibilities:

  • • Developmental supervision. Work closely and systematically with employees to help them deepen and broaden their skills.
  • • Developmental assignments. Make work assignments designed to improve employee skills.
  • • Delegation. Delegate responsibility and authority for appropriate assignments as a developmental opportunity. Employees who do well can be delegated correspondingly more responsibility and authority.
  • • Cross-training. Use on-the-job and more formal training to introduce employees to the areas their coworkers are responsible for, usually at the same grade level. This gives employees a bigger picture of your unit's mission and where their job fits in. It is also useful when staff shortages hit or workload shifts.
  • • Details. Transfer employees temporarily to other units that need their skills. This further broadens their understanding of the agency and its mission, and how your unit fits into it.
  • • Developmental positions. Check with your boss and HR to see if some of the positions in your unit could be converted into developmental ("trainee") positions, which may be filled at any of two or more (typically three) levels, depending on the qualifications of the incumbent or new hire. An employee at a lower pay grade can then be promoted after obtaining further experience and qualifications, without the need to apply for a new position.

As you develop your employees, be aware of these pitfalls:

  • • Do not expect employees to work significantly above their grade level for more than brief periods without at least temporary promotion. Prolonged work above grade level creates pay and classification problems. If you feel positions should be upgraded, consult your boss and HR.
  • • Developmental opportunities must be provided in accordance with merit principles. You must always have a good justification for selecting some employees and not selecting others to avoid workplace disputes and respond effectively to those that do occur. In some cases, formal competitions may be required. Check with HR.
  • • Reap the benefits of employee development. The benefits of employee development to the employee are obvious, and it is your obligation as a supervisor. But you get a lot more out of successful employee development than the satisfaction of doing your job.

To be sure, there is a downside. Employee development takes staff time, and your time, away from other things. And when it works well, your best employees are "developed" to the point where you no longer have positions that can accommodate their career growth. They may even accept promotions outside your unit and go elsewhere to work.

But consider the upside. The employees whose skills and knowledge you nurture will do a better job. Your unit will be more effective and this will improve your bottom line. Furthermore, nothing encourages employee retention in your unit so much as your employees' trust that you are interested in helping them to reach their full potential. Employees who are learning new things, expanding their skills, and working toward possible promotion enjoy high job satisfaction. They tend to stay where they are as long as they can continue their career growth and move on only when they move up.

And once they move on and up, employees who know you were the boss who helped them on their way are not likely to forget it. You may find these former employees form a valuable part of your professional network in their new positions. And remember: Supervisors are judged by the performance of their employees. The better your employees do, the better you look. If your best employees continue their top performance once they've been promoted out of your unit, you'll look even better.

Warning: When you speak to employees about potential promotion -- for example, if they successfully complete a developmental assignment -- do not promise or even appear to promise what you may not be able to deliver. It is reasonable to point out that an assignment may make an employee a better candidate for promotion, but it would be best to avoid even mentioning a particular position.

Mistakes to avoid

When it comes to mentoring and developing your employees, you don't want to create problems by taking inappropriate actions.

Do not:

  • • Confuse the roles of supervisor and mentor. They are distinct, although supervisors do at times act in ways similar to mentors. Confusion can result in the violation of merit principles.
  • • Allow mentors to cross the line into favoritism. Mentors are supposed to advise their proteges on career development, not to try to rig things in their favor.
  • • Assign employees to work above their pay grade. Developmental assignments may call for employees to stretch their skills, but prolonged work above their pay grade without temporary promotion creates pay and classification problems.
  • • Promise promotions you can't deliver. You can explain that developmental opportunities may make employees better candidates for promotion, but don't get too specific.

Ask FMG

Q. As a supervisor I try to act as a mentor to my employees. Why should they need other mentors?

A. You may act like a mentor, but when it comes down to it, you are not their mentor, you are their boss. Let them benefit from the point of view of an experienced employee who is not their boss and with whom they can develop a different sort of relationship. And be careful. Bosses who treat employees as proteges may be tempted to practice favoritism.

Q. I'm a mentor in an agency program. Is it OK to push my proteges on other managers as candidates for promotion?

A. You can certainly provide an objective recommendation as part of the selection process, but avoid anything that even looks like cronyism or favoritism. Selection in violation of merit principles is illegal.

Q. I'm having trouble getting people to volunteer to serve as mentors in my onboarding program. Can I order them to serve?

A. Technically, you can, but you shouldn't. Mentor-protege relationships only work well when they are mutually voluntary. It would be better to meet with prospective mentors and explain how the unit would benefit from their help -- and how much you would appreciate it. But mentor buy-in is really essential.

Q. I picked my best employee to serve as my deputy as a developmental opportunity. It's great for them, but other employees are complaining, and I may face a grievance. Did I do something wrong?

A. If your choice is clearly justifiable based on your chosen deputy's qualifications and record of performance as compared with that of other possible choices, probably not. Your best option is to explain your choice when asked and to work with your other employees to find suitable developmental opportunities for them. If your choice is not clearly justifiable, you might work out a rotation of deputy duties among several qualified employees.

Q. Can I assign an employee to work above their pay grade for six months as a developmental assignment?

A. No, not without a temporary promotion. Otherwise, you may create some serious pay and classification problems. Consider other alternatives. Perhaps the employee can perform similar duties, but under the close supervision of a higher-graded employee. Check with HR.
