B. CONTENTS OF FINAL DECISION

Formerly, when the agency issued a final decision without a hearing, it was required do so in a form that set forth "findings by the agency on the merits of each issue in the complaint and, when discrimination is found, appropriate remedies and relief. . . ." 29 CFR 1614.110 (1992). If the agency failed to sufficiently articulate the facts, law, and reasons behind its final decision, the proper remedy was remand to the agency head for the issuance of a new decision. Roy v. Department of Defense, 01830395 (1984). Perhaps surprisingly, the Commission has written very little about final agency decisions in either its amendments to Part 1614 or its revisions to MD-110. The 1999 version of 1614.110(b) is identical to the 1992 version of 1614.110.

Under the former Part 1613, a one-paragraph decision stating that the agency head reviewed the complaint file and found no discrimination was insufficient to comply with the requirements of the regulation. See Perkins v. Department of Air Force, 01832208 (1985). Likewise, under the 1992 version of Part 1614, a final agency decision that merely cited to the Commission's regulations on dismissal was insufficient. See McIlwain v. Secretary of Army, 01965905 (1997). There is no reason to believe that the result of either case would change under the 1999 amendments.

The final agency decision must be in writing and should be transmitted to the complainant or his representative by return receipt mail. If the final agency decision enters a finding of discrimination, the decision must inform the complainant of the remedial action being taken, and advise the complainant of the right to submit a documented request for attorney fees within 30 calendar days. See 29 CFR 1614.501(e)(2). The agency must also review the situation to determine if disciplinary action is warranted and record the basis for its decision on disciplinary action in the complaint file. The decision must fully inform the complainant of appeal rights and the right to file a civil action. See 29 CFR 1614.110(b).

Most cases involving issues of credibility go through the hearing stage in order to resolve those issues. However, that is not required. A complainant has the right, even in cases where credibility is the primary issue, to waive a hearing and request a decision on the record. But, when the agency decides credibility issues on the record, it must ensure the investigative affidavits are detailed and specific. Campos v. Postmaster General, 01956793 (1996).

Once an agency issues a final agency decision with a finding of discrimination, it is bound by this decision and may not later revoke it. "Once an affirmative finding of discrimination has been made, appellant 'became entitled to [his] remedy as a matter of law'" one "which cannot be forfeited." Rousseau v. Secretary of Education, 01920410 (1992).

When an agency issues a final decision that does not meet the requirements of the Commission's regulations, the proper remedy is to remand the case for a new decision. For example, in Rochelle v. Secretary of Army, 01964205 (1997), the Commission ordered a new final agency decision where it could not determine the nature of the claims that were dismissed on the basis of timeliness or the dates on which any discriminatory acts were alleged to have occurred. See also Boaz v. Secretary of Army, 01961351 (1997) (agency failed to address all allegations raised in the complaint). An agency may not cure deficiencies in its final decision through submission of an appellate brief to OFO. See Parker v. Secretary of Defense, 05A10141 (2002).

Of course, the Commission does not always follow its own advice. For example, in Rochowiak v. Social Security Administration, 01963285 (1998), the Commission, after acknowledging that the agency's inadequate findings deprived the complainant of a meaningful right of appeal, affirmed its finding of no discrimination, without remand:

As a final matter, we remind the agency of its obligation pursuant to EEOC Regulation 29 CFR § 1614.110 to issue a final decision which "consist[s] of findings by the agency on the merits of each issue in the complaint" [emphasis added]. Failure to provide appellant with a complete and accurate statement of the law applicable to her case and a detailed explanation of how appellant's complaint failed to meet legal requirements, in effect, deprives appellant of a meaningful right of appeal. See Dorsey v. Dept. of the Air Force, EEOC Appeal No. 01941767 (Nov. 29, 1994) (in adopting the findings and conclusions of the investigator for its own final decision, the agency abdicated its duty to make its own findings based upon the factual record). Accordingly, in the future the agency shall issue a more complete and accurate statement of the facts of a complainant's case and indicate how appellant failed to meet the relevant legal requirements.

But see Walker v. Secretary of Navy, 01A12803 (2002) (quoting the same language and remanding the case for a new final agency decision).

The agency may not rely upon boilerplate discrimination law and summary conclusions in issuing a final decision. In Pitchford v. Postmaster General, 01973982 (2000), the Commission found:

Based on the record before us, we find that the agency miscast complainant's claim based on disability discrimination and that its investigation did not address her claim that the agency failed to afford her a reasonable accommodation for her medical condition and place her work site in an area free from drafts. Further, we note that the agency's FAD is limited to a statement of boilerplate law and its conclusions. In general, a summary FAD addressing the merits of a complaint that does not explain the agency's reason for its action does not respond to the Commission's requirement that the agency issue a decision containing findings and, if appropriate, a rationale on each issue(s) in the complaint. See 64 Fed. Reg. 37,644, 37,657 (1999) (to be codified and hereinafter referred to as 29 CFR § 1614.110(b)). The agency is advised of its obligation under this provision.
