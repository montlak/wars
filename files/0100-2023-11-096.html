broida eeo Chapter 02: The Precomplaint Process--Counseling Chapter 02: The Precomplaint Process--Counseling, Subchapter 05: Duties of EEO Counselor, Section A: Advising Complainants on Procedures

A. ADVISING COMPLAINANTS ON PROCEDURES

The EEO counselor is responsible for advising a potential complainant of his or her rights and the various procedures which can be used by the complainant to pursue a suspected violation of those rights. Often, the EEO counselor, with a minimum of training, must provide advice on complex issues and procedures to persons who have little understanding of the law and the complaint procedures. In the 1999 version of MD-110, new counselors must have 32 hours of training and all counselors must have eight hours of training annually. This requirement has been carried forward in the latest revision. MD-110 at 2-3 to 2-4, 2-5.

The first duty of the Counselor is to give the complainant detailed information about the EEO complaint process. As MD-110 at 2-6 explains, the Counselor must:

Advise the aggrieved individual about the EEO complaint process under 29 C.F.R. Part 1614. The EEO Counselor should explain the reasonable accommodations available throughout the EEO process. The EEO Counselor should explain the agency EEO ADR program, stating that the program is available to the aggrieved individual or advising whether the program will be made available. The EEO Counselor should further explain that if the EEO ADR program is available, the aggrieved individual will have to decide whether to seek pre-complaint resolution through the EEO ADR process or through the traditional EEO counseling process. In this regard, the EEO Counselor should inform the aggrieved individual about the differences between the two processes. (Educator)

A basic problem that remains despite the new mandatory training requirements is that the EEO process, particularly as it relates to election of remedies, is exceedingly complex. Counselors can be required to advise complainants on procedures that, at times, are not well understood by the EEOC, the MSPB and the federal courts.

The Commission requires that potential complainants be given written notice of their rights and responsibilities at the initial counseling session. Appendix C of MD-110 contains an EEO Counselor checklist, that provides:

At the initial counseling session, EEO Counselors must advise individuals in writing of their rights and responsibilities. At a minimum those rights include the following:

a. The right to anonymity.

b. The right to representation throughout the complaint process including the counseling stage. The EEO Counselor should make clear to the aggrieved person that the EEO Counselor is not an advocate for either the aggrieved person or the agency, but acts strictly as a neutral in the EEO process.

c. The right to choose between the agency's EEO alternative dispute resolution (ADR) process or EEO counseling, where the agency agrees to offer EEO ADR in the particular case, and information about each procedure.

d. The possible election requirement between a negotiated grievance procedure and the EEO complaint procedure. See Chapter 4, Section III of this Management Directive.

e. The election requirement in the event that the claim at issue is appealable to the Merit Systems Protection Board (MSPB), i.e., the dispute is a mixed case. See Chapter 4, Section II of this Management Directive.

f. The requirement that the aggrieved person file a complaint within 15 calendar days of receipt of the EEO Counselor's Notice of Right to File a Formal Complaint in the event s/he wishes to file a formal complaint at the conclusion of counseling or EEO ADR.

g. The right to file a notice of intent to sue when age is alleged as a basis for discrimination and of the right to file a lawsuit under the ADEA instead of an administrative complaint of age discrimination, pursuant to § 1614.201(a).

h. The right to go directly to a court of competent jurisdiction on claims of sex-based wage discrimination under the Equal Pay Act even though such claims are also cognizable under Title VII.

i. The right to request a hearing before a Commission Administrative Judge, except in a mixed case, after 180 calendar days from the filing of a formal complaint or after completion of the investigation, whichever comes first.

j. The right to an immediate final decision after an investigation by the agency in accordance with § 1614.108(f).

k. The right to go to U.S. District Court 180 calendar days after filing a formal complaint or 180 days after filing an appeal.

l. The duty to mitigate damages, for example, that interim earnings or amounts that could be earned by the individual with reasonable diligence generally must be deducted from an award of back pay.

m. The duty to keep the agency and the Commission informed of his/her current mailing address and to serve copies of appeal papers on the agency.

n. Where counseling is selected, the right to receive in writing within 30 calendar days of the first counseling contact (unless the aggrieved person agrees in writing to an extension) a notice terminating counseling and informing the aggrieved of:

(1) the right to file a formal individual or class complaint within 15 calendar days of receipt of the notice,

(2) the appropriate official with whom to file a formal complaint, and

(3) the complainant's duty to immediately inform the agency if the complainant retains counsel or a representative. Any extension of the counseling period may not exceed an additional sixty (60) calendar days.

o. Where the aggrieved person agrees to participate in an established EEO ADR program, the written notice terminating the counseling period will be issued upon completion of the dispute resolution process or within ninety (90) calendar days of the first contact with the EEO Counselor, whichever is earlier.

p. That only those claims raised at the counseling stage or claims that are like or related to those that were raised may be the subject of a formal complaint, and how to amend a complaint after it has been filed.

q. The identity and address of the Commission field office to which a request for a hearing must be sent in the event that the aggrieved person files a formal complaint and requests a hearing pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.108(g).

r. The name and address of the agency official to whom the aggrieved person must send a copy of the request for a hearing. The EEO Counselor should advise the aggrieved person of his/her duty to certify to the Administrative Judge that s/he provided the agency with a copy of a request for a hearing. See also Chapter 7, Section I of this Management Directive.

s. The time frames in the complaint process.

t. The class complaint procedures and the responsibilities of a class agent, if the aggrieved person informs the EEO Counselor that s/he wishes to file a class complaint. See Chapter 8, Section II of this Management Directive.

u. That rejection of an agency's offer of resolution made pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109(c) may result in the limitation of the agency's payment of attorney's fees or costs. See Chapter 6, Section XIII of this Management Directive.

v. That the agency must consolidate two or more complaints filed by the same complainant after appropriate notice to the complainant. See 29 C.F.R. § 1614.606. The EEO Counselor should advise the complainant that when a complaint has been consolidated with one or more earlier complaints, the agency shall complete its investigation within the earlier of 180 days after the filing of the last complaint or 360 days of the filing of the first complaint and that the complainant may request a hearing before a Commission Administrative Judge at any time after 180 days of the filing of the first complaint.

w. The proper contact to request any needed reasonable accommodations to navigate the EEO process.

(Footnote omitted). It is not sufficient for an EEO counselor to merely tell a potential complainant that he has not stated a claim for relief because he has not asserted a basis of discrimination. The counselor must specifically advise the complainant on the protected base. Banks v. Secretary of Air Force, 01944161 (1994). As the Commission explained in Banks:

Based on the record herein, the Commission finds that the agency improperly dismissed appellant's complaint. The agency properly noted that actions occurring after and because appellant called in sick do not constitute reprisal under federal EEO law and that being from the north or a yankee are not covered bases. Nonetheless, the Commission finds that the record does not indicate whether the EEO Counselor properly advised appellant during counseling. Under EEOC Management Directive for 29 CFR Part 1614, EEO-MD-110, at 2-19 to 2-20 (October 22, 1992), the EEO Counselor should have determined the issues and the bases for appellant's informal complaint. She further should have made clear to him that federal EEO law prohibits discrimination on the bases of race, color, religion, national origin, sex, age, and disability and that the reprisal claim must concern discrimination on one of the protected bases. There is no evidence that the EEO Counselor asked appellant whether his disparate treatment allegation involved one of the covered bases or that she described the coverage of federal EEO law. Merely advising appellant that his claims of discrimination likely were inadequate and that he did not belong to a protected group is insufficient. We find that in the absence of such additional EEO counseling, the agency should not have dismissed appellant's complaint on the grounds that it failed to state a claim.

However, the EEO counselor must balance the duty to inform the complainant as to EEO bases and procedures with the obligation to remain neutral. The Commission addressed this issue in Schulte v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 01A53588 (2005):

We do not fault the EEO Counselor. She dutifully carried out her responsibilities in accordance with our Regulations and our Management Directive. See 29 C.F.R. § 1614.105(b)-(c); Equal Employment Opportunity Management Directive for 29 C.F.R. Part 1614, Ch. 2, III (Nov. 9, 1999). Contrary to what complainant states on appeal, an EEO Counselor's role is not to coach complainant on how to adequately state his claim. The EEO Counselor merely serves as an impartial party who initially guides complainant through the EEO process. We note that the Counselor did not check off the box marked "reprisal" on her Counselor's Report and that she noted that "no basis [was] stated." Counselor's Report at 1. However, complainant himself checked off the "reprisal" box on his Formal Complaint. This subsequent notation by the complainant makes the claim legally sufficient at this stage because by doing so he in fact stated a basis for the alleged discrimination. Later, it becomes the EEO Investigator's role to collect and discover factual information concerning the claim....

Normally, when a prospective complainant contacts a counselor, a duty arises on the part of the counselor to properly advise that person on the EEO procedures. For example, if an employee contacted a counselor and indicated that he wanted to file a formal EEO complaint, it would be incumbent upon the counselor to inform the employee of the need to first engage in precomplaint counseling. However, no such obligation arises if the prospective complainant used the EEO process in the past. In Suarez v. Secretary of Treasury, 01973507 (1998), the complainant contacted a counselor for the purpose of securing a formal complaint form. The Commission upheld the agency's dismissal of the complaint for failure to bring the matter to the attention of a counselor. The Commission specifically noted the affidavit of the EEO counselor stating that she knew the complainant had previously used the EEO process and that the counselor assumed that he had completed counseling with another EEO counselor.

EEO counselors are required to be impartial. This means they should refrain from counseling persons with whom they have close relationships and also refrain from acting as an advocate for the employee. Page v. Secretary of Defense, 01970939 (1999).

EEO regulations do not require that counseling take place in person. Kleinman v. Postmaster General, 01931055 (1993).
