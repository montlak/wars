broida eeo Chapter 03: Formal Complaint Process--Acceptance and Dismissal Chapter 03: Formal Complaint Process--Acceptance and Dismissal, Subchapter 03: Bases for Dismissal of Complaints, Section O: Matters Not Raised With Counselor

O. MATTERS NOT RAISED WITH COUNSELOR

Under 29 CFR 1614.107(a)(2), the agency is required to dismiss any formal complaint of discrimination that "raises a matter that has not been brought to the attention of a Counselor and is not like or related to a matter that has been brought to the attention of a Counselor...." This expands upon the previous regulation, which permitted dismissal of complaints that raised matters not brought to the attention of a counselor. In its section-by-section analysis, the Commission explained the revision as follows:

In response to a comment, we have added the clause "and is not like or related to the matter(s) brought to the attention of a Counselor" to clarify dismissal of complaints or issues that have not been raised with a Counselor. The Commission agrees with the line of cases following the holding in Sanchez v. Standard Brands, 431 F.2d 455 (5th Cir. 1970), that bases of discrimination (e.g., race, sex, age, handicap) can be changed or added during the complaint process. We have added a provision for remanding like or related issues for counseling and other appropriate action when such an issue is raised at a later time. The agency retains the authority to dismiss any such issue for the reasons delineated in this section.

Id. It is error for the agency to dismiss a complaint for failure to bring the matters to the attention of the counselor where the matters referenced in the formal complaint are like or related to incidents of harassment brought to the counselor's attention. Mazurek v. Secretary of Army, 0120121235 (2012).

The provision applies to more than just the basis of discrimination. It also covers situations in which one action by the agency leads directly to another. For example, when an employee alleges that the agency has discriminatorily initiated an investigation into her conduct, a reassignment based on the existence of that investigation is like or related to the oriGINAl allegation. In Anderson v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01941339 (1994), the Commission noted:

[A]n agency shall dismiss a portion of a complaint "that raises a matter that has not been brought to the attention of a Counselor and is not like or related to a matter that has been brought to the attention of a Counselor." On appeal, appellant argues that she was informed by the Chief of Personnel that it was past practice of the Medical Center to reassign employees under investigation to the kitchen. Therefore, we find that appellant has alleged that her charge/arrest led to her reassignment. Therefore, the Commission finds that the reassignment allegation is like and related to the charge/theft allegation.

In Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120140614 (2014), the Commission found that an allegation that the complainant was not allowed to return to work part-time that he did not raise with the counselor was like or related to the denial of continuation of pay benefits that he did raise:

According to EEO counseling report in this case, Complainant claimed he was denied continuation of pay (COP) benefits for an on-the-job injury from May 15-25, 2013.

In his formal EEO complaint, Complainant stated that the Agency retaliated against him for seeking EEO counseling concerning the alleged discriminatory handling of his workers' compensation claim. Specifically, Complainant alleged that he was not allowed to return to work until he was medically able to work an eight-hour shift. Essentially, he appears to be alleging that the Agency denied him a reasonable accommodation by refusing to allow him to work reduced hours until he was medically able to return to work full-time.

While Complainant did not specifically raise the denial of reasonable accommodation during counseling, it is clear that this claim is sufficiently related to the claim he sought counseling for. The events all arise out of the same factual background--management's treatment of him with respect to his May 2013 on-the-job injury. Significantly, Complainant has alleged that he was denied reasonable accommodation (put off work rather than being allowed to work a reduced schedule within his medical restrictions) in retaliation for seeking counseling on the COP benefits. We find that the Agency should have accepted Complainant's reasonable accommodation claim (both disability discrimination and retaliation) because it is sufficiently related to the matter he raised in counseling.

When a complainant raised in counseling his concern that a fully successful rating would make him ineligible for future promotions and identifies the specific individuals who received promotions in his formal complaint, the second allegation is "like or related to" the performance appraisal issue. The agency errs in dismissing the allegation based on failure to raise it with the counselor. Battle v. Secretary of Army, 01953680 (1996). The proper course, if the agency needs further information, is to refer the issue back to counseling.

An allegation that the agency has prohibited the complainant from competing for several positions is like or related to an allegation that the agency abolished a specific position to keep the complainant from competing for it. See Hernandez v. Secretary of Army, 01955072 (1996). An allegation of a specific nonpromotion is like or related to a complaint that the supervisor is biased against males and regards them as stupid which hampers the complainant's career. See Caros v. Attorney General, 01A03630 (2000).

In Perillo v. Secretary of Treasury, 01964148 (1997), the Commission found that an allegation that the complainant was discriminatorily reassigned was like or related to allegations regarding to her performance appraisal and lack of training. Even though the complainant had not specifically raised the issue of her reassignment during counseling, the Commission in Perillo reversed the agency's dismissal of that portion of her complaint:

In the case at hand, neither the EEO Counselor's report nor the memorandum which appellant provided to the Counselor makes any mention of the September 1994 reassignment. However, appellant did raise the issue of her performance appraisal during counseling and in her formal complaint. In addition, appellant asserted that the performance appraisal covered only the period since September 6, 1994, that is, the date of her reassignment. Further, appellant indicated that, in September 1994, she was assigned duties for which she received no training. Thus, we find that the matter concerning appellant's September 6, 1994 reassignment is related to the allegation concerning her performance appraisal on which she received counseling.

In Garrison v. Secretary of Navy, 01971860 (1998), the Commission found that allegations that the agency denied the complainant adequate training, denied her wage and salary increases, demoted her and took away job opportunities were like or related to an allegation that the agency was trying to provoke her into quitting.

An allegation that the agency has removed the complainant is like or related to an allegation that the agency improperly requested medical information from the complainant in order to secure his removal. Moore v. Secretary of Defense, 01974339 (1998).

A complaint of a hostile work environment is like or related to a complaint over one of the incidents that allegedly created the hostile environment. The latter is an issue that helps to comprise the overall claim of harassment. For example, in Brown v. Secretary of Defense, 01993885 (2001), the Commission found that the claim that the RMO created a hostile work environment was like or related to an allegation that the RMO issued a negative memorandum for the record.

Still, the Commission has articulated no specific test to determine if one allegation is like or related to another.
