22. Severity

Factors that affect the severity of the conduct include the relationship between the employees involved, whether physical contact is involved, and whether the conduct is private or observed by others. As the Commission noted in Complainant v. Secretary of Navy, 0120130704 (2015):

In this case, Complainant was subjected to C1's unwanted romantic overtures and threatening bodily contact of a sexual nature. Moreover, C1 sometimes served as an acting supervisor over Complainant, which created an intimidating power dynamic. Additionally, C1's outrageous conduct occurred in the presence of coworkers, which heightened the demeaning nature of the behavior. Thus, we find that a reasonable person would conclude that C1's conduct created a hostile work environment for Complainant. See Complainant v. Dep't of Justice, EEOC Appeal No. 0120132393 (June 25, 2015) (Commission found complainant subjected to sexual harassment where coworker breathed heavily on her neck while making sexual noises in close proximity to her); Ramirez v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01997206 (Feb. 13, 2002) (Commission found complainant subjected to hostile work environment where coworker came up behind her while in the break room and pressed his penis into her buttocks).

In Scott v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, 01A62453 (2006), the Commission found that a few incidents where the complainant alleged that a coworker made comments about her breasts, some to managers, was sufficient to state a claim of hostile environment harassment. The Commission in Scott explained:

Whether the harassment is sufficiently severe to trigger a violation of EEO statutes must be determined by looking at all of the circumstances, including the frequency of the discriminatory conduct, its severity, whether it is physically threatening or humiliating, or a mere offensive utterance, and whether it unreasonably interferes with an employee's work performance. See id.; Enforcement Guidance on Harris v. Forklift Systems, Inc., EEOC Notice No. 915.002 (March 8, 1994). Consistent with the Commission's policy and practice of determining whether a complainant's harassment claims are sufficient to state a hostile or abusive work environment claim, the Commission has repeatedly found that claims of a few isolated incidents of alleged harassment usually are not sufficient to state a harassment claim. See Phillips v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 05960030 (July 12, 1996); Banks v. Health and Human Services, EEOC Request No. 05940481 (February 16, 1995).

After careful review of the record, the Commission finds that complainant's allegations in this matter, if true, are sufficient to state a viable claim of harassment based on sex. While the incidents are relatively few, it appears that SJ's actions were particularly humiliating and designed to harm complainant at work because he sent several emails to various managers in her chain of command criticizing the appropriateness of her office attire and asserting her breasts were visible to the audience during a presentation she made to agency personnel as well as outside visitors. Complainant asserts that SJ also discussed her appearance, and in particular, her breasts, during her presentation with a member of the audience and, several weeks later, forwarded the oriGINAl email he sent to complainant's supervisors about the purported revealing nature of her attire to other agency employees. More than a month later, complainant further alleged that SJ also spoke to a female coworker about complainant's breasts. Complainant asserts that the female coworker told her she told SJ that he was "obsessed and to leave [complainant] alone." Finally, even after her managers told SJ to stay away from her, complainant alleges that he continued to go "out of his way" to have contact with her such as "purposefully jumping on the elevator with her, which is a confined space and very intimidating."

Taken together, we conclude that complainant's allegations are sufficient to state a viable claim of harassment based on sex. Therefore, the agency incorrectly dismissed her complaint. Moreover, we advise the agency that complainant has raised a single hostile work environment claim of which the incidents identified by the agency in her complaint are only examples. As the Supreme Court explained in National Railroad Passenger Corp. v. Morgan, 122 S. Ct. 2061 (June 10, 2002), the incidents that make up a hostile work environment claim collectively constitute one single unlawful employment practice. In fact, the very nature of a hostile work environment claim involves repeated conduct that occurs over a series of days, months or even years. Id. Therefore, in investigating complainant's claim, any other related incidents should also be examined.

In Cruz v. Postmaster General, 0120091577 (2009), the Commission found an allegation that a supervisor had yelled at the complainant, a Filipino woman, in Tatalong and called her, among other things, a "mother fucker" was sufficient to state a claim of hostile environment.
