broida eeo Chapter 05: Settlement of Complaints Chapter 05: Settlement of Complaints, Subchapter 06: Voiding Settlement Agreements, Section B: Bad Faith, Subsection 01: Coercive Agreements

1. Coercive Agreements

A common refrain in breach allegations is that the complainant was forced or coerced into entering the settlement agreement in the first place. Such allegations rarely succeed and generally center around the fact that the complainant was presented with a difficult choice; namely, accept the settlement proposal offered by the agency or risk facing the consequences of losing his or her EEO complaint. The choice, particularly where the agency is offering little in the way of settlement, may be an unpleasant one for the complainant, but does not constitute coercion.

In Durfee v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01993414 (2001), the Commission set forth the basic elements of a coercion claim:

The Commission examines coercion claims with much scrutiny. The party raising the defense of coercion must show that there was an improper threat of sufficient gravity to induce assent to the agreement and that the assent was in fact induced by the threat. Such a threat may be expressed, implied, or inferred from words or conduct, and must convey an intention to cause harm or loss. A complainant's bare assertions will not justify a finding of coercion. See Rephel v. Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05921042 (May 6, 1993).

See also Marshall v. Secretary of Agriculture, 0120062971 (2008); Davis v. Secretary of Army, 01A14297 (2002).

In Durfee, the Commission found no coercion where a management official and EEO counselor pointed out during negotiations that the complainant's refusal to work weekends, combined with his inability to drive, meant that he could not perform the essential functions of his position and that his continued refusal to work weekends could result in disciplinary action, including removal.

In Palmer v. Secretary of Interior, 01A20783 (2003), the Commission rejected an allegation of coercion noting that the coercion must consist of a threat of "sufficient gravity to induce assent to the agreement...." The Commission in Palmer found:

The Commission will find the contract void if coercion, misrepresentation, misinterpretation, or mistake occur during the formation of the contract, making assent to the agreement impossible. See Shuman v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05900744 (July 20, 1990). Here, complainant submits a letter from his psychiatrist, dated November 20, 2000, stating the following:

[Complainant's] symptoms at the time included difficulty concentrating, difficulty thinking clearly, difficulty making decisions, anxiety, insomnia, and low self-esteem. These symptoms resulted in a predisposition for [complainant] to feel intimidated and overwhelmed by the court proceedings. Therefore, though he had previously spoken of his desire to fight for what he believed he was owed for the extreme mental and emotional stress on-the-job, he was unable to do so at the time of the hearing. He was not able to marshal his resources to demand what he believed was a fair hearing...[Complainant] felt a great deal of pressure from [his attorney] and from the [Administrative Judge], to settle the case....

First addressing the contention that complainant was pressured or coerced into entering the settlement agreement, we note that the Commission examines coercion claims with much scrutiny. The party raising the defense of coercion must show that there was an improper threat of sufficient gravity to induce assent to the agreement and that the assent was in fact induced by the threat. Such a threat may be expressed, implied or inferred from words or conduct, and must convey an intention to cause harm or loss. A complainant's bare assertions will not justify a finding of coercion. See Lenihan v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05960605 (December 5, 1995).

In the instant case, complainant has submitted no persuasive evidence of coercion. Nor has he demonstrated that his assent to the agreement was induced by any improper threat. We, therefore, cannot find that complainant was coerced into signing the settlement agreement, particularly since the agreement specifically states that it was entered into "freely and without coercion."

After a review of complainant's appeal, we cannot find that coercion, or any other condition, existed to such an extent as to make complainant's assent to the agreement impossible. Accordingly, the agency's decision not to void the settlement agreement is AFFIRMED.

An allegation of coercion was rejected in Craig-Ziyad v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A51806 (2005), where the complainant alleged her supervisor presented her with the agreement 30 minutes before her shift, would not let her consult with a representative and she had just returned from leave from attending a family member's funeral.

Administrative judges place a high premium on settlement. The Commission encourages its administrative judges to actively pursue resolution of cases. Often, this means the administrative judge may give assessments of a case or advise the parties of the downside of continuing to pursue litigation. It is not coercion for an administrative judge to tell a complainant that the agency can appeal an unfavorable decision and substantially prolong the proceedings. It is "rather provid[ing] complainant with valuable information to consider while he considers the agency's offer." Caballero v. Secretary of Air Force, 01A22127 (2003).

Where the complainant did not speak English, the terms of the agreement were read to her by an EEO counselor and she was not provided with time to consult with her representative before signing, the Commission found the agreement was coercive and set it aside as void. In Cintron v. Postmaster General, 0120111786 (2011), the Commission also found that the agency had altered evidence as to when the agreement had been signed:

The record reveals several factors which support a determination that the subject agreement was not signed voluntarily. Complainant argues she was pressured into signing the settlement agreement under the threat that her case would remain closed. Complainant could not read the settlement agreement or the letter prepared for her because she does not speak English, and relied solely on the EEO Counselor's verbal assurances. Complainant was not given sufficient time to review the agreement or to consult with her representative before signing.

Moreover, the Agency submitted two copies of the settlement agreement. The copy submitted as part of the complaint file shows the Complainant having signed the settlement agreement on July 21, 2011 and the Agency representative having signed on June 21, 2010. However, this copy indicates that the date was altered from a "7" [July] to a "6." [June] The copy that was attached to the Agency's brief on appeal shows that the date next to Complainant's signature was altered from "7/21/2010" to "6/21/2010," which creates the appearance that both Complainant and the Agency representative signed the agreement on June 21, 2010.

Misrepresentation by an agency as to facts relevant to the complaint is grounds for setting aside a settlement agreement. Wolf v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Request No 05900417 (June 14, 1990). The Commission does not expressly determine that the Agency had pressured and deceived Complainant into signing the settlement agreement. However, the Commission notes the particular facts of the instant case. Most specifically, we find that there was a formidable language barrier attendant Complainant's comprehension of the proceedings. Therefore, the Commission concludes that Complainant did not knowingly and voluntarily sign the settlement agreement. Thus, the settlement agreement is void.

The Commission found there was no coercion where the complainant alleged that she under medication and incapable of entering an agreement but submitted no medical evidence supporting her claim. In Charles v. Secretary of Army, 0120122899 (2012), the Commission also noted that the complainant was represented by an attorney and she was given time and a private room to consult with her attorney prior to signing the agreement.
