4. Relapses

Unfortunately, with drug or alcohol abuse, relapse is always a possibility. It is clear that the agency's obligations to an employee are diminished when a relapse occurs, but they are not eliminated entirely. An agency may not hold pretreatment conduct against an employee when problems reemerge subsequent to treatment. See Drummond v. USPS, 01841888 (1987). But the agency may discipline the employee for similar offenses that occur after treatment and without giving rise to an automatic presumption that the discipline is based on pretext. See Tobe v. Department of Agriculture, 03840011 (1987). In Tobe, the Commission held:

In a disciplinary context, reasonable accommodation of an alcoholic employee requires forgiveness of his past alcohol-induced misconduct in proportion to his willingness to undergo and favorable response to treatment. Moreover, knowledge that the employer may resurrect his alcohol-related infractions for penalty-enhancement purposes if he errs in the future may well be disincentive for the alcoholic employee to enter, continue or complete necessary treatment....

The agency does, however, have an obligation to consider discipline short of removal when employees suffer relapses. However, an agency that issues one reprimand and two suspensions in response to relapses is justified in removing an employee for repeated misconduct due to alcoholism. See Moten v. Department of Army, 03870002 (1987). The Commission noted that previous progressive discipline "did not produce the desired result--a rehabilitated employee." See also Hicks v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 01873038 (1989) (an employee previously given lengthy periods of leave for three rehabilitation attempts is not entitled to a fourth chance).

While the agency may not have an obligation to give an employee repeated chances for rehabilitation, once it grants such a chance it must give the employee an opportunity to demonstrate that he is rehabilitated. Kean v. Department of Army, 03850053 (1988). In that case, the employee first declined the agency's suggestion of a residence treatment program in favor of a self-help program. The employee later engaged in alcohol-related misconduct and the agency permitted the employee to enter a residence program. The Commission found that the agency could not remove the employee for his misconduct once it had permitted him leave to enter the program, but must give him an opportunity to show he was recovered.

Likewise, where the agency does nothing to fulfill its affirmative obligation to the employee, but the employee undergoes rehabilitation on his own initiative, the agency cannot act overly harsh if the employee has a recurrence of attendance problems. Perez v. Postmaster General, 01902361 (1990). This is particularly true where the agency has tolerated excessive absences prior to the time it was advised of the employee's alcoholism.

In Johnson v. Secretary of Navy, 03910140 (1992), the Commission reviewed an MSPB decision upholding the removal of an employee who suffered from substance dependency. The existence of the condition and its effect on the employee's attendance was undisputed. It was further undisputed that the employee performed his job in a satisfactory manner when he was in attendance at work.

The Commission found the employee had been given the then-requisite "firm choice" when he entered into a last chance settlement agreement that provided for treatment of his addictions and further provided for his removal if he failed to successfully complete the treatment program. Perhaps predictably, the employee in Johnson suffered a relapse:

[T]he evidence in the record indicates that petitioner was satisfactorily performing his job when he was at work. In fact, his performance improved when he entered rehabilitation. Petitioner's major impediment to job performance was his absenteeism, and the parties do not dispute that petitioner's absenteeism was related to his drug addiction. Accordingly, the issue to be decided in this case is whether the agency satisfied its duty to reasonably accommodate petitioner's drug addiction.

The agency posits that petitioner was offered reasonable accommodation, inasmuch as his removal was held in abeyance while he sought treatment, and he was given a "firm choice" between treatment and discipline. The agency's duty of reasonable accommodation to an employee handicapped by alcoholism or drug addiction includes the requirement "to exert substantial affirmative efforts to assist (the employee) toward overcoming (the employee's) handicap before firing (the employee) for performance deficiencies...." See Whitlock v. Donovan, 598 F. Supp. 126, 131 (D.D.C. 1984), aff'd sub nom., Whitlock v. Brock, 790 F.2d 964 (D.D.C. 1986). Providing such an employee with a "firm choice" between treatment of his drug addiction and termination constitutes one such affirmative effort. Cf. George N. Ruggles v. H. Lawrence Garrett, III, Secretary Department of the Navy, EEOC Petition No. 03840216 (November 17, 1989); Francis P. O'Brien v. Robert S. Mosbacher, Secretary, Department of Commerce, EEOC Petition No. 03850216 (November 17, 1989) (firm choice requirement upheld for alcoholic employees).

In the present case, the Commission finds that the agency provided petitioner with a firm choice on April 28, 1989, wherein it agreed to hold petitioner's removal in abeyance if he sought treatment; complied with all the terms of the treatment program; and maintained satisfactory attendance, conduct, and performance. Examining the agreement, and the terms of the treatment program, we find that it unambiguously placed petitioner on notice that he had to choose treatment for his drug addiction and remain drug free, or face the consequences--the termination of his employment. The record establishes that although petitioner continued with rehabilitation, he suffered a relapse, and therefore violated the firm choice agreement. Thus, the agency was obligated to enforce the agreement.

Petitioner argues that he should have been given an opportunity to complete rehabilitation before any adverse action was taken against him. The Commission has held that once an agency authorizes, or constructively authorizes, an employee to enter a rehabilitation program, the agency must afford the employee a reasonable opportunity to demonstrate success in the program. Mitchell Davis v. Michael P.W. Stone, Secretary, Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 02890004 (November 21, 1989); rev'd on other grounds, Mitchell Davis v. Michael P.W. Stone, Secretary, Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05900280 (June 21, 1990). Nonetheless, we concur with the Board that this holding applies to cases such as Davis, supra, wherein the agency authorizes treatment, but attempts to issue discipline, during the treatment phase, for conduct which occurred prior to the treatment. Thus, the agency is not required to forego discipline if the employee relapses, thereby violating a firm choice agreement. See Felipe J. Ray v. Jack Kemp, Secretary, Department of Housing and Urban Development, EEOC Petition No. 03910045 (May 2, 1991). To hold otherwise would in effect invalidate the meaning of "firm choice."

We note that the evidence in the record reflects that petitioner's efforts at rehabilitation were sincere and successful. Indeed, the Rehabilitation Act and its accommodation provisions were enacted to assist employees like petitioner in overcoming their addiction problem. Our decision is not intended to detract from petitioner's success, and we applaud his efforts. Nonetheless, the question presented by this case is whether the agency satisfied its duty to reasonably accommodate petitioner. We must answer "yes" to this question, inasmuch as petitioner's removal was held in abeyance, a "firm choice" was clearly communicated to petitioner, and the agency enforced the "firm choice" agreement when its terms were violated.

Subsequently, the Commission has held that a settlement agreement which provides a firm choice for an employee suffering from alcoholism is not required to also provide for relapses, even though relapses are frequently experienced. Mello v. Postmaster General, 01944734 (1995). Allowing for relapses would "invalidate the meaning of a firm choice."
