ABSENCE WITHOUT LEAVE

When employees stay away from or leave their workstation without proper cause or your specific approval, they may be considered absent without leave. Absence without leave, also known as unexcused absence and generally referred to as AWOL, is a challenge to the core of your supervisory authority, and you must be prepared to respond appropriately.

AWOL and discipline. AWOL is one of the most common causes of disciplinary action. Unplanned and unexcused absences from duty are inherently disruptive to organizational efficiency and productivity. Therefore, the Merit Systems Protection Board has long recognized a nexus between these absences and the efficiency of the service, a requirement to justify disciplinary penalties. In the majority of cases, the MSPB has found that disciplinary action for unexcused absence promotes the efficiency of the service.

Generally speaking, the agency does not have to show that an employee's AWOL had a specific adverse impact on operations. And if you find an employee is AWOL, the employee has the burden of proving that the absence was, in fact, authorized by approval, law or regulation.

Situations may not be clear-cut. Be aware that not all situations are clear-cut, and you should exercise caution before you label an absence unexcused.

Employees do not have a general right to cover their AWOL period with available annual or sick leave, FMLA leave, leave without pay, or any other form of leave. However, you must determine whether this coverage is justified. If it is, , you may have to approve the leave to cover the absence and not designate it as AWOL. As always in such matters, you should consult with HR before reaching a decision.

Know your agency system. Agency systems of recording attendance and calculating pay vary. Some, for example, allow noting AWOL to the minute of absence. Others require longer blocks of time -- 15 minutes for example. Check with HR. And be sure you understand the actual process of documenting and changing an employee's attendance record.

These records are usually kept automatically and may be invisible to you -- until something goes wrong, when you need to know how they work. It is better to know in advance. And you will need to know if you are to monitor attendance. How else, after all, can you know what an employee's attendance record has been -- and prove it if you have to justify discipline?

When to use AWOL

A determination that an employee is or was AWOL is a serious matter. When you make this determination, you are saying, in effect, that the employee has done something wrong, something that may justify a formal disciplinary procedure and, in serious cases, lead to the employee's removal.

Make sure you are justified on the facts as you know them when you determine an employee is AWOL:

  • • Be prepared to change your decision if new facts come to light.
  • • Document your decision.
  • • Consult with HR.

How does this work in practice? Let's look at two scenarios.

Nina says she was ill. Your employee Nina hasn't shown up or called in to work as expected. You decide she is AWOL. After an absence of two days, she shows up with an explanation that she was too ill to come to work, with a physician's letter to prove it. She says she didn't call because she felt too ill to request sick leave within one hour of the start of her shift, as agency rules require.

What will you do? As an initial step, you don't really have any choice. Nina has proof she was entitled to sick leave -- she simply neglected to follow proper request procedures. You will have to change her AWOL status to sick leave if she has any available. Or the absence may be covered by annual leave or LWOP. She was absent from work for a legitimate reason.

However, you may determine that she could have followed proper sick leave request procedures. At the least, she could have asked someone else to call, or called as soon as she felt well enough.

Your next step? Counsel her accordingly -- with documentation -- and consider discipline if the offense is repeated.

Marcos says he had a personal problem. Your employee Marcos hasn't shown up or called and after two days you decide he is AWOL. He returns to work on the third day with the explanation that he and his wife had to work out some personal difficulties and he was too embarrassed and upset to call with a last-minute annual leave request.

What will you do? That would be your judgment call. You would have every right to let your AWOL determination stick. If he has a bad attendance record or a history of improper leave requests, you probably should stick to AWOL and consider discipline. But if you find his explanation credible and his record is otherwise good, you may and probably should permit him to use annual leave to cover his absence, with a documented warning that a repeat performance could lead to a finding of AWOL and to disciplinary action.

Last-minute annual leave requests. Annual leave has no restrictions on its purpose, and you should always approve it when operational conditions allow without asking why it is needed -- even if it is requested at the last minute. This is generally good policy. Employees are less likely to try to abuse sick leave, or to cause you problems by simply not showing up, when they know you care about them, understand that personal problems need to be dealt with, and will approve last minute annual leave requests whenever you can.

However, this is your decision, and an employee who insists on staying away from work despite your disapproval of an annual leave request may be found to be AWOL. If the employee claims to have a legitimate reason for the absence, you then have the right to ask for an explanation.

Short periods of AWOL. AWOL can be used for relatively short periods of time -- for example, to combat chronic tardiness or to take action against a wandering employee who appears for work but who then can't be found (or can't be found working). In particular, employees are responsible for getting to work on time. If they chronically fail to do so, they can be considered AWOL for the time they miss, no matter how bad the traffic or how unreliable their childcare provider.

Consistency. Do you have to treat all your employees the same when it comes to AWOL? No, provided you treat them all fairly and reasonably.

You may be more lenient with those who have good attendance records and less lenient with those whose poor attendance records justify it. Just be sure you can justify these differences if an AWOL employee decides to file a grievance or EEO complaint or appeals an eventual disciplinary action.

You may (and often should) use AWOL when employees:

  • • Fail to report for work or they leave their workstations for no good or authorized reason and fail to provide any explanation.
  • • Fail to return from approved leave as scheduled, without proper reason. If employees want to extend annual leave, for example, this should be approved in advance (even at the last minute) in the usual way. Otherwise, you may find them AWOL for the period of unscheduled absence, even if they have annual leave available.
  • • Fail to return to work once workers' compensation benefits have been terminated. Consult with HR and your agency's workers' comp specialists.
  • • Provide explanations for unexcused absences that are insufficient for retroactive or last-minute approval of leave.
  • • Fail to provide medical documentation or the documentation is insufficient or not submitted within the allowable time frame.
  • • Are chronically late or absent.
  • • Have a pattern of failure to follow proper leave request procedures.

You should not use AWOL when employees:

  • • Fail to follow proper leave request procedures but turn out to have legitimate reasons for their absences (e.g., illness, family emergencies) under sick leave or FMLA leave regulations. An AWOL determination must be reversed in such cases, since sick leave and FMLA leave for authorized purposes are statutory employee rights.
  • • Require greater flexibility because of a disability. Qualified employees with disabilities may be entitled to "reasonable accommodation" of those disabilities. In some cases, this may include greater flexibility in your enforcement of attendance rules. Get with HR immediately if the employee refers to any disability or medical impairment as the reason for a failure to appear for or during a shift -- or if you have any reason to suspect this may be so.
  • • Request annual leave at the last minute -- if you can approve it for operational reasons.

Not available for work. Even if employees have a legitimate reason to stay away from work that precludes AWOL, they must show up sooner or later. In instances of prolonged absence or very irregular attendance, employees may be removed because they are not available for work, whether or not they are charged with AWOL. Consult HR.

Follow through

Your use of AWOL is likely to have consequences, and you should be prepared for them.

Use it when you need it. Although you have the authority to determine when to approve leave that has not been properly requested, you should not use that authority to let attendance problems slide. If you do, you will have a major morale problem on your hands. You will give your employees the impression you don't much care whether they show up for work, or you are too weak to enforce leave and attendance rules.

Use AWOL when it is justified and follow up with appropriate discipline. Even your most motivated employees don't want to be "the only ones working around here." Abuse of your failure to enforce attendance rules can only lead to depressing effects on your unit's performance -- and it is your unit's performance that is likely to make or break your career as a federal supervisor. The use of AWOL when it is required should be an essential part of your performance management strategy.

Be prepared to follow through. If you find an employee is AWOL, even before you proceed to discipline, the employee may well respond with a grievance, or even an EEO complaint. After all, when you determine an employee is AWOL you declare that the employee has broken the rules and done something wrong. The employee will not be paid for the time worked.

AWOL is not leave without pay. Of course, employees who are granted LWOP aren't paid for that time either. But they are on approved leave. Most employees, and most union officials, know the difference. So should you.

If you approve LWOP for an unplanned and unexcused absence you may deny the employee pay, but LWOP does not allow for discipline, since it is approved leave. Employees may not like it, but they are unlikely to take further steps, such as filing a grievance or making an EEO complaint. And employees with chronic attendance problems are unlikely to improve if you put them on LWOP when they are late for work.

Since lengthy or repeated AWOL can lead to discipline, employees are far more likely to appeal a determination of AWOL than an approval of LWOP. And if you do move to progressive discipline for AWOL, serious disciplinary penalties (suspensions of more than 14 calendar days, demotions, disciplinary transfers and removals) are subject to appeal to the MSPB.

You should not let the prospect of an employee grievance, complaint, or appeal stop you from using AWOL or disciplining serious cases. Just remember the points we've gone through, use AWOL when it is justified, and -- above all -- document what you have done, and why.

Know the rules, consult HR. It all becomes easier if you know the rules, know your agency policy, and understand any governing provisions of the collective bargaining agreement if you supervise bargaining unit employees.

Work from the beginning with HR, follow the advice and instructions of your servicing employee and labor relations specialists, and you will have the confidence to use AWOL when you should, and deal with the consequences when you must.

Mistakes to avoid

Do not:

  • • Insist on AWOL when employees have a legitimate entitlement to leave. If employees have an entitlement to leave (e.g., under the FMLA or sick leave regulations), you may have to approve it, even when it was not properly requested.
  • • Treat all employees the same way. A finding of AWOL should depend on the record of the employee as well as on the facts of the absence. Employees with good attendance records should be treated more leniently than those with a history of failure to follow proper leave request procedures or of unexcused absences.
  • • Ignore claims of disability or medical impairment. Qualified employees with disabilities are protected under EEO law against discrimination and are entitled to "reasonable accommodation" of their disabilities under some circumstances. This may require you to exercise greater flexibility in enforcing attendance rules. Always check immediately with HR.

Ask FMG

Q. Can I differentiate between employees when it comes to finding them AWOL?

A. Yes. A finding of AWOL should depend on the record of the employee as well as on the facts of the absence. Employees with good attendance records should be treated more leniently than those with a history of unexcused absences or failure to follow proper leave request procedures.

Q. Can I find an employee AWOL even if it eventually turns out there was a legitimate reason for the absence?

A. No. Employees may have a right to approved leave in certain instances, especially under sick leave and FMLA provisions, even if they did not follow proper leave request procedures. Employees with disabilities may require greater flexibility as a "reasonable accommodation." Check with HR.

Q. Do employees have a general right to use available sick leave, LWOP, or annual leave to cover unexcused absences, in place of AWOL?

A. No. You may permit them to do so, but there is no requirement that you allow it, beyond certain limited circumstances that may be governed by sick leave or FMLA provisions or the right of an employee with a disability to reasonable accommodation. In these cases, consult HR.

Q. What's the difference between AWOL and LWOP?

A. A finding of AWOL is different from a decision that an employee is on leave without pay, although both have the effect of stopping the employee's pay for the period of absence. If you allow an employee to cover an absence with LWOP, you give the employee your approval to be absent. If you approve leave, even LWOP and even after the fact, you cannot discipline an employee for being AWOL (although in some cases you may be able to charge the employee with the less serious offense of failure to follow proper leave request procedures).

Q. Can I charge employees with being AWOL if they are at the workplace, but not at their workstations?

A. Yes. If employees can't be found or can't be found working, they may be charged with being AWOL. Just make sure you get all the facts.

Q. Can I use AWOL in instances of tardiness?

A. It depends on the situation. Isolated instances of tardiness can be handled as excused absences or, for repeated instances, with annual leave or LWOP. However, if the employee has a chronic tardiness problem and counseling has not worked, AWOL may be effective.
