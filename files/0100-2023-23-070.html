13. Essential Functions and Position Requirements

It is not at all unusual that employees with alcohol problems eventually lose their driving privileges as the result of arrest for operating under the influence. The determination of whether the employee is required to drive as an essential function of his or her position can be a complicated one. The issue is specifically addressed In Chapter 15 under the heading, "Alcohol and Substance Dependency."

However, when driving is an essential function of a position, the agency is not required to provide the complainant with a driver as a reasonable accommodation. To do so would require the elimination of an essential job function--something an agency is not required to do to accommodate an employee. See McCann v. Postmaster General, 01A45426 (2006).

When a security clearance is required for a position, the failure to obtain or maintain the required clearance means the employee is unable to perform the essential functions of his position. Such an employee, even if disabled, is not entitled to reasonable accommodation. As the Commission explained in Zimmerman v. National Archives, 01941377 (1995):

The Commission finds that appellant is a disabled individual. Prior to his employment with the agency appellant was diagnosed as having OCD [Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder]. The diagnosis necessitated the treating physician to prescribe a medicine to help appellant control the OCD manifestations. Moreover, during his employment appellant acknowledged that he had manifestations of OCD. The symptoms he experienced affected his ability to work and concentrate on the tasks assigned. Furthermore, appellant's history of OCD was on record with the agency. For the foregoing reasons, appellant was a disabled individual.

Appellant also must show that he is a "qualified" individual with a disability within the meaning of 29 CFR § 1614.203(a)(6). That section defines qualified individual with a disability as meaning, with respect to employment, a disabled person who, with or without reasonable accommodation, can perform the essential functions of the position in question. In order to perform the essential functions of the position for which appellant was hired he had to have a top secret security clearance. Appellant did not obtain a top secret security clearance, an issue which is not before the Commission. As such, appellant was not qualified for the position in question because he did not meet a requirement for the position. There is no evidence that the requirement of a security clearance was disparately applied to appellant or individuals with disabilities.

Since appellant was not a qualified individual with a disability, he is not entitled to protection under the Rehabilitation Act. Having reached this conclusion, the Commission need not reach the agency's arguments set forth in its statement in opposition to appellant's appeal.

But see Thomas v. Secretary of Air Force, 03910088 (1991) (agency has duty to consider reassignment of qualified individual with a disability who is denied necessary security clearance).

Where all applicants for an intern program were required to sign a three-year mobility agreement to gain both headquarters and field experience, this was an essential element of the position. In Baer v. Secretary of Navy, 0120060801 (2007), the Commission found that the complainant's failure to execute the agreement and request for an accommodation of having a worksite within 45 minutes of her home meant that she could not perform an essential function of the position and, therefore, was not a qualified individual with a disability.

Likewise, where the employee's duties require him to carry a gun, a psychiatric condition that prevents him from doing so precludes the employee from establishing that he is a qualified individual with a disability. The court found the employee, a deputy for the U.S. Marshal's Service, could not perform the essential functions of his position unless he could carry a firearm. Lassiter v. Reno, 885 F. Supp. 869 (E.D. Va. 1995), aff'd, 86 F.3d 1151 (Table) (1996).

In one case, the Commission found that the complainant's inability to follow supervisory instructions that were given repeatedly meant that he was unable to perform the essential functions of the position and, therefore, he was not a qualified individual with a disability. In Gruber v. Secretary of Interior, 0120064068 (2008), the complainant, who had suffered a head injury and had short-term memory loss, was a temporary worker at a national park. After learning that a female employee at the gift shop alleged that the complainant was subjecting her to conduct that she viewed as unwelcome and frightening, the complainant's supervisors instructed him not to have any further contact with the employee and to refrain from entering the gift shop. Despite considerable effort to impress the instructions on the complainant, he continued to enter the gift shop. The Commission found that the ability to follow instructions was essential to the job and that the complainant, for medical reasons, could not follow those instructions and could not perform an essential job function.
