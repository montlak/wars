3. One-Time Incidents

As with sexual harassment, generally, one-time incidents do not rise to the level of severity or pervasiveness to constitute a hostile work environment. In the context of race-based harassment, one exception to that general rule is single incidents that occur against a backdrop of racial or ethnic tensions, or conduct that by its very nature conjures up a history of discrimination. For example, in Tootle v. Secretary of Navy, 07A40127 (2006), the Commission found that both factors were present. In Tootle, a hangman's noose was placed in the middle of a workspace used by black employees. In finding that the one incident created a hostile environment for the complainant, the Commission noted that ample evidence was introduced at the hearing to demonstrate that prior to the incident there was a racially--charged environment at the agency. However, the Commission in Tootle went on to find:

The evidence further supports the finding that the appearance of a hangman's noose in the middle of the workspace of African-American employees in and of itself, even discounting the charged racial climate of the agency, created a hostile work environment for complainant. There is no question that a noose evokes an image, particularly among African-Americans, of a disgraceful past of extreme violence and racial bigotry. As the AJ noted, the Commission has found that a noose in the workplace is a single event that directly involves a threat to life. Reasonable minds are sure to agree, finding a noose hanging in one's workspace is cause to feel physically threatened, particularly if one is an African-American and working in a racially charged environment.

Agencies can still avoid liability, even in undeniably severe instances of race-based harassment, if the response is swift and effective. That was the case in Gilda M. v. Secretary of State, 0120182560 (2019), where the complainant received an anonymous letter depicting the complainant and a coworker with nooses around their necks. The letter was subsequently distributed around the office. Dispensing quickly with whether the letter was severe enough to create a racially hostile work environment, and finding that it was, the Commission turned to the agency's response. The agency in Gilda M. avoided liability by immediately investigating the letter, informing management, and meeting with staff to discuss the "inappropriate" and "hateful" letters:

In this case, S2 learned about the letters on June 8, 2015. In response, on June 8, 2015, S2 contacted the Agency's Office of Civil Rights (OCR) for guidance, which referred S2 to the Diplomatic Security (DS). S2 forwarded the letters to DS on June 8, 2015. On June 9, 2015, S1 called Complainant and informed her about the letters. On June 10, 2015, S1 met with Branch employees, informed them about the letters, told staff that the letters were "inappropriate" and "hateful," and read and mailed the Agency's anti-discrimination policy to them. Additionally, S1 told employees to come to him if they were aware of any harassment or conduct that violated the Agency's antidiscrimination policy.

On June 16, 2015, Complainant returned to work, and on June 17 and 18, 2015, S2 and Complainant exchanged emails and met about the matter. On June 17, 2015, S2 gave Complainant a copy of the letter and informed her that DS was investigating the matter. The Office of Special Investigations (OSI) investigated the matter from June 2015 until December 2015. After over 20 interviews with employees and a forensic analysis of the letter for fingerprints, OSI concluded that it was unable to identify the sender of the letter.

After a thorough review of the record, we find that the Agency took immediate and appropriate corrective action that included immediate investigation of the letters, notification of Complainant, and meeting with staff to denounce the conduct and reiterate the Agency's antidiscrimination policy. Additionally, the Agency undertook an investigation that included interviews and forensic analysis in an unsuccessful attempt to identify the sender of the letters.

Although Complainant contends that this case is analogous to the TVA case, the TVA case involved a situation wherein a noose remained in the workplace days after that complainant reported it to management. In this case, management immediately investigated and addressed the letters with staff after they were delivered to the office. After management became aware of the noose letter, there was no recurrence of this type of incident, which indicates that the Agency's response was effective. Therefore, we find that Complainant has not provided any evidence from which a reasonable fact-finder could conclude that the Agency is liable for the harassment.

The Commission had little trouble finding that the supervisors' actions were sufficiently severe to state a claim of harassment based on race, in Vernie M. v. Postmaster General, 0120160771 (2016), where two supervisors, on the same day and during the same incident, accused the complainant of "playing the race card and causing racial tension." One supervisor also commented, "you know how they do" and called the complainant "ghetto and arrogant." The other supervisor allegedly screamed in the complainant's face, pointing his finger at her and made a threatening gesture. In Wilson v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A30907 (2004), the Commission found that a series of racial comments made in one day, which included the use of the word "nigger," created a hostile environment. The Commission relied, in part, upon case law holding that "nigger" is a racially charged term that "dredges up the entire racial history in this country." Id.

However, a supervisor's comment to the complainant (African American) about keeping a secret "like the Underground Railroad" was not sufficiently severe to create an abusive work environment in Natalie F. v. Secretary of Defense, 0120141527 (2016):

Upon review of the record, we find that Complainant did not establish a claim of harassment. Regarding incident 1, we find that, even if true, Complainant did not show that it was sufficiently severe or pervasive to create an abusive working environment. Complainant alleged that S2 made a comment to her in August 2011 about keeping a secret "like the Underground Railroad." According to Merriam-Webster's Dictionary, the Underground Railroad was "a system of cooperation among active antislavery people in the United States before 1863 by which fugitive slaves were secretly helped to reach the North and Canada." Examples of the types of single incidents that can create a hostile work environment based on race include: an actual or depicted noose or burning cross (or any other manifestation of an actual or threatened racially motivated physical assault), a favorable reference to the Ku Klux Klan, an unambiguous racial epithet such as the "N-word," and a racial comparison to an animal. EEOC Compliance Manual Section 15, "Race and Color Discrimination," No. 915.003, at 15-VII.A.2 (Apr. 19, 2006). An example of the type of single incident that, while racially offensive, does not create a hostile work environment based on race includes a supervisor's comment to an African-American librarian about not wanting to create a "ghetto corner" in the library devoted to books of interest particularly to African-Americans. See id. We find that S2's comment to Complainant about keeping a secret "like the Underground Railroad" is more similar to the latter than the former.

One instance of a coworker calling the black complainant "boy" stated a claim in Nelson R. v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, 2020002784 (2020):

The Agency found that because the action was a single action and SPM was not in a supervisory position over Complainant, Complainant fails to state a claim. We note, however, that we have previously held that the use of the term "boy," when used as a racial slur, is sufficient to state a claim of harassment. See McAllister v. Dep't of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05960416 (May 22, 1997). The Agency cites Sang v. Department of State, EEOC Appeal No. 0120152717 (October 24, 2017) for the proposition that a single incident between coworkers involving ethnic slurs and threats of physical violence does not state a claim of hostile workplace harassment. We note, however, that in Sang the issue was not whether or not the complainant stated a valid claim, but whether, having stated a claim, Complainant was able to establish that the actions amounted to a hostile work environment under the law. The Agency next cites Pearman v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 07A40063 (November 18, 2004) for the proposition that an Agency cannot be held liable if the alleged harasser was not complainant's supervisor and, once alerted, the Agency acted to end the harassment. As with, Sang, however, we note that such an argument addresses the merits of Complainant's complaint without a proper investigation as required by the regulations. Whether or not the Agency can establish an affirmative defense is irrelevant to the procedural issue of whether Complainant has stated a justiciable claim under Title VII. See Osborne v. Department of the Treasury, EEOC Request No. 05960111 (July 19, 1996); Lee v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05930220 (August 12, 1993); Ferrazzoli v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05910642 (August 15, 1991).

Here, because of the context of the alleged use of the term "boy" suggests a racial slur, we find that Complainant has stated a valid claim of discrimination that requires investigation and further processing. Therefore, we will reverse the Agency's dismissal of the complaint and remand for further processing.

Context, however, is always an important consideration. In Michel H. v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, 2019005675 (2020), the Commission found that a single instance of the "N" word being used in a diversity and inclusion video did not rise to the level of actionable harassment:

In this case, Complainant does not allege that the "N word" was used by any Agency employee, or with respect to any Agency employee, but rather, that the word was used within a training video during a Diversity and Inclusion training. As we have found previously, "in the context of conducting EEO training it is sometimes necessary to mention the 'N' word; because unfortunately, the offensive word is still used." see Tricia B., v. Dep't of Veteran Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 0120150474 (Nov. 17, 2017). In Tricia B., we found no discriminatory harassment "even if the full word was uttered" because "the undisputed record shows that it was stated during a training session," and the use of the term was not "objectively offensive given the unique context in which it was uttered." Id.

Complainant has not alleged that he was personally subjected to any unwelcome verbal conduct based on race or color, as his entire complaint is based on a second-hand account of a single incident (during a training session) for which he was not present. Here, the single isolated alleged incident is not sufficiently severe or pervasive to set forth an actionable claim of harassment under Title VII. Accordingly, we find that the Agency properly dismissed the complaint for failure to state a claim.
