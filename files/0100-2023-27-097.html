M. OVERTIME COMPENSATION

An allegation that appellant was denied overtime equal to his coworkers on the basis of race, color and/or sex has stated a claim within the EEO purview. See Hall v. Department of Army, 01832057 (1984). An employee does not have a right to work overtime, however, and the mere allegation that overtime has been denied does not amount to a prima facie case of discrimination in the absence of some indication that other employees outside of the protected group were granted more overtime than appellant. See Rinker v. USPS, 01842349 (1986).

In a case that is confused because of a short analysis section, the Commission apparently draws a distinction between overtime lost as the result of a suspension or termination and denial of overtime in the absence of an adverse personnel action. In Brown v. USPS, 01850857 (1987), the Commission found that an offer of two hours of "makeup overtime" was full relief where the employee alleged that he was only given two hours of overtime while a similarly situated white employee received four hours. The Commission rejected the complainant's argument that he was entitled to two hours of pay, noting that time lost due to suspension or termination can never be recaptured and, thus, monetary compensation is appropriate. The Commission held that the complainant lost only the "opportunity" for overtime.

The fact that assignment of overtime is covered by a collective bargaining agreement does not remove an allegation of discriminatory allocation of overtime from the purview of Title VII. See Garcia v. USPS, 01870669 (1987). But absent an allegation that the bargaining provision is applied in a discriminatory fashion, the Commission has no jurisdiction to review the application of the provision generally. See Sanchez v. USPS, 01870982 (1987).

Whether an employee is aggrieved by an agency's overtime decision is a function of whether that decision concerns the determination to assign overtime and when the overtime will be worked or who will be assigned the overtime. Only the latter determination is one that can aggrieve an employee. In Hooper v. Postmaster General, 05880694 (1988), the Commission explained:

As a preliminary matter, we note that the agency, in its comments on appeal and in its request to reopen, quoted selectively from the arbitrator's decision above. The case involved two agency employees who had filed a grievance because a Supervisor had scheduled overtime work at 6:00 A.M. and assigned it to another employee. The two grievants, who started work at 3:00 A.M., complained that the overtime should have been scheduled at 2:00 A.M., so that they could have been assigned to it. The decision points out that there was 25-30 feet of mail to be sorted when the grievants arrived at work. Another truck arrived at 3:15 A.M., and another truck came in at 5:30 A.M., while the grievants were still working the mail which had arrived earlier. The third employee was asked to come in before his regular stating time of 7:15 A.M., to help the two grievants with the mail, as Monday incoming mail was normally heavy. The real thrust of the arbitrator's decision was that the grievants had no standing to grieve when overtime was scheduled, not that they had no right to grieve the decision of who would be scheduled to work the overtime when the time had been determined. The section of the arbitrator's decision quoted by the agency in the FAD, with the sections deleted by the agency replaced with emphasis, reads as follows:

I find no merit whatsoever in this grievance, which is simply an attempt to challenge the exercise by management of exclusive rights which are guaranteed by Article 3 and seventeen years of tradition since the enactment of the Postal Reorganization Act. The decision of whether or not to schedule overtime, how much overtime should be scheduled, and when it should be worked, are within the exclusive discretion of management. These decisions are not grievable and not arbitrable. Such decisions may not be challenged on the grounds that they are arbitrary, capricious, discriminatory, wasteful or inefficient; except by the manager's own superiors. It is only after these decisions have been made that Article 8.5 comes into effect to determine which specific employees shall be assigned to overtime. Article 8.5 (and any local implementation thereof under Items 13 and 14 of Article 30) sets up a system of priorities designed to give first choice for available overtime, when practicable, to employees who have indicated a desire to earn overtime pay. But, nothing in the National agreement guarantees that any employee will ever be assigned to work overtime....

From the complete text of the arbitrator's decision, it is obvious that he found exactly what the agency states he did not find, that once the decision to schedule a block of overtime has been made, the agency's employees do have a negotiated expectation, recognized by the agency and the union, to a system of priorities to offer first choice for available overtime.

The Commission, adopting the arbitrator's rationale, found that once a block of overtime has been scheduled, "the equitable distribution of overtime is a term, condition and/or privilege of employment within the agency...." Id.

In a series of cases involving one postal installation, the Commission did reject an entire group of complaints that overtime was being assigned in a discriminatory fashion. The finding was based on the Commission's determination that the complaints were all fostered by the union involved and were merely an attempt to have collective bargaining issues addressed by overburdening the EEO complaint process. See Parks v. Postmaster General, 0580463 (1988).
