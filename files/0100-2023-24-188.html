2. Modified Duty Positions

It is not discriminatory for an agency activity to give preference to local employees injured on the job over similar employees from other agency activities in granting modified duty assignments. Edwards v. Postmaster General, 01902860 (1990). The practice is justified by the financial burden that would be placed upon the agency if it were required to give up a modified duty slot to an outside employee. In Edwards, the agency demonstrated that it had only a limited number of modified positions available and that if it was required to give one to an outside employee, that would deprive one of its employees of the position. The agency showed that it would still be required to pay workers' compensation benefits to the employee who was deprived of the position. The Commission found the agency would be required to pay two employees for the same amount of work. This sustained the agency's burden of demonstrating an undue hardship.

The fact that the agency has satisfied its obligations under FECA through a modified job offer does not mean it has satisfied its reasonable accommodation obligations under the Rehabilitation Act if the employee is also a qualified individual with a disability. For example, in McGee v. Postmaster General, 0720060059 (2008), the Commission found that the agency's modified job offer under FECA did not constitute reasonable accommodation and also rejected the agency's argument that the modified job offer constituted a good faith effort at accommodation that should have precluded an award of damages.

The complainant in McGee was a letter carrier who has sustained several on-the-job injuries to her shoulder and elbows. Following surgery in 2001, the complainant requested that she be assigned to case and deliver only business mail. She was restricted from lifting over 15 pounds, could not lift above her shoulders, could do no more than three hours of fine manipulation and had to use a push cart. She also sought to continue using her personal car for mail delivery since it had been modified to meet her restrictions. The agency denied the requests, in part, asserting that the complainant could not use her personal car because of liability issues. It then reassigned her to a position as a video coding technician in another facility working a 2:30 to 11:00 A.M. shift.

The Commission, in McGee, found:

After a careful review of the record, we find there is substantial evidence in the record to support the AJ's finding that the agency failed to accommodate complainant. Complainant's physician informed the agency that if complainant could use her own car, she could perform the functions in the position because her car had been outfitted with equipment that limited her need to push, pull and lift. Complainant's supervisor testified that others were allowed to use their own cars under "emergency" situations, such as when postal equipment was not available. However, he failed to elaborate as to why complainant's situation did not permit the use of her own car. There was some testimony that complainant's supervisor thought it would increase the agency's liability if complainant used her own car. (HT III at p. 512). However, no evidence to that effect was introduced. Therefore, we find the agency failed to establish that it would be an undue hardship to allow complainant to use her own car. Complainant's physician also suggested that complainant deliver only business mail, which required less fine manipulation. The agency never argued that to do so would be an undue hardship. Rather, the agency's Injury Compensation Specialist testified that it was not appropriate for doctors to be "dictating" what job duties its employees should be performing. (HT at p. 625).

So, instead of allowing complainant to use her car, as she had previously done, and to limit her delivery to business mail only, the agency reassigned complainant to the graveyard shift of a VCT position, in another facility. The Commission notes that reassignment is the reasonable accommodation of last resort and is required only after it has been determined that (1) there are no effective accommodations that will enable the employee to perform the essential functions of her current position, or (2) all other reasonable accommodations would impose an undue hardship. See Enforcement Guidance at 3, 8. Therefore, before considering reassignment as a reasonable accommodation, employers should first consider those accommodations that would enable an employee to remain in his/her current position.

As to the agency's arguments on appeal, we are not persuaded that this case is a "mixed motive" case due to the competing interests involved in the agency's compliance with both the Commission's Regulations and the Federal Employees Compensation Act (FECA). As the agency points out in its brief, a "mixed motive" case is one in which the agency acted on both lawful and lawful reasons. The agency argues that the agency's obligation to comply with FECA was the lawful motive in this case. We disagree with the agency's analyses. The agency's obligations under the Rehabilitation Act are not lessened just because complainant's claim was approved as an on-the-job injury. The question of whether an agency has satisfied its obligations under the Rehabilitation Act concerning reasonably accommodating a particular employee, is a separate inquiry from any determinations that OWCP might make about that employee. See Cleary v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 0120050911 (December 21, 2006). Accordingly, we are not persuaded by the agency's argument that its compliance with its FECA requirements limits its liability under the Rehabilitation Act.

The agency argues that the AJ erred in finding that it failed to operate in good faith. After a review of the record, we find there is substantial evidence to support the AJ's finding that complainant was entitled to compensatory damages. Under Section 102 of the Civil Rights Act of 1991 (CRA), compensatory damages may be awarded for pecuniary losses, emotional pain, suffering, inconvenience, mental anguish, and loss of enjoyment of life. Finally, we note that under certain circumstances, an agency may escape liability for compensatory damages if it acted in "good faith" in its effort to reasonably accommodate an employee. See Enforcement Guidance at 13, footnote 24; Reiley v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 07A10019 (June 5, 2002).

In the instant case, we find that the agency failed to establish that it acted in "good faith" and, accordingly, we find that complainant is entitled to proven compensatory damages. A review of the hearing transcript reveals that neither complainant's supervisor nor the Injury Compensation Specialist were at all concerned with its obligations pursuant to the Rehabilitation Act. Instead of continuing with its interactive process, and accommodating complainant in her own position, the agency placed her in another facility, on a graveyard shift. Accordingly, the agency is not relieved of its obligation to pay appropriate compensatory damages. Glaude v. Department of Defense, EEOC Appeal No. 01A33351 (March 16, 2006) (no good faith found when agency failed to look for accommodation in complainant's position, and did not allow complainant to use a stool); see also Teshima v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01961997 (May 5, 1998).

The Commission upheld a damage award of $50,000.
