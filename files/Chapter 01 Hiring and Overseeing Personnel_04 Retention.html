RETENTION

As a manager, you not only have to keep your best people motivated to perform at a high level, you need to convince them that your unit is a good place to work.

You must be actively involved in the retention of your good employees or you're likely to lose them, especially if they are in sought-after occupations. Not only are you competing with the private sector but with other agencies. Even within your agency, your top performers may seek reassignment to a manager who makes the effort to be a "great boss."

Regardless of performance levels or experience, the costs associated with employee turnover are enormous when you consider the loss of productivity in your unit, your recruiting and hiring time, the training time for a new hire, and the impact on employee morale. Keeping a current employee is a lot easier than hiring a replacement.

Challenging assignments

Offering exciting and challenging work is your most important strategy to retain quality employees. When the Office of Personnel Management asks HR directors to rate the relative importance of 10 factors in their recruitment and retention efforts, challenging work and opportunities for advancement are consistently rated highest overall.

The nature of the work is largely under the control of the individual agency. This is not to say that all agencies have exciting and challenging jobs. But all agencies are capable of providing personal challenges for their most valued employees. As a manager, you are charged with delivering those challenges to the employees who work for you.

Create and assign new kinds of tasks. Job enrichment is the key to creating new and exciting challenges. It's not a difficult concept, but it seems to get lost in the shuffle of everyday work life. Encourage your employees to suggest ways to enrich their own jobs. This may be the most effective and easiest way to keep your employees challenged.

Here are ways to provide enrichment opportunities:

  • • Rotate and delegate assignments. New responsibilities can help an employee feel challenged and valued. Employees can acquire important new skills that add depth to the workforce. And the next time a key employee leaves or is gone for a while, you'll have an easier time covering for that employee.
  • • Combine tasks. The auto industry discovered long ago that an employee doing a single, simple, repetitive task will not be as challenged and motivated as an employee involved in a related set of tasks.
  • • Regularly solicit feedback. Do more than annual reviews. Find ways to develop peer review and client review opportunities. Use stay interviews with current employees to keep open the lines of communication and to find out what you can do to keep them happy and challenged.
  • • Establish widespread participation. Employees who can see the big picture tend to find their contribution meaningful. Empower and motivate your employees to take part in decisions that have an impact on their work, such as ways to organize work and schedules. Within limits, you can even ask for employee input on budgeting decisions and workforce structure.
  • • Nurture creativity. Untapped creativity dwindles when employees simply go through the motions, feeling unmotivated and disengaged. Give your employees the freedom and resources to be creative.
  • • Form teams. With the right tasks, self-directed work groups can make a lot of their own decisions. They can redistribute work so team members learn more, have more variety, and follow more projects through to completion.

Remember that in most cases employees do not work for the agency, they work for their boss. Not every unit can support self-directed teams, but there is always something you can do to make employees want to stay in their jobs.

Talk with your employees regularly about their abilities, ideas, and choices. Engage them in brainstorming meetings. Above all, reward individuals and provide recognition for a job well done.

Help employees develop their careers

In addition to exciting and challenging jobs, employees cite "opportunities to grow" as top reasons why they stay in their current jobs. Managers can provide opportunities for learning, challenges, and growth on the job that match employee abilities. A good supervisor will work with employees to encourage them to improve their skills and to keep up with the latest developments in their field. Coaching, mentoring, and informal learning opportunities should be provided regularly to help employees achieve and learn more.

Training. Employee development is critical to the long-term mission of your agency. Government studies have shown that agency training decisions often are based on self-nomination by employees rather than on a thoughtful joint effort between the agency and the employee to promote the efficiency of the service.

As a manager, you should be in control of the training process, even as you encourage input from your employees. When employees analyze their development goals in terms of agency needs and the strategic intent of the organization, everyone wins. You can help them to do that.

Mentoring. Mentoring is a relationship between senior and junior employees for the purpose of supporting learning and development. A mentor holds a higher position and is usually outside the employee's chain of supervision. Informal mentor relationships often develop in the workplace or in a professional group.

When agencies establish formal mentoring programs, they assign mentors to protégés and provide formal orientation about their roles. Agencies typically target groups of employees who would most benefit from the mentoring process and identify mentors who are able to provide guidance effectively.

Mentoring techniques include coaching, role modeling, and career development advice to foster the protégé's individual growth and career development.

Recognition. Publicly recognizing your employees' contributions can help them connect their efforts to your unit's goals and your agency mission. This strengthens their connection to their jobs.

Even with limited budgets, there are a number of low- or no-cost ways to highlight superior work, including:

  • • Employee recognition programs.
  • • Recognition in internal newsletters and other communications.
  • • Town hall meetings, brown-bag luncheons, and other events where top leaders can thank employees for their contributions and stress how their work contributes to the mission.

Incentives

Programs and work arrangements that allow employees to balance work and life outside of work will help you retain valuable employees. Employees have personal lives, families, and everyday needs that sometimes take priority over their jobs. Agencies and supervisors that have tried to incorporate a more flexible working environment have been able to retain employees more successfully.

Providing financial incentives also may encourage employees to stay in their jobs. Performance awards and assistance with tuition and student loan repayment show that agencies and managers appreciate their employees' efforts and give them additional reasons to continue their employment.

Consult HR about the availability of incentives for employees you want to make sure stay around. Here's an overview of several options.

Flexible work arrangements. Flexible work arrangements include various types of flexible schedules. Consider these principles:

  • • Focus on output, not face time. Control the product, not the process.
  • • Stay in touch. Include frequent and consistent phone or email communication between manager and employee with either initiating contact as needed.
  • • Deal with individuals. Consider the needs of the specific job and the individual employee. Everyone does not perform at the same level or have the same work requirements. Opportunities for flexibility must be equitable, not equal.
  • • Begin with a pilot program. This provides a good way to test the situation and determine what options work best.
  • • Jump in. Think about using a flexible work arrangement yourself. This will improve your morale and make you understand and appreciate these arrangements.
  • • Be consistent. Use a consistent approach for requesting and approving flexible work arrangements.

As a manager, you should focus on making flexible work arrangements effective. Work needs to get done, and the quality of work should not be compromised by constant catering to the needs of the employee. However, the more flexible a manager can be, the more likely it is that employees will respond positively and their quality of work will increase.

Telework and remote work. Telework and remote work programs enhance an agency's ability to both recruit and retain employees. The savings in commuting costs, the increased choice in picking a place to live, and the work/life benefits of saved commuting time are powerful incentives, especially in major cities like Washington, where most federal offices are located.

Incentive pay. A current employee may be paid a retention incentive if the agency determines the employee's unusually high or unique qualifications or its special need for the employee's services makes it essential to retain the employee, and the employee would be likely to leave federal service in the absence of a retention incentive. The employee's rating of record must be at least "Fully Successful" or equivalent to qualify for retention pay. The limit on the incentive rate is 25 percent of the employee's rate of basic pay, unless OPM approves a higher rate of up to 50 percent based on a critical agency need.

Awards. Performance awards are an important way for agencies to recognize the performance of their employees and mission accomplishments. They help people feel valued and can be a great retention incentive.

Student loan repayment. The federal student loan repayment program allows government agencies to repay federally insured student loans as a recruitment or retention incentive for candidates or current employees. Each agency sets up its own plan.

An agency may make payments to the loan holder of up to a maximum of $10,000 for an employee in a calendar year, with a total of not more than $60,000 for any one employee.

To receive this benefit, the employee must sign a service agreement to remain in the service of the paying agency for a period of at least three years. If the employee is separated voluntarily or separated involuntarily for misconduct, unacceptable performance, or a negative suitability determination, they must reimburse the paying agency for all benefits received. The employee also must maintain an acceptable level of performance to continue to receive repayment benefits.

Tuition assistance. Agencies may offer employees assistance to attend academic courses that are job-related. An agency would pay for individual courses or classes, and employees are not required to undergo a competitive process, as is required for an agency academic degree program.

Workplace culture. To retain your best workers, you need to create a workplace culture where they will be comfortable. You may supervise employees spanning multiple generations.

Managed correctly, the generations can come together and harness the creativity of their different perspectives. Those differences, however, can also be a source of tremendous conflict.

Getting to know your employees and appreciating their perspectives is a good approach to bridging the generation gap. Every generation needs its own kind of empowerment, and a multi-generational approach can lower the barriers between employees and harness everyone's strengths.

In particular, you can:

  • • Look for ways to cut bureaucracy and red tape.
  • • Accept overt ambition and learn to harness it.
  • • Keep up with technology and cross-train. For example, if only one employee knows how to set up a WebEx session, what happens if that employee leaves or goes on vacation?
  • • Remember that most employees want to do a good job and that there are many ways to accomplish the same task.
  • • Focus on goals, not process.
  • • Set biases aside when conflicts between employees arise. Even though you may share the same values as one of the employees, remain objective.
  • • Include everyone and encourage each generation to mentor the other. This can go from older to younger, but also the other way around, especially when new technology is involved.
  • • Break the bonds of tradition but with care. Older workers may want to proceed with undue caution, but younger workers may want to forge full steam ahead without appreciating all the risks involved.
  • • Invest in talent. Help employees develop needed competencies to succeed in the agency and in their careers.
  • • Show employees the future. Explain where the agency is headed and how their career goals fit with the agency's plans.
  • • Build an organization that values and rewards performance and collegiality. Although their attitudes may differ in detail, employees of all generations value recognition and the sense that they are contributing to a team.

Stay interviews

Stay interviews are one-on-one discussions between a manager or HR professional and an employee to identify the reasons the employee stays with the organization and what might cause them to leave. This type of interview can be a successful retention tool. Instead of waiting to talk to employees when they are walking out the door, managers try to find out from current workers what's important to them and how to help develop their careers.

Stay interviews are particularly helpful because they help determine whether employees' talents are being fully utilized and if there are things that may be inhibiting their success. Managers can also ask about what energizes employees, what would help retain them, and what would lure them away. The key is to be authentic, by listening to each employee and being honest about what can and can't be done.

For example, you can ask employees what makes them want to stay at the agency and what makes them want to just keep pushing the snooze button in the morning. You might learn that their work and colleagues are reasons they stay, but commuting issues are a negative. You might be able to address the commuting problems with telework, remote work, or an alternative work schedule.

Taking the time to solicit employees' perspectives about their jobs and organization goes a long way towards conveying to them that you and your agency value their engagement. Employees appreciate knowing their opinions matter.

Who should be interviewed?

You can interview any employees, but you definitely want to make sure you interview:

  • • High performing employees.
  • • Employees who have mission-critical skill sets.
  • • Employees who fill unique roles in your work unit.

Because these individuals provide the foundation for mission success, you need to know the factors that keep them committed, engaged, and motivated in their jobs. This will help you adopt supportive practices. At the same time, awareness of factors that could be improved provides you an opportunity to make changes before critical staff members are tempted to take jobs elsewhere.

Caution: Be cautious about interviewing only top performers. Employees who are not invited to be interviewed may wonder if they are not as valued. You could ask top performer employees interviewed for strict confidentiality -- and hope they comply -- but otherwise you'll have to accept the risks to workplace morale. You also might find that higher performers tend to say more positive things about the agency, which highlights the need to gather information across performance levels.

Conducting the interview

Stay interviews need to be conducted on a regular basis to be effective. They should focus on employee goals and career development plans and promote two-way discussion. You also can use stay interviews to recognize your employees' performance and show appreciation.

Structure. While there's no specific format for a stay interview, it's generally most effective to formulate several main questions, each with some follow-up questions. This should lead to more comprehensive and detailed answers while still allowing the interview to follow a standardized format.

A good length of time for each interview is about 30-40 minutes, with about 12-15 questions. Your questions should be related to engagement, racial equity, leadership, organizational/work culture, promotional pathways, and recruitment. Specific content areas could include the employees' appraisal of their:

  • • Work, role, and responsibilities.
  • • Working conditions and flexibilities.
  • • Feedback, review, and performance management practices.
  • • Appreciation and recognition practices.
  • • Growth and development opportunities.
  • • Work unit or organizational culture.
  • • Communication, interpersonal, and teamwork dynamics.
  • • Leadership.

Employees may also be asked to discuss their career aspirations and ways in which their current job or organization is or is not conducive to achieving them.

Trust is essential. Emphasize to your employees that their participation in a stay interview is voluntary. You also need to make clear that their views will be kept strictly confidential and will not influence their performance appraisals in any way. Employees must be able to trust you for stay interviews to have value.

Using stay interview results

It's critical that the information gleaned from stay interviews leads to visible action and isn't just filed away. This means taking steps to support the job-related, environmental, and organizational factors that keep employees committed, motivated, and engaged. This also working to remedy the factors that detract from employee morale. The overall goal is using the stay interview to identify strategies for making good employees ... stay.

Get creative and think about how stay interview results can be used to improve both recruitment and retention strategies at your agency. The data can help with development of:

  • • Recruitment and training as a means to market and brand your unit or agency.
  • • Workplace improvement/efficiency initiatives.
  • • Building bench strength (the ability to immediately fill critical positions with a talented internal candidate if an employee leaves).
  • • Succession planning.
  • • Leadership development efforts.
  • • Designing realistic job previews and vacancy announcements.

Concerns about stay interviews

Problems you can't solve. Don't shy away from stay interviews because you're afraid of a conversation where issues can't be solved, such as salary demands. You can find areas where progress can be made. For example, you can help employees better understand future opportunities and career paths, and discuss mentoring opportunities and work-life balance options. You also can cover the potential for details within and outside the agency, as well as other cross-training opportunities.

Self-censoring. If you're concerned employees who participate in stay interviews may censor their answers when being interviewed by you or other management officials, you could use an HR specialist, trained non-supervisory employees, or contractors to conduct the interviews.

Planting the idea of exiting. It's possible a stay interview might plant the idea of leaving the agency in the minds of employees. However, sometimes you must take this risk. People will leave whether you talk about it or not. It would be helpful to discuss the challenging aspects of the workplace in the middle of the interview so that employees don't leave the interview with these negatives in their mind.

Time commitment. Yes, it will take a significant amount of time to interview employees. Devoting almost an hour to each interview could be difficult. But retaining employees and keeping them happy in their positions outweighs the cost of the time you need to set aside.

Mistakes to avoid

Your best employees won't stay around unless you provide them with the proper motivation. Keep in mind these retention points.

Do not:

  • • Ignore employees. Very few good employees want to work in a leadership vacuum.
  • • Lie. Be sure that whatever information you convey to your employees is honest and truthful even if you cannot disclose certain information. Never lie to your employees. They always figure it out, and their resulting mistrust will destroy your ability to lead.
  • • Fear empowering your employees. You can afford to share some control. Employees want to feel their role is important and they are a part of the much bigger picture. Don't be afraid to delegate responsibility. You are still in charge.
  • • Play favorites. All employees with similar qualifications who are performing well should get the same shot at the good stuff. Favoritism hurts morale and may lead to EEO complaints and grievances. Consult with HR.

Ask FMG

Q. What do good employees want from an agency?

A. While there is no single most important thing, it's essential that you effectively communicate and recognize that good employees want to feel they play an important part in the agency's mission.

Q. How can I provide exciting and challenging work?

A. The most important thing you can do, especially if you recognize that your unit doesn't offer the most desirable jobs in the world, is to provide challenges for your employees through rotating work assignments and teamwork initiatives. You must break the monotony of everyday work to challenge your employees.

Q. How do I connect with workers from different generations and backgrounds?

A. Get to know your employees and try "walking in their shoes." Learn, accept, and appreciate each employee's perspective. You also should discuss expectations with new employees right away. To promote their connection with each other, it's also a good idea to use employees as mentors. This generally works from older to younger, but in the case of new technology, it may go the other way around.

Q. How should I handle a good employee with pressing family needs?

A. Be as flexible as possible. Work needs to get done, and the quality of work should not be compromised by constant catering to the needs of the employee. However, the more flexible a manager can be, the more likely it is that employees will respond positively and their quality of work will increase. Work/life programs are essential to retaining quality employees.

Q. Why do some agencies and units suffer more turnover than others?

A. There may be many reasons for individual agencies, but one of the most common is workplace boredom. Job enrichment is the key to creating new and exciting challenges. If you have a turnover problem, try to create new tasks, delegate work, and provide new incentives. It's also possible that your agency is not paying competitively. Find out about any pay flexibilities available.

Q. At what point in the employment process should I start thinking about retention?

A. From the beginning, when you check the job announcement to make sure it is not misleading. When you welcome new employees, make sure everything is ready at their new workstation. You and your agency also can make a good initial impression that will lead to early loyalty by offering high-quality orientation, initial training, and mentoring programs.

Q. Does career development aid retention?

A. Yes and no. Top employees want the training and work challenges that keep them on the cutting edge and get them ready for promotion. If you provide this, they'll stick around -- until they get promoted out of your unit. But this is not a bad thing. You get the most out of them while they are with you, you establish a network of appreciative former employees, and you attract top applicants who have heard your unit is a place where they can get ahead.

Q. Should I take high turnover personally?

A. Perhaps you should. People often don't quit their jobs; they quit their bosses. If your unit has unusually high turnover and there is no obvious reason, the cause may lie in your management style. Make sure you are open, supportive, and above all honest.
