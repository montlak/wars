7. Harassment

When the discrimination relates to harassment, an order of full relief must contain adequate provisions to ensure the harassment does not continue. It is not sufficient to counsel the alleged offender and make vague assurances that no further harassment will occur. "To remedy such an allegation, the agency should do something more than assure appellant that it has already once counseled the alleged harasser months ago. Full relief would contemplate additional action designed to ensure that the harassment ceased, either by transferring the alleged harasser or appellant to another worksite, or the establishment of other procedures reasonably guaranteed to end the harassment." Scarfo v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 019002695 (1990). Nor is it sufficient to distribute a general memorandum to all employees on sexual harassment. "When a complainant files a specific complaint of sexual harassment in the workplace, requests certain specific remedial actions to remedy the situation, the agency should address the concerns raised by the complainant.... At a minimum appellant is entitled to written assurance that steps will be taken to assure that the behavior of which she complaints will not recur." Gamble v. Secretary of Navy, 01902155 (1990). Similarly, in Holt v. Secretary of Navy, 01956226 (1996), the Commission held that where there is an allegation of harassment, an offer of full relief must contain assurances that similar discrimination will not recur. But, where the complainant alleges only a single discriminatory incident and makes no allegations of harassment in general, an offer of full relief need not provide for such preventative measures as separating the supervisor and the complainant. See Denson v. Postmaster General, 05921048 (1993).

An offer of full relief that provides sexual harassment training for employees may be appropriate in some cases. However, it does not constitute an offer of full relief if the agency targets the wrong group for training. In Weiler v. Postmaster General, 01923090 (1992), the Commission held the training must be specifically tailored to the discrimination:

The proffered sexual harassment training is an inadequate remedy for the alleged incidents of sexual harassment because it is geared to the wrong group of employees. Since the allegation cited male coworkers, an appropriate remedy would provide training for male coworkers. The proffered training for managers and supervisors therefore is not specifically tailored to cure the particular source of the alleged discrimination, although we note that if the same managers and supervisors were aware of the alleged harassment, yet took no action to correct the situation, they also would be required to engage in the training. (footnote omitted.)

The Commission has applied similar guidance with respect to relief from other forms of harassment, such as racial harassment. In Davis v. Secretary of Defense, 01910481 (1991), the complainant alleged racial harassment. The agency in Davis dismissed the complaint after the employee rejected an offer that provided the agency "has taken appropriate action" and "will take action as appropriate" to insure a work environment free of harassment:

The offer of settlement made by the agency was vague and nonspecific as to the nature of relief offered. The agency's offer falls short of placing appellant in the position he would have occupied but for the alleged discrimination. This Commission finds that an offer of make whole relief would include a proviso specifying that appellant shall not be subjected to racial discrimination including racially motivated harassment. Further, appellant should be assured that he will be treated fairly and equitably, in the same manner as his fellow employees, regardless of race. Make whole relief would also include a provision for giving the alleged discriminatory official appropriate EEO and related civilian personnel training including refresher courses on previous training. Finally, this Commission finds that make whole relief would include the assurance that appellant will not be subject to the control or supervision of the alleged discriminating official.

See also O'Dell v. Postmaster General, 01912578 (1991) (applying the same rule with respect to harassment based on national origin).

The agency does not have to alter its management structure in order to ensure that the harassment will not recur. In Gordon v. Postmaster General, 01913824 (1992), the complainant received a promise of transfer in a certified offer of full relief. The complainant rejected the offer because the agency declined to guarantee that the alleged discriminating officials would not be transferred or reassigned to his new facility. The Commission held that the agency was not required to "revise its personnel policies regarding managerial officials' rights to reassignments, transfers, and promotional opportunities within another division as full relief...." Id.

The requirement of assuring that no future harassment will occur is not satisfied by vague, nonspecific promises to that effect. See Gant v. Postmaster General, 01922908 (1992). The assurance must be specifically tailored to the harassment to which the complainant alleges he or she has been subjected.
