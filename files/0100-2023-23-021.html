2. Major Bodily Functions

Another significant change brought about by the ADAAA was the inclusion of several bodily functions as major life activities. In part because of the consideration of mitigating measures, many lower court decisions had excluded several of the bodily functions as major life activities. In reality, many of these decisions confused the distinction between an impairment that affects a major life activity and one that substantially limits the activity. As noted directly above, Congress has now included these bodily functions as major life activities.

Just as the Supreme Court's interpretation of substantial limitation greatly restricted the number of individuals to whom the Act applied so, too, did the lower court decisions excluding bodily functions as major life activities. The Commission's regulations defining major bodily functions, at 29 CFR 1630.2(i)(2), define major life activities as including:

The operation of a major bodily function, including functions of the immune system, special sense organs and skin; normal cell growth; and digestive, genitourinary, bowel, bladder, neurological, brain, respiratory, circulatory, cardiovascular, endocrine, hemic, lymphatic, musculoskeletal, and reproductive functions. The operation of a major bodily function includes the operation of an individual organ within a body system.

Note that it is the deviation in the bodily function itself that is the impairment of a major life activity.

It is also worth noting that the inclusion of such individuals in the coverage of the ADA does not necessarily require reasonable accommodation of such individuals. Take, for example, the individual whose ability to produce insulin is in fact substantially limited. Although the individual now has a disability under the Statute, if the condition is controlled through insulin and diet, it may well be that the individual has no limitations in the workplace that require accommodation.

By including major bodily functions among the nonexhaustive list of major like activities, the ADAAA significantly expands the conditions that will be substantially limiting even when other major life activities are not significantly impacted, as evidenced in the examples immediately above involving diabetes and cancer.

Eating is an example of a major life activity that may not be substantially limited in a particular individual, but the underlying impairment of the bodily function that impacts eating may be substantially limiting. The problem with eating as a major life activity has been in determining what constitutes a substantial limitation of that activity. Obviously, mere dietary quirks, habits or preferences do not constitute substantial limitations and, more fundamentally, are often not the result of any underlying impairment.

One area where whether a limitation on eating was substantial arose with some frequency was when a complainant suffered from diabetes. In Jambora v. Postmaster General, 07A40128 (2006), the Commission found that the complainant had established a substantial limitation:

The Commission has found that some individuals with diabetes mellitus are individuals with disabilities within the meaning of the Rehabilitation Act, while others are not. In cases where the Commission has found a substantially limiting impairment, the diabetes itself has caused debilitating complications; medication has not successfully controlled the condition; or the regimen involved with monitoring and controlling the condition itself imposes a substantial limitation. See Ortiz v. Social Security Administration, EEOC Appeal No. 01990911 (January 19, 2001), request for reconsideration denied, EEOC Request No. 05A10357 (May 3, 2002). In cases where the Commission has not found coverage under the Rehabilitation Act, individuals have failed to show that the diabetes substantially limits them in a major life activity. See Medina v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01990709 (February 15, 2000).

Here, based on the medical assessments of complainant's diabetic condition, as well as her own testimony, and that of her spouse, we find that complainant is substantially limited in the major life activity of eating. Specifically, in hearing testimony, complainant attests that she eats three times a day, and must also take a morning snack, afternoon snack, and bedtime snack. Complainant states that if she does not strictly adhere to this eating schedule, she experiences confusion, headache, problems concentrating, extreme irritability, extreme sweating, slurred speech, and anxiety. She testifies that in the early stages of a hypoglycemic episode, she may act silly, and then go blank and stare, and that she will eventually lose consciousness if someone does not bring her a carbohydrate rich food or beverage. Complainant also testifies that she often has no memory of what occurred during these episodes, even when she does not lose consciousness. Complainant testifies that as a result of her frequent symptoms of hypoglycemia in the workplace, her supervisor referred to her as "ditzy." Complainant indicates that she uses an insulin pump, but must also routinely give herself insulin injections as well to control episodes of hyperglycemia.

Complainant's spouse testifies that complainant must be vigilant as to her sugar intake and her carbohydrates, noting that it is a complicated formula complainant uses as to what she eats and how much insulin she has to give herself. He attests that when her blood sugar goes below 120, to as low as 30's and 40's, he would have to give her sugar because she doesn't realize what is going on, noting that she often becomes combative when he tries to assist her. He testified that complainant carries a tube of glucose in case of an emergency, and noted that he had soda for her during the hearing because the stress could trigger an episode of hypoglycemia. Complainant's spouse also testifies that when he worked the same day shift as complainant, he could check on her frequently during the work day for symptoms of diabetic complications. He testified that he often found complainant to be exhibiting early symptoms of hypoglycemia, and would need to bring her juice and render other assistance to halt its progression. Additionally, complainant's spouse testified that he assisted complainant with certain procedures on her insulin pump. Both complainant and her spouse also testify that complainant must check her blood glucose levels frequently, including during the night, usually at 2 am and 4 am, when her blood sugar typically dropped and she had to eat a snack to compensate for it.

Based on this evidence, with reference to the above cited legal principles, we conclude that complainant is substantially limited in the major life activity of eating and we modify the AJ's decision to reflect this finding.

Similarly, in Preston v. Postmaster General, 0120054230 (2007), the Commission found that the complainant was substantially limited in the major life activity of eating, contrasting the case with its holding in Can v. Postmaster General, 01A43665 (2006), and explaining:

Next, we consider whether complainant's impairment renders him substantially limited in a major life activity. Complainant states that in order to prevent major diabetic complications, he needs to perform a "daily balancing act," including controlling his diet, medication administration, sleep cycles, stress levels, work cycles, and family responsibilities. ROI, Affidavit A, at 6. He notes that an "exhorbitant amount of time is needed to manage these tasks." Id. He states that for instance, he needs to "administer blood sugar tests four or more times a day, examine test results four or more times a day, determine response reactions to test results, make adjustments to diet or medications and respond to any hyperglycemic and hypoglycemic reactions that may happen." Id. Complainant states that his medical care requires constant adjustments in multiple medications, adjustments in diet and exercise." Id., at 7. He further states "I have been instructed by my medical care team to maintain regular sleep patterns, regular diabetic eating patterns, to use stress reducing techniques, exercise regularly at as close to the same time every day..." Id.

The record also contains documents from complainant's physicians concerning the limitation on complainant's ability to eat; documents dated December 21, 1995, and June 23, 2000, indicate that complainant was going to have to learn to "carb count" with each meal. ROI, Affidavit A, at 36 and 26. In the December 21, 1995 letter, the physician notes that complainant was to be referred to a dietician. Id., at 36. Complainant additionally asserts that his condition is permanent. Id. In a medical note dated March 16, 2000, complainant's physician indicates that complainant required a "sensible, high fiber, six feeding ADA diet." ROI, Affidavit A, at 28. The physician also states that complainant needed to "walk an extra ten minutes after each meal." Id. In a June 23, 2000 letter, the physician states that complainant "will always carry some candy with him." Id., at 27. In sum, we find that the record evidence indicates that complainant must regulate his food intake far more closely, compared to the average person in the general population, because swings in his food intake would render it impossible to maintain a schedule that would conform to his metabolic needs. Accordingly, we find that there is ample documentary and testimonial evidence establishing that complainant's diabetes is an impairment that substantially limits him in the major life activity of eating.

The instant case can be distinguished from Can, in that in Can the record was devoid of evidence concerning "whether complainant must adhere to a restricted diet and/or a strict eating schedule, or the measures he must take regarding the medical consequences, if any, associated with what he eats, how much he eats, and when he eats." Can, at 3. Here, however, complainant has presented the type of evidence (as previously described) concerning his eating requirements which was lacking in Can. We therefore conclude that complainant is an individual with a disability within the meaning of the Rehabilitation Act. See Jambora v. United States Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 07A40128 (May 16, 2006); Gamelin v. United States Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01A22307 (January 5, 2004).

By extending major life activities to include major bodily functions, it is now the body's inability to produce insulin that in and of itself can be substantially limiting.
