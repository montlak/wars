5. Lump Sum Payments and Taxes

Not all payments provided for in a settlement agreement are intended as back pay. In some instances, the interests of the respective parties may be better served by simply designating the payment as a "lump sum" without specifying the components of that lump sum. When a settlement provides for a lump sum, it is error for the agency to deduct state and federal taxes and Medicare. See Barnett v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01970961 (1998). Such deductions are only appropriate for back pay awards. See also Greenwalt v. Secretary of Air Force, 01A00224 (2002) (noting the award may be taxable as gross income but not as wages subject to withholding). However, it is not a breach of agreement for an agency to pay the amount provided for in the settlement without withholding taxes where the provision is silent on the issue of taxes and the amount is not specifically designed as compensatory damages. See Puissegur v. Postmaster General, 01A42621 (2004).

Also in Barnett, the Commission held that a lump sum is not converted into a back pay award because the method of calculating the lump sum refers to general schedule salary levels. As a result, a provision of the agreement that required the agency to calculate the lump sum by multiplying the difference between two GS levels for a specified period of time did not convert the lump sum into a back pay award.

The authority of an agency to award a lump sum payment without a corresponding personnel action is addressed in MD-110 at 12-5:

Settlements of EEO disputes may contain monetary payments that are independent of any personnel action, provided that the monetary payment does not exceed the amount of back pay, attorney's fees, costs or damages the employee would have been entitled to in the case if discrimination had been actually found.

The Comptroller General has considered settlements of EEO disputes comprised of monetary payments unconnected to personnel actions on at least two occasions and held that they were authorized and appropriate.

[W]e conclude that Federal agencies have the authority in informally settling discrimination complaints filed under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, as amended, to make awards of backpay, attorney's fees or costs, without a corresponding personnel action and without a finding of discrimination, provided that the amount of the award agreed upon must be related to backpay and may not exceed the maximum amount that would be recoverable under Title VII if a finding of discrimination were made.

(Citations and footnotes omitted).

Even when settlement of a complaint involves a corresponding personnel action, lump sum payments can be made in lieu of back pay provided the payments do not exceed the total scope of monetary relief that would be available if the complainant prevailed. However, if the agreement is intended to provide enhanced retirement benefits, this must be spelled out in the agreement and a corresponding deposit must be made in the retirement fund:

Since the Civil Rights Act of 1991 provided for award of compensatory damages in appropriate cases, settlements often provide for one lump sum amount covering monetary relief even when there is a personnel action involved as well. In these cases, parties can agree to an overall figure in the settlement that represents damages, back pay and attorney's fees. That figure can reflect the maximum amount a court could award, and need not be limited to an amount that the agency believes a complainant can prove in court. The settlement agreement does not need to contain a separate breakdown of the lump sum showing individual amounts of back pay, damages and fees. The lump sum agreed to by the parties can be equal to or less than the total amount of back pay, damages and fees that would be awarded if a finding of discrimination were made. A lump sum cannot, under any circumstances, exceed the amount that the agency concludes, in light of the facts and recognizing the inherent uncertainty of litigation, a court could award if a lawsuit were brought.

If a lump sum settlement is intended to award enhanced retirement benefits as part of its terms, the rates of basic pay or grade and step deemed to be received by the complainant, and the periods during which each rate of pay was received, must be specified in the settlement terms. OPM advises that if this specific information is not set out in the settlement document, the terms of the settlement will not be included in the calculation of the complainant's retirement benefits.

Id. at 4.6; see also Braziel v. Secretary of Army, 01995275 (2001) (no breach where the agency advised OPM that retirement benefits should be calculated on the former grade level based on the settlement agreement that provided for promotion and lump sum payment).

If a settlement agreement provides for one lump sum payment to the complainant, it is a breach of the agreement for the agency to issue one check to the complainant and another check for the remainder of the lump sum to the complainant and her attorney jointly. Reedy v. Postmaster General, 01974751 (1999).

However, when a settlement agreement provides that the agency will withhold taxes from a lump sum payment and it neglects to do so, it is not a material breach of the settlement agreement. Thornsbury v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01995951 (2001). The Commission noted that the complainant ultimately received the same amount of money after she paid the taxes as she would have if the agency withheld the taxes.

An agency does not breach an agreement calling for a lump sum payment where the agency issues a check for the full amount without withholding taxes but submits a Form 1099 to the Internal Revenue Service designating the payment as "Other Income." Stringfellow v. Postmaster General, 01A13898 (2002). The Commission held that, if the complainant intended the lump sum to be reimbursement for out-of-pocket expenses, that should have been expressed in the settlement agreement.

In Ortiz v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0720120022 (2013), the agency entered into an agreement that provided it would pay to the complainant $290,000 minus any wages for services rendered from January 1 to August 31, 2012. The agency then deducted taxes for wages earned prior to that period but paid during that period. The agency contended that it was not in breach because the withholdings was required by the Internal Revenue Code. In rejecting the argument in Ortiz, the Commission explained:

It is unclear, however, what relevance the Internal Revenue Code, which addresses tax payments owed to the Federal government, has to do with the instant matter in this case. As noted above, in ascertaining the intent of the parties with regard to the terms of an agreement, the Commission has generally relied on the plain meaning rule. See Hyon. This rule states that if the writing appears to be plain and unambiguous on its face, its meaning must be determined from the four corners of the instrument without resort to extrinsic evidence of any nature. See Montgomery Elevator Co. A review of the Agreement shows that it states that the $290,000.00 will be "reduced by any wages earned by her for services rendered during the time period of January 1, 2012, to August 31, 2012." The plain meaning of such language is that the payment amount will not be reduced by wages earned for services rendered outside of this period, even if such wages happened to have been paid during the relevant period. If the parties had intended for the reduction to include wages earned outside of the relevant period, the parties could have changed the wording of the Agreement to something like "the Agency will pay to Complainant $290,000.00, reduced by any wages paid during the time period of January 1, 2012, to August 31, 2012." By using the language "wages earned by her for services rendered during the [relevant] time period" instead of "wages paid during the [relevant] time period" it is clear that, regardless of how the IRS treats wages and earnings for Federal tax purposes, based on the plain meaning of the language used in the Agreement, wages earned for services rendered outside of the relevant period should not have been used to reduce the total payment amount even if actual payment of said wages occurred during the relevant period.

As the Agency does not appear to dispute that the amounts of $11,079.00 and $7,179.16, totaling $18,258.16, were earned outside of the relevant period and were deducted from the total payment about, we find that such a total should not have been deducted from the $166,319.62 paid to Complainant. By deducting more than permitted by the Agreement, we find the Agency to be in breach of the Agreement.

When it is the Department of Treasury and not the employing agency that makes a deduction for a lump sum settlement that does not constitute a breach of the agreement. For example, in Nigel S. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120152487 (2015), the Department of Treasury deducted over $22,000 from the $170,000 specified in the lump sum agreement because of the complainant's arrears in child support and taxes. The Commission stated, "The Agency had no control over the actions of the Department of the Treasury, and it is clear that the Agency paid the $170,000 as set forth in the agreement. We have previously held that an off-set such as the one at issue by the Department of the Treasury, rather than the Agency, does not constitute a breach of a settlement agreement. See Stercz v. Department of Commerce, EEOC Appeal No. 01201036114 (November 12, 2010), request for reconsideration denied, EEOC Request No. 0520110185 (February 15, 2011)."
