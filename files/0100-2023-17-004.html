B. FINAL AGENCY DECISIONS

Only the complainant has a right of appeal from a final agency decision. This is because, under the 1999 amendments to Part 1614, the agency only issues a final decision in those cases where the complaint has not been subject to a decision by an administrative judge. Final agency decisions are issued only in those cases where the complaint is dismissed, the complainant requests a final agency decision on the record, or the complainant fails to timely request a hearing before an administrative judge.

MD-110 at 9-5 to 9-6 describes the procedure for agencies to follow when issuing final decisions:

C. Agency Final Decision

In any case where the agency issues a final decision (e.g., where the complainant elects to have the agency issue a final decision following completion of the investigation or in a class action case, where the agency issues the decision on the merits of class complaints), the agency must inform the complainant of his/her right to file an appeal with the EEOC and provide the complainant with a copy of EEOC Form 573, Notice of Appeal/Petition (Appendix P). The agency further must inform the complainant that any such appeal must be filed within 30 days of complainant's receipt of the agency's final decision.

D. Agency Procedural Decision

Where the agency issues a decision dismissing a complaint in its entirety pursuant to § 1614.107(a), the agency must inform the complainant of his/her right to file an appeal with the EEOC and provide the complainant with a copy of EEOC Form 573, Notice of Appeal/Petition (Appendix P). The agency further must inform the complainant that any such appeal must be filed within 30 days of complainant's receipt of the agency's dismissal decision.

A complete discussion of final agency decisions is contained in Chapter 7, but the basic elements of final agency decisions are reviewed here for the convenience of the reader.

Although the Commission prefers the final agency decision to be signed and dated, the absence of a signature and date is not a fatal flaw when the decision is accompanied by an introductory letter containing appeal rights that is signed and dated. Patrick v. Secretary of Air Force, 05920742 (1993).

The final agency decision must include findings of fact essential to its ultimate finding regarding discrimination. See Vasquez v. Postmaster General, 01913105 (1991). The failure to include findings of fact and conclusions of law in a final decision effectively deprives a complainant of his right to a meaningful opportunity to appeal. In Smithson v. Social Security Administration, 01954393 (1997), the Commission ordered the agency to issue a new final decision, noting:

With regard to the non-selections, the Commission notes that the present FAD fails to set forth any of the material facts of the case; fails to set forth the law applicable to the case; and falls to apply the law to those facts in an instructive way, thereby depriving appellant of a meaningful right of appeal. See Arquer v. Dept. Of Health and Human Services, EEOC Appeal No. 01943728 (August 9, 1995). Therefore, on remand, the agency is instructed to provide appellant with a FAD which sets forth the material facts of the case and explains with particularity not only the law which is applicable to his complaint, but whether and why appellant has or has not established his claim. See Beckley v. Dept. of Agriculture, EEOC Appeal No. 01940224 (May 8, 1995).

See also Coleman v. Secretary of Army, 01952785 (1997).

If a complainant is dissatisfied with the final agency decision on his original complaint, the proper procedure is to file an appeal with the EEOC Office of Federal Operations and not to institute a new EEO complaint over the final agency decision. Morrison v. USPS, 01832010 (1984).

The fact that the agency does not characterize its communication with a complainant as a final decision and that the communication contains no appeal rights is not dispositive of whether the communication actually constitutes a final agency decision. If the communication effectively terminates the processing of the allegations, then it is a final agency decision. Otherwise, the complainant would be denied any opportunity for review of an agency's action. LIBERTAD v. Secretary of Air Force, 05910768 (1992).

The time limit for filing an appeal can be extended where the agency's final decision is unclear and the complainant has no reason to believe appeal is necessary until the occurrence of a subsequent event. In Major v. Defense Mapping Agency, 01851860 (1987), the final agency decision informed the complainant that he would be awarded a retroactive promotion and attorney fees. It was not until the complainant attempted to accept the agency's offer that she was told she would receive back pay but no promotion. The complainant then sent a letter to the agency requesting clarification of the agency's final decision and filed her appeal within 20 calendars days of receiving the clarification. The Commission accepted the appeal as timely, noting the agency's failure to give an adequate explanation in its final decision prevented the complainant from promptly filing her appeal.

Once a final agency decision with a finding of discrimination has issued, the complainant is entitled to a remedy as a matter of law. Handy v. Secretary of Transportation, 01933298 (1993). Thereafter, the agency cannot rescind its initial decision and reissue a decision with a finding of no discrimination.

It is not unusual for an agency to issue a final agency decision on liability and, if a finding of discrimination is reached, to issue a second decision on relief. A final agency decision is not final for appeal purposes until the agency has also issued a decision on all aspects of relief. See Adina P. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120132450 (2015), rejecting the agency's arguments that the complainant's appeal of no discrimination findings was untimely because it was not filed within 30 days of those findings. "The Agency's December 5, 2012 decision finding discrimination did not contain a determination of the amount of back pay or compensatory damages that Complainant would be awarded, and therefore, was not final because it did not make a determination on all the issues in the complaint." Id.
