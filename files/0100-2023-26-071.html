p. Threats and Proposed Agency Actions

Generally speaking, a proposed action does not sufficiently aggrieve an employee and the agency is required to dismiss complaints based on proposed actions. 29 CFR 1614.107(a)(5). However, an allegation that the agency has proposed an adverse action in retaliation for protected activity does state a claim for relief. See, e.g., Perry v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A44510 (2006). See the subheading in Chapter 3, "Standing: Failure to State a Claim--Reprisal Complaints." Similarly, an agency's action in proposing, but not requiring, a complainant to submit to a fitness-for-duty examination based on information the complainant provided in a prior EEO complaint may constitute reprisal. See Elbert H. v. Attorney General, 0120170676 (2018).

In Sanders v. Secretary of Education, 05990744 (2000), the complainant's supervisor announced that he would terminate employees who lied on applications for vacant agency positions, and in turn filed EEO complaints when they were not selected for the positions. The complainant stated that he felt threatened by the comment because he had already filed an EEO complaint based on his nonselection. The Commission in Sanders found that the supervisor's remarks could have a chilling effect on the complainant's efforts to assert his rights:

In the instant case, an agency official, rather than encouraging the realization of equal opportunity in the workplace, purportedly created a chilling effect upon complainant's efforts to avail himself of the opportunity to pursue the EEO complaint process.... In the present case, clearly the agency official's threat could reasonably deter the employees present, including complainant, from engaging in protected EEO activity.... Consequently, we find that complainant's claim regarding the August 1997 threat by the agency official states a processable claim.

See also Emmons v. Secretary of Education, 01985056 (1999), the Commission held that a supervisor's statement, that he would fire anyone who filed an EEO complaint against him, sufficiently raised the prospects of a chilling effect to aggrieve the complainant.

Proposed actions that otherwise would not state a claim may be actionable in reprisal cases if the proposed actions are "reasonably likely to deter protected activity." Goodwin v. Secretary of Air Force, 01991301 (2000). In Goodwin, a notice of proposed reprimand was issued to the complainant while her two complaints were pending, and the commander who issued the notice was aware of the complaints. The Commission found that the proposed notice "put the complainant on the defensive, to the point where she suffered an anxiety attack after being called into the commander's office and informed of the proposal," and found that it was likely to discourage participation in EEO activity.

In Wittren v. Secretary of Army, 0120073308 (2007), the Commission found actionable retaliation where a postmaster threatened to terminate the complainant if he hired a lawyer or pursued an EEO complaint. However, the administrative judge also found that the complainant's ultimate removal was because of budgetary reductions that allowed only one temporary worker to remain employed. The agency presented evidence that the coworker was technically superior to the complainant and also that it renewed the coworker's contract in two implements of six months, instead of a more standard term of one year.

Rescinding a proposed action does not render a complaint of retaliation moot. In Zelikman v. Postmaster General, 0120092250 (2009), the Commission found that the agency erred in dismissing a complaint of retaliation over a notice of removal where the action was rescinded through the grievance process. The Commission found the notice, even if rescinded, was likely to deter EEO activity.

The Commission has found that an alleged threat to report the complainant to her state bar in retaliation for complaint activity stated a claim for relief. McCarthy v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120062666 (2007).
