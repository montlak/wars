5. Postal Service Oath

Postal Service employees are required to take an oath. Title 39 USC 1011 states:

Before entering upon their duties and before receiving any salary, all officers and employees of the Postal Service shall take and subscribe to the following oath or affirmation:

"I, __________, do solemnly swear (or affirm) that I will support and defend the Constitution of the United States against all enemies, foreign or domestic; that I will bear true faith and allegiance to the same; that I take this obligation freely, without any mental reservation or purpose of evasion; and that I will well and faithfully discharge the duties of the office on which I am about to enter."

In Anderson v. Postmaster General, 05900703 (1990), the complainant, a Jehovah's Witness, refused to take the oath on the basis that his religion prohibited him from taking any part in the "world's conflicts, wars, or political system" and also prohibited him from giving his devotion to any government. The agency told the complainant he could affirm, rather than swear, to the oath and that it would not require him to bear arms in times of conflict. The appellant refused and was terminated.

The Commission found the agency failed to accommodate the complainant's bona fide religious beliefs when it failed to alter the oath to accommodate those beliefs. The Commission first rejected the argument that the agency accommodated the complainant, noting that its proposed measures would still have required the complainant to compromise his religious beliefs. Next, the Commission in Anderson rejected the agency's arguments that it could not modify the oath:

Although the agency contends that 39 USC 1011 does not allow modification of the Oath, we agree with the Commission's previous finding that such a reading of the statute is overly restrictive and results in an unwarranted failure to accommodate appellant's religious beliefs. Neither the language of the statute nor the legislative history indicates that modification of the oath to accommodate bona fide religious beliefs is impermissible. As noted in our previous decision, such a statute should not be construed in a manner which allows the agency to violate Title VII.

The agency cites case law which indicates that one statute should not be violated in order to ensure compliance with another statute. However, as noted above, modification of the Oath does not constitute a violation of the terms of 39 USC 1011. The agency relies on Morton v. Mancari, 417 U.S. 535 (1974), to support its position, but the Commission's previous decision is consistent with the holding in that case. The Supreme Court found that when two statutes are capable of co-existence, each should be given effect. Administering a modified Oath allows the agency to comply with both 39 USC 1011 and Title VII....
