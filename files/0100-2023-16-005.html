A. COMPLAINANT'S CONSIDERATIONS

The complainant may elect to have a final decision rendered by the agency head or his designee without a hearing. 29 CFR 1614.108(f) and 1614.110(b). However, under the 1999 amendments to the Commission's regulations, there are now special considerations for the complainant prior to choosing that route.

First, the option should be chosen only when the complainant is satisfied that there is no additional evidence that can be presented at a hearing. Even when the complainant believes the investigative record is complete, there is an advantage to requesting a hearing. Under the Commission's current regulations, EEOC administrative judges have the authority to issue summary judgment decisions. See Chapter 6 under the heading "Summary Judgment." Through the summary judgment process, a complainant can receive a decision by a neutral party prior to the agency taking final action on the matter. Given the Commission's own emphasis on the neutrality of decisionmakers, it is hard to understand why a complainant, even one who does not desire an actual hearing, would elect a final decision on the record by the agency and forego a decision by the administrative judge. Note, however, that an administrative judge cannot decide a case without holding a hearing if there are material facts in dispute. Summary judgment is inappropriate when material facts, including credibility determinations, remain in dispute. As a result, if, after requesting a hearing and having a judge assigned to the case, a complainant withdraws the request for a hearing and material facts remain in dispute, the case must be remanded to the agency for a final decision. See, e.g., Coleman v. Postmaster General, 01A02986 (2000).

The completeness of the investigatory record will be of primary concern when a complainant elects a decision by the agency without a hearing. When a hearing is requested in such a case, the administrative judge is responsible for overseeing the development of the record. See 29 CFR 1614.109(a); MD-110 at 7-6 to 7-8. The question for the complainant becomes what remedy, if any, will there be for inadequate reports of investigation when an agency decision on the record is requested. The question should arise quickly under the current regulations because a complainant is not required to await the completion of the agency investigation to request a decision on the record. A complainant may request an "immediate" decision on the record at any time after the formal complaint has been pending for 180 days. 29 CFR 1614.108(f).

Early case law developed under Part 1613 suggested that if a complainant failed to request a hearing, she would not be heard to complain on appeal that the factual record upon which the final agency decision was based was inadequate. In Cosby v. Secretary of Army, 01900196 (1990), the Commission found that the failure of the complainant to request a hearing resulted in an incomplete record for review, and therefore the Commission was "constrained to find that [the complainant] failed to carry her burden of proof. . . ." Id. The Commission in Cosby explained:

The investigatory hearing is an integral part of the investigatory process with a purpose of resolving conflicts in the evidence developed during an investigation. Once appellant rejected the proposal embodied in the proposed disposition, it was incumbent upon her to determine whether the record contained sufficient evidence to meet her burden of proof. If it did not, she could have requested additional investigation or a hearing where she could have sought to discredit the agency witnesses' versions of the circumstances surrounding her employment and termination. . . .

Despite the Commission's ruling in Cosby, it did remand the complaints for supplemental investigations. It was less than clear, however, what criteria the Commission used to distinguish between cases adjudicated without a hearing where remand was appropriate and those where the Commission decided the appeal on the basis of the existing record, even where inadequate. The distinction could have been that the Commission ordered remand where there was a factual omission in the record that could be cured through further investigation, though the Commission's case law was never clear on that point. In Cosby, the alleged deficiency appeared to relate to witness credibility--an issue that is not easily resolved without a hearing.

In all likelihood, the Commission would not follow its holding in Cosby today. For example, in Fagan v. Postmaster General, 01955858 (1997), the Commission found that the agency's finding of no discrimination was erroneous where the investigation was never completed, and there was sparse information in the file:

Despite the agency's finding of no discrimination, the Commission finds that this case is not in posture for decision. Although appellant was entitled to request a hearing before an EEOC AJ, given that it had been more than 180 days since he filed his complaint, he later withdrew this request. As a result, the investigation was never completed in this matter, and there is little or no information in the file. The agency notes this in its final agency decision. Specifically, the record lacks information documenting that appellant in fact made a request for reassignment, and that it was in fact refused or ignored. Moreover, it is not clear that the agency had access to appellant's updated medical reports. This and other evidence is necessary to fully adjudicate appellant's complaint. As such, it must be remanded to the agency for further investigation at the point where the investigation ceased, as outlined below.

Given that the agency is required to engage in investigation of a complaint, Fagan clearly represents the better view. Moreover, the Commission now defines the adequacy of investigation in terms of whether the record would "allow[] a reasonable fact finder to draw conclusions as to whether discrimination occurred." 29 CFR 1614.108(b).

An agency may not dismiss a complaint on the basis of insufficient evidence when it is responsible for the evidence being omitted from the report of investigation. In Palmer v. Secretary of Defense, DLA, 01952878 (1997), the Commission found:

In reviewing the FAD and the record, the Commission finds that there is insufficient information to render a decision in this matter. Integral to appellant's allegation is his contention that not only he but other black applicants were discriminated against in this instance and that there is a pattern of discrimination in the selection of blacks to GS-11 grade level positions by the SO. The agency, in its FAD, states that the record does not support these allegations. However, it was the agency that failed to comply with the investigator's request for information regarding race and grade. Instead it instructed the investigator not to hold up the issuance of the report and to submit the report without this critical information. The agency cannot now come before the Commission and credibly argue that the record fails to support appellant's allegations when it is responsible for the insufficiency. Additionally, the third member of the interview panel refused to cooperate with the investigation, instead insisting that questions be submitted to him in writing prior to responding. Such omission and behavior on the part of the agency and its employees is proscribed by our regulations and may lead to a finding of an adverse inference against the agency as well as other appropriate action. 29 CFR § 1614.108(c)(3).

The Commission, also, finds the record incomplete as it did not include information (i.e., SF-171s, rank, rating, etc.) regarding the other applicants, both black and white, which is needed in determining, not only appellant's relative qualifications but whether the four black candidates were discriminated against. Therefore, after a careful review of the record in its entirety, it is the decision of the Commission to REVERSE the agency's final decision and appellant's complaint is REMANDED to the agency for further processing in accordance with this decision and applicable regulations.

See also Liberato v. Postmaster General, 01954258 (1997).

Previously, the Commission held that when a complainant does waive the right to a hearing and the case is remanded for further investigation, the complainant may not request a hearing at the conclusion of the second investigation. In a case decided under Part 1613, the Commission held the proper procedure was not for the agency to issue a second proposed disposition, but to proceed immediately to a final agency decision, subject to Commission appeal. See Green v. Postmaster General, 05900939 (1990). Similarly, in Baker v. Postmaster General, 05960598 (1997), the Commission found that the complainant was not entitled to a hearing on remand for consideration of compensatory damage where she requested a decision without hearing on the underlying complaint of discrimination. But see Cerda v. Postmaster General, 01910522 (1991) (holding that the Commission may exercise its discretion to grant a hearing after a supplemental investigation).

In Allmon v. Postmaster General, 01974821 (2000), the Commission also followed the trend of Cerda and Baker where the complainant requested a final agency decision on the record but the investigation was inadequate.
