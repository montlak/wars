5. Remanding for Further Investigation After a Final Decision or Hearing

In Bloomquist v. Postmaster General, 01955656 (1997), the Commission found that the agency's investigation into allegations that the complainant was discriminated against on the basis of race in his removal was "woefully inadequate." The investigator failed to interview coworkers as well as a favorable eyewitness to the events that led to the complainant's removal. Still, the Commission upheld the final agency decision finding no discrimination.

By contrast, in May v. Postmaster General, 01954860 (1997), the Commission vacated and remanded a final agency decision finding no discrimination because of inadequacies in the agency's investigative record. In that case, the agency neglected to include information about the severity and duration of the complainant's medical condition in a reasonable accommodation case.

What is puzzling about the Bloomquist and May decisions is that both cases went to an administrative hearing to supplement the record. No clear rule with respect to remand versus a decision on the existing record emerged, and the Commission did not attempt to articulate any rule in its decisions.

Likewise, in Green v. Postmaster General, 01955142 (1997), the Commission sent the case back to the agency for a supplemental investigation even though a hearing had been held in the case. Significantly, the Commission found that the administrative judge erred in excluding the testimony of two witnesses who may have been able to corroborate the complainant's sexual harassment allegations. Of interest in Green, the Commission held that it would continue to retain jurisdiction over the case during the supplemental investigation to expedite a decision upon completion.

Another example is provided by King v. International Boundary & Water Commission, 0120114319 (2013), where the Commission reversed a grant of summary judgment for the agency and remanded the case for a supplemental investigation:

An agency shall develop an impartial and appropriate factual record upon which to make findings on the claims raised by the written complaint. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.108(b). An appropriate factual record is one that allows a reasonable fact finder to draw conclusions as to whether discrimination occurred. Id.

Upon review, we find the record to be insufficiently developed for us to determine if the Agency discriminated against Complainant on the basis of reprisal for prior protected EEO activity. Specifically, we find that the record contains inadequate documentary and testimonial evidence to allow us to determine if the Agency's reasons for Complainant's termination are not believable or if the Agency treated Complainant differently from similarly situated employees.

Regarding the failure to follow proper procedures to request leave and the failure to provide requested medical documentation allegations, the record reflects that this concerns a request made by Complainant during a November 8, 2010 meeting to discuss accommodations for a work-related injury. Specifically, Complainant requested permission from management to go home during the day to change the dressing for his injury. Although Complainant, S2, 51, FS1, and the Union President attended the November 8, 2010 meeting, the record contains

testimonial evidence about the meeting from Complainant, S2, and S1, but not from FS1 or the Union President. As Complainant, S1, and S2 provided conflicting testimony about management's response to the request, we find that obtaining testimonial evidence from all the individuals present at the meeting would help us determine what occurred at the meeting. Moreover, we note that the record does not contain a copy of the leave procedures that Complainant allegedly failed to follow.

Regarding the falsification of time cards allegation, S2 averred that Complainant made fraudulent entries on his time card on three occasions: November 15, 2010; December 6, 2010; and December 15, 2010. The record, however, is missing documentary evidence relating to those dates. First, the record does not contain any documentary evidence (Complainant's time card, handwritten maintenance logs, etc.) pertaining to November 15, 2010. Second, while the record contains Complainant's time card and a handwritten maintenance log for December 6, 2010, we are unable to ascertain who made the entries in the log. Third, while the record contains Complainant's time card for December 15, 2010, the record does not contain any handwritten maintenance logs for that date. Moreover, the record is missing testimonial evidence relating to those dates. For example, 52 averred that Complainant was supposed to but did not report to the Maintenance Work Leader (MWL) on December 6, 2010. In contrast, Complainant averred that he reported to and received work assignments from MWL on December 6, 2010 and December 15, 2010. The record, however, contains no testimonial evidence from MWL or from any of Complainant's co-workers that could verify his absence or presence on the cited dates.

Regarding the inattention to duty allegation, S2 averred that FS1 told Complainant to report to the warehouse for work on December 2 and 3, 2010, but Complainant did not report to anyone and just sat around the warehouse without doing any work for two days. The record, however, contains no testimonial evidence from FS 1 or anyone from the warehouse on this issue.

Regarding the recording of conversations in the workplace allegation, the record reflects that this concerns an incident that occurred in June 2010 when Complainant recorded his interview with the Ethics Official. While S2 averred that Complainant's recording violated Agency policy, the record does not contain a copy of the policy. Moreover, although Complainant averred that the Ethics Official gave him permission to record the interview, the record contains no testimonial evidence from the Ethics Official on this issue.

Regarding the discourtesy towards a co-worker allegation, the record reflects that this concerns an incident on December 21, 2010. While S2 averred that Complainant told a co-worker to "fuck off," Complainant averred that he said "buzz off." Although the record contains testimonial evidence from Complainant and the co-worker, the record reflects that at least three other employees were present at the time. The record, however, contains no testimonial evidence from those three witnesses.

Finally, we note that comparative evidence must be sought in every case alleging disparity in treatment on a basis protected by a law enforced by the EEOC. EEO MD-110, Ch. 6, § VII.B.1. In general, similarly situated means that the persons who are being compared are so situated that it is reasonable to expect that they would receive the same treatment as the complainant in the context of a particular employment decision. Id. The EEO Investigator would be obligated to find out whether there are persons, not named by the complainant but similarly situated, whose treatment could be compared to the complainant's treatment. Id. Here, there is no indication that the EEO Investigator attempted to obtain any comparative evidence to show whether employees who engaged in misconduct of comparable seriousness were terminated or retained.

Based on the above, we conclude that the present record lacks the necessary information upon which to adequately determine if the Agency's actions were discriminatory. Accordingly, we remand this matter back to the Agency to conduct a supplemental investigation.

Once again, the Commission did not articulate in King why it choose remand instead of imposing sanctions for an inadequate investigation or finding the existing record did not contain legitimate reasons for the agency's actions.
