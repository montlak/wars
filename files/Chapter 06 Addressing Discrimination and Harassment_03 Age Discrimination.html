AGE DISCRIMINATION

The federal workforce is notably older on average than the American workforce as a whole. The potential for age discrimination -- or for misunderstandings that may lead to complaints of age discrimination -- is correspondingly greater.

As a manager, you are expected to treat older workers with respect, and to leverage their experience and institutional memory as you integrate less experienced workers into your unit. You may be evaluated on your use of older workers as part of the key managerial competency "leveraging diversity."

Furthermore, EEO law, specifically the Age Discrimination in Employment Act, protects all workers age 40 or older from discrimination on account of age. (No one under age 40 is protected by the ADEA.) It is your responsibility to ensure that neither you nor any of your employees discriminate against older workers and applicants. Unless you supervise employees in law enforcement or other positions in which maximum ages are formally established, you may not legally consider any employee or job candidate "too old for the job."

The ADEA also allows older employees and job applicants to claim discrimination if they are adversely impacted by an age-neutral policy or practice. For example, a rule allowing only recent college graduates to apply for traveling jobs would be a problem if it tended to exclude older workers.

The ADEA

Under the ADEA, agencies are prohibited from discriminating against employees age 40 or older, based on their age, with respect to any term or condition of employment, including hiring, firing, promotions, compensation, benefits, job assignments, and training.

Employees may be protected under the ADEA if they:

  • • Are 40 years of age or older.
  • • Are the recipients of an adverse personnel action.
  • • Can show a connection between their age and the action.

The ADEA generally prohibits agencies from including age limitations and specifications in job announcements. It also protects older workers from policies that affect them in greater numbers than younger workers.

The law does provide some staffing flexibility to agencies. Employment policies based on a "reasonable factor other than age" don't violate the law, even if they have an adverse impact on older employees. You must be able to show that your policy or employment decision is reasonable and designed to achieve a legitimate business purpose. Consult HR to make sure you are complying with the latest legal developments in this area.

A valid business purpose may justify, for example, sending a younger employee to a training course when you know that an older employee is just about to retire. But care is needed to avoid assumptions -- or pointed inquiries -- about likely retirement that could be interpreted as age discrimination. And you definitely should not make a habit of choosing younger over older workers for training. This could provide statistical evidence that you favor younger workers over those protected by the ADEA.

The federal government may establish maximum ages for certain positions, particularly in law enforcement. However, these exemptions from age discrimination are limited. Check with HR before establishing a formal or informal age cut-off for any job.

The ADEA protects older workers who are treated less favorably than workers who are younger but still over 40. In other words, a 60-year-old employee may complain that a 45-year-old coworker is receiving better promotion opportunities because of age, even though they are both over 40.

Reasonable accommodation

The ADEA does not require agencies to provide reasonable accommodation based solely on age. When an employee requests a workplace accommodation because of age-connected impairments, you are not required to make it simply because the employee is 40 years of age or older.

If the accommodation is a minor matter that does not require any major adjustment of the workplace or the employee's duties, it may be made informally. Federal employees seeking a more serious accommodation fall under the Rehabilitation Act. They must be qualified to do their jobs, with or without accommodation, and be substantially limited in a major life activity.

Your agency has specific policies and procedures in place. Employees who are no longer qualified to do their jobs, even with reasonable accommodation, may be transferred or removed. Consult with HR.

The following examples are taken from actual cases that appeared before the EEOC:

  • • The Social Security Administration subjected the complainant to age discrimination when it did not select him for an external position. His qualifications were plainly superior to those of the selectees. However, the requirement of a four-year college degree was added after all the applications were reviewed. The agency was aware that the complainant did not have a degree.
  • • The Department of Health and Human Services subjected the 57-year-old complainant to age discrimination when it hired an individual who was 14 years younger for a public affairs specialist position. The complainant plainly was more qualified for the position at issue. Further, the selecting official's explanation that the complainant was "overqualified" for the position and would likely be dissatisfied and resentful working in a low-level job lacked credibility.

Discriminatory language

As we have noted, unless you supervise positions with maximum age limits, you cannot legally consider any employee or candidate "too old for the job." Careless talk about age exposes your agency to liability under the ADEA.

In one real-life example, an employee said he was improperly denied a promotion due to his age (58) in favor of a younger candidate (39). He had heard through the grapevine that his manager had been "wondering if he was going antiquing." The manager also suggested that the employee retire. Such comments, made by managers involved in the selection process, may be direct evidence of age discrimination.

Comments found to trigger an inference of discrimination include deriding older employees as "dinosaurs," "old farts," and "no spring chicken." Expressing a preference for "new blood" has been found to be discriminatory language in some cases, but it has also been found to mean a preference for candidates from outside of the agency, which was not linked to age. Nevertheless, any of these expressions can trigger a complaint that you don't need.

Talk like this on the part of a supervisor may encourage similar behavior by younger employees. This is clearly no way to leverage diversity. And even if no adverse employment action is taken, if an older employee complains, this sort of language may be evidence of harassment due to a hostile work environment, a form of illegal discrimination.

Succession planning

Succession planning is an important part of your job as a manager. You need to have a general idea of when employees, especially key employees with years of experience and valuable institutional knowledge, will need to be replaced so you can train, or plan to hire, their successors.

Succession plans, put together with the aid of HR, are a useful and accepted tool of workforce management. They may be required by agencies from managers at specified levels. These plans often work best when they benefit from the contributions of the experienced employees involved, who know what their jobs entail, how they might develop in the future, and what qualifications should be looked for in new hires or promotions. Experienced older employees can also be invaluable in training, coaching, and mentoring younger workers being prepared for greater responsibilities in accordance with succession plans.

It is also common for experienced employees who plan to retire to give their supervisors plenty of advance notice, which assists in succession planning.

But care is needed whenever you talk about retirement, replacing current employees, or age in connection with hiring or promotion. Derogatory statements on age are often accompanied by discussions of retirement, which will strengthen a case for discrimination.

This does not mean you may never discuss retirement with your employees, but any discussion should be in the clear context of succession planning and general staffing needs. It would be a very bad idea for you to say to an employee something like, "this job is getting to be too much for someone your age" or "face facts, you're past it now," and recommend the employee consider retirement.

Succession planning is not a blanket excuse for age discrimination. If you believe, for example, that younger employees are better at dealing with new technology, and you promote a younger over an older employee based on that belief, the older employee you passed over may be able to claim age discrimination.

Remember: Whenever you promote (or take any other employment action), you must be able to justify your choice based on objective criteria, such as differences in qualifications -- not on assumptions that may invite a charge of discrimination.

Mistakes to avoid

Communication is often the key to avoiding discrimination. But people -- and their perceptions -- differ. There is no way to guarantee that your efforts, no matter how sincere and well-executed, will end all possible discrimination complaints. However, you can sharply reduce the possibility that someone will file an age discrimination complaint by avoiding the following mistakes.

Do not:

  • • Hire with a maximum age limit in mind. The only time this does not violate the ADEA is if the position is designated "law enforcement officer" or equivalent, with a legally established age limit.
  • • Ask job applicants how long they intend to continue working.
  • • Ask an employee about "retirement plans," except in the general context of succession planning. Avoid any suggestion an older employee should retire.
  • • Assume that older employees cannot handle new technology or any other change (or that younger people are better at it).
  • • Make comments such as "no spring chicken." This could indicate a discriminatory basis for employment decisions. Also avoid terms such as "dinosaur" and "old man."
  • • Make any age-related references, even when affectionate, such as "oldie-but-goodie."
  • • Make comments about your employees' physical appearance. This is never a good idea and could indicate bias, particularly when the comments seem age-related. For example, don't comment on an employee's "thinning hair."

Ask FMG

Q. Can an employee file a viable claim of age discrimination when the individual allegedly treated more favorably is also over 40?

A. Yes, the comparative employee does not need to be under 40 in order for the complainant to establish a prima facie case. There must simply be a significant difference in ages, although how much is not clear. For example, the U.S. Supreme Court has ruled that an inference of age discrimination would not be greater when a 40-year-old person is replaced by a 39-year-old person than when a 56-year-old person is replaced by someone who is 40. The statutory language does not ban discrimination against employees because they are aged 40 or older; it bans discrimination against employees because of their age, but limits the protected class to those who are 40 or older.

Q. Is it permissible under the ADEA to make an employment decision on the basis of age if it is favorable to an individual over the age of 40?

A. Not if the individual, who is also over the age of 40 and is significantly older than the favored person, is disadvantaged.

Q. Is it permissible for an agency to require recent experience or education in connection with a position?

A. Yes, if it can provide a reasonable explanation for the requirement. Although it is not an automatic violation of the ADEA to consider how recent an individual's education or work experience is, it will likely be deemed a violation if the agency cannot establish a bona fide reason for requiring more recent experience or education.

Q. Younger employees are just better at dealing with new technologies. Can't I focus on younger applicants when I need to fill a position requiring tech skills?

A. No. Whenever you hire (or take any other employment action), you must be able to justify your choice based on objective criteria, such as differences in qualifications -- not on assumptions that may invite a charge of discrimination. Don't assume that older candidates lack technology or other skills.

Q. Can age-based comments help to establish a case of age discrimination?

A. Yes. Age-based comments can provide direct evidence of discrimination, help create an inference of discrimination, or help rebut an agency's explanation of its action. Inquiries into how long an individual intends to continue working in advance of a selection decision can constitute evidence of age discrimination. In Harrison v. Department of Justice, the EEOC found that the agency discriminated against an employee based on his age when it transferred him to another facility. The employee's supervisor referred to him as an "old man" and a "dinosaur." He also claimed he was "too old" to work in the unit.
