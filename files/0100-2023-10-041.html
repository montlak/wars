7. Disposition of Complaints

The adoption of Part 1614 in 1992 streamlined the final stages of the administrative process following investigation of the complaint. Significantly, as discussed below, the Commission eliminated the mandatory informal adjustment attempt and the proposed disposition of complaints. Under Part 1614, once an investigation is completed, the complainant has 30 calendar days either to request a hearing before an EEOC administrative judge or to request an immediate final agency decision on the basis of the investigative record. 29 CFR 1614.108(h).

The thrust of the EEO process under Part 1613 was to provide informal resolution of complaints at the earliest possible stage. In line with that emphasis, the agency was required to provide the employee and his representative with an opportunity to meet with the appropriate officials following completion of the investigation. This was really nothing more than a mandatory bringing together of the parties to see if settlement of the case could be affected at that point in time. Neither side was obligated to enter into an informal adjustment of the case.

Any adjustment was required to be in writing and made a part of the investigative file. See 29 CFR 1613.217(a). Relief obtained through informal adjustment could be as broad as the statutory relief authorized, including back pay and attorney fees. If the parties agreed on all issues during informal adjustment except attorney fees, the attorney fees portion of the complaint could be severed and proceed to a final decision under 29 CFR 1613.221. See 29 CFR 1613.217(a).

Part 1614 retained the Commission's emphasis on the early resolution of complaints. However, the emphasis has been shifted to alternative dispute resolution, as discussed earlier in this chapter, subheading "Alternative Dispute Resolution." The mandatory informal adjustment attempts and proposed dispositions under the earlier Part 1613 procedures did little to resolve complaints and only resulted in further delay in the processing of complaints.

Under the initial enactment of Part 1614 in 1992, the emphasis on early resolution of complaints was maintained in part by carrying forth a 1987 amendment to Part 1613 allowing agencies to dismiss any complaint if the complainant refused a certified offer of full relief. 29 CFR 1614.107(h) (1992). Whatever the intent of the certified offer of full relief, the result was that few cases actually settled. Instead, copious litigation ensued over how the agency should make an offer of full relief, what constituted full relief, how to resolve issues of damages in offers of full relief, whether full relief included attorney fees, and whether an agency could offer full relief after the case had been assigned to an administrative judge. In addition to increasing rather than reducing litigation, the offer of full relief generated several cases where the Commission was confronted with the inherent unfairness of dismissing a complaint that the agency was willing to settle if the complainant rejected an offer because of a mistaken belief that he or she was entitled to further relief.

Offers of full relief have been eliminated from the Commission's regulations under the 1999 amendments. However, it does not appear to be the intent of the Commission to completely eliminate offers of full relief. As discussed fully in Chapter 5, subheading "Settlement Policies," MD-110 has preserved the offer of full relief in limited circumstances.

In the 1999 amendments to Part 1614, the Commission replaced the certified offer of full relief with a so-called offer of resolution. The offer of resolution is loosely based on an offer of judgment under Fed. R. Civ. P. 68. Agencies may tender offers of resolution which limit their liability for attorney fees if a complainant refuses the offer and, in a subsequent decision, is awarded less than what the agency offered. Pursuant to 29 CFR 1614.109(c):

(1) Any time after the filing of the written complaint but not later than the date an administrative judge is appointed to conduct a hearing, the agency may make an offer of resolution to a complainant who is represented by an attorney.

(2) Any time after the parties have received notice that an administrative judge has been appointed to conduct a hearing, but not later than 30 days prior to the hearing, the agency may make an offer of resolution to the complainant, whether represented by an attorney or not.

(3) The offer of resolution shall be in writing and shall include a notice explaining the possible consequences of failing to accept the offer. The agency's offer, to be effective, must include attorney's fees and costs and must specify any non-monetary relief. With regard to monetary relief, an agency may make a lump sum offer covering all forms of monetary liability, or it may itemize the amounts and types of monetary relief being offered. The complainant shall have 30 days from receipt of the offer of resolution to accept it. If the complainant fails to accept an offer of resolution and the relief awarded in the administrative judge's decision, the agency's final decision, or the Commission decision on appeal is not more favorable than the offer, then, except where the interest of justice would not be served, the complainant shall not receive payment from the agency of attorney's fees or costs incurred after the expiration of the 30-day acceptance period. An acceptance of an offer must be in writing and will be timely if postmarked or received within the 30-day period. Where a complainant fails to accept an offer of resolution, an agency may make other offers of resolution and either party may seek to negotiate a settlement of the complaint at any time.

Under the Commission's revised 1987 regulations, the agency could cancel a complaint when it made an offer of full relief that was rejected by the complainant. Title 29 CFR 1613.215(a)(7) provided that the agency may cancel a complaint:

If the complainant refuses within 15 calendar days of receipt of an offer of settlement to accept an agency offer of full relief in adjustment of the complaint, provided that the agency's Director of Equal Employment Opportunity, or a designee reporting directly to the Director, has certified in writing that the agency's written offer constitutes full relief. An offer of full relief under this subsection is the appropriate relief in § 1613.271. The offer need not contain the decision whether disciplinary action is necessary, but the basis for the decision shall be recorded separately from the complaint file.

This provision was maintained in the oriGINAl Part 1614, but the Commission expanded the complainant's response time to 30 calendar days. See 29 CFR 1614.107(h) (1992). Although the offers of resolution under the amended Part 1614 eliminate the possibility that a complaint will be dismissed for failure to accept an offer, they can still require a complainant to seriously evaluate an agency's offer by cutting into the entitlement to attorney fees and costs.

Under Part 1613, if the parties were unable to come to an agreement, the agency was required to make a proposed disposition of the complaint. 29 CFR 1613.217(c). The proposed disposition included a finding as to whether or not discrimination had occurred and, if so, what the agency proposed in the way of relief. The proposed disposition was not binding on the complainant, even if the agency made a finding that discrimination occurred. The complainant could dispute whether the relief proposed was adequate.

In the proposed disposition, the agency was required to inform the employee of the right to request a hearing within 15 calendar days of receipt of the proposed disposition, and the right to have a final decision rendered by the agency head without a hearing. Failure to make an election within 15 calendar days resulted in the agency adopting the proposed disposition as final. See 29 CFR 1613.217(d). Proposed dispositions have been eliminated under Part 1614.

Under Part 1614, there is no disposition of the complaint by the agency at the conclusion of the investigative stage. The investigative stage concludes with the agency issuing to the complainant a notice advising him or her of the right to request a hearing within 30 days of receipt of the notice before an EEOC administrative judge or the right to request a final agency decision on the basis of the investigative record. See 29 CFR 1614.108(h). Failure to request a hearing within the 30-day period will result in a final agency decision on the record.
