3. Denial of Leave as Retaliation

Because requesting accommodation is a protected activity, denial of accommodation can also constitute retaliation. For example, in Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120112858 (2014), the complainant used a right-handed personal vehicle to deliver mail. When she was unable to use her vehicle while it was being repaired, the complainant requested to use annual leave. The agency denied the request and placed the complainant on leave without pay. The Commission in that case found:

Upon review, we find that Complainant has established that the Agency's reasons were pretext for discrimination. In particular, the Agency was on notice that Complainant requested reasonable accommodation for her disability for March 16, 2010, and it penalized her for doing so. The record reflects that Complainant's medical restrictions limited her to only driving LLV's, a vehicle with a right-hand driver's position. The Agency was aware that Complainant would be without her personal LLV on March 16, 2010, while the vehicle was under repair, and that she could not perform her duties without a vehicle with a right-hand driver's position due to her medical restrictions. S1 stated that Complainant was given LWOP for that day because, pursuant to Agency policy, an employee must have a backup vehicle. S1 also advised Complainant to rent a vehicle for that day. We find that the alternatives S1 presented to Complainant were unreasonable given that vehicles in the United States overwhelmingly are equipped with left-hand drive. The Agency could have allowed Complainant to use her accrued leave, as requested, or loaned her a LLV, a vehicle common throughout the Agency. The Agency did neither, which left Complainant with no alternative but to forgo a day's pay. The Agency asserts that Complainant never requested leave for March 16, 2010, because she never filled out the appropriate form to request annual leave. Notwithstanding, the record reflects that Complainant verbally requested and was verbally denied annual leave by S1.

We note that Complainant was also threatened with AWOL for not attending work on March 16, 2010. As noted above, an employer may not penalize an employee for leave which is taken as a reasonable accommodation, and to do so constitutes retaliation for the employee's use of a reasonable accommodation. Enforcement Guidance on Reasonable Accommodation at Question 19. By providing Complainant with LWOP instead of allowing her to use her accrued annual leave or sick leave, the Agency clearly penalized Complainant, and therefore subjected Complainant to reprisal for requesting accommodation for March 16, 2010.

In the same case, the Commission also found that the incidents of that day created a hostile work environment.
