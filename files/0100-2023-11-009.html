2. Events Occurring During Counseling

In some cases, events occur during the course of precomplaint counseling which the employee believes are discriminatory. It is not necessary for the employee to initiate counseling anew. It is sufficient if the employee brings these matters to the attention of the counselor while being counseled on other matters. See Dripps v. Secretary of Navy, 01991909 (2001) (complainant was in counseling before, during, and after termination); McGowan v. USPS, 01831826 (1983).

Moreover, when events occur during counseling and are brought to the attention of the counselor, their timeliness is determined by examining whether they were presented to the counselor within the proper time limit from their occurrence, not from the oriGINAl contact with the counselor. The fact that the first contact may have been untimely does not make all subsequent contacts untimely. Frazier v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 05890912 (1989).

In DiSante v. Postmaster General, 01970303 (1998), the Commission found that the complainant's initial counselor contact was timely, and that allegations that arose while the complainant was waiting for the EEO counselor to get back to him were also timely:

In its final decision, the agency dismissed appellant's complaint on the grounds that he failed to seek timely EEO counseling. The agency determined that appellant's initial EEO contact on June 14, 1995 was beyond 45 days of the incidents occurring on March 3, 1995, March 16, 1995, and April 24, 1995.

On appeal, appellant asserts that he initially sought counseling on March 6, 1995. He was told on that day by the Senior EEO Specialist that a counselor would soon contact him. He then went on sick leave beginning in April 1995 to August 7, 1995. Appellant argues that he had not heard from the EEO Counselor thus he finally contacted the Counselor in late May or early June 1995, but that the Counselor was not available. On June 14, 1995, the Counselor contacted him and he indicated that he wished to include additional allegations due to continuing incidents. He asserts that the EEO Counselor sent him a form by which to withdraw the first allegations for which he sought counseling and included instructions to complete a new complaint and include the initial allegations with the new allegations. Appellant contends that in doing this, he was not advised that the initial allegations could be dismissed as untimely. Appellant argues that counseling on the initial allegations took longer than 30 days, yet he did not agree to an extension of that period. Appellant's appeal submissions contain a Precomplaint Counseling form which indicates that appellant sought counseling on March 6, 1995 concerning his removal from duties at the Frankford Station.

...

Based on a review of the record, the Commission finds that the agency erred in dismissing the allegations as untimely counseled. The record indicates that appellant sought counseling on March 6, 1995, just shortly after the first alleged incident. The completed Counselor's Report indicates that the Counselor interviewed appellant on April 2, 1996 regarding continuing instances when the Customer Service Manager advised appellant and others that appellant would be removed from his position. The March 16, 1995 incident occurred while appellant was waiting for a counselor to contact him. Thus the allegations concerning the March 3 and 16, 1995 instances are timely. With regard to the incident occurring on April 24, 1995, appellant asserts that he contacted the Counselor in late May or early June about the counseling process, which would apparently make initial contact for that allegation timely. Moreover, since the EEO Counselor had failed to maintain appropriate contact with appellant and complete counseling within 30 calendar days, appellant was deprived of the opportunity to submit the allegation to counseling earlier.
