B. BASIC RULES OF LIABILITY

The rules of liability, at least with respect to harassment by supervisors, were finally settled in Burlington Indus., Inc. v. Ellerth, 524 U.S. 742, 118 S. Ct. 2257, 141 L. Ed.2d 633 (1998). The Supreme Court, noting that quid pro quo was merely a label and not a distinct form of discrimination, held that it would employ a standard of strict liability in cases involving tangible employment actions. In other words, in those cases where the harassment takes the form of a tangible employment action or a hostile environment culminates in a tangible employment action, the agency is liable for the harassment and there is no legal defense. The agency would still have factual defenses, such as the harassment did not occur or was not based on a protected category. But, if the complainant demonstrates the harassment occurred, the agency is automatically liable.

Of course, taking a tangible employment action because a subordinate has rejected sexual advances or premising a tangible employment action on a subordinates submission to sexual advances is not within the scope of anyone's employment. So, the Supreme Court, as discussed below had to find a different basis for imputing liability in the case of supervisory harassment involving a tangible employment action.

The rules of liability are different when the case does not involve a tangible employment action, but the employee is subjected to a hostile work environment on a prohibited basis. An employee, be they coworker or supervisor, is simply not acting within the scope of his or her employment in harassing another employee. In cases of hostile environment harassment where the supervisor is responsible for creating the hostile environment, the Court in Burlington Industries, 118 S. Ct. at 2270, held:

[A]n employer is subject to vicarious liability to a victimized employee for an actionable hostile environment created by a supervisor with immediate (or successively higher) authority over the employee. When no tangible employment action is taken, a defending employer may raise an affirmative defense to liability or damages, subject to proof by a preponderance of the evidence, see Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 8(c). The defense comprises two necessary elements: (a) that the employer exercised reasonable care to prevent and correct promptly any sexually harassing behavior, and (b) that the plaintiff employee unreasonably failed to take advantage of any preventive or corrective opportunities provided by the employer or to avoid harm otherwise....

See also Faragher v. City of Boca Raton, 524 U.S. 775, 118 S. Ct. 2275, 141 L. Ed.2d 662 (1998).

The Supreme Court did not, in either Burlington Industries or Faragher, set down the rules of liability in hostile environment cases involving coworkers. The Commission, however, by regulation and case law has established that an agency is liable for prohibited harassment by a coworker where the agency knew or should have known of the harassment and failed to take prompt and effective corrective action. See 29 CFR 1604.11(d). Essentially, the Commission has adopted a negligence standard when it comes to harassment by coworkers. The agency, as an employer, has a duty to provide every employee with a workplace free of prohibited harassment. When an agency knows, or should know, of harassment and does not take prompt action to stop it, the agency breaches that duty and will be liable. In other words, the employer is negligent. See Restatement (2d) of Agency, 219(1)(b). Coworkers do not have the authority to take an employment action against a fellow worker and, as a result, no special rules of liability are necessary for coworkers in tangible employment action cases.

Because the liability rules are relatively simple for harassment involving a tangible employment action, the discussion of liability starts there.
