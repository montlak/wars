E. INTERNAL HARASSMENT INVESTIGATIONS

An employee who assists in an internal investigation into employee allegations of harassment has engaged in protected activity under Title VII. In Crawford v. Metropolitan Government of Nashville and Davidson County, TN, 129 S. Ct. 846 (2009), the Supreme Court continued a trend established in Robinson v. Shell Oil Co., 117 S. Ct. 843 (1997) and Burlington Northern and Sante Fe Railway Co. v. White, 126 S. Ct. 2405 (2006), of broadly interpreting the anti-relation provision of Title VII.

In Crawford, the employer began an investigation into rumors of sexual harassment by the school district's employee relations director. The plaintiff, Vicky Crawford, when asked by a human resources officer if she had witnessed "inappropriate behavior" by the director, disclosed several instances of sexual harassment. The school district took no action against the director, but shortly after the investigation fired Crawford for alleged embezzlement and two other employees who had participated in the internal investigation. Crawford then filed a retaliation charge with the EEOC.

The U.S. Court of Appeals upheld a decision by the district court granting summary judgment to the school district on the basis that Crawford had not engaged in protected activity because "she had not 'instigated or initiated any complaint,' but had 'merely answered questions by investigators in an already-pending internal investigation, initiated by someone else.'" The Supreme Court granted certiorari and reversed.

In a unanimous decision authored by Justice Souter, the Court held that Crawford had engaged in opposition activity under the anti-retaliation clause. Justice Souter, in Crawford, wrote:

"Oppose" goes beyond "active, consistent" behavior in ordinary discourse, where we would naturally use the work to speak of someone who has taken no action at all to advance a position beyond disclosing it. Countless people were known to "oppose" slavery before Emancipation, or are said to "oppose" capital punishment today, without writing public letters, taking to the streets, or resisting the government. And we would call it "opposition" if an employee took a stand against an employer's discriminatory practices not by "instigating" action, but by standing pat, say, by refusing to follow a supervisor's order to fire a junior worker for discriminatory reasons. Cf. McDonnell, supra, at 262 (finding employee covered by Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 where his employer retaliated against him for failing to prevent his subordinate from filing an EEOC charge). There is, then, no reason to doubt that a person can "oppose" by responding to someone else's question just as surely as by provoking the discussion, and nothing in the statute requires a freakish rule protecting an employee who reports discrimination on her own initiative but not one who reports the same discrimination in the same words when her boss asks a question.

The decision is significant because of the duty imposed on employers to investigate and promptly correct illegally harassing behavior under the Court's decisions in Burlington Industries, Inc. v. Ellerth, 118 S. Ct. 2257 (1998) and Faragher v. City of Boca Raton, 118 S. Ct. 2275 (1998). The Court in Crawford rejected the Sixth Circuit's rationale that protecting employees who participated in internal investigations would somehow undermine those cases. "[W]e find it hard to see why the Sixth Circuit's rule would not itself largely undermine the Ellerth-Faragher scheme, along with the statute's 'primary objective' of "avoid[ing] harm" to employees," the Court noted. Indeed, employees might well be reluctant to be forthcoming in those investigations if they knew they might, with impunity, be punished for disclosing harassment.

Justice Alito, joined by Justice Thomas, issued a concurring opinion arguing the Court went to far in broadly interpreting the opposition clause because it may include conduct that was not active and purposive.

The surprise was not necessarily the Supreme Court's finding in Crawford, which is consistent with its caselaw both in retaliation and hostile environment cases. The surprise, perhaps, was that the Sixth Circuit that Crawford's activity was not protected under Title VII.
