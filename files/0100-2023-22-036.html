5. Remarks About Pregnancy

Remarks about pregnancy, particularly remarks expressing doubt that an employee will return to work after delivery, do not constitute direct evidence of pregnancy discrimination, at least in the Seventh Circuit. In Ilhardt v. Sara Lee Corp., 118 F.3d 1151, 1156 (7th Cir. 1997), the court explained:

[A]s her evidence of pregnancy discrimination, Ilhardt points to the incident where her name was left off the floor plan of the legal department's new office space as well as comments Newman made to the effect that he was sure she would not return to work full-time after having her third child because his daughters were extremely busy with just two children, and that he thought it was better for mothers of young children to stay at home. As her most damaging evidence, she presents a statement Newman made to her when she was trying to persuade him to choose another attorney for the RIF. When she asked him, "What if I miscarried tomorrow? What would you do then?" he responded, "I don't know what I'd do in that situation. We'd have to reevaluate it then." Ilhardt cites Hennessy v. Penril Datacom Networks, Inc., 69 F.3d 1344 (7th Cir. 1995), to support her characterization of these statements as direct evidence of pregnancy discrimination. But Newman's statements, even viewing them in the light most favorable to Ilhardt, do not rise to the level of pregnancy discrimination that was demonstrated in Hennessy, 69 F.3d at 1347-48, and they do not suggest that he disapproved of her getting pregnant, or, as Ilhardt puts it, that he considered her third pregnancy to be her "third strike." Ilhardt presents no evidence suggesting that Newman regretted hiring her because she later became pregnant, nor does she show, for example, that he was surprised that she was pregnant because he thought she was a career woman, as was the supervisor in Hennessy. Furthermore, we have already held that statements expressing doubt that a woman will return to work full-time after having a baby do not constitute direct evidence of pregnancy discrimination. Troupe, 20 F.3d at 736.

But in Bernardi v. Postmaster General, 01954090 (1997), the Commission held that such statements not only constitute direct evidence of discrimination, but are sufficient to establish discrimination:

The record in this case clearly shows that S1's reasons for refusing to provide appellant a higher-level training assignment were based on his own assessment of appellant's pregnant condition and because, due to her projected maternity leave following childbirth, he believed that she would not be available to assume the higher-level position immediately following her training. S1 stated that he "didn't think it was prudent to place her into a training assignment...because she was in her second or third trimester of pregnancy." Most significant, however, was S1's conviction that to "just place a woman who is in her second or third trimester into a general supervisor position in my judgment just would not have been prudent. I basically just didn't do it for that reason."

The Commission finds that S1's comments constitute direct evidence of intentional discrimination based on appellant's pregnancy and subsequent childbirth and that there is a direct correlation between his bias and the agency's failure to afford higher-level training to appellant. S1's statements exhibit outmoded attitudes of bias and animus that Title VII and the PDA sought to eliminate.

In reversing a grant of summary judgment to the agency in Wright v. Secretary of Interior, 0120111333 (2013), because the record was not adequately developed, the Commission found that the statements of a supervisor to another employee who was pregnant could be highly relevant to management's attitude toward pregnant workers:

The Pregnancy Discrimination Act provides that discrimination on the basis of pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions constitutes sex discrimination under Title VII. The basic principle of the Act is that women so affected must be treated the same, for all employment related purposes, as other employees not so affected in their ability or inability to work. Carlson v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Request No. 05910335 (June 7, 1991).

If an employee is temporarily unable to perform her job because of her pregnancy, the employer must treat her the same as any other temporarily disabled employee. If temporarily disabled employees are allowed to take leave without pay, then an employee who is temporarily disabled because of pregnancy must be allowed to do the same. In addition, when determining an employee's ability to work, the employer may not single out pregnancy-related conditions for special procedures or heightened scrutiny.

Here, the supervisor claimed to adhere to the relevant personnel policies and past practices for evaluating unpaid leave requests due to medical reasons. Yet the record contains no policy documents or procedures that are relevant to this case.

One document titled "Guidance for a Great Place to Work" merely says that "LWOP will only be granted on rare occasions (emergency situations) and must be approved by the manager on a case-by-case basis." Another document governing "Leaves of Absence" outlines various scenarios (union activity, job-related studies, political activities), none of which are relevant here

The most analogous regulations and leave procedures in the record are those for sick leave. Under article 34 of the National Agreement, employees are generally "required to furnish acceptable evidence to substantiate a request for approval of sick leave if sick leave exceeds three (3) consecutive workdays." But there is an exception, under section 3, paragraph (e):

Employees...will not be required to furnish a doctor's certificate on a continuing basis if the employee suffers from a chronic condition which does not necessarily require medical treatment although absence from work may be necessary and the employee has previously furnished medical certification of the chronic condition. The Employer may periodically require further medical certification to substantiate an employee's continued use of this provision.

Because the record contains no evidence of the relevant personnel policies and past practices for evaluating requests for leave without pay due to medical reasons, we cannot determine whether the first-level supervisor uniformly applied this policy to pregnant employees and to other temporarily disabled employees.

In addition, we find that the record is not adequately developed in that there is no comparator evidence or testimony on how the first-level supervisor evaluated the medical documents from other temporarily disabled employees who requested leave. Such evidence is necessary for us to evaluate whether the first-level supervisor treated Complainant and other pregnant employees the same as other temporarily disabled employees.

The record should be further supplemented with testimony from management about their views and attitudes on pregnancy-related medical conditions, particularly those that affect women early in their pregnancies. Hyperemesis, in particular, is a condition that is often not understood, in which people may have preconceived notions or attitudes about. The condition may be viewed as the more mild morning sickness and may lead coworkers and managers alike to believe that a pregnant woman is exaggerating about her condition and that she should just "deal with it" rather than take leave.

Such an attitude is hinted at in a written statement by one of Complainant's coworkers, who had also experienced similar treatment by management when she was pregnant:

I had never had a problem with use/overuse of leave before nor had I ever had any type of leave restriction of leave issues....I was also aware that every woman's pregnancy is different, so while some women get through the "sick" phase, mine was ongoing. I met with [the second-level supervisor] after my first "violation" letter and he was very rude. He told that he was not a doctor, obviously, and wouldn't know if what I was telling him was the truth. ...He made me feel like a worthless liar and I did not appreciate his attitude with me...I was not asking to be completely excused from work until the birth of my baby, I was only requesting that I be allowed to use FMLA/LWOP for the hours I missed due to sickness until my baby was born....I did not feel I had shown any type of misconduct. I was pregnant. Simply, and unfortunately, this caused me to miss work and I didn't feel it should result in my suspension or removal. I shouldn't have been punished for being pregnant, but I feel like I was, severely.

Because the record was inadequately developed, and because the Al failed to address claim (6), which was accepted and investigated by the Agency, we conclude that judgment as a matter of law for the Agency should not have been granted.
