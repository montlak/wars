4. Current Employees

For employees, the first requirement of standing is that the complainant must be an employee of the agency against which the complaint is lodged. See 29 CFR 1614.106(a). Title VII, the Rehabilitation Act, the ADEA and GINA make it clear that the acts apply to all covered employees in the agencies and departments specified. An employee is "an individual employed by an employer." 42 USC 2000e(f).

The definition itself does very little to clarify the situation. In most cases, however, whether the individual is an employee will be self-evident. When it is not self-evident, the Commission has held that "[t]o determine whether an individual is an employee under this statute, we must analyze the 'economic realities' of the situation and view them in light of the common law principles of agency and the right of the employer to control the employment relationship." Arcilla v. Veterans Administration, 01860689 (1986) (citing McClure v. Salvation Army, 460 F.2d 553 (5th Cir. 1972)). The method of compensation is not determinative of the issue of whether the individual is an employee. In Arcilla, the Commission found that a VA resident in pathology paid by means of a "stipend" was an employee within Title VII. The Commission noted that all forms regarding appointment and termination of the resident were approved by a personnel office and that the agency retained the right to terminate the resident at all times. Based on the common law definition of an employee, this constituted sufficient control to establish the employee-employer relationship. Likewise, in Samuels v. Veterans Administration, 01852350 (1986) and 01852941 (1987), the agency unsuccessfully argued that a medical resident was not an employee. The record demonstrated that the agency controlled all of the complainant's work assignments, paid her directly and made deductions for traditional withholdings, and, at all times, retained the right to terminate her relationship with the agency. For a thorough discussion see section below on "Independent Consultants and Contractors."

Usually, there is an employee of the agency alleged to have engaged in discrimination. However, agencies may be found to be jointly liable if the complainant is an employee of one agency but under the control and supervision of another. See Drew N. v. Postmaster General, 2019003270 (2019) (reversing State Department's dismissal for failure to state a claim of a USAID employee's complaint).

As discussed in the section below on former employees, whether an employee has suffered harm and is aggrieved by an agency's action may be a function of whether any record is kept or whether the nature of the action is communicated to anyone else. In Allen v. Postmaster General, 05910045 (1991), the Commission found the employee was not aggrieved by a conversation with her supervisor during which he questioned her restroom use. The Commission found that even if other employees were not subjected to such questioning, there was no harm where no record of the conversation existed, the supervisor did not disclose the nature of the conversation to anyone else, and there was no indication the discussion would be used for any future disciplinary action. The Commission noted that if any future disciplinary action did, in fact, rely upon the conversation, then the complainant would have a basis for a new complaint at that time.

A similar result was obtained in White v. Postmaster General, 01921696 (1992), where the Commission noted "that a remark or comment, unaccompanied by concrete action is not a direct and personal deprivation sufficient to render an individual aggrieved for purposes of EEO law...however, because that discussion does not appear in any records, appellant can expect that the agency will not use the discussion in the future as the basis for any disciplinary action or progressive disciplinary action."

Title VII does not apply to aliens employed outside of any state. Nicolas v. Panama Canal Commission, 05940401 (1994).

Even if the complainant is an employee of the agency against which the complaint is filed, he fails to state a claim if he does not allege harm as the result of an employment action taken by the agency. For example, in Fratini v. Postmaster General, 01992838 (2002), the Commission dismissed the complaint for failure to state a claim where the complainant alleged he was not waited on by a window clerk. In finding the complainant failed to state a claim, the Commission noted the complainant was in the post office as a private citizen, not as an employee, and no employment action was involved.
