11. Hearing Preparation

Anyone with even a moderate amount of litigation experience knows that hearings require an extensive degree of preparation. Documents must be reviewed, organized, and prepared as exhibits. Trial notebooks must be assembled. Witness statements and depositions need to be reviewed and digested. Questions for witnesses need to be prepared. Even the witnesses themselves need to be prepared. The bottom-line is that it takes far more hours to prepare for a hearing than it does to actually try a case.

There is no general rule of thumb for the number of hours permitted for case preparation. Not only do hearings vary in length, but they vary in the number of exhibits and witnesses presented and the complexity of the legal and factual issues involved. In addition, there is the variable of the experience of the attorney involved. As a result, it is impossible to fashion a general rule that "x number" of hours of preparation is reasonable for each hour of hearing. Evaluating the reasonableness of the hours spent in preparation for hearing requires an assessment of all of the above factors.

Most EEO hearings are concluded within a day, though there are certainly exceptions with some hearings running days and even weeks. In Tilden v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01982768 (1999), the Commission gave some insight into what it considered reasonable hearing preparation in reviewing the hours expended by a paralegal:

The agency determined that it was not reasonable that the paralegal spent 47.6 hours prior to the hearing preparing this case. We note that based on the agency's calculation, the lead attorney spent 56.1 hours on this case prior to the hearing. Combining these hours, approximately 2 1/2 work weeks (103.7 hours) were spent preparing for the formal administrative hearing wherein appellant prevailed on her claim of retaliation. The paralegal's tasks primarily included conferences with appellant and witnesses; the preparation of memoranda for the lead attorney concerning witness statements; and the preparation of a trial book for the hearing. The Commission finds it reasonable that the paralegal spent approximately six work days performing these tasks, and we conclude that the agency erred in reducing her hours.

Unfortunately, the Tilden decision does not reflect the length of the hearing.

However, in Sinnott v. Secretary of Air Force, 01973683 (2000), the Commission found it was reasonable for an attorney to spend 21 hours in witness and hearing preparation for a one-day hearing.

Again, while providing little in the way of detail, the Commission found that 25.5 hours of preparation for closing arguments was reasonable for a case that consolidated four complaints with multiple issues. However, the Commission reduced from 5.5 to 3 as the reasonable number of hours for reviewing the decision of the administrative judge and considering the options. Donelson v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01996394 (2001).

In Nolan v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 07A40119 (2006), the Commission found that 60 hours of preparation for experienced counsel for a two--day hearing was reasonable.
