7. Complaint, Failure to Sign

Although there appears to be some inconsistency in the Commission's treatment of whether failure to sign the formal complaint is a fatal or curable defect, ultimately the case law reflects that the Commission approaches these situations on a case-by-case basis with the determination of whether to accept the case as timely being a factually-intensive decision.

The Commission has held that the failure of the employee to sign the formal complaint is not a fatal defect and that the agency may not dismiss the complaint as untimely because the employee failed to sign the complaint within the 15-day period. The employee must be given an opportunity to cure the defect, which is technical in nature. See Terry v. Postmaster General, 01953744 (1996); Flatter v. USPS, 01842185 (1985). If necessary, the complaint should be referred back to the counselor to assist the employee in fulfilling the formalities. See Acevedo v. USPS, 01870495 (1987). If the formal complaint is filed within the 15--day period and advises the agency of the claims raised, the agency may not dismiss the complaint because a signed copy was not filed until after expiration of the 15--day period. Perm v. Postmaster General, 01A60138 (2006).

Even those represented by attorneys have not been immune from falling prey to the formal complaint requirements. In Dolan v. Department of Air Force, 01870977 (1987), the employee's lawyer sent in an unsigned formal complaint. The agency then dismissed the complaint when it was resubmitted with the employee's signature because the signed complaint was not filed within the applicable time period. The Commission found that the failure to provide a signature was beyond the employee's control where there was no evidence that either the agency or the complainant's attorney made him aware of the requirement.

Part 1614 provides that the formal complaint may either be signed by the complainant or the complainant's attorney. 29 CFR 1614.106(c). Note, however, that the regulation does not appear to extend the signature privilege to complainants' representatives who are not attorneys. The use of the specific word attorney appeared significant in the regulation, since later in the same section is a requirement that the formal complaint contain an address and telephone number where the complainant or "the representative" can be contacted. However, in McIntosh v. Postmaster General, 01971471 (1998), the Commission specifically found that an agency must give a complainant a chance to cure the defect where the complaint is signed and submitted by a representative who is not an attorney. Dismissal is improper without such an opportunity. Id. This trend has continued in recent cases. See, e.g., Tyler v. Postmaster General, 01A30162 (2003) (holding that signature by complainant's nonattorney representative did not fulfill requirements of the regulation, but the complaint could not be dismissed without giving the complainant a chance to cure the defect).

In Chestra v. Postmaster General, 01A51383 (2005), the Commission reversed the dismissal of a complaint where the complainant's non-attorney representative had signed the complaint but also submitted a signed statement from the complainant describing the allegations of discrimination. The Commission found that the statement signed by the complainant satisfied the signature requirement of its regulations. However, there is precedent for declaring an unsigned complaint untimely where the complainant is aware of the requirement but fails to comply. In Rodriguez v. Postmaster General, 01A61276 (2006), the Commission found an unsigned complaint untimely where the complainant was informed of the signature requirement and was familiar with the EEO process. Submission of a signed complaint on appeal did not cure the defect.

However, in McLean v. Secretary of Army, 0120102477 (2010), the Commission specifically rejected the agency's argument that Rodriguez was controlling and reinstated a complaint that was originally submitted unsigned. There was some evidence in the record that the complainant was mildly retarded, the formal complaint was filed within four days of receipt of the notice of final interview, the complainant maintained that he had the form reviewed by an EEO counselor prior to submission and the agency did not give the complainant an opportunity to cure the defect within the 15-day filing period.

In Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120140093 (2014), the Commission reversed dismissal of the complaint where it was signed by the complainant's non-attorney representative. In reversing the dismissal, the Commission noted that the complainant signed the information provided in the precomplaint stage, there were other documents bearing the complainant's signature and that the agency had not given the complainant an opportunity to cure the defect prior to dismissal.
