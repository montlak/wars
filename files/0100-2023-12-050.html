36. Wrong Agency

In its notice of final interview, the agency is required to inform the employee of the proper official with whom to file the formal complaint. 29 CFR 1614.105(d). Occasionally, agencies omit this information from the notice and, more frequently, employees or their representatives fail to read the notice closely enough. The result is that complaints are filed with the wrong agency, or with the wrong official in the right agency. Pursuant to 5 USC 7702(f), when "an employee timely files the action, appeal, or petition with an agency other than the agency with which the action, appeal, or petition is to be filed, the employee shall be treated as having timely filed the action, appeal, or petition as of the date it is filed with the proper agency."

The EEOC interprets 7702(f) as applying to the time from the effective date of the challenged action to the date of filing with the agency in question. An employee who filed a complaint of discrimination with the Merit Systems Protection Board was deemed to have filed his EEO complaint with the agency in an untimely fashion; his MSPB appeal was not filed within the proper time period and he did not contact an EEO counselor within 30 days of receiving the Board's notification that it did not have jurisdiction over the matter. See Rivas v. Department of Navy, 0183066 (1983). The jurisdictional analysis is confused by the fact that the MSPB has jurisdiction over personnel actions, as defined by 5 USC 7512 for competitive service employees. If the Board otherwise has jurisdiction over an action, it may review an allegation of discrimination raised an affirmative defense. However, it has no general jurisdiction over allegations of discrimination. (The jurisdiction of the MSPB is beyond the scope of this book. For a thorough treatment of Board law, see Peter Broida, A Guide to Merit Systems Protection Board Law and Practice (Dewey Publications, Inc. 2023).)

Although the Statute would seem to apply in any instance where the formal complaint is filed with the wrong agency, that is not how the Commission interprets it. There is no substitute for reading the notice of final interview carefully. The EEOC has rejected the late filing of a formal complaint where the employee had filed the complaint with the Office of Personnel Management, but the notice of final interview clearly had stated where the complaint must be filed. Swidecki v. USPS, 01822256 (1983). Similarly, in Finley v. Postmaster General, 01A60931 (2006), the Commission found that the formal complaint was untimely filed where the complainant, within the 15-day time period, filed his complaint with the EEOC. On appeal, the Commission rejected an argument that the complainant had never learned to read properly and, thus, had misunderstood the notice of final interview, which plainly stated that the formal complaint should be filed with the Postal Service.

By contrast, in Thurman v. Attorney General, 0120080808 (2010), the Commission deemed a formal complaint filed with the EEOC itself instead of the employing agency was timely. The Commission determined that the complainant had been legitimately confused by instructions in the notice of final interview that related to her age discrimination claim. Although the notice did inform the complainant of the proper agency and address for filing an EEO complaint, it also contained instructions about how to file a civil action on her ADEA claim, which does not require administrative exhaustion of remedies. As a prerequisite to filing a civil action, the complainant must notify the EEOC in writing of his or her intent to file a civil action at least 30 days prior to filing the action. The Commission also noted that the complainant's confusion was likely increased by the fact that the address for filing the administrative complaint and the address for notifying the EEOC of intent to file were similar Washington, D.C. addresses. Because the complaint was timely filed with the Commission, it waived the time period for timely filing.

But in the absence of legitimate confusion, filing the formal complaint with the EEOC instead of the employing agency does not toll the time limit for filing the complaint. For example, in Pinckney v. Postmaster General, 0120114299 (2012), the complainant filed his formal complaint with the EEOC instead of the Postal Service. By the time EEOC forwarded the complaint to the agency, the time period for filing the formal complaint had expired. The Commission, in Pinckney, noted:

The Commission has previously held that when provided with the proper address, filing at the wrong address does not constitute a proper filing. See Pacheco v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05930700 (September 10, 1993) (appeal untimely when sent to wrong address despite receipt of proper instructions); Meggitt v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01A40408 (February 3, 2004) (above principle applied to a formal complaint that was untimely filed).

See also Complainant v. Social Security Administration, 0120151819 (2015) ("Even if the request for the hearing was viewed as the filing of the formal complaint, the Commission has previously held that when provided with the proper address, filing at the wrong address does not constitute a proper filing.").

An agency may not dismiss an EEO complaint for failure to cooperate because it has been filed with the wrong part of the agency. If the agency believes the complaint is properly processed by another part of the agency, the proper recourse is to transfer the complaint to that part of the agency. Kruckenberg v. Attorney General, 01985911 (2000) (filing of breach of settlement allegation with a subcomponent of the agency--INS--has the same effect as filing with agency itself); see also Boykin v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01975813 (1998); Capatosto v. Secretary of Defense, DLA, 01951373 (1996).

In Bull v. Secretary of Treasury, 01A02619 (2000), the Commission found that filing a complaint with the FAA Civil Rights Office instead of the Departmental Office of Civil Rights, as instructed in the notice of final interview, constituted constructive notice of the complaint on the part of the agency. Since the FAA filing was timely, the Commission reversed the agency's dismissal of the complaint.
