a. Alleging Harm and the Terms, Conditions and Privileges of Employment

The requirement that an employee allege harm because of an agency's discrimination does not mean the employee must allege she was treated less favorably than a comparative employee. Knight v. Postmaster General, 01971125 (1998). The issue of comparative employees goes to the actual merits of a complaint. For acceptance purposes, the only issue is whether the complainant has alleged harm, not whether the complainant can ultimately prove harm.

Most of the Commission's cases dealing with the issue of standing focus on whether the complainant has alleged some form of harm. Of course, the Commission does uphold the dismissal of cases because the complainant has failed to allege prohibited discrimination. There is little to be said about these latter cases, except that the complainant must allege in his or her complaint that the agency acted on the basis of discrimination prohibited by the statutes that the EEOC enforces. If the complainant sets forth such an allegation, that element of the standing requirement has been satisfied. Far more complex questions can arise about the allegation that the complainant has suffered some form of harm.

Although the Commission's regulations express the harm component of a claim in terms of whether the complainant is "aggrieved," the Commission and the courts often speak of whether the complainant has suffered an "adverse employment action" at the hands of the agency. Both the courts and the Commission have had great difficulty dealing with the array of injuries that are alleged by employees who believe they have been subject to discrimination or retaliation. Generally speaking, the Commission has been far more liberal than the courts in construing the adverse treatment requirement of the antidiscrimination statutes. Part of the difficulty in this area is that the antidiscrimination statutes not only prohibit employment actions based on discrimination, but also cover terms, conditions and privileges of employment.

The failure of the employee to receive a term, condition or privilege of employment is sufficient to aggrieve her for complaint purposes. For example, in Miller v. Secretary of Navy, 01971880 (1998), the Commission held that the employee was aggrieved by her failure to receive a coffee mug as an informal recognition of her accomplishments. Although the mug was not an official agency award, the Commission noted the agency admitted that mugs were given out and a list of the recipients was posted in the office. "The Commission finds that the agency's statements on appeal show that the 'mug' award is a term, condition, or privilege of appellant's employee. Whether or not the 'mug' award is 'official,' it is still an award given out by agency officials." Id. Similarly, in Farm v. Secretary of Energy, 01975235 (1998), the Commission held that use of an agency fitness facility was a privilege of employment. As a result, an allegation that there was a "peephole" into the women's locker room in the facility stated a claim for relief.

The Commission has drawn a distinction in the standing requirement for discrimination cases and retaliation cases. Noting that the antidiscrimination statutes prohibit discrimination in the terms, conditions and privileges of employment, but the anti-retaliation provisions prohibit any discriminatory treatment of an individual, the EEOC has taken an extremely liberal view of what constitutes an injury for purposes of stating a retaliation claim. See EEOC Compliance Manual, § 8-II(D)(3). This view has been upheld by the Supreme Court in Burlington Northern & Sante Fe Railway Co. v. White, 126 S. Ct. 2405 (2006). See Chapter 17, subsection on "Adverse Treatment Requirement."

With regard to the allegation that RO-1 solicited negative comments and written complaints, the Commission notes that, as the AJ suggested, increased scrutiny of an employee's performance following participation in protected activity may constitute an adverse action. See Francis v. American Telephone and Telegraph Co., Long Lines Department, 55 FRD 202 (D.D.C. 1972). In Francis, however, the employee presented evidence to demonstrate that immediately following her complaint of race discrimination, the agency embarked on an orchestrated campaign of intense scrutiny and supervision, culminating in the employee's removal. In this case, by contrast, the record shows that on one occasion when he became aware of a recurring problem with an activity under appellant's control, RO-1 solicited written comments from other persons affected by the problem, for the stated purpose of having documentation to show appellant when he discussed the matter with her. Moreover, there is no evidence that appellant was reprimanded on account of this incident and, contrary to appellant's contention on appeal, there is no evidence that this incident in any way affected her performance appraisal or otherwise disadvantaged appellant. Accordingly, the Commission finds that appellant has not established even a prima facie case of reprisal discrimination in this regard.

With respect to appellant's charge of harassment, the Commission notes that harassment of an employee that would not occur but for a prohibited discriminatory motive is unlawful if it is sufficiently patterned or pervasive. See McKinney v. Dole, 765 F.2d 1129, 1138-39 (D.C. Cir. 1985). However, in order for harassment to be considered conduct in violation of Title VII, it must be pervasive or severe enough to significantly and adversely affect the employee's emotional or psychological well-being. Hicks v. Gates Rubber Co., 833 F.2d 1406 (10th Cir. 1987). The conduct in question is evaluated from the standpoint of a reasonable person, taking into account the particular context in which it occurred. Highlander v. K.F.C. National Management Co., 805 F.2d 644 (6th Cir. 1986). The Commission notes that, unless the conduct is very severe, a single incident or group of isolated incidents will not be regarded as discriminatory harassment. Walker v. Ford Motor Co., 684 F.2d 1355, 1358 (11th Cir. 1982). In order to support a finding of a hostile work environment, more than a few isolated incidents of discriminatory enmity must have occurred. Hicks, supra, at 415. There must be a steady barrage of opprobrious comments and not a casual comment or accidental or sporadic conversation, in order to trigger equitable relief. Snell v. Suffolk Co., 782 F.2d 1094 (2d Cir. 1986).

In this case, the Commission finds that appellant has not established that she was harassed on the basis of reprisal. Not only do the incidents identified by appellant not appear "patterned and pervasive," but there is no evidence from which to infer that these incidents were motivated by reprisal. The only incident identified by appellant which appears to be related to her prior EEO activity is the incident in which RO-1 requested other persons to provide written documentation of the problem with the bid solicitations. While there is clear evidence in the record that RO-1 documented transactions involving appellant because of his history with her, which included prior EEO activity, this particular occurrence was a single incident, and the Commission does not find RO-1's behavior on that occasion to have been so severe as to constitute harassment.

Note that the Supreme Court in Harris v. Forklift Systems, Inc., 510 U.S. 17, 114 S. Ct. 367, 126 L. Ed.2d 295 (1993), a case involving sexual harassment, held that an employee alleging hostile environment harassment is not required to show severe psychological injury in order to prevail. See also the Commission's proposed Guidelines on Harassment Based on Race, Color, Religion, Gender, National Origin, Age, or Disability, 58 Fed. Reg. 51266 (October 1, 1993). Many courts that have considered the question of what constitutes a term or condition of employment have adopted the test of the Seventh Circuit Court of Appeals in Spring v. Sheboygan Area Sch. Dist., 865 F.2d 883 (7th Cir. 1989). Under the test, the employee must establish that the alleged injury constitutes a "material change in working conditions." In Spring, the court found the plaintiff, a school principal, failed to allege injury under Title VII where she was transferred from a position as principal of one school to one as principal of two schools, with an attendant raise and extension of her contract. The court held that the employee had not alleged she was harmed in any way by the transfer and that the change in location as to where the plaintiff performed her duties was not actionable under Title VII. See also Burger v. Central Apartment Management, 168 F.3d 875 (5th Cir. 1999) (denial of a request for a lateral transfer does not materially alter conditions of employment).

In Connell v. Bank of Boston, 924 F.2d 1169 (1st Cir. 1991), the First Circuit Court of Appeals applied the Spring test to a case under the ADEA alleging reprisal. In that case, the employee was informed he would be terminated on a certain date. He also was informed that he could continue to use his office to contact prospective employers. However, after receiving a letter from the employee's lawyer, the defendant ordered the plaintiff to vacate his office immediately. The plaintiff received full pay and benefits through the date of termination. The First Circuit found the plaintiff had failed to allege that the employer's actions harmed him in a way that constituted a "material change in working conditions." But see Russell v. Secretary of Navy, 01A03207 (2000) (the allocation of office space is a condition or privilege of employment); Schneider v. Secretary of Navy, 01976252 (1998) (holding that an allegation that a former employee was not allowed to use the agency office to seek new employment states a claim for relief).

However, the court in Connell based its decision, in part, on the opinion of the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia in Passer v. American Chemical Society, 701 F. Supp. 1 (D.D.C. 1988). In that case, the court found the plaintiff, who was terminated, failed to allege any harm when he contended that his employer canceled a testimonial after he filed an EEO complaint. But, in Passer v. American Chemical Society, 935 F.2d 322 (D.C. Cir. 1991), the D.C. Circuit reversed that decision and found that the former employee had alleged an injury that was actionable under the ADEA. See also Randlett v. Shalala, 118 F.3d 857 (1st Cir. 1997) (the retaliatory refusal to grant a hardship transfer is actionable). As noted above, the Supreme Court, in Burlington Northern, supra, adopted a liberal standard of injury under the retaliation clause and held that conduct which would likely deter a reasonable person from filing or pursuing a complaint is actionable. Failure to state a claim based on retaliation is discussed more fully below. See also "Standing in the Federal Courts" in this chapter.
