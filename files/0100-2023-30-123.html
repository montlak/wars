4. Documenting/Proving Out of Pocket Expenses

Claims for pecuniary damages must be properly documented. Failure to document the claimed expenses will result in a denial of pecuniary damages. See, e.g., Jones v. Secretary of Commerce, 01A13671 (2002). The Commission denied the complainant compensatory damages in Lam v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01961589 (1998), stressing the importance of careful documentation of costs:

Appellant did not provide copies of her telephone bills or identify to whom or why calls were made, she did not explain why a word processor and fax machine were necessary, and she did not describe where, when, and why she expended her travel. Therefore, we find that the agency properly denied appellant's claims for reimbursement of telephone expenses, office equipment, and travel costs.

See also Almera v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A13618 (2002); Darrell v. Secretary of Navy, 05A10286 (2001) ("[T]he absence of receipts and verified records of amounts paid and amounts reimbursed defeats [a] claim for pecuniary damages.").

However, the mere presentation of receipts for services rendered after the discriminatory event does not entitle the complainant to an award of pecuniary damages. In addition, the complainant must present evidence that there is a causal connection between the services rendered and the discrimination. Gordon v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01997073 (2002). In Johnson v. Postmaster General, 0120054278 (2007), the Commission upheld an award of $40,000 in nonpecuniary damages while, at the same time, upholding the decision of the administrative judge not to award any past pecuniary damages because the complainant failed to demonstrate a nexus between the claimed damages and the discrimination. The Commission noted that a number of the claimed expenses appeared to relate to a workplace incident that involved chemical exposure and was not the subject of the EEO complaint.

Similarly, in Bostick v. Secretary of Army, 0120093611 (2010), the Commission awarded $2,250 in past pecuniary damages for medical treatment while denying slightly over $20,000 additionally requested by the complainant. The distinction was that the $2,250 was documented by an actual bill for services from the health care provider. With respect to the remaining $20,000, the complainant presented only a letter from a "patient administrator" of a "behavioral sciences team" stating that the complainant had received some $19,950 in treatment over a three-year period. The Commission noted, in denying the additional damages, that there was "no bill or itemized list of dates of services and costs associated with any visits."

In Richards v. Postmaster General, 01A20471 (2003), the Commission found that a pecuniary damage award can be properly based on a combination of documentation and the complainant's testimony. In increasing the award beyond that of the documented medical expenses in Richards, the Commission found:

As noted above, the AJ determined that complainant was entitled to "$320.00," the total of her cancelled checks, plus the "$480.00" balance reflected on the physician's invoice, which totals $800.00 for physician visits. However, the AJ also acknowledges that complainant presented un-rebutted testimony that she visited this physician twice per month, from August 1995 to the present (August 10, 2001 is the date of the hearing and decision on compensatory damages), all discrimination related, and that exclusive of two visits incurring only $35.00 charges each, these visits cost complainant $40.00 each.

Therefore, notwithstanding the lack of documentation to confirm each individual expenditure, we find that complainant's testimony regarding the frequency and duration of her physician visits, and that documentation which she did submit establishing the per visit cost of these visits, demonstrates that complainant's award is properly calculated by multiplying the total number of complainant's office visits by the cost of each visit. Moreover, we find that the record confirms that complainant's office visits covered a six year period, for a total of 72 months, such that her twice per month visits results in a total of 144 visits. Multiplying this figure by $40.00 (the cost per visit), minus $10.00 (to factor in the two visits costing only $35.00), we find that the proper award of pecuniary damages associated with complainant's physician visits is $5,750.00.

Accordingly, we find that the AJ and the agency erred in findings that complainant's award for pecuniary damages associated with her physician visits was only $800.00, and that complainant should instead be awarded $5,750.00 as a remedy.

Although the case is not entirely clear, it appears that past pecuniary damages cannot be based solely on the complainant's testimony without some corroboration in terms of documentation, but an award can be based on the testimony of a health care professional. In Green v. Social Security Administration, 01A22077 (2003), the Commission awarded $380 in past pecuniary damages based on the report of a social worker indicating she had seen the complainant four times and charged $95 per session. However, in the same case, the Commission only awarded $195 in future pecuniary damages because there was no specific indication of how long treatment would continue. See also Valentine v. Attorney General, 07A30098 (2003) (overturning administrative judge's award of past pecuniary damages based solely on the complainant's testimony). By contrast, in Villanueva v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 01A34968 (2006), the Commission awarded $1,680 in past pecuniary damages based on the testimony of the complainant's psychiatrist that he saw the complainant for 15 hours at the rate of $112 per hour.

In Glockner v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 07A30105 (2004), a case in which the Commission awarded $200,000 in nonpecuniary damages, it also found that the complainant should be given a chance to substantiate her claimed pecuniary damages of $7,233. The Commission held the complainant could be awarded pecuniary damages up to that amount to the extent that she could submit objective evidence of her claims. In so doing, the Commission noted that there was no dispute that the complainant had undergone the procedures on which the claim rested.

But the failure to properly document pecuniary damages does not bar an award for emotional distress damages. Id. But see Gause v. Social Security Administration, 01972427 (2000) (an award of $100 in moving expenses was approved without documentation because of the relatively small amount).
