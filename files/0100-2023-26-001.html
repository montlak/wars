CHAPTER 17

REPRISAL

It is a protected activity for an employee to file an EEO complaint alleging discrimination on any basis under Title VII, the ADEA, the Rehabilitation Act, the EPA, or GINA, or to participate otherwise in the EEO process, or for an employee to oppose any discriminatory employment policy or practice of an agency. This protection also extends to agency officials who are involved in complaint processing. Some federal courts had held that reprisal against federal employees was not prohibited by the ADEA, but that was not a position that has been embraced by the EEOC. In Gomez-Perez v. Potter, 128 S. Ct. 1931 (2008), the Supreme Court found that protection from reprisal is contained in the general prohibition on age discrimination in the federal sector. See subsection on "Reprisal" in Chapter 16.

The Commission's previous regulations under Part 1613, at 29 CFR 1613.261, provided:

It is unlawful to restrain, interfere, coerce or discriminate against complainants, their representatives, witnesses, Directors of Equal Employment Opportunity, Equal Employment Opportunity Officers, Investigators, Counselors and other agency officials with responsibility for processing discrimination complaints because of involvement with a discrimination charge during any stage in the presentation and processing of a complaint, including the counseling stage under § 1613.213, or because an individual filed a charge of discrimination, testified, assisted or participated in any manner with an investigation, proceeding or hearing or because of any opposition to an unlawful employment practice under this part.

Part 1614 is briefer and more to the point. Specifically, 29 CFR 1614.101(b) states:

No person shall be subject to retaliation for opposing any practice made unlawful by title VII of the Civil Rights Act (title VII) (42 USC 2000e et seq.), the Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA) (29 USC 621 et seq.), the Equal Pay Act (29 USC 206(d)) or the Rehabilitation Act (29 USC 791 et seq.) or for participating in any stage of administrative or judicial proceedings under those statutes.

Under 29 CFR 1614.105(g), EEO counselors are specifically warned against restraining an individual from filing a complaint.

Note that the regulation has not been amended to include GINA, but the Commission's regulations under that statute at 29 CFR 1635.7 provide that "[a] covered entity may not discriminate against any individual because such individual has opposed any act or practice made unlawful by this title or because such individual made a charge, testified, assisted, or participated in any manner in an investigation, proceeding, or hearing under this title."

The protection for participation in EEO proceedings and opposition to unlawful employment practices is broad in scope and should be interpreted liberally in its application if employees are to exercise their right to a workplace free from discrimination. Any doubt about this proposition was erased by the Supreme Court in Burlington Northern & Sante Fe Railway Co. v. White, 126 S. Ct. 2405 (2006). See subsection on "Adverse Treatment Requirement," below.

For several years running now, reprisal complaints have topped the list of federal sector EEO complaints in terms of the basis alleged. While many in the federal sector see this as evidence that the Commission is too liberal in its treatment of reprisal cases and that, in turn, encourages employees who have previously engaged in EEO activity to file reprisal complaints whenever something they don't like happens in the workplace, there remains a good reason why the right to engage in EEO activity has been vigorously protected by both the EEOC and the Supreme Court. One of the primary objectives of the antidiscrimination statutes is to identify prohibited workplace discrimination when it occurs, provide remedies to the victims, and to prevent discrimination from happening in the future. When Title VII was enacted and long before the EEOC became responsible for federal sector discrimination complaints, Congress recognized that the government, acting alone, could not enforce the law. Yes, the EEOC could investigate and even prosecute when it so chose complaints of employment discrimination in the private sector. But in order to do so, the Commission had to have a mechanism for learning of alleged employment discrimination. Giving employees and applicants for employment the right to file complaints and to provide protection for those who exercised that right provided the mechanism.

Complaints of reprisal are not at all uncommon and any agency that makes a decision adverse to an employee, subsequent to some protected activity by that employee, runs the risk that a complaint of reprisal will follow. The solution here is not to restrict the right of employee to file complaints or weaken the protections for doing so. The solution for agencies, in most cases, is relatively simple. Document instances of poor performance and misconduct when they occur. Nothing in the law permits the employee who legitimately exhibits poor performance or engages in misconduct to use the reprisal provisions of the antidiscrimination laws as a shield from performance-based actions or discipline. However, failure to contemporaneously document instances of poor performance and misconduct can lead to a finding of pretext when they are raised as legitimate, nonretaliatory reasons subsequent to the filing of a complaint.

A hostile environment claim based on reprisal, as with other bases of discrimination, is actionable. See, e.g., Patrick v. Postmaster General, 01A05642 (2000). Harassment based on reprisal is discussed in Chapter 12 under the heading "Reprisal."

As with any other prohibited basis, a claim of retaliation can be made in the alternative; that is, an employee can allege that a certain personnel action is based on Title VII discrimination or retaliation. But, the Commission held that claims of reprisal may not be combined with other bases of discrimination. See Freeman v. Secretary of Labor, 01940775 (1994) (holding that a complainant could not establish a prima facie case of discrimination based on race and reprisal). The complainant in Freeman argued that the agency discriminated against blacks who spoke out about agency practices. The Commission's only rationale for denying the subclass was that it could find "no authority for extending the scope of subclass coverage to 'black employees who participate in the EEO process.'" Id. Although the Commission has not specifically addressed the issue of combining retaliation with other bases of discrimination since the Freeman decision, its rationale in that decision is less than compelling. Indeed, the Commission allows other bases of discrimination to be combined to create subclasses, and offers no persuasive reason why reprisal should be excluded from this practice. See, e.g., "'Sex Plus' Discrimination," Chapter 13.

In August 2016, the Commission issued Enforcement Guidance on Retaliation and Related Issues, No. 915.004, replacing the Commission's 1998 Compliance Manual Section 8: Retaliation, EEOC Directive 915.003. The guidance is available on the Commission's website and provides significant instruction on reprisal cases.

Retaliation under Title VII is prohibited by 42 USC 2000e-3, which provides:

It shall be an unlawful employment practice for an employer to discriminate against any of his employees or applicants for employment...because he has opposed any practice made an unlawful employment practice by this subchapter, or because he has made a charge, testified, assisted, or participated in any manner in an investigation, proceeding, or hearing under this subchapter.

The retaliation provision is different from the substantive provision of Title VII that prohibits discrimination on the basis of race, color, national origin, religion, or sex. That provision, 42 USC 2000e-2, provides:

It shall be an unlawful employment practice for an employer to fail or refuse to hire or to discharge any individual, or otherwise to discriminate against any individual with respect to his compensation, terms, conditions, or privileges of employment, because of such individual's race, color, religion, sex or national origin.

Though subtle, the difference in the provisions is that the substantive provision of Title VII is directed to employment actions and the terms, conditions, and privileges of employment. The retaliation provision contains no such qualifiers and prohibits any discrimination. A similar distinction is made under the ADEA. Cf. 29 USC 623(a) and (d). In the federal courts, there was a split of opinion as to whether retaliation under the ADEA is prohibited in the federal sector. The federal government was not included in the oriGINAl version of the ADEA as an employer and the statutory prohibition against age discrimination came about as the result of an amendment to the ADEA. In passing the amendment, 29 USC 633a, Congress did not specifically mention retaliation or reprisal and subsection (f) of the provision excludes the federal government from all other sections of the ADEA with one exception that is not relevant to the issue of reprisal. In Gomez-Perez v. Potter, 476 F.3d 54 (1st Cir. 2007), the court found that the ADEA did not prohibit retaliation against federal employees under the ADEA. The Supreme Court granted certiorari and in Gomez-Perez v. Potter, 128 S. Ct. 1931(2008), found that the general prohibition against discrimination in the federal sector included protection against retaliation. See discussion in Chapter 16.

In University of Texas Southwestern Medical Center v. Nassar, 133 S. Ct. 2517 (2013), the Supreme Court, in a private sector retaliation case, held that a "but for" test applies to the plaintiff's burden of proof and a mixed motive analysis is not applicable. In Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0720120041 (2015), the Commission held that Nassar does not apply to federal sector cases. See section on "Nassar and the 'But For' Test," below.

As discussed more fully below in the section on "Adverse Treatment Requirement," the Commission has found the difference in the wording of the two sections to be significant and has concluded that the retaliation provision is broader in its sweep than the antidiscrimination provision.
