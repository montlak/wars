5. Misconduct and Performance Cases

Under the ADA, an employee with a disability is held to the same standards of conduct and performance as employees who do not have disabilities. In a disability discrimination case based on misconduct, the issue is not whether the employee's misconduct was caused by his disability, but whether he was treated less favorably than similarly situated employees without disabilities who committed similar offenses. See, e.g., Shoemaker v. Postmaster General, 01880332 (1988).

Similarly, an agency is not required to provide "reasonable accommodation" for misconduct or poor performance by excusing it. See, e.g., Complainant v. Pension Benefit Guaranty Corp., 0120120153 (2014) ("To the extent that Complainant is arguing that her behavior resulted from the frustration that she experienced while requesting reasonable accommodation, we note that such frustration does not excuse Complainant's behavior."). See also Complainant v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120123065 (2015) ("To the extent Complainant alleges that he was being denied a reasonable accommodation in connection with his suspension, the Agency is not required to excuse past misconduct as a reasonable accommodation, even if that misconduct is the result of the individual's disability.").

In Gallagher v. Secretary of Air Force, 03A10007 (2002), in upholding a decision of the MSPB to sustain the complainant's removal, the Commission explained:

The Commission assumes for purposes of analysis that petitioner is an individual with a disability. We, however, find that the agency did not discriminate against her based on disability when it terminated her employment. The agency does not have to excuse misconduct that is job-related and consistent with business necessity by an employee with a disability that it would not allow by an employee without a disability. Enforcement Guidance on the Americans with Disabilities Act and Psychiatric Disabilities, No. 915.002 (March 25, 1997). Petitioner was in a supervisory position and she disregarded management's progressive discipline regarding her charge card delinquency and led management to believe that its allegations against her were inaccurate. The agency's table of penalties allows for a reprimand for failure to honor valid debts and for a reprimand to removal for deliberate misrepresentation. The agency argued that petitioner's actions were different from the misconduct that "failure to honor valid debts" was intended to cover in that her debt arose with her employer, rather than a private creditor. The agency contended that petitioner's misconduct constitutes damage to property (abuse of a government program); which allows for a reprimand for the first offense, a reprimand to a five-day suspension for the second offense, and a reprimand to a removal for the third offense. This is the progressive discipline that the agency chose to render. In addition, the guidance for compliance with the government credit card program stated that delinquency would not be tolerated, and that payments were due upon receipt of monthly statements. Also, petitioner signed an agreement of understanding acknowledging that she could be disciplined for abusing the charge card program. The agency indicated that there were no civilian employees to whom to compare petitioner and that military persons were disciplined appropriately.

See also Rowland v. Postmaster General, 01A43211 (2004) ("[A]n agency never has to excuse a violation of a uniformly applied conduct rule that is job related and consistent with business necessity even when the rule is violated by an individual with a disability if the agency would impose the same discipline on an employee without a disability."); Richardson v. Postmaster General, 01A02177 (2003) (agency not required to excuse complainant's cursing at supervisor as reasonable accommodation). The Commission came to a similar conclusion in Reith v. Secretary of Navy, 01A55857 (2006), where the complainant wrote a letter indicating he was "very mad" and threatening to harm himself after not being selected for a position. The agency sent an investigator to interview the complainant and, after the interview, the complainant drove his car toward the investigator forcing him to jump between two other cars to avoid being hit. The Commission upheld the termination of the complainant on the basis that the Rehabilitation Act "does not require employers to excuse a violation of a uniformly applied conduct rule that is job--related and consistent with business necessity." Id.

Cases involving allegations of misconduct or poor performance can be confusing. In some cases, the complainant may be alleging a failure to reasonably accommodate a disability. In other cases, the complainant may be alleging that he or she, because of a disability, has been treated less favorably than similarly situated employees without a disability. In still other cases, it is possible that the complainant is alleging both a failure to accommodate and disparate treatment. Proper analysis of the cases requires a clear understanding of precisely what the complainant is alleging.

Requests for reasonable accommodation are always prospective and do not require an agency to excuse past misconduct. See, e.g., Sabados v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 01A23952 (2004) (holding that the agency was justified in giving chemistry superintendent who tested positive for alcohol while on an emergency call a choice between resignation and termination where the agency had no prior notice of the employee's alcoholism).

As a corollary, the failure of an agency to provide reasonable accommodation does not excuse misconduct. In Degnan v. Postmaster General, 01A53689 (2006), the agency was aware that the complainant was hearing--impaired and did occasionally provide accommodation in the form of an interpreter, primarily for safety--related job discussions. However, on one occasion, two supervisors approached the complainant without an interpreter and began discussing work practices. The complainant became agitated. He became "belligerent and boisterous," made an obscene gesture and told the supervisors to "go fuck themselves," verbally attacked two coworkers and slammed equipment in the work area. The Commission found that the agency failed to accommodate the complainant by not providing an interpreter for the work discussion, but also found no discrimination in the complainant's subsequent removal for misconduct.

The case of Suppi v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120072665 (2007), demonstrates how both principles can operate. In that case, the complainant alleged disability discrimination when she was terminated after urinating in a bucket while a patient was under anesthesia and undergoing surgery. One witness testified that she appeared disoriented and confused after the incident. There were also other incidents of bizarre behavior. The complainant alleged that the agency failed to accommodate her bipolar disorder and that the agency failed to present a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for her termination. After assuming that the complainant was a qualified individual with a disability, the Commission, in Suppi, went on to find:

According to the agency, complainant's conduct and performance deficiencies were both considerations in the separation decision. First, we address the conduct issues. Even assuming complainant's alleged bizarre/inappropriate conduct (including, but not limited to the July 2, 2004 urination incident) was caused by her disability, an employer may discipline an employee with a disability for engaging in misconduct if it would impose the same discipline on an employee without a disability. See EEOC Enforcement Guidance on the Americans with Disabilities Act and Psychiatric Disabilities, Notice 915.002, at Question 30 (March 25, 1997).

Next, even assuming complainant did not have conduct problems which would warrant her termination, the agency also states that complainant had performance problems. In the Board's Notice, there is a list of "Supplemental Factors considered by the Board." One of the factors is that complainant's coworkers stated that complainant often took excessively long lunch breaks and had long, unaccounted for absences from the Operating Room that made it problematic to plan surgical case scheduling, and that complainant made inappropriate comments that affected her professional relationship with coworkers.

Although complainant argues that the agency had an obligation to provide her with reasonable accommodation instead of terminating her employment, the record reveals that complainant did not request reasonable accommodation until after the performance deficiencies and conduct violations had already occurred. An agency cannot be held responsible for providing reasonable accommodation which it did not know was needed. The Commission's Enforcement Guidance on the Americans with Disabilities Act and Psychiatric Disabilities at Question 31 states that reasonable accommodation is prospective and employers are not required to excuse past misconduct. Here, the agency's reasons for refusing to place her back in her regular position in October 2004, and for ultimately terminating her in January 2005, are legitimate (because they are based on incidents occurring before complainant requested reasonable accommodation), and complainant has not presented evidence that the agency's actions were based on discriminatory animus.

When the agency has knowledge of a medical condition and the possible need for accommodation prior to discipline, it may have to demonstrate that it would have taken any subsequent action for misconduct even absent the disability. For example, in Clark v. Secretary of Air Force, 01A52900 (2006), the complainant advised his supervisor that he had bipolar and obsessive compulsive disorders and that his absences were being caused those disorders. The agency made no attempt to accommodate the complainant and, subsequently, he was suspended for five days for making inappropriate comments, failing to follow established leave procedures and unauthorized absences from work. The Commission, after finding that the record was not sufficiently developed to determine if the complainant was entitled to reasonable accommodation, remanded the case noting that, if the complainant was a qualified individual with a disability, the agency would have to demonstrate that it would have suspended him for five days for making inappropriate comments.
