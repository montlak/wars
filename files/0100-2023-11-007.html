C. DETERMINING THE PERIOD FOR INITIATING COUNSELING

In most cases, when the complainant initiates precomplaint counseling, particularly if the complaint involves a personnel action or other discrete event, it is easy to determine if counseling was requested within the applicable 45-day period. However, when a complainant initiates precomplaint counseling beyond the regulatory time period, it is incumbent upon the counselor to inquire into the reasons for delay. "Such an inquiry is necessary to evaluate the suitability of an equitable extension of the applicable time limits." Morris v. Postmaster General, 01932460 (1993).

Moreover, an agency errs in dismissing a complaint for failure to cooperate when it already has adequate information in its possession to determine that contact with the counselor was timely. Przygoda v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 01940900 (1994). In that case, the agency sent a request to the complainant for additional information explaining why he contacted a counselor more than 45 days after the selection at issue. The agency advised the complainant that it would dismiss his complaint for failure to prosecute if he did not respond within 15 days of receipt. Although the complainant did respond, it was beyond the 15-day period since he was on a travel assignment at the time the notice was delivered to his home. In reversing the dismissal, the Commission noted that the complainant had provided sufficient information at the time the complaint was dismissed for the agency to determine that the complainant contacted a counselor within 45 days of when he had a reasonable suspicion that his nonselection was discriminatory.

Simply contacting an EEO counselor within the requisite time period is not sufficient; the complainant must contact the counselor and exhibit an intent to pursue a complaint of discrimination. However, when an agency dismisses a complaint for failure to exhibit the requisite intent, the burden is on the agency to prove lack of intent and it must sufficiently develop the evidence on that issue. As the Commission explained in Galaviz v. Small Business Administration, 01993493 (2000):

It appears that complainant did not reasonably suspect discrimination until August 14, 1998, when he learned that someone else was hired for the position previously offered to him. Therefore, in order to be timely, complainant had to initiate EEO contact by September 28, 1998, at the latest. In the present case, complainant states on appeal that he initiated EEO contact on September 17, 1998; the EEO Counselor's Report indicates that complainant contacted the EEO Office on September 23, 1998; however, he did not exhibit an intent to pursue a discrimination complaint at that time. According to the agency, complainant did not initiate EEO contact with an intent to pursue an EEO complaint until September 30, 1998. We note, however, that there is no evidence to show that complainant failed to exhibit the requisite intent to pursue an EEO complaint on September 23, 1998, when, according to the EEO Counselor's Report, he first contacted the EEO Office. Indeed, the purported cancellation of EEO counseling and its resumption only two days later, would seem to indicate that complainant intended to pursue an EEO complaint when he first went to the EEO Office on September 23, 1998. Where, as here, there is an issue of timeliness, "[a]n agency always bears the burden of obtaining sufficient information to support a reasoned determination as to timeliness." Guy v. Department of Energy, EEOC Request No. 05930703 (January 4, 1994) (quoting Williams v. Department of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05920506 (August 25, 1992)). In addition, in Ericson v. Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05920623 (January 14, 1993), the Commission stated that the agency has the burden of providing evidence and/or proof to support its final decisions. See Gens v. Department of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05910837 (January 31, 1992). Therefore, we find that the agency failed to show that complainant's EEO Counselor contact was untimely and that the agency's dismissal was improper.
