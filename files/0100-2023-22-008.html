C. MARITAL STATUS AND OTHER ASSOCIATIONS

Title VII does not prohibit discrimination on the basis of marital status alone. A complainant's "marital/parental status" is also not covered. Darius C. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120171085 (2017) (divorced). But see 5 USC 7702; 5 USC 2302(b). However, complaints involving the race of a spouse must be analyzed to determine correctly whether an issue within the province of Title VII has been raised. In Forsythe v. Department of State, 01871674 (1987), the Commission reversed the rejection of a complaint alleging discrimination against a white employee because of his marriage to a black woman:

[I]t is clear that the gist of appellant's claim is that the alleged prejudice of the agency is directed against him because he is of the Caucasian race and chose to marry a black woman. Implicit in appellant's claim is that, if he were black and married a black woman, the agency would not have discriminated against him and his job opportunity would not have been restricted. Accordingly, such a claim has been construed as an allegation of discrimination directed primarily against the complainant because of his race and only collaterally against the race of his wife. Cf. Miller v. Canteen Food and Vending, Inc., 11 F.E.P. Cases 773 (Cir. Ct. Wayne Cty. Mich. 1975) (an allegation that a Caucasian woman's employment was terminated because she was married to a black man is within the purview of the Michigan Fair Employment Practices Act).

Similarly, federal courts have held that a claim that a Caucasian's employment was adversely affected due to marriage of a black constitutes an allegation within the purview of Title VII. See Holiday v. Belle's Restaurant, 409 F. Supp. 904 (D.C. Pa. 1976); Gresham v. Waffle House, Inc., 585 F. Supp. 1442 (N.D. Ga. 1984).

See also Rogers v. Postmaster General, 01971950 (1998) (discrimination found where white male employee married to black female was given bad evaluation to prevent transfer).

The Commission in Alexandra L. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 2020000360 (2021), similarly found that a coworker's comments to the complainant (Caucasian) were sufficiently severe to rise to the level of harassment based on the complainant's association with her African-American husband:

Complainant stated that a coworker asked her if she knew what they called "Brazil nuts" and then stated that they are called "N...r toes." Complainant claimed that the coworker then told her to ask her husband because he would know what she was talking about. Complainant said that she did not respond at first because she was shocked that the coworker said this to her because the coworker had met her husband in the past. Complainant stated that she told the coworker that she did not think her comment was funny.

...

The record reflects that Complainant, Caucasian, stated that she has an African-American husband and she found her coworker's joke hurtful and offensive. Complainant belonged to a statutorily protected class and she clearly was subjected to harassment in the form of unwelcome verbal conduct that involved and was based on her race association with her husband. The nature of the remark was of such magnitude that it created an intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment for Complainant.

However, Title VII's prohibition against race and color discrimination reaches farther than just marital status. It can include any association if employment decisions are made on that basis. The Technical Assistance Manual, "Race & Color Discrimination," at p. 31 explains:

The law also prohibits discrimination against individuals because of their association with members of a particular race. Specifically, an employer may not discriminate against an individual because of the race of the people the individual associates with in his or her family, professional, social or other circles.

Because the purpose of Title VII is to create a work environment that is free of any racial taint, an employer that allows race to enter into its employment decision-making, through an employee's association with members of a particular race, violates Title VII.

Example: For six months, William, who is White, has worked at a car dealership, receiving praise from his supervisor Scott (who is also White) for his job performance. William's family then visits him at work, at which Scott learns that William's spouse is African-American. Two weeks later, William overhears Scott worrying that the dealership's image will suffer if the public learns of William's interracial marriage. Later that month, Scott fires William. If the discharge was motivated, at least in part, by William's interracial marriage, Scott has discriminated against William based on race, and the dealership has violated the law.

Note that a Title VII claimant is not required to prove that race or color was the sole factor in an employment action. Rather, race or color need only be a substantial factor. See, e.g., Phillips v. Martin Marietta Corp., 400 U.S. 542, 544 (1971).
