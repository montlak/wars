7. Annual Hearing Tests

In Mills v. Secretary of Navy, 01995665 (2002), the Commission found that it did not violate the Rehabilitation Act when an employee who failed an annual hearing test was required to undergo a second test. In Mills, the Commission found:

Generally, a disability-related inquiry or medical examination of an employee may be job-related and consistent with business necessity when an employer has a reasonable belief, based on objective evidence, that: (1) an employee's ability to perform essential job functions will be impaired by a medical condition; or (2) an employee may pose a direct threat due to a medical condition. See Disability-Related Inquiries Guidance, question 5. In addition, periodic medical examinations and other monitoring under specific circumstances may be job-related and consistent with business necessity. For example, an employer may require employees to submit to medical examinations that are mandated or necessitated by another federal law or regulation, such as the Occupational Safety and Health Act. See id., question 21; see also Appendix to part 1630--Interpretive Guidance on Title I of the Americans with Disabilities Act, 1630.14(d) (Interpretive Guidance).

Here, complainant was required to have his hearing tested annually under the agency's Hearing Conservation Program (HCP). In keeping with the Occupational Safety and Health Act, components of the Department of Defense, including the Navy, are required to create and conduct a comprehensive Hearing Conservation Program to protect all military and civilian personnel. This program requires employees who work around loud noises to undergo annual hearing tests. See DOD Hearing Conservation Program, Department of Defense Instruction No. 6055.12 (April 22, 1996) (DODI).

The hearing test conducted by the Navy is designed to determine if employees have experienced any hearing loss and, as such, it is a "medical examination" subject to the requirements of the Rehabilitation Act. The record establishes, however, that the test is job-related and consistent with business necessity, as it is necessitated by the Occupational Safety and Health Act. See Disability-Related Inquiries Guidance, at question 21.

The record also establishes that all employees at the agency who work, as complainant does, around loud noises are required to undergo these hearing tests. The Occupational Hearing Conservation Technician (OHCP) who conducted the hearing test in question testified that when a test establishes that an individual has hearing outside of the acceptable range, they are told to come back for a retest after being "noise free" for fifteen consecutive hours. If at that time, they still have a hearing loss, OHCP advises them to come in again after being "noise free" for forty hours. If at this point, the test reveals a hearing loss, OHCP sets up an appointment with an audiologist. OHCP testified that an October 1997 hearing test revealed that complainant's hearing was not in the acceptable range. OHCP told complainant to return for the retest, but when complainant returned OHCP discovered that he had a very bad ear infection and "holes in his ear drums." OHCP advised complainant that the retest could not be conducted and that complainant should seek treatment from his physician.

S1 testified that at some point after this, he learned that complainant failed to attend the mandatory hearing test and therefore called OHCP to find out what happened. S1 noted that OHCP told him that complainant had taken the test, but had failed. S1 briefed his supervisor on the situation and then advised complainant to set up another appointment with OHCP for further tests. Because requiring employees to undergo periodic medical examinations that are mandated or necessitated by the Occupational Safety and Health Act is job-related and consistent with business necessity, the fact that complainant was required to have his hearing tested and ordered to undergo a retest when he failed the initial test is not a violation of the Rehabilitation Act. Accordingly, the agency's finding that complainant was not subjected to discrimination when he was ordered to undergo a hearing test is AFFIRMED.
