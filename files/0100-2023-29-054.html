2. Consideration by EEOC

When presented with a petition to review an MSPB decision on allegations of discrimination, the Commission must determine first whether to review the Board decision and issue its own decision. See 29 CFR 1614.305(a). An agency may file opposition to a petition for consideration by the Commission. An opposition must be filed within 15 days of receipt of the original petition by the Commission. Id.

The Commission must issue its decision on review of the Board decision within 30 days of receiving the petition for review. A decision of the Commission not to review a Board decision is not admissible as evidence in any subsequent judicial proceeding on the allegations of discrimination. See 29 CFR 1614.305(b).

The process after the Commission decides to review a Board decision is set forth at 29 CFR 1614.305(c):

If the Commission makes a determination to consider the decision, the Commission shall within sixty (60) calendar days after the date of its determination, consider the entire record of the proceedings of the Merit Systems Protection Board and on the basis of the evidentiary record before the Board as supplemented in accordance with paragraph (d) of this section, either:

(1) Concur in the decision of the MSPB, or

(2) Issue in writing a decision which differs from the decision of the MSPB to the extent that the Commission finds that, as a matter of law:

(i) The decision of the MSPB constitutes an incorrect interpretation of any provision of any law, rule, regulation, or policy directive referred to in 5 USC 7702(a)(1)(B), or

(ii) The decision involving such provision is not supported by the evidence in the record as a whole.

Subsection (d) referred to above permits the Commission to remand the case to the Board for the taking of additional evidence, or to take additional evidence on its own, if the Commission determines it is necessary. The taking of additional evidence does not extend the time period for Commission review and a decision still must be issued within 60 calendar days.

The Commission has no jurisdiction over a complaint that the agency filed a petition for review of an MSPB decision reinstating appellant, even when it is alleged that the PFR was filed for discriminatory reasons to delay the reinstatement of appellant. Torres v. Department of Navy, 01821712 (1983). The proper forum to raise such allegations is in the response to the agency's PFR before the Board.

The Commission may only review MSPB decisions on issues of discrimination. "The Commission may differ with the decision of the MSPB to the extent that the Commission finds, as a matter of law, that the Board's finding of no...discrimination constitutes an incorrect interpretation of law, rule, regulation or policy directive, or the decision is not supported by the evidence of the record as a whole." Hand v. Department of Army, 03910126 (1991).

The EEOC also has no jurisdiction to review a decision of the MSPB that has not included a ruling on the merits of a discrimination claim and it may review only that portion of the decision that addresses the claim of discrimination. See Hatler v. Department of Air Force, 03820034 (1983) (Commission lacked jurisdiction to review a decision of the Board dismissing the appellant's complaint on the ground that the agency rescinded its actions and granted appellant back pay).

Only final decisions of the MSPB may be reviewed by the Commission. When a petition for review of an initial decision is pending before the Board, an appeal to the EEOC is premature. Banks v. Department of Navy, 03830074 (1984). But note that the Board procedures provide that an initial decision becomes final 35 days after issuance if no petition for review is filed. An appellant may allow an initial decision to become final in this manner and then seek review by the EEOC.

As noted above, the MSPB follows the unfortunate practice of tape recording its administrative hearings and does not provide the parties, or even itself, with written transcripts of the hearing. In addition to being cumbersome--most of us can read faster than we can listen--problems can arise when the tapes are inaudible. In Barrowclough v. Department of Navy, 03870181 (1987), the Commission denied a petition for review of a Board decision because the tapes were inaudible and Commission review "based only on the present, partial record would not be equitable or appropriate." The Commission noted that its denial of consideration should not be construed as a determination on the merits in any subsequent judicial proceeding.
