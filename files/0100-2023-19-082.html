I. EVIDENCE OF BIAS

As discussed below, in order to constitute direct evidence of discrimination, the evidence must link discriminatory animus to the disputed personnel action. Evidence of bias is not, in the legal sense, direct evidence of discrimination. Of course, it can be considered along with other evidence of pretext in determining motive.

For example, in Perry v. Secretary of Transportation, 01961849 (1998), evidence that the selecting official favored Hispanics was not considered direct evidence of discrimination where there was no further evidence linking that favoritism to the disputed selection. As the Commission in Perry explained:

[W]e note that there is evidence that the RMO did treat Hispanic employees favorably. However, even though such evidence of favoritism by RMO may lead to the perception that he treated employees unequally, such favoritism and poor management do not conclusively establish discrimination when there is ample evidence to support the agency's decision in this case. . . .

Similarly, in Klover v. Secretary of Defense, 01A53416 (2005), the Commission found a tendency by a school principal to view males' teaching styles as harsher had no nexus to his termination. The Commission in Klover held:

Additionally, although the AJ determined that the principal displayed a tendency to view males 'teaching styles as harsher than females,' we are precluded from automatically attributing her behavior to the termination action at hand. Rather, complainant must show a nexus between the allegedly discriminatory remarks and his termination. See Yates, 255 F.3d at 548; Abdu-Brisson v. Delta Air Lines, 239 F.3d 456, 468 (2d Cir. 2001) (holding that the stray remarks of a decision-maker, without more, cannot prove a claim of employment discrimination in termination cases); Marques v. Bank of America, 59 F. Supp.2d 1005, 1019 (N.D. Cal. 1999) (holding that discriminatory comments must be related in time and subject matter to the decisional process). Although the principal may have seen males as harsher educators in general, we note that complainant was terminated for his failure to implement effective classroom management, not for his failure to be "motherly "or for using excess discipline. The principal's comments in regard to complainant's masculinity are insufficiently related to the reasons she cites for his discharge; in fact, they are somewhat counterintuitive. Moreover, the principal's comments regarding male teaching styles appear mutually exclusive from her decision to discharge complainant, as is evidenced by the fact that she retained the other male teacher who was subject to similar comments. Complainant has failed to prove otherwise. Accordingly, we find that the principal's comments were too far removed to prove that her proffered reasons for terminating complainant were pretext for sex discrimination.

But evidence of bias, even if not linked specifically to a personnel action, can support a finding of pretext. For example, in Counts v. Secretary of Defense, 0191293 (1999), the Commission relied, in part, on testimony that the selecting official shunned black employees and showed them little respect, in finding a promotion of a white, less qualified employee was discriminatory. See also Hunter v. Secretary of Army, 01994517 (2001) (supervisor's use of a racial epithet combined with a statement that she would never be promoted was sufficient to sustain a finding of discrimination where employee was required to perform higher level duties without commensurate pay).

As a general rule, evidence of bias on the part of a selecting official that is not linked to a particular action will not be sufficient, standing alone, to demonstrate pretext. See, e.g., Margeson v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A13404 (2002); Redmond v. Secretary of Interior, 01A10226 (2002). The fact that the complainant in Chris A. v. Secretary of Defense, 0120140357 (2016), worked with the same coworkers and supervisors for 30 years without incident tended to evidence that the individuals involved did not hold racial bias against the complainant.

A mere belief by the complainant is not evidence of bias. As the Commission stated in Lynne E. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120132261 (2015):

When asked by the investigator why she believed that her race, sex, age, and previous EEO activity were motivating factors in the incidents at issue, Complainant did not present evidence of any of the indicators of discriminatory motive described above. Instead, she made vague and conclusory assertions such as, "S1 treats me differently than the other nurses with respect to assignments," or "all my coworkers are African-American and they are treated differently than me." IR 103, 107-08. In relying on such statements as evidence of discriminatory motive, Complainant implicitly advances the proposition that the acts of a management official in and of themselves are sufficient to establish that motive. This is simply not true. The statutes the Commission enforces are not a civility code. See McFadden v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 0120122405 (October 12, 2012). Neither Title VII nor the ADEA can protect Complainant from her management chain's behavior toward her unless that behavior arises from a motivation based upon Complainant's race, sex, age, or prior EEO activity. And on this crucial issue, she has not presented any sworn statements from other witnesses, emails, records, documents, or other evidence that contradict the explanations provided by S1, S2, and S3, or which call their veracity into question. We therefore agree with the Agency that Complainant failed to establish the existence of an unlawful motive on the part of the responding management officials with respect to any of the incidents described in her complaint.

See also Season H. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120133231 (2015) ("Complainant, by contrast, has not presented evidence of any of the indicators of discriminatory motive listed above. Instead, she appears to be arguing that an official's bad acts in and of themselves are sufficient to establish the existence of the requisite motive. This is simply not true.").
