27. Trivial or Inconsequential Conduct--Sexual Harassment

Like most other discrimination cases, complaints of sexual harassment are fact-intensive. It is often difficult to determine the line between sexual conduct that is merely annoying and that which constitutes sexual harassment. In Ferrington v. Postmaster General, 01933360 (1994), the Commission found the incidents were too trivial to constitute sexual harassment:

Sexual flirtation, innuendo, or vulgar language that is merely trivial or annoying will not establish a hostile environment. EEOC Policy Guidance, supra, p. 14. The harasser's conduct should be evaluated from the objective viewpoint of a reasonable person. Id.; Harris, 62 U.S.L.W. at 4005.

We find that appellant failed to prove, by a preponderance of the evidence, that she was subjected to unwelcome sexual advances, requests for sexual favors, or other verbal or physical conduct of a sexual nature. Appellant learned of an allegation by an employee that a supervisor made an obscene gesture. The same supervisor apparently hung an arguably offensive picture of a woman that, on the Postmaster's advice, appellant promptly tore down. More directly, the Manager of Customer Relations suggested she was sleeping with a colleague and the Postmaster described her with in an admittedly profane manner. While the aforementioned instances are offensive and unprofessional, they are not sufficiently sexual in nature, severe or pervasive to warrant a finding of sexual harassment by hostile environment. Accordingly, we conclude that appellant failed to establish her claim of hostile environment sexual harassment.

In Peppard v. Chairman, Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 01950588 (1997), the Commission found that a couple of unwelcome remarks were insufficient to constitute sexual harassment. The Commission in Peppard stated:

In this case, the evidence supports a finding that the Unit chief on one occasion remarked upon the length of appellant's skirt, and on another occasion asked Employee A, within appellant's hearing, whether Employee A had engaged in sexual activity the previous evening. These remarks constituted unwelcome conduct of a sexual nature. However, neither these two remarks, nor the Unit chief's use of profanity directed toward both male and female employees, sufficiently altered appellant's work environment so as to establish appellant's claim of sexual harassment. Even if the Unit Chief's behavior had sufficiently altered appellant's work environment, the agency took prompt, appropriate remedial action to eliminate the objectionable behavior. Accordingly, the Commission finds that appellant has not established her claim of sexual harassment.

By contrast, in Taylor v. Secretary of Defense, 01981718 (1999), the Commission found that allegations that a coworker was making sounds and faces at the complainant, constantly asking about her "purple bra," and frequently blocking her path in the hallway was sufficient to state a claim of hostile environment harassment. But in Lynch v. Postmaster General, 01981027 (1999), the Commission found an incident where a supervisor gave the complainant a bra, as a joke, did not create a hostile environment. In Lynch, the complainant alleged that a coworker had a habit of looking at her breasts while talking to her. After she complained to her supervisor, he gave her a black bra as a joke. Examining the totality of the circumstances, including the supervisor's contention that the gift was a joke, that it was given to the complainant in a discrete manner, as well as conflicting evidence on her reaction, the Commission in Lynch found:

With respect to allegations 2 and 4, the record shows that shortly after the above incident, PM gave appellant a black Wonder Bra as a "birthday gift." The package was wrapped plainly, and PM told appellant to open it in the ladies' room. The affidavit testimony of a female co-worker, provided directly to appellant's attorney and submitted on appeal, indicated that she witnessed appellant open the package and that appellant was upset, asking the witness what she would do with the bra, and whether she would show it to her husband. By contrast, PM testified that appellant emerged from the ladies room in good humor, taking the gift as a joke, as intended. He also testified that appellant later told him that her husband thought it was funny. PM testified that the bra was meant as a gag gift in reference appellant's annoyance with the OIC1 matter. Although this incident certainly has all the trademark characteristics of sexual harassment, we find that the non-offensive and discrete manner in which PM delivered the "gift" belies a conclusion that PM intended to create a hostile work environment based on sexual harassment. Moreover, although the evidence is conflicting with respect to appellant's initial reaction to the "gift," it does appear that it was intended as a joke. According to the record, the other Postmaster who joined PM, testified that it was meant only as a joke, and the female eye witness stated that she thought it went "over the line of a joke," apparently inferring that while it may have been intended as a joke, it wasn't funny. PM acknowledged that he used poor judgment.

The Commission went on in Lynch to explain

the actions alleged are common workplace occurrences, and unless it is reasonably established that the actions were somehow abusive or offensive, and were taken in order to harass appellant on the basis of sex, such everyday events are not sufficiently severe or pervasive so as to offend the general sensibility of an individual experiencing such occurrences in the workplace. See Wolf v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01961559 (July 23, 1998); Long v. Veterans Administration, EEOC Appeal No. 01950169 (August 14, 1997).

The Commission's emphasis on the supervisor's intent in Lynch is questionable. The Commission's guidelines prohibit harassment that has the "purpose or effect of unreasonably interfering with an individual's work performance or creating an intimidating, hostile, or offensive working environment." 29 CFR 1604.11(a)(3) (emphasis added). The supervisor's response of giving a bra to the complainant after she complained about a coworker staring at her breasts is gender-related, and there was testimony by a coworker that the complainant was upset when she got the bra. Although in the end the Commission relied on the fact that the conduct was not sufficiently severe or pervasive to create a hostile working environment, its overemphasis on the supervisor's intent is somewhat misleading. See, e.g., Winsor v. Hinckley Dodge, 79 F.3d 996 (10th Cir. 1996) (intent is not relevant where conduct has the effect of creating a hostile environment, is based on gender, and is severe or pervasive).
