EEO RETALIATION

Have you been the target of an employee's EEO complaint? Unfortunately, the experience is not unusual for federal supervisors. Even the greatest care cannot eliminate the possibility of an EEO complaint against you.

You would never knowingly discriminate against an employee and yet an employee says you did. What may have happened? It may be that you:

  • • Inadvertently did something with a discriminatory effect.
  • • Did something that was misunderstood as discriminatory by the employee.
  • • Otherwise offended an employee who decided to use an EEO complaint to get back at you.

Whether or not the complaint has any merit, what happens to that complaint may depend on your reaction. You could make things worse by reacting with anger, or you could limit damage and encourage resolution by reacting calmly and professionally.

Retaliation. It is understandable that your initial reaction to an EEO complaint directed against you, or against a respected subordinate or colleague, would be fueled by feelings of hurt or anger. But you need to control those feelings. If you react badly to an employee's EEO complaint -- or to any EEO activity by an employee, whether or not it is directed at you -- you may make things worse for your agency by providing a new basis for a new or amended complaint: retaliation. Retaliation occurs when a manager, supervisor, or sometimes even a coworker, takes action against an employee or applicant because that person participated in an EEO activity or opposed a perceived EEO violation.

The worst thing about retaliation complaints, from management's point of view, is that they may succeed even when an employee's original EEO activity goes nowhere. This suggests you should take extra care with your behavior whenever you become aware that an employee has engaged in EEO activity.

What does retaliatory behavior look like? How can you tell if something you want to do might be considered retaliation? Ask yourself if your intended action or statement could be interpreted by a reasonable person as discouraging a particular employee or applicant -- or any employees subject to your influence -- from freely participating in the EEO process. Retaliation encompasses any conduct -- based on an individual's EEO activity -- that might well deter someone from engaging in EEO activity, including:

  • • Any materially adverse action directed against employees (or applicants) because of EEO activity.
  • • Harassment of these employees, even when the conduct is not severe or pervasive.
  • • Threats directed against them.
  • • General statements suggesting that EEO activity might hurt those who engage in it.
  • • Harassment or adverse action against the relation or close associate of an employee who engaged in EEO activity (known as "third-party retaliation").

This does not mean you cannot take needed employment actions. It does mean that you must be sure you can justify them on the merits. And of course, you must avoid anything that might reasonably be interpreted as harassment. Even conduct that falls short of adverse employment actions or harassment may still be considered retaliation if it has the effect of "chilling" EEO activity. Do not hesitate to consult HR for guidance.

Anger prevents resolution. You should control your feelings when EEO complaints are raised, not only because retaliation may provide the basis of a new complaint, but because anger on your part may prevent early or easy resolution.

Many complaints are based on simple misunderstandings. If you stay calm and professional, avoid retaliation, and cooperate in good faith with the EEO process and the attempts to achieve resolution, you may be able to make a complaint go away.

Even if it does not, your positive behavior will strengthen your agency's case, and minimize any damage to workplace morale and productivity, and to your reputation and career.

Unfair employment decisions

One way you can get into trouble for retaliation is by making unfair employment decisions that penalize an employee who filed an EEO complaint or participated in the EEO process in another way. This type of action is sometimes called disparate treatment, because some employees are treated less favorably than others.

Whatever name is used, it boils down to this: a supervisor is irritated about an employee's EEO activity and takes an action that they wouldn't have taken if the employee had not been involved in EEO matters. Examples of unfair treatment an employee might protest in a retaliation complaint include:

  • • Denial of a promotion or award.
  • • Removal for conduct that normally would lead to lesser discipline.
  • • An undeserved poor performance rating.
  • • Denial of training or favorable work assignments.

If you know about an employee's EEO activities and then say things like "that EEO complaint is going to cost her a promotion," it will be difficult for your agency to argue that you subsequently denied the promotion for a legitimate reason. Similarly, if you deny a qualified employee a promotion soon after they file an EEO complaint, but you can't explain why, your agency will have a hard time convincing anyone that the EEO complaint didn't influence your decision.

With this in mind, make sure you:

  • • Make decisions based on merit. EEO activity should play no part in promotion decisions, performance ratings, or other employment decisions.
  • • Document the reasons for your decisions. This will help you remember and explain your reasoning. Justifying a decision after the fact can sometimes be difficult, and may even seem suspicious.
  • • Discipline equitably. If two employees with similar employment histories engage in similar misconduct, don't use the opportunity to treat the employee with EEO activity more harshly. Make sure any discipline is reasonable under the circumstances.
  • • Follow standard procedures. If you already have procedures for making discipline or selection decisions, following them can help you show that your action wasn't motivated by a retaliatory intent.
  • • Keep your negative thoughts to yourself. It's natural to be hurt or angry if an employee files an EEO complaint against you, but even statements that you view as just blowing off steam could be used to show your decisions were influenced by the employee's EEO activity.

Remember that getting rid of an employee does not eliminate the possibility of an EEO complaint for retaliation. Although most EEO complaints are filed by current employees, it is entirely possible for a former employee to file a complaint.

Examples of unfair employment decisions. The following examples are taken from actual cases similar to situations you may encounter.

  • • Shortly after an employee filed an EEO complaint, she was not selected for a temporary, one-year appointment. Her supervisor attempted to explain his decision. The EEOC did not believe his explanation because other employees said managers viewed the employee as a troublemaker, a "frequent filer," and not a team player because of her EEO complaint, as well as her previous EEO activity. Although the complainant was on the best qualified list, the selecting official interviewed only applicants without EEO activity. The agency's description of the general selection process mechanics was insufficiently specific to provide a legitimate explanation for the agency's decision not to interview the complainant for a position. Accordingly, the EEOC found her nonselection was the product of retaliation.
  • • An employee was subjected to retaliation for his prior EEO activity when he was not selected for a promotion. A link was established to his past EEO activity when a supervisor commented he would never be promoted because of the "stink he made to come on board."

Retaliatory harassment

A supervisor can also get into hot water if she harasses an employee because of that employee's EEO activity. Successful or not, EEO complaints can damage the careers of those accused. Because the matters at stake can cause tempers to flare, supervisors have to be careful not to make matters worse by letting their anger or frustration lead them to harass an employee involved in EEO activity.

What constitutes retaliatory harassment? That depends on the circumstances, but it's more than an occasional frown. The key is to remember that retaliatory harassment encompasses actions that are severe or pervasive enough to create a hostile work environment or change the terms and conditions of employment.

Of course, even if you are accused of creating a retaliatory hostile work environment, it doesn't mean you actually did. You'll have an opportunity to explain your version of events, and it may ultimately be determined that the actions the employee complained about didn't actually happen or weren't enough to constitute actionable harassment. But it still is in your best interest to be careful. Little slights and snide comments could add up to a problem. Adopting a calm, business-like manner will help you weather the situation without creating further problems.

One other thing to remember -- it's a supervisor's duty to stop discriminatory harassment immediately and effectively. Allowing a group of coworkers to gang up on an employee who filed an EEO complaint against you may be tempting, but it will only lead to more trouble and should be stopped immediately. Likewise, if you are aware of another supervisor engaging in retaliatory harassment, you should act to make sure it ends.

Examples of retaliatory harassment. Let's look at some real examples of retaliatory harassment so you'll know what kind of behavior is unacceptable:

A postal clerk's supervisors harassed him in retaliation for his prior EEO activity. Testimony indicated that the supervisors frequently disparaged the complainant and yelled at him in front of customers to the point where he developed emotional problems. Further evidence linked the harassment to the complainant's EEO activity, including the postmaster's query if he was "starting trouble again."

After an employee reported that a manager sexually harassed a female subordinate, the manager physically threatened that employee and spread lies that resulted in the revocation of her security clearance. The employee was awarded over $340,000 in compensatory damages for the physical, emotional, and financial harm she suffered as a result of the manager's misconduct.

It may be human nature for you to resent an employee who files an EEO complaint, but acting on the resentment by harassing the employee only makes a difficult situation worse.

Chilling conduct

EEO law prohibits comments or other conduct that would "chill" the willingness of employees to exercise their EEO rights, or dissuade a reasonable employee from pursuing EEO activity. This chilling conduct may fall short of harassment, being neither severe nor pervasive. Conduct raised under this theory also may not be considered disparate or unfair treatment because it's not an employment action.

Nonetheless, this sometimes rather subtle conduct is unlawful, regardless of the supervisor's motive.

Chilling conduct can be difficult to define and depends on the facts of each situation. Most of the time, it involves interference with the employee's EEO plans or a suggestion by a manager that EEO activity would not be in an employee's best interest. Even general statements that don't appear to be directed at a specific employee may be regarding as chilling.

In some cases, the EEOC has determined that chilling statements are an absolute violation of antidiscrimination laws. The employee doesn't have to show the manager's statement actually stopped them from asserting their EEO rights. The fact that the words were said or the conduct undertaken is enough to violate the law.

Examples of chilling conduct. Here are some actual examples of acts that have been considered unlawful chilling conduct:

  • • A supervisor made a comment about an employee's EEO activity in his performance appraisal and told the employee he would discuss his EEO history with any prospective employers who contacted him.
  • • After an employee testified in support of an EEO complaint filed by a coworker, her supervisor remarked in a meeting that "what goes around, comes around" with regard to EEO activity.
  • • An employee and his wife worked at the same facility. After the employee's wife contacted an EEO counselor, he was denied substitute teaching assignments.

Mistakes to avoid

As a federal manager, sooner or later you are bound to be involved in EEO matters, and you may be the target of an EEO complaint. You need to treat these situations with care.

Do not:

  • • Take an EEO complaint personally. It is tough to keep your cool when you or your colleagues are charged with discrimination, but it is in your and your agency's best interest to remain calm and professional.
  • • Disparage or retaliate against any participant. If you express your displeasure at the participation of employees in an investigation, whether as employees, witnesses, or representatives, or take any action against them not otherwise justified, you open yourself up to a separate EEO complaint of retaliation.
  • • Make negative comments about the EEO process or specific complaints. In general, it is best to keep any negative thoughts to yourself. As the investigation and complaint process progresses, stay calm, professional, and objective.
  • • Turn a blind eye to coworker harassment. You might have subordinates who feel an EEO complaint against you is unjust, but you can't allow them to harass the complainant out of loyalty to you.
  • • Obstruct the process. If you stonewall, refuse to provide documents, or otherwise slow the process, you expose your agency to the possibility that an administrative judge will draw an adverse inference from your lack of cooperation.
  • • Overlook the guidance you can receive from your HR department. Being named in an EEO complaint can be a disorienting experience, but HR can answer your questions and provide you with the direction you need to handle the situation properly.

Ask FMG

Q. What are protected EEO activities?

A. The term "protected EEO activities" encompasses participation in the EEO process or opposition to unlawful discrimination. The EEOC has identified many activities that it considers "protected" for the purposes of establishing a retaliation claim. They include, but are not limited to:

  • • EEO counseling.
  • • Acting as an EEO representative.
  • • Opposing discriminatory practices.
  • • Speaking out against reasonably perceived discrimination.
  • • Providing evidence in connection with an EEO complaint.
  • • Testifying at an EEO hearing.
  • • Requesting reasonable accommodation for a disability or religion.
  • • Filing an EEO complaint.

Q. What can I do to prevent a complaint of retaliation?

A. The best way to prevent retaliation complaints is to not retaliate. Some employees will file EEO complaints, even if you do your utmost to avoid retaliation. But don't be disheartened because these steps will help prove your actions were not based on the employee's EEO activity. Make sure you:

  • • Don't treat an employee who has engaged in EEO activity less favorably than a similar employee who didn't pursue EEO activity. This means you must be careful not to let anger about an EEO complaint lead to an unfair performance assessment, an inequitable job assignment, more severe discipline than normal, or any other unfair treatment.
  • • Document every aspect of an employment decision that may be considered retaliatory.
  • • Don't let the annoyance about an employee's EEO activity lead you to subject the employee to unwarranted scrutiny, or to make threatening comments about the EEO activity.

Above all, you must be able to support your employment actions. Poor documentation can prevent an agency from being able to provide a legitimate explanation for an action.

Q. I suspect my subordinate supervisor of retaliating against an employee who filed an EEO complaint against them. Should I intervene?

A. Yes. You have an obligation to your agency, and the law, to take effective action against your subordinate's retaliatory conduct, as you would against any other form of discriminatory behavior.

Q. Should I change the way I manage an employee after they file an EEO complaint?

A. No, although you may want to be particularly sensitive to any possible misunderstanding. As long as your actions are reasonable under the circumstances, you need not shy away from treating an employee who has engaged in EEO activity like any other employee in need of correction. Be sure to document the reasons for your actions and be very sure that you aren't consciously or unconsciously punishing the employee for their EEO activity.

Q. An employee said I retaliated against them because they testified in an EEO hearing. I didn't even know they were a witness. Don't they have to show that I knew about their role at the hearing?

A. Yes. An employee claiming retaliation has to show that the management official accused of retaliation knew about their protected EEO activity when the official took the action.

Q. Can I discuss an employee's discrimination or retaliation complaint with my fellow managers?

A. Use extreme caution. Unless your colleagues have a legitimate business need to know, you should avoid discussing an employee's EEO complaint. Poorly considered statements you make to other supervisors about a complaint may become evidence in the case and could also become fodder for a claim of retaliation.

Q. Where can I find out more about how I should handle EEO complaints?

A. Be sure to check with your HR office. They can explain the EEO process and reassure you that you should not take the complaint personally or try to address it with the employee on your own.
