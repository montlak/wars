6. Sexual Harassment

The subject of sexual harassment is covered in detail in Chapter 12. As a general rule, an agency is liable for sexual harassment by a supervisor unless it can show that it took prompt action to stop and prevent any harassing conduct and that the employee unreasonably failed to take advantage of corrective opportunities provided by the agency, or otherwise failed to avoid harm. An agency is liable for sexual harassment by a coworker when the agency knew, or should have known, of the harassment and failed to take prompt and effective remedial action.

However, whether the agency took prompt and effective corrective action goes to the merits of the complaint or the damages arising from the claim. It does not impact whether a complainant has stated a claim for relief. Jones v. Postmaster General, 01A12463 (2001). The fact that the agency disciplined the offending supervisor does not provide a basis for dismissing the complaint for failure to state a claim. Id.

The Commission has held that one form of prompt and effective remedial action is to remove the alleged harasser from the work environment. A reassignment of the alleged harasser does not give rise to any violation of Title VII. In Pickworth v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01965079 (1997), the Commission dismissed the appellant's case for failure to state a claim where the appellant was reassigned because of allegations of sexual harassment against him:

In his complaint, appellant alleged that he was assigned to another location from his job as housekeeping aid in the pharmacy. The agency stated that management reassigned appellant away from the pharmacy in response to complaints which were made by two female employees in the pharmacy claiming that they were harassed and stalked by appellant. We note that Title VII affords all employees the right to work in an environment free from discriminatory intimidation, ridicule, and insult. Accordingly, when an employer receives a complaint or otherwise learns of alleged harassment in the workplace, the employer is required to take immediate and appropriate corrective action by doing whatever is necessary to end the harassment. In the present case, the agency, after learning about the alleged harassment, took prompt remedial action by moving appellant, the alleged harasser, away from the alleged offensive work place. Thus, we find that appellant's complaint fails to state a processable claim. Accordingly, the agency's final decision is hereby AFFIRMED.

Although the Commission does little to explain its position in Pickworth, the problem with the allegation is that the complainant did not contend that he was reassigned because of his sex. Instead, the allegation, taken at face value, is that the agency transferred him because there were pending allegations of sexual harassment.

A complaint that the agency has not properly responded to allegations of sexual harassment does not state a claim. Such a claim is not an independent basis for a complaint. Rather, it is an element of proof in the liability phase of a sexual harassment complaint. Taylor v. Postmaster General, 01973854 (1998). See Chapter 12 fully discussing sexual harassment.
