F. CONTENTS OF CONTACT

There are no magic words that an appellant must recite to initiate precomplaint counseling, but the employee must exhibit the intent to begin the complaint process. See Mack v. Secretary of Army, 01A53853 (2005) (ultimate contact with counselor untimely even where first contact occurred within 45 days because complainant exhibited no intent to file a complaint). The Commission will examine the circumstances of the contact to determine intent. See Osborn v. USPS, 01842680 (1985) (requesting a counselor to inquire into an incident exhibits an intent to initiate the complaint process).

There is no requirement that the appellant actually meet face-to-face with the counselor within the time period for initiating counselor contact. The only requirement is to bring the matter to the attention of a counselor or the EEO office within the regulatory time period. Monagle v. USPS, 01830283 (1983).

An employee who contacts an EEO counselor must allege that the incident in question is based on discrimination. Otherwise, such contact does not satisfy the requirement of the regulation. In Sanchez v. USPS, 01831884 (1983), the appellant contacted a counselor stating only that she desired clarification of the selection requirements for a certain position. She specifically stated that she did not want to file a complaint. The EEOC ruled this contact was not for the purpose of initiating informal counseling and also that a subsequent contact to initiate counseling did not relate back to the earlier contact.

When a complainant contacts a counselor and is informed of her right to proceed but elects to "wait out" the transfer of the alleged harasser, she has not exhibited an intent to initiate counseling. Butler v. Secretary of Army, 01932487 (1993). Similarly, merely discussing past nonpromotions with an EEO counselor during the course of counseling on a hostile environment complaint is not sufficient to exhibit an intent to initiate counseling over the nonpromotions. Tom v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01996598 (2002).

In the oriGINAl appeal of Turner v. Secretary of Defense, 05970779 (1999), sworn statements of agency officials were submitted. These statements supported a finding that complainant did not exhibit an intent to commence the EEO process until January 17, 1996. In his request for reconsideration, the complainant submitted a sworn statement that he repeatedly attempted to seek relief from the discriminatory practices of the agency by: (1) refusing to sign his inaccurate performance appraisals; (2) by filing a grievance; and (3) seeking legal counsel on November 30, 1995, with regard to discrimination issues. The complainant argued that all of these actions were evidence of his intent to commence the EEO process when he initially contacted an EEO Counselor on December 12, 1995, not on January 17, 1996, as alleged by the agency. On reconsideration in Turner, the Commission held:

The agency dismissed, for failure to timely contact an EEO Counselor, allegation (a) and the portion of allegation (c) addressing the denial of promotional assignments and training from September 1993 to November 1995. The agency argued that despite the fact that appellant previously initiated contact with the EEO Office on December 12, 1995, appellant did not wish to pursue a formal EEO complaint at that time. Thereafter, the agency found that even though appellant initiated contact on January 17, 1996, he insisted that agency officials indicate that his initial EEO Counselor contact actually occurred on December 12, 1995. However, the record establishes, and the agency does not dispute, that appellant first contacted the agency's EEO office on December 12, 1995, which was well within the forty-five-day limit. There is also conflicting evidence in the record as to whether appellant began EEO counseling on that date. Under these circumstances, the Commission finds that appellant's initial EEO Counselor date was December 12, 1995, and therefore, timely.

Approaching an agency official logically connected to the EEO process in a public area and expressing a desire to initiate counseling is sufficient contact for purposes of timeliness. In Marshall v. Government Printing Office, 0120073941 (2007), the complainant approached an EEO counselor in the agency cafeteria shortly before Christmas and told the counselor she wanted to initiate counseling. The counselor was not able to meet with her that day, and the following day the complainant went on vacation. She contacted the counselor a second time by email after her return, which was outside of the 45-day limit. In dismissing the complaint for untimely counselor contact, the agency used the date of the email as the date of initial contact. The Commission found that the complainant had timely made contact with an official logically connected to the EEO process and exhibited an intent to initiate counseling.
