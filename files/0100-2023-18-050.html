4. Representation of Former Employees

Under previous Commission case law, an agency employee was not entitled to official time to represent former employees of the agency. Enterline v. Postmaster General, 01883754 (1988) (agency was justified in requiring employee to use annual leave for representation of employees terminated during the probationary period).

However, since Enterline, the Commission has held that whether or not a representative is entitled to official time depends on the status of the complainant at the time the alleged discriminatory action is taken. Kwok v. Postmaster General, 05940368 (1994). Where the challenged action involves the complainant's removal, the complainant was an employee at the time of the action and the representative is entitled to official time.

In Fuller v. Postmaster General, 05950345 (1996), the Commission applied the Kwok decision to find that the agency violated a settlement agreement providing the complainant with official time to represent employees. In finding that the agency had breached the agreement, the Commission in Fuller noted:

The Kwok decision cited by appellant involved the issue of whether a complainant must be an employee in order for his or her representative to be allowed official time. That is not the exact issue presented here. However, it is applicable precedent because in Kwok as in this case, the appellant wished to represent a complainant who had been removed from employment, and as here, a crucial question was whether the complainant was an "employee" within the meaning of 29 CFR § 1614.605(b). The Commission in Kwok found that the complainant did hold employee status at the time of the adverse employment action which was the subject of the EEO complaint, and therefore was an employee for representation purposes. We accordingly found that the appellant/representative was entitled to official time to represent the complainant in his removal complaint.

Applying these findings to the instant case, the Commission finds that the agency erred in its construction of the settlement agreement. Contrary to the agency's interpretation, the settlement agreement does permit appellant to represent a person so long as the person was an employee at the time of the adverse employment action that is at issue in the person's EEO complaint. The ex-employees Persons A and B are such employees for representation purposes and thus are within the definition of the word "employee" as used in the settlement agreement. The commission finds that the agency's refusal to allow appellant to represent Persons A and B on the grounds that they were not employees was erroneous and constitutes a breach of the settlement agreement. The Commission further finds that the most appropriate resolution for this matter decision referred to is Steve Kwok v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05940368 (December 15, 1994).

Clearly, the better test is to focus on the status of the complainant at the time of the alleged discriminatory action and not the status of the complainant at the time representation services are rendered. See Streiff v. Postmaster General, 01966179 (1998).
