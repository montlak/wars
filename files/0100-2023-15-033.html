I. WITNESS LISTS

By prehearing order, each party is required to submit a proposed witness list to the administrative judge. Each witness should be identified by name and position title, if an agency employee. A brief description of the witness' expected testimony should also be included. Because the employee bears the burden of proof, the complainant should designate as witnesses all those who have relevant testimony, including those persons who are not expected to give favorable testimony. For example, the RMO should be designated as a witness so that he or she is available for cross-examination. Witnesses who are not expected to give favorable testimony should be designated as "adverse" or "hostile" witnesses. This designation, if approved by the judge, allows a party to use techniques of cross-examination, such as leading questions, on direct examination. The complainant also should include a request that the judge issue an order compelling the attendance of witnesses who are federal employees. The parties should not be lulled into excluding a witness from their list because that person has been designated as a witness by the opposing party. A change in strategy prior to the hearing may result in a decision not to call a certain witness. Designating witnesses the other party has indicated that it will call prevents a witness from becoming unavailable at the last minute.

The description of expected testimony need not be lengthy and need not set out the expected testimony in detail. However, the description should list, in general fashion, all matters on which the witness is expected to testify. Failure to list a matter in the description can constitute a basis for the administrative judge to preclude questioning on that matter at the hearing.

A party is not deprived of the right to cross-examine a witness when the other party decides not to call that witness to testify. A party only has a right to cross-examine witnesses who are actually called to testify by the other party. Teece v. Postmaster General, 01911057 (1991).

A complainant or agency is required only to provide the administrative judge with a list of witnesses and a brief explanation of their expected testimony so the judge may determine the necessity of obtaining the testimony of proposed witnesses. It is arbitrary and capricious for a judge to require detailed explanations from the complainant while approving the agency's witness list on the basis of brief descriptions. See Spanton v. Defense Contract Audit Agency, 01831294 (1984). The failure of a complainant to respond to an administrative judge's request for designation of representative and a witness list does not waive the complainant's right to a hearing when the employee planned to represent himself and rely solely upon his own testimony. See Mavronikolas v. USPS, 01830547 (1983). Similarly, in Streitman v. Postmaster General, 01956861 (1998), the Commission found that cancelation of the hearing was improper where the complainant failed to submit a witness list but exhibited an intent to proceed by naming a representative and engaging in discovery.

Objections to proposed witnesses should be submitted promptly to the administrative judge. The most common objection is that the witness has no relevant testimony to give. A witness also may be objected to on the grounds that his or her testimony would be unduly repetitious. Subsequent to receiving the parties' proposed witness lists and any objections, the judge will issue an order of the witnesses approved for the hearing. Often this is done after the prehearing conference where the administrative judge has the opportunity to hear further argument on whether the witness should be allowed to testify. However, the judge is not required to hold a prehearing conference in order make rulings on witnesses.

Administrative judges have wide discretion both in the approval of witnesses to testify at the hearing and as to the scope their testimony. Witness rulings are reviewed on appeal under an abuse of discretion standard. See, e.g., Jackson v. Postmaster General, 01A62911 (2006) (no abuse of discretion where judge disallowed two witnesses who would have testified on matters related to past EEO complaints and further restricted scope of complainant's testimony to exclude any matters related to past complaints).
