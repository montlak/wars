E. RELIANCE ON AGENCY REGULATIONS

A rationale advanced frequently by agencies for their actions is reliance on their own internal regulations or policies. Certainly, an agency is entitled to rely on its regulations and policies to the extent they do not have a discriminatory effect or are not applied in a discriminatory fashion.

Agencies sometimes make the mistake of relying solely on the language of the regulation or policy and the mere assertion that the employee has failed to comply with the stated requirements. When an employee alleges disparate treatment and satisfies a prima facie case, the agency does not satisfy its burden merely by producing the regulation or testimony on the contents of the regulation. Marcum v. USPS, 01852764 (1987). In that case, the agency relied upon its own leave policies in advancing a legitimate reason for denying the employee leave without pay. The Commission did not dispute the agency's representations as to its leave policies, but found there was insufficient evidence in the record to determine whether the complainant's situation fell within the ambit of the policies. The case was remanded for further investigation because the EEOC could not determine if the agency had a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its treatment of the complainant.

The fact that a regulation is facially legitimate does not preclude a finding that the regulation is applied in a discriminatory or retaliatory fashion. In Moore v. Postmaster General, 01922046 (1992), the Commission found that an agency regulation prohibiting outside employment did not prevent a complainant from demonstrating pretext where the agency knew of the complainant's outside employment for two years and did nothing. The agency's demand for the complainant's resignation and, when she refused, her termination were deemed retaliatory where they occurred subsequent to the complainant's EEO activity. The Commission specifically declined to rule on the legitimacy of the regulation and further declined to grant the complainant's requested remedy of permission to resume her outside employment.

An agency may not simply hide behind its own policies without explanation of how those policies apply to a particular case. In the absence of any such explanation, the agency fails to meet its burden of production. For example, in Jones v. Postmaster General, 01950129 (1996), the Commission found that the agency failed to meet its burden in a nonpromotion case where the agency contended that the complainant's placement on the Performance Eligibility Register (PER) prevented his selection. The agency then refused to provide any information regarding the manner in which the panelists' scores were weighted in developing composite rankings on the PER. The Commission held in Jones that, absent exceptional circumstances, an agency may not derogate an appellant's Title VII rights by referring to the dictates of agency policy:

[W]e find that the agency was not excused by exceptional circumstances from supplying appellant with information regarding the make-up of the relevant PER scores. Furthermore, we find that without such information, appellant was essentially unable to support his primary contention--that the discriminatory treatment he received from the Panelists during the interview process was ultimately responsible for his low ranking on the PER and his nonselection to the Position.

In addition, appellant was significantly impaired in prosecuting his case because of the absence of evidence regarding his interview scores. Despite repeated questioning by the AJ and appellant's attorney, none of the Panelists could offer any specific explanations regarding the reasons for the Panelists' rating of appellant on the various KSAs. None of the Panelists could remember any specific facts about appellant's interview performance nor was adequate information provided by the agency about the evaluation guidelines utilized by the Panel. We find that the lack of such information gave appellant little opportunity to prove pretext, especially considering that appellant's primary contention was that the Panelists' rating was responsible for his low placement on the PER.

In an interesting case, the Commission held that comments by the selecting official that, "all things equal," he would like to promote a minority candidate were not sufficiently indicative of discrimination where the agency demonstrated the complainant was not promoted because he would have been supervising his brother-in-law. In McMorrow v. Secretary of Commerce, 01970666 (1999), the Commission held:

Under the circumstances of this case, the Division Chief's comments, viewed in their context, do not rise to the level of discrimination. The Division Chief specifically stated that "if all things were equal," he would prefer to see a minority candidate selected. However, in this case, there were no equally qualified candidates. The Division Chief, the Section Chief and other management officials unanimously agreed that appellant was the best qualified applicant; that he was their first choice for the position; and had it not been for the anti-nepotism regulations, appellant would have been promoted. Therefore, after a careful review of the record, including appellant's contentions on appeal, the agency's response, and arguments and evidence not specifically addressed in this decision, we AFFIRM the FAD.

The absence of a specific agency regulation or policy to deal with a particular situation is not carte blanche for a supervisor to be arbitrary and capricious. The Commission has found that persuasive evidence of a discriminatory motive exists where a supervisor fails to follow his own past practices. See Presley v. Postmaster General, 01962983 (1998) (agency's failure to adhere to policy offered as legitimate reason for two comparable employees outside of the complainant's protected class); Dabney v. Veterans Administration, 05880391 (1988). Cf. Sibon v. Postmaster General, 01962631 (1998) (failure to follow past practice is not evidence of discrimination where such failure is adequately explained).
