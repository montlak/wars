CHAPTER 19

CLASS ACTION COMPLAINTS

Class action complaints provide a vehicle to challenge agency policies and practices that adversely affect large numbers of employees with a common characteristic of race, color, sex, religion, national origin, age, or disability status. Reprisal may also serve as a basis of discrimination in a class complaint. Most often, class actions are used as a means of grieving a facially neutral policy that has the effect of discriminating against a protected group or groups. As such, most class action complaints involve the theory of adverse impact. However, it is possible to bring a class action under the intentional discrimination theory.

Class actions are brought by a named complainant or complainants and also include all unnamed and, at the outset, unidentified persons who are members of the class and have been subject to the same alleged discriminatory policies or practices. Because class actions have an impact that reach far beyond the actual named complainants, there are unique requirements to fulfill before a class action can be brought. There also is a separate procedure for class complaints that is substantially different from the individual complaint procedure, though a 2012 rulemaking eliminated some of those differences.

The class complaint procedure was revised through the adoption of 29 CFR Part 1614, effective October 1, 1992. As discussed in more detail below, the most significant revision was that the Commission deleted the previous provision of its regulations allowing class members to "opt out" of class complaints. Also, in November 1999 the Commission amended Part 1614, resulting in several changes to the class complaint process. All those changes to Part 1614 apply to class actions as of November 9, 1999, including those pending in the complaint process as of that date. Lee v. Secretary of Army, 01990384 (2000).

Effective September 24, 2012, the Commission made two significant changes to its class complaint procedures. First, the Commission amended its regulations to adopt a procedure that it uses in individual complaints to class complaints. The Commission has eliminated the recommended decisions of administrative judges on the merits of class action complaints and now all decisions of administrative judges on class complaints are subject to final agency action. An agency can either fully implement the decision of the administrative judge or not fully implement the decision and file an appeal with OFO. The agency only has to appeal those parts of the decision it does not wish to fully implement. The Commission also adopted a provision to shorten the time period for appeal of class certification decisions and now OFO must decide appeals of certification decisions within 90 days of receipt of the appeal. As the Commission explained in its Notice of Final Rulemaking, 77 Fed. Reg. 43498, 43503:

The Workgroup carefully considered the class complaint process and made a number of recommendations to improve its effectiveness. As a result of those recommendations, in the NPRM the Commission proposed to revise the class complaint regulations to make an administrative judge's decision on the merits of a class complaint a final decision, which the agency can fully implement or appeal in its final action. Currently, the administrative judge issues final decisions on the acceptance of class complaints, and the merits of individual complaints, but only issues recommended findings and conclusions on the merits of class complaints, which the agency may accept, reject, or modify in its final decision. Previously, in a 1999 rulemaking, the Commission changed the administrative judge's recommended decisions on the merits of individual complaints and on the acceptance of class complaints to final decisions that must be fully implemented or appealed by the agency in its final action. With the current change, all administrative judge decisions will be final decisions which the agency can either implement in full or appeal. If the agency does not fully implement the administrative judge's decision, it only has to appeal the parts of the decision that it wishes to contest. For example, if an administrative judge finds that the agency discriminated against the class and awards reinstatement and backpay, and if the agency disagrees with the award of reinstatement, the agency's appeal need only challenge the reinstatement award.

The Commission also proposed in the NPRM to provide for expedited processing of appeals of decisions to accept or dismiss class complaints (certification decisions) to shorten the class certification process. Specifically, the Commission proposed to amend § 1614.405 to provide that decisions on appeals of decisions to accept or dismiss class complaints will be issued within 90 days of receipt of the appeal. We received uniform comments supporting both class complaint process proposals. Therefore, the final rule retains both provisions.

A class complaint proceeds through four stages. MD-110, at 8-1, describes the four stages of class complaint processing:

The first stage is the establishment of a class complaint. At this stage, the class agent is required to seek counseling from an agency EEO Counselor. The second stage is a determination from a Commission Administrative Judge, subject to agency final action, as to whether to certify the complaint as a class action. The third stage, assuming that the complaint has been certified as a class action, involves a recommended decision from an Administrative Judge on the merits of the class complaint, subject to final agency action in the form of a final decision. The fourth stage, where there has been a finding of class-based discrimination, is the determination of the claims for relief of the individual class members.

The most significant change brought about by the 1999 amendments involves the second stage. Under the 1992 version of Part 1614, the administrative judge's decision regarding certification was a recommended decision that the agency could accept, reject or modify. If the agency rejected or modified the administrative judge's recommended decision, the burden was on the class agent to file an appeal with OFO. The 1999 regulation amendments require the agency to appeal to OFO any administrative judge's decision regarding certification that it does not fully implement. In its 2012 amendments, the Commission has to amended 29 CFR 1614.204(j) to be consistent with its processing regulations for individual complaints. The amendment eliminates the recommended decisions of administrative judges in class action complaints described in stage three. The agency now had 60 days to determine whether it would fully implement the decision of the administrative judge. If the agency determines not to fully implement the decision, it must, simultaneous with notifying the class representative of that fact, file an appeal of the administrative judge's decision with OFO.

The Commission provided additional guidance to its administrative judges on the processing of class complaints in Chapter Ten of its Judges' Handbook. As general guidance, the Judges' Handbook, at 10-1 provides:

This chapter provides general guidance for the processing of class complaints. Administrative Judges should also consult the regulations at 29 C.F.R. § 1614.204 and EEO MD-110, Chapter 8. Whenever possible, class complaints should be assigned on a priority basis and processed as expeditiously as possible. Unique to the federal sector EEO administrative process, an individual cannot opt out of a class complaint. EEO MD-110, 8-7. An individual complaint that is filed before or after the class complaint is filed and that comes within the definition of the class claim(s) will not be dismissed but will be subsumed within the class complaint. EEO MD-110, 8-4.
