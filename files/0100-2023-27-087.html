E. DEROGATORY COMMENTS AND ACTIONS

Comments with negative connotations that reflect on race, color, sex, national origin, religion, age, or disabled status must be examined in light of the totality of the circumstances to determine whether they constitute prohibited discrimination. The mere use of epithets does not in and of itself amount to discrimination. See Doe v. Environmental Protection Agency, 01840519 (1985) (supervisor congratulated an employee for "Jewing down" a government contract). Likewise, the keeping of "unofficial" personnel records on employees is not an indication of discrimination where the files are kept on all employees. See Blevins v. USPS, 01852992 (1987). However, if the employee claims discrimination is the motive for keeping such records, he continues to be aggrieved until all such records are expunged, including records solely to jog the memory of supervisors. Wooten v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01912221 (1991). Moreover, the failure to acknowledge an employee's greeting, unaccompanied by any concrete action, does not aggrieve an employee. See Kelly v. Postmaster General, 01941599 (1994).

To file a complaint, the employee must be aggrieved by the terms and conditions of employment. In other words, the employee must allege some direct and personal injury at the hands of the employer. See Price v. USPS, 01870011 (1988) (verbal insult by a coworker on one occasion does not allege sufficient harm). For more on the concept of harm as it relates to standing to file a complaint, see Chapter 3 under the heading "Standing: Failure to State a Claim."

"[A]n agency is responsible for supervisors whose use of ethnic slurs or other abusive language has the effect of creating an intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment that unreasonably interferes with an individual's work performance." In re Postmaster General, 05870508 (1988); 29 CFR 1606.8. An employee who alleges that the comments of coworkers about his workers' compensation claims made him feel like an "outcast" has stated a complaint "which may be illustrative of the circumstances affecting the conditions of his employment." Id. In order for actions or gestures to be actionable, the employee must present a prima facie case by showing the actions or gestures were directed at him and took place under circumstances giving rise to an inference of discrimination. Lines v. Department of Interior, 01880317 (1988).

Of course, the complainant must present evidence that the comments were indeed derogatory. In Pittman v. Postmaster General, 01883851 (1989), the employee complained that her supervisor permitted coworkers to bring in a cake with derogatory comments to celebrate the complainant's last day of work. Testimony on precisely what the inscription on the cake said was sketchy and contradictory. In a rare display of humor, the Commission noted, "[u]nfortunately for appellant's case, the best evidence (the cake) was eaten." Id. The reader also is referred to the discussion of harassment in Chapter 12.
