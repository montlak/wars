7. Invalid Settlement Agreements

Although it may seem elementary, there can be no enforcement of a settlement agreement where the agreement was never fully consummated. For example, in Chandhok v. Secretary of Commerce, 01953890 (1997), the Commission found that there was no valid settlement agreement because the agency had not signed the proposed agreement. As a result, there could be no specific enforcement. More significantly, the Commission further held that the agency's processing of the underlying complaint in Chandhok should not have ceased simply because the appellant tried to enforce a proposed settlement agreement. After the appellant filed a breach allegation, the agency stopped processing the complaint even though it took the position there was no valid agreement to enforce. On appeal, the agency also argued that the appellant had failed to exhaust his administrative remedies because he had not appealed the final agency decision determining there was no settlement agreement. The agency further argued that the appellant could not seek both specific enforcement of the agreement and "reinstatement" of his complaint. As the Commission explained in Chandhok, there was no need to reinstate the complaint because, in the absence of a valid settlement agreement, it had never been withdrawn:

The agency agrees that there is no valid settlement agreement and that appellant is not now attempting to specifically enforce such an agreement. The issue before the Commission, using the parties' terminology, is whether the underlying complaint dated December 19, 1991 can be "reinstated." The agency provides numerous arguments on appeal as to why the December 19, 1991 complaint should not be reinstated. The agency groups the arguments into three categories: (1) "Appellant was entitled to request that the terms of the settlement agreement be specifically implemented or that his complaint be processed, but not both"; (2) appellant "failed to exhaust his administrative remedies"; and (3) "[a]ppellant did not make a timely request to have his case reinstated."

The Commission finds that because the complaint was never settled, there is no need to "reinstate" the complaint. While reinstatement is allowed under § 1614.504(a), the prerequisite to reinstatement under § 1614.504(a) is that there be a breach of settlement agreement. There is no breach of settlement agreement in this case because there is no settlement agreement. Because there was no settlement agreement, the instant complaint should have been processed from the point processing had ceased--which appears to be at the hearing stage in this case.

Simply because appellant attempted to have the purported settlement agreement specifically enforced does not mean that the underlying complaint can never thereafter be processed. There is nothing in the Commission's regulations that suggests such a result. The case cited by the agency on appeal, Harris v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 01934480 (Nov. 12, 1993), also does not suggest that the underlying complaint can not now be processed. Appellant's complaint should not have been dismissed merely because appellant sought enforcement of a purported settlement agreement. The Commission reiterates that appellant is not choosing "reinstatement"; his complaint was always there to be processed because it had never been settled.

The agency argues that appellant failed to exhaust his administrative remedies by not appealing the agency's August 31, 1993 determination finding that there was no settlement agreement to the Commission. Appellant, however, was not obligated to appeal the August 31, 1993 determination and is not appealing the August 31, 1993 decision in the instant decision. Rather, appellant reasonably requested on November 17, 1993 that the administrative judge recommence processing the complaint and reasonably and properly requested on January 17, 1994 that the agency recommence processing the complaint. The Commission finds that the exhaustion of administrative remedies argument does not apply to the instant situation--ultimately because appellant is now properly appealing the agency's decision received on March 27, 1995.

The Commission finds that after the August 31, 1993 decision the agency should have, without any prompting from appellant, continued processing the underlying complaint. The Commission finds that appellant was not obligated to make any request to "reinstate" his complaint because the agency was obligated to continue processing the complaint once it determined on August 31, 1993 that there was no settlement agreement. Furthermore, contrary to the agency's assertions on appeal, the August 31, 1993 decision did not inform appellant that he had to take any action to ensure that the agency would resume processing of his complaint. The 30 day deadline specified in the August 31, 1993 decision referred to appellant's right to appeal the August 31, 1993 determination to the Commission. The August 31, 1993 decision did not indicate how appellant could request resumption of the processing of his complaint. Because of the confusing procedural history of this case we find it useful to summarize our finding in this matter: The December 19, 1991 complaint was not settled and once the agency determined that the complaint had not been settled and that appellant was not appealing that decision to the Commission, then the agency should have resumed processing the complaint from the point processing had ceased.

It appears to the Commission that the processing of the complaint had ceased while appellant was waiting for a hearing from an administrative judge. Therefore, we shall remand the matter so that the agency can request that the Commission assign an administrative judge to conduct a hearing on the December 19, 1991 complaint.

The simple moral of the story is that there can be no enforcement of a settlement agreement unless there is, in fact, a settlement agreement. See also "Voiding Settlement Agreements," below.

When several provisions of an agreement are unenforceable and the remainder lack consideration, the entire agreement is void. Marty v. Postmaster General, 01A46019 (2006). However, if any benefits have been conferred on the complainant, they must be returned for the complaint to be reinstated.
