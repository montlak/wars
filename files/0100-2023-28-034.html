E. CERTIFICATION DECISION

The administrative judge must issue a decision on whether to accept or dismiss the class complaint. The decision is not a recommended decision, and therefore the agency must either implement the decision or appeal it. Pursuant to 29 CFR 1614.204(d)(7):

The administrative judge shall transmit his or her decision to accept or dismiss a complaint to the agency and the agent. The agency shall take final action by issuing a final order within 40 days of receipt of the hearing record and administrative judge's decision. The final order shall notify the agent whether or not the agency will implement the decision of the administrative judge. If the final order does not implement the decision of the administrative judge, the agency shall simultaneously appeal the administrative judge's decision in accordance with 1614.403 and append a copy of the appeal to the final order. A dismissal of a class complaint shall inform the agent either that the complaint is being filed on that date as an individual complaint of discrimination and will be processed under subpart A or that the complaint is also dismissed as an individual complaint in accordance with 1614.107. In addition, it shall inform the agent of the right to appeal the dismissal of the class complaint to the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission or to file a civil action and shall include EEOC Form 573, Notice of Appeal/Petition.

The administrative judge must issue a certification decision, and does not have the authority to remand the case to the agency in lieu of issuing a decision. In Howard v. Secretary of Commerce, 01956455 (1997), the administrative judge remanded the case to the agency to permit designation of a new class representative. The Commission noted that 29 CFR 1614.204(d)(7) requires the administrative judge to transmit a decision, and does not provide for a remand.

Despite what appears to be the plain language meaning of section 204(d)(7), the Commission has held that an agency lacks standing to appeal the decision of an administrative judge denying class certification. Tyler v. Postmaster General, 07A50048 (2006).

The doctrines of res judicata and collateral estoppel do not preclude a class representative from redefining a class and seeking certification even though certification of a previous proposed class has been denied. In Turner, et al. v. Attorney General, 0720060041 (2007), the Commission rejected such an argument by the agency, explaining:

There is no question that the doctrine of collateral estoppel, or issue preclusion, applies to discrimination claims. See, e.g., Fitz-Gerald v. Term. Valley Auth., EEOC Request No. 05910573 (Jan. 16, 1992). However, we have recognized that "suits addressed to particular claims may present issues relevant to suits on other claims. Thus, issue preclusion bars the relitigation of issues actually adjudicated and necessary to the judgment in a prior litigation between the parties. Ormond v. Dep't of Justice, EEOC Appeal No. 01A63227 (Oct. 19, 2006) (quoting Magallanes v. Dep't of Justice, EEOC Request No. 05900176 (July 13, 1990) (emphasis added); Caple v. United States Postal Serv., EEOC Request No. 05940808 (Mar. 28, 1996). This means that for the preclusion doctrine to apply there must be a decision on the merits of the case. Collateral estoppel does not apply here because the December 5, 2000 class certification decision was not such a decision on the merits. See Powers v. Dep't of Transportation, EEOC Appeal No. 07A40067 (May 5, 2005) (remanding case for a reevaluation of the certification question because AJ improperly considered the merits of a class claim in making a certification decision); see also Brady v. United States Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01920136) (Feb. 11, 1992) (noting that a class certification decision is not a decision on the merits of a case).

Once the administrative judge issues a decision on merits of the class action, an appeal of certification is not properly before the Commission. Complainant v. Attorney General, 0520130561 (2014).
