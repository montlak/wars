WHISTLEBLOWER REPRISAL

Because of the heightened consideration paid to protecting whistleblowers by both the Merit Systems Protection Board and the Office of Special Counsel, whistleblower protections are stronger than they have ever been.

If the employee's disclosures are protected under the Whistleblower Protection Act, you must never give in to the temptation to retaliate through adverse personnel actions. If you do, you are committing a prohibited personnel practice under 5 USC 2302(b)(8).

Whistleblower reprisal refers to the actual or threatened taking or withholding of a personnel action in retaliation for a disclosure of fraud, waste, or abuse protected by the WPA.

Whistleblower Protection Act, Whistleblower Protection Enhancement Act of 2012, and Dr. Chris Kirkpatrick Whistleblower Protection Act of 2017

The WPA and WPEA protect employees who risk their own job security for the advancement of the public good by disclosing abuses by government personnel. The acts prohibit agencies from retaliating against employees, former employees, and applicants for employment because they disclosed information that they believed evidenced a violation of law, rule, or regulation; gross mismanagement; gross waste of funds; abuse of authority; or substantial and specific danger to public health or safety.

An agency may not use a confidentiality agreement to silence a whistleblower and may not have an employee sign a nondisclosure agreement unless it notifies the employee, using specific statutory text, that agency restrictions on disclosures are superseded by statutory rights to communicate with Congress, the Office of Special Counsel, or an Inspector General, whistleblower rights, and other statutory rights and obligations.

The Kirkpatrick WPA, as revised by the National Defense Authorization Act of 2018, enhances accountability for whistleblower retaliation, requires agencies to propose discipline for supervisors committing whistleblower retaliation, requires annual supervisor training, creates a new PPP on unauthorized access of medical records, and requires training all employees on whistleblower protections.

Mirroring the Kirkpatrick WPA's mandate to discipline supervisors who retaliate against whistleblowers, the NDAA 2018 makes whistleblower protection a job requirement. Agencies are directed to develop supervisory performance criteria that add promoting whistleblower protection as a critical element for performance. Supervisory performance appraisals must cover principles for protecting whistleblowers, such as:

  • • Whether the supervisor responded constructively to whistleblower disclosures.
  • • If the supervisor took responsible actions to resolve these disclosures.
  • • Whether the supervisor fostered an environment where employees felt comfortable approaching the appropriate officials about disclosures.

In addition, performance evaluations must also address whether the agency entered into an agreement to resolve PPP allegations on the supervisor's behalf, and how many times these agreements were made for that supervisor.

Initiating a whistleblower retaliation charge. The OSC or an employing agency can initiate a charge of reprisal for whistleblower activity.

The OSC has jurisdiction over PPP complaints with respect to a broad range of personnel actions, including appointments, promotions, details, transfers, reassignments, and decisions concerning pay, benefits, awards, education, and training. A whistleblower may file a complaint with the special counsel with respect to most personnel actions allegedly based on whistleblowing. They may file an individual right of action appeal with the Merit Systems Protection Board if the special counsel does not seek corrective action on their behalf.

An agency can bring the charge as a PPP or as a violation of agency standards of conduct. In raising a charge of whistleblower reprisal, the agency must be able to establish that the employee -- usually a supervisor, manager, or other official -- knew (or believed they knew) of a protected disclosure, and took or recommended an adverse personnel action based on it.

In addition, an employee may make a claim of whistleblower reprisal as an affirmative defense to an adverse personnel action.

Managing after a whistleblower reprisal claim. In terms of your duties as a manager, nothing significant should change after one of your employees makes a whistleblower reprisal claim. They are still expected to perform their duties and follow your directions, and you should continue to treat the employee as you do other employees. Finally, be sure to consult your HR office for specific guidance.

What is whistleblowing?

Under 5 USC 2302(b)(8)(A), protected disclosures include information an employee (or applicant) reasonably believes demonstrates:

  • • A violation of any law, rule or regulation.
  • • Gross mismanagement.
  • • Gross waste of funds.
  • • Abuse of authority.
  • • A substantial and specific danger to public health or safety.

Not protected are disclosures specifically prohibited by law or specifically required by executive order to be kept secret in the interest of national defense or the conduct of foreign affairs.

The OSC has jurisdiction over PPP complaints with respect to a broad range of personnel actions, including appointments, promotions, details, transfers, reassignments, and decisions concerning pay, benefits, awards, education and training.

A whistleblower may file a complaint with the OSC with respect to most personnel actions allegedly based on whistleblowing. They may appeal directly to the MSPB if the OSC does not seek corrective action on their behalf.

For a reprisal claim to succeed, the employee must show:

  • • A disclosure protected under the WPA was made.
  • • An adverse employment action was taken or recommended by an official with knowledge of the disclosure.
  • • Absent direct evidence of influence, the adverse action took place soon enough after the disclosure that a reasonable person could conclude the action was influenced by the disclosure.

If the employee shows the action was influenced by a protected disclosure, the agency may successfully defend by demonstrating with clear and convincing evidence that it would have taken the same action had the disclosure not taken place.

Protected disclosures

The Whistleblower Protection Act protects employees who risk their own job security for the advancement of the public good by disclosing abuses by government personnel. The act prohibits agencies from retaliating against employees, former employees, and applicants for employment because they disclosed information that they believed evidenced wrongdoing.

The Whistleblower Protection Enhancement Act of 2012 is an enhancement to the WPA. The WPEA expands the protection for disclosures of government wrongdoing; expands the coverage of those protected to include employees of the Transportation Security Administration; and adjusts administrative authorities such as those carried out by the OSC.

Prior to the enactment of the WPEA, certain disclosures were not protected. Some examples of the stronger protections afforded by the WPEA are:

  • • A disclosure is not unprotected because it was made to a supervisor or to a person who participated in an activity that the employee reasonably believed to be covered.
  • • Whistleblowers are protected even if the disclosure reveals information that was previously disclosed.
  • • The whistleblower is protected regardless of their motive.
  • • Disclosures are protected even if a long period of time has passed since the alleged wrongdoing occurred.
  • • Employees who reveal wrongdoing as part of their normal duties through normal channels may receive protection.

If an employee makes a disclosure during the normal course of their duties, the disclosure is not excluded from protection if a supervisor takes, fails to take, or threatens to take personnel action in reprisal for the disclosure.

The WPEA changes involving disclosures made to supervisors and to wrongdoers regarding their own conduct directly contradict pre-WPEA case law in most situations. Therefore, future MSPB and court decisions will reflect those revisions to the WPA.

Disclosures to the Office of Special Counsel, Inspector General, and Congress

Whistleblower law also protects qualifying disclosures made to the Office of Special Counsel, to the Inspector General of an agency, and to Congress (including any congressional committee). To be protected, disclosures to the OSC or an IG must be of information which the employee reasonably believes evidences any violation of any law, rule, or regulation, or gross mismanagement, a gross waste of funds, an abuse of authority, or a substantial and specific danger to public health or safety.

For disclosures to Congress to be protected, the employee must reasonably believe that the information evidences any violation of any law, rule, or regulation, or gross mismanagement, a gross waste of funds, an abuse of authority, or a substantial and specific danger to public health or safety. The information also must either be unclassified, or if classified, it must have been classified by the head of an agency that is not an element of the intelligence community, and it must not reveal intelligence sources and methods.

Disclosures not protected by the WPA

Disclosing certain types of information may be prohibited by statute, meaning revealing this information would not entitle the whistleblower to WPA protection. For example, revealing another individual's private health information is generally prohibited, as well as disclosing certain information specifically prohibited by law or required by executive order to be kept secret in the interest of national defense or the conduct of foreign affairs. This does not mean you should make unilateral decisions to discipline employees because you believe the disclosure was prohibited by law. Always work with HR before taking any action to ensure that you are not engaging in reprisal against a protected whistleblower.

However, it should be noted that the disclosure must be prohibited by law to be unprotected. Prohibition by agency regulation, rule, policy, or practice is not enough. In Jan. 2015, the U.S. Supreme Court made this clear. It held that an employee's disclosure of sensitive security information, which violated agency regulations, was not "specifically prohibited by law" because regulations do not qualify as a law under the statute. Therefore, the disclosure was protected.

Statements concerning lawful, discretionary policy decisions by management are not protected by the WPA. The exception is if the employee making the disclosure reasonably believes that the disclosure evidences a violation of any law, rule, or regulation; gross mismanagement; a gross waste of funds; an abuse of authority; or a substantial and specific danger to public health or safety.

Whistleblower reprisal

In raising a charge of whistleblower reprisal, the employee must be able to establish that the deciding official -- usually a supervisor, manager, or other official -- knew (or believed they knew) of a protected disclosure and took (or did not take) or recommended (or did not recommend) an adverse personnel action based on it. Here are some examples of what constitutes whistleblower reprisal:

  • • A manager removes an employee based on their knowledge that the employee engaged in whistleblowing activity.
  • • A manager threatens to take an adverse personnel action in retaliation for the employee's protected disclosure.
  • • A manager does not recommend a within grade increase for a qualified employee in retaliation for the employee's protected disclosure.
  • • A manager takes an adverse personnel action in response to a mistaken belief that an employee made a protected disclosure.

The MSPB will consider the following factors:

  • • The strength of the agency's evidence in support of its personnel action.
  • • The existence and strength of any motive to retaliate on the part of the agency's officials who were involved in the decision.
  • • And any evidence that the agency takes similar actions against employees who are not whistleblowers but who are otherwise similarly situated.

Counseling is not a covered personnel action for the purposes of proving whistleblower reprisal, even if the counseling session is documented by a memo from the supervisor to the employee.

Therefore, if you know -- or you believe you know -- one of your employees made a disclosure that may be protected by the WPA, you cannot take any retaliatory action against the employee. If you need to take a personnel action against the employee after such a disclosure is made, you must be sure that you have sufficient evidence to support taking the action.

You also need to remember that if you do end up facing a whistleblower reprisal claim, whether you had a motive to retaliate against the employee will be a factor. In addition, whether the agency takes similar actions against employees who are not whistleblowers but who are otherwise similarly situated will be considered.

Retaliatory investigations. The National Defense Authorization Act of 2018 contains a provision that holds agencies accountable for investigations that are conducted in retaliation for whistleblower activity. The OSC can petition the MSPB to order corrective action, including fees, costs, or damages reasonably incurred by an employee due to an investigation started in retaliation for whistleblowing activity. This also includes any retaliatory investigation related to whistleblowing activity like:

  • • Exercising an appeal.
  • • Filing a complaint or grievance.
  • • Helping any individual in the exercise of their whistleblower rights.
  • • Cooperating with or disclosing information to the agency's inspector general or special counsel.
  • • Refusing to obey an order that would require the individual to violate a law.

It is irrelevant whether a personnel action was actually taken as a result of the retaliatory investigation.

Managing despite whistleblowing issues

Even though managers need to be aware of the potential for retaliation claims, they should not feel threatened or forget how to manage.

Whistleblower laws do not require employees who make protected disclosures to reveal their identities to their agencies or their managers -- even if it is the manager who is accused of violating a law, rule or regulation, or of committing gross mismanagement.

Keep in mind that whistleblowers serve a vital function in keeping government honest. These men and women risk their careers and reputations to disclose graft, waste and corruption, helping to hold their agencies and colleagues accountable for misdeeds.

They can also be viewed as non-team players or disgruntled employees out to embarrass their employers. Regardless of how the employee is perceived, it is up to you to treat them just like any other member of your staff.

Remember: You can't be accused of retaliation if you are unaware of the employee's disclosures, so sometimes ignorance is bliss and will help you carry on with your day-to-day activities, free from distraction.

If you find out you are the focus of a whistleblower complaint, you should head straight for the agency's general counsel's office and find out more about the situation and your rights and protections. You should also consider outside counsel if the agency decides to take formal action against you.

But in terms of your duties as a manager, nothing significant should change. The employee is still expected to perform their duties and follow your directions. And you should continue to treat the employee as you do other employees.

Consult HR to find out what actions you should take if an employee who you know to be a whistleblower is underperforming or is involved in some form of misconduct.

In fact, one of the worst things you can do is start letting the employee break the rules or hold them to a different standard than the rest of your staff. The employee might feel like an outsider or become more sensitive after making the disclosure. If you start treating them differently, you could be setting yourself up for a retaliation complaint.

When a retaliation claim is made, the OSC looks at a number of factors, including whether the manager was aware of the protected disclosure and the length of time between the manager finding out about the disclosure and the personnel action. If the action was taken soon after the manager became aware of the disclosure, that manager better be able to justify their reasoning.

Whistleblowers can be extra sensitive about personnel actions, especially adverse ones, so not all retaliation claims are upheld. But many are.

Remember: Personnel actions should be taken against an employee based on their conduct or performance and not on the content of any disclosure they may have made.

Individual right of action appeal

Most employees with complaints about whistleblower retaliation bring them to the OSC. Employees who fail to get favorable OSC action have the right to file appeals -- known as individual right of action appeals -- with the MSPB.

To succeed, the employee must show they made a protected disclosure and that the protected disclosure was a contributing factor in the agency's personnel action.

If the employee shows that the protected disclosure was a contributing factor in the personnel action, the agency may successfully defend the action by showing that it would have taken the same action had the disclosure not taken place.

However, you should be aware that your agency may decide to settle, even if there seems little reason to do so on the merits. The settlement of a case may be completely unrelated to whether the agency thinks the manager did anything wrong. Rather, the decision to settle is motivated by a desire to avoid the cost of defending the action.

Knowledge and timing

The knowledge/timing test allows an employee to demonstrate that their disclosure was a contributing factor in a personnel action and that the personnel action occurred within a period of time such that a reasonable person could conclude the disclosure was a contributing factor in the personnel action.

The MSPB will look at a manager's knowledge of the protected disclosure and the timing of the adverse action. Let us look at what the knowledge and timing requirement is and how it works.

Example: A supervisory trial attorney complained to the MSPB that she disclosed incidents of gross mismanagement, abuse of authority, and violations of laws, rules or regulations, including misrepresentations made by her supervisor, in her district office.

About a month later, her supervisor gave her a performance evaluation that included a "minimally successful" rating. Her agency also reassigned her to a trial attorney position six weeks after making the disclosure, which she alleged the deciding official knew about.

Under the knowledge/timing test, this employee met the threshold test for showing her whistleblower disclosures were a contributing factor in the performance evaluation and the agency's decision to reassign her because of the knowledge of the supervisor and the proximity between the action and her evaluation. She established that the MSPB had jurisdiction over her individual right of action appeal.

Although a deciding official may not know the details of an employee's disclosure, the fact that they knew the employee made the disclosure is sufficient to establish the knowledge element of the knowledge/timing test.

An employee also can show a disclosure was a contributing factor in a personnel action by stating that the official taking the personnel action had an implied knowledge of the disclosure.

When analyzing timing, the relevant inquiry is the time between when the agency official taking the action had actual or constructive knowledge of the disclosure -- not necessarily the date of the disclosure itself -- and the time the action was taken. The MSPB will look at whether the length of time between the employee's disclosure and the agency's decision to take a personnel action is sufficiently close that a reasonable person could conclude that the disclosure was a contributing factor.

However, some cases have found that an interval of more than two years is too long to show that a disclosure contributed to a personnel action.

Discipline and supervisor training

The Dr. Chris Kirkpatrick Whistleblower Protection Act of 2017, as revised by the National Defense Authorization Act of 2018, enhances accountability for whistleblower retaliation, including requiring agencies to propose discipline for supervisors committing whistleblower retaliation. The act provides for alternative ways for a whistleblower reprisal charge to be initiated against a supervisor. The law also prescribes the penalties to be sought for a first and second instance of a supervisor engaging in whistleblower retaliation.

While the act states that an action carried out under the new provisions is subject to the same requirements and procedures (including appeal procedures) as other Chapter 75 adverse actions, there are Chapter 75 requirements that do not apply. They are:

  • • At least 30 days' advance written notice.
  • • A reasonable time of at least seven days to respond.
  • • The option of a hearing.

Proposed disciplinary action. Under the act, if the head of an agency, an administrative law judge, the MSPB, the special counsel, a judge of the United States, or the inspector general of an agency determines that a supervisor has committed a prohibited personnel action, the head of the agency:

  • • For the first prohibited personnel action committed, must propose suspending the supervisor for not less than three days and may, in addition to the suspension, propose any other action, including a reduction in grade or pay, that the head of the agency determines appropriate.
  • • For the second prohibited personnel action committed, must propose removing the supervisor.

A "prohibited personnel action" is taking or failing to take an action against an employee. The statute describes the following PPPs:

  • • Retaliating against an employee for whistleblowing activity.
  • • Retaliating against employees or applicants for exercising their appeal rights, for testifying or cooperating with an inspector general or the OSC, or for refusing to comply with an instruction that would require the employee to break the law.
  • • Accessing the medical record of another employee or an applicant for employment as a part of, or in furtherance of, any conduct described in the other PPPs.

Notice and opportunity to respond. When an action is proposed against a supervisor, they are entitled to written notice stating the specific reasons for the proposed action and informing them of their right to review the material which was relied on to support the reasons given in the proposal notice. Once the supervisor is notified of the proposed action, they are entitled to 14 days to answer and furnish evidence in support of the answer.

After the end of the 14-day period, if the supervisor does not furnish evidence supporting their answer or if the head of the agency determines that the evidence is not sufficient to reverse the proposed action, the head of the agency must carry out the proposed action.

Training. The Kirkpatrick WPA requires agencies to train new supervisors and all other supervisors annually on responding to complaints alleging whistleblower protections violations. The head of your agency must consult with the OSC and the agency inspector general in preparing the training. This measure is intended to increase awareness of whistleblower protections at the managerial levels of your agency.

Whistleblower protections

The Dr. Chris Kirkpatrick Whistleblower Protection Act of 2017, as revised by the National Defense Authorization Act of 2018, provides additional protections to federal employees who are retaliated against for disclosing waste, fraud, or abuse in the federal government. To ensure all agencies are providing crucial information about whistleblower protections to their employees, the act sets a deadline for agencies to provide their employees with the required information. The act also provides more detail on what type of information the agency needs to include when it informs its employees of their general whistleblower rights.

Agency responsibilities. Your agency is accountable to ensure all employees are well-informed of the facets comprising whistleblower protections and practices. The head of each agency is responsible for:

  • • Preventing PPPs.
  • • Ensuring (in consultation with the OSC and the agency's inspector general) that employees are informed of the rights and remedies available to them, including:
  • • Information regarding whistleblower protections available to new employees during their probationary period.
  • • The role of the OSC and the MSPB with regard to whistleblower protections.
  • • How to make a lawful disclosure of information that is specifically required by law or executive order to be kept classified in the interest of national defense or the conduct of foreign affairs to authorities designated to receive such disclosures.

The head of each agency must ensure that this information regarding whistleblower protections is provided to each new employee not later than 180 days after the employee's start date. In addition, this information must be available on the public website of the agency and on any online portal that is available only to employees.

Information regarding suicide by employees who have made protected disclosures. The Kirkpatrick WPA also requires agencies to refer information about employee suicides to the OSC if the agency took a personnel action after the employee made potential whistleblower disclosures. The head of the agency must receive written permission from the next of kin of the deceased employee before making a referral.

The head of an agency must refer to the OSC any information indicating that:

  • • An employee of the agency committed suicide;
  • • Prior to the death of the employee, the employee made any disclosure of information which reasonably evidences:
  • ○ Any violation of any law, rule, or regulation; or
  • ○ Gross mismanagement, a gross waste of funds, an abuse of authority, or a substantial and specific danger to public health or safety;
  • • After the disclosure, a personnel action was taken against the employee.

The head of the agency must also provide the OSC with any information known to the agency regarding the disclosure(s) and the personnel action.

Mistakes to avoid

Whistleblowing is a tricky area, and you would best be served to avoid these pitfalls.

Do not:

  • • Consider a whistleblower a complainer. Whistleblowers should not be seen as a problem, but a potential benefit to the federal government. Whistleblowers are federal employees or applicants who risk their careers to disclose information they believe shows a violation of law, rule or regulation, gross mismanagement, gross waste of funds, abuse of authority, or a substantial and specific danger to public health or safety.
  • • Retaliate. If you find out who the whistleblower is, do not retaliate against them. Whistleblowing is a protected activity. Retaliating against a whistleblower, even by doing something as minor as denying them sick leave out of spite, is prohibited.
  • • Give the whistleblower special treatment. Trying to smooth things over by affording the whistleblower special privileges or looking the other way if they break regulations can worsen the situation.
  • • Keep HR out of the loop. Remember to rely on HR for any questions or concerns you have about what to do in these situations. Don't act alone.
  • • Confuse protected disclosures with unprotected statements. You are allowed to lawfully exercise discretionary authority concerning policy decisions. An employee who disagrees with a decision you made and discloses it in the context of whistleblowing is making an unprotected statement and therefore is not protected by the WPA.

Ask FMG

Q. What does an employee have to show to win their whistleblower reprisal claim?

A. They need to show they made a protected disclosure as described in 5 USC 2302(b)(8) and that the protected disclosure was a contributing factor in the agency's personnel action taken against them.

Q. What can an agency do if an employee has enough evidence to show that their protected disclosure contributed to the personnel action taken against them?

A. The agency would need to demonstrate by clear and convincing evidence that it would have taken the same action even if the employee didn't make the disclosure.

The Merit Systems Protection Board will consider the following factors: 1) the strength of the agency's evidence in support of its personnel action; 2) the existence and strength of any motive to retaliate on the part of the agency's officials who were involved in the decision; and 3) any evidence that the agency takes similar actions against employees who are not whistleblowers but who are otherwise similarly situated.

Q. What kind of corrective action can the MSPB order if it finds that an agency violated the WPA?

A. The board can order an agency to rescind a personnel action and to reinstate the employee, provide them with back pay, and even grant a retroactive promotion. The employee also has a statutory right to pursue consequential damages and corrective action, including attorney's fees and discipline against any official who may have retaliated against them, as well as future medical expenses resulting from an agency's retaliation that can be proven with reasonable certainty.

In addition, corrective action may occur if an agency conducts a retaliatory investigation. Following a petition by the OSC, the MSPB can order corrective action including fees, costs, or damages reasonably incurred by an employee, if an investigation was commenced, expanded, or extended in retaliation for whistleblowing or related activity.

Q. What are the burden of proof and degree of proof in whistleblower retaliation appeals?

A. In whistleblower appeals, the employee has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that whistleblowing was a contributing factor in the personnel action threatened, taken, or not taken against them.

The board will order the agency to correct the action if the employee can demonstrate that the whistleblowing was a contributing factor in the personnel action. The employee may demonstrate that the whistleblowing was a contributing factor by showing that the official or supervisor taking the action knew about the whistleblowing and that the action occurred within a time period such that a reasonable person would conclude that the whistleblowing was a contributing factor in the personnel action.

The MSPB will not order corrective action, however, if the agency demonstrates by clear and convincing evidence that it would have taken the same action in the absence of the whistleblowing. The clear and convincing standard of proof is a higher standard than the preponderance of the evidence standard that you must meet.

Q. What should a manager do if they believe they are the target of a whistleblower reprisal claim?

A. The manager should head straight to the agency's general counsel's office and find out more about the situation and their rights and protections. They should also consider outside counsel if the agency decides to take formal action against them. But in terms of their duties as a manager, nothing should change. They should continue to treat all their employees the same way.

Q. What is the knowledge/timing test and how does it apply?

A. Congress established the knowledge/timing test that allows an employee to demonstrate that their disclosure was a contributing factor in a personnel action taken against them and that the personnel action occurred within a period of time such that a reasonable person could conclude the disclosure was a contributing factor in the personnel action.
