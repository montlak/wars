17. Reassignment to Different Supervisor

A common allegation is that a particular supervisor or supervisors are, through harassment, actually causing or aggravating a disabling condition. As reasonable accommodation for such a condition, the employee usually seeks either his or her own reassignment to a different supervisor, or the reassignment of the offending supervisor. With few exceptions, these complaints are generally unsuccessful. The principal problem is presenting persuasive medical evidence that isolates the supervisor as the source of the problem and demonstrating that the employee could perform the essential functions of the position under a different supervisor. This problem is compounded by the Commission's Enforcement Guidance on Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship under the ADA, "Types of Reasonable Accommodations Related to Job Performance, Reassignment," which categorically states that reassignment to a new supervisor is not required as reasonable accommodation. See also Morris-Johnson v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A21624 (2003).

For example, in Day v. Secretary of Army, 03910078 (1991), the employee was removed for inability to perform the essential functions of the position. The employee alleged that her anxiety condition was the result of harassment by her supervisors and requested a change of supervision or reassignment as an accommodation. The Commission found that the complainant was an individual with a disability, but noted there was no persuasive medical evidence as to the cause of her condition or that she could perform the essential functions under different supervision. Of particular significance, the Commission rejected the opinion of the employee's psychiatrist because he had never met the involved supervisors and never observed the employee in the work environment. A genuine proof problem is presented in these cases because an agency will likely be unwilling to permit an employee's psychiatrist to interview supervisors or to directly observe work performance and interactions. See also Corie E. v. Postmaster General, 2021002204 (2022) ("There is nothing in this case that suggests the need to deviate from this general rule. Because the Agency was not obligated to provide Complainant with a reassignment away from his supervisors in Derry, we find that the Agency did not err in denying her reasonable accommodation requests for reassignment.").
