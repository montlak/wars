broida eeo Chapter 02: The Precomplaint Process--Counseling Chapter 02: The Precomplaint Process--Counseling, Subchapter 04: Requests for Counseling, Section E: Contact Not Involving Counselors, Subsection 01: "Logically Connected"/Wrong Agency Officials, Part a: Background Caselaw
a. Background Caselaw

In prior years, the general rule was that only contact with an EEO counselor would initiate the precomplaint counseling process. Contact with an EEO specialist at the employing agency would not meet the requirement for initiating counseling. Long v. Department of Air Force, 01842751 (1985).

However, many agencies had, and still have, an EEO officer coordinating the activities of counselors, including assignment of cases. In such instances, contacting the EEO officer did constitute an initiation of precomplaint counseling. See Evans v. USPS, 01842865 (1985); see also In re National Archives, 05870080 (1987) (when an EEO specialist or manager is responsible for assigning counselors, the date of contact with that person is the relevant one for determining the timeliness of the complaint).

The requirement is that a counselor be contacted within the regulatory time period. The fact that actual counseling, due to backlogs, does not begin before the end of the time period does not make such a complaint untimely. See Evans v. USPS, supra.

Some indication that the Commission was backing off from earlier rulings regarding mistaken contact of the wrong agency official began in 1987. In In re Veterans Administration, 05850095 (1987), the Commission soundly rejected an agency argument that contact with the EEO counselor was untimely where initial contact was with an EEO manager. In so ruling, the Commission noted "it is clear that the intent of EEOC Regulations is not to get tangled in such word games." This view undoubtedly is correct, especially when the complainant contacts someone in the agency with EEO responsibilities. If nothing else, anyone with such responsibilities should be held accountable for promptly informing a potential complainant that the proper course is to contact a counselor and to provide the complainant with the names and locations of counselors at the agency. However, prior to In re Veterans Administration, it was not entirely clear that the Commission consistently subscribed to that view.

The Commission's interpretation of the counselor contact requirement was broadened again in McGourty v. Secretary of Interior, 05870576 (1988). In that case, the Commission held that the agency improperly dismissed a complaint as untimely where the employee contacted an EEO investigator and the investigator waited three months before advising the employee to contact a counselor. The Commission noted that "a layman could reasonably conclude that a prior complaint is amendable at a later date by raising a new allegation before the EEO Investigator, particularly where reprisal for the prior complaint is alleged." Still, in the wake of McGourty it was unclear whether the Commission believed there was a "bright-line" rule that EEO officials have an obligation to properly advise a prospective complainant when contacted, or whether the Commission's decision was restricted to the amendment of complaints already under investigation. With its 1999 amendments to Part 1614, the Commission now permits the amendment of formal complaints that are like or related to a pending complaint. 29 CFR 1614.106(d). In such cases, counseling on the new issues is no longer required.

Finally, in Baldwin v. Secretary of Treasury, 05890561 (1989), the Commission found it would handle cases of contact with the wrong agency official on a case-by-case basis when the contact was with an official logically connected to the EEO process. In that case, notification of the EEO program director in Washington was deemed insufficient where the complainant was clearly put on notice in his termination letter that he must contact an EEO counselor within 30 days and the names and telephone numbers of two counselors were provided. See also Shank v. Social Security Administration, 01982800 (1999) (applying a "logically connected" standard to contact with an EEO investigator to make an allegation of retaliation); Stephens v. Secretary of Air Force, 01912482 (1991). But see Samual H. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120152557 (2015) (complainant terminated in July did not contact a counselor until September, but within 45 days sent email to official at headquarters asking for the EEO contact in Wyoming).
