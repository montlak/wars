7. Scope of Relief

As noted above, the standard for relief is to, as nearly as possible, put the victim of discrimination in the position he or she would have been in but for the discrimination. Albemarle, supra. A corollary of that rule, however, is that the victim is not entitled to be placed in a better position than he or she would have been in but for the discrimination. In many cases, the remedy is clear-cut. In other cases, though, it is hard to tell where the "but for" ends and independent events take over. Many of these specific situations are addressed below, but the fact that an employee would not have been in a certain position but for discrimination does not entitle the employee to relief for subsequent events when there is an intervening cause.

As an example, in Banks v. Postmaster General, 0720070043 (2008), the employee, a probationary employee, had her position at one post office terminated. She was assigned to another post office to continue her probationary period and her employment from that position was terminated when she lost some post office box keys. The administrative judge specifically found that the termination of the first assignment was discriminatory and that the subsequent termination of employment was not discriminatory. However, he ordered the employee reinstated reasoning that but for the initial discrimination she would not have been in a position to be terminated from the second position. The Commission, in Banks, rejected that reasoning:

The Commission agrees with the AJ that the agency discriminated against complainant when it terminated her from the Dundalk Station on February 7, 2004. Although the agency argued otherwise, we find that the AJ's finding of discrimination with respect to the first termination is supported by substantial evidence in the record. Nevertheless, we disagree with the remedy he awarded to complainant because, after her termination from Dundalk Station, complainant was assigned to another station to continue her probationary period. While at the new station, complainant was terminated from her position on February 18, 2004, after losing accountable keys to postal boxes. In spite of finding the second termination was not discriminatory, the AJ determined that, for purposes of determining appropriate relief, the second termination "would not have occurred but for the first" and that it was "irrelevant" and "should be ignored." AJ Decision on Damages (December 23, 2006), at 7. We find, however, that the AJ erred as a matter of law when he considered complainant's second termination in framing the remedy for the discrimination proven. To do so allows for a damage award that inappropriately encompasses the second termination on which the AJ found no discrimination. Therefore, because the AJ found that the second termination was not discriminatory, the remedy for complainant must be adjusted.

With respect to reinstatement and back pay, the AJ ordered the agency to reinstate complainant and provide her back pay from the date of her termination through the date she was reinstated or declined the agency's offer. We, however, find that she is not entitled to reinstatement because the agency has established that she was terminated on February 18, 2004, for nondiscriminatory reasons. Moreover, complainant is entitled to back pay solely for the period from February 7, 2004, the date she was terminated from the Dundalk Station, to February 18, 2004, the date she was terminated from the other station where she lost accountable keys.
