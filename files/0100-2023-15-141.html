4. Failure to Produce Evidence

When a party fails to produce evidence pursuant to an order of the administrative judge, it is proper for the judge to draw not only an adverse inference against that party, but to exclude other evidence offered by that party related to the same point.

In Riddle v. USPS, 01860645 (1987), the complainant was terminated for defacing an agency document. During the course of the agency's investigation of the matter, a handwriting expert determined that the writing was that of the complainant. The complainant's attorney then sought production of the original document for analysis by his own expert and the agency refused to produce it. The agency also refused to produce a witness for testimony. The administrative judge not only credited the testimony of complainant's expert based on examination of a copy, but prohibited the agency from introducing any evidence tending to link the complainant to the document in question.

In upholding its administrative judge, the Commission in Riddle stated:

A second argument made by the agency is that the Administrative Judge was mistakenly applying the federal rules of discovery in trying to compel surrender of the evidence. The agency states that the Code of Federal Regulations make no provision for discovery or the use of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.

The Commission has recognized in its prior decisions that the hearing and appeal processes in an EEO matter are continuations of the investigative process. [T]he person conducting the investigation is authorized to:

(1) ...investigate all aspects of complaints of discrimination, (2) to require all employees of the agency to cooperate with him in the conduct of the investigation, and (3) to require employees of the agency having any knowledge of the matter complained of to furnish testimony under oath or affirmation without a pledge of confidence.

Thus, the agency has a duty to cooperate fully with the EEO investigative process. From the time appellant's attorney first requested the production of the original...for examination the agency was on notice that this would be an issue in the case and that the EEO investigation might eventually require examination of the document. Thus the agency had duty to preserve the documents and maintain them under agency control until the EEO process settled the issue.

Furthermore, under 29 CFR Section 1613.218(c) the Administrative Judge is given the authority to

conduct the hearing so as to bring out pertinent facts, including the production of pertinent documents. Rules of evidence shall not be applied strictly.... Information having a bearing on the complaint or employment policy or practices relevant to the complaint shall be received in evidence. The complainant, his representative, and the representatives of the agency at the hearing shall be given the opportunity to cross-examine witnesses who appear and testify.

Thus, while rules of evidence are not strictly applied, the Administrative Judge does have the authority to request the production of documents and the duty to preserve underlying fairness of the hearing.

In order to ensure fairness, the Commission held the administrative judge in Riddle was within her authority in preventing the agency from presenting any evidence tending to link the complainant with the document in question. As noted above, the Commission has now adopted this as a regulatory stance and specifically authorizes the imposition of sanctions for failure to comply with discovery orders. See 29 CFR 1614.109(f).

A similar result was obtained in Kitchen v. National Labor Relations Board, 0720110017 (2013), where the agency did not produce documents for the hearing in accordance with the prehearing order of the administrative judge and then attempted to introduce the documents at hearing. The Commission in Kitchen found that the judge properly excluded the documents as well as the testimony of witnesses related to the documents:

By pre-hearing order, the Agency was required to identify and provide copies of the documents that it intended to use at hearing and instead brought them in at the eleventh hour. The Agency knew or should have known that these documents, which related to alleged examples of Complainant's work product, existed. To allow their admission at the eleventh hour would be prejudicial to Complainant's ability to respond to the documents which could have been handpicked for litigation purposes. Likewise, to have witnesses testify to what the Agency knew or should have known would have been prejudicial to Complainant's preparedness and ability to defend.

Likewise, in Starr v. Postmaster General, 01982530 (1999), the Commission found an adverse inference by the administrative judge was proper where the agency refused to respond to a discovery request about the method it used to adjust the complainant's route. Finding that the requested information was critical for preparing to cross-examine the agency witness produced at the hearing to explain the methodology, the Commission upheld an adverse inference drawn by the judge that allowed the complainant to demonstrate the agency's reasons for the changes were pretextual. Id.; see also Van Desteene v. Postmaster General, 01975928 (1999) (an adverse inference is appropriate where an agency fails to produce documents related to the discipline it imposed on the complainant).

An adverse inference is proper when the agency refuses to produce documentation regarding its candidate evaluation procedure if the hearing issue relates to whether or not the complainant's evaluation was discriminatory. Without such documentation, the Commission is unable to determine if the complainant's evaluation was conducted in accordance with proper procedures. Bertels v. Postmaster General, 01890365 (1989).

Striking the agency's legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason was appropriate as a sanction where the agency twice failed to produce documents ordered by the administrative judge prior to the hearing. The agency did finally produce the documents on the third day of the hearing, but the administrative judge noted that by then, witnesses had already been called and excused. Zoila P. v. Attorney General, 0720130036 (2015). The Commission rejected an agency argument that the sanction of excluding the documents, a less severe sanction, was appropriate, noting that the documents could have been helpful to the complainant had she received them prior to the hearing.

An agency cannot be faulted for failing to produce documents relative to a selection where the complainant changes tactics at the hearing and shifts the focus from the relative qualifications of himself and the selectee, to the ranking process used by a screening committee. It would not be appropriate to draw an adverse inference under these circumstances. The Commission noted that it would have been preferable for the administrative judge to adjourn the hearing to allow production of the documents, but the error could be cured by remanding the complaint for a supplemental investigation. Fausto v. Secretary of Interior, 05900543 (1990).

In Comer v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01912386 (1991), the Commission held that the administrative judge erred in drawing an adverse inference against the agency for failing to produce a document because there was no showing of bad faith on the part of the agency. The administrative judge had further prevented the agency from explaining what might have happened to the document and what it possibly contained. The Comer case does not mean that a party must show bad faith in the failure to produce documents in order to receive the benefit of an adverse inference. The Commission's former regulations, at 29 CFR 1613.218(e), provided that an adverse inference may be drawn if the failure was in bad faith or if the party failing to produce the document did not tender an adequate explanation for its actions. In Comer, the administrative judge erred by not permitting the agency to offer an explanation and then ruling on its adequacy.

When the destruction of records is at issue, the fact that such destruction is contrary to agency regulation does not require a finding of discrimination. Grantham v. Secretary of Treasury, 05930539 (1993). Of course, such evidence may be combined with other evidence to support a finding of discrimination. For example, it may be relevant to a finding of discrimination if the agency routinely keeps such notes but did not in particular case. However, in Grantham, the agency demonstrated that the office responsible for the selection had a policy of destroying all interview notes, even though it was contrary to agency regulation. But see also section on "Sanctions Against Agencies" in Chapter 8.

Where the agency relies on internal investigations to take actions against an employee that are the subject of an EEO claim, it is error for the administrative judge not to order production of the reports when the complainant requests them. "[W]e find it reasonable that complainant would want to review these reports to search for any inconsistencies between earlier statements...and the testimony at the hearing." Jones v. Secretary of Defense, 0120080673 (2010).
