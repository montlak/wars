9. Fear of Reprisal

Fear of further adverse personnel actions does not result in a waiver of the limitations period. See Breanne H. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 2020004873 (2021) (nonselection case; rejecting complainant's argument that extension was warranted because she was prevented from timely contacting an EEO Counselor reasoning that as a "paramilitary organization," the Agency "frowns upon" questioning orders or the underlying reasons for an order.); see also Complainant v. Secretary of Army, 0120151199 (2015); Alejandro B. v. Secretary of Navy, 0120141148 (2015); Lee v. Social Security Administration, 01A20661 (2002); Tocco v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01994164 (2001). An appellant who does not file a timely complaint over a performance rating because it might influence her chances for selection for a promotional opportunity is deemed to have waived her rights by inactivity. Ayers v. Department of Army, 01841314 (1985).

Likewise, the Commission has held that "fear of reprisal is insufficient justification" for waiving the time period for contacting a counselor in cases involving hostile environment harassment. Sztukowski v. Secretary of Treasury, 01960956 (1997).

Although fear of retaliation generally does not excuse untimely EEO contact, the Commission in Armstrong v. Postmaster General, 01965904 (1997), found that the supervisor's threat to fire the appellant on the spot, coupled with the appellant's fear that he would have to leave the country if unemployed due to his immigration status, warranted a waiver of the time limits:

On December 20, 1993, appellant met with Supervisor C to discuss the December 18 incident. Also present were Supervisors A and B and the appellant's union steward and union president. At the end of the meeting, Supervisor C asked everyone but appellant and the union president to leave the room. Supervisor C then asked appellant what he planned to do about the incident. Appellant stated that he intended to file a grievance for the lost pay and an EEO complaint against Supervisors A and B. Supervisor C stated that if appellant pursued an EEO complaint, he would fire appellant "right now." Supervisor C further stated that he knew appellant could not afford to be out of work for the year or more it would take to process and resolve appellant's complaint.

...

The agency also dismissed appellant's allegations on the alternative grounds of failing to contact an EEO Counselor within forty-five days. Specifically, the agency found that the alleged discriminatory acts occurred on September 16, December 18, and December 20, 1993, and that appellant did not contact an EEO Counselor until September 19, 1995, well beyond the forty-five day limitation.

On appeal, appellant asserts that the delay in filing his formal complaint was a result of the threat made by his supervisor on December 20, 1993, that appellant would be fired if he filed an EEO complaint. The record contains a statement from appellant's union president stating that he affirms appellant's claim regarding the actions of Supervisor C. Appellant further asserts that he was an immigrant whose conditional resident status would be endangered if he was unemployed. Appellant alleges that his supervisor knew of his immigrant status "and used it to ensure my silence about his discriminatory treatment of me." When appellant's employment was terminated, appellant claimed that his supervisor's threat became a nullity and appellant immediately contacted an EEO Counselor.

EEOC Regulation 29 CFR 1614.604(c) provides that these time limits are subject to waiver, estoppel and equitable tolling. We find in this case that equitable considerations require waiving the forty-five day time limit for contacting an EEO Counselor. Appellant alleges that Supervisor C threatened to fire appellant if he brought an EEO complaint. The record contains evidence supporting appellant's allegation. The threat made by Supervisor C constituted an act of severe intimidation that prevented appellant from contacting an EEO Counselor in a timely fashion and justifies an extension of the applicable time limit.

Similarly, in Harris v. Secretary of Air Force, 0120103332 (2012), the Commission extended the time limit for counselor contact where the complainant submitted specific evidence that his life had been threatened if he disclosed the harassment:

In the instant case, Complainant contacted the EEO Counselor on September 28, 2009 regarding events that occurred as recently as 2008. Complainant's contact is well beyond the forty-five day time limitation. However, Complainant claims that he was unable to contact an EEO Counselor because of his incapacitation and fear of physical harm. Although the Commission routinely holds that fear of reprisal is not sufficient grounds for extending the time frames, see Duncan v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 05970315 (July 10, 1998); Kovarik v. Dep't of Def., EEOC Request No. 05930898 (Dec. 9, 1993), we find this case is distinguishable.

In the instant case, Complainant alleges that S1 made a threat against his life if he ever informed anyone about the harassment. Complainant's account of the threat is specific. Two statements from Complainant's brother and niece, report that Complainant informed them that a threat against his life had been made. Accordingly, in light of the evidence to support Complainant's legitimate fear of violent, physical reprisal, the Commission finds reason to extend the time frames in this matter.

The discrimination statutes contain provisions protecting employees from reprisal for filing complaints. See Chapter 17.
