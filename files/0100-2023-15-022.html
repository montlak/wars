D. RIGHT TO NEUTRAL ADMINISTRATIVE JUDGE

Under the previous regulations, at 29 CFR 1614.109(a) (1992), the administrative judge designated to conduct an EEO hearing was required to be an EEOC administrative judge or an employee of another agency. The new regulations are silent about the matter, but MD-110, at 7-1 provides that administrative judges from the EEOC will adjudicate claims of discrimination. The agency's notice of the right to a hearing should clearly state that the administrative judge will be an administrative judge from the EEOC to prevent a chilling effect on the exercise of hearing rights. See Stacy v. Department of Health and Human Services, 01821693 (1983). The failure of the hearing notice to clearly state that the administrative judge is not an employee of the involved agency is harmful error and the case will be remanded to provide the complainant with an opportunity to assert the right to a hearing. See Coleman v. Secretary of Navy, 01922117 (1993). When an EEOC employee is the complainant, the right to a neutral hearing official still applies and the case may not be heard by an EEOC administrative judge. In such cases, the Commission must appoint an independent contract administrative judge. See Logan-King v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 05A10082 (2002).

The right to a neutral administrative judge does not mean that the judge is required to sit idly by while one party takes advantage of the other, particularly when complainants are proceeding pro se. In Moss v. Secretary of Defense, 01902252 (1990), the Commission found it was error for the administrative judge to hold a pro se litigant to the same standards as agency counsel. The Commission noted:

The AJ is under a duty to allow a full and fair hearing, and to allow appellant to produce a full disclosure of all relevant facts and issues; in this case the AJ did not. The hearing is intended to obtain all relevant facts pertaining to appellant's claim. In furtherance of this, and in recognition that many of the EEO complainants appear pro se, the evidentiary standards are not strictly enforced....

Many administrative judges engage in their own questioning of witnesses, in addition to the questioning conducted by the parties. There is no prohibition against such questioning. In many instances, the administrative judge may simply be trying to clarify the record or his or her own understanding of the testimony of the witness. Clarity aside, the administrative judge is charged with assisting both parties in developing the evidence.

The administrative judge may instruct a pro se complainant on the proper method of examining witnesses. Judges' Handbook at 7-3. See also "Role of Judge," below.
