5. Failure to Assert Timeliness

An agency may be considered to have waived the time limit for initiating counseling, or to be estopped from raising it as a defense, if it is not timely asserted. The mere acceptance of an untimely allegation for investigation does not constitute a waiver. Teal v. Department of Army, 01832621 (1985). The general rule is that the agency does not waive objections to timeliness by acceptance and investigation of a complaint as long as it does not make a finding with respect to discrimination. See Kamoven v. USPS, 01831562 (1987) (citing Oaxaca v. Roscoe, 641 F.2d 386 (5th Cir. 1981)). The Commission will consider any objections as to the timeliness of a complaint to be waived when the agency issues a final decision with a finding of discrimination. Ziman v. USPS, 01842595 (1986).

The seminal case on estopping an agency from asserting a timeliness defense is Tanski v. Defense Investigative Service, 05840036 (1985). In Tanski, a recommended decision with a finding of discrimination was issued after a hearing. In the decision, the administrative judge, at that time called a complaints examiner, noted the complaint appeared to be untimely but that the agency had waived the defense by not asserting it. The agency rejected the recommended decision and dismissed the complaint on timeliness grounds. After initially upholding the decision, the Commission, on reconsideration in Tanski, reversed:

The previous decision concludes that the agency did not waive its right to reject the untimely claim. The general rule is properly stated as:

[I]f an agency accepts a complaint containing a stale, untimely filed charge of discrimination and considers it during its processing and investigation of other timely allegations in the same complaint, then that agency...will not be deemed to have automatically waived the requirement that (an Equal Employment Opportunity Counselor be contacted) within thirty (30) days of the occurrence of the alleged discriminatory act.

The previous decision also correctly noted that such an automatic waiver does not occur unless the agency has also found that discrimination exists on the merits of the claim. Oaxaca v. Roscoe, 641 F.2d 386 (5th Cir. 1981); Ward v. Califano, 443 F. Supp. 89 (D.D.C. 1977); Scott v. Weinberger, 416 F. Supp. 221 (D.D.C. 1976). However, EEOC Regulation 29 CFR 1613.214(a)(4)(ii) provides agencies with discretion to extend, or waive, time limits for reasons considered sufficient. When reasons sufficient to require extension or waiver are apparent and the agency decides instead to invoke its authority to reject an untimely claim under 29 CFR 1613.214(a)(1), the agency may be said to have abused its discretion. See De Medina v. Reinhardt, 444 F. Supp. 573, 577 n.2 (D.D.C. 1978).

It is under circumstances in which an abuse of discretion may occur through the invocation of 29 CFR 1613.214(a)(1) that agencies may be held to be estopped from asserting their authority to reject untimely claims. Id. at 577 (characterizing the result of denying agencies the right to rely upon the untimeliness of claims as a quasi-estoppel theory, citing Ward v. Califano, 443 F, Supp. 89 (D.D.C. 1978), and Scott v. Weinberger, 416 F. Supp. 221 (D.D.C. 1976), as illustrative). The fact that a Complaints Examiner rather than the agency found discrimination to be present does not present a distinction sufficient to find the estoppel theory of Ward v. Califano, 443 F. Supp. 89 and Scott v. Weinberger, 416 F. Supp. 221, to be inapplicable. The conditions for estoppel exist in this case as they exist in cases in which the agency itself finds discrimination. The agency accepted the allegation; investigated the allegation; issued a proposed disposition on the allegation; and participated fully in the hearing which focused almost exclusively on the nonselection allegation. Thus, there is no prejudice or surprise to the agency in denying its right to reject the untimely claim.

The agency, faced with the knowledge that record evidence, gathered in proceedings to which it was a party, is sufficient to lead to a reasonable conclusion that discrimination may have occurred, had sufficient reason to expressly waive the time requirements of 29 CFR 1613.214(a)(1). Although the agency did not waive the time requirements, it is estopped from asserting them, where, as in this case, to do so would constitute an abuse of the discretion conferred upon agencies by 29 CFR 1613.214(a)(4)(ii).

Furthermore, this application of estoppel furthers the policy of Title VII, as expressed in federal sector equal employment opportunity complaint processing regulations. EEOC Regulation 29 CFR 1613.203(b) requires that agencies "[c]onduct a continuing campaign to eradicate every form of prejudice or discrimination." As noted in the previous decision, it is also the Commission's policy to encourage voluntary efforts by the agency to uncover and remedy discrimination. The previous decision correctly notes that a rule which finds an automatic waiver of the time requirements merely by virtue of acceptance and processing of a complaint could undermine these policies. However, applying the general rule of no automatic waiver to a case, such as this, where the agency is aware of a recommended finding of discrimination is also subversive of the policies noted above. The result endorsed by the previous decision allows agencies, faced with record evidence which has led an independent Examiner to recommend a finding of discrimination, to avoid their obligation to confront instances of employment discrimination by refusing to exercise their discretion to waive regulatory time limits. This would permit agencies that have not asserted authority to reject the claim to avoid liability and ignore evidence of discrimination through the expediency of rejecting untimely claims. The policies expressed above are better served by a rule that estops agencies from asserting theretofore unasserted authority to reject untimely claims when faced with their own findings or a Complaints Examiner's recommended findings of discrimination, after the agency has fully participated in the evidentiary proceedings of investigation and/or hearing.

The estoppel rule, rather than a rule of constructive waiver, is also in greater conformity with the law applied in judicial opinions than is the conclusion of the previous decision. For these reasons the previous decision is reopened and reversed insofar as it held that the agency properly rejected appellant's claim. In light of the fact that the agency's rejection of the Complaints Examiner's recommended decision was limited to the exercise of its authority to reject untimely claims, rather than a rejection of the merits of the recommended finding; and in a light of the fact that the exercise of such authority was improper, the agency's final decision is ineffective to reject the Examiner's findings. The findings and relief recommended by the Complaints Examiner are reinstated and binding upon the agency.

Although it was decided prior to the 1999 amendments to Part 1614, under the Tanski rationale, an agency would be estopped from asserting timeliness on appeal to OFO where the administrative judge has issued a decision finding discrimination that the agency refuses to fully implement.

In Hill v. General Services Administration, 05890383 (1989), the Commission restated its position that acceptance and investigation of a complaint do not estop an agency from asserting a timeliness defense:

"[A] federal agency, by merely accepting and investigating a tardy complaint, automatically waive its objections to the complainant's failure to comply with prescribed time [periods]." Oaxaca v. Roscoe, 641 F.2d 386, 390 (5th Cir. 1981). See also Boyd v. United States Postal Service, 752 F.2d 410 (9th Cir. 1985). While an agency may waive or extend the applicable time limit for any "reason considered sufficient by the agency," 29 CFR 1613.214(1)(4), an agency will not have been deemed to have made such a decision as a result of mere acceptance and investigation of tardy claims since "[t]his regulation...clearly contemplates a deliberate decision by the agency to grant a waiver and not an inadvertent one as would have occurred here." Scott v. Claytor, 469 F. Supp. 22, 24 (D.D.C. 1978). Similarly, "[a]bsent an application for waiver under [1613.] 214.(a)(4), and absent any express determination by the agency that a waiver of the 30-day limit for filing administrative charges is warranted," mere acceptance and investigation of the allegations does not constitute a waiver. DeMedina v. Reinhardt, 444 F. Supp. 573, 577 (D.D.C. 1978).

Similarly, in Cramer v. Postmaster General, 01911530 (1991), the Commission reaffirmed the position it took in Tanski by holding that there is no distinction between a recommended decision by an administrative judge and a final agency decision, in terms of the doctrine of estoppel. In either case, the agency is estopped from asserting a timeliness defense.

An agency also will be estopped from raising the issue of timeliness if it already has issued specific findings on the timeliness issue. See Shields v. Secretary of Transportation, FAA, 01941919 (1994) (citing Henderson v. U.S. Veterans Administration, 790 F.2d 436, 440-41 (5th Cir. 1986)). However, an agency will not be estopped from raising the defense where it indicates the complaint may be untimely, but requests further information from the complainant prior to issuing a final decision. Horton v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01961514 (1997). When an agency requests information on the reasons for delay in contacting a counselor and then, in accepting the complaint for investigation, indicates its acceptance is based upon the complaint and information provided, it has "deliberately" waived the time period. Harris v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, 01932365 (1993).

Even if the agency takes some affirmative action to waive a timeliness defense, it may be insufficient. In Balli v. Department of Commerce, 01830726 (1983), the Commission, citing 29 CFR 1613.221(a), noted that only an agency head or his designee can issue final agency decisions on a complaint. The Commission construed the regulation to apply to dismissal of complaints on the basis of timeliness. No waiver was established by the actions of the EEO officer in recommending acceptance of the complaint despite untimely allegations. But an agency does waive the issue of timeliness when a properly issued final decision states that because the complaint has been processed the issue must be deemed to have been waived. Lawson v. Department of Navy, 01842588 (1986).

The passage of five years between the submission of the complaint and the dismissal of the allegations as untimely does not act as a waiver of the time limit. Chicarella v. Department of Army, 01810966 (1981). This is consistent with the finding in Hill v. General Services Administration, 05890383 (1989), where the Commission went on to find that the agency could not be estopped from raising the timeliness requirement. In Hill, the Commission relied on Sims v. Heckler, 725 F.2d 1143 (7th Cir. 1984), where the court refused to apply estoppel against the federal government when a complaint was in the administrative process for more than five years. In Sims, the court found that estoppel requires an affirmative act on the part of the government. Cf. Huntley v. Department of Health, Education and Welfare, 550 F.2d 290 (5th Cir. 1977) (an agency was estopped from raising the timeliness issue where the final agency decision includes a finding of discrimination).
