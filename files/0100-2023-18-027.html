4. Exclusion Through Settlement

In a very strange case, the agency and the complainant entered into a settlement agreement entered into an agreement that resolved four complainants but also provided that the complainant would "not to serve as a representative to any EEO complainants in the future, except as detailed in paragraph 8," which the AJ wrote identified four cases she was already acting on as a representative. Because of the Commission's insistence on making all complainants anonymous, the specific facts of the case are somewhat difficult to ascertain. However, it appears that the complainant who entered into the agreement became involved in representation in a complaint that was not one of the four identified in the agreement. The administrative judge found that unless the settlement agreement was declared unenforceable to OFO she had no choice but the exclude the complainant's representative.

In finding that the complainant did not have standing to claim breach of the agreement in Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120140280 (2014), the Commission held:

In her Order, the AJ found that in February 2008 Complainant's representative and an Assistant U.S. Attorney signed a settlement agreement resolving three of the representative's cases, two of which were pending in federal district court and one which was pending before the EEOC's Los Angeles District Office. The AJ recounted that in the settlement agreement the representative agreed "not to serve as a representative to any EEO complainants in the future, except as detailed in paragraph 8," which the AJ wrote identified four cases she was already acting on as a representative.

The AJ found that while she did not have jurisdiction over the enforceability of the settlement agreement or whether it was breached, there undisputedly was a settlement agreement signed by Complainant's representative waiving the representative's right to represent future EEO complainants. The AJ ruled that unless the representative gets the settlement agreement deemed unenforceable by the EEOC's Office of Federal Operations (which handles administrative appeals) or Federal Courts, she had no choice but to exclude the representative in Complainant's cases against the Agency.

On appeal Complainant argues that the exclusion of her representative constitutes a denial of due process, that the Agency should not be permitted to bribe or pay off representatives, that the intent of the settlement agreement was not followed, that the settlement agreement is contrary to precedent regarding an Agency disqualifying a representative, and the AJ did not have jurisdiction to enforce the settlement agreement.

ANALYSIS AND FINDINGS

EEOC Regulation 29 C.F.R. § 1614.401 permits appeals, in relevant part, by a Complainant from an Agency's final action or dismissal of a complaint or an Agency's alleged noncompliance with a settlement agreement.

Here, Complainant is alleging that a settlement agreement prohibiting her representative from representing EEO complainants is against public policy (i.e., an Agency should not be permitted to pay off a representative not to represent EEO complainants), that the intent of the settlement agreement was not followed, and that the settlement agreement was contrary to precedent regarding an Agency disqualifying a representative. While the EEOC routinely adjudicates the merits of appeals on the validity of settlement agreements under authority of 29 C.F.R. § 1614.401(e) & .504, we decline to do so here because the settlement agreement Complainant challenges was between her representative and the Agency, not Complainant.

Complainant does not have standing to challenge the settlement agreement. If the representative wants to challenge the validity of her settlement agreement and/or claim it was breached, she should raise the matter with the Agency's EEO Director pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.504, and if the matter is not resolved file an appeal in accordance with that regulation.

Where an AJ excludes or disqualifies a representative as a sanction, the AJ will certify the matter to the Director of the Office of Federal Operations for a determination. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission Management Directive for 29 C.F.R. Part 1614 (EEO MD 110), Chapter 7, Section V (pages 7-23 through 7-29, at 7-29). The AJ properly did not certify her order to the Director of the Office of Federal Operations because she did not issue her exclusion order as a sanction.

Likewise, we note that the EEOC has exercised its discretion to permit an appeal from a Complainant whose representative was disqualified by an Agency for conflict of interest. Celmer v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 01903408 (Sep. 12, 1990). This case does not apply because the Complainant's representative was excluded from the hearing process by an EEOC AJ, not the Agency.

Complainant's appeal that the AJ's exclusion of her representative constitutes a denial of due process is premature. Like an AJ's rulings on the admissibility of witnesses and discovery, the AJ's ruling excluding Complainant's representative is not ripe for appeal until there is an Agency final order or action. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.401.
