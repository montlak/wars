11. Seniority

One term the Commission has had some difficulty in accurately defining, perhaps because it is not truly a term of art, is "seniority date." In Barbour v. Postmaster General, 05921004 (1993), the Commission found that an agreement to revise a complainant's seniority date extended to all benefits related to seniority. The Commission in Barbour explained:

The Commission agrees with the finding made in the previous decision that the language of the settlement agreement is unambiguous. We differ, however, as to the appropriate application of this finding. A review of the settlement agreement provision in question reveals that following appellant's reinstatement and successful completion of her probationary period and LSM training, appellant was to receive a seniority date of October 27, 1984. The concept of seniority generally encompasses both the allocation of benefits among competing employees (competitive seniority) and noncompetitive benefits earned under the employment agreement (benefits seniority). See, Franks v. Bowman Transportation Co., Inc., 424 U.S. 747, 767 (1976), citing S. Slichter, J. Healy, & E. Livernash, The Impact of Collective Bargaining on Management, 104-115 (1960). The settlement agreement in the present case contains no language which in any way limits or qualifies the definition of seniority. Therefore, the Commission finds that it is reasonable to interpret the term "seniority," without such qualifying language, as encompassing seniority for all purposes, including competitive status seniority and benefits seniority. See, Jerome Griffin v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05920009 (April 28, 1992); Joanne Villanueva v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05920223 (May 14, 1992). Accordingly, we conclude, contrary to our previous decision, that the settlement agreement at issue was breached when the agency altered the information in several categories on appellant's Form 50, including her enter-on-duty date, leave computation date, retirement computation date, and leave data.

But, in Dunton v. Postmaster General, 01962093 (1996), the appellant claimed that the agency failed to comply with a settlement agreement providing that his "seniority date will be changed to August 21, 1993." The Commission held that the changing of his seniority date did not imply that other related dates would be changed. As the Commission explained in Dunton:

The record contains a seniority roster showing 8/21/93 as the seniority date for the appellant. The appellant does not dispute the accuracy of this evidence of record. Instead, the appellant alleges that the above-quoted term of the settlement agreement also required the agency to change to August 21, 1993, all other dates pertaining to his entry in the postal service. He claims that he was verbally assured that the records would reflect that date as his "entered on duty" date. And because the agency did not do so, he alleges a breach of the settlement agreement.

We agree with the FAD that the settlement agreement only required the agency to assign August 21, 1993 as the appellant's seniority date. The agency maintains that the seniority date is used primarily for bidding purposes and that it can be a date different from an employee's "enter on duty" date. We reject the appellant's contention that the agency has failed to comply with the terms of the settlement agreement. It is the words of the settlement agreement, and not the appellant's expectations based on alleged verbal assurances by an agency official, that are determinative.

A contrary result was reached in Barry v. Postmaster General, 01971041 (1998), where the Commission held that if the term "seniority" is not qualified in a settlement agreement, then it will be given an inclusive definition:

In the present case, we find appellant's arguments persuasive. The Commission has held that without language that limits or qualifies the definition of seniority, that term will encompass both the allocation of benefits among competing employees (competitive seniority) and noncompetitive benefits earned under the employment agreement (benefits seniority). Barbour v. USPS, EEOC Request No. 05921004 (May 20, 1993).

As the settlement agreements at issue on appeal do not have any language that limits or qualifies the definition of seniority, it must encompass both competitive and benefits seniority. Consequently, the agency breached the terms of the agreement by failing to use May 24, 1991, as appellant's seniority date for the purpose of appellant's retirement eligibility.

On reconsideration in Dunton, without reference to Barry, the Commission once again held that the failure to specify the effects of a change in seniority date in a settlement agreement would result in a restrictive reading of the clause. In Dunton v. Postmaster General, 05970415 (1999), the Commission distinguished the Barbour case. In its reconsideration decision in Dunton, the Commission found:

The facts of the instant case, however, are distinguishable from those in Barbour. In Barbour, the appellant was reinstated pursuant to a settlement agreement that provided for adjustment of her seniority date and addressed re-crediting of leave. The agency waited nearly six years to change appellant's entry on duty date, leave and retirement computation dates, and leave category to dates inconsistent with Barbour's seniority date. The Commission's view of how the term "seniority date" was to be construed therefore was supported by the agency's actions during a nearly six year period following the execution of the settlement agreement.

In the instant case, by contrast, when the parties executed the settlement agreement, appellant had already been reinstated pursuant to a grievance settlement which did not address adjustment of any of the dates discussed above. Upon execution of the settlement agreement, the agency adjusted appellant's "seniority date" as it asserted the term is used in the agency: the date affecting appellant's bidding fights and other job assignment issues. The agency engaged in no action or inaction which would support the construction of "seniority date" as referring to any date but the one changed by the agency. Further, unlike the case in Barbour, it is clear from appellant's notice of breach that appellant was aware at the time he entered into the settlement agreement that "seniority date" and entry on duty date" were two different terms; otherwise, there would have been no reason for him to question the Senior Labor Relations Specialist as to the terms of the agreement. If appellant was in any doubt as to what the written terms of the agreement meant, he should have clarified the terms of the agreement in writing before signing it. With regard to appellant's allegation that the Senior Labor Relations Specialist told him that his entry on duty date would also be changed, the Commission notes that the Specialist denied making such a statement. In any event, such a verbal representation would be given no effect in the interpretation of the settlement agreement. See Eggleston, EEOC Request No. 05900795. Based upon the foregoing, the Commission finds that the agency did not breach the settlement agreement when it did not adjust appellant's entry on duty date as well as his seniority date.

Given that it is virtually impossible to resolve the conflict in the cases above, there is no clearly articulated trend in cases where the term "seniority" is used in a settlement agreement. The best explanation of the Commission's cases in this area may be that the term seniority will be treated as a term of art unless there is some evidence indicating the parties attached a special meaning to the term. For example, in Barbour, there is no indication the parties intended the term to be in any way restrictive until several years after the agreement was entered into when the agency began changing certain dates connected to length of service. By contrast, in Dunton, the Commission focused on the employee's awareness that "seniority" and "entry on duty date" were not synonymous.

The safest conclusion to draw from the cases is that the effects of a change in seniority date should be specifically addressed in the agreement itself.
