J. GENDER-BASED SUBJECTIVE CRITERIA

Gender-based criteria for position selection, when apparent, are addressed essentially under the rubric of bona fide occupational qualifications. However, in many cases, subjective criteria can be gender-based in a more subtle fashion. For example, what is perceived as a good quality in a female job applicant can be perceived as a detriment in a male candidate. The fact that a criterion is not facially gender-based does not remove it from the ambit of Title VII. It does make it more difficult to identify. Subjective selection criteria always should be carefully examined for neutrality.

An example of hidden gender-based discrimination will best serve to illustrate. As the Commission explained in Caldwell v. USPS, 01880601 (1988):

What both favored applicants have in common is the Director finding them "cheerful." Amid all the explanations, what the Director's argument boiled down to was the fact that he liked the two women in their interviews, but found the appellant "reserved." When an employment decision rests on such subjective criteria, it provides "a ready mechanism for discrimination," Rowe v. General Motors Corp., 457 F.2d 348, 359 (5th Cir. 1972), especially, as here, when the selecting official's characterizations are weighted with sexual significance. Considering someone better suited for a job because that job requires someone "cheerful," "chipper" or "spunky" can mean that the employer considers the job better suited to a woman, as those descriptions are used approvingly of women. A "reserved" and "thoughtful" man, though a good communicator and skilled in human relations, may belong in a more traditionally "male" role in the employer's mind. The Director states that he finds two cheerful women right for the role of Personnel Assistant in his office, and also that he has detailed a third woman into that position. He more than once criticizes the appellant for not seeking the Safety Specialist position, to which he states he had recently promoted two males. The record indicates that the Director has found the appellant wholly unsuitable for Personnel Assistant, where he values cheerfulness, but somehow suitable for the Safety Specialist position, to which he has promoted other males.

An inquiry by the selecting official into a candidate's marital status is not necessarily an indication of discrimination. The inquiry is reasonable when it is related to the travel requirements of a position and there is no indication that the same inquiry was not made of male applicants. Evans v. Secretary of Air Force, 01912188 (1991).

One way that gender-based subjective criteria are infused into the employment arena is through the use of stereotypes. When such stereotypes are based on sex, their use is prohibited. In Harrell v. Secretary of Army, 05940652 (1995), the Commission found that a supervisor's reaction to a female subordinate's crying was evidence of a sex-based stereotypical response. Combined with other evidence that the supervisor was patronizing and condescending toward females, this was sufficient in Harrell to warrant a finding of discrimination:

Appellant further asserts that the Supervisor's explanation that she had to be reassigned because of her crying is nothing more than sex-based stereotyping. The Supervisor provided several specific instances in which appellant became tearful or cried: just prior to the General's visit on August 8, 1991; when she was unable to get another employee to cooperate with her in December 1990; and, when a female employee reported to him that appellant had become emotional and started crying. The Supervisor, however, never explained how these incidents--at least two of which took place a number of months apart--had such an impact on the other employees or the operation of the office as to require appellant's reassignment. With respect to the August 8, 1991 incident, for example, the record shows that appellant cried before the General arrived, when she was arranging for other employees to cover certain areas in her absence. Appellant testified that she did not leave the facility until she had arranged for coverage. The Supervisor did not assert--and the record failed to show--that any problems arose during the General's visit as a result of the crying incident or appellant's emergency absence.

Hearing testimony established that the Supervisor was patronizing and condescending toward female employees. That is, the Supervisor tended to ignore or make a joke of issues raised by female staff members, while pursuing those of concern to male staff members and treated female staff members as though they were secretaries by expecting them to make coffee and to type letters. Moreover, the Supervisor appeared to have different expectations of his male and female employees. Hearing testimony indicated that male employees were permitted to express anger, frustration, or stress through emotional outbursts such as shouting or yelling or pounding the desk. By contrast, the Supervisor apparently expected female employees to act like "ladies" and to be pleasant and cheerful and smiling at all times no matter what the circumstances. When appellant failed to meet the Supervisor's expectations in this regard, i.e., she cried, he unilaterally decided that her emotionalism was sufficient to relegate her to a less prestigious, less visible, and nonmanagerial position.

Based on these facts, we find that the agency's articulated reason for its action was pretextual and that the Supervisor discriminated against appellant on the basis of her sex when he proposed her voluntary reassignment to the Program Analyst position. Consequently, we reverse the previous decision's finding of no sex discrimination.

Similarly, the Commission entered a finding of sex discrimination in Thorogood v. Secretary of Navy, 0720070072 (2007), where, during the promotion interview, the selecting official told the female complainant that "as a mother with children he knew she would not want a job that required travel."
