1. Procedure for Disqualification and Suspension

The procedure for disqualification or suspension of a representative is explained at MD-110 at 7-32 to 7-33:

2. Procedure for suspension and disqualification

Before suspension or disqualification from future hearings, the representative must be given:

a. notice of the specific conduct that is the basis for the proposed disqualification;

b. notice of the proposed sanction; and

c. the opportunity to be heard.

This is accomplished through a show cause order. The show cause order must describe in detail the incident(s) constituting the grounds for suspension or disqualification, describe the proposed sanction, and give the representative a period of time in which to explain in writing why s/he should not be suspended or disqualified.

For improper conduct or a refusal to follow orders at the hearing stage, the Administrative Judge will issue the show cause order and certify the matter to the Director, Office of Federal Operations, for a determination. In addition, the Administrative Judge may, separately or simultaneously, issue an order excluding the representative from the hearing process in the case at bar, in accordance with the provisions discussed above. If the representative is an attorney, referral to the appropriate bar association normally should be considered as well, pursuant to section C below.

For improper conduct during the appeal, the Office of Federal Operations will issue the show cause order. In all cases, the representative must submit his/her response to the Director of the Office of Federal Operations. The Director or his/her designee will issue a final order, which is not appealable.

An order suspending or disqualifying a representative from future hearings must specify the time period the penalty will be in effect, which must be commensurate with the severity of the conduct.

When the Administrative Judge or the Commission proposes to suspend or disqualify the agency's representative, a copy of the show cause order and subsequent decision must be provided to the agency's EEO director.

An AJ's failure to follow the procedures listed above may invalidate the disqualification. The Commission in Santo D. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120181017 (2020), vacated and remanded the case where the AJ dismissed complainant's counsel and excluded her as the representative at the hearing for recording the prehearing conference without the consent of the parties. The facts related to the recording in Santo D. were:

During the prehearing processing, on October 31, 2017, Counsel filed a motion to recuse the AJ from the matter at hand. In support of her motion, Counsel included exhibits D and E which were provided to the AJ in a file format that was not compatible with the AJ's noted acceptable formats for e-submission. Following emails between the AJ and Counsel dated November 1, 2017 and November 3, 2017, it was revealed that the exhibits were voice recordings surreptitiously made by Counsel during prehearing meetings without the authorization of the parties who were recorded, including the AJ. The AJ responded to Counsel and informed her that the exhibits would not be taken into consideration for the motion.

The AJ further informed Counsel in an Order served by email dated November 3, 2017, that she would be dismissed as a representative for Complainant and excluded from the hearing. The AJ stated the following:

If these are audio recordings from a court reporting service, then there should have been a corresponding transcript. If they are audio recordings of telephonic conversations that were not on the record or without permission of the parties and AJ in this federal proceeding, then they should not have been taken and should not be used.

Counsel used the recordings and had them transferred into transcript form to support her motion to recuse the AJ. The AJ found that the recording and use of the recording constituted misconduct. Further, the AJ noted that Counsel challenged the AJ's authority regarding settlement discussions and noted her "unprofessional, disrespectful" statements toward the AJ in emails. The AJ determined that Counsel was culpable for her deceit and unprofessional conduct in making and authorizing audio recordings of the prehearing conference without notice or consent of the AJ or Agency counsel; filing the uncertified transcript of the surreptitious audio recordings as attachments to a motion; and unprofessional conduct in emails with the AJ.

As such, the AJ found that the appropriate action would be to dismiss Counsel as representative to Complainant and to exclude Counsel as the representative through the hearing. The AJ noted that she would assist Complainant through the hearing, noting the late date of the order, as it was close in time to the scheduled date of the hearing.

The Commission vacated the disqualification of complainant's representative because the AJ failed to notify the representative and provide her an opportunity to be heard in Santo D.:

EOC Management Directive for 29 C.F.R. Part 1614 (EEO MD-110), Chap. 7, § V.A. (Aug. 5, 2015) governs the exclusion and disqualification/suspension of representatives. Exclusion by an AJ applies to the hearing process on the current case on which a representative is representing a complainant. The general standard for exclusion of a representative is contumacious conduct, i.e., willfully stubborn and disobedient. See William G. v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 0120162273 (Sept. 26, 2018) (holding that the AJ properly excluded complainant's representative for engaging in contumacious conduct); see also In the Matter of Arnold T., EEOC Doc. No. 1120160001 (April 30, 2018) (affirming the AJ's exclusion of the representative for obstructionist behavior).

Contumacious behavior or disruptive conduct warranting exclusion may include unprofessional or disruptive behavior. A person's conduct is contumacious when it is "willfully stubborn and disobedient." Black's Law Dictionary (6th ed. 1990). Contumacious behavior or disruptive conduct may include any unprofessional or disrespectful behavior; degrading, insulting, or threatening verbal remarks or conduct; the use of profanity; or conduct engaged in for the purpose of improperly delaying the hearing. A finding of contumacious conduct or disruptive behavior may be based on a series of disruptive incidents, a pattern of acts, or a single sufficiently obstructive episode. Normally, any pattern should be manifest within a single case. EEO MD-110 at Chap. 7, § V.A.3. See In the Matter of Lamar W. Sessoms, Jr, EEOC Doc. No. 1120060003 (April 7, 2008).

Unless the improper conduct is so egregious as to compromise the order required for a fair and orderly proceeding, the Administrative Judge normally should first warn the offending person to stop the conduct. The warning should give notice that if the conduct continues, the person will be excluded from the hearing. EEO MD-110 at Chap. 7, § V.A.5; William G., supra (noting that, before excluding a representative, an AJ should first warn the offending person to stop the conduct, as well as give notice that if the conduct continues, the representative will be excluded from the hearing).

Upon review of the record, we find that the AJ failed to provide Counsel with notice that, if the conduct continued, Counsel would be excluded from the hearing. Based on the AJ's failure to adhere to the Commission's procedures for exclusion as noted above, we find that the AJ erred in excluding Counsel from the hearing. Although we reverse the AJ's decision to exclude Counsel, we place Counsel on notice that she is not to record conversations involving any party, witness, counsel, or government official connected with proceedings before the EEOC without the express consent of the person(s) being recorded, else face exclusion as a representative before the EEOC.

We also remind the AJ that, if a complainant's representative is excluded, the complainant should be given the option of either proceeding without the representative or requesting a limited continuance to obtain a new representative. The AJ may, in the AJ's discretion and in appropriate circumstances, terminate the hearing process and decide the case based on the record if the record is sufficient to permit adjudication. EEO MD-110 at Chap. 7, § V.A.5. Complainant was not provided with any such option when the AJ excluded the Counsel.

For several years, the Commission's disqualification and suspension procedures set forth in MD--110 remained little more than procedures written on paper. However, in 2006, the Commission finally had an opportunity to elaborate on its procedures, as well as the standards for disqualification, in two cases involving improper conduct on the part of complainant's respective counsel.

In Quinones v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 01A53109 (2006) and Arredondo v. Postmaster General, 01A51491 (2006), counsel for complainants managed to demonstrate, at the least, boorish behavior and, in one instance, sanctionable misconduct. First, the cases set down a clear procedure that must be followed prior to the imposition of any sanction against counsel for misconduct. Relying upon its guidance in MD 110, the Commission in Quinones held that:

Unless the improper conduct is so egregious as to compromise the order required for a fair and orderly proceeding, the AJ normally should first warn the offending person to stop the conduct. The warning should give notice that if the conduct continues, the person will be excluded from the hearing. Id. at V.A.5. (page 7-26).

. . .

Before a representative is disqualified or suspended for misconduct by an AJ, the AJ must give notice of the specific conduct that is the basis for the proposed disqualification or suspension, describe the proposed sanction, and the opportunity to be heard, which is accomplished by a show cause order. Id. at Chapter 7, Section V.B.2. (pages 7-28, 7-29). Where a show cause order is issued by an AJ on why a representative should not be disqualified for improper conduct or a refusal to follow orders at the hearing stage, the AJ will certify the matter to the EEOC, Director, Office of Federal Operations, for a determination. Id. at page 7-29.

The requirement that, except in cases of egregious misconduct, the administrative judge warn the offending party to stop his or her conduct and make clear that failure to heed the warning can result in exclusion from the hearing may go a long way in explaining the dearth of case law on disqualification and exclusion. Most prudent counsel would heed such a warning.

Another important aspect of the procedure for sanctioning misconduct by representatives highlighted by Quinones and Arredondo is that the administrative judge assigned to the case does not make the decision on sanctions. After issuing an appropriate show cause, the judge must certify the matter to the director of OFO for a decision. This aspect of the procedure was overlooked by the judges in both cases. As a result, the Commission brushed aside arguments made by both involved agencies that counsel for the complainant, having been disqualified, could not file an appeal on behalf of the complainant. In reality, no appeal is necessary since the matter should directly go to OFO for a decision.
