IV. REDUCTION-IN-FORCE

Not every action taken by an agency that affects its individual employees is directed personally at those employees. In the first instance, an agency has the legitimate right to determine how to best utilize its employees to carry out the agency's mission. Agencies often accomplish this allocation of resources through "reductions-in-force" or "reorganizations." Congress has established retention and reemployment priorities in the event of a RIF or reorganization at chapter 35 of Title 5 of the U.S. Code. The Office of Personnel Management has published detailed guidance at 5 CFR Part 351.

Theoretically, RIFs and reorganizations are based on the objective needs of the agency and its budgetary limitations and are not based on the performance or conduct of individual employees. Of course, as with any action an agency takes, RIFs and reorganizations can provide a means for discriminating against individual employees.

Allegations that an agency discriminated against an employee in a RIF or reorganization are analyzed under McDonnell Douglas v. Green, 411 U.S. 792, 93 S. Ct. 1817, 36 L. Ed.2d 668 (1973). The employee must first establish a prima facie case by showing that he is a member of a protected group and was treated less favorably in the RIF than similarly situated employees outside of his protected group. The agency rebuts a prima facie case of discrimination involving a RIF by demonstrating that the RIF was based on proper management considerations, such as to carry out new functions of the agency. Morris v. Department of Army, 03820045 (1983). The employee then has an opportunity to demonstrate that the RIF was a pretext for prohibited discrimination.
