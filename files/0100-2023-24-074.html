6. Offers of Employment

The Commission in Cordell H. v. Veterans Affairs, 2021001434 (2022), explained what is permissible from an employment standpoint when an offer of employment is extended to a candidate with a history of substance abuse:

EEOC Regulation 29 C.F.R. § 1630.3(a) excludes individuals who currently engage in the illegal use of drugs from the definition of "qualified individual with a disability" for the purposes of the Rehabilitation Act. However, in accordance with 29 C.F.R. § 1630.3(b)(1), an employer may not discriminate against an individual with a history of addiction but is no longer engaging in illegal use of drugs, and who successfully completed a supervised rehabilitation program or who has otherwise been rehabilitated successfully. After a conditional offer of employment, an employer may ask questions concerning past or present substance abuse. However, the employer may not use such information to exclude an individual with a substance abuse disability, unless it can show undue hardship or that that the reason for exclusion is job-related and consistent with business necessity, and that legitimate job criteria cannot be met with a reasonable accommodation. See Technical Assistance Manual on the Employment Provisions (Title I) of the Americans With Disabilities Act at § 8.8 (Jan. 1992). So long as the employer would impose the same conduct standard on an employee without a disability, an employer is not required to excuse past misconduct even if it is the result of the individual's disability. Enforcement Guidance Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under the Americans With Disabilities at Question 35 (Oct. 17, 2002).

In Cordell H. v. Veterans Affairs, 2021001434 (2022), the agency rescinded a conditional offer of employment where the complainant, an applicant for a clinical pharmacist position, received an "unfavorable suitability screening/background check." The agency determined that the complainant was not suitable to the job because he had previously stolen narcotics in the course of his duties as a pharmacist. The complainant maintained that while he suffered from a substance abuse disorder, he successfully completed a recovery program and was drug-free. According to the complainant, the agency's "determination to rescind its employment offer based on misconduct prior to his rehabilitation is tantamount to rejecting him for employment based on stereotypes regarding job applicants with substance abuse issues." The Commission rejected the complainant's claims, finding that the agency was not obligated to accommodate the disability:

Complainant had a substance abuse disability but successfully completed a rehabilitation program and is no longer engaging in illegal drug use. Following a conditional employment offer, the Agency conducted further suitability screening. The Agency determined that Complainant was not suitable for employment in an Agency pharmacy because of past misconduct. Specifically, the record revealed that a hospital in Juneau, Alaska, had removed Complainant as a pharmacist for diverting of two syringes containing hydromorphone. Moreover, in a sworn statement voluntarily relinquishing his Alaska pharmacy license, Complainant stated that he had attempted to conceal this misconduct. In a sworn statement, an assistant human resources specialist who rescinded Complainant's offer stated that, considering the duties of the pharmacy position for which Complainant applied, and the seriousness and recency of the misconduct, Complainant received an unfavorable suitability determination which caused the tentative offer being rescinded. The affidavit from the employment relations and labor relations specialist explained that this prior history of theft from a pharmacy rendered Complainant unsuitable to work as a clinical pharmacist for the Agency where Complainant would have access to narcotics. The Agency articulated a conduct-based, job-related and valid hardship reason to rescind Complainant's employment offer. Complainant lacks contradictory evidence to demonstrate that the Agency excused past theft by pharmacists without a substance abuse disability. We find there was adequate evidence that Complainant's prior misconduct with pharmaceuticals while employed in another hospital could not be accommodated by the Agency.
