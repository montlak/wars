NEGOTIATED GRIEVANCE PROCEDURES

Under law, all federal sector collective bargaining agreements must contain a grievance procedure that meets some very specific criteria. The procedure must:

  • • Be fair and simple.
  • • Permit both the union and individual employees to file grievances.
  • • Provide an opportunity for arbitration of disputes submitted by either the union or the agency.

The term "grievance" is broadly defined in the law. Most grievances involve a claim that the CBA has been violated. They may also allege a violation of a law, rule, or regulation. An even more sweeping provision states that a grievance can encompass "any matter relating to the employment of the employee."

The law also specifies that certain types of disputes may not be submitted to the grievance procedure for resolution. Examples include disputes over position classifications, retirement, life insurance, and health insurance benefits.

All negotiated grievance procedures (or NGPs) have specific rules that must be followed, outlined in the CBA and in agency policy. In particular, they specify the management levels at which types of grievances must be raised, and the steps of an internal appeal process (most typically three, for grievances raised at the supervisory level), which must be followed before a grievance may be taken by the union to arbitration. An agency that fails to follow the procedures of its NGP may be charged by the union with an agreement violation, and if the violation is severe enough, an unfair labor practice.

Many agencies require that grievance responses be written or approved by HR before they are transmitted. Be sure to check with your labor relations specialist before responding to a grievance.

Remember: NGPs apply only to bargaining unit employees. Non-bargaining unit employees may avail themselves of administrative grievance procedures as established by your agency. These are usually far easier to manage. NGPs are more complex, because:

  • • They involve the union, under the rules negotiated in the CBA and under federal law.
  • • Management decisions may be appealed by the union (although not by individual employees) to outside arbitrators.
  • • Appeals to arbitration awards -- known as exceptions -- may be decided by the Federal Labor Relations Authority.

It is, therefore, very unwise for managers to do anything in a grievance matter under the NGP, or what looks like it might be a grievance matter, without consulting the LR specialists in HR -- in advance of management action whenever possible. Are you unsure whether a specific employee is in a bargaining unit? This question can only be answered under labor relations law. Union and management may disagree. Again, consult the specialists in HR.

Alternative appeal routes. Must a bargaining unit employee use the NGP to appeal a decision impacting the employee personally? Generally yes, if the matter is covered by the NGP, unless the employee believes illegal discrimination may be involved. The employee may then choose to contact an EEO counselor and eventually make a formal EEO complaint. Under federal labor law, these EEO actions are considered "grievances," but they do not follow the rules of the NGP.

In some cases, such as major adverse actions (suspensions of more than 14 calendar days, demotions, some disciplinary or performance-based involuntary transfers, and removals), the employee has a choice of filing either a grievance under the NGP or an appeal to the Merit Systems Protection Board -- but not both.

Executive Orders

In 2018, President Trump signed Executive Order 13839, which excluded from negotiated grievance procedures challenges to: removals for performance or misconduct; the assignment of performance ratings; and the award of any form of incentive pay (including cash awards), quality step increases, and recruitment, retention, or relocation payments. That EO has since been rescinded by Executive Order 14003, signed by President Biden in 2021.

Because there has been political action in this area, it's possible that each new administration will issue its own orders regarding NGP exclusions.

Recognize grievances and potential grievances

Is someone just complaining, or are they raising, or about to raise, a formal grievance? How can you tell?

When the union is involved. If a union representative comes to you with a question about working conditions or your specific decisions -- whether or not a specific employee is involved -- and you can't give the rep an answer that satisfies both of you in the course of an informal chat, you may well be dealing with a matter that is about to become a formal grievance. It is time to consult with your LR specialist, and probably with your boss.

There is nothing wrong with having that informal chat without running to the LR specialist every time the rep wants to talk. In fact, it's better if you develop good relations with the employees who represent the union. If an issue can be resolved informally, go ahead and do it.

Be sure that in your desire to be agreeable, you don't give away the store. Management has rights, and your agency has negotiated very specific CBA provisions for very specific reasons. Your actions can compromise those rights, and even establish unwritten practices, in contradiction to the CBA, that your agency may later find it hard to disavow.

In other words, you can be a lot more relaxed in your approach to the union rep when disputes come up if you have a good working knowledge of your CBA and of agency labor relations policy, so you can be sure you stay on the same page with both. Get LR training or coaching from HR in advance of issues, as well as after they come up.

When the union is not initially involved. What about an employee complaint where the union is not involved? In that case, it may be harder to tell.

An employee who is not in a bargaining unit (consult HR if you are in doubt) does not fall under the NGP. The union cannot become involved at any stage, and any grievance would have to be under an agency grievance procedure.

If the employee is in a bargaining unit and is not backed by a union representative when an issue is raised, there is nothing wrong with having an informal chat and working out a resolution within the established rules and your agency policy and the practices you've been following in your unit.

Often it is sufficient for you to explain why things are as they are, or why you did what you did -- the employee accepts your answer, and that ends the matter. Or the employee may point out something you overlooked, you change your decision or your practice, and that ends the matter. You would clearly be foolish to ignore an opportunity to make a dispute go away at an early stage. Just make sure you do not violate the rights of the union.

And again, don't ignore the rules, especially those in the CBA. It may be easy to say, "Okay, I'm sorry you think you haven't gotten your share of overtime. You'll get the next shot." But if that promise would violate the provisions governing overtime in the CBA, or any agency rules, don't give it. For one thing, you don't want to face a well-grounded grievance from another employee who lost overtime. Nor do you want employees constantly coming to you with reasons why they should get the next overtime slot.

If bargaining unit employees do not accept your informal answers to their requests or complaints, you may ask them if they are filing a formal grievance. If the answer is yes, or if they follow up with a written or oral complaint, the NGP comes into play. In some cases, you may be tempted to give an immediate answer when a grievance is first raised, but some preparation -- and consultation with HR -- is usually a better idea.

The union's representative role. Remember that the union must represent the interests of all bargaining unit employees, whether or not they are union members. An employee who is not satisfied with your initial response or with the way the grievance procedure is playing out may request union assistance at any time. The assistance of the union is necessary to appeal a grievance decision to an outside arbitrator, since only the union can make such an appeal.

If an employee makes an appointment to talk about a problem that may be a grievance (as opposed to raising something in a spontaneous chat), this may be a "formal meeting" under federal labor relations law. You must notify the union and give it a chance to send a representative to look after the interests of the bargaining unit -- whether or not the employee wants the union present. All meetings to resolve or discuss formal grievances are formal meetings under federal labor relations law, and the union has the right to be notified and to be present regardless of the wishes of the employee.

Unions are often reluctant to represent employees whose grievances seem to have little merit, especially in arbitration, but an employee who believes the union has not fulfilled its representation duty may charge it with an unfair labor practice before the Office of General Counsel of the FLRA. This may explain why you wind up responding to a formal grievance filed by a union rep who should know there is no merit to it. Don't take it personally or give the rep a hard time.

The union is only doing its job, and there is no point in allowing a grievance to hurt the agency's general LR climate. Unions that have properly represented an employee in an NGP usually feel they can safely decline to take the dispute to arbitration if it seems to have little merit. And any harassment or retaliation against an employee for carrying out union representational duties can result in ULP charge against the agency.

Employees may not ask for or want union representation. They may represent themselves. But the union is legally the "exclusive representative" of all employees in the bargaining unit. This means the union may have to approve any non-union representative the employee wishes to use (unless there is some other provision in the NGP). Be careful here. If you discuss a grievance through a representative the union has not approved, you may be guilty of a ULP.

What does a formal grievance look like? A formal grievance may not be in writing, or specifically labeled "grievance," but you can recognize it when you see or hear it. It will almost always contain all these components:

  • • A written or oral statement, which describes the events, decisions or conditions the grievant feels are improper. This could range from dissatisfaction at a reprimand to a contention that overtime is being distributed incorrectly.
  • • At least a general identification of the provisions of the CBA or laws, rules or regulations the grievant feels apply or have been violated. For example, "that's not the way the discipline -- or overtime -- provisions of the contract should work."
  • • The remedy sought. What the grievant wants you to do about the situation. For example, rescind the reprimand, or give the grievant the next overtime assignment.

Treat such complaints as formal grievances under the NGP.

What about ADR? Some agencies have provided alternative dispute resolution to resolve grievances under their NGP -- under mutual agreement with the union like all other aspects of the CBA. Before you consider ADR for a specific grievance, you will need to understand your agency's rules and policies. Consult with your servicing LR specialist. ADR may not be available at all in your agency's NGP, or your LR specialist may advise against offering it for a particular agreement.

The same principles apply if the union rep suggests ADR.

Analyze and resolve grievances

When you deal with a grievance, it helps to analyze and respond to it in an organized manner.

Step 1. Check out procedural issues.

Before you respond to the matter being raised in the grievance, take note of several important procedural matters, under the guidance of your LR specialist. For example:

  • • Determine if the employees involved are in a bargaining unit. The union may use the NGP only on behalf of bargaining unit employees.
  • • See if you can determine the date on which the matter being grieved occurred. Then you will be able to determine whether the grievance was filed within the time limits specified in the grievance procedure.
  • • Similarly, check to see whether the grievance is being presented at the right step of the procedure. If your position in the agency places you at the second level in the grievance procedure, check to make sure the matter was raised at the initial level. In almost all NGPs, the initial level is the grievant's immediate supervisor. If the grievant is the union, the initial level is generally the lowest management level responsible for the matter being grieved. If the matter affects employees of more than one supervisor, that will raise the initial level. If the grievance has been raised at a lower level, make sure you know what happened. There should be paperwork.
  • • Finally, check to see whether the grievance concerns a matter covered by the CBA. Some things, like the proper classification of positions, are not covered. If you are not sure if the matter is covered, do not under any circumstances reply before a consultation with your servicing LR specialist.

If the grievance is procedurally defective -- for example, it has been presented after the time limit for filing expired, or it concerns a matter not covered by the CBA -- it is important that you point out that fact in responding to the grievance. If you simply respond to the grievance, it may be difficult for your agency to later raise procedural defects before an arbitrator or the FLRA.

Step 2. Check out the substance of the grievance.

After reviewing these procedural issues, carefully note the facts claimed by the grievant.

  • • If you are not already familiar with the facts, double-check to make sure they are complete and accurate before attempting a reply.
  • • When you have what you feel to be a solid version of the facts, find the provisions of the CBA -- or of other applicable rules, laws, and regulations -- that apply to the situation. If the appropriate answer to the grievance is clear from the language of the provisions, you are ready to develop your reply.
  • • If the answer isn't clear, you will need to check with your LR specialist and learn how similar situations have been handled.
  • • When you feel you have developed the correct answer to the grievance, outline it for delivery in writing or verbally. In either case, first point out any procedural flaws -- such as untimeliness -- that might render the matter nongrievable, then briefly explain your answer and the reasons for it clearly.

Step 3. Resolve the grievance.

Most agency policies require a manager to consult with HR before responding to a grievance. Be sure to check with your agency's HR or LR office before proceeding.

Should you meet face-to-face with a grievant, or respond in writing? NGPs generally don't specify. But unless the atmosphere surrounding the grievance has become highly adversarial, a face-to-face meeting may help resolve the matter, even if you can't make a positive response to the substance of the grievance.

If you meet face-to-face to deliver a grievance response, you will boost your chances of successfully resolving the matter if you:

  • • Maintain a low-key, professional tone.
  • • Focus on facts and issues, not personalities.
  • • Don't try to browbeat or sell the grievant on your answer.
  • • Explain your reasoning without attacking or putting down the grievant's viewpoint.

Written responses. If you are providing a written response to the grievance, the following should be included:

  • • A short statement of purpose. For example, "This is in reply to your second-step grievance concerning overtime assignments, dated Feb. 12, 20XX."
  • • A brief outline of the most important facts. For example, "On Jan. 23, you were not chosen for a two-hour overtime assignment. The individual chosen for the assignment, Ms. Jones, was listed ahead of you on the overtime roster. On Jan. 24, you filed a first-step grievance, contending you were improperly denied the assignment. On Jan. 31, your immediate supervisor denied the grievance."
  • • Your conclusions and the reasons for them. For example, "I have concluded there is no basis for granting your request that you be paid two hours of overtime pay because you were improperly denied the assignment. Ms. Jones was next on the overtime roster, and therefore was properly assigned to do the work in accordance with the procedures established in Article 27, Overtime, of the labor agreement."
  • • The bottom line. For example, "For the reasons stated above, the grievance is denied."
  • • Signature and date.

A final word. Remember, grievances can take up a substantial amount of time as they work their way up the chain of command. It is important that you put forth your best effort to fully investigate and resolve the grievance at your level. Supervisors and lower-level managers generally have much better access to the facts and understanding of the grievance than higher-level managers more removed from the matter.

You should also be prepared to make yourself available for consultation with agency LR specialists and lawyers, and possibly witness testimony before an arbitrator, if the matter goes to arbitration.

Mistakes to avoid

Handling grievances under a negotiated grievance procedure can be tricky. Keep a few basics in mind.

Do not:

  • • Kick grievances up the chain without trying to resolve them. Grievances take a great deal of time and effort to resolve. It is your job to do your best to resolve them at your level. Your bosses will not thank you if your unwillingness or inability to deal with them lands grievances in their laps.
  • • Fail to recognize a grievance. Grievance procedures are very strict in their application. Don't casually or informally try to solve an issue that should be handled as a formal grievance.
  • • Give away the store. You should not try to resolve pre-grievance disputes or first step grievances by violating the provisions of the CBA or agency policies.
  • • Overlook procedural issues. Work with HR to make sure you are doing the right thing. Because the procedural issues are so strict, you need proper expert guidance.
  • • Take things personally. You may be offended or hurt by a grievance, or by the manner of the employee or union rep involved. Don't show it, and, above all, don't respond in kind. You will only compromise your agency's position and make a favorable resolution more difficult. In some agencies with poor LR climates, union reps may deliberately try to get a rise out of managers. This is not a game you should play.

Ask FMG

Q. If a grievance is untimely, should I still address the matter being grieved?

A. That depends on your agency's policy. Most recommend that you respond first to the procedural issue, then state what the answer would be on the substance if the matter were grievable.

Q. Does an employee have to use the contract's negotiated grievance procedure?

A. In most cases, yes, if the matter is covered by it. In some cases, such as major adverse actions, the employee has a choice of either filing a grievance under the collective bargaining agreement (using the NGP) or an appeal to the MSPB -- but not both.

Q. Does an employee have to use a union representative?

A. No, employees can represent themselves if they don't want union representation. An employee generally can't use any other representative on a grievance under the NGP without the union's permission. NGPs often address this point.

Q. What if an employee requests that a union representative not be present at a grievance meeting?

A. The union still has a right to have a representative present, even if it is not representing the employee in the matter and the employee does not want a union presence. These are "formal meetings" under federal labor law.

Q. I get on well with my union reps, who are normally sensible people. Why do they persist in supporting employees in grievances that have no merit?

A. Well, they may not agree with you on the merits. However, it is entirely possible that they do agree, quietly, but are constrained by the union's legal and organizational responsibilities to represent the employees anyway. Let them do their job and don't give them a hard time about it. (Harassment of employees for carrying out their duties as union reps can involve you in a ULP charge.) If they've done a good job of representation in the NGP, they may feel they can safely decline to take a dubious case to arbitration.

Q. Do I have to reply to grievances in writing?

A. It depends on what your negotiated grievance procedure says. Most allow first-step replies to be oral, but require written answers at higher steps. However, a written response may be a good idea even at the first level, if you expect the grievant will want to take things further. It is the best way to create a record for the use of the next manager up the chain -- who may be your boss.

Q. What happens if the grievant doesn't accept my response?

A. If there are further steps available in your grievance procedure, the grievant may take it to the next step. If yours is the final step, the grievant may ask the union to take the matter to arbitration.

Q. Do I have to put up with obnoxious behavior in grievance meetings?

A. You may have to put up with aggressive representation of the matter by union reps or individual grievants, and maybe some harsh or unpleasant words. Stay calm and professional. But you do not have to tolerate personal abuse or threats of physical harm. Take up any problems with your HR and labor relations specialists -- not directly with the union rep or grievant. Unions may be responsive to detailed complaints about reps who abuse their privileged positions. In very serious cases, discipline may be appropriate.
