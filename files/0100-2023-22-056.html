g. Desk Audits/Job Classification Systems

An agency does not carry its burden of showing that a wage differential is legitimate merely by citing desk audits that conclude the positions at issue are properly classified. See Adams v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01992806 (2000). In Adams, the administrative judge analyzed the claim under Title VII, having found that complainant had not asserted a claim under the Equal Pay Act (EPA). On appeal, the Commission considered the complainant's claim of gender-based pay discrimination under the standards set forth in the EPA, citing Corning Glass Works v. Brennan, 417 U.S. 188, 195 (1974) and Arnold v. Secretary of Treasury, 01960490 (1998). In Adams the Commission found:

The Commission initially agrees with the AJ's finding that complainant demonstrated that she was discriminated against on the basis of her gender when she was paid a GS-101-09 salary while performing duties substantially similar to those of male Addiction Therapists paid at the GS-101-11 level. In addition, although the agency maintained that complainant was graded at the GS-101-09 level due to an internal classification system and confirmed by agency desk audits, we find that the agency failed to meet its burden under the EPA in establishing a valid affirmative defense to the presumption of gender-based wage discrimination. As the AJ noted, a job classification system is not a defense to a prima facie case of gender-based wage discrimination if an investigation shows that it does not accurately reflect the actual duties performed by individuals in the different classifications. We find that a review of the record supports the AJ's conclusion that complainant was performing substantially equal work as were the comparative male Addiction Therapists who were graded at the GS-101-11 level. The AJ noted that the difference in their work was minimal despite the agency's classification system, and the alleged differences listed by the agency are at odds with the desk audits. As the record reflects that complainant and her male comparators were performing substantially equal work, the fact that the agency performed three desk audits and each time concluded that complainant was properly graded does not carry the agency's burden.

Similarly, the Commission in Heidi B. v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 0120152308 (2016), noted that a job classification system is not a defense to a prima facie violation of the EPA where an investigation shows that it does not accurately reflect the actual duties performed by individuals in the different classifications. "Where an investigation shows that people in different grades perform equal work, the grade classifications are artificial, and not a valid defense to a prima facie violation of the EPA." In Jones v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01994717 (2000), the Commission found a violation of the EPA where the agency took into account the previous salaries of male pharmacists in setting their rate of pay, but not the previous salary of a female pharmacist. The Commission found the agency's asserted reason--the males had asked for more money and the female had not--insufficient to satisfy the agency's defense burden. The Commission reaffirmed its decision on reconsideration. See Jones v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 05A10136 (2001).

However, in Turnage v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A53804 (2006), the Commission found the situation was not similar to Jones. The complainant in Turnage alleged that two male pharmacists were receiving pay at a higher step than she was for the same work. The agency maintained that the difference in pay was the result of the "two step" rule that provides that a General Schedule employee promoted to a higher grade is entitled to basic pay at the lowest level that exceeds the existing grade by not less than two step increases from the previous grade. The Commission in Turnage found:

On appeal, complainant contends that her case is similar to Jones v. Department of Veterans Affairs, supra. There, the agency failed to analyze the case under the EPA, despite complainant's claim of discrimination due to a pay differential between her and the named male comparators. Id. The Commission considered the reasons the agency provided for the differential under Title VII. Id. Specifically, the agency presented evidence that the male employees asked for a higher salary, while complainant did not, and that the agency was not aware that complainant was paid a higher salary at her previous agency job. Id. The Commission concluded that the agency's explanation was insufficient to overcome the established prima facie violation of the EPA, because the agency could not justify paying a female less money than males performing the same job by arguing that, unlike her male comparators, she failed to ask for more money; and it could not claim that its own failure to consider a female's salary history to the same extent that it considered the salary history of males excused its negligence. Id. Jones, however, is distinguishable from the present case. Unlike the situation in Jones, where the agency provided higher salaries to males because they asked for more money, the agency here applied the two-step promotion rule across-the-board to complainant and her two comparators. The agency did not claim that the higher salary was due to negotiations, or to a failure to consider past salaries. Instead, the salaries were due to a bona fide job classification system, which has not been challenged here. In this regard, complainant asserts that the discrepancy between her pay and that of the comparators is a violation of the EPA regardless of her initial boarding or the two-step promotion process. We disagree, and find that these factors are, in fact, proper justifications for the pay differential between complainant and the comparators. Accordingly, we find that the agency has proven by a preponderance of the evidence that the salary difference between complainant and the comparators fell into a permissible statutory exception, namely, that the salary difference was due to a reason other than sex.

Position classification standing alone is not an EPA defense. In Hanley v. Federal Labor Relations Authority, 0720060033 (2008), the complainant, a female GS-13 attorney, established that she performed essentially the same work as a male attorney who was paid at the GS-14 and later GS-15 level. In rejecting the agency's defense that the pay differential was based on a factor other than sex--the classifications of the respective positions--the Commission, in Hanley, held:

We concur with complainant that the evidence established that both complainant and E1 primarily handled unfair labor practice (ULP) cases and that both did other work (representation, settlement negotiations, litigation) as assigned. Nothing in the record established that the work assigned to E1 was either more complex, required greater skill, or was assigned in greater quantity than that assigned to complainant. Furthermore, on appeal, the agency states that it "does not contest the AJ's finding that [c]omplainant and the comparat[ive] performed substantially equal work...." Thus, we find that complainant established a prima facie case of an EPA violation and a Title VII violation.

On appeal, the agency argues that the difference in pay was based on the fourth affirmative defense, "a differential based on any other factor other than sex." The AJ disagreed with this argument and so do we. The agency is apparently relying on a job classification system as its defense. The Commission has held that a "job classification system is a defense to an EPA claim only where the classification categories are related to and/or accurately reflect actual differences in the job duties and qualifications." Id. (citation omitted). The Commission explained that the "mere existence of written job descriptions does not necessarily constitute a bona fide merit system." Id. (citation omitted). Furthermore, the Commission has held: "Where an investigation shows that people in different grades perform equal work, the grade classifications are artificial, and not a valid defense to a prima facie violation of the EPA." Id. (citation omitted). The Commission finds that the agency's affirmative defense, however it is characterized by the agency on appeal, is ultimately insufficient. The agency is therefore found to have violated the EPA.
