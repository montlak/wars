8. Vague Provisions

In Chester v. Postmaster General, 01A24856 (2003), the Commission found that a settlement agreement that provided the complainant would be provided an opportunity to participate in the 204B acting supervisor program, and it was the postmaster's intent to provide the opportunity as soon as possible, was void for lack of consideration. The Commission found the postmaster's "intent" to do something "as soon as possible" was simply too vague to be enforceable. Similarly, in Vagts v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 01A61926 (2006), recon. denied 0520060906 (2006), the Commission found that a provision to the effect that the agency would provide the complainant with certain experience on an ad hoc, as needed basis lacked consideration. The Commission noted that "agency action under the provision is completely discretionary, and the agency is not obligated to ever provide the complainant the promised experience." Id. See also Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120150588 (2015) (vague promise to check records and see if complainant was eligible for rehire).

When a settlement agreement provides benefits that are contingent upon the fulfillment of a condition and that condition is void for lack of consideration, the remainder of the agreement also is void. For example, in Lampkins v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01A34016 (2004), the agreement provided:

(1) [Complainant] has agreed to participate in a new medical exam with a Postal doctor. Management has agreed to request a "Focused" exam from a medical doctor. The resulting opinion of the doctor shall be a binding decision as far as any restrictions are concerned once;

(2) Management shall check with the Colorado Springs Facility and find out what positions are actually open and/or available for hire.

(3) Said available/open positions shall also include any "career" positions that [complainant] can qualify for, given the fact that the postal service has qualified her as being "career" qualified." This fact will be double checked by management due to previous errors and concerns of [complainant] (Reinstatement Consideration List) "Reinstatement eligible."

(4) If an immediate position is not available that [complainant] desires or [qualifies] for, she will be placed on a wait list, if necessary.

However, the agreement in Rhenetta also provided that the above benefits were contingent upon the Denver District Reasonable Accommodation Committee approving the complainant to begin working for the agency. The Commission found this final provision failed for lack of consideration and, as a result, the complainant was provided with no benefit under the agreement and it was void.

In many cases, agency representatives negotiate tentative settlements that require approval by higher level officials at the agency. An agreement that provides for a monetary payment but is subject to agency approval fails for lack of consideration. Thomason v. Postmaster General, 01A13298 (2002). Agreeing to pay a monetary settlement subject to approval does not incur any legal obligation on the agency's part.
