broida eeo Chapter 05: Settlement of Complaints Chapter 05: Settlement of Complaints, Subchapter 06: Voiding Settlement Agreements, Section E: Lack of Consideration--Illusory Agreements, Subsection 05: Mutual Mistake of Fact or Law, Part d: Unilateral Mistake of Fact
d. Unilateral Mistake of Fact

When there is a unilateral mistake of fact, it will not, under ordinary circumstances, work to make an agreement void. In Blount v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A62035 (2006), the complainant entered into an agreement that reassigned her to another position. After being reassigned, the complainant learned that the new position was not covered by the collective bargaining agreement. Refusing to set the agreement aside, the Commission, in Blount, explained the limited circumstances in which a unilateral mistake would serve to void an agreement:

As an initial matter, we find that the agency did not address complainant's contention that complainant would not have entered into the settlement agreement if had she realized that the position at issue was not covered by the collective bargaining agreement. In this regard, we find that complainant does not contend that the agency induced her to execute the settlement agreement by withholding this fact, or that she was otherwise misled to believe that the position was in the collective bargaining unit. As such, we find that complainant appears to contend that she reasonably assumed that the secretarial position at issue was in the collective bargaining unit, and she agreed to withdraw her EEO claim based, in part, on this belief. Therefore, we find that complainant does not contend that the settlement agreement should be rendered void due to a mutual mistake of material fact, but rather because her unilateral mistaken belief entitles her to rescind the agreement.

Where only one party to the contract makes a mistake, and the mistake was not known to the other party, then the contract is not avoidable unless the other party induced the mistake, except when: (1) the mistake is of so great a consequence as to make enforcement of the contract unconscionable; (2) the mistake relates to a material feature of the contract, and is made regardless of the exercise of ordinary care; and (3) the rescission of the contract does not result in prejudice to the other party other than the loss of his bargain. See Shuman v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05900744 (July 20, 1990).

Here, complainant contends that upon learning that her position was not covered by a collective bargaining unit, her supervisor engaged in harassing conduct, to include issuing proposed suspensions. Complainant avers that she is vulnerable to such adverse actions without union representation.

As previously determined above, we find that complainant does not claim that the agency induced her mistaken believe that the position at issue was covered by the collective bargaining agreement, such that she cannot rescind the settlement agreement for this reason. Furthermore, we find that complainant fails to satisfy all of the criteria to establish an exception to the 'one party mistake rule.' As to requirement (2), we find that whether or not the position at issue was covered by the bargaining unit is not a "material feature" of the settlement agreement. Instead, the record reflects that to resolve her underlying EEO claim (which concerned complainant's claim of sexual harassment by a co-worker), she desired to work in an office other than the mental health unit so as to avoid any contact with the alleged perpetrator. In fact, the language of the above referenced provision reflects complainant's desire to be moved from the mental health unit to another secretarial position, at equal pay, but with no mention of collective bargaining agreement coverage. Moreover, as to requirement (1), we find that complainant's mistake does not make enforcement of the settlement agreement "unconscionable," in that she has other remedies available to her, outside of the grievance process, such as the agency's internal process, and/or the EEO process, to address the adverse actions taken by her supervisor. Therefore, failing to satisfy these criteria, and failing to otherwise show that the settlement agreement should be rescinded, we find that the settlement agreement at issue is valid and enforceable.
