C. FAILURE OF EMPLOYEE TO COMPLY

When the agreement calls for both parties to perform or refrain from performing certain acts, an employee must comply with the requirements of the agreement or he will not be heard on a petition for enforcement. An employee may not seek reinstatement of a complaint on the basis that the agency failed to comply with a settlement agreement, when the failure is occasioned by the employee's own conduct. See Dalton v. USPS, 01830714 (1983) (a delay in re-employment was due to the employee's failure to follow agency regulations on the submission of required paperwork). Conversely, a complainant's failure to perform his or her commitments under an agreement will be excused where that failure is due to agency actions or inactions. See Redschlag v. Secretary of Army, 01972651 (1998). In that case, the agreement provided that the complainant would apply for every position of greater or equal salary for which she was qualified. However, the complainant did not apply for some vacancies because the agency had not completed its commitments to purge her personnel file of certain actions and revise her performance standards. Under the circumstances, the Commission excused the complainant's failure to comply with the terms of the agreement.

The Commission's position is really just an application of the common law doctrine that those who seek equity must come with "clean hands." An agency will not be found in breach of a settlement agreement where the failure of the agency to comply with the terms of the agreement is attributable to the subsequent conduct of the complainant. For example, in Osler v. Secretary of Treasury, 01930824 (1993), the Commission found the agency was not in breach of an agreement to detail the complainant to a certain position where the reason for its failure was her proposed removal on charges unrelated to the agreement. The agency proposed the complainant's removal and placed her on administrative leave for destroying government computer files and removing government documents from the agency premises.

Where a settlement agreement provided the complainant is responsible for paying for a medical examination to determine fitness for duty, the complainant did not breach the agreement by failing to pay. The agreement only allocated the responsibility for payment and did not mandate actual payment as a condition precedent to the agency's obligations to offer employment. Mahone v. Postmaster General, 01932833 (1993). In Miles v. Postmaster General, 01A53512 (2006), the Commission denied the complainant's enforcement petition on the basis that he had failed to discharge his obligations under the settlement agreement. The agreement provided that the complainant would be paid at the GS--7 level while he received training and applied for promotions to that level. Two years later, the agency withdrew the complainant's GS--7 level pay. The Commission, in denying the enforcement petition, held that the complainant had not performed his obligations under the agreement in that he had not taken advantage of available training opportunities and had not applied for any promotions to the GS--7 level.

An agency will not be found to have breached an agreement where any failure to comply is the result of the complainant's conduct. For example, in Complainant v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120141126 (2014), the Commission found that the agency did not breach its obligation to provide mentoring, training and independent study time to the complainant where the complainant was absent from the workplace during the time period the benefits were to be provided. The Commission also noted that the agency had offered to extend the time period under the settlement agreement to provide the benefits.

When an agency refuses to reinstate a complaint on the basis of failure to comply with the agreement, it must permit sufficient factual development of the record to support its claim that the failure is due to the actions of the employee. The agency's mere assertion of failure to comply is an inadequate factual explanation. Mendez v. USPS, 01861679 (1986).

Just as it found with agencies, there have been instances in which the Commission has found "substantial compliance" on the part of the complainant even where there has not been technical compliance. For example, in Massie v. Postmaster General, 01983146 (1999), the Commission found that the agency improperly denied the appellant's claim for attorney fees pursuant to a settlement agreement on the basis that her request was untimely:

In the instant case, the agency received the appellant's request for attorneys fees only thirteen days after the 30 day period the parties had agreed to in their settlement agreement. Although the appellant's attorney could have proceeded with more diligence in verifying the agency's receipt of their correspondence, we find that there was substantial compliance with the settlement agreement on the part of the appellant. In addition, the agency did not articulate any harm or prejudice suffered as a result of the delay in getting the fee request and therefore, it should be accepted and acted upon by the agency.

The Commission did not dispute the contention of the complainant's attorney that he had notified the agency within the 30-day period that a death in his family meant he would be unable to timely file the fee request. The agency denied receiving such a notice. However, the Commission's decision does not appear to hinge upon that notice, except to the extent it noted the complainant's attorney could have been more diligent about verifying the agency's receipt of the notice. Instead, it appears the Commission decided the case solely on the basis that the fee request was not significantly late and the agency was not prejudiced by the short delay in receiving the request.

When the breach of the agreement is a result of the employee's conduct, the agency is excused from performance. In Pickett v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120072569 (2008), the agency agreed to appoint the complainant to an attaché position in October 2006. In June 2006, the complainant had an accident with a government vehicle. Because the investigation was ongoing and the agency had a need to immediately fill the attaché position in October, the Commission found that the agency was relieved of its obligation under the settlement agreement. See also Duncan v. Secretary of Air Force, 0120070064 (2008).
