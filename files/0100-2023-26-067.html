l. Limits on the Chilling Effect Doctrine

Still, the chilling effect doctrine does have some limits. In McDermott v. Postmaster General, 01A61348 (2006), the Commission held that the complainant was not aggrieved when she alleged that a coworker who had previously assisted her with an EEO complaint was placed on administrative leave. The Commission found:

The Commission also agrees with the agency that complainant fails to state a claim regarding reprisal. In a claim of reprisal, the alleged adverse actions need not materially affect the terms and conditions of employment to constitute retaliation. Lindsey v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05980410 (Nov. 4, 1999) (citing EEOC Compliance Manual, No. 915.003 (May 20, 1998)). Instead, the statutory retaliation clauses prohibit any adverse treatment that is based upon a retaliatory motive and is reasonably likely to deter the charging party or others from engaging in protected activity. Id. However, complainant has not claimed that she was retaliated against for protected activity. Rather, she claims that another employee suffered retaliation, and brings no allegations of any adverse treatment she endured.

Furthermore, the Commission finds that a "chilling effect" on the EEO complaint process if complainant is held not to have standing is very unlikely. On appeal, complainant alleges that dismissal of her claim would deter others from filing complaints, or assisting complainant with her future and current complaints. We find that this is an unlikely effect because her co-workers are already filing complaints. According to the agency in its brief in opposition to this appeal, the co-worker who complainant alleges to have suffered reprisal with Administrative Leave has initiated her own EEO complaint on July 27, 2005. Accordingly, we affirm the agency's decision to dismiss complainant's claim as to reprisal for prior EEO activity.

Similarly, in Young v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01A62299 (2006), the Commission found that the complainant failed to state a claim where he alleged that the agency asked him for documentation as to whether he was serving as the union representative or personal representative of a coworker in an EEO complaint. The Commission found that such a request was not reasonably likely to deter someone from engaging in protected activity. The Commission also found in Cordita v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120122797 (2013), that the complainant failed to state a claim where she alleged that the agency's request for additional medical information in response to an accommodation request was retaliation. The Commission noted that the agency had already granted the complainant's request to work on the day shift as an accommodation and, based on the initial information provided by the complainant, was inquiring into the effect of medication on her ability to perform her nursing duties. The Commission determined that the request was not likely to deter a reasonable person from filing or pursuing a complaint.

In Hinds v. Postmaster General, 0120072170 (2007), the Commission found that the complainant had not demonstrated retaliation where, during a selection interview, she was asked if she would ever discourage an employee from filing an EEO complaint. The Commission found that the question was asked of all interview candidates because it was situation that arose with some frequency at the agency.

The Commission also found no chilling effect where the manager's comments about the complainant filing frivolous EEO complaints was in response to comments by the complainant that his past abusive behavior had caused her to file complaints. In Gibbs v. Postmaster General, 0120122208 (2012), the Commission explained:

When the context in which the comment was made is considered, this is not a situation in which [M1] was making any kind of threatening or harassing comment. Rather, [M1] legitimately believed that Complainant had provided false information in a past EEO, and he was responding to Complainant's statement that he had exhibited abusive conduct to her in the past causing her to file an EEO. Overall, it cannot be said that [M1's] comment is reasonably likely to deter the charging party or others from engaging in protected activity. See Nurriddin v. National Aeronautics and Space Administration, EEOC Appeal No. 0IA23148 (September 30, 2004) (single derogatory comment made by the supervisor in reference to the complainant's EEO complaint did not rise above the level of a petty slight or trivial annoyance and was not actionable retaliatory conduct).

We agree with the AJ that the single comment at issue given the context in which it arose is insufficient to result in a finding of discrimination.

Sometimes a complainant can protest too much. For example, in Lisoyo v. Postmaster General, 05990684 (2001), the Commission rejected the complainant's assertion that the comments of a supervisor had a chilling effect. The Commission noted that, in his request for reconsideration, the complainant stated he had filed two more complaints of reprisal since the incident in addition to the claim under appeal. Obviously, the Commission found the supervisor's statements had not had a chilling effect on the complainant.

The Commission found no chilling effect where the complainant alleged that, as part of an investigation into damage of a police vehicle, he was ordered to unload his weapon, report to an investigative interview, denied a union representative, and questioned about his body marks and tattoos. Eason v. Secretary of Navy, 0120064566 (2007).
