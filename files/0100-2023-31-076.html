2. Change in Counsel

It is a fact of life that complainants occasionally change counsel. Given the length of some EEO cases, at times a change in counsel is required by the death, retirement or illness of counsel. Other times, the complainant, for a variety of reasons, may simply become disenchanted with his or her oriGINAl counsel. When there is a change in counsel, the complainant is entitled to a fee award that encompasses the services of all attorneys who worked on the case, provided the work is not duplicative or otherwise excessive or unnecessary.

In Schuldenfrei v. Secretary of Energy, 01966664 (2000), the Commission rejected an argument by the agency that the complainant was only entitled to a fee award that covered the counsel of record at the time of settlement. The Commission in Schuldenfrei explained:

In the instant case, the record reflects that Attorney 1 was the attorney of record who initially filed the formal complaint of discrimination against the agency on May 7, 1993. The record also reflects that there were settlement negotiations in process before Attorney 2 was retained by complainant, which is reflected by letters containing settlement offers and counter-offers dated September 24, 1993 and November 19, 1993. Attorney 2 was not designated as complainant's legal representative of record until July 17, 1995, which is approximately two years and two months after Attorney 1 filed the formal complaint on complainant's behalf. The agency's argument that only the attorney of record at the time of resolution of a complaint is entitled to attorney's fees is a specious argument unsupported by existing case law or regulatory standards. Moreover, the record clearly demonstrates, contrary to the agency's assertion, that Attorney 1 was involved in the negotiations leading up to the final settlement by virtue of the fact that he (and his law firm) was the attorney of record during from 1993 until July 17, 1995, during which time settlement agreements and counter-offers were memorialized and documented as a part of the record. In addition, the agency did not cite specific references of possible duplication of effort or waste. In light of the above, we find the agency's cited rationale for reducing Attorney 1's fees not supported by the record or regulations.

The agency in Schuldenfrei also tried to reduce the fees by stating that it should not be penalized by the complainant's decision to change attorney midstream, to which the Commission replied:

Once again, we find the agency's rationale for reducing fees unsupported by existing case law and regulatory standards. Absent some evidence of duplication of effort, we find no legal standard which supports the contention that changing attorneys during the processing of a formal EEO complaint voids reimbursement of attorneys fees for previous attorneys. Moreover, complainant has supplied the agency with a rationale for the change of legal representation. Specifically, complainant asserted that he decided to retain Attorney 2 when he became aware that Attorney 2 successfully negotiated a settlement with this agency in a nearly identical claim arising from the non-selection in the same SES program. Therefore, in light of the above, complainant believed Attorney 2 would be intimately familiar with the legal parameters of his case. The agency has also failed to cite specific charges which it claims the two attorneys may have duplicated hours. Having failed to do so, we find that the agency's second cited rationale for reducing Attorney 1's fee request is unsupported by the record or regulations.

It is unclear from the Schuldenfrei decision whether the complainant must advance an explanation for the change in counsel or the Commission was simply responding to the agency's rationale for reducing the fee award. The better view is that no explanation for the change is necessary and that the focus should be on whether there are duplicative services that should not be compensated. One of the problems with requiring a rationale for the change is the possible intrusion on the attorney-client privilege.

When there is a change in counsel, the prior counsel has no standing in an appeal for purposes of protecting his or her interest in attorney fees. In Scott v. Secretary of Energy, 0720070018 (2007), the Commission also held that it had no authority to issue a lien against any award of fees:

Finally, we note that complainant's former attorney has petitioned the Commission to allow him to intervene in the instant appeal because he maintains that the AJ miscalculated attorney's fees, and complainant owes him a "substantial sum of money for unpaid fees and expenses." The former attorney requests that the Commission place a lien on any award to complainant. The record reveals that complainant hired a new attorney to represent her in this matter on March 20, 2006. Upon review of this matter, we determine that the dispute between complainant and her former attorney is a private contractual matter not within our purview, and we therefore have no authority to issue a lien on complainant's award. We further determine that complainant's former attorney has no standing to contest the AJ's award of attorney's fees apart from complainant's right to appeal the matter to the Commission, which complainant chose not do in this case. Consequently, the former attorney's requests are denied.

Similarly, in Zoila P. v. Attorney General, 0520160112 (2017), the Commission denied reconsideration of the administrative judge's finding that the complainant's former attorney lacked standing to pursue an appeal on the sole issue of attorney fees where the complainant had withdrawn from the case and terminated the lawyer-client relationship. The Commission in Zoila P. further rejected the attorney's argument that the complainant had signed an assignment of rights agreement which permitted the attorney to further pursue the appeal:

In its request for reconsideration, Complainant's former attorney requests that we now find the law firm has standing to appeal the award of attorney's fees. The attorney asserts that "Nevada permits the assignment of the rights to pursue attorneys' fees." However, we find that our prior decision properly addressed this matter. Specifically, we found that "[t]he Assignment Agreements in this case evince the fact that Complainant withdrew from pursuing an appeal of the AJ's award of attorney's fees and terminated her lawyer-client relationship with Firm 1 and Firm 2. [Complainant's former attorneys] cannot pursue this appeal on their own because they are not the prevailing parties in this case and therefore lack standing to pursue an appeal on the sole issue of attorney's fees without the participation of Complainant.... An attorney lacks standing to contest an AJ's award of attorney's fees apart from a Complainant's right to appeal the matter to the Commission. Scott v. Dep't of Energy, EEOC Appeal No. 0720070018 (April 24, 2007); see also Raney v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 01A51839(2005) (dismissing appeal solely filed by a complainant's former attorney and concluding the attorney does not have standing to file an appeal for attorney's fees); Crews v. Dep't of Defense, EEOC Appeal No. 0120111196 (Aug. 22, 2012) (dismissing appeal after determining that it was filed solely by former attorney and attorney did not have standing to file an appeal for attorney's fees because attorney was not a party in the case)." Based on the foregoing, we find that our prior decision correctly followed Commission precedent on this issue.
