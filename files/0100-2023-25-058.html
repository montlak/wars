VI. ATTORNEY FEES

The ADEA does not provide for attorney fees. See Jarmillo v. Postmaster General, 01991009 (2001). However, the Commission previously intimated that fees may be available for reprisal claims based on an underlying ADEA complaint because the Commission's regulation barring reprisal is independent of the ADEA. In Terracina v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01912460 (1991), the Commission rejected an agency's argument that it was not obligated to pay reasonable attorney fees under a settlement agreement specifically providing for fees because the underlying claim, on which the reprisal claim arose, was made under the ADEA. In addition to finding that the agency could not unilaterally repudiate an express provision of the settlement agreement, the Commission in Terracina went on to note:

EEOC Regulation 29 CFR 1613.511 states:

The agency shall provide regulations governing the acceptance and processing of complaints of discrimination on account of age which, subject to 1613.514, comply with principles and requirements in 1613.213 through 1613.222, 1613.241 and 1613.261 through 1613.271 of this part.

The allegation in appellant's reprisal complaint is that because of reprisal for having filed an age discrimination complaint, she received an unfair performance appraisal....

Nothing in EEOC Regulations or in the policy of this Commission precludes an appellant from receiving payment of reasonable attorney's fees incurred in the processing of her reprisal complaint as claimed by the agency. This is especially true where, as here, the complainant's charge is premised upon a separate complaint, filed under a provision independent of the ADEA....

The suggestion in Terracina was that the omission of the attorney fee provision in the ADEA did not affect reprisal claims because those claims arise independent of the ADEA. But see Numrich v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01911429 (1991) (noting, in dicta, a previous Commission decision holding that attorney fees are not available in reprisal cases based on the ADEA). See also Chapter 22, subheading "Age Discrimination," for a discussion on attorney fees for age discrimination.

The Commission reversed itself in Terracina v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 05910888 (1992). The Commission held that it erred when it found that the complaint was based on a reprisal provision independent of the ADEA. Because reprisal is specifically prohibited by the ADEA and the Statute does not provide for an award of attorney fees in the administrative process, the agency could not agree to award fees through settlement.

When an agency does agree to pay attorney fees in an age discrimination case and later reneges on the settlement as being outside EEOC precedent, the proper remedy is to reinstate the complaint. See Patten v. Secretary of Defense, 01991047 (1999).
