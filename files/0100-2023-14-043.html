G. SELECTIVE INTERPRETATION

In interpreting a settlement agreement, the Commission will attempt to give meaning to all provisions of the agreement, where possible. To support its interpretation of an agreement, a party may not focus on one clause of a provision to the exclusion of another clause. As the Commission explained in Wong v. Postmaster General, 05931097 (1994):

Initially, we note that the language of the settlement agreement at issue establishes that the agency was required to "provide the [appellant] with a casual appointment of 89 days in this calendar year as soon as possible; and to consider [appellant] for a Temporary Employee position without prejudice." The settlement agreement further provided that "[appellant] understands the appointment(s) will be processed in accordance with existing rules and regulations." The evidence of record reveals that the agency did consider appellant for a tentative casual appointment in August 1993, which was contingent upon appellant's successful completion of the required Medical Examination & Assessment. In his request, appellant asserts that, despite the terms of the settlement agreement, the agency afforded other individuals casual appointments from January 27, 1993 to August 1993. The agency does not dispute appellant's contentions in this regard. Rather, the agency adamantly relies upon the language in the settlement agreement which provided that appellant would be provided a causal appointment within "this calendar year," to justify its decision to hire other individuals for casual appointments during the disputed time period.

In reviewing the terms of a settlement agreement to determine whether a breach has occurred, we are obligated to consider the full context of the language cited therein. In this matter, we find the agency's reliance upon the term "in this calendar year" without considering the full context of the settlement agreement is entirely misplaced. We find that the agency's reliance upon this specific language, while clearly ignoring the remaining language "as soon as possible," demonstrates that the agency did not act in good faith in complying with the terms of the settlement agreement. The agency has failed to show that it could not have given appellant a casual appointment earlier than it did. In light of appellant's assertion that the agency afforded other individuals casual appointments prior to appellant, we find that the agency breached the settlement agreement's requirement that he be given a casual appointment "as soon as possible." Any other reading of the agreement would render the term "as soon as possible" meaningless.

Of course, it is possible for a settlement agreement to contain mutually exclusive provisions or promises. In such cases, the Commission generally will find that the parties failed to reach a "meeting of the minds" and that no settlement exists. For further discussion of such situations, see section on "Intent of the Parties," above.
