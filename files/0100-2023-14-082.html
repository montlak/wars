3. Interest as Sanction

In a case that indicated the Commission's departure from finding a breach where there is a delay in the execution of terms but awarding no remedy, the EEOC ordered that interest be paid on a compensatory damage award that was not timely paid by the agency. In Damario v. Postmaster General, 01983665 (1999), the settlement agreement called for the agency to pay the damages within 14 days, but the agency delayed payment for 97 days. The Commission in Damario found:

Unlike the cases above where the breaches were de minimis, the agency here failed to comply with a clear stipulation that appellant receive prompt payment within 14 calendar days from the date of ratification. The agency tendered payment 97 days beyond the date agreed upon by the parties, and only after appellant pressed her claim by appealing to the Commission. Based upon the foregoing, the Commission finds that the agency has clearly not shown substantial compliance with the terms of the agreement.

Because of the agency's breach of what we find to be a significant stipulation in the agreement, the Commission finds that appellant is entitled to an award of interest for the agency's delay in paying compensatory damages. See Cole v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05910450 (February 1997). Section 717(d) of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, 42 USC § 2000e 16(d), as amended by section 114(2) of the Civil Rights Act of 1991, Pub. L. 102-166 (Nov. 21, 1991), provides that "the same interest to compensate for delay in payment shall be available [to federal employees] as in cases involving nonpublic parties."

In Brown v. Secretary of Defense, 01985765 (1999), the Commission found that not only was the agency in breach of settlement, having delayed more than ten months despite the agreement containing exact specifications, but that it then delayed compliance another four months after the complainant alleged breach. Therefore, in Brown, the Commission held:

Complainant requests payment of interest to remedy the agency's unreasonable conduct. The Commission has held that where an agency fails to act in "good faith," interest may be assessed for delayed payments even though interest was not contemplated within the settlement agreement. See April v. Department Of Agriculture, EEOC Appeal No. 01963775 (June 5, 1997); cf. Donald v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01973329 (Feb. 9, 1998) (finding that because back-pay was reimbursed within a reasonable time, no interest was due). Further, the agency cannot breach an agreement by unreasonable delay, and then attempt to use the provisions of that agreement to shield it from an obligation to pay interest. Accordingly, the agency must reimburse complainant for the interest on the unreasonably withheld differential pay from December 8, 1997, until September 1, 1998.

See also Fallwell v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01A14685 (2002), holding that where the agency agreed to pay the complainant within 30 days of settlement, but made no attempt to make the payment for 17 months, interest accrued from the day the check should have issued until the date it actually did.

When an agency does not execute the terms of the agreement in the time specified in the agreement, but responds to a complaint of breach by issuing the disputed payment with interest, the breach is cured and any subsequent petition for enforcement will be denied. Schafer v. Secretary of Interior, 01A11542 (2002).
