10. Shortage of Investigators/Outsourcing Investigations

Fewer and fewer agencies actually conduct their own EEO investigations and more and more agencies use contract investigators. While the Commission has not issued any express guidance in the area of contract investigations, it has made clear that when there is a contract investigation, it is the agency that remains responsible for the timeliness and quality of investigation. In Cox v. Social Security Administration, 0720050055 (2009), the Commission, in upholding a sanction of default judgment against the agency, observed:

The fact that the agency contracts with an outside company to conduct the investigation does not absolve it of its responsibility to ensure that the ROI is adequately developed on which to base a decision. See 29 C.F.R. § 1614.108; EEO MD-110, p. 5-27. The agency has a duty to develop an impartial and appropriate factual record upon which to make findings on the claims raised by the written complaint. An appropriate factual record is one that allows a reasonable fact finder to draw conclusions as to whether discrimination occurred. See Carr v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Appeal 01A43665 (May 18, 2006); 29 C.F.R. § 1614.108(a). The purpose of discovery is to perfect the record in the hearing process, but it is not a substitute for an appropriate investigation. Moreover, the Commission notes that every complainant does not choose the option of requesting a hearing.

The regulations found at 29 C.F.R. § 1614.108(b) squarely place the responsibility for an accurate, complete investigation, completed within 180 days, upon the agency. Even if an agency contracts with a company to produce the investigation, it retains control of the outcome of that investigation, and is well within its rights to review the result and require the contractor to complete it in a satisfactory manner. Contracting out the investigation does not relieve an agency of its responsibility to ensure that a complete and timely investigation has occurred.

Citing Cox, the Commission in Genaro G. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 2020004241 (2022), rejected the following agency argument as a show of good cause for its failure to timely complete the investigation:

The Agency further argued that sanctions were not warranted because it had good cause for the delay in completing the investigation: namely, the unavailability of investigators. In the Agency's view, its situation was distinguishable from other cases because the delay was attributable to "an unanticipated security clearance issue that arose in 2015 in regard to FEMA's contract investigators," rather than inadequate or untimely performance by Agency employees or contract investigators. According to the Agency, the delay did not prejudice Complainant nor undermine the integrity of the system. The 223-day delay in completing the investigation, asserted the Agency, was not as significant as the delay in cases where sanctions were imposed.

...

On October 3, 2016, when the Agency failed to complete the investigation within 180 days, Complainant requested a hearing. On December 8, 2016, AJ-1 ordered the Agency to provide the complete investigative file, within the earlier of 15 days from receipt of the order or from receipt of Complainant's hearing request. The Agency did not respond to the December 8, 2016, order until April 12, 2017, and it never responded to the June 12, 2017, Order to Show Cause. The Agency provided the report of investigation to the Commission on June 20, 2017, 442 days after Complainant filed his complaint. It waited yet another week to transmit the report of investigation to Complainant. As AJ-2 noted in the Sanctions Order, not only does the unavailability of investigators to timely complete investigations EEO complaints not amount to good cause, the Agency's failure to ensure it had enough investigators is an affront to the Commission's regulations and the federal sector process. The Agency is ultimately responsible for timely completion of investigations of EEO complaints, whether the delay is attributable to contractors and Agency employees, or the failure of Agency officials to ensure the availability of contractors. See Cox v. Soc. Sec. Admin., EEOC Appeal No. 0720050055 (Dec. 24, 2009); Germain v. Envt'l. Prot. Agency, EEOC Appeal No. 07A10048 (Dec. 23, 2002); Mitchell-Wilcox v. Tenn. Valley Authority, EEOC Request No. 05920176 (Apr. 23, 1992). We agree with AJ-2's determination that the Agency failed to establish good cause for its failure to timely complete the investigation, timely provide the investigative file to Complainant, and comply with the AJ's order.
