broida eeo Chapter 03: Formal Complaint Process--Acceptance and Dismissal Chapter 03: Formal Complaint Process--Acceptance and Dismissal, Subchapter 03: Bases for Dismissal of Complaints, Section K: Failure to Prosecute or Cooperate

K. FAILURE TO PROSECUTE OR COOPERATE

Under 29 CFR 1614.107(a)(6) and (7), an agency may dismiss a complaint on the basis of failure to prosecute or cooperate only after giving the complainant written notice. The sections provide for dismissal of the complaint:

(6) Where the complainant cannot be located, provided that reasonable efforts have been made to locate the complainant and the complainant has not responded within 15 days to a notice of proposed dismissal sent to his or her last known address;

(7) Where the agency has provided the complainant with a written request to provide relevant information or otherwise proceed with the complaint, and the complainant has failed to respond to the request within 15 days of its receipt or the complainant's response does not address the agency's request, provided the request included a notice of the proposed dismissal. Instead of dismissing for failure to cooperate, the complaint may be adjudicated if sufficient information for that purpose is available....

The regulation represents a vast improvement from the case-by-case approach previously used by the Commission to decide whether the agency was justified in dismissing the complaint without notice. Even given the clarity of the new regulation, there are still cases where the employee fails to respond and the complaint is subsequently dismissed. And, the Commission still reviews such dismissals not only to ensure compliance with the notice provisions, but to ensure that complaints are not wrongfully dismissed. Some discussion of the previous case law may be helpful in assessing those situations.

As discussed further below, dismissal for failure to cooperate or prosecute has three elements: (1) the complainant must receive notice of the agency's intent to dismiss the complaint and be given 15 days to cure any defect; (2) in order to dismiss the complaint, the agency must establish a clear record of "delay and contumacy" on the part of the complainant; (3) the agency must establish that the existing record is not sufficient to permit a determination on the merits in lieu of dismissal. The Commission's position on dismissal under 29 CFR 1614.107(a)(6) and (7) is simple; it favors adjudication on the merits and disfavors dismissal on procedural grounds. See, e.g., Hodnett v. Secretary of Army, 01A04379 (2001) ("It is only in cases where the complainant has engaged in delay or contumacious conduct and the record is insufficient to permit adjudication that the Commission has allowed a complaint to be dismissed for failure to cooperate.").

A complaint may be dismissed for failure to prosecute only if there is a "clear record of delay and contumacy on the part of the complaining party." Rudolph v. USPS, 01830515 (1983). It was error for the agency to dismiss a complaint for failure to prosecute when it requested clarification of the allegations and the complainant, through her representative, made a timely and good faith response to the request. The proper remedy, where the allegations remain unclear after the complainant's response, is to remand the complaint to the EEO counselor for further clarification. Id. There are cases holding that, if the complainant declines to clarify the allegations of a complaint with sufficient specificity after the agency has so requested, then it may dismiss the complaint for failure to prosecute. See, e.g., Lewis v. Department of Navy, 01830356 (1983). However, those cases are only correct to the extent that the Rudolph requirement of a "clear record of delay and contumacy" is met.

As the Commission further explained in Przygoda v. Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, 01940900 (1994):

In the case at hand, we find that appellant did not respond to the agency's September 16, 1993 request within 15 calendar days. Although appellant asserted that the letter did not "come to [his] attention" until October 1, 1993, the record shows that the letter was received at appellant's residence on September 21, 1993. The Commission has held that the receipt of a document by a member of the complainant's family at the complainant's home address constitutes effective receipt by the complainant. See Fischer v. USPS, EEOC Request No. 05900389 (May 3, 1990). Although that decision dealt with the receipt of a final agency decision, we find the rationale to be equally applicable to this case.

Nevertheless, the Commission notes that at the time the agency issued the final decision herein, it had received appellant's response stating that he continually requested the particular assignments referred to, and that he did not receive any information concerning his nonselection for the Review Examiner positions until obtaining the agency's response to his FOIA requests on April 6, 1993. The Commission has found that an agency's decision to dismiss a complaint for failure to cooperate should be made only when there is a clear record of delay or contumacious conduct by the complainant. See Connally v. Papachristid Shipping Ltd., et al., 504 F.2d 917 (5th Cir. 1974). We find that the record in this case fails to show that appellant engaged in delay or contumacious conduct as described in Connally, supra. In so finding, the Commission notes that appellant represented that he was on a road assignment at the time the agency's request was received at his home. In addition, it appears that the record contained sufficient information from which the agency could have determined whether appellant timely contacted the EEO Counselor. Accordingly, the agency's decision to dismiss appellant's complaint for failure to cooperate is REVERSED, and appellant's complaint is hereby REMANDED to the agency for further processing in accordance with this decision and the applicable regulations.

Compliance by an agency with the letter of 107(a)(6) or (7), though a necessary predicate to dismissal, does not always justify dismissal of a complaint. For example, in Semien v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01955516 (1996), the agency sent the required notice letter and subsequently issued a final decision dismissing the case after the letter was returned unclaimed. However, the Commission reversed the dismissal on appeal, noting that the complainant was homeless but that she attended frequent meetings at the agency. Of particular significance, the Commission noted that the agency had been able to locate the complainant to deliver the final decision dismissing her complaint to her.

A failure to inquire about the status of a complaint is not a failure to prosecute where the complainant received acknowledgment of the complaint and resolution of the complaint was discussed in the context of settlement negotiations over another complaint. Bull v. Secretary of Treasury, 01A02619 (2000).

"[T]he sanction of dismissal for failure to prosecute should only be imposed where the complaining party demonstrates a willful refusal to cooperate or an intentional delay of the proceedings." Wilson v. Secretary of Transportation, 05880634 (1988) (Emphasis in oriGINAl.) In Wilson, the Commission concluded that the agency failed to show that the complainant "willfully or knowingly" delayed the proceeding by not responding to the proposed disposition within 15 calendar days where the agency sent the disposition to the complainant's former attorney. The Commission was unwilling to assume that the proposed disposition had been received simply because the complainant had received other documents sent to his former attorney.

"As a general rule, a complainant fails to prosecute a complaint when he acts to obstruct the complaint process, or when his action or failure to act clearly indicates a lack of interest in pursuing the complaint." Lopez v. Secretary of Army, 01912709 (1991) (citing Connolly v. Papachrisid Shipping Ltd., et al., 504 F.2d 917 (5th Cir. 1974)). The agency must provide needed information that is within its possession and must help a complainant clarify a complaint prior to dismissing for failure to prosecute.

In most cases, an agency will be required to give a complainant more than one opportunity to provide information prior to dismissing a complaint for failure to cooperate. It appears, however, that in doing so, the agency must advise the complainant of the consequences of failure to cooperate on each opportunity. For example, in Angel v. Postmaster General, 01A34412 (2003), the Commission reversed the dismissal of a complaint where the first request to provide an affidavit, which included the 15-day notice, did not elicit a response. The agency then sent the complainant a second and third letter requesting that he provide an affidavit. However, neither of those letters included the 15-day notice. In response to the third letter, the complainant called the EEO office and indicated he was sending 100 exhibits. He asked and was informed that he still had to complete an affidavit. Under these circumstances, the Commission found that dismissal was not warranted because the complainant was not warned that his failure to cooperate could result in dismissal of his complaint.

A complaint may not be dismissed for failure to prosecute because of the complainant's conduct in other, unrelated cases. Wrenn v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01921354 (1992). In that case, the appellant failed to appear for the EEO hearing after submitting a request for postponement of the hearing due to an emergency medical appointment. While the Commission's decision did not go into specifics, it did make clear that, in dismissing the complaint, the agency relied on several instances of "contumacious" conduct in previous cases. However, it should be noted that the Commission does occasionally dismiss cases for abuse of process and has now codified its position on abuse of process dismissals at 29 CFR 1614.107(a)(9). See below.

Even when the sanction of dismissal for failure to prosecute is proper, it must be applied narrowly. If an agency's determination that a complainant has failed to cooperate relates only to one issue in the complaint, the agency errs in dismissing the entire complaint. Riddick v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01932104 (1993).

Once the agency establishes a "clear record of contumacy and delay," it also must determine whether a lesser sanction than dismissal of the complaint would serve the interests of justice. See Markman v. Department of Treasury, 01830761 (1983). The interests of justice are served by dismissal when the complainant fails to cooperate with the agency investigation by insisting it be conducted by the EEOC rather than an agency employee. Id. The fact that an employee does not deem his complaint too vague does not justify his failure to respond to an agency request for additional information when the agency put the employee on notice that failure to respond could result in dismissal of the complaint. See Keeler v. Department of Health and Human Services, 01831291 (1983). The Commission upheld the agency's dismissal without reviewing the allegations to see if they were, in fact, vague. But in Wright v. USPS, 01831406 (1983), the Commission overturned the agency's dismissal of the complaint where it made no demonstration of the additional information it sought, and the EEOC found the counselor's report to be sufficiently clear to frame the issues for investigation. The Commission has also held that an agency is not justified in dismissing a complaint for failure to prosecute because the complainant fails to respond to a letter requesting clarification of her allegations, when the allegations were sufficiently clear in the first instance. See Tarry v. Secretary of Commerce, 05920653 (1993). The complainant's failure to respond did not constitute contumacious conduct where the agency could identify no ambiguity or lack of clarity in the oriGINAl allegations. But, when the documents attached to a complaint do not clarify the issues being raised and the complainant refuses to respond to requests to clarify the issues, the agency properly dismisses the complaint for failure to cooperate. See Shapiro v. Secretary of Army, 01961476 (1996).

The fact that the employee also has registered complaints with persons outside of the EEO process is not grounds for dismissing a complaint on the basis of failure to prosecute. Bitters v. USPS, 01873428 (1988) (EEO regulations do not prohibit employees from contacting members of Congress about a complaint).
