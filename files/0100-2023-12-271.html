V. WITHDRAWAL OF COMPLAINTS

As a general rule, a complainant may not withdraw a complaint and then file another complaint on the same matter. Williams v. Postmaster General, 05950696 (1996). As the Commission explained in Williams, once the claim was has been withdrawn, the complainant forfeits all rights to refile the claim:

The previous decision found the agency's dismissal to be improper, stating that appellant did not file a formal complaint regarding the matter prior to withdrawing the allegation in question. However, the Commission has previously affirmed the agency's decision to dismiss a complaint as stating the same claim as that raised previously where the complainant had earlier withdrawn the matter at the informal counseling stage. Puissegur v. USPS, EEOC Request No. 05920650 (April 15, 1993). Specifically, the Commission stated that, by knowingly and voluntarily withdrawing an allegation from EEO counseling, and expressly waiving the right to further administrative processing, the complainant forfeited his right to subsequently file a complaint on the same matter.

As stated, appellant withdrew her allegation concerning the denial of backpay and benefits from counseling in writing on April 22, 1994. While appellant stated that she was confused by a letter she received from the union concerning her grievance, appellant does not assert that she was misled or otherwise given erroneous information by agency personnel. Further, we find the language of the withdrawal notice which appellant signed to be sufficiently clear and specific.

See also Sarmiento v. Postmaster General, 01A00867 (2001).

Of course, as with any rule there are exceptions. One of those exceptions is where the complaint is withdrawn under duress. When a complainant alleges duress in the withdrawal of her complaint and wishes to reinstate her complaint, she must support the allegation with specific and persuasive evidence. In Cobey v. Secretary of Defense, 05940355 (1994), the Commission explained the type of evidence that would demonstrate duress:

The appropriate issue to be decided by the Commission was and is whether appellant's rescission of that complaint was made under duress or as a result of judgment impaired by mental illness. In this regard, we find that the record is insufficient to support the previous decision's finding that appellant failed to prove her allegations of duress or mental incapacity. Appellant raised very specific and serious allegations regarding the conduct of agency EEO officials towards her while she was on sick leave recuperating from a hospital stay for treatment of a mental illness. The agency has thus far failed to offer a response to these very specific and serious allegations. Therefore, we will remand this case in order to obtain such a response from the agency. Both appellant and the agency shall have the opportunity on remand to address the aforementioned allegations through sworn testimony taken in affidavit form from appellant and from the subject agency EEO officials.

Moreover, appellant also asserts that at the time she returned to work and submitted the letter withdrawing her complaint, she was under the influence of certain medication that had been prescribed for her and was still mentally incapable of making the determination to withdraw her EEO complaint. Relevant medical authority indicates that confusion, disorientation, anxiety, decreased concentration, drowsiness, dizziness, and lightheadedness are all possible adverse effects of the medications appellant was taking. However, it is not clear from the record whether, and to what degree, appellant experienced such adverse effects at the time she withdrew her complaint. Therefore, on remand, appellant shall provide any evidence available concerning specific adverse effects that she experienced from the medication she was taking at that time, which could have interfered with her judgment on the date that she withdrew her EEO complaint.

To further support her assertion of mental incapacity, appellant submits a letter from a psychiatrist that refers to her hospitalization, indicates that her illness resulted in impaired judgment and then states that decisions and actions she made "at that time" were a product of that impaired judgment. From the statement given, it is unclear whether the psychiatrist is referring only to decisions made and actions taken during the period of appellant's hospitalization, or whether his statement would embrace actions taken by appellant shortly thereafter, including specifically March 16, 1993, the date appellant withdrew her EEO complaint. In view of this lack of clarity, on remand, appellant should submit a specific statement from this psychiatrist as to whether he intended that the actions taken by appellant on March 16, 1993 be included in his assessment of actions taken based on impaired judgment. Appellant shall also be given the opportunity to produce for the record, any other medical documentation which would indicate that she was mentally incapable of making the determination to withdraw her complaint at the time she did so.

Finally, appellant's letter requesting that her withdrawal of her complaint be rescinded and subsequent documents indicate that she also intended to raise an allegation before the agency that its failure to permit her reinstatement in her former position as a Police Officer after her hospitalization was taken in retaliation for her action in filing a sexual harassment complaint. Therefore, on remand the agency is directed to provide appellant with EEO counseling on this additional allegation.

Note that the complainant must not only prove the existence of circumstances that constitute duress, but a causal connection between the duress and the action of withdrawing the complaint.

Withdrawing a complaint because of ill health and insufficient resources does not make the withdrawal involuntary. Tellez v. Secretary of Transportation, 05930805 (1994). "Appellant has not shown that his capacity at the time he withdrew his complaint was so diminished that he did not comprehend the ramifications of his actions. Moreover, there is no evidence that the withdrawal was coerced." Id.

In Grappone v. Secretary of Navy, 01964459 (1998), the Commission found that the complainant's withdrawal of his complaint was not voluntary where he was told that because he had not filed any EEO complaints he could not pursue a reprisal complaint. At the time, the complainant had a grievance pending that alleged discrimination. Similarly, in Mauro v. Secretary of Air Force, 01980455 (1998), the Commission found the complainant's withdrawal of her complaint was voidable at her option where the agency investigator told her the complaint could be consolidated with other complaints at the hearing stage. The Commission found its administrative judge did not err in dismissing the complaint at the hearing stage because he was not bound by the unauthorized representations of an investigator who exceeded his authority.

When an EEO officer tells a complainant that his chances of prevailing are "slim to none," and the complainant withdraws his complaint, this is not a voluntary withdrawal. Roberts v. Postmaster General, 01982738 (2000).

Where there is a question as to whether the complainant intends to withdraw a complaint, the agency must seek clarification before closing complainant's file. In Maynard v. Postmaster General, 01970741 (1998), the agency sent the complainant a notice of final interview, a form to withdraw her informal complaint, and a formal complaint form. The complainant returned the form withdrawing her formal complaint, along with the formal complaint form. The Commission held that it was error for the agency to close the complaint without determining the complainant's intentions. The Commission made no comment on the agency's practice of sending both a withdrawal and formal complaint form with the notice of final interview. Because the notice of final interview terminates precomplaint counseling, there is no need for the complainant to "withdraw" the informal complaint. Including both forms with the notice of final interview can serve only to confuse a complainant.
