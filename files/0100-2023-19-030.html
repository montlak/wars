3. Intent of Third Parties

Generally, it is the motivation of the agency official taking the disputed action that is the focus of an EEO case. However, when discipline is based on complaints generated by third parties, it may be appropriate to examine the motivation of those initiating the complaints. In Stokes v. Secretary of Defense, Defense Logistics Agency, 01933573 (1994), the Commission examined the motives of Marine Corps officers who had made the complaints that led to employee discipline:

We therefore turn to appellant's race claim. The gravamen of his argument regarding race is that the PDO acted on the basis of the complaints from the Marine officers, that those complaints were based on racial animus, and that the PDO's action is therefore discriminatory.

In this regard, we note that the Commission has held that it may examine the allegedly biased motivations of third parties whose recommendations may have tainted a disciplinary action. Otis Parker v. USPS, EEOC Appeal No. 01851777 (September 22, 1987) (executing official's discipline tainted by complaint of supervisor with "history of blatant racial animus" that was known to agency officials); also see Allan Bradley v. USPS, EEOC Appeal No. 01912371 (October 16, 1991) ("longstanding racial animosity and threats by the [supervisor]. . .establish a prima facie case"). In the matter herein, the genesis of appellant's allegations of discrimination arises from persons not within the agency's authority. Nevertheless, we conclude that since the PDO acted on the basis of complaints from the Marine officers, their motivations may be examined.

To maintain his claim of discrimination, appellant must initially show that the complaints made by the Marine officers, on which the PDO's actions were based, were generated by racial animus. We conclude that the record evidence and hearing testimony are insufficient to support a finding that the complaints of the Marine officers were racially motivated and that the AJ erred in so finding. There is no evidence that the Director's letter or the complaints from 01, 02, and 03 were racially motivated. The Director's letter to the PDO sought cooperation from appellant and a reversal in what he called a "propensity for non-cooperation." Also, the reports of the three officers contain complaints of non-cooperation and, while reflecting a lack of goodwill toward appellant, do not demonstrate a racial basis or animosity based on appellant's race.

Further, the testimony of appellant's witnesses do not show a pattern of "blatant racial animus" or "longstanding racial animosity and threats" on the part of the three Marine officers. See Parker and Bradley, supra. Only E3 testified to two incidents of racially based conduct, but overall he thought that the complaints made about appellant were not based upon racial animus and related to his direction of yard operations. Also, while E2 stated that he observed the Director asserting himself to appellant, he did not indicate any demonstration of a racial motivation.

Finally, the AJ considered the Director's actions indicative of a "lack of respect" toward appellant. Such comments and actions may well have sprung from the hostile relationship created by appellant's strict observance of agency work rules and his high standards for acceptance of materials, but they are not sufficient to support a finding of racial bias or racial animus.
