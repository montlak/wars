UNFAIR LABOR PRACTICES

The part of the law that governs labor relations activity in the federal service, the Federal Service Labor-Management Relations Statute, outlines the rights and responsibilities of agencies, unions, and employees. If a party fails to live up to one of its obligations under the statute, or violates another party's rights under the statute, it is said to have committed an unfair labor practice, or ULP.

In almost every instance where a ULP is found, the offending party is ordered to cease and desist, and to post a notice to all employees acknowledging its violation. There will also often be a status quo ante remedy, returning the parties to the situation that existed before the violation. ULPs are serious business, and can be very expensive to resolve, so they should be avoided whenever possible.

On the agency side, ULPs may be caused by the actions of top management, agency headquarters, attorneys, and other practitioners who have daily contact with the union, as well as the managers who encounter bargaining unit employees every day on the shop or office floor. The most common ULPs by federal managers involve:

  1. 1. Unilateral changes in conditions of employment.
  2. 2. Careless statements that could be viewed as coercive or retaliatory.
  3. 3. Formal discussions and Weingarten meetings (covered in a separate section).

Unilateral changes in conditions of employment

Included among management's rights is the authority to make changes that are necessary to better accomplish its mission, or to use its resources more efficiently. For example, it may decide -- as agencies often do -- that a different organizational structure would work better.

On the other hand, unions have the right to represent employees in the establishment of personnel policies, practices, and conditions of employment that apply to represented employees. Consequently, even though management may have the legal right to make a change, it has an obligation to notify the union in advance and to engage in bargaining at the union's request.

Unfortunately, this fact is often overlooked or misunderstood by managers, resulting in many unnecessary ULP charges. In fact, failing to give notice of a change in conditions of employment is probably the most common ULP occurring at the working level.

Managers who commit ULPs by making unilateral changes generally do so for two reasons:

1. A failure to recognize a condition of employment. COEs come in both big and small packages and it's the small ones that get the manager in trouble. Everyone recognizes the big items such as telework and work schedules as important conditions of employment. But what about such things as seating assignments and the timing of breaks and lunches? These are issues that affect employees every day. Change those and you're changing conditions of employment.

2. A failure to recognize the union's institutional interests. "It's not a big deal; the employees won't care; heck, most of them want it changed." Versions of this statement have been uttered many times by managers who have been charged with a ULP. Sure, it might be a minor, even agreeable, change, but the law gives the union the right to be involved and to represent all employees, including those who might not support the change.

How to spot change situations

The key to avoiding unilateral changes in conditions of employment is in knowing how to correctly spot and deal with change situations. Here are three key questions to ask before making a workplace change:

  1. 1. Is this an actual change to a current rule, policy, or way of performing work, and not merely the exercise of an existing rule, policy, or method? Adopting a new way of assigning overtime work, for example, would be a change. Assigning an employee to work overtime under the current policy, however, would not.
  • • Note: Some union contracts specify that the union be notified in advance of mandatory overtime. It is always wise to check with your labor relations specialist.
  • • Note: Another consideration involves past practices. When management knowingly acquiesces in a practice for a substantial length of time, it becomes an established practice and may not be changed unilaterally. In this situation, a change may be in accordance with a stated policy and still violate a past practice.
  1. 2. Will the intended change affect or apply to bargaining unit workers? If it will apply to or have impact upon employees in a bargaining unit, their union will have the right to be involved. If the intended change will only affect persons not included in the bargaining unit, however, there is no obligation to inform or bargain with the union.
  2. 3. Will the intended change affect employees' conditions of employment in a significant enough way? Agencies do not need to negotiate over changes that don't have only a "de minimis" impact on conditions of employment. However, some surprisingly minor things have been deemed by the FLRA to be more than de minimis. If in doubt, it's usually best to assume the impact is sufficiently significant and notify the union before making a change.

If the answer to all three questions is "yes," then the union has a right to be notified of the change and must be given the opportunity to bargain.

Note: Statute provides that an agency may "take whatever actions may be necessary to carry out the agency mission during emergencies." This means that agencies have a lot of leeway during emergencies and generally aren't precluded from taking unilateral action. However, this right to act in emergencies isn't absolute, and there still may be situations when bargaining is required, including after the changes are implemented. If an agency needs to act quickly due to the circumstances of the emergency, the agency is strongly encouraged to begin communicating with the appropriate union representatives as soon as possible.

Careless statements

Most federal employees have the right to form, join, or assist a union -- or to not do so. The law also guarantees them the exercise of these rights free from coercion, restraint, or retaliation by the agency.

A manager's statement to an employee about union business or participation in a union could be considered coercive or discriminatory, even if that is not the manager's intent. Examples of such careless statements could be, "Mary, your work would be better and your appraisal higher if you didn't spend so much time on union business," or "John, the employees who move ahead are those who avoid creating waves with grievances."

These may be well-intentioned pieces of advice doled out to employees by a caring manager, but to the casual observer -- and probably to the employees receiving such words of wisdom -- they are threats to retaliate for the exercise of statutory rights. Statements such as these are more likely than not ULPs.

For this reason, it is important for managers to maintain their neutrality when discussing the union with employees.

Elections. This neutrality must especially be maintained when a union election is pending. At such times, the law requires strict neutrality, and managers can do no more than provide information about the election, encourage voting, and correct inaccurate statements made by any party.

A manager's opinions given while an election is pending might be viewed as coercive, so regardless of your personal feelings toward federal unions in general, or the particular union candidates in the election, you may not share these opinions with the workers who will do the electing.

When employees are still trying to make up their minds about whether to form a union, or which union to elect, the best course of action is to remind them of the government's policy: employees have a right to choose whether or not they want representation, and no action will be taken against them if they so choose.

Mistakes to avoid

Many avoidable labor disputes start because individual supervisors and managers make mistakes in dealing with union officials and labor agreements. Avoid these common errors:

Do not:

  • • Suggest to an employee that they discuss their gripes with you before going to the union. Employees have the right to engage in protected activity, such as filing grievances, without fear of retaliation. An employee who persistently gripes may see such a statement as a threat and take action against you.
  • • Express a cynical opinion when discussing the union with employees. A manager's statement to an employee about union business or participation in a union could be viewed as coercive or discriminatory, even if that is not the manager's intent.
  • • Tell a union officer that you believe their involvement with the union is the cause of their performance problem. To suggest that union work is the cause of a performance problem is to invite a ULP charge. The best strategy is to point out the performance problems, tell the officer of your expectations for improvement, and let them decide how to deal with the problem.

Ask FMG

Q. My employees want to change the procedure for requesting and approving annual leave at holiday time. If I agree to the employees' proposal, must I involve the union?

A. Yes. You are changing a condition of employment, namely leave approval procedures. The union represents all employees in the bargaining unit and has the right to negotiate over a change such as this. The fact that the employees want the change doesn't negate the union's statutory right.

Q. We have some new furniture and it will necessitate a change in employee seating assignments. We're not moving to another building or even another floor. Changes will be small. Must I negotiate over this?

A. Maybe. Agencies do not have to negotiate over a change that has only a "de minimis" impact. However, whether a change has a more than de minimis impact is in the eye of the beholder. Such things as the movement of an employee from a windowed cubicle to one without could be sufficient to trigger the agency's obligation to negotiate.

Q. I have an employee who drags the union into every controversy and threatens to file a grievance every time something makes them unhappy. Can I suggest that they discuss their gripes with me before going to the union?

A. That's a bad idea. Employees have the right to engage in protected activity without fear of retaliation. Filing grievances is one form of protected activity. If this employee is already prone to bringing the union into every dispute, they will likely see a statement such as this as a threat.

Q. My employee is a very dedicated union officer. However, they concentrate so heavily on their union duties that the quality of their work suffers. When I discuss their performance with them, can I mention that I believe their involvement with the union is the cause of the performance problem?

A. No. The employee has the right to be a union representative and you have the right to expect quality work. To suggest that their union work is the cause of the problem is to invite a ULP charge. The employee is an adult. Point out their performance problems. Tell the employee of your expectations for improvement, and let them decide how to deal with the problem.

Q. If my employees want to discuss something that is important to them -- such as lunch schedules or seating assignments -- and they ask to do so without union interference, can I honor that request?

A. No. The union was elected as the exclusive representative of employees. As such, it speaks for all bargaining unit members on conditions of employment, including such matters as seating assignments and work schedules. The union must be given the opportunity to participate in discussions about conditions of employment whether the employees it represents like it or not.

Q. In a regularly scheduled staff meeting, employees want to discuss the possibility of telecommuting. Does the union have the right to be there?

A. Yes, telecommuting is a general condition of employment. The union has the right to be notified of such a discussion with bargaining unit employees and to be present to offer its own views.

Q. I'm approached by one employee looking to change their work schedule. Do I need to involve the union in this discussion?

A. Probably not. If the matter involves only the conditions of employment of a single employee and does not extend to general schedule changes or otherwise impact other unit employees, it may be discussed with the employee.
