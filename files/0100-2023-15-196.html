2. Bench Decisions

The administrative judges of the Commission are empowered to enter so-called bench decisions in appropriate cases. Bennett v. Department of Army, 01871309 (1988). A bench decision merely means that the judge will make his or her decision following the close of the taking of evidence and the decision is orally entered onto the record of the hearing. When a bench decision issues, the administrative judge is not required to issue a separate written decision.

The EEOC now includes the following notice to the parties when it schedules the hearing:

The Administrative Judge is empowered to render a recommended decision from the bench in appropriate cases, by reading findings of fact and conclusions of law into the record at the end of the hearing.

Selection of cases for bench decision will depend on:

(a) The nature of the issues;

(b) The completeness of the evidence; and

(c) The theory of the complaint.

Prior to the hearing, after review of the issues and documentary evidence, the Administrative Judge will make an initial determination of the suitability of the case for a bench decision. The parties will be advised of the proposed bench decision and will have the opportunity to object to this procedure at the beginning of the hearing and prior to the rendering of a bench decision on the merits. Any objection made, as well as the Administrative Judge's ruling on the objection, will be entered on the record.

See also Judges' Handbook at 7-9.

In addition, the Judges' Handbook at 8-1 to 8-2 gives some guidance on the procedures to follow in cases involving bench decisions as well as the criteria to apply in determining if a case is appropriate for a bench decision:

Administrative Judges are encouraged to issue bench decisions where appropriate because they generally expedite the hearing process. When rendering a bench decision, the Administrative Judge must inform the parties that the announcement of the decision from the bench does not trigger the time limit within which an agency must take final action or a complainant may file an appeal. When the Administrative Judge issues a bench decision, the agency's obligation to take final action will not commence until the agency receives the transcribed decision, a copy of the hearing record, and the Order Entering Judgment.

When planning to issue a bench decision, Administrative Judges may find it helpful to prepare in advance of the hearing an outline of known issues and uncontested material evidence, standard language on legal principles involved, and other standard statements that would ordinarily be made when rendering a formal decision.

An Administrative Judge should consider the following when determining whether to issue a bench decision: the number of witnesses who testified; the number of material facts in dispute; the number of credibility determinations to be made; his/her familiarity with the record; and whether the outcome is clear based upon applicable legal standards. If an Administrative Judge determines that the case is appropriate for a bench decision, he/she should consider the possibility of announcing a short recess at the conclusion of the hearing to prepare for the issuance of a bench decision. In the event the bench decision is not delivered at the conclusion of the hearing, the decision can subsequently be announced to the parties and the court reporter in person or via telephone.

In order to challenge the issuance of a bench decision on the basis that the administrative judge did not consider all of the evidence, the complainant must point to specific evidence that he alleges was not considered. Bennett, supra.

Bench decisions, issued without the kind of thought that normally goes into written decisions, can prove embarrassing. In Schnur v. Secretary of Air Force, 01920384 (1992), the administrative judge dictated a bench decision into the record after the hearing closed and the parties left. That decision entered a recommended finding of discrimination on one issue. Later, after reviewing the decision with her supervisor, the decision was revised to recommend findings of no discrimination on all issues. Unfortunately for the administrative judge, a copy of the voided bench decision was sent to the complainant. The Commission found that, although issuing two conflicting recommended decisions was "unfortunate and irresponsible," it did not violate EEOC regulations. Id.

In some instances, an administrative judge will issue a bench decision anywhere from a few days to a few weeks after the hearing. Generally, this is done by having the representatives participate by teleconference. Nothing in the Commission's regulations prohibits such a procedure and the extra time gives the administrative judge a better opportunity to organize his or her thoughts prior to issuing a decision.
