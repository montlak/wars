E. HEARING STAGE

Complainants and witnesses who are federal employees are entitled to reasonable accommodation in order to permit them to participate in the hearing. Such accommodations are the responsibility of the employing agency and not the EEOC. In addition to holding the hearing in an accessible facility, some of the more common accommodations deal with readers and interpreters. For a full discussion, see Chapter 15 under the heading "Interpreters and Reading Assistants."

In one case, Memmer v. Environmental Protection Agency, 0120071285 (2007), the Commission found that the agency was required to accommodate the complainant by providing an audio version of the report of investigation and certain exhibits. On appeal, the Commission itself provided part of the record in audio form and ordered the agency to produce other parts in audio form, though there were also portions of the record that the Commission determined would not assist the complainant in preparing an appellate brief. The Commission, in Memmer, found:

The record indicates that while the case was before the AJ, complainant sought a reasonable accommodation during the hearing process through an alternate, human voice reading of the complaint file, including the Report of Investigation. In response, the agency contended that complainant could use the Closed Circuit television magnification system (CCTV) available at the agency's Regional Office in San Francisco, which complainant alleged was more than 450 miles from her home. Complainant's treating optometrist suggested that there were three (3) alternative audio formats containing a human voice which could accommodate complainant's need for access to the complaint file: (1) audio cassette tapes; (2) digital CD ROM; or (3) digital multimedia format. Complainant informed the AJ that when she tried to read the EEO Counselor's Report, it took her almost two (2) days using her reading telescope. Complainant also stated that reading many documents using the telescope is painful to her. The AJ issued a Show Cause Order on May 18, 2004, requiring the agency to, submit detailed arguments for the reasons it should not be ordered to provide the requested accommodation. Ultimately, the AJ ordered the agency to have the complaint file converted to either audio cassette tapes, a digital CD Rom or a digital multimedia format, in a version containing a human voice. The agency complied with the AJ's order.

In response to complainant's request for documents in an audible format so she could prepare her appeal brief, on May 11, 2007, the Commission issued an Interim Order. The Interim Order indicated that the record did not establish why the agency, having already been placed on notice about complainant's need for an accommodation, did not provide the hearing record to her in a manner similar to that ordered by the AJ with regard to the complaint file. As such, the Commission found that the evidence was insufficient to allow a determination as to whether complainant should be reasonably accommodated here by having the agency provide her with audio versions of the hearing record, hearing transcripts and the AJ's decision.

Accordingly, the Commission ordered the parties to submit briefs on the issue of whether the Commission should order the agency to provide complainant with the accommodation of alternative, audio versions of the hearing transcript, hearing record and AJ's bench decision. The present appeal was held in abeyance for supplementation of the record by the parties.

On June 13, 2007, the agency responded to the Commission's Interim Order. In its response, the agency maintained that it had reasonably accommodated complainant's visual impairment and further accommodations are not reasonable or necessary. Notwithstanding its position, however, the agency requested an additional sixty (60) days in order to initiate and complete procurement actions to: (1) reproduce an audio version of the AJ's decision; (2) make or obtain copies of the audio tapes of the court reporters such that reproduction of an audio version of the hearing transcript for the dates of September 11-15 2006, as well as September 18, 2006, can occur as requested by complainant; and (3) reproduce audio versions of any agency exhibits which were admitted into evidence by the AJ during the hearing. The agency did not agree to reproduce any of complainant's exhibits which may be included in the hearing record, as the agency assumed that complainant was aware of the information contained in the exhibits she submitted. As such, the agency alleged that it should not be directed to reproduce complainant's hearing exhibits as a reasonable accommodation. In addition, the agency noted that the AJ ordered the agency to convert many of complainant's pre-hearing exhibits into an audio format. Finally, the agency stated that it learned that the Office of Federal Operations (OFO) would provide complainant with a CD-ROM copy of the hearing record in the instant case. The agency noted that this would allow complainant to print out and magnify all documents in the hearing record, including the AJ's decision and all hearing transcripts in the case to a level beyond a 14 or 16 point font.

In response, complainant alleged, among other things, that: (1) the agency's counsel is a "co-conspirator" of the agency's interference with her reasonable accommodation requests which are necessary for her to prosecute her discrimination complaint; and (2) the agency's counsel "fabricated pretext" in order to conceal the agency's "unlawful strategy" of interfering with her reasonable accommodations.

After reviewing the submissions of the parties in response to our Interim Order, we concur with the submission of the agency. The agency has agreed, in its response brief, to provide within sixty (60) days, many of the documents in audio form which complainant will need to prepare an appellate brief. Specifically, the agency has agreed to initiate and procure audio versions of the AJ's decision, the hearing transcript and any agency exhibit which was admitted as evidence by the AJ at the hearing which occurred in September of 2006. We concur with the agency's contention that it is not reasonable to require the agency to reproduce in an audio version, complainant's hearing exhibits. We find this to be the case especially when, prior to the hearing, the AJ ordered the agency to convert many of complainant's pre-hearing documents into an audio format which she found to be relevant to the issues being adjudicated. We find that requiring the agency to reproduce into an audio format these same documents would not be necessary for complainant to produce an appellate brief which addresses the issues covered by the AJ in her decision.
