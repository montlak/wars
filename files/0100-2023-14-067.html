R. AGENCY POLICIES AND PROCEDURES

In some cases it may be necessary to refer to an agency's standard policies and procedures in order to fully interpret a settlement agreement. For example, in Royster v. Postmaster General, 05950843 (1997), the employee and the agency entered into an agreement that provided for his transfer to another facility. Subsequent to the transfer, the agency changed the employee's seniority date to the date of his transfer. In reviewing the employee's allegation of breach, the Commission held that where a settlement agreement is silent on the issue of seniority, the agency's offer to transfer the appellant may have implied that seniority would be a consideration. In order to resolve the issue, the Commission held it needed to look to the agency's standard seniority policies. The Commission in Royster stated:

In considering appellant's seniority allegation, it is not apparent whether a breach has occurred. In so finding, we are aware that the settlement agreement contains no provision relating to the question of seniority. At the same time, however, we find that the agency's agreement to transfer appellant to a similar position at the same grade level carries with it certain implicit terms and conditions. One such condition, potentially, is that appellant would retain his seniority. In this regard, if the agency's policy is to allow employees who transfer within craft to another facility to retain their seniority, then the agency's failure to allow appellant to retain his seniority would arguably constitute a breach of the settlement agreement. However, because there is insufficient evidence in the record to determine what the agency's policy is regarding the retention of seniority in a transfer situation, it is not possible to determine whether a breach occurred.

See also "Seniority," above.

However, when a settlement agreement provides a complainant with a benefit that is contrary to the requirements of internal agency policy, the settlement agreement is controlling. In Vasquez v. Postmaster General, 01A23605 (2003), the Commission held:

The record indicates that complainant requested and was denied leave to observe Good Friday on March 14, 2002. Good Friday that year fell on March 29, 2002. The agency admits to denying complainant's leave request, but argues it is not in breach of the settlement agreement. Specifically, the agency argues that an internal procedure was put in place mandating that anyone requesting leave for Good Friday must do so by March 8, 2002. The agency argues that complainant's March 14, 2002 requested leave for Good Friday was beyond the internal policy requiring requests by March 8, 2002. Here, the settlement agreement between the parties trumps such an internal policy. Complainant, in compliance with the settlement agreement, requested leave for Good Friday at least one week in advance of the holiday. Yet, the agency denied his request. We find the agency in breach of the settlement agreement. Complainant appears to want specific enforcement of the agreement and we find that to be the appropriate remedy (rather than reinstatement of the settled matter). We remand the matter back to the agency for specific enforcement of the agreement (including reimbursement for any monies lost by complainant due to being denied leave for Good Friday) and we remind the agency that the terms of the settlement agreement are valid for future requests for leave for future Good Friday holidays.

However, a complainant will generally be held to knowledge of rules and regulations relative to a settlement agreement provision. For example, in LePree-Williams v. Secretary of State, 01A24154 (2004), the complainant entered into an agreement that abolished her position and made her eligible for a discontinued service annuity. Because the complainant was not age 55, she was subject to a reduced annuity. In finding that the agency had not breached the agreement, the Commission in Lepree-Williams explained:

Provision (3c) states that the agency will immediately move to abolish the Supervisory Passport Specialist GS-13 (pilot program) position, allowing complainant to qualify for an immediate annuity based on discontinued service. The provision further stated that this means that complainant will be able to retire as of the first day of the pay period following implementation of the June 11, 2002 addendum, with full vested privileges, at the level of GS-13, Step 5 employee. The record contains a copy of an undated letter wherein the letter notified complainant that her Supervisory Passport Specialist position would be abolished effective May 1, 2002. The letter also stated that complainant apparently qualified for discontinued retirement, but because she was retiring before age 55, her basic life annuity rate would be reduced by 2% for each year that she is under age 55. The record also contains a letter signed by complainant dated July 26, 2002, wherein complainant informed the agency that she was opting to pursue discontinued service. At the bottom of the letter, complainant stated that she was signing the document "under protest as I have been informed by my attorney that the State Dept. is insisting on penalizing my retirement annuity, in contravention of the settlement agreement all parties signed in Dec. of 2001." Complainant further noted that she was reserving her right to appeal this action and file suit for breach of contract. On appeal, complainant objects to the agency's reduction of her retirement annuity annually until she reaches age 55. Complainant maintains that "there is no mention of a reduction in the value of the annuity and there were never any negotiations with regard to that subject."

5 C.F.R. 831.114(2)(k) provides that an employee who separates from service voluntarily after completing 25 years of service, or becoming age 50 and completing 20 years of service is entitled to an annuity if on the date of separation the employee is serving in a position covered by a voluntary early retirement offer. If an employee is under age 55, the annuity is computed using a voluntary optional retirement annuity calculation based on total creditable years and months of service and average "high-3" salary. The "high-3" average pay is the highest average basic pay the employee earned during any 3 consecutive years of service. The annuity is then reduced at the rate of two percent for each year (or by 1/6th of one percent for each full month) that a complainant is under age 55. See OPM Restructuring Information Handbook, Section 43A2.1-1 (December 1998). Complainant contends that the terms of the settlement agreement provide that she will receive an unreduced annuity amount; however, we find that the terms of the agreement only provide complainant with what she can receive as an employee of her age, with the years of service she possessed at the time of retirement. The agreement's terms provide complainant with "full vested privileges," which does not mean that complainant would be granted an annuity beyond or in contravention of the applicable OPM laws and regulations, only that which she is entitled to upon "discontinued service." As such, we find that the agency did not breach the agreement by refusing to give complainant an unreduced annuity amount.
