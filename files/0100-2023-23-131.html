2. Conditional Offers of Employment

As some of the cases discussed above suggest, there is a mid-stage between preemployment and postappointment for purposes of medical inquiries and examinations. Once an agency makes a "conditional offer of employment," it can engage in medical inquiries and examinations even if they are not job-related as long as it makes the same inquiries of all those offered conditional employment in the same category of position. Enforcement Guidance on Disability-Related Inquiries, at pp. 3-4. A conditional offer of employment is one that if the applicant fulfills the conditions, he or she is given the position. The offer can be conditioned on more than the medical inquiries or examinations, such as eligibility for a security clearance.

In Ewing v. Postmaster General, 0120065172 (2008), the Commission explained the distinction between preemployment inquiries and inquiries made after a conditional offer of employment:

Under the Rehabilitation Act, "an employer may ask disability-related questions and require medical examinations of an applicant only after the applicant has been given a conditional job offer." Enforcement Guidance on Pre-employment Disability-Related Questions and Medical Examinations, EEOC Notice No. 915.002, at 2 (October 10, 1995); see also 29 C.F.R. § 1630.13(a); Nolan v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01975113 (November 1, 2000); McKinley v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01933326 (September 8, 1994); request for reconsideration denied, EEOC Request No. 05950027 (December 8, 1995) (finding that the agency violated the Rehabilitation Act when agency officials made prohibited pre-employment inquiries, but concluding that the complainant would not have been selected absent the prohibited inquiries, and ordering the agency to correct its pre-employment process).

After an applicant is given a conditional job offer, but before he starts work, an employer may make disability-related inquiries and conduct medical examinations, regardless of whether they are related to the job, as long as it does so for all entering employees in the same job category. Enforcement Guidance on Disability-Related Inquiries and Medical Examinations of Employees Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), EEOC Notice No. 915.002, at 3-4 (July 26, 2000). However, if an individual is screened out because of a disability, the employer must show that the exclusionary criterion is job-related and consistent with business necessity. Id.

The Commission could not determine if the agency in Ewing had made an improper medical inquiry because the record was unclear as to when the complainant was given a conditional offer of employment and the precise parameters of the medical inquiries. The Commission did, however, suggest that the complainant might succeed on a retaliation claim even though the agency contended that the reason for withdrawing the offer was that the complainant gave inaccurate and untruthful responses to the medical inquiry.

In Ewing, the complainant applied for a position as a custodian, was determined to be eligible and then advised to report for a medical examination. On the basis of that examination, the agency determined that the complainant was not medically suitable because of a knee condition. The complainant initiated an EEO complaint, which was settled in an agreement that called for the complainant to submit a medical report from his personal physician, the agency to review the report and consider the complainant for the position, and for the complainant to pursue getting a reasonable accommodation. As part of the accommodation request, the complainant released his Department of Veterans Affairs medical records to the agency. The agency made an offer of employment but then withdrew it contending that the complainant had untruthfully stated in his preemployment application that he had not had psychiatric treatment in the past ten years.

The Commission, in remanding Ewing for further development of the record on the timing of the offer and medical inquiry, noted:

[C]omplainant may have established that the agency was motivated by retaliatory reasons when it rescinded the job offer. The record reflects that M1 sent an e-mail on February 21, 2006, stating "I don't want this guy on the rolls, but he has filed [EEO complaints] already and I'm sure another one is on the way. I want to be sure there isn't more info out there I don't know about." Even though this e-mail was sent out after M1 rescinded the job offer, we note that this statement may be direct evidence of M1's retaliatory motive. Therefore, it may be appropriate to analyze the claim under a mixed-motive analysis since the agency provided that the offer was initially rescinded due to his failure to be forthcoming with regard to his prior medical history. However, the legitimacy of the reasons proffered by the agency for rescinding the job offer is not determinable given the gaps in the record involving complainant's medical documentation. First, a determination needs to be made as to whether the documents relied upon by M1 to rescind the offer were legally obtained by the agency. Therefore, since the legality of the agency's request for medical information is intertwined with the question of whether complainant provided misleading information regarding his medical condition to the agency, we remand the claim of whether the agency discriminated and retaliated against complainant when it rescinded the job offer.

When the complainant was given a conditional job offer to switch from a rural carrier to city carrier position, the Commission found that subjecting the complainant to a physical examination was proper where there was no evidence to suggest that other employees making the switch were not also subjected to the examination. Szafran v. Postmaster General, 0120070161 (2008). The Commission further found that a follow-up inquiry to the complainant's physician as to whether she could safely perform the essential functions of the position when she disclosed she was four months pregnant was also proper.

In Hadnot v. Secretary of Army, 0120080049 (2009), the Commission rejected a challenge to a physical examination with an EKG component after a conditional offer of employment. The complainant, who had applied for a job as a surveying technician, did not allege an ADA violation, but age discrimination because the form indicated that EKG was for persons 40 years of age or older. The Commission deferred to the credibility findings of the administrative judge that the coordinator of occupational health explained that all new employees were required to take the EKG regardless of age and that the notation on the form related only to the annual health examination for all employees.

That an improper medical inquiry comes from a third party acting does not exonerate an agency from liability where the third party is acting on behalf of the agency. In Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120131850 (2013), the complainant was a certified class member in the Hill v. Postmaster General, 0720080004 (2008), recon. denied, 0520080632 (2008), class action complaint. That action resulted in settlement where the agency agreed not to make preemployment medical inquiries. Subsequent to that, the complainant was contacted by a private contractor screening applicants for agency positions and was asked to complete a medical questionnaire. The Commission, in Complainant found:

In the instant case, we find that the Agency, through the actions of its contractor, has breached the terms of the settlement agreement prohibiting it from requesting medical information prior to extending a conditional offer of employment to applicants.' It is undisputed that, in the selection process at issue, the Third Party requested medical information from Complainant. The settlement agreement provided that, "[c]onsistent with the Rehabilitation Act, the Postal Service has been and will continue to be committed to not seeking medical information, in the form of documents or otherwise, before extending a conditional offer." Therefore, we determine that the Third Party's medical form constitutes a violation of the explicit terms of the settlement agreement. Whether or not the Agency was aware of the actions of its agent contractor, or whether or not Complainant actually supplied the requested information, have no relevance to determining if a breach of the agreement occurred. Therefore, we reverse the Agency's finding of no breach of the settlement agreement.
