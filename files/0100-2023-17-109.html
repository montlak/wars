A. PROCEDURES AND TIME LIMITS

Either party may request reconsideration. MD-110, at 9-19, sets forth the procedures that complainants and agencies requesting reconsideration must follow:

1. Requests for reconsideration and any supporting statement or brief must be filed with Office of Federal Operations (OFO) within thirty (30) days of receipt of a decision of the Commission or within twenty (20) days of receipt of another party's timely request for reconsideration. OFO will accept statements or briefs in support of the request by fax transmittal, provided they are no more than ten (10) pages long. The request must also include proof of service on the opposing party.

2. The requesting party must submit any supporting documents or brief at the time the request is filed. The burden is on the requesting party to make a substantial showing that its request meets one of the two prerequisites for a granting of reconsideration.

3. The opposing party shall have 20 days from the date of service in which to submit any brief or statement in opposition. Such brief or statement must be served on the requesting party and proof of service must be included with the submission to OFO. OFO will accept briefs or statements in opposition to the request by fax transmittal, provided they are no more than 10 pages long.

4. Failure to provide a proof of service or to submit comments within the prescribed time frame will result in the denial of the request.

If it is any consolation to appellants, agencies also on occasion miss filing deadlines. In William v. USPS, 05850053 (1986), the agency's request for reconsideration was denied as untimely where it was filed 33 days after receipt of the Commission's final decision. See also Complainant v. Secretary of Defense, 0520150063 (2014) (receipt date of agency attorney not controlling where agency received decision earlier); Oates v. Postmaster General, 05970702 (1998) (a time limit begins upon receipt in the agency's mail room and not receipt by a particular office).

As with all other mailings, the Commission presumes that OFO decisions are received by the parties within five days of mailing absent evidence to the contrary. If an agency or a complainant contends that the decision from which reconsideration is sought was not received within five days, evidence of actual receipt must be presented. For example, in Johnson v. Attorney General, 05A30094 (2003), the Commission found that the agency's request for reconsideration was untimely where the agency failed to submit evidence in support of its contention that is was received outside of the five-day period. "[T]he agency's unsupported contention is insufficient to overcome the presumption of timely receipt." Id.

However, in Green v. Postmaster General, 05A60234 (2005), rejected an agency attempt to extend the deadline for a request for reconsideration based on its argument the presumptive receipt would have been on a weekend and, therefore, the counting of the 30-day period should not begin until the following Monday. The Commission in Green explained:

The agency presented objections to the EEOC's decision in EEOC Request No. 05A51166. As a result, the EEOC decided to re-docket the case as EEOC Request No. 05A60234. In a letter dated October 7, 2005, the agency argues that the dismissal was based on a presumption that the agency received the decision in five days. However, the agency argues that because the fifth day was a Sunday, under 29 C.F.R. § 1614.604(d), the agency's time period did not begin to run until the following Monday, July 25, 2005. Therefore, the agency argues that it had until August 24, 2005 to timely file.

Our regulation provides that when computing time periods for filing "the first day counted shall be the day after the event from which the time period begins to run and the last day of the period shall be included, unless it falls on a Saturday, Sunday or Federal holiday, in which case the period shall be extended to include the next business day." 29 C.F.R. § 1614.604(d). This regulation, titled "Filing and computation of time," applies to filing deadlines, therefore, if the agency's filing deadline for the request for reconsideration was a Saturday, Sunday or a Federal holiday, the agency would have been timely had it filed on the proceeding Monday. In this case, however, the agency argues that the presumption of receipt should also be extended if its receipt date is a Saturday, Sunday or a Federal holiday. The agency proffers no case law in support of its argument. We note that the last date of the presumption of receipt is not the same as a filing deadline. As such, we decline to extend the provisions of 29 C.F.R. § 1614.604(d) to include extending the presumption of receipt. Furthermore, it is stated in the original decision EEOC Appeal No. 01A44490, that requests for reconsideration must be filed with the Commission "within thirty (30) days of receipt of this decision." Therefore, given the presumption that the agency received the decision on July 24, 2005, the agency should have filed its request for reconsideration by August 23, 2005. The Commission affirms the decision in Green v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05A51166.

Parties should note the difference between filing a notice of appeal with OFO and a request to reopen. On appeal, the supporting brief is not due until either 20 or 30 days after the filing of the notice of appeal, depending on whether the agency or the complainant files the appeal. However, supporting argument must accompany the request to reopen. See Osolinik v. Secretary of Interior, 05870101 (1988). In light of an untimely request for reopening, the Commission has authority, sua sponte, to reopen when the case has significant public policy implications. Id.

Even though the reconsideration regulation does not have a provision for requesting an extension of time to file arguments on the request to reopen, the Commission has the authority to grant such requests. Cornelius v. Postmaster General, 05900234 (1990). The decision to grant a request for reconsideration is purely discretionary.

The Commission will grant extensions in extenuating circumstances. However, the moving party must not only show that extenuating circumstances entitle them to an extension of the time period, the party must also show that the extension sought is justified. In Johnson v. Secretary of Treasury, 05900145 (1990), the agency requested an extension where the 30-day deadline fell just 10 days after the San Francisco earthquake. The agency filed its request for an extension with its request to reopen, some 11 days after the deadline. The Commission denied the extension, noting that while the earthquake presented extenuating circumstances, the agency had presented no evidence to demonstrate the length of disruption caused by the earthquake. Of particular significance to the Commission was the fact that the agency had 10 days after the earthquake to file a request for an extension but chose to wait until after the deadline had passed.

"[W]hen a complainant claims that a physical condition prevented him or her from meeting a particular filing deadline, we have held that the complainant must [be] so incapacitated by the condition as to render him or her physically unable to make a timely filing." Verdeal v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 05990243 (2001). In Verdeal, the Commission applied this general rule to the complainant's request for reconsideration, declining to extend the time limit where the evidence was insufficient to show she was incapacitated during the time period for filing the request.
