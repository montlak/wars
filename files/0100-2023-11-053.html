III. CONTINUING VIOLATIONS

In many instances, an employee does not contact a counselor within 45 days of an alleged discriminatory act. When the complainant finally does contact a counselor, he or she advances the argument that the agency has discriminated against him or her in the past and continues to discriminate against the employee in the present. In other words, the employee alleges the agency is engaged in a "continuing violation" of the antidiscrimination statutes. Although complaints alleging continuing violations are accepted or dismissed at the formal complaint stage of the EEO process, the concept of a continuing violation is discussed here because the ultimate issue is one of timeliness, as it relates to contact with an EEO counselor.

The theory of continuing violations brings two competing goals directly into conflict. First, there is the goal of deciding discrimination cases on the merits instead of dismissing them on procedural grounds. The EEOC is charged with eradicating discrimination in the workplace. As a result, public policy favors an examination of the merits of discrimination claims. But, there also is the goal of giving employers reasonable notice of discrimination claims. Employers should not be forced to defend against stale charges of discrimination. Not only is the defense of discrimination claims impaired by time, but there is a substantial public interest in bringing finality to employment decisions. The statute of limitations--in this instance the 45-day time limit for counselor contact--is imposed to protect agencies from stale discrimination charges. The doctrines of tolling, waiver and estoppel are devices for protecting an individual complainant's interests, as well as the public interest in eradicating discrimination by acknowledging there are circumstances where the time limit should be set aside.

The continuing violation theory is an attempt to balance these two competing goals. It is a balancing test that the EEOC has not always successfully passed. The Seventh Circuit, reviewing the EEOC's position on continuing violations in a hostile environment sexual harassment case, pronounced, "[w]e do not think the Commission's position can be right. It gives too little weight to the social interest in the prompt filing especially of suits charging employment discrimination." Galloway v. General Motors Serv. Parts Oper., 78 F.3d 1164, 1166 (7th Cir. 1996) (emphasis in oriGINAl). Indeed, it seems at times in the Commission's decisions on continuing violations that the EEOC has forgotten why the doctrine of continuing violations came into being. At other times, its decisions have shown confusion over what the doctrine actually is. Perhaps all of this confusion is due to the fact that the label of "continuing violations" is really misleading. While the doctrine gives consideration to whether the acts of the employer are interrelated, the doctrine is primarily one dealing with the issue of timeliness.

One of the problems with the Commission's approach to continuing violations is that it has used the theory to encompass at least three distinct types of claims. The Commission has applied the theory to: 1) so-called "serial violations" which are claims involving discrete personnel actions when those claims are "interrelated," e.g., linked by a common supervisor or analogous theme; 2) claims of hostile environment harassment; and 3) claims involving an employment policy or practice that results in a "recurring" or "systemic" violation. The problem with that approach is that it does not account for the fact that each type of claim raises different timeliness considerations. The problem is further compounded in that, regardless of the type of claim being asserted, the complainant may reasonably be unaware that a particular action is discriminatory at the time it occurs. See the discussion found earlier in this chapter under the subheading "Uncertainty of Law."

Fortunately, in National Railroad Passenger Corp. v. Morgan, 122 S. Ct. 2061 (2002), the Supreme Court significantly clarified its views on the continuing violation theory as it applies to discrete personnel actions and hostile environment claims. Following up on Morgan, the Supreme Court then weighed in on so-called "serial" or "recurring" violations in Ledbetter v. Goodyear Tire & Rubber Co., 127 S. Ct. 2162 (2007). Finally, Congress weighed in and passed the Lilly Ledbetter Fair Pay Act of 2009 reversing the Ledbetter decision and reviving the notion of serial violations. We begin the discussion of continuing violations with Morgan because it presents a succinct statement of the current law.
