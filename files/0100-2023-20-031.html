1. Court Orders and Consent Decrees

In prior years, the Commission followed the lead of the Supreme Court and other courts in holding that employment decisions made under a consent decree that took otherwise prohibited factors into account were not subject to challenges under Title VII. The Commission later reversed that position and held that such challenges must be considered on a case-by-case basis. In Anderson v. Secretary of the Air Force, 01902182 (1990), the Commission explained the reversal:

We note that, prior to the Supreme Court's recent decision in Martin v. Wilks, 109 S. Ct. 2180 (1989) (footnote omitted), the Commission interpreted Title VII to denote that "actions taken pursuant to the direction of a court order cannot give rise to liability under Title VII." See EEOC Regulation 29 CFR Section 1608.8. However, the Court in Wilks clearly established that such charges must be resolved on the merits of each particular case. Therefore, challenges to court orders no longer can automatically give rise to no-cause findings pursuant to 29 CFR Section 1608.8.[5]

[5] Pursuant to the Commission Policy Guidance on Wilks (October 17, 1989), we are unable to ascertain specifically what standard would be applied to determine whether the employment practices under a court ordered-consent decree violate Title VII. While the Court stated that challenges to court-ordered consent decrees by non-parties must go forward, it did not specifically state that the court order or consent decree is irrelevant to that suit; nor did it alter the substantive standards for "reverse discrimination" suits challenging affirmative action plans, whether adopted under a consent decree or otherwise. See Steelworkers v. Weber, 443 U.S. 193, 208 (1979); Johnson v. Transportation Agency, Santa Clara County, 480 U.S. 616, 627 (1987); Local 28 of Sheet Metal Workers v. EEOC, 106 S. Ct. 3019, 3050 (1986); United States v. Paradise, 107 S. Ct. 1053, 1067 (1987). Under the aforementioned cases, the plan agreed to by the parties would not violate Title VII if it is narrowly tailored, does not involve rigid quotas, and does not unnecessarily infringe upon the rights and expectations of other employees. See EEOC Regulation 29 CFR 1608.4(c)(2) (plan should "be tailored to solve the problems identified [by the employer]. . .and to ensure that employment systems operate fairly in the future, while avoiding unnecessary restrictions on opportunities for the workforce as a "whole" and "should be maintained only so long as is necessary to achieve these objectives").

The Commission did not address the standards to be applied to such a challenge to affirmative action plans, since under the facts of Anderson it found there was no discrimination.

In the 1991 Civil Rights Act, Congress specifically enumerated the conditions under which an affirmative action plan implemented as the result of a court order or consent decree can be challenged. See Chapter 13 under the subheading "Civil Rights Act of 1991."
