1. Work Discussions

In a recurring scenario, employees are called into various "discussions" with supervisors about alleged disciplinary or performance problems. From the agency's perspective, these discussions are intended to make the employee aware of conduct or performance problems and to put the employee on notice that further misconduct or unacceptable performance will result in the agency taking some action. From the employee's perspective, these can simply be one more form of harassment.

The general rule is that unless documented, such discussions do not constitute a sufficient injury in fact to make an employee aggrieved under Title VII. See Roberson v. Postmaster General, 01A11395 (2001); White v. Postmaster General, 01921696 (1992). However, the employee has a right to expect that the agency will not use such discussions as the basis of any disciplinary action or to enhance any future disciplinary action. Any such use of the discussions will provide grounds for an EEO complaint.

The Commission has treated the maintenance of supervisor notes with a great deal of inconsistency. For example, the Commission has held that once the comments are documented, even if they have not been used as the basis for any discipline, the employee is aggrieved within the meaning of Title VII. See Royster v. Postmaster General, 01A55512 (2006); Moncur v. Postmaster General, 01922448 (1992). The Commission also has held that once an adverse notation is made in an employee's record, the removal of that notation does not render the case moot. See Nicosia-Wagner v. Secretary of Treasury, 01953234 (1996). But see Arndt v. Secretary of Navy, 05930583 (1993) (holding the fact that a supervisor keeps notes of a performance counseling session does not aggrieve an employee, if the notes do not make it into any official agency record). Similarly, the Commission found the agency properly dismissed a claim that an oral counseling about tardiness aggrieved the complainant when it issued a "Confirmation of Conference" memorandum. Cook v. Attorney General, 01A34890 (2004). The burden is on the agency to demonstrate that no record of the discussion has been kept. See Fucci v. Postmaster General, 01956625 (1996) (agency presented no evidence that the discussion was not cited in the complainant's personnel file).

In Aspaas v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 0120063875 (2006), the Commission set forth its current policy on written work discussions, also noting that the policy broadens when the allegation is that a written work discussion is based on reprisal:

In general, this Commission has held that official discussions alone do not render an employee aggrieved. See Miranda v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05920308 (June 11, 1992); Devine v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05910269 (April 4, 1991). However, we have distinguished those cases in which a complainant alleges that the discussion was recorded or otherwise entered into their personnel records. Here, complainant has claimed that the February 13, 2006 Record of Discussion was used as the basis for the recommended 5-day suspension of June 16, 2006. Although the agency argues that there is no evidence in the record that the February 13, 2006 Record of Discussion will be used as a basis for future disciplinary actions, the June 16, 2006 Record of Discussion clearly shows that it was considered in his supervisor's proposal to suspend complainant. In such cases, the Commission has found complainants aggrieved. See Schneider v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01951787 (June 28, 1995); Pruneda v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01951126 (May 25, 1995); Avagliano v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01950056 (March 22, 1995). Moreover, under the Commission's broad view of reprisal, a complainant need not demonstrate that he is "aggrieved" under the Diaz standard to state a legal claim, but only that the alleged adverse treatment was based upon a retaliatory motive and is reasonably likely to deter the charging party or others from engaging in protected activity. See Lindsey v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05980410 (November. 4, 1999) (citing EEOC Compliance Manual, No. 915.003 (May 20, 1998)). Because complainant has alleged facts sufficient to demonstrate that he is aggrieved under these standards, claim 3 states a claim under our regulations.

In Nosar v. Postmaster General, 01921450 (1992), the Commission found that the agency had not made an offer of full relief when it proposed to resolve a complaint over a memorandum of alleged work deficiencies by stating that the memorandum "is not being directed as a form of reprisal/harassment, discipline, and no official record was made of such." The Commission noted the memorandum itself was a record of the alleged deficiencies and an offer of full relief would have to include assurances that the memorandum was removed from all agency records, including any personal records being kept by the supervisor.

When a letter of warning is reduced to a nondisciplinary work discussion, the employee is no longer aggrieved, but if the employee alleges that issuance of the letter is part of a pattern of harassment, the agency must include a review of the administrative act of issuing the letter as part of its investigation of the underlying harassment allegations. See Phillip v. Postmaster General, 01921189 (1992). However, in Bujalski v. Postmaster General, 01944948 (1994), the Commission found an employee was aggrieved where the agency reduced a letter of warning to a work discussion. The employee was "aggrieved" when the letter of warning was issued and the subsequent retraction did not change that fact. The Commission further found that notations of the letter of warning remained in a grievance file. But the Commission also has held that when a letter of warning is reduced to a work discussion and the letter is expunged from the agency files, the case is moot unless the complainant can specifically explain how she continues to be injured. See Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120151651 (2015) (letter of warning reduced to work discussion rendered complaint moot); Douglas v. Postmaster General, 05950053 (1996); see also Farmer v. Postmaster General, 01A32484 (2003) (complainant no longer aggrieved when letter of warning was reduced to work discussion in resolution of grievance).

Reducing a letter of warning to a work discussion does not render a complaint moot where the complainant has specifically requested compensatory damages. Gonzalez v. Postmaster General, 01A60282 (2006). In those cases, the agency must give the complainant an opportunity to demonstrate the harm accrued, as well as a causal connection to the warning, and then pay any proven compensatory damages in order to render the complaint moot.

Apparently, it also makes no difference if the event is referred to as a reprimand instead of a work discussion. Although oral reprimands generally are understood to be disciplinary, in Galle v. Postmaster General, 01953873 (1996), the Commission held:

Upon review, we find that appellant's complaint failed to state a claim. The Commission has held that a verbal reprimand unaccompanied by any concrete action does not render an employee aggrieved. See Banks v. Department of Health and Human Services, EEOC Request No. 05940481 (February 16, 1995) (the supervisor threw a file down on appellant's desk and told her in a loud voice to handle the matter herself). Furthermore, there is no evidence in the record that appellant sustained any personal harm or injury affecting his employment as a result of the alleged comment, i.e., instructing him to quit talking too loud. Based on the foregoing, we find that appellant is not aggrieved; thus, the complaint fails to state a claim.

Similarly, in Walton v. Postmaster General, 01A03592 (2002), the Commission held that a "predisciplinary" discussion did not aggrieve the employee.

In Simmons v. Postmaster General, 01955205 (1996), the Commission held that an employee is not aggrieved when a letter of removal is reduced to a work discussion. The Commission found there was no evidence the agency maintained any record of the discussion. The decision in Simmons reflects a basic misunderstanding and misapplication of the mootness doctrine on the part of the Commission. It is hard to believe that all harm occasioned by a letter of removal is eradicated simply by rescinding the letter. This is particularly true in any case where compensatory damages are available. The Commission simply ignores reality by holding that an employee is not aggrieved by such an action even where the agency later reconsiders its oriGINAl action and rescinds it.

A suspension reduced to an official work discussion through the grievance procedure does not normally aggrieve an employee but does when the employee alleges the suspension was based on retaliation. Rezek v. Postmaster General, 0120123453 (2013).

The Commission has consistently held that counseling sessions, unless documented, do not constitute a sufficient injury in fact to make an employee aggrieved under Title VII. Most of the cases also indicate there is a requirement that the documentation be kept in some agency records that could be accessed by others. However, in Fair v. Secretary of Navy, 01961886 (1996), the agency specifically argued that the memo prepared by the alleged discriminating official was informal and not part of any official record. On appeal the agency also argued that the fact that the official made a memo for his own records was not a "concrete action" sufficient to render the appellant aggrieved. The Commission disagreed with the agency's position, and held that the memo, no matter how informal and even though kept in the supervisor's personal records, was sufficient to render the complainant aggrieved.

Although a work discussion does not generally aggrieve an employee, if it is alleged that the work discussion is part of a pattern of harassment then such a claim, if true, would in fact injure the employee. Ramirez v. Postmaster General, 01955488 (1996).

In Davis v. Postmaster General, 01A11429 (2001), the Commission found that an allegation that the employee was given a work discussion because he complained about unsafe work conditions did state a claim for relief. "As this claim relates to complainant's refusal to work in an area that he deemed unsafe, the claim is related to the terms, conditions, or privileges of his employment." Id.
