14. Termination of Employment

Termination subsequent to the filing of an EEO complaint over work conditions does not render the complaint moot until the termination proceeding is finally adjudicated. This includes the exhaustion of all judicial remedies. Thornhill v. Secretary of Army, 0191342 (1992). In Thornhill, the complainant contended that the agency discriminated against him when it refused to extend his term of overseas duty. Subsequent to filing his EEO complaint over the nonextension, the complainant in Thornhill was terminated:

Turning to the non-extension allegation, although the termination decision has been affirmed by the MSPB and this Commission, it is not a final decision because appellant retains the right to appeal the Commission's affirmance in the federal court system. See Thornhill, supra. Furthermore, if a federal judge were to find the termination invalid, appellant could be reinstated, and, upon a finding of discriminatory non-extension, appellant could be granted an extension of his overseas tour. Accordingly, the alleged violation could recur, and the termination does not constitute an interim event which has eradicated the effects of the alleged violation....

A complaint is not mooted by the approval of a disability retirement application where the outcome of the case could affect the appellant's rate of pay at time of retirement and, thus, increase his annuity. See Powderly v. Secretary of Navy, 01972111 (1997) (retirement does not moot a complaint over classification because it could effect retirement benefits); see also Brenda v. Department of Navy, 01853317 (1987). Where a complainant is claiming that he was constructively discharged, dismissal for mootness following his disability retirement may be improper. See Monk v. Secretary of Defense, 01956671 (1996). The Commission had previously held that voluntary disability retirement constitutes an interim event that completely eliminates the possibility that the alleged discrimination would occur in the future. See Vitti v. Secretary of Labor, 01952390 (1995). However, when a complainant alleges, as in Monk, that the discrimination against him "led to his condition" and that he applied for disability retirement because of the agency's failure to accommodate his disability, he is making a constructive discharge claim which prevents his complaint from being moot.

Similarly, a complaint is not rendered moot because the complainant left the agency and that component of the agency has closed. In Birdsong v. Secretary of Navy, 05970026 (1998), the Commission noted that, although there was no reasonable likelihood the violation would recur, the effects of the past alleged discrimination had not been eradicated.

In Patrick v. Postmaster General, 0120130441 (2013), the Commission found that a complaint of disability discrimination and retaliation was not moot when the agency removed the complainant after he accepted a disability retirement. The Patrick case contains a good explanation of the standards of harm for a retaliation claim as well as the doctrine of mootness:

Complainant alleged that he was subjected to disability discrimination and unlawful reprisal when he was issued a letter of removal on April 16, 2012. The Agency has reasoned that he was not "aggrieved" because the removal became effective after Complainant had retired from the Agency on April 7, 2012. We conclude that the Agency erred in dismissing the complaint.

Among other things, Complainant has alleged that Agency management knew he was retiring, yet continued to pursue the removal action in retaliation for his prior EEO complaints that had been filed against the same responsible management officials. Complainant asserted that the removal was pursued in order to "hinder" his retirement and to subject him to "needless mental and emotional anguish, pain and suffering." The anti-retaliation provisions of the employment discrimination statutes seek to prevent an employer from interfering with an employee's efforts to secure or advance enforcement of the statutes' basic guarantees, and are not limited to actions affecting employment terms and conditions. Burlington Northern & Santa Fe Railroad Co. v. White, 548 U.S. _____, 126 S. Ct. 2405 (2006). To state a viable claim of retaliation, Complainant must allege that: he was subjected to an action which a reasonable employee would have found materially adverse, and 2) the action could dissuade a reasonable employee from making or supporting a charge of discrimination. Id. While trivial harms would not satisfy the initial prong of this inquiry, the significance of the act of alleged retaliation will often depend upon the particular circumstances. See also EEOC Compliance Manual, No. 915.003 (May 20, 1998) (any adverse treatment that is based upon a retaliatory motive and is reasonably likely to deter the charging party or others from engaging in protected activity states a claim). Clearly, a removal action is of the type of adverse actions that states a viable retaliation claim.

The Agency has asserted that Complainant's retirement rendered his complaint about his removal moot. The regulation set forth at 29 C.F.R. § 1614.107(a)(5) provides for the dismissal of a complaint when the issues raised therein are moot. To determine whether the issues raised in Complainant's complaint are moot, the fact finder must ascertain whether (1) it can be said with assurance that there is no reasonable expectation that the alleged violation will recur; and (2) interim relief or events have completely and irrevocably eradicated the effects of the alleged discrimination. See County of Los Angeles v. Davis, 440 U.S. 625, 631 (1979): Kuo v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05970343 (July 10, 1998). When such circumstances exist, no relief is available and no need for a determination of the rights of the parties is presented. In this case, we find that Complainant's retirement did not render his removal claim moot because it cannot be said that the retirement irrevocably eradicated the effects of the alleged discrimination. Regardless of Complainant's employment status at the time the removal went into effect, it still remains as part of his official employment history with the Agency. There is no suggestion in the record that Complainant's retirement resulted in the rescission of the removal decision and expungement from his personnel records. Disability retirement is not always a permanent status, and if Complainant recovers sufficiently to return to work, the termination could serve as a real impediment to his rehire. This harm alone is sufficient to find that his removal claim is not moot.

A complaint of harassment is not rendered moot by the complainant's subsequent termination where the termination is still subject to challenge and reversal. Cancel v. Secretary of Commerce, 059007532 (1990). The conditions that gave rise to the complaint are subject to recurrence in the event of the complainant's reinstatement.
