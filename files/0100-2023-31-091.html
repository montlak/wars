Q. LAYMAN REPRESENTATIVES

Fees for services rendered to represent an employee in EEO proceedings may only be awarded to attorneys and other qualified legal personnel. See 29 CFR 1614.501(e)(1)(iii); see also Jenkins v. Secretary of Air Force, supra. An employee is not entitled to a fee award for hours spent representing himself. See Gonzalez v. USPS, 01841050 (1985).

As noted above, fees may be awarded for the services of a non-attorney representative when that non-attorney is working under the direction of an attorney. When a fee claim is based on a claim that the work was performed under the direction of an attorney, the Commission will examine the underlying relationship between the attorney and the non-attorney to determine if fees are warranted. In Young v. Secretary of Treasury, 01962021 (1998), the Commission explained:

However, where the complainant is not in fact represented by an attorney, no such fee award can be made for the services of the non-attorney representative even if the latter occasionally consulted an attorney in connection with such representation. Therefore, in order to decide whether an appellant is entitled to an attorney's fee award for the services rendered by a non-attorney, we must look to the nature and substance of the relationship between the non-attorney representative and the attorney under whose supervision the former claims to have rendered professional services to determine whether the appellant was in fact represented by an attorney and is, therefore, entitled to an award for attorney's fees.

...

The record shows that the October 1992 designation of representative, submitted by the appellant to the agency, listed only the non-attorney representative's name and address. The attorney, under whose purported supervision the non-attorney representative claims to have rendered the services here, has a different address. Never before the submission of the attorney's fee request by the non-attorney representative in November 1995 did the name of the attorney appear in the record of this case. Only the non-attorney representative appeared at the administrative hearing in this matter, and all submissions on the appellant's behalf throughout the administrative proceedings were made by him. The appellant's lay representative's appearances and submissions identify him as being associated with a consulting firm in Labor and Industrial Relations bearing his last name and address, which differ from the last name and address of the attorney under whose purported supervision he claims to have rendered the services in this matter. Lastly, not a single minute of the claimed 74 hours of professional time is for the supervisory legal representation allegedly provided by the attorney in the prosecution of this complaint.
