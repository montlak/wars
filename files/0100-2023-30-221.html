1. Sanctions for Noncompliance

In Hashimoto v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, 0420110011 (2012), the Commission noted that it can sanction an agency for failing to comply with a relief order. In Hashimoto, however, one wonders what an agency has to do to actually incur sanctions. Over a span of some seven years and three enforcement petitions, the agency still had not provided the complainant or the Commission adequate documentation to determine if it had correctly issued a back pay award. The Commission in Hashimoto stated:

The Commission notes that the Agency was ordered to reimburse Petitioner over seven years ago for compensation he would have earned had he not been subjected to discrimination. Petitioner has now filed three Petitions for Enforcement seeking compliance with respect to the same order of back pay. In all instances, the Agency has failed to provide the Commission with adequate documentation to support its assertion that it has complied with the order. In response to the instant petition, the Agency submitted no evidence of its efforts to comply with the Commission's order. As a result, the Commission finds that the Agency has again failed to comply with the Commission's order in EEOC Appeal No. 01A24642.

The Commission has the inherent power to control and prevent abuse of its orders, processes and procedures. The Commission can and will impose sanctions for Agency malfeasance. See Byrd v. Department of Agric., EEOC Petition No. 04980004 (Dec. 12, 1997); Turner v. Dep't of the Interior, EEOC Petition No. 04980037 (Aug. 5, 1999). To that end, should the Agency fail to comply with the actions mandated by the Order set forth below, the Commission is issued a "Show Cause" notice to the Agency to appear in person before the Commission to explain the Agency's continued failure to comply is authorized. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.503(e). Continued failure to comply also may result in this case being certified to the Office of Special Counsel for enforcement action. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.503(f).
