1. Record of Impairment

The term disability includes individuals who have a record of "such an impairment'--i.e., a record of an impairment that, when active, substantially limited a major life activity. 42 USC 12102(1)(B). The Commission defines the term "has a record of such impairment" in its proposed regulations at 29 CFR 1630.2(k):

In general.--(1) An individual has a record of a disability if the individual has a history of, or has been misclassified as having, a mental or physical impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities.

(2) Broad construction. Whether an individual has a record of an impairment that substantially limited a major life activity shall be construed broadly to the maximum extent permitted by the ADA and should not demand extensive analysis. An individual will be considered to have a record of a disability if the individual has a history of an impairment that substantially limited one or more major life activities when compared to most people in the general population, or was misclassified as having had such an impairment. In determining whether an impairment substantially limited a major life activity, the principles articulated in paragraph (j) of this section apply.

(3) Reasonable accommodation. An individual with a record of a substantially limiting impairment may be entitled, absent undue hardship, to a reasonable accommodation if needed and related to the past disability. For example, an employee with an impairment that previously limited, but no longer substantially limits, a major life activity may need leave or a schedule change to permit him or her to attend follow-up or "monitoring" appointments with a health care provider.

Of particular note is subsection (3), which actually requires reasonable accommodation of an individual who has a record of disability, if it is not an undue hardship. Examples in the regulation include such things as leave or a schedule change for health care appointments--measures that might assist the individual in keeping the impairment at a level where it does not again become a substantially limiting condition.

An employee who demonstrates that the agency officials responsible for taking an action were aware of her previous hospitalization for schizophrenia and that during her hospitalization she was unable to work or care for herself demonstrated a record of impairment and is a person with a disability. Kharoud v. Postmaster General, 01911941 (1991).

While the ADAAA did not directly change the second prong of the definition of a disability, it did include a provision that can potentially impact individuals with a record of a disability and an agency's obligations to that individual. At 42 USC 12102(4)(C), the ADAAA provides that an "impairment that is episodic or in remission is a disability if it would substantially limit a major life activity when active." The result is that individuals that previously qualified only under the second prong of the definition of a disability, may now be included within the first prong of having a disability. For example, an individual who has a past history of cancer that is currently in remission, would now be included under the first prong of the definition of a disability if, when active, the cancer substantially limited a major life activity.

The Commission considered the complainant's service-connected disability rating, agency knowledge and that complainant's disability was obvious to find a "record of disability" in Kyle S. v. Secretary of Agriculture, 2019005694 (2021):

Here, the record shows that both parties stipulated that Complainant was hired as a disabled veteran through the Veteran's Recruitment Act hiring authority, with 90% service-connected disability rating. While this, in and of itself, is not conclusive as to the matter of whether Complainant was an individual with a disability, for the purposes here, it is highly suggestive, especially when viewed in light of the other evidence of record. The Occupational Safety and Health Specialist/Veterans Special Emphasis Program Manager, OSHA Specialist, attested that management was aware that Complainant was disabled because Complainant discussed his condition with them. Complainant attested that he discussed his medical condition with management, including telling them he had neurological problems. OSHA Specialist testified that Complainant's disability was obvious at work, including his limp, tremors, symptoms of eye-twitching, lips quivering, and difficulty speaking. Complainant's therapist and his wife also attested that Complainant visibly shook and had tremors. Therefore, we find the record is sufficient to support the AJ's finding that Complainant was an individual with a disability, as defined above.

A qualified individual with a disability is an "individual with a disability who satisfies the requisite skill, experience, education, and other job-related requirements of the employment position such individual holds or desires, and who, with or without reasonable accommodation, can perform the essential functions of such position. 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(m). The physical demands section in the position description for Engineering Technician, GS-9 state: "The work requires some physical exertion such as long periods of standing, walking, and carrying moderately heavy items over rough, uneven, mountainous, or rocky surfaces." Complainant's supervisor, Supervisor1, stated that Complainant performed some maintenance himself but also worked with others to perform maintenance for Agency facilities. He added, initially, Complainant worked inside as he got acclimated to the position and gained access to Agency systems. Supervisor1 stated, during the winter months, a lot of work is done inside rather than in the field compared to the summer when all facilities are accessible and there is more work done in the field. Supervisor1 stated that Complainant seemed to perform his duties well. We find the record sufficiently supports the AJ's finding that Complainant was a qualified individual with a disability. Accordingly, we find the Agency had an obligation to provide reasonable accommodation absent a showing of undue hardship.
