6. Failure to Attend Hearing

When the appellant fails to appear for a scheduled hearing without good cause, the proper procedure is for the administrative judge to remand the case to the agency for a final decision based on the investigative record. It is error for the agency to dismiss the complaint for failure to prosecute. It must issue a final decision on the merits. Dodson v. Environmental Protection Agency, 01832732 (1984).

The administrative judge does not err in canceling the hearing, despite a proffer by appellant that he was sick, where the complainant failed to appear for the scheduled hearing. Luzier v. Secretary of Navy, 01923265 (1992). In Luzier, the administrative judge waited over an hour and a half, then contacted appellant's workplace and was advised that his shift had not started.

But in McDaniel v. Postmaster General, 01960897 (1997), the Commission found that the agency erred by issuing a final decision without a hearing where the administrative judge had remanded the case to the agency to locate the complainant and then resubmit the case for a hearing:

Notification was sent to the parties that a hearing before an Administrative Judge was scheduled for June 30, 1994; however, by letter dated June 27, 1994, appellant's representative informed the AJ that appellant's whereabouts were unknown, and, therefore, requested that the AJ remand the case back to the agency in order to locate appellant and prepare him for the hearing. Noting that appellant may not have been able to act on the notice of the hearing nor meet with his representative to prepare for the hearing, the AJ remanded the case back to the agency and ordered the agency to allow the representative a reasonable length of time to locate appellant. The AJ further ordered the representative to contact the agency's EEO office after locating appellant so that the case could be resubmitted for a hearing.

On December 6, 1994, the agency issued a final decision on appellant's complaint without submitting the case back to the AJ for a hearing. The agency noted that because the representative failed to locate and prepare appellant for the scheduled hearing on June 30, 1994, the final decision was issued on the merits of the complaint.

On appeal, appellant argues that he was wrongfully denied a hearing. Appellant asserts through his representative that the agency erred in issuing a final decision without affording him the opportunity of an administrative hearing. Appellant's representative points to the AJ's order instructing the agency to resubmit the case back to the AJ's hearing docket after the representative located appellant. Appellant further asserts that he complied with the AJ's order by requesting, on November 28, 1994, that his case be resubmitted to the AJ for a hearing.

The Commission finds that the agency erred in issuing a decision on the merits of appellant's case without providing him with the opportunity for a hearing. See 29 CFR 1614.109(a) (stating in part that when a complainant requests a hearing, the agency shall request the Commission to appoint an administrative judge to conduct a hearing). The record clearly reflects that by letter dated November 28, 1994, appellant requested that his case be resubmitted to the AJ for a hearing. We find, under the particular facts of this case, that the agency did not comply with the AJ's order allowing appellant an opportunity to again request a hearing. Thus, we VACATE the agency's final decision and REMAND this case back to the agency so that appellant may be afforded an administrative hearing. The agency is directed to take the actions ORDERED below.

Note that the Commission specifically limited the McDaniel decision to the facts of that particular case. Presumably, the Commission was referring to the order of the administrative judge instructing the agency to give the complainant an opportunity to submit a request for a hearing if located.

But the Commission vacated the agency decision on the merits and ordered a hearing for the complainant, despite the fact that the complainant in Cohens v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01972495 (1998), had not appeared for his first scheduled hearing:

On appeal, appellant requests that the agency's final decision be vacated and he be afforded a hearing on his complaint. He averred that he did not appear at the hearing because he did not know it was occurring. He represented that had he known about the hearing he would have attended. Appellant noted that the AJ referred to a certified mail return receipt which she said indicated that appellant had notice of the hearing. However, appellant stated that the address on the receipt was a former residence, and "David," the person whose name appears in the signature block of the return receipt is unknown to him. Appellant asserted that he had informed his attorney of his new address when he moved and relied upon the attorney to convey this information to all relevant parties.

Appellant further asserted that, despite his former attorney's representations to the contrary, the attorney never informed him of the hearing date. Appellant provided documentary evidence that he filed a charge against his former attorney over this matter with the Florida Bar. The Sixth Judicial Circuit Grievance Committee found probable cause for violations of Rule 4-1.1 (Competence), Rule 4-1.3 (Diligence) and Rule 4-14(a)(b) (Communications) of the Rules of Professional Conduct. Thereafter, the Florida Supreme Court instituted disciplinary proceedings against the attorney and, by order dated May 7, 1998, appellant's former attorney was suspended from the practice of law in the state of Florida for a period of three years for his mishandling of appellant's EEO case.

In light of this evidence presented by appellant, and in the interests of justice, the Commission is persuaded that it would be appropriate to vacate the agency's final decision and provide appellant with the opportunity for a hearing on his complaint as requested. Accordingly, the agency's decision is VACATED and the case is REMANDED to the agency, pursuant to the following Order, for a hearing.

Where the complainant is represented by a nonattorney lay person, the hearing notice must be served on the complainant as well as the representative. In Hittle v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120080588 (2013), the Commission set aside an order by the administrative canceling the hearing and remanding the case for a final agency decision where the complainant failed to appear for the hearing. The Commission in Hittle explained:

Upon review, we find that the AJ abused her discretion in dismissing Complainant's request for a hearing when Complainant and B-1 failed to appear on the scheduled date of the hearing, because there is no evidence that Complainant received copies of official correspondence sent to his non-legal representative that indicated the hearing date.

EEOC regulation 29 C.F.R. § 1614.605(d) provides that "all official correspondence shall be" transmitted to the complainant's representative, with copies to the complainant. When a complainant is properly notified of the hearing date, but fails to appear for the hearing due to either his own negligence or the negligence of his non-legal representative, the Commission has generally found that negligence does not constitute good cause for failing to appear at a hearing, and has upheld AJ dismissals of hearing requests as appropriate sanctions for such misconduct.'

See, e.g., Riley v. Tennessee Valley Authority, EEOC Appeal No. 01840358 (Jan. 28, 1985) (dismissing a hearing request when the complainant was properly notified of the hearing, but failed to appear because he "misread the letter"); Fricks v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01A10042 (June 29, 2001) (dismissing a hearing request when the AJ informed the complainant of the hearing date, but the complainant failed to appear because of "simple negligence"); see also Kovalick v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 0120082910 (Oct. 26, 2010) (dismissing a hearing request for the complainant's failure to respond to discovery request, when the complainant's non-legal representative did not adequately represent complainant, but the complainant received adequate notice about the discovery request).

But when a complainant is not sent copies of all official correspondence mailed to his non-legal representative, we have not construed the complainant to have constructive notice of the official correspondence.' Here, the record indicates that B-1 received official correspondence from the Agency and AJ indicating the hearing date. But the record does not contain sufficient evidence showing that Complainant personally received copies of those official correspondences. Therefore, we find that Complainant did not have notice of the date of the hearing, and this lack of notice constitutes good cause for his personal failure to appear at the hearing. Under the circumstances, we find that the AJ's sanction against Complainant, the dismissal of his request for a hearing, to have been inappropriate.

In Henry v. Postmaster General, 01A11242 (2002), the Commission held that it was not an abuse of discretion for the administrative judge to continue with the hearing in the absence of the complainant and her representative where they knew two days before the hearing of a scheduling conflict that would require they leave the hearing early, but did not advise the judge of that conflict until the morning of the hearing.

Complainants are not the only ones who fail to attend hearings. In Viramontes v. Postmaster General, 01980929 (1998), the agency failed to make arrangements for the hearing, appear at the hearing or present evidence after its motion for a continuance was denied. The Commission upheld the imposition of sanctions on the agency, which included the judge's determination that, had a hearing been held, the complainant would have established a prima facie case of discrimination and, because the agency presented no evidence, it failed to rebut the prima facie case.

Cancelation of the hearing and remand to the agency for a decision on the record is a common sanction for complainant misconduct. For a full discussion, see Chapter 8 under the heading "Sanctions Against Complainants."
