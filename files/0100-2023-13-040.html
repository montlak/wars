C. EX PARTE INVESTIGATIONS

It is improper for an agency to conduct an ex parte supplemental investigation after receiving a recommended decision with a finding of discrimination. Terrell v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, 01961030 (1996). As the Commission explained in Terrell:

As an initial matter, we find, for several reasons, that it was clearly improper for the agency to conduct an ex parte supplemental investigation after it had received the recommended decision. In so finding, we initially note that the regulations contained at 29 CFR Part 1614 contain no provision which permits the agency to conduct a supplemental investigation after it has received the AJ's recommended decision. What the regulations do state is that an agency has 180 days from the filing of a complaint to complete its investigation of that complaint. 29 CFR § 1614.105(f). In this regard, we note that the agency's decision to conduct its supplemental investigation occurred more than two years after appellant had filed his complaint, and was clearly beyond the 180-day time limit.

Furthermore, we find that the agency's decision to conduct the supplemental investigation clearly violated appellant's right to due process. In so finding, we note that one of the purposes of a hearing is to afford a complainant the opportunity to cross-examine witnesses and controvert evidence gathered during the investigation. Although appellant was afforded this opportunity with regard to the initial investigation, he was afforded no such opportunity with regard to the supplemental investigation. In this regard, it is apparent to us that the agency, by not informing appellant about the investigation until after he had appealed the FAD, intentionally deprived him of the opportunity to challenge this evidence. Additionally, the content of the supplemental investigation strongly suggests that it was nothing more than a self-serving attempt on the part of the agency to cure some of the deficiencies cited by the AJ in finding discrimination. For all of these reasons, we shall not consider any of the evidence obtained during the supplemental investigation.

Under the 1999 amendments, an agency no longer receives a recommended decision. However, the Terrell case remains viable for the proposition that an agency may not conduct an ex parte investigation between receiving a finding of discrimination from the administrative judge and issuing a final action advising the complainant of whether or not it will fully implement the decision.

An agency may conduct a supplemental investigation after the report of investigation issues. However, it must give the complainant an opportunity to respond to evidence collected in the supplemental investigation. In Keller v. Postmaster General, 01964879 (1998), the Commission remanded the case with instructions to provide the supplemental investigation to the complainant, give the complainant an opportunity to respond, and issue a new notice of right to hearing.
