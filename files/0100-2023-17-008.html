A. TIME LIMITS

Under 29 CFR 1614.402, complainants have 30 days from receipt of the final agency action, whether it takes the form of a final agency order or final decision, to file an appeal:

(a) Appeals described in 1614.401(a) and (c) must be filed within 30 days of receipt of the dismissal, final action or decision. . . . Where a complainant has notified the EEO Director of alleged noncompliance with a settlement agreement in accordance with 1614.504, the complainant may file an appeal 35 days after service of the allegations of noncompliance, but no later than 30 days after receipt of an agency's determination.

(b) If the complainant is represented by an attorney of record, then the 30-day time period provided in paragraph (a) of this section within which to appeal shall be calculated from the receipt of the required document by the attorney. In all other instances, the time within which to appeal shall be calculated from the receipt of the required document by the complainant.

The agency bears the burden of proving delivery of the final agency decision or action. As the Commission explained in Couch v. Secretary of Energy, 0120131136 (2013):

EEOC regulations require that appeals to the Commission be filed within 30 days of receipt of the Agency's final decision. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.402(a). Appeals that are filed beyond 30 days of receipt of the Agency's final decision shall be dismissed by the Commission as untimely. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.403(c). We note that "[a]n Agency always bears the burden of obtaining sufficient information to support a reasoned determination as to timeliness." Guy v. Department of Energy, EEOC Request No. 05930703 (January 4, 1994) (quoting Williams v. Department of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05920506 (August 25, 1992)).

The Agency asserted that the appeal should be dismissed as untimely. The Agency stated that Complainant received its final decision on August 9, 2012, and was informed that he must file an appeal within 30 days of receipt of the final decision. The Agency contends that as a result, Complainant's November 6, 2012 appeal is untimely filed.

We find that the record contains insufficient evidence to establish that Complainant received the final agency decision on August 9, 2012. The Agency provided a FedEx tracking slip that only contained a city and state of delivery, along with a note that the package was left at the front door. This generalized reference to a city and state, without any further details about the specific address of delivery, is insufficient evidence to establish when Complainant received the final agency decision. See Robertson v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 0120114064 (March 28, 2013) (A U.S. Postal Service "track and confirm" print out which only includes the city and state of delivery and not the address is not sufficient to establish the date that Complainant's representative received the Agency's final decision); Talarico v. Dep't of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 0120122550 (Oct. 15, 2012) (A FedEx tracking print out that only contains the city and state of delivery without any further details of the address is insufficient evidence to establish that Complainant received the Notice). As a result, we find that the Agency did not establish that Complainant's appeal is untimely.

The Commission adopted a presumption that if evidence of actual receipt is lacking, the final decision will be deemed to have been received five days after the date of the decision. See Harbin v. Department of Health and Human Services, 01830500 (1983). This presumption was adopted via Commission regulation 29 CFR 1613.240(a) and carried forward into Part 1614. See 29 CFR 1614.604(b). In order to rebut this presumption, the appellant must submit a written statement justifying noncompliance with the time limit for filing an appeal. See Hester v. USPS, 01830994 (1983). Merely filing a written statement will not toll the statute of limitations if that statement does not satisfy the requirements of the regulation. See Lamar v. Department of Air Force, 01831061 (1983). The agency must inform an employee of appeal rights in its final decision. See 29 CFR 1614.110.

The notice of appeal need not be a lengthy document. All that is required is that the Commission be notified of the intent to appeal and identify the decision being appealed. The notice of appeal is not required to state the grounds to be asserted on appeal. All of that can be addressed in the supporting brief. The EEOC has an appeals form which agencies are required to provide with the final agency decision.

Given the minimal requirements of the notice of appeal, timeliness should not be a problem. Still, it is. For example, in In re Prohaska, 05870245 (1987), the Commission waived the time limit where the employee's representative filed a request for an extension of time within the time period for filing a notice of appeal. The representative then filed the actual notice of appeal outside of the time period, but within the time requested in his extension. All of this was unnecessary. The notice of appeal easily could have been prepared within the amount of time it takes to file a motion for an extension of time, if not less. If an extension of time is required to file the brief in support of appeal, then a separate request for the extension of that time period should be filed with the notice of appeal.

In the past, the Commission has been very erratic in its treatment of whether receipt by the appellant or his or her representative started the running of time periods. Fortunately, the Commission has now clarified its position and the answer depends on whether or not the representative is an attorney. Pursuant to 29 CFR 1614.402(b) the time period begins running upon receipt by the attorney when the complainant is represented by an attorney. If the complainant is not represented, or is represented by a nonattorney, then the time period is calculated from receipt by the complainant. Id. Service of all documents, however, must be made on both the complainant and his or her representative of record, whether or not the representative is an attorney. See 29 CFR 1614.402(b), 1614.605(d). See discussion of computation of time below.
