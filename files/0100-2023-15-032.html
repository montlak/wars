4. Prehearing Conference

When it is feasible, the administrative judge will schedule a prehearing conference to refine the issues for hearing, to discuss prehearing matters such as approval of witnesses and the burdens of proof, and to discuss the possibility of settlement. Since settlement will be a topic of discussion at the prehearing conference, most judges prefer the complainant to participate personally, even if represented by counsel. It is expected that the agency representative will come to the conference with authority to settle the case.

In the past, when it was not possible to schedule a prehearing conference in advance of the hearing, most administrative judges would hold a conference immediately prior to the hearing to discuss the possibility of settlement. If an agreement was reached and reduced to writing, the hearing was canceled. The Handbook, at 7-1, specifically provides that the prehearing conference must be held sufficiently in advance of the hearing to issue all appropriate rulings and reduce them to writing:

A pre-hearing conference is the opportunity for the Administrative Judge to discuss and rule upon any outstanding matters that must be resolved prior to hearing. These matters may include the following if they have not already been addressed: advising the parties of hearing procedures; explaining applicable burdens of proof; refining issues on which the hearing will be held; approving witnesses; determining whether complainant seeks compensatory damages; determining whether the hearing will be bifurcated between liability and relief; ruling on outstanding motions; ruling on the admissibility of exhibits; obtaining stipulations of fact and stipulations as to the authenticity of documents; exploring settlement options, including ADR; arranging for a court reporter; arranging for telephone or video testimony; and arranging for any special accommodations. The Administrative Judge may consider having the parties, jointly or individually, submit the order of witnesses, showing the expected length of direct and cross examination for each witness.

A pre-hearing conference may be held in person or by telephone call with notice to the parties. The Administrative Judge shall issue, sufficiently in advance of the hearing and in writing, all necessary rulings and explanations regarding hearing procedures.

The administrative judge is not required to schedule the prehearing conference on a day when the complainant is scheduled to work. The regulations only require that the complainant be on "official time." The administrative judge does not abuse her discretion in scheduling the prehearing conference on the complainant's day off, provided he is granted official time for attending. Emerald v. Postmaster General, 01923130 (1992).

Most prehearing conferences are, and always have been, held by telephone. Given that, it is surprising that the Commission did not have an opportunity until 1999 to rule that telephone conferences are permissible. In Zukowski v. Secretary of Defense, 01973081 (1999), the Commission rejected an argument that the judge should have held an "in-person" prehearing conference:

With respect to the "telephonic" versus "in-person" prehearing issue, the Commission finds that appellant does not have a right to an in-person prehearing conference. We find that the AJ did not err in conducting a telephonic prehearing conference. The AJ has the authority to determine to conduct a telephonic prehearing conference instead of an in-person conference when determining, as she did here, that an in-person prehearing conference was not necessary. Furthermore, appellant did not present any evidence that he was prejudiced as a result of not having an "in person" prehearing conference....

At the conclusion of the prehearing conference, the administrative judge is required to prepare a memorandum summarizing the matters discussed at the conference and setting forth any rulings. As an alternative, the administrative judge may direct one of the parties to prepare the prehearing conference memorandum and order. Judges' Handbook at 7-1. In complex cases, some administrative judges require the agency to provide a court reporter for the conference and the transcript serves as the prehearing memorandum.
