2. Acceptance or Rejection of Accommodation

The agency cannot force an employee to accept an accommodation when one has not been requested and is not needed. See Estate of Nason v. Postmaster General, 01A01563 (2001). Conversely, if the employee rejects a reasonable accommodation offered by the agency, that ends the agency's obligation to provide reasonable accommodation. See Scott K. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120130779 (2016) (agency met its obligation to reasonably accommodate complainant where it offered him a reassignment to a position that was the equivalent in grade level and salary to complainant's position, but was also within his medical restrictions, but complainant refused the reassignment); Contractor v. Secretary of Defense, 01970386 (1999).

In Estate of Nason, the Commission held:

EEOC Regulation 29 CFR 1630.9(d) provides that an employer or other covered entity may not compel a qualified individual with a disability to accept an accommodation, where that accommodation is neither requested nor needed by the individual. See Interpretive Guidance on Title I of the Americans With Disabilities Act, Appendix, to 29 CFR 1630.9(d). Here, the agency sought to make an accommodation for complainant by reassigning her to the Coquille Post Office. However, complainant was not seeking an accommodation. Rather, she believed that a stigma would attach to her because of the reassignment, she would lose seniority when she was reassigned to the Coquille Post Office, and she would no longer be considered as a candidate for Postmaster. Compounding this was complainant's belief that if she did not accept the reassignment she would be involuntarily terminated. In sum, because complainant did not seek an accommodation, and according to 29 CFR 1630.9(d), an employer may not compel a qualified individual with a disability to accept an accommodation, we find that the agency discriminated against complainant when it reassigned her to the Coquille Post Office.

When the agency offers an accommodation which is within the medical parameters of the employee's disability it is justified in terminating the employee if he refuses to report for duty on medical grounds. Filion v. Department of Navy, 03820077 (1983).

"Once an employer accommodates an employee's limitations, the employee has an obligation to work safely within the accommodation provided by the agency, and to work within their own limitations." Richardson v. Postmaster General, 01912009 (1991). It does not constitute a failure to accommodate if the employee exceeds accommodation limits, even unintentionally.

In Durr v. Secretary of Treasury, 0120080078 (2010), the agency was found to have denied accommodation where the complainant elected not to request the agency to reconsider its denial, but submitted an alternative request that the agency failed to act on. The complainant in Durr was diagnosed with prostate cancer and was required to undergo an extensive period of radiation therapy. As accommodations, he requested to take four hours of sick leave a day and work four hours a day at home for a two-month period. Despite providing extensive documentation and the recommendation of a Federal Occupational Health doctor to grant the accommodation, the agency denied it. The complainant informed the agency that he was not going to request reconsideration of the denial and also withdrew his permission for the agency to engage in direct contact with his physician. The complainant then requested four hours of sick leave and four hours of office work per day, although he did not specifically request "reasonable accommodation." The agency requested medical documentation, which the complainant declined to provide because he had already given it to the agency. The agency then charged the complainant with AWOL instead of granting sick leave.

The Commission, in Durr, had little difficulty in affirming the agency' own decision that it had denied the complainant reasonable accommodation when it rejected his initial request and finding that it had further denied accommodation when it rejected his second request and declared him AWOL. The Commission, in Durr, explained:

Initially, we note that the agency does not contest that complainant is a qualified individual with a disability, and found that it is liable for complainant's delay and ultimate denial of a reasonable accommodation to work from home. We affirm the agency's finding of discrimination on this claim.

However, the agency found that it is not liable for the denial of complainant's request to use sick leave and his subsequent AWOL charges because complainant did not request reconsideration of his reasonable accommodation request, he withdrew the authorization for the agency to contact his doctor, and he did not provide additional medical documentation. The agency also asserts that this claim should not be analyzed as a denial of a request for a reasonable accommodation, but should be treated as an allegation of disparate treatment.

We note that a reasonable accommodation means, "Modifications or adjustments that enable a covered entity's employee with a disability to enjoy equal benefits and privileges of employment as are enjoyed by its other similarly situated employees without disabilities." 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(o)(1)(iii). The request need not be in writing or use the magic words of "reasonable accommodation." The employee or the employee's representative need only inform the agency that he or she needs an adjustment or change at work for a reason related to a medical condition. See Triplett-Graham v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01A44720 (Feb. 24. 2006).

Here, it is clear in the record that complainant was continuing to request a reasonable accommodation when he asked to have four hours of sick leave each day due to his cancer treatments, even though he no longer used the words "reasonable accommodation." Complainant's choice not to request reconsideration of the initial reasonable accommodation denial did not absolve the agency of its duty under the Rehabilitation Act to accommodate complainant when he later requested sick leave for his cancer treatments. Further, we find that the medical documentation that complainant initially provided was sufficient, as it explained complainant's diagnosis, prognosis, side effects of the treatment, and an estimate that the treatments would last two years. Therefore, we find that the agency is also liable for failing to reasonably accommodate complainant by denying his requests for sick leave and charging him with AWOL. Because of our finding of additional discrimination, we shall order the agency to reconsider the amount of compensatory damages complainant should be paid.
