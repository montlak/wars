Federal Managers Guide 02/09/2023 Chapter 05: Demonstrating Professionalism Chapter 05: Demonstrating Professionalism, 02: Ethics

ETHICS

Ethical behavior by federal employees is vital, both to the public's confidence in government and to the accomplishment of your agency's mission. Ethics are not just nice to have but are essential to effective government.

As a federal manager, you are concerned with your own ethical behavior and that of your employees. Ethical conduct issues are governed by various laws and regulations and are often collectively referred to as the Standards of Ethical Conduct. You must be familiar with these standards, and make sure your employees are familiar with the points that may affect them.

As a manager, you are expected to be a model of ethical behavior, both in substance and appearance to encourage the employees you lead to behave themselves ethically.

Here we can only deal with general principles. Difficult individual ethical situations come up all the time. That's why your agency has a designated agency ethics officer. It's a good idea to find out who your DAEO is and how to make contact. HR can help.

It is always best to ask your DAEO for an opinion before you take an action involving an ethics issue and then to follow the advice you get.

Warning: The Standards of Ethical Conduct require you to do more than simply stay within the technical letter of laws and regulations. They require you to avoid even the appearance of impropriety in carrying out your official duties.

Standards of Ethical Conduct. These 14 principles identify the ethical conduct expected of every federal employee. These principles are based on the legal obligations of federal employees and failure to follow them can result in disciplinary or criminal penalties.

1. Public service is a public trust, requiring employees to place loyalty to the Constitution, the laws, and ethical principles above private gain.

2. Employees shall not hold financial interests that conflict with the conscientious performance of duty.

3. Employees shall not engage in financial transactions using nonpublic government information or allow the improper use of such information to further any private interest.

4. An employee shall not, except as permitted by the Standards of Ethical Conduct, solicit or accept any gift or other item of monetary value from any person or entity seeking official action from, doing business with, or conducting activities regulated by the employee's agency, or whose interests may be substantially affected by the performance or nonperformance of the employee's duties.

5. Employees shall put forth honest effort in the performance of their duties.

6. Employees shall not knowingly make unauthorized commitments or promises of any kind purporting to bind the government.

7. Employees shall not use public office for private gain.

8. Employees shall act impartially and not give preferential treatment to any private organization or individual.

9. Employees shall protect and conserve federal property and shall not use it for other than authorized activities.

10. Employees shall not engage in outside employment or activities, including seeking or negotiating for employment, which conflict with official government duties and responsibilities.

11. Employees shall disclose waste, fraud, abuse, and corruption to appropriate authorities.

12. Employees shall satisfy in good faith their obligations as citizens, including all financial obligations, especially those -- such as federal, state, or local taxes -- that are imposed by law.

13. Employees shall adhere to all laws and regulations that provide equal opportunity for all Americans regardless of race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age, or disability.

14. Employees shall endeavor to avoid any actions creating the appearance that they are violating the law or the ethical standards set forth in the Standards of Ethical Conduct. Whether particular circumstances create an appearance that the law or these standards have been violated shall be determined from the perspective of a reasonable person with knowledge of the relevant facts.

Conflicts of interest

You should avoid placing yourself in situations in which your official actions affect -- or might create the appearance of affecting -- your private interests. That is, you should steer clear of "conflicts of interest," whether your personal interests are financial or nonfinancial.

Direct financial conflicts. As a federal employee, you are legally prohibited from participating in any matter in which either you, a close family member (spouse or minor child), or a business associate has a direct financial interest.

Seeking employment. You may not participate in decisions involving organizations with which you are seeking employment. This refers to bilateral discussions or negotiations with another organization that are conducted with a view to reaching an agreement for possible employment. It also includes such things as sending an unsolicited resume or other employment proposal, and contacts by or through an agent or intermediary. However, it does not include simply rejecting an unsolicited employment overture or requesting a job application.

You are generally considered to be seeking employment until either you or your prospective employer rejects the possibility of employment and all discussions end. If you send out an unsolicited resume or proposal, you would usually no longer be considered to be seeking employment after two months have passed with no indication of interest from the prospective employer or more substantive discussions.

Avoiding conflict of interest

To avoid situations where conflicts of interest -- or even the appearance of such conflicts -- exist, you have these options:

  • • Disqualify yourself from participation.
  • • Divest yourself of the financial interest.
  • • Obtain a written waiver permitting your continued participation.

If you are not certain whether your connection with a person or organization or your financial interest in a matter would constitute a conflict of interest, seek advice from your DAEO before going further.

You may be required to file a financial disclosure report that can be used to identify potential or actual conflicts of interest. Check with your agency DAEO if you are uncertain of whether you need to file.

It would be wise to divest yourself of any stocks or other financial instruments that give you an interest in companies operating in areas your agency regulates or otherwise affects. Divestment must be real and completely sever you from the interest. A transfer of stock to your spouse or close relative would not pass ethical scrutiny.

Generally, you may invest in large mutual funds that in turn hold investments in companies potentially affected by your official actions. However, there may be a problem if you invest in a mutual fund that narrowly targets an area where there could be a conflict. For example, if your agency regulates the energy sector, investment in a mutual fund that specializes in that sector would likely be a conflict of interest.

Other financial conflicts. You may not have a direct financial conflict of interest, but there may be an indirect one. Here are common examples:

  • • Forfeited property. Federal employees are generally prohibited from purchasing property that has been forfeited to or seized by the government. To avoid problems, it's a good idea to check with your DAEO to find out whether any restrictions apply in your situation. If so, be sure to secure any necessary approvals before purchasing such property.
  • • Extraordinary payments. If you received a payment in excess of $10,000 from a former employer and the payment was not pursuant to an established employee benefit plan, you must disqualify yourself from matters affecting that former employer for two years, unless you receive a specific waiver.

Nepotism

Not all conflicts of interest are financial. It is an impermissible conflict of interest for you to engage in nepotism, the appointment, employment, or promotion of a relative to a position in the federal government. You also may not advocate or pressure other officials to select a relative for appointment, employment, promotion, or advancement of any kind.

Pay close attention to your agency's nepotism rules because they may extend beyond relatives. Some agencies include "other persons" in their restriction policy. This could include any individual with a personal financial interest or business relationship with a manager or anyone with a close personal relationship with a manager, such as a boyfriend, girlfriend, housemate, boarder, or significant other. Such relationships could call into question a manager's impartiality in whatever employment matter may be at issue.

Gifts

Soliciting or accepting gifts based on your official position from a person or organization outside the federal government is prohibited, although in limited circumstances you can accept certain items of negligible value.

Similarly, it is inappropriate for you to solicit or accept gifts from subordinates, except in a few limited circumstances.

This section applies only to gifts from "prohibited sources," that is, sources outside government whose interests are in some way affected by your agency's actions or your specific actions in your official position, as well as your subordinates in government. The restrictions described below do not apply to personal friends and family whose interests are not actually or potentially affected by your agency or your official position. If individuals may be considered prohibited sources, even gifts motivated entirely by a personal relationship will create at least the appearance of a conflict of interest -- which is sufficient to activate these restrictions. In doubtful cases, always consult your DAEO before you accept or solicit a gift.

What constitutes a gift? Gifts are anything of value, including:

  • • Cash.
  • • Securities.
  • • Physical items.
  • • Event tickets.
  • • Travel.
  • • Services.
  • • Meals.
  • • Lodging.
  • • Tuition.
  • • Use of facilities.

For example, if "business friends" invite you to use their spacious house and grounds to host your daughter's wedding, you are looking at the offer of a gift -- and a giant ethics problem if you accept. But if the same offer were made by your elderly aunt and uncle who've known your daughter since toddlerhood, there would be no problem unless they happened to have active interests in the economic sector your agency regulates. In that case, you would need to consult your DAEO before you accept their lovely offer. In fact, it would be a good idea to consult your DAEO before soliciting or accepting any sizeable gift from any source.

What does not qualify as a gift? Some items of minimal or negligible value fall below the threshold of what is considered a "gift" and consequently may be accepted. Examples of "negligible value" items include:

  • • Coffee and doughnuts.
  • • Plaques.
  • • Greeting cards.

Discounts and similar benefits that are offered to the public in general or to a broad class of government employees do not constitute prohibited gifts.

Most honorary degrees and similar awards may be accepted, though you may want to ask for an opinion before accepting.

Similarly, food, refreshments, and materials provided to attendees at a conference, training session, or certain other social events that you attend -- with official approval -- in your official capacity are also outside the definition of a gift.

You may accept things of greater value in some highly limited circumstances. For example, you may accept reimbursement for travel expenses related to a job interview.

You cannot accept gifts:

  • • When they are of more than negligible value and are awarded based on your official position or duties.
  • • When they constitute additional pay ("supplementation") for performing your official duties. In other words, "no tipping allowed."
  • • When they come from a subordinate unless they are given voluntarily on the event of a special occasion and are worth no more than $10.
  • • When, even though they are given on the basis of personal friendship rather than your official position, they are worth more than $20 on a single occasion or add up to more than $50 within a year.
  • • When they are gained as the result of the expenditure of government funds. However, you may keep for personal use any benefits received from a travel service provider so long as they were obtained under the same conditions offered to the general public and at no additional cost to government. This includes frequent flier miles.

You should refuse to accept or return gifts that do not meet these requirements -- or contact your DAEO for advice on how to dispose of them. Perishable items usually may be given to charity or may be shared by your office staff with prior approval.

Warning: It is not permissible to suggest that an otherwise prohibited gift be given to someone else. It is not even permissible to suggest that the gift be given to a favorite charity or any charity. A prohibited gift is just that -- prohibited.

Crowdsourced funding. Crowdsourced fundraising, or "crowdfunding," is the practice of obtaining needed funding by soliciting contributions from a large number of people. Online crowdsourced fundraising usually is conducted through social media or on a dedicated crowdfunding platform (for example, GiveSmart, GoFundMe, IndieGoGo, or Kickstarter).

The gift rules apply to crowdsourced donations. Employees may not solicit or accept gifts from a prohibited source or given because of their official position, unless an exception applies. The personal relationship exception -- which allows gifts clearly motivated by a family relationship or personal friendship rather than the employee's federal position -- extends to donations given via online fundraising tools.

If the solicitation directly ties the intended use of donations to the employee's federal job, there's going to be a strong inference that a gift is being given based on an employee's official position. Here's an example: the employee sets up a GoFundMe specifically to pay for a professional certification that will qualify them for a promotion at their agency. Similar concerns will arise if the employee highlights their federal position in the solicitation.

Virtual events. Virtual events are treated like in-person events when it comes to the gift rules. If a virtual event has monetary value and an exception to or exclusion from the gift rules does not apply, employees should not accept invitations to these events if the invitation is offered by a prohibited source or because of the employee's official position.

If a fee is charged to some or all attendees to attend a virtual event, an employee cannot accept a free or discounted invitation from a prohibited source or if the invitation was offered because of the employee's official position, unless an exception or exclusion to the gift rules apply.

If a virtual event is free of charge to all attendees, it will usually not have monetary value, as organizers usually do not provide attendees with any tangible benefits. However, as with in-person events, some virtual events could have monetary value if organizers offer entertainment or other benefits to attendees in connection with the event, even if no fee is charged.

Here's an example of how this could occur: A virtual event may include entertainment or the shipment and delivery of items to attendees ahead of the event, such as books, alcoholic beverages, food, or gift bags. In those circumstances, the event would have monetary value, and an employee cannot accept if it was from a prohibited source or offered because of the employee's official position, unless an exception or exclusion to the gift rules applies.

Misuse of position

You may not use your official position for your own private gain or for the benefit of persons or organizations with which you are associated personally. This general principle covers a variety of prohibited activities, including:

  • • Use of nonpublic information for financial gain.
  • • Help to secure government positions, promotions, contracts, or other benefits for associates or relatives.
  • • Use of government time for inappropriate purposes.
  • • Use of government property or vehicles for personal purposes.
  • • Use of your official status to endorse a product or service.

In short, your position should not be used as a lever to obtain advantage, financial gain, or improper benefits for yourself, relatives, friends, or business associates.

Use of nonpublic information. You cannot use nonpublic information -- or knowingly allow the use of such information -- in connection with financial transactions to provide an advantage to yourself or another person. Nonpublic information is that which you gain in the performance of your official duties and which has not been made available to the general public.

For example, if you passed nonpublic information to a relative concerning the pending announcement of a contract about to be awarded to a particular company -- thereby providing your relative a possible advantage in making a stock purchase -- you would violate this requirement and be subject to administrative penalties. If your relative acted on your information or passed it on to someone else who did act on it, this would result in a federal crime in which you would be implicated. You may recall several very high-profile cases in the past that involved "insider trading."

Improper use of government property and time. As a general rule, government property and official time should be used only for official business. In other words, you may not use government vehicles, equipment, or paid duty time for the personal benefit of yourself, other persons, or outside organizations. It is also inappropriate to direct, urge, or coerce another federal employee to use government time or property for unofficial purposes.

So, if you need a new deck for your home, you'll have to build it without the supplies and equipment your federal facility's building and grounds unit has on hand. What about asking for help from the skilled employees who work there? That could get dicey, even if you do it outside of duty time and they are not your employees. Better check with your DAEO before you talk to the guys in the shop.

However, most agencies do permit the occasional, limited personal use of office equipment and library facilities, phones, computers, and electronic devices if it does not result in significant additional cost to the government.

Official endorsement of products or services. As a general rule, it is also inappropriate to use your official title, the agency name, logo, or stationery to endorse the products or services of a nongovernment organization. At the very least, doing so creates the appearance of improper furtherance of the interests of a nongovernment organization. Worse, it can create the suspicion -- or indicate the reality -- of a personal benefit accruing to you as a result of your endorsement.

Outside activities

You should not engage in outside employment or other outside activity that conflicts with your official duties. There are a variety of ways in which outside activity may create a conflict, including:

  • • Paid employment that substantially involves the same or similar work you perform in your official position.
  • • Receipt of pay for writing or speaking about your official duties or on matters directly related to your official position.
  • • Use of information not available to the public to further or conduct outside employment activities.
  • • Use of your official position title, other than in a biographical note, in the conduct of outside speaking, writing, or other employment activities.
  • • Use of government time or property to prepare materials used in outside employment activities.
  • • Representation of a third party before a federal court or agency in a matter in which the government is a party or in which it has a substantial interest.
  • • Service as an expert witness on behalf of a third party in any proceeding before a federal agency or court in which the United States is a party or in which it has a substantial interest.
  • • Solicitation of subordinates or agency clients for funds.
  • • Running for partisan political office.
  • • Engaging in illegal activities.

Teaching. Employees are usually permitted to accept compensation for teaching a course as part of the curriculum of an institution of higher learning, an elementary or secondary school, or a program sponsored by the federal government, or a state or local government. However, employees in noncareer positions above GS-15 usually require advance authorization before engaging in teaching for compensation.

Professional practice. Employees in most agencies require written approval before engaging in the practice of law outside the agency. Similarly, written approval may be required for the practice of other professions or occupations that are directly related to the mission of the agency. However, this type of practice is quite possible with approval, which is often given if there is no real conflict of interest.

Shutdown ethics issues

When a government's lapse in appropriations leads to a shutdown, employees may have to go without pay for some time. Although trying to find outside work, accepting gifts, or raising money are tempting ways to pay bills, federal employees must be careful not to violate ethics rules. The Office of Government Ethics issued guidance clarifying that employees may generally accept free meals and other resources while furloughed or working without pay during a lapse in appropriations, but strongly advised consulting with an ethics official before crowdsourcing for support like starting a GoFundMe campaign.

The guidance stressed that employees stay subject to all ethics rules while they are in non-pay status and directed agencies to "proactively provide employees with guidance about agency-specific rules with their furlough or exempt notice and make sure such advice is easily accessible on the agency website."

Outside employment

While career employees may generally take outside employment during a funding lapse, the OGE specifically "cautioned" employees to coordinate with their agency ethics officials because some agencies have additional restrictions, such as prior approval requirements. Before a funding lapse occurs, managers should ensure their employees are able to access those ethics requirements and resources during a shutdown. The OGE clarified that:

  • • Employees may not represent outside parties before the federal government. This means, for example, that "a furloughed employee may not contact a federal government agency or court that is open during a lapse in appropriations in order to sell a company's products or services."
  • • Employees may not use their federal government title or position to benefit a nonfederal employer.
  • • Once the shutdown ends, employees should discuss with an agency ethics official whether any outside employment during the lapse would require them to recuse themselves from matters involving their former employer for a period of one year.

Gifts

During extended shutdown periods, companies and individuals may provide free meals, discounts, and support for affected federal employees. According to the OGE, when your agency is affected by a shutdown, generally employees in a non-pay status:

  • • May accept free meals, discounts, and other support only if the offer is extended to all federal employees and not limited to a class, to the military, or by geography.
  • • May accept gifts offered to a class of government employees that does not discriminate on the basis of official duties or rank only if the organization or person providing the gift is not a prohibited source, i.e., an organization or person that has business with the employee's agency. An employee, for example, may accept a free month of gym membership available to all furloughed federal employees only if the gym is not a prohibited source for that employee.
  • • May accept loans from banks or financial institutions available to the general public or all federal employees so long as offeror is not a prohibited source for that employee.
  • • May accept a small gift offered as an individual gesture of goodwill if the offer is unsolicited, is worth $20 or less, is not cash or a cash equivalent -- such as a gift card -- and does not add up to more than $50 from the same source in a year. This is permitted for career government employees, but most political appointees cannot use this exception to accept a gift from a lobbyist.
  • • May accept gifts that are based on personal relationships if the employee's federal position is not the motivating factor and the individual is personally offering assistance. So, generally an employee may accept a $1,000 cash loan offered by a family member or close friend but likely would need to decline a similar offer from a friend who works for an agency's contractor where the employee's official duties affect the contractor.
  • • May not accept gifts from employees receiving less pay and may not give, donate to, or solicit contributions for an official supervisor. However, employees may accept the following gifts from subordinates or employees receiving less pay: noncash gifts with a value of $10 or less, gifts of personal hospitality of a type and value customarily provided, or food and refreshments to be shared in the office.

Crowdfunding campaigns

When in a non-pay status during a shutdown, OGE "strongly" encourages employees to consult an ethics official before any crowdfunding campaign, such as GoFundMe, because they trigger a "number of potential ethical pitfalls." Generally, employees -- or even someone acting on their behalf -- may not start a crowdfunding campaign because they:

  • • May not solicit a gift based on the employee's federal employment.
  • • May not use "an employee's agency, title, or status as a federal employee" in "any such solicitation."
  • • Cannot accept gifts from prohibited sources. To avoid an ethics violation, an "employee would need to be able to identify and reject such prohibited donations."

Mistakes to avoid

Government ethics can be tricky, but it helps if you keep a few hints in mind.

Do not:

  • • Aim for "technical" compliance. Don't cut corners close enough to give you what you want while not technically violating ethics requirements. This is a dangerous game and one that can ruin your career. Remember, you are expected to avoid even the appearance of unethical behavior.
  • • Act without certainty. If you have any doubts or questions whatsoever as to the propriety of what you're about to do, ask your DAEO.
  • • Assume no one will ever find out. Sooner or later, your ethics violation is likely to come out.
  • • Dismiss the importance of the Standards of Ethical Conduct. It's easy to tune out the annual ethics briefing as just another paper requirement. It's also a major mistake. Ethics transgressions can, and do, provide the basis for a substantial number of disciplinary and criminal penalties every year.
  • • Rely solely on intent. Although you would never knowingly act unethically, "ignorance of the law" may be no excuse. Know the rules and get authoritative advice. That's what the DAEO is there for.

Ask FMG

Q. Is it a conflict of interest to own shares in a mutual fund that, in turn, may hold stock in a company that may be doing business with my agency?

A. Generally not, although you may want to discuss the matter with your DAEO. If the mutual fund targets a specific sector that may be affected by your agency's actions, such as energy or communications, you may well have a conflict of interest.

Q. What is meant by the term "seeking employment"?

A. It refers to bilateral discussions or negotiations with another organization that are conducted with a view to reaching an agreement for possible employment. It also includes such things as sending an unsolicited resume or other employment proposal, and contacts by or through an agent or intermediary. However, it does not include simply rejecting an unsolicited employment overture or requesting a job application.

Q. Is it considered acceptance of a gift if I receive lunch or other refreshments while attending a conference or seminar as part of my official duties?

A. No, such refreshments are usually minimal in value or already included in the tuition the agency paid for your attendance -- or both.

Q. If a company rep I do business with on my agency's behalf offers me a gift of significant value, is it OK if I suggest they give the equivalent in cash to one of my favorite charities?

A. No. The gift must be turned down, not redirected anywhere.

Q. Is it improper to purchase certain items from a company regulated by my agency at a discount it offers to its own employees?

A. If the discount is not generally available to the public, it is improper.

Q. Can I get in trouble if one or more subordinates solicit other employees in my organization for a gift for me?

A. It depends on the circumstances. There is generally no problem if the gift is for a special event (e.g., birth of a child), the donation sought is minimal (no more than $10), and participation is truly voluntary with no overtones of retaliation for refusing to participate. If the solicitation fails to meet these criteria, however, and it is conducted with your knowledge or approval, it will be a problem.

Q. Can my sister, who is not a federal employee, start a GoFundMe page to raise money to pay for my bills when I am not getting paid during a shutdown?

A. The Office of Government Ethics warns that GoFundMe and other similar crowdsourced fundraising campaigns trigger a "number of ethical pitfalls" and advises you to consult the agency ethics official before participating or allowing others to raise money on your behalf. Under the ethics rules, generally employees -- or even someone acting on their behalf -- may not start a crowdsourcing campaign because they may not:

  • • Solicit a gift based on the employee's federal employment.
  • • Use an employee's agency, title, or status as a federal employee in any such solicitation.
  • • Accept gifts from prohibited sources. To avoid an ethics violation, an employee would need to be able to identify and reject such prohibited donations, which would be difficult in a crowdsourced format.
