E. DECIDING OFFICIAL

The former regulations provided that the final decision of the agency was required to be rendered by the agency head or his designee. A final decision not rendered by the agency head, or someone specifically designated by the agency head, was null and void and did not constitute a final agency decision. See Tatem v. National Guard Bureau, 01830702 (1984). The current regulations do not contain a similar provision and only require that the "agency" issue a decision. 29 CFR 1614.110. The agency EEO director is, however, required to be under the immediate supervision of the agency head. See MD-110 at 1-1. Presumably, the decision must still be rendered by the agency head, or by someone specifically designated by the agency head to perform that function, such as the EEO director.

The official designated to issue the final agency decision must be one "who is fair, impartial and objective." Tritz v. USPS, 01842336 (1986), citing 29 CFR 1613.221(a). When the deciding official is someone who was in the chain of command of the agency responsible for filling the position at issue, those requirements are per se not met. As the Commission observed in Tritz:

We hold that 29 CFR 1613.221(a) requires an official, who has formally concurred in a subordinate's recommendation of a candidate, to recuse himself or herself from serving as a designee of the head of the agency for the purpose of making the final agency decision with regard to a complaint challenging the selection of the candidate.

The Commission vacated the final agency decision and remanded the case to the agency with instructions to designate a deciding official who was not involved in the selection process. There is nothing in the new regulations that would indicate the Commission has, in any way, retreated from this position.

Clearly, it is a conflict of interest for the alleged discriminating official to issue the final agency decision on a complaint that involves him. Scott v. Secretary of Navy, 01952948 (1996). But, the Commission came to a different conclusion when the agency official named in the complaint merely signed the decision, and the agency rescinded the decision and gave it to another official to sign in Ford v. Social Security Administration, 01974920 (2000). The Commission in Ford stated:

Finally, the Commission notes that the complainant raises the issue of conflict of interest on appeal because MT, the Director of OCREO and the selecting official for the vacancies at issue in Agency No. 647-94 signed the May 13, 1997 final agency decision. The complainant also asserts that MT was the principal agency official involved in a prior EEO complaint that is the basis for her claim of reprisal.

. . .

We agree with the complainant that when MT signed off on the May 1997 final agency decision, his action created a conflict of interest. It is a conflict of interest for the alleged discriminating official to issue the final agency decision on a complaint which involves the official. Scott v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 01951948 (March 26, 1996). However, the agency rescinded its May 13, 1997 decision. As reason for its rescission, the agency stated that the May 13, 1997 decision was mistakenly submitted to MT for signature, although MT was involved in the complaints. The agency further stated that MT did not provide any input into the May 13, 1997 decision, but simply signed it. The new final agency decision was issued by the agency's Deputy Commissioner for Human Resources. Accordingly, we find that no conflict of interest existed with regard to the August 1997 decision. Moreover, we find no evidence in the record suggesting that MT's participation in the EEO process resulted in prejudice to the complainant, although we caution the agency to avoid even an appearance of a conflict of interest.

(Footnote omitted). That the agency had rescinded and reissued the decision, along with the fact that the agency official named in the complaint was not involved in deciding or writing the final agency decision, were key to the Commission's decision that no conflict of interest existed.

By contrast, it is a conflict of interest for an EEO officer to render a final agency decision in a case in which he has been involved. In Lewis v. General Services Administration, 01A03973 (2001), the Commission found it was a conflict of interest for an EEO officer, who had also served as the complainant's EEO counselor, to issue a final decision dismissing the complaint. However, there is no particular remedy for such a violation and, in Lewis, the Commission simply warned the agency against such conduct in the future:

As an initial matter, the Commission finds that there is a conflict of interest with regard to the agency decision. The decision was issued under the signature line of "Regional EEO Officer" and contains repeated first person references. For example, the decision states that the "Agency file created by me at that time contains a letter similar to [complainant's]. . ." and "[complainant] asked me to talk. . .with her second-line supervisor (emphasis added)." In addition, much of the correspondence exchanged between the agency and complainant was directed to, or written by, the individual who wrote the agency decision. That same individual is also named in some documents as complainant's EEO Counselor.

It is important that EEO officials have the confidence of the agency and its employees. Therefore, EEO counselors, EEO investigators, EEO officers, and EEO program managers cannot serve as representatives for agencies or complainants, because to do so would be inconsistent with their neutral roles. See the EEOC Management Directive for 29 CFR Part 1614, EEOC MD-110, at page 1-3 (November 9, 1999). In this case, it appears as if complainant's EEO Counselor issued the agency decision that dismissed complainant's complaint. The agency is advised to avoid such conflicts in the future by issuing decisions with impartial agency officials.

However, having the present record before us, we will address the agency's dismissal of the complaint. . . .

It is a conflict of interest for the attorney who represented the agency in litigation to issue the final decision on the complainant's fee petition. Porter v. Secretary of Navy, 0120061837 (2008). However, the Commission again found there was no specific remedy and that the complainant suffered no prejudice because the Commission had independently reviewed the fee petition on appeal.
