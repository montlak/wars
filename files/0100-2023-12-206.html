12. Retaliatory Investigations

The fact that an agency is justified in taking a conduct action against an employee does not preclude a separate claim that the agency conducted a retaliatory investigation after the filing of an EEO complaint over that action. In Complainant v. Attorney General, 0120132527 (2013), the complainant filed two EEO complaints that were consolidated for hearing. The agency conducted an "EEO complaint background check" that revealed the complainant had previously been terminated from a halfway house position she held prior to working for the agency as a corrections officer. The complainant was then removed for failure to provide accurate information during a preemployment interview. The Commission rejected the agency's argument that the complaint was essentially a collateral attack on the removal and found they were distinct complaints. Moreover, the Commission, in Complainant, found:

While the record shows Complainant filed her grievance before filing her EEO complaint, she argues that they do not regard the same matter. She argues that the grievance regarded her removal, and her EEO complaint regards the retaliatory "EEO complaint background check" and resulting OIA investigation, which constituted harassment.

She cites EEOC precedent and guidance for the proposition that the "EEO complaint background check" and resulting OIA investigation, which constituted harassment claim is distinct from her termination claim. Specifically she cites EEOC Enforcement Guidance on after-acquired evidence and McKennon v. Nashville Banner Publishing Co., EEOC Notice No. 915.002, 1995 WL 1789069 (Dec. 14, 2005). It contains the following example:

Example--CP files a charge alleging that he was discriminatorily denied a promotion. R launches an extensive background investigation of CP and learns that he falsified his application. Accordingly, R fires CP. The Commission investigation reveals that R does terminate employees who have falsified their applications. It also shows that the failure to promote was not discriminatory. R contends that CP suffered no loss until the termination, that the termination was for legitimate reasons and that R is not, therefore, liable to CP.

However, the Commission finds that R did not simply discover the information in the course of investigating the charge, but purposefully sought derogatory information about CP in retaliation for his challenging the failure to promote.

As in McKennon, reinstatement would be inappropriate because the employer does terminate those known to have engaged in similar misconduct. However, because the evidence of wrongdoing was not simply unearthed during an investigation of CP's complaint, but was deliberately sought to retaliate against CP and to discourage similar charges...this is the kind of "extraordinary equitable circumstance" that warrants extending backpay to the date the complaint is resolved. An employer who chooses to wage a retaliatory investigation must lose the advantage of equities that would, absent the retaliation, favor that employer, especially since retaliation is an independent violation of the federal employment discrimination laws.

She also cites Smith v. Department of the Navy, 03950060 (Nov. 5, 1995). The petitioner therein previously filed an EEO complaint regarding not being selected for a promotion for which he applied. Thereafter, the Agency conducted a background check to verify the information on his application in reprisal for filing the EEO complaint, and discovered that he falsified his application. The Agency terminated him for the falsification. The petitioner filed an appeal with the Merit Systems Protection Board (MSPB) regarding the removal. While the MSPB found that the background check "smacks of" retaliatory intent, it upheld the removal. The Commission found that because this case came to it as a petition to review a decision of the Board on the issue of removal, it was limited to reviewing only this issue, and upheld the removal. The Commission went on to find, at footnote 4, that:

However, it is undisputed that petitioner's supervisor initiated the background check in retaliation for his filing of a prior complaint of discrimination, and the agency is advised that her conduct may contribute to a discriminatory work environment. We further advise the agency that it has a duty under 29 C.F.R. § 1614.102(a) to eliminate discriminatory practices in the workplace. That duty includes providing training for its employees to prevent discrimination in the workplace, and where necessary, taking appropriate disciplinary action against employees who engage in discriminatory practices to ensure that such practices do not continue. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.102(a)(6). The Commission cautions the agency that if this case had come to us in another posture, i.e., an appeal of a final agency decision, the agency would be held liable for its admitted discrimination [the background check].

In opposition to the appeal, the Agency cites cases for the proposition that Complainant's grievance on her removal is inextricably intertwined with the issue in her subsequent EEO complaint. It cites, for example, Eitches v. Department of Housing and Urban Development, EEOC Appeal No. 0120080468 (June 9, 2010). In Eitches, the EEOC ruled that Complainant's grievance that he was reprimanded was inextricably intertwined with her subsequent EEO complaint issue regarding the manner the reprimand was issued.

The Commission has a strong public policy interest in ensuring that agencies do not conduct informal or formal investigations to obtain derogatory information on a Complainant in response to her filing an EEO complaint. For this reason, we are inclined to treat claims about such alleged investigations, especially where there is some evidence that they occurred, as distinct matters which independently state a claim of discrimination. Such is the case here, where the OIA Investigative Report refers to a related prior "EEO complaint background check," albeit we are not making any rulings on the merits here. Our treatment is supported by the guidance and case Complainant cited recounted above.

The Commission in Elayne C. v. Secretary of Labor, 0120162090 (2016), reversed the agency's dismissal for failure to state a claim where a supervisor's accusations of threatening behavior, levied against the complainant after she alleged reprisal against the supervisor, led to an investigation of the complainant. Though the results of the investigation were not contained in the record, the complainant noted that limitations were placed thereafter on her access to certain areas of the office. According to the Commission in Elayne C., the investigation, coupled with allegations that the supervisor had vowed to "get even" with the complainant, was enough to state a claim:

The Commission has previously held that, in most circumstances, merely conducting an internal investigation into purported misconduct does not, without more, cause injury sufficient to render the subject of the investigation aggrieved. See Heard v. Department of Justice, EEOC Appeal No. 0120092680 (August 27, 2009); Shelly v. Department of Treasury, EEOC Appeal No. 01996655 (October 27, 2000). However, in this case, we find that Complainant has alleged that the supervisor stated that she was going to "get even" with Complainant for assisting the coworker in filing an EEO complaint against her. Shortly thereafter, Complainant alleged, the supervisor falsely reported to the Regional Director that she was fearful of Complainant, resulting in an investigation and limitations on Complainant's access to certain areas of the office. Viewing these allegations in the light most favorable to Complainant, we find that she has stated a viable claim of retaliation that requires investigation and further processing.

Agencies cannot dismiss a reprisal complaint for failure to state a claim based on a finding that the complainant did not engage in EEO activity. Such findings go to the merits of the case and are irrelevant as to whether the complaint stated a claim. Zola R. v. Secretary of Agriculture, 0120161927 (2016). The agency in Zola R. dismissed the complaint, concluding that the complainant's unfair labor practice complaint did not amount to EEO activity. The complainant countered that her unfair labor practice complaint included allegations of discrimination. The Commission reversed the agency's dismissal and remanded the case for further investigation into the complainant's contention that the agency had place her under investigation for misconduct and issued her a proposal for significant disciplinary action in reprisal for her unfair labor practice complaint.
