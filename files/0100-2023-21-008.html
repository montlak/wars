1. Causal Connection With Gender

As noted above, the complainant must show that a tangible employment action was based on the rejection of or submission to sexual advances--i.e., the complainant must establish that there is a causal connection between the alleged sexual harassment and the subsequent employment action. The requirement that a complainant establish a causal connection is grounded in the statutory language of Title VII which prohibits, in part, employment actions taken "because of...sex." In other words, in a tangible employment action sexual harassment case, the complainant must establish the element of intent just as in other cases involving intentional discrimination.

As discussed fully in Chapter 10, there are two methods available to the complainant to establish intent: 1) circumstantial evidence; and 2) direct evidence. The EEOC has acknowledged the use of circumstantial evidence in its Enforcement Guidance: Vicarious Liability, stating that a tangible employment action claim

is analyzed like any other case in which a challenged employment action is alleged to be discriminatory. If the employer produces evidence of a non-discriminatory explanation for the tangible employment action, a determination must be made whether that explanation is a pretext designed to hide a discriminatory motive....

It has not issued any guidance on the use of direct evidence. At the present time, case law has not served to shed much additional light on the subject.

There are likely two principle reasons the EEOC has not addressed the use of direct evidence in establishing causal connection in any significant way in its enforcement guidance memoranda or decisions. First, the vast majority of sexual harassment cases are hostile environment cases and, as a result, the Commission has had less opportunity to address the questions. Second, most tangible employment action cases involving direct evidence come down to the complainant testifying that the supervisor or manager premised an employment action on the rejection of or submission to sexual advances. The supervisor or manager denies that sexual advances took place or contends that submission to the advances was voluntary. Seldom are there other witnesses to tangible employment action harassment. This leaves the factfinder in the precarious position of deciding who to believe.

However, existing case law presents the factfinder with a way out of basing a decision solely on witness credibility. When a factfinder determines that the direct evidence is insufficient to establish the complainant's case under Price Waterhouse, he or she must analyze the case under the McDonnell Douglas framework with the conflicting testimony of the witnesses being one issue to be resolved in the analysis. As a result, in addition to evaluating the testimony of the complainant, the factfinder evaluates the legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason advanced by the agency for taking the action to determine if it is pretextual.

When harassing behavior culminates in a tangible employment action into which the harasser has input, there is a strong presumption of causal connection because it is presumed that the harasser cannot act in an unbiased manner. For example, in Greene v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120090106 (2011), the complainant, a probationary food service worker alleged that her employment was terminated after she rejected requests by her first-line supervisor for a date. The Commission, in Greene, explained:

Regarding element 4, we find that the harassment affected a term or condition of employment because it culminated in a tangible employment action--Complainant's termination. A strong inference of discrimination will arise whenever a harassing supervisor undertakes or has significant input into a tangible employment action affecting the victim, because it can be "assume[d] that the harasser...could not act as an objective, non-discriminatory decision maker with respect to the plaintiff." Enforcement Guidance, at § IV.C. However, if the employer produces evidence of a non-discriminatory reason for the action, the employee will have to prove that the asserted reason was a pretext designed to hide the true discriminatory motive. Id.

First, we find that S1 undertook and had significant input into Complainant's termination. Specifically, the Request for Personnel Action listed S1 as the management official who requested the termination. In addition, S2 attested that he discussed the termination with S1. ROI, Ex. B3, at 8. Further, the record reflects that S1 wrote three out of the four ROCs attached to S2's email to HR requesting the termination. Despite all the above, S1 seemed to try to minimize his role in Complainant's termination when speaking to the EEO Investigator. When asked by the EEO Investigator if he recommended the termination to S2, S1 attested:

Actually we sent all this stuff to [HR] and they pretty much make their decision based on that. Would I have made a recommendation to him? Yes. Do I remember making a recommendation per se like that to him? No, because it's not totally up to us anyway so that wouldn't make sense to me, but it's possible that I said she needs to go.

Second, we find that Complainant established a temporal nexus between S1's unwelcome conduct on April 20 and 26, 2007 and her termination on July 6, 2007. Specifically, the record reflects that Complainant's performance problems, as documented by S1, began in an ROC dated April 26, 2007--the same day that she rejected S1's advances. In addition, the record reflects that, in the three months before S1's unwelcome conduct, Complainant received a "Satisfactory" performance evaluation from S2 and no negative ROCs about her performance. However, in the two months after S1's unwelcome conduct, Complainant received six negative ROCs from S1 and was terminated.

Third, we find that Complainant proved, by a preponderance of the evidence, that the Agency's asserted reason for her termination was a pretext to hide a discriminatory motive. Specifically, we find that S1 (the requesting official) and S2 (the authorizing official) each provided a different reason for the termination, both of which are unsupported by the record.

S1 did not explicitly state a specific reason for the termination, but attested that Complainant "had a really negative attitude," "was just being a really nasty individual," and would "come to work and cuss [him] out everyday." In addition, S1 attested that Complainant made comments to other employees or under her breath to him, such as: (a) "I don't give a f*ck what he says. F*ck him, you don't have to listen to him, he's nobody"; and (b) "He gets on my nerves, that's why I'm calling off tomorrow." Id. at 4. Although the record contains numerous ROCs from S1 about Complainant, we find that none of those ROCs demonstrate that Complainant had a bad attitude towards S1, cussed him out on a daily basis, or regularly made rude comments to him.

In contrast, S2 attested, "From the documents that I had, again there were a number of AWOL documents and a number of disrespectful conducts towards customers.... There were a couple of customer complaints..." ROI, Ex. B6, at 8. Although S2 attested that there were "a number of AWOL documents," the record contains only one June 11, 2007 ROC about Complainant being AWOL for 15 minutes. Although S2 attested that there were "a number of disrespectful conducts towards customers" and "a couple of customer complaints," the record contains only two ROCs (June 14 and 15, 2007) about a single incident involving a customer.

The credibility of the employer's explanation is key and must be judged in light of all the evidence obtained during the investigation. EEOC Compliance Manual Section 15, "Race and Color Discrimination," No. 915.003, at 15-V.A.2. (Apr. 19, 2006). If an employer's explanation for the employee's treatment ultimately is not credible, that is powerful evidence that discrimination is the most likely explanation. Id. An employer's credibility will be undermined if its explanation is unsupported by or contrary to the facts. Id. Similarly, the credibility of the explanation can be called into question if it is unduly vague, appears to be an after-the-fact explanation, or appears otherwise fabricated (e.g., the explanation shifts, or inconsistent reasons are given). Id. Based on our review of the record, we find that S1 did not request Complainant's termination for performance reasons, but instead recommended her termination because she rejected his unwelcome sexual advances.
