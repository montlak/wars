II. AGENCY BURDEN OF PRODUCTION--CIRCUMSTANTIAL EVIDENCE

The agency's burden of producing a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for taking an employment action is not onerous, yet it is a burden that must be satisfied through the introduction of evidence that gives sufficient details as to its reasons to permit the complainant a chance to rebut those reasons. As a general rule, the more subjective the criteria for an employment decision, the more detail the agency will have to provide to sustain its burden of production.

For example, as the Commission explained in Beasley v. Postmaster General, 07A40096 (2005), in finding that the agency had not presented sufficient evidence to meet its burden of production in a promotion case:

In the instant case, it is not disputed that complainant applied for the permanent RS position at issue. Although complainant was deemed qualified for the position by the agency when it was re-posted, she was not selected. The record reflects that a White applicant was selected for this position. Consequently, we concur with the AJ's finding that complainant has established a prima facie case of race discrimination. The burden of production now shifts to the agency to rebut the presumption by articulating a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for the non-selections. While the agency's burden of production is not onerous, it must nevertheless provide a specific, clear, and individualized explanation for the treatment accorded complainant. Complainant is entitled to some rationale for her non-selection that provides her with an opportunity to attempt to satisfy her ultimate burden of proving that the agency's explanation was a pretext for discriminatory animus.

In the case before us, we find that the agency did not meet this burden. In an effort to articulate its reasons, the agency presented hearing and affidavit testimony from the SO involved in the selection of the position at issue. We concur with the AJ's finding that the SO's testimony however, does not contain any reasons for complainant's non-selections. While the SO made reference at the hearing to the performance of the applicants during the interview, the SO also testified that there was no documentation of the applicants' responses to interview questions, nor was there any documentation presented regarding the weight accorded to the educational qualifications of the applicants or their prior work experiences. AJ's Decision at 18. We concur with the AJ's finding that SO or the agency did not present any interview notes or other written documentation that would have provided some evidence to support the selection of C1. In addition, the AJ found that the SO's statements that C1's selection was based primarily on subjective criteria did not afford complainant a full and fair opportunity to demonstrate pretext. AJ's Decision at 16. Despite the agency's appellate contentions, the ability to conduct a comparative analysis of the applicants was effectively thwarted. We thus find that the evidence presented by the agency is not sufficient to provide that specific, clear, and individualized explanation that is required by the holding in Texas Department of Community Affairs v. Burdine, 450 U.S. 248, 253 (1981) to explain why complainant was not selected for the RS position for which she was qualified. Instead, the agency's articulation consisted of conclusory statements that complainant was not the best qualified and therefore not selected. We further note that in finding the agency failed to articulate legitimate reasons for its actions, the AJ rejected the hearing testimony of the SO and found her to not be a credible witness. AJ's Decision at 18. The Commission has held that it generally will not disturb the credibility determinations of an AJ, where, as here, such determinations are based on the credibility of the witnesses. See Esquer v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 05960096 (Sept. 6, 1996). Under these circumstances, we conclude that the agency did not articulate a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for not selecting complainant. Thus, we conclude that in light of the agency's failure to meet its burden of production, that the agency discriminated against complainant on the basis of her race when she was not selected for the position at issue.

Although the agency's burden of articulating a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason is not onerous and it is not incumbent on the agency to prove it had legitimate reasons for taking action--that burden of proof squarely rests with the complainant, the agency cannot sustain its burden of production by giving conflicting reasons for an employment decision. For example, in Carmody v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 07200600991 (2006), during the investigative stage, the agency contended that the complainant was not hired for a screener's position with TSA because there were no available positions and his conditional appointment was invalid because it was not signed by a TSA representative. At the hearing, the agency contended that the selection process was handled by private contractors due to the need to hire 50,000 people in six months in the wake of September 11th. The agency contended that it was impossible to oversee all the contractual work because of the volume and that the complainant was not hired because of he was inadvertently not invited to training. The Commission found that "taking into account the agency's inconsistent positions" the agency had not rebutted the complainant's prima facie case of discrimination.

Similarly, the agency cannot sustain its burden without submitting evidence of who made a specific employment decision and why. For example, in Dang v. Postmaster General, 0120060488J (2007), the complainant established a prima facie case by showing that he was required to work beyond his eight--hour medical restrictions while white employees on similar restrictions were not required to work more than eight hours. The Commission found that the agency failed to rebut the prima facie case when it simply submitted evidence from a supervisor stating he was not supervising the complainant at that time. The Commission noted that the agency failed to submit any evidence as to who was supervising the complainant or why he was required to work more than eight hours.

On the other hand, the agency does not need to provide excruciating detail to sustain its burden of production. For example, in Tkachuk v. Secretary of Navy, 01200644641 (2006), the Commission found that the agency sustained its burden where selection panel members could not recall the specific interview answers that the complainant gave, but could recall his answers were short, that one answer was incorrect, and that the scoring reflected that incorrect answer.

There is a difference between discriminatory motive and poor judgment on the part of an agency manager. The Commission is concerned only with the former. As the Commission noted in Complainant v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120112074 (2014):

An employer's business decision cannot be found discriminatory simply because it appears that the employer acted unwisely, or that the employer's decision was in error or a misjudgment. The fact that the supervisor appeared to poorly handle Complainant's return to work after her extended leave in rotating her to medicine service does not by itself demonstrate that the supervisor had been motivated to move Complainant because of her sex or disability.
