26. Work on Appeal

It is not unexpected that an employee's representative would conduct some preliminary work in preparation for an appeal in the event the complaint is unsuccessful. In Munoz v. Department of Air Force, 01821507 (1983), the Commission held the complainant's attorney was entitled to four hours of compensation for preliminary work on an appeal conducted prior to the agency's final decision. Without a great deal of comment, the EEOC found that such contingency preparations were a "matter of course."

Just as work on appeals on the merits of a case are compensable, so is work on appeals related solely to the remedy granted. Konchar v. Postmaster General, 01902070 (1990). The complainant is entitled to be made whole and the appellant is entitled to an award of attorney fees for the successful appeal of a remedy that is less than complete.

The fact that a complainant is not successful on appeal does not affect his right to attorney fees for issues on which he prevailed in the final agency decision. But, the complainant is not entitled to fees for those issues raised on appeal on which he did not prevail. See Bell v. Secretary of Navy, 01933320 (1994). When the Commission denies a complainant's request for reconsideration but reopens the case on its own initiative, the complainant is not entitled to fees for the work done on reconsideration. See Sherrod v. Secretary of Navy, 01981244 (2001). Similarly, where the complainant's oriGINAl appeal results in a finding of discrimination but a request for reconsideration results in no further relief, the complainant is not the prevailing party for purposes of that request and is not entitled to fees for the work on that request. Wilson v. Postmaster General, 0120051771 (2007). See also Olison v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120072707 (2007) (complainant entitled to fees for successful defense of agency appeal, but not for cross-appeal or request for reconsideration on which she was unsuccessful).

The length of time reasonably spent on appeal can be affected by the detail of the decision below and the posture of the parties on appeal. For example, in Tilden v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01982768 (1999), the Commission found it was excessive for an associate with seven years experience to spend over 34 hours writing a 14-page appeal brief. In reducing the number of hours by 50%, the Commission noted that the complainant prevailed before the administrative judge and that the recommended decision set forth, in detail, the factual findings and relevant law. A similar finding was reached in Alston v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01A54981 (2006). The Commission upheld an across-the-board reduction in the requested fees for appellate work, noting that the claimed appellate fees were almost as much as the fees for litigating the case at hearing and the complainant's brief was relatively short and relied heavily upon arguments found in the decision of the administrative judge.

The Commission's regulations do not expressly permit the filing of a reply to the agency's response to an appeal. On the other hand, nothing in the regulations prohibits a reply. Even though a reply is not mandatory or even expressly permitted, time spent on such a reply is compensable if it is reasonable and necessary. A reply is considered reasonable and necessary when the Commission relies on arguments in the reply in issuing its decision. Goodwin v. Postmaster General, 01960485 (1998). On the other hand, fees for a supplemental brief are not warranted where it merely restates arguments made in the oriGINAl brief. Costello v. Postmaster General, 0120064018 (2006).
