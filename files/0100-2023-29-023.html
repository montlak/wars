H. REVIEW BY EEOC

The EEOC does have appellate jurisdiction over final decisions on grievances raising allegations of discrimination. The Commission has no general jurisdiction to review grievance decisions. It only has jurisdiction to review grievance decisions where allegations of discrimination have been raised. The EEOC describes an employee's options in 29 CFR 1614.401(d):

A grievant may appeal the final decision of the agency, the arbitrator or the Federal Labor Relations Authority (FLRA) on the grievance when an issue of employment discrimination was raised in a negotiated grievance procedure that permits such issues to be raised. A grievant may not appeal under this part, however, when the matter initially raised in the negotiated grievance procedure is still ongoing in that process, is in arbitration, is before the FLRA, is appealable to the MSPB or if 5 USC 7121(d) is inapplicable to the involved agency.

See also Grays v. Department of Air Force, 02850026 (1986).

The Commission's jurisdiction on appeal is restricted to review of the discrimination allegations, and it does not have general jurisdiction to review the decision with respect to matters involving contract interpretation or other aspects of labor-management relations. Interestingly, the EEOC was granted an appellate function not shared by the federal courts with respect to the FLRA. Decisions of the FLRA on arbitration awards are final and not subject to judicial review. See 5 USC 7122. When an arbitrator rejects an issue on the basis that it is not subject to arbitration, the Commission may not review that decision. It may only review issues of discrimination on which the arbitrator issued a decision on the merits. See Lucas v. Secretary of Agriculture, 02990016 (2002) (Commission has no jurisdiction to review an arbitrator's decision that a grievance was untimely because it is purely a contractual matter); Solberg v. Attorney General, 02920004 (1993).

When the relief ordered in an arbitration decision is not the result of a violation of Title VII, the Commission will not review a complaint where the remedy is not complete relief. See Brazil v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 02930009 (1993). In that case, the arbitrator found the agency had not engaged in race and sex discrimination under Title VII, but that its underrepresentation of white females violated a provision of the collective bargaining agreement. The FLRA upheld the arbitrator's award, as well as the remedy of priority consideration. The Commission declined to review the issue of the appropriate remedy because its award was based on the collective bargaining agreement and not on a violation of Title VII.

When a grievance does include allegations of prohibited discrimination, it is the final grievance decision that is reviewable by the EEOC. Chavez v. Department of Air Force, 01821748 (1983).

When the FLRA rejects consideration of a grievance award on the basis that the underlying action is not subject to the negotiated grievance procedure, the employee may timely initiate the EEO complaint process by contacting a counselor within 30 days of the FLRA decision. See Gholston v. Veterans Administration, 02850028 (1987). The 30-day period is extended to 45 days under Part 1614. See Chai v. National Aeronautics and Space Administration, 05970016 (1998).

In its final grievance decision, the agency must inform the employee of his right to seek review by the EEOC within 30 days. See 29 CFR 1614.402(a) (1992). In its 1999 amendments to Part 1614, 1614.402(a) omits any reference to final grievance decisions in establishing time limits for appeal. This would appear to simply be an oversight on the part of the Commission. Failure to include a notice of EEOC appeal rights means the decision is not final and effectively tolls the time period. The agency's decision also must make specific findings on the allegations of discrimination or it is not considered final. See Doe v. Defense Mapping Agency, 01831593 (1985).

The right of appeal to the EEOC is one that is particular to the "aggrieved employee" and not the union. The Commission has no jurisdiction to review decisions on grievances filed by unions. Palido v. Department of Air Force, 0285007 (1987).

Although the Commission has jurisdiction to review issues of discrimination in a final grievance decision, it does not have jurisdiction to directly review such a decision when the personnel action is one over which the Merit Systems Protection Board has jurisdiction. In such cases, the grievant must first seek review before the MSPB. See 29 CFR 1614.401(d). After the MSPB has completed its review, the EEOC can accept jurisdiction to review the findings on issues of discrimination. As the Commission explained in Cantrell v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 02920010 (1992):

After a thorough review of the record as a whole, the Commission declines to consider the merits of appellant's allegations of racial discrimination. In reaching this conclusion, we find that appellant was required to file an appeal with the MSPB from the Arbitrator's decision prior to seeking Commission review. Appellant chose to grieve her removal through the agency's negotiated grievance procedure, as she is permitted to do under 5 USC 7121(d). (footnote omitted). This subsection also provides, however, that selection of the negotiated procedure does not prejudice "the right of an aggrieved employee to request the Merit Systems Protection Board to review the final decision pursuant to Section 7702 of this Title in the case of any personnel action that could have been appealed to the Board, or, where applicable, to request the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission to review a final decision in any other matter involving a complaint of discrimination of the type prohibited by any law administered by the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission."

The adverse actions over which the MSPB has jurisdiction are specifically enumerated in 5 USC 7512. See also 5 CFR 1201.3. Pursuant to 5 USC 7512, the MSPB has jurisdiction over removal actions. Both 5 USC 7121(d) and the MSPB's regulations permit an aggrieved employee, who has raised discrimination allegations in the grievance process and received a final decision on the grievance, to request the MSPB's review of the matter. See 5 CFR 1201.3(c)(3) and 1201.154. Moreover, the MSPB has held that it has jurisdiction to review arbitration decisions, where the employee has been affected by a prohibited personnel practice involving discrimination (as set forth in 5 USC 2302(b)(1)) and where the action could have been oriGINAlly appealed to the MSPB pursuant to 5 USC 7702. Ogden Air Logistics Center and AFGE, Local 1592, 6 MSPB 531 (1981). See also Sanders v. Department of the Army, 38 MSPR 675 (1986). Accordingly, the Commission concludes that appellant's case is a "mixed" case, and she could have appealed the Arbitrator's decision on her removal to the MSPB. The Arbitrator's decision must be appealed to the Board before an appeal can be taken to the Commission.

The fact that an appeal to the MSPB is a condition precedent to an appeal to the Commission in "mixed" cases processed in the grievance process was first decided in Helen Marenus v. Department of Health and Human Services, EEOC Appeal No. 02860018 (June 17, 1987). The Commission recently followed the reasoning Marenus in Rosie Rupp v. Louis W. Sullivan, M.D., Secretary, Department of Health and Human Services, EEOC Appeal No. 02910003 (November 30, 1990) and Mitchell Davis v. Michael P.W. Stone, Secretary, Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05900280 (June 26, 1990). These cases, and other cases decided by the MSPB, interpreted Sections 7121(d) and 7702 to provide a statutory and administrative process in which the MSPB must first decide the issue of discrimination and the appealable action. Thereafter the employee may file a petition for review with the Commission. In Davis, we noted that, while EEOC Regulation 29 CFR 1613.231 provides that an employee can appeal a decision of an arbitrator on an issue of employment discrimination to the Commission, this section governs only matters which cannot be appealed to the MSPB, i.e., "non-mixed" matters. Thus, we held that placement of Section 1613.231(b) in Subpart B of the Commission's regulations, rather than Subpart D which governs "mixed" cases was significant. We emphasized then, and reiterate at the present, that if the underlying personnel action at issue is not of the type appealable to the MSPB (a "non-mixed" case), then a complainant may appeal the agency's decision, an arbitration award, or FLRA decision directly to the Commission pursuant to 29 CFR 1613.231(b).

Under the circumstances presented in this case, we find that appellant's appeal has been prematurely filed with this Commission. Appellant is advised that, if she so chooses, she may have the right to file a petition for review with the MSPB. She should file said petition as expeditiously as possible. After the MSPB issues its decision, appellant can then request the Commission to review the MSPB's decision on her allegation of discrimination.

However, in Barber v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, SSA, 02950009 (1997), the Commission held that when the arbitrator bases his decision on procedural aspects of EEO regulations and not on the merits, the proper course is an appeal directly to the EEOC. As the Commission in Barber explained:

The Commission finds that the instant matter differs in a significant respect from the situations that were present in Cantrell, Rupp, Davis, Martinez, and, possibly in Marenus. In the instant matter the Arbitrator did not consider the merits of appellant's claim that she was discriminatorily removed; instead, in the instant matter the Arbitrator interpreted a "labor contract" and EEOC Regulations to determine that the removal issue was not arbitrable because appellant had elected to pursue the proposed removal matter in the EEO complaint process. In Cantrell, Rupp, Davis, and Martinez, however, the Arbitrator in each case had issued a decision on the merits of the removal allegation at issue. Cantrell, EEOC Appeal No. 02920010; Rupp, EEOC Appeal No. 02910003; Davis, EEOC Request No. 05900280; Martinez, EEOC Appeal No. 02900025. In Marenus the Commission did not indicate in its decision whether the Arbitrator had addressed the merits of the removal allegation. Marenus, EEOC Appeal No. 02860018 ("Arbitration was invoked and a decision reached on December 26, 1985").

The Commission finds that the precise initial issue in the instant appeal is whether a matter appealable to the MSPB (removal from federal service) which has been processed through the grievance procedure must first be reviewed by the MSPB before an appeal (i.e., the instant appeal) from an Arbitrator's decision on the procedural aspects of the grievance can be taken by the Commission. The Commission finds that its prior decisions in Cantrell, Rupp, Davis, Martinez, and Marenus, did not directly address the instant issue concerning whether the Commission has the authority to decide an appeal from an Arbitrator's decision concerning the procedural aspects of a grievance of a matter that is appealable to the MSPB.

The practice of the Commission has been to entertain appeals directly from dismissals by agencies under EEOC procedural regulations of allegations contained in what appear to be mixed case complaints. See, e.g., Tucker v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 01953599 (Aug. 22, 1995), request for reconsideration denied, EEOC Request No. 05950928 (Nov. 9, 1995); Reyes v. Department of Defense, EEOC Appeal No. 01951498 (June 1, 1995); Goosens v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01950264 (Mar. 27, 1995). The Commission finds the instant situation analogous to the situations in Tucker, Reyes, and Goosens. Although the instant appeal is from an Arbitrator's decision concerning a grievance, and the appeals in Tucker, Reyes, and Goosens, concerned appeals from final agency decisions concerning EEO complaints, both the instant matter and the situations in Tucker, Reyes, and Goosens raise the issue of whether EEOC procedural regulations have been properly interpreted.

In Tucker, Reyes, and Goosens the Commission considered whether the agency in question correctly applied EEOC procedural regulations in dismissing allegations from what appeared to be mixed case complaints. The Commission finds that the instant matter is sufficiently analogous to the situations in Tucker, Reyes, and Goosens, to conclude that the Commission has the authority to rule on the Arbitrator's application of EEOC procedural regulations in dismissing appellant's grievance of a matter (removal) that could have been first appealed to the MSPB.

The Commission recognizes that 29 CFR 1614.401(c) provides: "A grievant may not appeal under this part, however, when the matter initially raised in the negotiated grievance procedure...is appealable to the MSPB..." We find that 1614.401(c) does not specifically mention appeals by grievants from decisions which were based on an application of EEOC procedural regulations. The Commission finds that the Arbitrator's reliance on EEOC procedural regulations means that the only matter at issue in the instant appeal is within the "ambit of the Commission's responsibilities" to interpret its procedural responsibilities. See generally, Shaw v. Department of the Air Force, EEOC Request No. 05930370 (July 15, 1994) ("Since the termination complaint [apparently a mixed case complaint] was inextricably intertwined with other EEO complaints throughout its processing, and since the sole question [appropriateness of a remedy] is within the ambit of the Commission's responsibilities under antidiscrimination statutes, the Commission has decided that appellant's appeal should be addressed in this forum.") (citing Simon v. United States Postal Serv., EEOC Request No. 05890464 (Sept. 27, 1990)).

The Commission finds that it is the appropriate forum for interpretations of the Commission's procedural regulations. The Commission shall not relinquish jurisdiction over the instant appeal and we find that 1614.401(c) does not relinquish the Commission's jurisdiction for interpreting its procedural regulations. Thus, the Commission shall entertain the instant appeal and determine whether the Arbitrator correctly applied EEOC procedural regulations in determining that appellant had made an election to pursue the EEO complaint process prior to filing the grievance.

The Commission subsequently found the complainant in Barber had made an election to pursue her claim through the EEO process prior to initiating the grievance process.
