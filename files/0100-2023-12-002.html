A. COMPLAINANT'S DUTY TO PROCEED/CHANGE IN REPRESENTATION OR ADDRESS

At all times in the EEO process, the complainant bears the responsibility for proceeding with the complaint, whether or not he or she has designated a representative. 29 CFR 1614.605(e). This includes the duty to keep the agency or the Commission, as appropriate, informed of the complainant's current address. 29 CFR 1614.105(b)(1). This duty implicitly requires the complainant to advise the agency or the Commission of any address changes for his or her representative.

In order to discharge this duty, the complainant or his representative must affirmatively notify the agency of any change of address. In Canizales v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01990647 (2001), the Commission specifically rejected any notion of constructive notice of a change of address. In Canizales, the complainant's appeal was untimely and he argued that neither he nor his attorney received the agency's final decision. The agency produced evidence that it had mailed the decision to the attorney's address of record and that it had been signed for at that address. In response to an argument that the attorney had moved and the agency should have known that because the new address was on the letterhead of correspondence sent to the agency prior to issuance of the decision, the Commission said "simply having a new address appear in a letterhead is not enough to put the agency on notice of the change of address." Id.

Reinforcing the notion that the complainant must be diligent in keeping the agency apprised of any changes in representation, in Yune v. Postmaster General, 01996272 (2001), the Commission found the complainant's formal complaint was untimely filed where she did not notify the agency of a change in attorney until after issuance of the notice of final interview. Although the complaint was filed within 15 days of the time the agency sent the notice of final interview to the complainant's new attorney, it was not filed within 15 days of receipt of the notice by the complainant's attorney of record.

In addition, the Commission held that receipt of a document at the complainant's designated address of record by a member of complainant's family or household of suitable age and discretion creates a rebuttable presumption of receipt. When the complainant uses his work address as the address of record, "receipt by the complainant's agent (such as the agency's mail room) creates the same presumption, and the time limitations period begins to run from the date on which the document was received at the workplace." Wimot v. Secretary of Commerce, 01997154 (2001).
