I. STATEMENTS MADE DURING TESTIMONY

Although it is unclear if the Commission intended to issue a brightline rule, it did, in St. John v. Postmaster General, 0120082828 (2009), finding that the agency retaliated against the complainant when it reported alleged threats that he made during testimony to the Postal Inspection Service and notified him that was being placed on emergency leave. The complainant in that case, while testifying on damages, was asked how the alleged discrimination affected him. He responded that he would have "throttled" the supervisor and stated, "I could kill him."

A Postal Service attorney reported the statements to a manager who called the Postal Inspection Service. The Commission, in St. John, found:

With regard to claim (1), the record reflects that complainant was instructed to meet with the Postal Inspector due to the statements he made during the course of an EEO proceeding. We find that the agency's request for complainant to do so on its face demonstrates a bias against this complainant. The Commission's policy on retaliation prohibits any adverse treatment that is based on a retaliatory motive and is reasonably likely to deter the charging party or others from engaging in a protected activity. See EEOC Compliance Manual Section 8, "Retaliation" No. 915.003 at pp. 8-13 (May 20, 1998). We find that the agency clearly engaged in adverse treatment that is reasonably likely to deter individuals from engaging in protected activity when it instructed complainant to meet with the Postal Investigator regarding his testimony at a hearing before an AJ.

Similarly, with regard to claim (2), we find that the agency's determination to place complainant on EPL due to his testimony in a hearing before an AJ demonstrates and a clear bias against complainant for participating in the EEO process. According to M1's affidavit testimony, the agency had not yet heard from the Postal Investigator but M1 still determined to place complainant on the EPL. As such, we find that the agency subjected complainant to adverse treatment that is reasonably likely to deter individuals from engaging in protected activity. We note that although, according to complainant, he was placed on EPL for only 15 minutes, the agency took an adverse action against complainant when he was notified that he was on EPL. The record is unclear as to when the EPL began; however, complainant provided affidavit testimony that he immediately began to clear out his locker and truck after the conclusion of the meeting. The agency has not offered any evidence to rebut or call into question complainant's testimony. Accordingly, we find that complainant was placed on the EPL immediately upon notification, even if he had not yet clocked out. The duration of the EPL is a matter that is significant only so far as it relates to the amount of compensatory damages that may be awarded.

Further, with regard to both claims, we find there is a direct link to the protected activity and the agency's actions to investigate and ultimately place complainant on EPL since it took action against complainant for the sworn testimony given during a hearing before an AJ. With regard to both claims, the record reflects that the M1 testified that she requested that complainant be investigated because of testimony elicited during a hearing before an AJ.

We find that the agency failed to put forth sufficient evidence to impeach complainant's evidence or present an affirmative defense, We recognize that the agency has a clear no-tolerance policy for threats of violence. Here, however, we find that given the context of the statements, the agency could not have reasonably discerned that complainant posed a threat to S1. We find significant that the incident occurred in September 2004 and complainant was testifying to how those incidents made him feel at that time during a hearing held in March 2007. We find that the agency could not have reasonably believed that complainant's testimony in March 2007 of how he was feeling in September 2004 was a current threat of violence. Furthermore, we find it especially problematic that the agency interpreted complainant's testimony that was given in response to questioning regarding his compensatory damages claim as a present threat against S1. When providing testimony regarding compensatory damages, complainant is expected to provide testimony about his emotional state. Complainant clearly was attempting to convey the degree to which S1's actions angered and affected him and used the terms "throttle" and "kill" as figures of speech. Additionally, we find that the affidavit testimony of M1, OIC, the Postmaster (PM), or the Human Resource Specialist lacks sufficient specificity as to what factors they considered when deciding that complainant should be interviewed by a Postal Inspector and ultimately placed on EPL. As such, we find that the agency failed to impeach or successfully present an affirmative defense for its actions.

In Complainant v. Office of Personnel Management, EEOC Appeal No. 012010146 (2013), the Commission reserved deciding whether Nassar applied in a federal sector case noting that the complainant had not succeeded in demonstrating retaliation even under the lesser standard of proving that retaliation was a substantial or motivating factor. In Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0720120041 (2015), the Commission held that the "but for" test of Nassar does not apply in federal sector cases.
