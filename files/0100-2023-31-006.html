D. FAILURE TO ISSUE FEE DECISION

An agency's failure to issue an expeditious decision on attorney fees may be considered a "concession to the reasonableness of the time expended and fees requested by the appellant." Sotirakos v. Postmaster General, 01883018 (1988). See also Dorathy M. v. Secretary of Transportation, 0120143074 (2016). In the Sotirakos case, the Commission noted that the determination of fees, in the first instance, is the responsibility of the agency. However, the Commission noted that the agency cannot use this as an opportunity for "unilaterally imposed dickering." If the agency and the complainant cannot agree on fees, the agency must issue a decision. However, the Commission cannot invoke the sanction of granting full fees when the appellant files an appeal over the agency's failure to issue a timely decision, but does not submit the original fee petition. See Mason v. Secretary of Interior, 05930678 (1994). In that case, the Commission remanded the case with an order for the agency to issue a final decision within 10 days.

In deciding whether to use the agency's failure to issue an expeditious decision on fees as a concession on the reasonableness of the request, the Commission will apply the doctrine of equitable estoppel. See Johnson v. General Services Administrator, 05910549 (1991). "The Commission, in its discretion, may award the full amount of a request for attorney's fees as a sanction where a federal agency is untimely in issuing a final decision on a fee request...." Id. However, the doctrine of estoppel will not be applied where the final decision was issued only three work days after the deadline, and the attorney failed to fully support his fee request and refused to respond to agency inquiries to provide additional support for the request. Similarly, in McDaniel v. Secretary of Interior, 01971968 (1999), the Commission declined to use the agency's delay in issuing a fee decision as a bar to challenging the reasonableness of the fee. In McDaniel, the Commission found the agency's delay was excusable given the complicated nature of the fee issues:

First, the Commission notes that, on appeal, appellant's attorney has not presented any argument in support of increasing his fee award from the amount set by the agency other than a general assertion that the agency, in essence, waived its right to object to the reasonableness of the hours and costs claimed because it exceeded the time limits for issuing its final decision of his fee petition contained in the Commission's regulations at 29 CFR § 1614.501(e)(2). This regulation provides that if an agreement cannot be reached on the amount of attorney's fees to be paid within 20 days from the agency's receipt of the fee request, the agency shall issue a final decision on the fee request within 30 days of receipt of a verified statement itemizing the attorney's charges with an accompanying affidavit. The agency concedes that it exceeded this time limitation. However, the Commission is persuaded by the agency's assertion that the delay was reasonable in light of the complex nature of considering a fee petition for three consolidated complaints, the agency's need for additional information, and the agency's good faith efforts to negotiate a settlement of this matter with counsel. In light of these factors, the Commission finds this to be the exceptional case where the delay can be excused, and the agency's waiver of its right to challenge the fee petition is not warranted. See Daniels v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01944633 (August 14, 1995), request for reconsideration denied, EEOC Request No. 05950959 (March 20, 1997) (agency, while admonished regarding its failure to process attorney's fee request within the prescribed time limitations, is ordered to consider and render a decision on the request).

The agency must issue a final decision on attorney fees. The agency cannot make an offer to settle the issue of attorney fees and make Commission appeal rights contingent upon rejection of that offer. See Hanson v. Secretary of Navy, 01921953 (1992). The final decision must make a finding on the issue of prevailing party status and entitlement to fees, as well as the amount to be awarded. At some point in time, if the agency refuses to issue a final decision on fees, that refusal may be treated as a denial subject to appeal. See Chan v. Secretary of Navy, 01953004 (1997). In the Chan case, the agency still had not issued a decision three years after submission of the fee request. The Commission, in Chan, did not necessarily suggest that three years is the minimal amount of time to wait. It simply appears that the complainant in Chan did wait that long before filing an appeal. See also Morrow v. Postmaster General, 0120111266 (2012) ("The Commission, in its discretion, may award the full amount of a request for attorney's fees where a federal agency is untimely in issuing a final decision on a fee request made pursuant to the provisions of its regulations.").
