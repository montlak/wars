5. Causation

In order to recover damages, it is not sufficient to merely show that harm occurred. The complainant must prove the harm was caused by the agency's discrimination. As the Commission explained in Fazekas v. Postmaster General, 01954627 (1997):

In support of his claim for non-pecuniary damages, appellant presented evidence indicating that the stress and related conditions were caused not specifically by the agency's actions at issue herein, but by his downgrade following the agency's restructuring. Nothing in the record specifically identifies any injury associated with the warning letters now expunged from his record. In determining damages, the agency is only responsible for those damages that are clearly shown to be caused by the alleged discriminatory conduct. Id.; see also Browne v. Department of Agriculture, EEOC Appeal No. 01944256 (July 17, 1995). The Commission finds that appellant's proof falls far short of the detail necessary to establish an entitlement to non-pecuniary damages.

(Footnote omitted). See also Light v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120123060 (2013) (no evidentiary link between one incident of retaliation and subsequent deterioration in health); Jenkins v. Postmaster General, 0120091289 (2009) (no showing of causal connection between emotional distress claimed and lowered performance rating); Armont v. Secretary of Army, 01970855 (1997) (complainant failed to show that Graves' disease was caused by discriminatory stress at work where there were several other factors that also could have caused the disease); Kinnett v. Secretary of Navy, 01964011 (1997) (complainant could not show that her distress was caused by discrimination, as opposed to previously existing degenerative disc disease, hypertension, and hearing loss). However, such cases must be distinguished from situations in which the discrimination results in additional harm because of preexisting conditions.

There is no requirement that the complainant present medical evidence on the issue of causation. The complainant's testimony, standing alone, may be sufficient to establish causation. See, e.g., Hatton v. Postmaster General, 01985377 (2000) (complainant testified that he was "stunned" by agency's determination that he was physically unfit for duty and stress was exacerbated by agency's persistence in maintaining that position, even in the face of additional medical evidence on his condition).

In McGill v. Secretary of Navy, 01955718 (1998), the Commission found that the complainant failed to establish that the break-up of his marriage was caused by the agency's action because the only evidence submitted was a doctor's "unsupported conclusory statements." Likewise, in Robison-Mathison v. Social Security Administration, 01961574 (1998), the Commission declined to award medical expenses to the complainant based on failure to show causation. The only evidence complainant presented was notes of her therapist reporting sleeplessness, loss of appetite, difficulty concentrating, and tearfulness; there was no statement that these conditions were related to discrimination.

Although the agency is liable for the harm caused when discrimination exacerbates a preexisting condition, see below, the complainant must prove both that the condition worsened and the discrimination caused the deterioration in the condition. It is also possible for a complainant to succeed on a discrimination claim and receive no damages if he cannot demonstrate causation. As the Commission observed in Milder v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01995254 (2000):

The record indicates that complainant, complainant's wife, complainant's daughters, two of complainant's former co-workers in the Police and Security Department, two of complainant's former supervisors in the EMS Section, a former EEO counselor, an employee relations specialist, and the agency attorney who represented the agency at the hearing provided affidavits on the issue of compensatory damages.

In the FAD, the agency denies any award of compensatory damages because complainant failed to prove that his damages were proximately caused by the agency's retaliatory conduct. Specifically, the agency found that complainant's medical/psychological problems antedated December, 1992 (the date of the first incident of discrimination). The agency notes that the only affidavits that date the changes in complainant's personality are those of complainant and his wife. Both individuals state that the emotional problems began before December, 1992 and even as far back as 1991. The agency psychologist compared complainant as he had examined him in 1989 with complainant as he examined him in October, 1992, with a later contact on November 30, 1992. Thus, the agency found that complainant's psychological problems were not caused by its retaliatory actions.

In addition, the agency determined that complainant failed to produce evidence that his pre-existing medical/psychological problems were exacerbated by the denial of sick leave, the progress review or the non-selection. The agency notes that complainant was represented by an attorney who was notified in writing that "if the medical condition existed prior to the discrimination, [complainant] must submit medical or other credible evidence indicating whether or not the condition worsened as a result of the discrimination, the length of time he had the condition prior to the discrimination, and the type of medical treatment received for the condition, and who provided the treatment." The agency also noted that testimony, mainly from complainant and his wife, indicated that complainant suffered from embarrassment and mental distress over not having a job. However, the agency found that the evidence indicates that such embarrassment and distress was the result of complainant's voluntary resignation in November, 1993, rather than any retaliatory action by the agency.

The agency also found that there was no pecuniary loss suffered due to the retaliatory conduct and that complainant's financial and credit problems were the result of his voluntary resignation (not at issue herein). In addition, even if the complainant had successfully proven that his financial difficulties were the proximate cause of the retaliatory conduct of the agency rather than his voluntary resignation, the agency, nevertheless, found that complainant failed to present evidence supporting a specific value of such loss. For example, the complainant failed to present a value of the alleged deterioration of his home or the value of the damaged pipes that were caused by the failure of complainant to afford propane for his central heating.

In Onie R. v. Secretary of Defense, 0120141870 (2016), the Commission required that the complainant, an assistant principal, provide evidence that the agency's actions were the proximate cause of the exacerbation of her preexisting conditions. In a prior decision, the Commission found that comments made by the district superintendent "were reasonably likely to deter the assistant principal or other managers present from personally engaging in the EEO process or keep them from carrying out their obligations to insure vigorous enforcement of the policy for equal employment opportunity." On appeal from the agency's final award of relief, the Commission noted that the complainant had filed other EEO complaints predating the at-issue complaint and implying that whatever discriminatory actions alleged by the complainant in the previous complaints may have also contributed to the exacerbation of her preexisting conditions and her award might be reduced by some untold amount to account for those other agency actions. The Commission in Onie R. wrote:

Here, the majority of Complainant's evidence addresses her pre-existing conditions and her contention that the Agency's reprisal discrimination exacerbated these conditions. Specifically, Complainant provided extensive medical documentation regarding osteoarthritis, hypertension, and morbid obesity, all of which predate the discriminatory act at issue. The record also shows that Complainant acknowledges that her anxiety and stress began as early as 2009, and that she has filed several other EEO complaints alleging that she has been subjected to a hostile work environment which predate the Agency's retaliatory discrimination. Although Complainant contends that the Agency's retaliatory act exacerbated her preexisting medical conditions of osteoarthritis, hypertension, or morbid obesity, we find that she failed to show that the Agency's actions were either the direct or proximate cause of this harm.

It is possible for a complainant to succeed in demonstrating that some of the claimed harm was caused by the agency's discrimination but fail to show other claimed harm was caused by the discrimination. For example, in Harris v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01966746 (1998), the Commission awarded the complainant $2,000 for the low self-esteem attributed to the discrimination, but failed to award damages for a claimed maternal disability. Although the medical evidence was undisputed that the complainant had a difficult pregnancy that required two weeks of leave to avoid a possible miscarriage, there was no evidence connecting the cause of that disability to the discrimination.

Though medical evidence of causation is not required, it generally will be when the complainant is seeking past pecuniary damages for medical treatment that she contends was necessitated by the discrimination. For example, in Light v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120123060 (2013), the complainant sought recovery for $270,000 in past pecuniary damages for medical expenses related to strokes, loss of vision, memory, and weakness and numbness on her left side. Setting aside the fact that most of her evidence was submitted on appeal, the Commission found the link between the discrimination and the claimed harm was too tenuous noting that one medical note indicated that the complainant had had hypertension from the age of 16.
