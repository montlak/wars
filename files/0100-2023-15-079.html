II. SUMMARY JUDGMENT

Under 29 CFR 1614.109(g), administrative judges may issue summary judgment decisions without a hearing, even where the complainant has requested a hearing, if there are no material facts in dispute and the decision can be made as a matter of law. The Commission's regulation is patterned after Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, rule 56, and administrative judges will often look to interpretation of Rule 56 for guidance. Usually, one of the parties will file a motion for summary judgment, the other party will respond, and the administrative judge will issue a ruling. Administrative judges also may determine that a case is likely appropriate for summary judgment, and issue a notice of intent to issue summary judgment. The notice will set forth the standards for summary judgment, and the material facts of the case, and will provide the parties with a time frame to respond. Although summary judgment is technically a prehearing matter, it is addressed separately here because of its significance.

Title 29 CFR 1614.109(g), which was oriGINAlly titled "Decisions without hearing" and was retitled "Summary Judgment" in the Commission's 2012 regulatory revisions provides:

(1) If a party believes that some or all material facts are not in genuine dispute and there is no genuine issue as to credibility, the party may, at least 15 days prior to the date of the hearing or at such earlier time as required by the administrative judge, file a statement with the administrative judge prior to the hearing setting forth the fact or facts and referring to the parts of the record relied on to support the statement. The statement must demonstrate that there is no genuine issue as to any such material fact. The party shall serve the statement on the opposing party.

(2) The opposing party may file an opposition within 15 days of receipt of the statement in paragraph (g)(1) of this section. The opposition may refer to the record in the case to rebut the statement that a fact is not in dispute or may file an affidavit stating that the party cannot, for reasons stated, present facts to oppose the request. After considering the submissions, the administrative judge may order that discovery be permitted on the fact or facts involved, limit the hearing to the issues remaining in dispute, issue findings and conclusions without a hearing or make such other ruling as is appropriate.

(3) If the administrative judge determines upon his or her own initiative that some or all facts are not in genuine dispute, he or she may, after giving notice to the parties and providing them an opportunity to respond in writing within 15 calendar days, issue an order limiting the scope of the hearing or issue findings and conclusions without holding a hearing.

In its notice of proposed rulemaking, 63 Fed. Reg. 8557 (1998), the Commission proposed expanding the summary judgment authority of its administrative judges to encompass cases where material facts were still in genuine dispute. Administrative judges would have been able to issue decisions in those cases, without a hearing, if they determined that there was an adequate report of investigation, the factual issues to be resolved did not involve credibility and the case otherwise lacked merit. The proposal to expand the summary judgment authority of administrative judges was abandoned when the Commission issued its draft notice of final rulemaking in February 1999, and is not part of the amended regulations.

Although the regulations provide that motions for summary judgment must be filed at least 15 days prior to the hearing, most administrative judges expand this time to 30 days in their acknowledgment orders. As a practical matter, the motion for summary judgment should be filed at the earliest opportunity because requests for extensions of time to respond are not unusual and the judge must have some opportunity after all submissions are filed to review those submissions and issue a decision. The caveat here is that a motion for summary judgment cannot be used by an agency as means of precluding the complainant from engaging in discovery. In the first instance, discovery is not automatically stayed by the filing of a summary judgment motion and, in the absence of a stay order issued by the administrative judge, discovery deadlines still must be met. Moreover, a motion for summary judgment can be defeated with a showing by the complainant that discovery is necessary in order to determine if there are material facts in dispute. A general conclusory statement to that effect by the complainant usually will not be sufficient to defeat a summary judgment motion and the complainant must identify with reasonable specificity what the discovery response could disclose that would put a material fact in dispute.

There is nothing improper about an administrative judge simply granting a motion for summary judgment, or adopting portions of a motion in a decision on summary judgment. If a thorough motion is filed, with appropriate citations to the record, and the response fails to adequately refute the motion, the administrative judge's decision may be as simple as a statement that the motion for summary judgment sets forth the relevant facts and applies the correct legal standards, and is therefore granted. The motion for summary judgment and any response to the motion will be attached to the decision. For guidance on drafting effective summary judgment motions, the reader is referred to Ernest C. Hadley and Eleanor J. Laws, Effective Summary Judgment Motions (Dewey Publications, Inc. 2008).

In cases involving intentional discrimination where there is no direct evidence, the Commission has articulated three situations in which summary judgment may be appropriate. The situations were set forth in Bridgeforth v. Secretary of Transportation, 01934222 (1994):

Summary judgment is appropriate when the judge determines that there is no genuine issue as to any material fact, as governed by the applicable substantive law. Anderson v. Liberty Lobby, Inc., 477 U.S. 242, 255 (1986). In a discrimination case, the governing law includes the methods of presumption and shifting burdens of production that the Supreme Court set forth in McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792 (1973) and Texas Dep't of Community Affairs v. Burdine, 450 U.S. 248 (1981). Accordingly, a summary judgment decision is appropriate when the undisputed facts show that: 1) the complainant fails to establish a prima facie case of discrimination, or 2) the employing agency fails to articulate a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its actions, or 3) the complainant fails to establish that the agency's stated reason was a pretext for discrimination. Celotex Corp. v. Catrett, 477 U.S. 317 (1986).

Application of the summary judgment standards by the Commission's judges has generated considerable controversy. Many agency representatives complain that judges seldom grant their motions for summary judgment and hold hearings in cases where there are no material facts in dispute. Many complainants and their representatives counter that administrative judges issue sua sponte notices of an intent to enter summary judgment without explaining to the parties the basis for their determination that it appears there are no material facts in dispute.

The criticisms represent two distinct problems with the Commission's summary judgment procedures. The latter problem is addressed in the Judges' Handbook at 5-1, which now requires that a statement of undisputed material facts must appear in an administrative judge's sua sponte notice of intent to issue a summary decision. If followed, this simple requirement should go a long way toward focusing the responses of the respective parties to a sua sponte notice of intent.

The problem of the frequency with which administrative judges grant agency motions for summary judgment is not as easily corrected, in part, because there are two aspects to the problem. The first aspect of the problem is that agency motions for summary judgment often are deficient. Although the factual basis for summary judgment may exist in the record, agency representatives often do not adequately present that basis in their motions for summary judgment. In other cases, agency representatives do not cure inadequacies in the investigative record by submitting additional evidence with their motions. In those cases, the reality is that it is both easier and safer for the administrative judge to hold a hearing and subsequently issue a decision. It is easier because writing a good summary judgment decision that will be upheld on appeal is a time-consuming task, particularly when the motion for summary judgment does not adequately set forth the factual basis for the judgment with appropriate citation to the record. It is safer because decisions after a hearing are subjected to less scrutiny by OFO than summary judgment decisions. The result is that most judges would rather spend the time holding a hearing and writing a subsequent decision or issuing a bench decision than writing a summary judgment decision. The remedy is for agencies to submit better summary judgment motions.

The second aspect of the problem is that some Commission judges simply lack the confidence to issue summary judgment decisions because of the greater scrutiny applied by OFO. This is a minority of the Commission's judges to be sure. Nonetheless, these few judges waste both agencies' and complainants' time on needless hearings. The remedy here is entirely an internal, i.e., the supervisors of those judges must intervene, if necessary, by ensuring that such practices are reflected in the judges' performance appraisals.

We can do little here for the second aspect of the problem other than to note its existence and, by doing so, hope it hastens a remedy. Hopefully, some assistance in attacking the first aspect of the problem is provided in the ensuing discussion on summary judgment.
