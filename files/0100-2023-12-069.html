9. Independent Consultants and Contractors

The EEOC has no jurisdiction over complaints of discrimination filed against independent consultants and contractors used by agencies. See Watson v. Veterans Administration, 01851413 (1986). Where, for instance, the agency does not exercise control over how the consultant performs services, the relationship is that of an independent consultant. In Watson, the Commission determined it had no jurisdiction over allegations of discrimination in a psychological examination conducted by a psychology association. There was nothing in the record to suggest the agency exerted any control over the association or how the evaluation was conducted.

To determine whether an individual qualifies as employee an adequately developed record should include a copy of the contract between the agency and the contractor and a position description for the at issue position. See Adena J. v. Attorney General, 2021003476 (2021) (nonselection case; finding record not adequately developed on the question of joint liability where the complaint file did not include a position description, the contact for work or notes on the selection process).

It is not, however, an all or nothing proposition. The Commission has long held that it is possible for an agency and a staffing firm, for instance, to be joint employers for liability purposes. Usually, the question in cases involving an independent consultant or contractor is whether an employee who works for that consultant or contractor can be considered an agency employee for purposes of filing a complaint. Whether an individual acts as an independent consultant, or an employee or agent of the agency, is determined by the common law test of control. See, e.g., MacDonald v. Postmaster General, 01A12444 (2002). Control is determined by assessing a number of factors. "Control may be partial and without even a majority of factors shown for there to be sufficient control over the individual to qualify as an employer." Georgia Z. v. Secretary of Army, 2021000668 (2021). The Commission explained in Serita B. v. Secretary of Army, 0120150846 (2016):

We begin our analysis by noting that some agencies have not applied the Commission's longstanding position on the issue of joint employment, a position that the Commission has announced a number of times and in a number of formats, including Compliance Manual chapters, formal enforcement guidance, and federal-sector rulings. We note that the Commission's long-standing position on "joint employers" is found in numerous sources. See, e.g., EEOC Compliance Manual Section 2, "Threshold Issues," Section 2-[II(B)(1)(a)(iii)(b) (May 12, 2000) (Compliance Manual) 4; EEOC Enforcement Guidance: Application of EEO Laws to Contingent Workers Placed by Temporary Employment Agencies and Other Staffing Firms (Dec. 3, 1997) (Enforcement Guidance), "Coverage Issues," Question 2; Ma v. Dep't of Health and Human Servs., EEOC Appeal Nos. 01962389 to 01962390 (May 29, 1998). We reiterate the analysis set forth in those decisions and guidance documents in this decision.

Agencies often conclude that an individual is not an employee based solely on the fact that the individual performs work pursuant to a contract between the federal government and an outside organization and the outside organization, not the federal government, controls the pay and benefits of that individual. See, e.g., Helen G. v. Dep't of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 0120150262 (Feb. 11, 2016); Nicki B. v. Dep't of Educ., EEOC Appeal No. 0120151697 (Feb. 9, 2016). These elements are just two of the factors relevant to joint employment under the Commission's long-standing position and it is not at all surprising that they would be present when an individual working under a federal contract for a federal agency raises a complaint of discrimination.

The term "joint employer" refers to two or more employers that each exercises sufficient control of an individual to qualify as the worker's employer. Compliance Manual, Section 2-III(B)(1)(a)(iii)(b). To determine whether the Agency has the right to exercise sufficient control, EEOC considers factors derived from common law principles of agency. See Enforcement Guidance, "Coverage Issues," at Question 2. EEOC considers, inter alia, the Agency's right to control when, where, and how the worker performs the job; the right to assign additional projects to the worker; whether the work is performed on Agency premises; whether the Agency provides the tools, material, and equipment to perform the job; the duration of the relationship between the Agency and the worker whether the Agency controls the worker's schedule; and whether the Agency can discharge the worker. EEOC Compliance Manual, Section 2-III(A)(1) (citing Nationwide Mut. Ins. Co. v. Darden, 503 U.S. 313, 323-24 (1992)); EEOC v. Skanska USA Bldg., Inc., 550 F. App'x 253, 256 (6th Cir. 2013) ("Entities are joint employers if they 'share or co-determine those matters governing essential terms and conditions of employment'") (quoting Carrier Corp. v. NLRB, 768 F.2d 778, 781 (6th Cir. 1985); see also Ma, EEOC Appeal Nos. 01962389 to 01962390.

The language of the contract between the agency and the staffing firm is not dispositive as to whether a joint-employment situation exists. In determining a worker's status, EEOC looks to what actually occurs in the workplace, even if it contradicts the language in the contract between the staffing firm and the agency. Baker v. Dep't of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01A45313 (Mar. 16, 2006) (while contract between staffing firm and agency provided that contract personnel were employees of staffing firm under its administrative supervision and control, agency actually retained supervisory authority over the contract workers).

On the factor of the right to control when, where, and how the worker performs the job and to assign additional projects, complete agency control is not required; the control may be partial or joint and still point to joint employment. Shorter v. Dep't of Homeland Sec., EEOC Appeal No. 0120131148 (June 11, 2013) (where both staffing firm and agency made assignments, this pointed to joint employment); Complainant v. Dep't of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 0120143162 (May 20, 2015), request for reconsideration denied, EEOC Request No. 0520150430 (Mar. 11, 2016) (where staffing firm wrote and issued complainant's appraisal with input from agency, this pointed toward joint employment). Likewise, where both the agency and staffing firm provided tools, material, and equipment to perform the job, this pointed to joint employment. Elkin v. Dep't of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 0120122211, 2012 WL 5818075 (Nov. 8, 2012). Similarly, where a staffing firm terminates a worker after an agency communicates it no longer wants the worker's services, this supports a finding that the agency has joint or de facto power to discharge the worker. See, e.g., Complainants v. Dep't of Justice, EEOC Appeal Nos. 0120141963 to 0120141762 (Jan. 28, 2015); see also Skanska USA Bldg., Inc., 550 Fed. App'x at 254, 256 (where defendant removed staffing firm's workers from job site without challenge from staffing firm, and after such removals staffing firm generally fired worker, this pointed to joint employment); Butler v. Drive Auto. Indus. of America, Inc., 793 F.3d 404, 414-15 (4th Cir. 2015). The EEOC considers an entity's right to control the terms and conditions of employment, whether or not it exercises that right, as relevant to joint employer status. Enforcement Guidance, "Coverage Issues," at Question 2, Example 5 (where an entity reserve the right to direct the means and manner of an individual's work, but does not generally exercise that: right, the entity may still be found to be a joint employer).

In assessing the right to control, EEOC does not consider any one factor to be decisive and emphasizes that it is not necessary to satisfy a majority of the factors. In particular, the fact that an individual performs work pursuant to a contract between the federal government and an outside organization and is paid and provided with benefits by that organization, on its own, is not enough to show that joint employment does not exist. Rather, the analysis is holistic; all the circumstances in the individual's relationship with the agency should be considered to determine if the agency should be deemed the worker's joint employer. Enforcement Guidance, "Coverage Issues," at Qs. 1 and 2. In sum, a federal agency will qualify as a joint employer of an individual if it has the requisite right to control the means and manner of the individual's work, regardless of whether the individual is paid by an outside organization or is on the federal payroll. See id., at Q. 2.

As discussed in Serita B., no single factor is dispositive. An agency's de facto power to terminate a complainant, however, may evidence sufficient control to find joint employment. See Nakesha T. v. Postmaster General, 2020003723 (2020) ("We find that the Agency had de facto power to terminate Complainant, which it exercised. Millicent H. v. Army, EEOC Appeal No. 0120171215 (June 22, 2017). Given this de facto power, and the absence of any information that the staffing firm made an independent decision to terminate Complainant apart from the Agency prohibiting her from working on Agency premises, we conclude that the Agency had sufficient control over Complainant's employment to be her common law joint employer.")

Note that when a determination is made that an agency exerts sufficient control over a contract employee or consultant to be considered an employer under the anti-discrimination laws, this does not necessarily exempt the contractor from all liability. In such situations, the agency is considered a "joint-employer" with the contractor. However, the question can also arise with regard to the agency's potential liability for the actions of independent consultants and contractors when those actions affect agency employees. We address the latter question first and then turn our attention to the more frequently recurring situation. See also discussion in Chapter 12, subsection on "Harassment by Third Parties."
