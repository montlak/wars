CHAPTER 5

SETTLEMENT OF COMPLAINTS

Settlement of an EEO complaint can come at any juncture of the process. Complaints are settled during the precomplaint stage, complaints are settled while appeals are pending, and complaints are settled at every stage in between. The vast majority of complaints, however, are settled sometime between the filing of the formal complaint and the issuance of a final agency decision. The most likely time for the parties to engage in settlement discussions is after the issuance of the report of investigation but before the hearing is held.

In fact, under Part 1613, immediately after the issuance of the report of investigation and prior to the issuance of the notice of right to a hearing, the parties were first given an opportunity to reach an informal adjustment in the case. If no adjustment was forthcoming, the agency was required to issue a proposed disposition of the case and to inform the complainant of its proposed findings of fact and conclusions of law, including a proposed finding on the ultimate issue of discrimination. The complainant was then either entitled to a final agency decision on the record, or the complainant could first request a hearing before an administrative judge of the EEOC.

The 1992 adoption of Part 1614 streamlined the administrative process. The informal adjustment and proposed disposition stages were eliminated. After receipt of the report of investigation, the complainant may either request a hearing or request a final decision based on the record of investigation.

To a large degree, the Commission has attempted to move the resolution of complaints to much earlier in the process. The 1992 regulation changes encouraged agencies to pursue early alternative dispute resolution (ADR) efforts by extending the precomplaint counseling period when the parties agreed to engage in ADR. The 1999 amendments to Part 1614 go even further. To encourage early settlement of complaints, for the first time the regulations require agencies to implement or have access to an ADR program that may be offered in lieu of traditional EEO counseling. "Alternative Dispute Resolution" is discussed in Chapter 2.

Another change is that the 1999 amendments to the Commission's regulations no longer permit agencies to dismiss a case for failure to accept a certified offer of full relief. Offers of full relief have been replaced by offers of resolution, as discussed below under the heading "Offers of Resolution." Offers of resolution are based on Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 68, which permits so-called "offers of judgment" in civil actions. Under the Commission's regulatory provision for offers of resolution, agencies may make a settlement offer to a complainant, and if the complainant rejects the offer and receives less than what was offered, he or she is not entitled to any attorney fees that accrue following the offer. The regulation is intended to induce agencies to make serious settlement offers by providing a reward in the way of reduced attorney fees and to encourage complainants to give serious consideration to those offers, by penalizing those who decide to gamble and lose. Only time will tell whether the regulation succeeds in attaining these goals. What is certain is that the provision for certified offers of full relief did not succeed. Instead of reducing litigation through settlement, the provision simply shifted the focus of litigation. Agencies and complainants argued over how to properly make an offer of full relief, what constituted full relief, and what were the consequences of rejecting such an offer on the erroneous belief that it was not full relief.

Although the regulations no longer permit certified offers of full relief at the agency processing stage of EEO complaints, MD-110 contains a provision permitting offers of full relief, but the relief must be defined by an administrative judge at the hearing stage. This provision is discussed only briefly in this chapter and covered in depth in Chapter 6 under the subheading "Offers of Full Relief," because the hearing stage is the only point in the process where on offer of full relief can occur.

With all of the emphasis on ADR and offers of resolution in the 1999 amendments, no one should lose sight of the time-tested method of settling cases--negotiation between the parties. Particularly given the number of pro se complainants and relatively inexperienced representatives who appear before the EEOC, there is a need to expand the mechanisms available to the parties to achieve settlement. But those mechanisms should be viewed as an adjunct to, and not a replacement of, the traditional method of settling cases.

I. SETTLEMENT POLICIES

As a matter of policy, the Commission continues to encourage settlement of cases. MD-110 at 12-1. There are many reasons for settling a complaint. A few of the most commonly cited include: that each party will get something they want, as opposed to the win-or-lose situation of a final decision; that both parties save the time, energy and expense of litigation; and that both the agency and employee can focus on the business of the day rather than dwelling on past events. In discrimination cases, there is an additional reason that merits consideration. With the exception of cases involving disparate impact, an employee cannot prevail without questioning the motivation of the RMO. In its simplest form, a discrimination complaint is an allegation that someone has taken an action for a reason upon which society frowns. Where a continuing work relationship exists or the relief may include reinstatement, settlement provides a means of dispute resolution without assigning fault.

From the Commission's viewpoint, there is an additional reason to encourage settlement. The EEOC is not simply charged with remedying discrimination in the workplace. It is also charged with eradicating discrimination in the workplace. The Commission, as a matter of policy, has determined the best way to eradicate discrimination is not by compensating victims and punishing offenders, but to secure voluntary compliance with the antidiscrimination laws. The Commission views settlement as a means of furthering its goal of eradicating discrimination in the workplace. This view may not have a direct effect on the parties in any particular case, but it strongly affects the Commission's practices in dealing with those cases.

Over the years, the Commission has tried various methods of encouraging settlement. The former 29 CFR 1613.217 required that the agency provide an opportunity for informal adjustment of a complaint after the complainant had a chance to review the investigative report. In reality, informal adjustment was a misnomer. "Adjustment," as used in this context, meant settlement.

Any informal adjustment was required to be reduced to writing and signed by the parties. The agency was not free to effect an informal adjustment unilaterally. See Edwards v. USPS, 01830784 (1983); Esters v. USPS, 01831707 (1983). On the other hand, the agency was not required to offer an informal adjustment. See Allen v. Postmaster General, 05900568 (1990). The complainant was not deprived of the opportunity for informal adjustment because the agency believed its action was justified and did not offer an informal resolution.

Generally, an agency's offer of settlement was expected to precede, and not accompany, the proposed disposition of the case. Brill v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01910547 (1991). The problem with having an offer of settlement accompany a proposed disposition was more perceptual than procedural or substantive. If the proposed disposition included a finding of no discrimination, the perception would likely be that the agency was using the proposed disposition to force settlement of the case.

The required informal adjustment opportunity was a clumsy attempt by the Commission to encourage settlement. It required the parties to at least go through the motions of considering settlement. But because the Commission could neither order an agency to offer resolution nor order a complainant to accept an offer, the informal adjustment opportunity simply served as one more procedural hurdle and did not bring about any great increase in the settlement of EEO complaints.

As noted above, the informal adjustment opportunity yielded to the certified offer of full relief and an increased emphasis on ADR. Offers of full relief now yield to offers of resolution and an even greater emphasis on ADR. In addition to the regulatory changes, over the years the Commission has increased the use of neutral settlement judges and training of judges in settlement techniques.

The Commission's current regulation reflecting its settlement policy, 29 CFR 1614.603, simply provides:

Each agency shall make reasonable efforts to voluntarily settle complaints of discrimination as early as possible in, and throughout, the administrative processing of complaints, including the pre-complaint counseling stage. Any settlement reached shall be in writing and signed by both parties and shall identify the allegations resolved.

Summarized above and discussed in detail below are some of the ways the Commission tries to advance that policy.

However, there is another reason not stated by the Commission, for encouraging settlement of cases. In fiscal year 1999, over 26,000 formal complaints were filed against agencies and the Commission received over 12,000 requests for hearing and over 8,000 appeals. With the 1999 regulation amendment, discussed in Chapters 6 and 7, that eliminates the recommended status of the decisions of EEOC administrative judges, there is the expectation that the number of requests for hearings will increase, at least in the short run. Commission judges had large backlogs of cases under the previous system. As a result, the Commission needs to find ways to resolve cases, short of holding lengthy hearings and the resulting commitment of the judge's time in issuing a written decision. Cases that settle do not go to hearing. And, if everyone abides by the settlement, they do not go to OFO on appeal. In short, settlement may advance the Commission's goal of eradicating discrimination in the workplace, but it also is a way of dealing with the practical problem of handling a large number of cases with limited resources.
