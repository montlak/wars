A. THE RULES OF AGENCY: THE MASTER-SERVANT RELATIONSHIP

In order to properly understand the development and resolution of the rules of liability in sexual harassment cases under Title VII, it is necessary to first understand the rules of agency as applied to employers. The basic rule is set forth in Restatement (2d) of Agency, 219, which provides:

(1) A master is subject to liability for the torts of his servants committed while acting within the scope of their employment.

This is the rule of respondeat superior and sometimes referred to as "strict liability."

The converse of the rule also is generally true. However, there are exceptions. Restatement (2d) of Agency, 219(2), provides:

A master is not subject to liability for the torts of his servants acting outside the scope of their employment, unless:

(a) the master intended the conduct or the consequences, or

(b) the master was negligent or reckless, or

(c) the conduct violated a non-delegable duty of the master, or

(d) the servant purported to act or to speak on behalf of the principal and there was reliance upon apparent authority, or he was aided in accomplishing the torts by the existence of the agency relation.

The courts soon reached a consensus on liability of employers in quid pro quo harassment cases, though the analyses were varied. The presence of a concrete employment action led to the conclusion that an employer would be held liable for quid pro quo harassment. Some courts employed various rationales in finding that the acts of harassment committed were within the scope of a supervisor's employment and, as a result, the employer was strictly liable. Other courts reasoned that supervisors accomplished their harassment through apparent authority and, as a result, the employer was liable. Still other courts found that supervisors were aided in the agency relationship in carrying out their harassment. There was far less consensus when hostile environment harassment was involved.

Setting aside the issues of whether acts of harassment actually occurred and whether those acts were unwelcome, which are factual defenses in their own right, two defenses to liability emerged for claims of hostile environment harassment. The agency could attempt to demonstrate that the conduct was not severe or pervasive enough to create a hostile environment or the agency could attempt to demonstrate that it acted promptly and effectively to eradicate the conduct when it was brought to its attention. The first defense was a legal defense to whether sexual harassment had actually occurred. The latter was a legal defense to the issue of liability. Of course, nothing prevented an agency from demonstrating more than one defense, as the Commission observed in Swartzel v. National Security Agency, 01941948 (1995):

The Commission finds that appellant was unable to establish a prima facie case of hostile environment sexual harassment. Record evidence shows that some of the male employees made a number of sexist remarks, e.g., referring to appellant as "the new girl," "honey," or "dear," referring to the work area as the Supervisor's "harem," and using the term "broad" to describe a female. Record evidence further showed that after a female employee announced an assignment for her Human Sexuality Course, appellant's co-workers held a discussion in the office regarding various terminology for the sexual organs. Even considered in the aggregate, the Commission finds that the incidents of which appellant complained are insufficient for us to conclude that they affected a term or condition of employment and/or had the purpose or effect of unreasonably interfering with the work environment and/or created an intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment. In this regard, we note that none of the other employees with whom appellant worked complained of a hostile environment. In addition, record evidence suggests that appellant herself at times engaged in the conduct of which she complained, e.g., making sexual innuendos. Finally, the record indicates that agency officials took appellant's allegations seriously when she reported them and took prompt and appropriate corrective measures.
