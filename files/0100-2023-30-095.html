IV. RELIEF UNDER THE ADEA AND EPA

The Age Discrimination in Employment Act is a "hybrid" statute; that is, it borrows from two statutes--Title VII and the Fair Labor Standards Act. To the extent that the ADEA prohibits discrimination against workers 40 years of age or older, it borrows conceptually from Title VII. However, unlike the Rehabilitation Act which specifically adopts the Title VII remedial scheme, the ADEA incorporates the remedies available under the FLSA. The result is that some remedies available to complainants under Title VII are not available to ADEA complaints and, conversely, ADEA complainants are entitled to at least one remedy that is not available to Title VII complainants, though the remedy is not available to federal sector complainants in the administrative process.

The Equal Pay Act is not a hybrid statute. OriGINAlly passed in 1963, it predates Title VII and was an amendment to the FLSA. See 29 USC 206(d). Because it is an amendment to the FLSA, it incorporates the remedies of that statute.

In many ways, the remedial schemes under Title VII and the FLSA are not substantially different. Both statutes authorize legal and equitable remedies to victims of discrimination. See, e.g., 29 USC 626(c) (authorizing legal and equitable remedies in age discrimination cases). As a result, remedies such as reinstatement, back pay, and adjustments to other employment benefits are appropriate relief under the ADEA and the EPA.

The main difference in the remedial schemes come in the form of "liquidated damages." The ADEA was not amended by the Civil Rights Act of 1991. Compensatory damages are not available under the ADEA. However, an ADEA or EPA complainant can get an award of liquidated damages in some cases. It is worth noting that an EPA complainant can be eligible for an award of compensatory damages not because the 1991 Civil Rights Act amended the EPA, but because the Commission has held that every EPA violation is also a violation of Title VII. See, e.g., Jones v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 01994717 (2000). Of course, the complainant is required to establish that the agency's actions constituted intentional discrimination under Title VII to be eligible for a compensatory damages award.

As noted above, the Commission has repeatedly stated that a violation of the EPA also constitutes a violation of Title VII and both statutes provide for back pay. However, the complainant cannot recover back pay under both statutes, but is entitled to the highest recovery under either statute. In many cases it does not make a difference in terms of the amount of back pay but it can because the EPA permits recovery of back pay up to two years prior to the filing of the complaint. As the Commission explained in Gervais v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0720070063 (2009):

Complainant is entitled to back pay under the EPA and Title VII, but not both; he is entitled to the "highest benefit" which either statute provides. 29 C.F.R. § 1620.27(b). Under Title VII, back pay is usually awarded from the date of the violation, while under the EPA, a complainant is generally entitled to back pay from two years previous to the date of the complaint. In this case, moreover, because we have found that the agency's actions constituted a willful violation of the EPA, complainant is entitled to back pay from three years prior to the date he filed his complaint, or February 24, 2003, and an equal amount in liquidated damages. Finally, complainant is also entitled to all other remedies under the EPA and Title VII.
