broida eeo Chapter 17: Reprisal Chapter 17: Reprisal, Subchapter 02: Protected Activity--Participation Clause, Section C: Agency Representatives

C. AGENCY REPRESENTATIVES

The fact that an agency representative has filed an EEO complaint does not serve as a basis for disqualifying the representative from continuing to represent the agency in EEO complaints filed by others. Moreover, if the agency removes the representative from his or her duties of representing the agency in EEO complaints, this constitutes retaliation.

In Monroig v. Commission on Civil Rights, 07A10012 (2002), the agency stripped the complainant of her EEO representational duties after she filed an EEO complaint. In addition to the fact that the Commission found that the agency had not raised disruption of her representational duties as a legitimate reason at the hearing, it also went on to note in Monroig:

Furthermore, the agency argues that the filing of her complaint was so disruptive that it rendered complainant ineffective for the job, and thus was not protected activity. Most significantly, we note that "the anti-discrimination statutes do not limit or condition in any way the protection against retaliation for participating in the charge process." EEOC Compliance Manual, No. 915.002 (May 20, 1998). Rather, the "reasonableness" standard, where the Commission balances the rights of individuals versus the rights of the employer to retain a stable work force applies to the opposition clause. See id. We do not agree with the agency's argument which implies that in-house counsel lose all rights to oppose discrimination or otherwise participate in the EEO process simply because they are in-house counsel. See Kachmar v. Sungard Data Systems, Inc., 109 F.3d 173, 179 (3rd Cir. 1997) (holding in-house counsel's duty of confidentiality was not a bar to retaliation claim). Nonetheless, complainant's situation can be distinguished from the federal case law cited by the agency in that complainant neither disclosed client confidences, nor did she solicit others to join in her lawsuit. The act of filing a complaint without more, does not "so interfere with [complainant's] performance of [her] job that it renders [her] ineffective in the position for which [she] was employed." Verney v. Pennsylvania Turnpike Commission, 903 F. Supp. 826, 832 (M.D. Pa. 1995), quoting Rosser v. Laborer's Int'l Union, 616 F.2d 221, 223 (5th Cir.), cert. denied, 449 U.S. 886, 101 S. Ct. 241, 66 L. Ed.2d 112 (1980) (employee's discharge following unsuccessful attempt to defeat immediate supervisor in election not protected activity and served as legitimate nondiscriminatory reason for discharge). We disagree with the agency's suggestion that the act of filing a complaint by in-house counsel is not protected activity. "Such a determination would remove in-house counsel from the protection afforded by Title VII." Verney v. Pennsylvania Turnpike Commission, 903 F. Supp. at 832.

We disagree with the agency's argument that complainant's April 18, 1995 memo does not constitute protected activity, and therefore, even if it was the triggering event for complainant's detail to the PAU, such detail was not retaliatory. We first note that complainant already had engaged in prior EEO activity, of which the agency was aware. Evidence of a retaliatory motive has already been shown. Nonetheless, we agree that the memo served to put the reader on notice that complainant opposed what she believed would have a chilling effect on others participating in the EEO process. See Torrez v. Social Security Administration, EEOC Request No. 05950947 (March 10, 1998) (complaint alleging remarks made by supervisor interfered with the EEO process and would have a chilling effect on other's participation stated a claim).

The EEOC has been resistant to most claims that an agency representative's actions constitute retaliation, but in Complainant v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120132859 (2014), the Commission found an actionable claim where the complainant alleged physical intimidation:

The Agency's citations to our previous decisions are unavailing. In Colonna [v. Secretary of Army, 01960347 (1996)], the Commission affirmed dismissal of a claim that "the agency's Staff Judge Advocate 'cross-examined' [complainant] before the Commanding General and encouraged the Commanding General to take certain disciplinary action against [complainant]." In that case, the Commission agreed with the Agency's argument that the attorney's conduct fell within attorney-client privilege, and the complaint constituted a collateral attack upon that privilege.

In addition to Colonna, the Agency offered several supporting cases. In Appleby v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 01933897 (March 4, 1994), the complainant alleged that the agency submitted false answers to several interrogatory questions. A complainant alleged that the agency's attorney made several statements in connection with the validity of his claim and instructed a witness not to talk to a complainant. Steinberg v. Federal Deposit Insurance Commission, EEOC Appeal No. 01950645 (May 14, 1997). In Clark v. Department of Transportation, EEOC Appeal No. 01986968 (October 6, 1999), the complainant alleged that the mere assignment of an agency attorney to her case, the attorney's requests for depositions, and the attorney's conduct in mailing a certified letter to complainant were discriminatory. Finally, the Agency cites a case where a complainant made several allegations concerning the processing of his EEO complaints in Ming-Chiang v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 01986508 (August 31, 2001). In none of these cases did the Commission dismiss the complaint on the grounds the agency's attorney was immune from prosecution.

In this case, Complainant is not alleging the Agency improperly processed his claim or that the Agency attorney submitted false information, or that his conduct in defending the Agency was improper. Complainant is alleging that Attorney engaged in physical conduct that was reasonably calculated to intimidate him. As Complainant has alleged the Agency attorney engaged in this conduct in reprisal for his prior EEO conduct, the matter states a claim.

By contrast, in Complainant v. Social Security Administration, 0120141020 (2014), the Commission found that an "absolute litigation privilege" covered information that an agency representative provided to the administrative judge regarding the complainant:

On June 22, 2013, Complainant filed a formal complaint alleging that the Agency subjected her to discrimination on the basis of reprisal for prior protected EEO activity (representation of other employees in EEO matters) when, on March 11, 2013, information regarding Complainant's professional disciplinary record was disclosed by the Agency's Assistant Regional Counsel (ARC) to an EEOC Administrative Judge (AJ) without Complainant's consent.

...

Complainant has raised a claim of unlawful retaliation based on the disclosures made by the ARC to an AJ during the processing of another employee's EEO complaint. We note that Complainant served as the representative for a witness during the EEO hearing in that complaint. As such, the alleged reprisal occurred in her capacity as a representative and not in her status as an employee with the Agency. As such, we note that Complainant has not shown that she has suffered any harm or loss with respect to a term, condition or privilege of employment. See v. Social Security Administration, EEOC Appeal No. 0120120067 (Mar. 21, 2012).

Furthermore, Complainant argued on appeal that the Agency's action would reasonably likely to deter Complainant or others from serving as representatives. However, Commission finds that complainant cannot raise such a claim based upon an absolute litigation privilege. An absolute privilege is provided for statements made as part of a judicial or administrative proceeding. See McBride v. Dep't of Justice, EEOC Appeal No. 01A00010 (Jan. 16, 2001) citing Cruey v. Gannett Co., 64 Cal. App. 4th 356, 368 (Cal. Ct. App. 1984); see also Hinds v. Magna Fabrics, Inc., 1997 WL 309378 (S.D.N.Y. June 9, 1997). Based upon this absolute privilege, we find that Complainant cannot raise such a claim based on information provided to the AJ during the EEOC's hearing process. Therefore, the Commission finds that the Agency's dismissal of the instant complaint was proper.
