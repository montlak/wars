J. SCHEDULING OF HEARING

The former 29 CFR 1613.218(b) required the administrative judge to schedule the hearing at a time and place that was convenient for both parties. There is no counterpart to this provision in Part 1614, but scheduling the hearing is part of the inherent powers of the administrative judge to regulate the conduct of the hearing. 29 CFR 1614.109(e).

Under 29 CFR 1614.109(i), the administrative judge is required to hold a hearing and issue a decision within 180 days. The administrative judge has discretion to extend the 180-day period when good cause exists. The regulation does not, however, give the administrative judge the discretion to cancel a hearing because it cannot be held within the 180-day period. As the Commission explained in Porter v. Secretary of Navy, 01940530 (1994):

"Unless the administrative judge makes a written determination that good cause exists for extending the time for issuing findings of fact and conclusions of law, within 180 days of a request for a hearing being received by EEOC, an administrative judge shall issue findings of fact and conclusions of law on the merits of the complaint...." 29 CFR § 1614.109(g). While this regulation does not expressly state that a hearing must be held within 180 days, such a requirement is implicit. However, the regulation plainly grants the AJ the discretion to extend that period of time by "mak[ing] a written determination that good cause exists...."

Neither the language of this regulation, nor the comments to the regulations when proposed, in any way suggests that an AJ should remand a case for a FAD without a hearing simply because the case cannot be scheduled for hearing within 180 days of the hearing request being received. The regulation plainly states that, "[u]nless the administrative judge makes a written determination that good cause exists for extending the time," the AJ will issue findings of fact and conclusions of law within 180 days. The regulation does not prohibit the extension of time; it merely places an affirmative obligation on the AJ to record a determination of good cause for the extension of time.

This interpretation of section 1614.109(g) is consistent with guidance found in the Administrative Judge's Handbook (AJH). With regard to scheduling a hearing, the AJH recommends that an AJ schedule the prehearing conference and the hearing without first consulting the parties "to avoid the time-consuming process of coordinating the schedules of the principles." EEOC Order No. 960.003, App. A, Sec. 300(3)(a). Under the heading "Requests for Postponement," the AJH recommends that the parties be informed that postponements normally will not be granted unless requested in writing within seven days of receiving the scheduling letter. The AJH further recommends that parties be informed that a request for postponement must be accompanied by an adequate explanation for the request and a list of alternative dates. Finally, the AJH provides, in relevant part:

Requests for postponements should be granted sparingly and only for good reason. The AJ should avoid being arbitrary in granting or denying requests for postponement....

Only in extreme unusual circumstances should postponement in excess of 30 days be granted. EEOC Order No. 960.003, App. A, Sec. 300(3)(b).

As is the customary practice, the AJ in this case set the dates for the prehearing conference and hearing without consulting the parties. It appears that AR promptly notified the AJ of her (AR's) scheduling conflicts, and requested a change of the dates. AR did not suggest particular alternate dates, nor had she been instructed to do so in the Acknowledgement Order; she did, however, provide the AJ with a copy of her (AR's) schedule for the period April through July 1993. The last exchange between AR and the AJ prior to the remand was that the AJ suggested the week of July 6, 1993, for the hearing; AR informed the AJ that she had just scheduled another matter for hearing that week, and suggested the alternative of the week of July 12, 1993, a continuance of one week which still would have allowed the case to be heard within 180 days. It may be that the proposed alternative week is the week that conflicted with the agency witness' schedule; as noted, the record contains no evidence on this point. Without any evident consideration of whether good cause existed to extend the time beyond 180 days, the AJ remanded the case for an immediate FAD, thereby depriving appellant of his right to a hearing. The Commission finds this action of the AJ to be an abuse of discretion.

The Commission notes the following circumstances in support of its finding: (1) attempts to set a hearing date were ongoing, and AR kept the AJ apprised of her (AR's) other commitments; (2) AR had just proffered an alternative date for the hearing, which would fall within the 180-day period; (3) only 71 days of the 180-day period had elapsed, leaving more than three months in which scheduling conflicts might be resolved and a hearing conducted; and (4) on the date on which the AJ remanded the case to the agency, there existed an unresolved conflict of interest which would have prevented the case from going forward in any event; to wit, the agency's representative was the supervisor of an agreed-upon agency witness who was expected to testify about actions taken within the scope of her official duties.

The Commission notes that the agency witness involved in the apparent conflict of interest is the same witness whose schedule reportedly conflicted with that of AR, preventing the hearing from being scheduled; further, unlike the case of AR, the record contains no evidence to substantiate the unavailability of this agency witness on any particular date. Given these circumstances, the Commission finds somewhat suspect the agency witness' reported unavailability.

Considering the totality of circumstances in this case, the Commission is persuaded that the AJ abused her discretion by remanding the case for a FAD without a hearing simply because she believed the hearing could not be scheduled to occur within 180 days of receipt of the hearing request. Therefore, the Commission will vacate the FAD, and remand the case for a hearing on the merits in accordance to 29 CFR § 1614.109.

However, the parties must be reasonably diligent in making themselves available. The administrative judge does not abuse her discretion in remanding a case to the agency for a decision without a hearing when the employee requests that a rescheduled hearing be held in abeyance indefinitely. Gwozdz v. USPS, 01831070 (1985).

As noted above, requests for a continuance of the hearing will be granted only on a showing of good cause. The parties should make every effort to proceed with the hearing on the schedule set by the judge. Especially when the judge is required to travel for the hearing, continuances likely will result in lengthy delays of the hearing and, ultimately, the decision on the case. It is error for the judge to change the hearing date, upon the request of the agency, without giving the complainant or his representative an opportunity to respond. In re Johnson, 05830358 (1986).
