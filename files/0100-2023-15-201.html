7. Directed Verdict

The Commission does not strictly follow the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure or the Federal Rules of Evidence. The administrative judge does, however, apply those rules as appropriate to EEOC hearings. However, in Collazo v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 01A45903 (2006), the Commission found that the judge abused his discretion in applying the rule governing directed verdicts in civil jury trials. In that case, the complainant and the agency sought the same witnesses, but the complainant's only listed witness was herself. The judge, prior to the start of the hearing, ruled that the complainant should testify and then rest her case. He also ruled that the agency would then present its case and that the complainant would be given the opportunity to cross--examine agency witnesses. However, at the conclusion of the complainant's testimony, the judge indicated he believed a directed verdict in favor of the agency was appropriate and ordered the agency to file such a motion, which he subsequently granted finding no discrimination.

In reversing the judge in Collazo, the Commission found:

We first note that, although the AJ and complainant used the term "directed verdict" to describe the determination of this case, the agency in substance moved for a judgment on partial findings under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure. Directed verdicts apply only in civil jury trials.

...

Fed. R. Civ. P. 52(c). There is no EEOC regulation addressing judgments on partial findings. However, while EEO hearings are governed by EEO Regulations, an AJ may in his discretion appropriately apply the principles governing judgments to partial findings to EEO cases.

In this case, the AJ set forth the framework for the hearing before beginning the hearing. The hearing transcript clearly reflects the AJ's explicit assurances that complainant would be given the opportunity to examine the parties' shared witnesses after the agency examined them. We further note that the AJ convinced complainant that this arrangement was appropriate because complainant would not have to lay foundations for evidence and cross-examination would be an easier task for a non-attorney. Complainant reasonably relied upon the AJ's assurances that this arrangement would govern the hearing and gave her testimony with the expectation that she would be given the opportunity to develop her case further upon examination of the witnesses. Upon review of the AJ's actions, we find that the AJ's issuance of judgment on partial findings after complainant testified was manifestly unfair to complainant.

While an AJ has broad discretion to limit the scope of a hearing or issue a judgment on partial findings, he must always do so in a manner that gives complainant a fair and reasonable opportunity to supplement the record and examine witnesses. See Equal Employment Opportunity Management Directive for 29 C.F.R. Part 1614 (EEO MD-110), 7-1 (November 9, 1999); see also 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109. In this case, the AJ failed to adhere to the hearing framework that the parties understood would govern the hearing proceedings, depriving complainant of the opportunity to develop her case through the examination of the parties' shared witnesses. Moreover, Rule 52(c) states that judgments on partial findings can only be issued against a party after that party has been fully heard.

In this case, complainant was not given the opportunity to put forth any witnesses besides herself. Complainant's testimony and the record of investigation are not complainant's full case. The AJ's failure to allow complainant to examine her approved witnesses denied her the opportunity to present her full case. The agency characterizes the AJ's actions as merely limiting complainant's witnesses, but the AJ approved complainant's requested witnesses for testimony at the beginning of the hearing. We conclude that issuing a judgment on partial findings under these particular circumstances risks undermining the integrity of the hearing process by casting doubt on the reliability of an AJ's assurances and contravenes the standards for issuing judgments on partial findings. Therefore, we find that the appropriate remedy is to remand complainant's complaint to the AJ for a hearing with the testimony of the relevant witnesses.

Finally, we note that the agency argues that claim 5 was initiated by untimely EEO Counselor contact. However, the record is unclear about the actual date of this alleged matter, and we determine that in the interest of judicial efficiency, complainant's complaint should be remanded intact without fragmenting its component claims. The agency further argues that complainant waived her right to object to the AJ's decision because she did not object to it during the hearing and cites a line of federal cases imposing a duty on plaintiffs to raise such objections during the hearing. However, we note that while the federal courts impose a duty on parties to raise objections during the hearing, the Commission may exercise its discretion to review and address all matters in the record on appeal.
