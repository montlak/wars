8. Essential Job Functions and Production Standards

An agency is not required to lower production standards for essential job functions. In Ramos v. Postmaster General, 01A44705 (2006), the complainant contended that he could perform the essential functions of a letter carrier position if the agency permitted him to use the "dismount" method of delivery, instead of a "park and loop" method that required more walking. In finding that the complainant was not qualified for the position, the Commission, in Ramos, noted:

In finding that complainant is not qualified for his position, we note that complainant states that he could do more of the required work if only the agency would allow him to use the dismount method where normally the "park and loop" method would be used. The agency has argued, however, and complainant has not disputed, that using the "park and loop" method, instead of the dismount method, enables carriers to be more productive in getting mail delivered. We find that the agency is not required to allow complainant to use the dismount method instead of "park and loop" when doing so would result in lowered productivity. See 29 C.F.R. pt. 1630, App. § 1630.2(n) ("It is important to note that the inquiry into essential functions is not intended to second guess an employer's business judgment with regard to production standards, whether qualitative or quantitative, nor to require employers to lower such standards."); EEOC Enforcement Guidance on Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under the Americans with Disabilities Act, Notice No. 915.002 (October 17, 2002)., at "General Principles" ("Nor is an employer required to lower production standards whether qualitative or quantitative that are applied uniformly to employees with and without disabilities."). Therefore, even assuming that "park and loop" is a mere method of performing the essential job function of delivering mail (as opposed to an essential function in and of itself) as complainant contends, we find that substantial evidence of record supports the finding that complainant is not qualified for the PTF City Carrier position, in part because for 45% of the deliveries, he is unable utilize the delivery method which would yield the highest productivity.

While an agency is not obligated to lower production standards, it may be required to modify the manner in which the duties are assigned, as the Commission in Michelle G. v. Secretary of Treasury, 0120132463 (2016), explained:

Complainant asked to be assigned fewer cases at one time and to receive new cases after she turned in completed work. S3 stated in the response to Complainant's reasonable-accommodation request that the Agency was not required to lower production standards or reduce inventory and that Complainant would receive a full case load. Complainant, however, did not ask for a lower production standard. Instead, she asked for an adjustment in the manner in which cases were assigned. Although the Agency does not have to lower a uniformly applied production standard as a reasonable accommodation, it does have to provide a reasonable accommodation that enables an employee to meet a production standard when doing so would not result in an undue hardship. See Enforcement Guidance on Reasonable Accommodation (General Principles). The Agency has not shown that it would have been an undue hardship to adjust the manner in which cases were assigned to Complainant by assigning fewer cases at once.

We note that S2 stated that she reduced Complainant's inventory to 10 cases and that Complainant stated that the Agency lowered her inventory for a short time but subsequently raised it. To the extent that the Agency only temporarily granted Complainant's request to have fewer cases in her inventory and to receive new cases after she completed other cases, the Agency has not shown that it would have been an undue hardship to continue this accommodation.

Kenneth W. v. SSA, 0120140286 (2016), the Commission rejected a failure to accommodate argument where the complainant, a probationary attorney-adviser, had never met the production standards of the position despite the agency's attempts to accommodate him by screening his cases and assigning him less complex matters. The complainant, who had suffered a traumatic brain injury, was always allowed to select his workspace to ensure a "distraction-free environment" where even the complainant admitted he was not distracted and the two coworkers in his vicinity assisted him in his work. The agency denied, however, his request to work time and a half to complete his work, noting that "achieving business results in a timely manner was an essential function of his position." With respect to his request to telework, the Commission, in Kenneth W., noted:

There is no evidence in the record to support the contention that the Agency failed to engage Complainant in an interactive process regarding his request to telework. The record reflects that Complainant was a probationary employee that had yet to achieve production requirements, and was therefore ineligible for telework. Complainant began the process of applying for an exception to the telework guidelines, but had not completed the necessary paperwork, or supplied the necessary documentation, before he was terminated as a result of poor performance.

Because he did not establish that he was a qualified individual with a disability, Complainant was not entitled to an accommodation.

In a bit of a twist on the usual set of facts, instead of requesting a decrease in workload, the complainant in Emma B. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120140064 (2016), alleged a hostile work environment and disparate treatment when she was told that the agency intended to add duties to her workload. Though she did not allege disability discrimination in her complaint, according to the Commission, she did request, as a reasonable accommodation, that her workload not be increased. The complainant noted "that she did not feel that her condition was presently interfering with her performance," "her condition 'does not restrict anything that I am doing right now. My current workload and my medication and my ability...are not impaired at this time.'" She explained that if her workload was to increase, her ability to perform her job would be impaired. The agency dismissed this claim, along with others, concluding that the alleged conduct was not sufficiently severe or pervasive enough to sustain a claim under Title VII, and the Commission ultimately affirmed the dismissal on the same grounds, but not before also discussing the standards under the Rehabilitation Act regarding reasonable accommodation and production standards.
