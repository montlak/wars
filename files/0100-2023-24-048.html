1. Reasonable Documentation

Reasonable medical documentation is also discussed in Chapter 14 under the heading "Medical Evidence." To summarize, in the context of an individualized inquiry, an agency may request "reasonable documentation" from the employee on the nature of his or her disability and the need for accommodation. Reasonable documentation, as the term suggests, is defined by the nature of the disability, its effects on the employee's major life activities and the position in question.

The Commission's Enforcement Guidance: Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under the ADA, "Requesting Reasonable Accommodation" at Question 6, provides:

When the disability and/or the need for accommodation is not obvious, the employer may ask the individual for reasonable documentation about his/her disability and functional limitations. The employer is entitled to know that the individual has a covered disability for which s/he needs a reasonable accommodation.

Reasonable documentation means that the employer may require only the documentation that is needed to establish that a person has an ADA disability, and that the disability necessitates a reasonable accommodation. Thus, an employer, in response to a request for reasonable accommodation, cannot ask for documentation that is unrelated to determining the existence of a disability and the necessity for an accommodation. This means that in most situations an employer cannot request a person's complete medical records because they are likely to contain information unrelated to the disability at issue and the need for accommodation. If an individual has more than one disability, an employer can request information pertaining only to the disability that requires a reasonable accommodation.

While not always necessary, reasonable documentation from a healthcare provider connecting the disabling condition to the accommodation request is helpful and can expedite the agency's accommodation assessments. This is especially where the need for an accommodation is not obvious. For instance, a list of medications, a Veterans' Affairs letter indicating the complainant's disability rating, and the complainant's "own list of diagnoses/impairments and their impact" did not "clearly establish" that an accommodation was needed in Taylor G. v. Secretary of the Army, 2021001612 (2022). The Commission in Taylor G. specifically noted that "Complainant submitted no documentation from a health care provider explaining why he needed the reasonable accommodation of telework, and not a lessor accommodation, such as, for example, a heater or limited exposure to cold to accommodate his Raynaud's syndrome."

If the employee's initial response to a request for reasonable documentation is insufficient, the agency should submit a follow-up request. See Gosa v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01972468 (2000). That request should inform the employee specifically of the information required. The agency does not discharge its obligation simply by stating the information provided is insufficient. Moreover, once the agency has reasonable documentation that establishes the right of the employee to a reasonable accommodation, such as leave usage, it is discriminatory to demand that the employee continue to submit medical documentation for each absence. See Irvin v. Secretary of Energy, 01990867 (2000).

In Tiffani G. v. Postmaster General, 2020002481 (2021), for instance, the agency requested medical documentation on several occasions from the complainant to support her request to be reasonably accommodated in a position offered. The complainant explained that she tried to get her medical records but that she had no medical insurance and couldn't afford to obtain updated documentation. After holding the job for several months, the agency rescinded its offer. The complainant lodged an EEO complaint alleging failure to reasonably accommodate. The Commission in Tiffani G. found that the agency's inquiries and actions were supported:

[W]e find that Complainant has not shown that she was denied a reasonable accommodation when her case was closed. While we are not unsympathetic to Complainant's difficulties in obtaining medical care without employee-provided medical insurance, we note that the DRAC provided Complainant several opportunities to obtain the required medical information and could not be expected to hold her case open indefinitely. Because medical conditions can change, the DRAC was entitled to request current medical information, but such information was never provided. We note in this regard that Complainant's list of her restrictions did not constitute medical documentation.

In Spiegel v. Environmental Protection Agency, 01A03590 (2000), the Commission found that several complainants who suffered from multiple chemical sensitivity stated a claim within the EEO purview when they alleged they were required to resubmit medical documentation every two years to remain eligible for an alternative work schedule program. Although the Commission did not rule on the merits in Spiegel, it is likely the outcome would be dependent upon the permanency of the conditions. It would be unreasonable for an agency to require resubmission of medical information for a disability that is permanent and not subject to improvement. However, in Parker v. Social Security Administration, 01A44610 (2005), the Commission rejected a claim that the agency was engaging in harassment where it asked the complainant to update medical information in support of accommodation request after a two-year period. In finding the agency's request was justified, the Commission also noted that no accommodations were withdrawn as a result of the request.

In McDonnell v. Secretary of Navy, 01A04036 (2002), the Commission found the agency unnecessarily delayed the complainant's accommodation by requesting further medical information when his condition and limitations were well-documented in agency files.

However, in Cordita v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120122797 (2013), the Commission found that the complainant's allegation that the agency had requested additional medical information in support her accommodation request failed to state a claim. At first glance, the outcome seems surprising but agency had already granted the complainant's request to work on the day shift based on the medical information she submitted and was further inquiring into the effects of medication to ensure she could perform her duties as a nurse. The Commission found that the complainant failed to allege that any term or condition of employment was affected by the agency's request. Similarly, in Complainant v. Secretary of Army, 0120113847 (2015), the Commission found that a follow-up request for medical documentation was reasonable where the initial documentation did not explain why the complainant's condition could not be treated elsewhere and the complainant sought a deferment of a deployment to Korea.
