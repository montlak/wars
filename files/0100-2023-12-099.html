24. Veterans

Although veterans preference can have a great deal to do with whether the Merit Systems Protection Board has jurisdiction over a case, it has little to do with EEOC jurisdiction. The Commission has no authority with respect to veterans preference.

In Norvell v. Postmaster General, 01956049 (1996), request for reconsideration denied, Norvell v. Postmaster General, 05960704 (1996), the Commission held:

On August 10, 1995, appellant filed a timely appeal with this Commission from a final agency decision (FAD), received by appellant on July 22, 1995, pertaining to his complaint of unlawful employment discrimination in violation of Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, as amended, 42 USC § 2000e et seq., the Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967 (ADEA), as amended, 29 USC § 621 et seq., and the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, as amended 29 USC § 791 et seq. In his complaint, dated June 12, 1995, appellant alleged that he was subjected to discrimination on the bases of race (Black), religion (Protestant), national origin (African American), sex (male), age (42), disability, and in retaliation for prior EEO activity when on or about March 16 or 17, 1995, appellant was not hired by the agency prior to the hiring of non-veterans. Appellant stated that the agency improperly hired non-veteran, disabled applicants to fill custodial positions reserved for veterans. In its FAD, dated July 20, 1995, the agency dismissed appellant's complaint for failure to state a claim.

[A]n agency shall dismiss a complaint that fails to state a claim under 29 CFR § 1614.103. Commission regulations further provide that an agency shall accept a complaint from any aggrieved employee or applicant for employment who believes that he or she has been discriminated against by the agency because of race, color, religion, national origin, sex, age, disability, or reprisal. The U.S. Supreme Court has stated that an employee is aggrieved when some personal loss or harm has been suffered with respect to a term, condition, or privilege of employment. Trafficante v. Metropolitan Life Ins. Co., 409 U.S. 205 (1972).

We find that the agency properly dismissed appellant's complaint for failure to state a claim. The only proper questions in determining whether an allegation is within the purview of the EEO process are (1) whether the complainant is an aggrieved employee and (2) whether he or she has alleged employment discrimination covered by the EEO statutes. See Hobson v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05891133 (Mar. 2, 1990). Here, appellant alleged that he was subjected to discrimination when the agency hired applicants without veteran status for certain positions prior to hiring applicants with veteran's preference. While appellant alleged that the agency's action was based on race, religion, national origin, sex, age, disability, and in retaliation for prior EEO activity, the record indicates that the alleged violation was based on veteran status. The Commission has consistently held that veteran's preference is not an enumerated basis for filing an EEO complaint, and that complaints concerning veteran's preference are not within the purview of the EEO process. See, e.g., Glenn v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 05910927 (Feb. 21, 1992); Roth v. Department of Commerce, EEOC Request No. 05910129 (Mar. 7, 1991). The Commission has no authority to enforce the laws providing assistance to veterans. Therefore, we find that the agency properly dismissed appellant's complaint for failure to state a claim.

See also Penn v. Postmaster General, 05960702 (1998).

However, in DeVaux v. Postmaster General, 01993302 (2000), the Commission held that an allegation that the agency failed to respond to the complainant's request to meet with a disabled veterans coordinator stated a claim for relief.
