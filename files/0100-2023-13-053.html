1. Retention of Records

Once an EEO complaint is filed, an agency is required to retain records relating to the personnel action involved, irrespective of any other retention schedule regulations. A willful failure to retain the records will result in an adverse inference being drawn by the Commission with regard to the action at issue. In re Department of Health and Human Services, 05860212 (1987). In that case, the Commission already had given the agency an opportunity to produce the selection records or to reconstruct them from the available information. The agency refused and on appeal informed the Commission it would produce the SF-171s and file supporting affidavits, if necessary. The Commission declined to give the agency this opportunity finding its previous order clearly gave the agency that opportunity. The Commission also rejected the agency's argument that, since the investigator reviewed and summarized the files in his report, there was no further need for anyone actually to view the files.

The failure of the agency to retain relevant records once it is on notice that a complaint has been filed "makes it impossible for the appellant to prove pretext." Russell v. Department of Army, 01852234 (1987). Such a failure merits invocation of the adverse inference rule, "namely, that if the information had been provided, it would have been unfavorable to the agency and favorable to the appellant." Id. (citing Insurance Corporation of Ireland v. Company De Bauxites, 456 U.S. 694 (1982)).

However, EEO files have a finite existence schedule and should be destroyed four years after final resolution of the case. As the Commission explained in Reynolds v. Postmaster General, 05910404 (1991):

The use of documents contained in an EEO investigative file is restricted to the EEO process; in other words, the documents may be used in the processing of the case or for historical purposes, for example, reconstructing an individual's EEO activity. The maintenance of all relevant documents in the EEO file is necessary to preserve the integrity of the file. Access to documents contained in the EEO investigative file is protected by the Privacy Act and the Freedom of Information Act; the documents are not accessible to unauthorized individuals, and may be accessed only for the above purposes. Specifically, the Privacy Act at 5 USC 552a(b) provides:

No agency shall disclose any record which is contained in a system of records by any means of communication to any person, or to another agency, except pursuant to a written request by, or with the prior consent of, the individual to whom the record pertains....

The Freedom of Information Act provides that records of information compiled for law enforcement purposes are exempt from disclosure. 5 USC 552(b)(7). According to EEOC Regulations, the Commission has interpreted this exemption to include investigatory files, internal procedures and communications, information given in confidence, and matters involving personal privacy. See 29 CFR 1610.17(a). Civil remedies are prescribed for the improper disclosure of documents contained in an EEO file, 5 USC 552a(g), as well as criminal penalties for knowing and willful disclosures, 5 USC 552a(i). Furthermore, in addition to these protections, the EEO file has a finite existence; pursuant to EEOC Management Directive (MD) 107, Chapter 3, § 2.4 (September 1987), the EEO file must be destroyed four (4) years after resolution of the case....

MD-110, at 6-23 to 6-24, provides the specific disposition schedule for complaint files:

E. Disposition of Complaint Files

1. Effective December 8, 1998, the National Archives and Records Administration (NARA) revised General Records Schedule (GRS) 1, Item 25, titled Equal Employment Opportunity Records, provides:

Equal Employment Opportunity Records.

a. Official Discrimination Complaint Files.

Originating Agency's file containing complaints with related correspondence, reports, exhibits, withdrawal notices, copies of decisions, records of hearings and meetings, and other records as described in 29 CFR Part 1614.

Authorized Disposition

Destroy four years after resolution of case.

2. The agency originating the equal employment opportunity case will retain the original ("official") file during the appeals process and send only duplicate copies of documents to EEOC for use in the appeal. The agency sending the duplicates will certify that the file contains everything that is in the original.

3. EEOC will create documents relating to the appeal, but will file such documents apart from the materials sent by the originating agency. After resolution of the appeal, the Commission will destroy all duplicate materials, but will retain the appeals documentation for four years. The originating agency will retain the original file for four years after resolution of the case. EEOC will retain the appeals documentation and will answer Freedom of Information Act requests on the appeals file. The EEOC will maintain the security of documents as required by Federal Statutes and Executive Orders.

4. The originating agency will be responsible for retiring the original case file to the Federal Records Center, and answering Freedom of Information Act requests on the original file. Requests for disclosure, which the EEOC determines are requests for the agency's complaint file, will be forwarded to the agency for a response.

5. Further information concerning the disposition of records under this section may be obtained by reviewing NARA GRS 1, Transmittal 8, which is available on the NARA web site at www.nara.gov or by contacting:
