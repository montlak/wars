14. New Employees and Applicants

In waiving the time limit, the Commission may take into account the length of the complainant's employment. A recently hired, temporary employee should not ordinarily be held to the same standard of knowledge of EEO procedures as a permanent, long-term employee. Wilkerson v. Secretary of Defense, 05910587 (1991). The employee in Wilkerson contacted two officials in the agency's personnel office and indicated he believed his termination was discriminatory. Neither official advised the complainant of the appropriate procedures for filing a complaint. The complainant also exhibited a prompt pursuit of his complaint by filing a complaint with the Texas Employment Commission (TEC). Although the TEC had no jurisdiction over complaints by federal employees, the complainant did make an earnest effort to bring discrimination proceedings against the agency.

Similarly, in Wilbert W. v. Postmaster General, 0120150842 (2015), the Commission excused a seven-month delay in seeking counseling where the employee was new and contended that he was unaware of his rights and the timeframe until he received No Fear Act training.

The mere fact that an applicant for employment is an attorney does not mean he should be held to a higher standard regarding EEO procedures in the federal government. Bostic v. Attorney General, 01932630 (1993). The Commission waived the time limit for contacting a counselor, noting that complainant was a prosecutor and "[i]n view of the ever-increasing specialization within the legal profession, we do not find a non-federal employee who happens to be an attorney should be held to a higher standard on matters of federal EEO procedure than non-lawyers in the absence of evidence that the attorney has a background in or reasonably should know employment law." Id.

Complainants who are not federal employees, i.e., applicants for employment or former employees, may not be held to the same knowledge of the time limit for initiating precomplaint counseling as complainants who are federal employees. For example, in Kensie v. Postmaster General, 01883649 (1988), the Commission waived the time limit for a former federal employee who was denied a position in the Postal Service. In waiving the period in Kensie, the Commission noted that most of the complainant's federal service predated the amendment of Title VII making it applicable to the federal government:

His only employment after that amendment was in 1979-80 for six months, and it does not appear in the record that he has been federally employed since. We are not inclined to hold a complainant who was a federal employee seven years before to an inference of constructive notice (or memory) of the thirty day time limit.... The appellant holds the status of a member of the public at large, and has asserted that he did not know of the time limit. In these circumstances, the burden is on the agency to prove that appellant had notice of the time limit in question. We do not find that the posting of an EEO poster in the agency's lobbies is sufficient to carry that burden.

The Commission has reaffirmed this rationale in other cases. See, e.g., Michaud v. Postmaster General, 01A03448 (2002); Kavalauskas v. Secretary of Air Force, 05900608 (1990).

An applicant for employment in Felton A. v. Attorney General, 0120170271 (2017), received a conditional offer pending a medical evaluation. Upon receiving copies of the complainant's medical records, the agency determined that the position required more strenuous activity than the complainant could endure and rescinded the offer. The rescission letter, received by the complainant on April 27, 2013, stated that his medical history suggested that he was unable to safely perform the essential functions of the position. Believing the agency's conclusion was in error, the complainant appealed to the Office of Personnel Management. On July 27, 2013, the complainant received a letter from OPM concurring with the agency's determination. He contacted an EEO counselor on August 1. The administrative judge granted the agency's motion to dismiss for untimely counselor contact. The Commission reversed in Felton A., explaining:

Here, the AJ found that the 45-day limitation period was triggered when Complainant received the letter rescinding his job offer on April 27, 2013. However, Complainant did not request EEO counseling until August 1, 2013, beyond the required 45-day period. The AJ reasoned that "[Complainant] does not assert that he was not aware of the limits and requirements [of the EEO process] but merely argues that he was pursuing rights with OPM during that time." She further explained that pursing another administrative process (such as an OPM appeal) does not justify an extension of the 45 day time limitation to contact an EEO counselor. As already noted, the Agency adopted the AJ's decision in its final order.

However, we find that while he may not have made an express "assertion," a fair reading of the record provides that Complainant did indicate to the Agency that he did not know about the 45 day limitation period to contact an EEO counselor. Complainant, as an applicant for employment, did not have an opportunity to review EEO materials and receive EEO training from the Agency prior to the alleged discriminatory action. Under the circumstances, Complainant's explanation that he was waiting for OPM to issue a decision on the recission so that he could bring an EEO complaint demonstrates of lack of knowledge of the EEO process. Further, we note that Complainant contacted an EEO counselor only four days after he received the decision from OPM, the point he believed triggered his obligation to initiate the EEO complaint process.

Where, as here, there is an issue of timeliness, an agency always bears the burden of obtaining sufficient information to support a reasoned determination as to timeliness." Guy v. Dep't of Energy, EEOC Request No. 05930703 (Jan. 4, 1994) (quoting Williams v. Dep't of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05920506 (Aug. 25, 1992)). In addition, the agency has the burden of proving evidence and/or proof to support its final decisions. Clay W. v. Dep't of the Interior, EEOC Appeal No. 0120161461 (Jun. 7, 2016) quoting Ericson v. Dep't of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05920623 (Jan. 14, 1993). In instances where an agency does not have evidence to support the complainant's actual or constructive knowledge of the limitation period for contacting an EEO counselor, as is the case here, a complainant's assertion that he or she did not know about the time limit is sufficient to reverse a dismissal based on untimely EEO contact. See Clay, supra.

In Francis v. Postmaster General, 01A04739 (2001), the complainant, who was an applicant for employment, stated he wrote to the agency's personnel office claiming discrimination and was not advised of the EEO process. The complainant indicated it was not until he contacted an EEOC district office that he was informed of the correct procedures. The Commission noted that complainant produced telephone bills that reflected several attempts to contact the EEOC's district office within the 45-day time limit. The Commission made no comment on the apparent inability of its district office to respond to the complainant within the 45-day period.

In Chong v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A20526 (2002), the Commission waived the time limit for counselor contact where the applicant filed a complaint with the Palm Beach County Office of Equal Opportunity. That office told the complainant it could not proceed in a case involving the federal government but did not advise the complainant on the proper procedures. Ultimately, Goodwill Industries advised the complainant to contact the EEOC Office of Resolution Management. The Commission found the complainant's subsequent contact with an EEO counselor was timely because it occurred promptly after the complainant first learned of the proper procedure.

Although the agency posted a notice of EEO rights and timeframes in the testing facility, in Houser v. Secretary of Commerce, 0120100340 (2010), the Commission found that the complainant, an applicant for employment, should not have been expected to read the notice where she was only at the facility for about an hour.

The Commission also applied a similar rationale in waiving the 15-day time period for filing a formal complaint of discrimination where the agency did not provide a formal complaint form to the applicant with its notice of final interview. Capps v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 01912562 (1991).

However, the fact that the complainant is an applicant for employment does not excuse a failure to initiate counselor contact in 45 days where the complainant previously worked for the agency and had previously signed a document notifying him of the time limit. In Thompson v. Secretary of Navy, 0120111185 (2011), the Commission found:

Following a review of the record, we agree with the Agency in this case. While it may be reasonable to excuse an outside applicant who only briefly entered the agency's facility to drop off an application, such is not the case here. Complainant had worked at the facility before and the record contains a document he signed on December 26, 2007 notifying him of the 45-day time limit for contacting an EEO Counselor to initiate the EEO complaint process. Complainant's argument on appeal that he had no way of knowing in 2010 if the time limits had since changed is without merit. We therefore find that Complainant has presented no persuasive arguments or evidence warranting an extension of the time limit for initiating EEO Counselor contact and that such Counselor contact was untimely.
