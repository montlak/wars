20. Unconditional Relief

An order of full relief must be unconditional. An offer of reinstatement that is conditioned upon the complainant meeting the physical requirements of a position was not full relief. See Salisbury v. Secretary of Navy, 01921480 (1992). Placing a condition on an offer of full relief gives the complainant cause to properly reject it. See Chatman v. Postmaster General, 01962971 (1996). In that case, the agency agreed to provide the complainant with the acting supervisory assignment at issue. But the agency placed a contingency on the offer; namely, that if she incurred any unscheduled leave the assignment would be terminated. The Commission noted the contingency would be improper unless the agency could establish that a similar contingency was placed on all acting supervisory assignments. In Millea v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01963673 (1997), the Commission found that an offer of full relief failed because it was conditioned upon the complainant signing a settlement agreement. The Commission held the agency's promise of full relief must be unconditional and not require the complainant to sign any agreement.

Likewise, an order of full relief cannot merely require the agency to make an illusory promise. For example, in Banks v. Secretary of Interior, 05920680 (1994), the Commission found that an agency promise to review the complainant's requests for religious accommodation and to grant them where they did not "adversely affect" the office was not an offer of relief:

We find, however, that the agency's offer regarding religious accommodation in the future is lacking. In the settlement offer, the agency stated that "within 30 days of reinstatement, appellant will produce a list of circumstances for which she would expect to request religious accommodation, and the agency will determine those situations in which reasonable accommodation will be granted, although no accommodation will be granted which adversely affects the work of the office." There are two major deficiencies in this offer. First, an offer to merely determine those situations in which reasonable accommodation will be granted is not at all the equivalent of an offer to provide reasonable accommodation to appellant in the future--the remedy in a religious accommodation claim.

Second, the caveat that no accommodation will be granted if it "adversely affects" the work of the office is on its face inconsistent with the legal standard defining reasonable accommodation. Under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, employers must accommodate the religious practices of their employees absent proof that such accommodation could not be made without imposing an undue hardship on the employer....

An order of full relief that is contingent upon the waiver of attorney fees does not constitute full relief. See Higgins v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 0188820006 (1988) ("An offer of complete make whole relief would provide for an award of reasonable attorneys fees"); see also In re Frame, 05870235 (1987).

An order of full relief may not be premised on the complainant agreeing to withdraw or forego rights she may have in other forums. In Weiler v. Postmaster General, 01923090 (1992), the Commission rejected an offer of full relief that contained a provision that would have required the complainant to withdraw a Federal Torts Claim Act suit:

Finally, the offer contains an impermissible term:

(T)he instant agreement is in full and complete settlement of all outstanding administrative complaints, appeals or grievances...relating to any matters that occurred prior to the execution of this Settlement Agreement. The below named complainant agrees to voluntarily withdraw any outstanding administrative complaint, appeal or grievance or to request that they be withdrawn...

Appellant challenges this term because, she states, it would force her to waive her right to proceed under the Federal Tort Claims Act. Because tort claims are outside the jurisdiction of the Commission, we are unable to state whether the language of the term would have that effect. However, the record suggests the possibility that both parties understand the term to encompass a tort claim. (Footnote omitted.) Assuming that the term encompasses claims other than discrimination claims, it would be impermissible for the agency to use it to extinguish appellant's rights under Title VII. Relinquishing rights under other statutes also would not be required of appellant if she were to prevail on her discrimination claim, and is therefore not an appropriate element of full relief.

An order of full relief must be permanent in nature. Milles v. Veterans Administration, 01883410 (1988). In Milles, the complainant alleged that a system of granting compensatory time was discriminatory. Although the complaint was based on a specific incident involving a denial of compensatory time, the Commission found that an offer of restoration of that time was not full relief because the problem might arise again.
