5. Performance Expectations

When an action is based on performance, the employee must demonstrate, as part of his prima facie case, that his performance met the legitimate expectations of his employer. In Calabria v. Postmaster General, 05910044 (1991), the complainant contended that this requirement placed an onerous burden on the employee when the performance deficiencies cited by the agency were based on the subjective evaluations of supervisors. The employee argued that the agency should be required to produce some objective evidence of performance deficiencies for rebuttal. The Commission rejected the argument, noting that the complainant could have presented testimony from coworkers that would have corroborated his contention that his performance met the legitimate expectations of the agency.

Similarly, an employee must meet the legitimate expectations of the position in order to prove a discriminatory discharge. In Malcolm v. Postmaster General, 01954999 (1997), the administrative judge found that the complainant was meeting all but one of his job requirements, and that he proved that his termination was likely motivated by race. The Commission reversed in Malcolm, noting that the complainant was not meeting the his job requirement of casing mail:

The record shows that appellant, a White male, entered on duty at the agency's Arlington Station facility on November 1, 1993, as a City Letter Carrier for a 90-day probationary period. He received periodic evaluations on November 29 and December 1993. In both of these evaluations appellant was advised that he must improve in the area of casing mail as well as in other areas. On January 18, 1994, appellant received notice that his employment would be terminated effective that date. Appellant was officially terminated by his first-line supervisor (S-1; Black, male). The manager of the Arlington Station was also a Black male, hereinafter referred to as the Manager.

Appellant filed this formal EEO complaint on January 26, 1994, alleging discrimination as set forth in the above-entitled statement, "Issue Presented." Following an investigation of this complaint, the agency informed appellant that he could request either an EEO administrative hearing or a final agency decision (FAD), based on the existing record. Appellant requested an EEO hearing, which was held on January 18, 1995. In his recommended bench decision (RD), the Administrative Judge (AJ) recommended a finding of discrimination. Thereafter, the agency issued a FAD, in which it rejected the AJ's finding of discrimination. Appellant now appeals the FAD.

In his RD, the AJ found that appellant established a prima facie case of race discrimination in that appellant was White, his first and second line supervisors were Black, and the bulk of the testimony presented indicated that appellant was meeting all expectations of his position with the exception of casing mail. Last, the AJ determined that appellant proved that the agency's articulated reasons for appellant's dismissal, that his rate of casing mail and his street time or delivery time were below agency standards, were pretextual and that, more likely than not, appellant's race was a motivating factor in the agency's decision.

. . .

After thoroughly reviewing the record, including appellant's contentions on appeal, the Commission finds that appellant failed to prove that he was terminated because of his race. We find that appellant failed to establish a prima facie case of race discrimination. While both of appellant's supervisors were of a different race than appellant, we find that appellant did not prove that he was meeting one of the basic job requirements of the position, adequately casing mail. Nor did appellant prove that there were similarly situated probationary employee(s), outside of his protected group, who failed to meet this expectation and were not terminated. We note that appellant was given two evaluations prior to the termination which informed him that he needed to improve in this area.
