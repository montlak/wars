IV. RECORD ON APPEAL

The record on appeal consists of the entire complaint file, including the report of investigation, the hearing transcript if a hearing was held, the decision of the administrative judge if one was issued, and the final agency action taken on the complaint. The agency is responsible for providing the complaint file to OFO once an appeal is filed.

If the complainant files a notice of appeal, the agency must submit the complaint file to the EEOC. See 29 CFR 1614.403(e). Once an appeal is received, the Commission will request and the agency has 30 days to submit the complaint file. Id. Any brief in support of the complainant's appeal must be filed within 30 days of the date the appeal was filed. 29 CFR 1614.403(d). A copy of the brief must be served on the agency. Any agency brief in opposition to the appeal must be filed within 30 days of receipt of the complainant's brief. See 29 CFR 1614.403(f). If the complainant did not submit a brief, the agency has 60 days from receipt of the notice of appeal to file an opposition brief. A copy of the brief or statement must be served on the complainant. Id.

If the agency files an appeal, it must submit the complaint file within 30 days of the date it submits the appeal. 29 CFR 1614.403(e). The agency has 20 days from the date it files the notice of appeal to submit a brief in support of its appeal. 29 CFR 1614.403(d). A copy of the brief must be served on the complainant. The complainant then has 30 days from receipt of the agency's brief to file an opposition brief. 29 CFR 1614.403(f). If the agency did not submit a brief, the complainant has 60 days from receipt of the notice of appeal to submit an opposition brief. Id. A copy of the brief must be served on the agency. Id.

MD-110, at 9-11 to 9-12, describes what must be included in the complaint file when there has been a hearing before an administrative judge:

Absent notice from the Commission that it has the case file from the hearing on the same matter, the agency must submit the complaint file to OFO within thirty (30) days of notification that the complainant has filed an appeal or within thirty (30) days of submission of an appeal by the agency. If the complaint was adjudicated by an Administrative Judge, the complaint file must include copies of all documents issued by or served on the Administrative Judge, including, but not limited to, all correspondence to and from the Administrative Judge, orders from the Administrative Judge, and motions and briefs of the parties. Agencies should develop internal procedures that will ensure the prompt submission of complaint files upon a determination not to fully implement an Administrative Judge's decision or notice that a complainant has filed an appeal.

The agency must submit appeals and complete complaint case file(s) to the Commission's Office of Federal Operations in a digital format unless they can show good cause for not doing so. Complainants and their representative(s), if applicable, are strongly encouraged to file all documents in a digital format. See, 29 C.F.R. § 1614.403(g). All documents may be uploaded to the Commission's electronic document submission portal. If a CD is used, it is preferred that all documents be provided in a PDF format.

The electronic complaint file must at a minimum have the following features:

• Electronic bookmarks corresponding to the file index and section dividers of the paper file, if a paper file was created;

• Sequentially numbered pages starting with the first page of the file. All pages in the report of investigation must be accounted for in the page numbering of the document, including the cover page and any administrative documents, in order for the numbers in the paper file to match precisely the numbers in the electronic file. An individual entering page number 150 into Adobe Acrobat should come to the exact same page as an individual turning to page 150 of the paper file. Administrative documents added after the paper file was compiled may be submitted in a separate PDF file.

The agency has an affirmative obligation to maintain and submit a complete file. Poiter v. USPS, 01832406 (1984).

The EEOC has traditionally exercised broad powers when it receives an incomplete complaint file. These powers include such things as ordering the agency to exclude all irrelevant documents, to arrange the file in sequential order, and to record in the file all documents relevant to the complaint. See Georgiou v. Department of Army, 01831306 (1985). Upon receipt of an appeal, the EEOC will order the agency to produce its entire file on the complaint. Failure to comply with this order may result in the EEOC maintaining an adverse inference against the agency in factual disputes on which evidence is unavailable. See Powell v. USPS, 01830645 (1983).

Despite the tough rhetoric, the Commission was very forgiving of agencies that did not comply with its directive to produce the complaint file within 30 days. See, e.g., Mendez v. Postmaster General, 05880005 (1988) (granting agency's request to reopen, even though the complaint file was not produced for 11 months and no explanation for the delay was given). Agencies are advised that the Commission appears to be taking a more aggressive posture in this area. See, e.g., Ferrante v. Postmaster General, 05910603 (1991) (holding that a request to reopen is not a vehicle for submitting evidence that should have been provided with the agency file on appeal); see also Dacus v. Postmaster General, 01912544 (1991) (using adverse inference rule against agency where it failed to respond to two requests to produce complaint file).

That more aggressive posture became apparent in Lebron v. Postmaster General, 01943052 (1996), where the Commission imposed an adverse inference on the agency for failing to comply with its directive for a supplemental investigation. In Lebron, the Commission wrote:

Appellant also asserts that the SI is incomplete. EEOC Regulations provide that where the agency

fail[s] without good cause shown to respond fully and in [a] timely fashion to requests for documents, records, comparative data, statistics, affidavits, or the attendance of witness(es). . .the Commission on appeal may, in appropriate circumstances:

(i) draw an adverse inference that the requested information, or the testimony of the requested witness, would have reflected unfavorably on the party refusing to provide the requested information.

. . .

A review of the SI file provided on appeal by the agency reveals that the agency failed to fully comply with instructions 2, 3, 4, and 6, and did not provide any explanation as to why this information was not provided. Therefore, the Commission will draw an adverse inference against the agency in all matters where the requested evidence from the SI was not provided by the agency. Id. See also Scura v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01943381 (July 20, 1995) (after determining that the initial investigation was insufficient, the Commission ordered the same agency facility (as the facility in this appeal) to conduct a supplemental investigation, and cautioned the agency that failure to provide the requested records on remand might result in an adverse inference.

. . .

In his supplemental affidavit, the MEO Manager indicated that he was aware of many acting 204-B assignments, but he denied any personal knowledge of such assignments, stating that this was the responsibility of BSS-1. As noted above, instruction 3 of the RTR Order specifically requested affidavits from BSS-1 and BSS-2, the supervisors who authorized the 204-B assignments of Comparatives 1, 2 and 3. The purpose of the affidavits was to provide the agency with an opportunity to explain why the managers chose Comparatives 1, 2 and 3 for 204-B assignments instead of appellant. Likewise, instruction 4 of the RTR Order specifically requested a list with the names and national origin of all employees who were authorized to work as 204-B supervisors under the direct or indirect authority of the MEO Manager from May 1990 through June 1991.

Despite these instructions, the agency failed to provide any information, either testimonial or otherwise, to explain why the comparative employees were provided with 204-B supervisory assignments in Maintenance while appellant was not. Nor did the agency document the amount of additional pay, if any, the comparatives received for their 204-B assignments. Therefore, we infer that if provided, the requested information and the testimony of the requested witnesses would have reflected unfavorably on the agency. . . . We further infer that the testimony of BSS-1 and BSS-2 would not have reflected a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for choosing the comparatives for 204-B assignments instead of appellant. Id. Based on these adverse inferences, we determine that the agency discriminated against appellant on the basis of national origin when it denied appellant 204-B supervisory assignments in the Maintenance Department while comparative employees were provided with these opportunities from May 1990 to June 1991.

In Sebek v. Attorney General, 07A00005 (2001), the Commission extended its aggressive posture, upholding a $200,000 damage award by an administrative judge where the agency failed to submit the complaint file on appeal after repeated requests from the Commission.

If the Commission determines that necessary information is not included in the agency complaint record, it may "supplement the record by an exchange of letters or memoranda, investigation, remand to the agency or other procedures." 29 CFR 1614.404(a). When the Commission does elect to supplement the record, any party providing information must serve a copy on the opposing party. 29 CFR 1614.404(b).
