D. RETIREMENT ELIGIBILITY

Using eligibility for retirement as a criterion for employment decisions can constitute age discrimination, but the rule is not iron-clad and complaints are assessed on a case-by-case basis. In Complainant v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120123119 (2015), the Commission, distinguishing the case from Harris v. National Capital Planning Commission, 01860613 (1988), request to reopen denied 05880452 (1988), found that the complainant had not presented any evidence that his eligibility for retirement was connected to his assignment to a sub-acute medical unit:

Complainant alleges, in essence, that in an effort to force him to retire, S1 did nothing to alleviate his patient workload while he was assigned to the sub-acute ward between May 10 and July 25, 2011. In support of that claim, he presents as evidence statements that he attributes to S1 in which she expressed a desire to purge older employees from the work force and inquired about whether he was going to retire. The issue of whether retirement eligibility can be a proxy for age discrimination rarely arises in federal sector EEO case law. The last time the Commission addressed this issue was in Harris v. National Capital Planning Commission, EEOC Appeal No. 01860613 (February 1, 1988), request to reopen denied EEOC Request No. 05880452 (May 27, 1988). In Harris, the Commission found that the Executive Director's consideration of complainant's eligibility for retirement was integral to his decision to abolish complainant's position and therefore constituted age discrimination. We noted in that decision that as a matter of law, the use of retirement eligibility as the basis for an employment decision is unlawful because retirement status is related solely to age and is therefore a proxy for age. Supra n.8.

The situation in the instant matter is completely distinguishable from the scenario in Harris. Here, there are no indications in the record beyond Complainant's own testimony that S1 had taken his retirement eligibility into account when requiring him to continue working in the subacute unit between May 10 and July 25, 2011. S1 testified that she capped Complainant's workload at 20 patients in order to arrest the decline in his performance that had been documented in the performance improvement plan mentioned in incidents (5) and (6). Moreover, S1 made her comments about the need for younger employees three years before the time frame covering the accepted allegation, and had inquired about Complainant's retirement one year before that time frame. Complainant has not presented any sworn statements from other witnesses or documents that establish a connection between those comments and S1's decisions regarding Complainant's workload between May 10 and July 25, 2011. Likewise, he has not presented any evidence that contradicts the explanation offered by S1 or which calls her veracity into question. The fact that S1 reduced Complainant's patient census to 20 casts further doubt as to whether S1 was motivated by unlawful considerations of Complainant's age. We therefore find, as did the Agency, that Complainant fell short of his burden of proof that he was subjected to disparate treatment by S1 because of his age.
