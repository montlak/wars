a. Alleged Wrongdoer

The degree of control analysis may also turn on who the complainant identifies as the alleged wrongdoer. The Commission in Luvenia S. v. Director of National Intelligence, 0120170733 (2017), noted that the complainant's claims of discrimination primarily centered around the actions of the contractors, not the agency. The Commission in Luvenia S. explained:

In the instant case, it appears that Complainant performed her duties at an Agency facility, using Agency equipment. She also received assignments from a government supervisor.

However, the record reflects that Complainant was hired by Tekmasters, which ensured not only that she was qualified for the position but also that she held the necessary security clearances. Tekmasters paid Complainant on a weekly basis, and withheld taxes. Tekmasters also provided her with health benefits and the opportunity to participate in a retirement plan. Complainant earned sick leave and annual leave with Tekmasters. If she was going to be absent, she notified the Project Manager, who is a Tekmaster employee. Evaluations of her performance were conducted by Tekmasters. Complainant was terminated based "upon [the] mutual agreement between GDIT and Tekmasters," due in part to her problematic communication with GDIT Program Managers.

Moreover, Complainant's statement on appeal reflects that while her government supervisor "liked her work ethic," her claims of discrimination lie primarily with GDIT and Tekmasters. For example, she disputes GDIT's claim that she failed to respond to them. Complainant contends that Tekmasters "needed to be more involved" and allowed GDIT "to have the majority of the control." She challenges the actions taken by GDIT and Tekmasters, not the Agency.

Therefore, based on the legal standards and criteria set forth in our previous decisions and guidance, we find that the Agency did not exercise sufficient control over Complainant's position to qualify as her joint employer for the purpose of the EEO process.

In Cleora W. v. Secretary of Defense, 2019005665 (2021), the Commission made much of the alleged wrongdoer's (an agency official) control over the complainant, as evidenced by the content of the compliant, to find that the complainant had standing:

Complainant contends that Yorktel informed her that Chief-K requested that she no longer telework, even though everyone else on the contract could telework. According to Complainant, and an Agency employee, Chief-K was opposed to telework. Complainant argues that when she, along with other employees, requested that Chief-K install FileMaker Pro on the server so employees could access the program from home, he refused. Irrespective of whether these actions were or were not discriminatory, they illustrate a level of control over Complainant's job. Finally, Complainant's daily work duties and responsibilities encompassed working closely with Agency employees. Moreover, she was viewed by many as an Agency employee. For example, in an email from an Agency management official to Chief-K commending the good work done by Chief-K's "team," he specifically named Complainant. Similarly, in correspondence on Agency letterhead, the Chief of Staff thanked Complainant for her work and support during an Agency ceremony. Therefore, when considering the factor of Complainant's daily work conditions, we find that the record supports the conclusion that this aspect of Complainant's employment suggests the Agency was her joint employer.

As for Complainant's termination, we note that the instant record does not contain a copy of the termination letter. According to the parties, the action was taken by Yorktel and based on leave abuses. However, the parties conflict with respect to what, if any, influence Chief-K may have had on the decision to remove Complainant from the Agency contract. Chief-K attested that he lacked the authority to remove Complainant. There are, however, indications that he may have had an impact upon Yorktel's decision, particularly in light of the fact that Yorktel did not remove her from their employment. In effectuating her removal from the Agency, e-mail correspondence between Agency officials and Yorktel also reveal that it was Chief-K who did not want Complainant to return to the facility to turn in Agency property and collect her personal belongings. As a result, an exchange through Yorktel was arranged. These actions also exhibit some measure of Agency control.

Therefore, on balance, we find the evidence shows sufficient control by the Agency over Complainant's employment to be considered a joint employer for purposes of the EEO complaint process. The AJ erred in dismissing the complaint on the ground that Complainant lacked standing to have her discrimination claims adjudicated through the 29 C.F.R. Part 1614 EEO complaint process.
