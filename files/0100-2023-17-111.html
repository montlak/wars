C. CRITERIA FOR RECONSIDERATION

The Commission's regulations set forth the criteria for reconsideration. Pursuant to 29 CFR 1614.405(b), a party must meet one of the following criteria in order for the Commission to grant a request for reconsideration:

(1) The appellate decision involved a clearly erroneous interpretation of material fact or law; or

(2) The decision will have a substantial impact on the policies, practices or operations of the agency.

The Commission also has the authority to reopen and reconsider its decisions without a motion from either party. See 29 CFR 1614.405(b). In the past, the Commission has not always made clear in its decisions the specific criterion on which it has granted requests for reconsideration. This trend appears to be continuing under the revised regulations. See Asendorf v. Social Security Administration, 05980203 (2000) (stating only that complainant's request met 29 CFR 1614.405(b) criteria, without specifying which criteria).

A request to reopen is not a second appeal. It must contain specific information that addresses one or more of the regulatory criteria. Gaskins v. Postmaster General, 05910471 (1991). Note that the criteria for reconsideration changed in accordance with the 1999 amendments to Part 1614. A party is no longer entitled to reconsideration upon submission of new and material evidence not previously available. The Commission may, however, reconsider any decision on its own motion, and the submission of new and material evidence may still lead to reconsideration as a matter of discretion.

The fact that one component of the agency does not agree with another component is not a proper basis for requesting reconsideration. Lagan v. Secretary of Transportation, 05930960 (1994) (finding irrelevant agency's chief legal counsel's belief that the EEO director erred in the final agency decision).

In one case, noted more for its curiosity than its legal value, the Commission established that divine intervention is not a basis for reopening a decision. Donald E. Long, 05850233 (1986). The Commission rejected the employee's request to reopen which started with a quote from the Scriptures and included documents such as the employee's birth certificate and those of his children, newspaper clippings and assorted correspondence. A request for reconsideration based on "humanitarian reasons" will also not suffice. Sylvester C. v. Secretary of State, 0120160697 (2016). Sylvester C. involved a complainant who lost his security clearance, and as a result, his job, and whose complaint was dismissed pursuant to 29 CFR 1614.103(d)(4), which provides that the EEO administrative process is not available to aliens employed in positions located outside the limits of the United States. His request for reconsideration based on humanitarian reasons was denied by the Commission.

In a request to reopen, the Commission found it was appropriate to waive the time limit for filing an appeal with OFO where both the complainant and her representative were legally blind and contended that the appeal was filed late because of their difficulty in reviewing the 400-page hearing transcript. The Commission also noted that the appeal was only one day late. Baker v. Secretary of Defense, 05921009 (1993). The Commission made no attempt to explain why the waiver was not granted by OFO in the first instance.

The agency is responsible for designating who shall receive Commission decisions on its behalf. Where the agency designates the EEO office, it is not grounds for reconsideration that the agency representative was never notified of the complainant's appeal or the Commission's subsequent decision in the case. Pratt v. Attorney General, 04A20007 (2002).

Arguments may not be raised for the first time in a request for reconsideration. For example, in Slack v. Postmaster General, 05A50225 (2006), the Commission denied the agency's petition for reconsideration where it raised timeliness of counselor contact as a basis for dismissal when it had not previously raised that argument. See also Lien v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0520110189 (2011) (argument that agency had already paid complainant for back pay could have been raised on appeal). An exception to the rule that the Commission will not consider for the first time arguments on reconsideration is where the original OFO decision was based on a procedural matter, such as timeliness, and that decision is reversed on reconsideration. In those instances, the Commission will then look at the merits of the appeal even though OFO did not rule on the merits. For example, in Langley v. Social Security Administration, 05A50413 (2006), OFO dismissed the appeal on the basis that the complainant had submitted a brief, but had never timely filed the notice of appeal. On reconsideration, the complainant presented persuasive evidence that she had timely filed the notice. As a result, the Commission entered the complainant's arguments on her damage award. The Commission also gave both parties an opportunity to seek reconsideration of the decision since the Commission had not previously considered the arguments raised.

"Mootness is a pre-adjudication issue." White v. Postmaster General, 05870481 (1988). Since one of the purposes of the doctrine is to ensure the complainant has a sufficient stake in the outcome to guarantee vigorous pursuit of the appeal, mootness is not a ground for reconsideration when, subsequent to a final decision, the employee voluntarily leaves the agency.
