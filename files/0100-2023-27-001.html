CHAPTER 18

PERSONNEL ACTIONS

Title VII, the Rehabilitation Act, and the ADEA include all personnel actions. Consistent with these acts as applied to the private sector, their actual coverage is much broader and also extends to the terms, conditions, and privileges of employment. The EEO process is not restricted to certain personnel actions. The jurisdiction of agencies and the EEOC over discrimination complaints is not affected by the nature, severity, or duration of the personnel action. Because discrimination can occur in such a wide variety of employment situations, some discussion of how various personnel actions, terms, and conditions are treated in the EEO process is in order.

This chapter assumes that the reader is already familiar with the theories of discrimination and burdens of proof discussed in Chapters 10-12, the bases of discrimination discussed in Chapters 13-16., and the section of Chapter 3 interrelating claims and standing. In this chapter, concepts earlier reviewed are applied to personnel actions and terms and conditions of employment. The goal is to provide the reader with an understanding of how the different kinds of personnel actions involved in discrimination complaints impact cases.

I. DISCIPLINARY ACTIONS

Disciplinary actions are those actions taken against an employee for engaging in alleged misconduct, whether it occurs on-duty or off-duty. There are a wide range of disciplinary actions, including oral and written reprimands, suspensions, demotions, and removals. In imposing discipline on an employee, an agency generally should be able to make a reasonable determination that the employee engaged in the conduct alleged, that there is a rational connection or "nexus" between the alleged misconduct and the efficiency of the service such that discipline is warranted, and that the actual discipline imposed is reasonable in relation to the offenses. Chapter 75 of Title 5 of the U.S. Code sets out the statutory guidance for so-called adverse actions taken against competitive service employees. Though lesser disciplinary actions, such as reprimands, are not covered by chapter 75, and not all employees are covered by chapter 75, most agencies follow its general guidance in imposing discipline.

In cases where the employee and the personnel action are covered by chapter 75, the employee is accorded certain procedural rights prior to the imposition of discipline. The employee has the right to advance written notice of the proposed action and the specific reasons for proposing the action. The employee has the right to make both an oral and written reply to the proposal, and the right to be represented by an attorney or other representative. The employee also has a right to a final written decision that enunciates the specific reasons for any discipline imposed. These procedural rights are important because the Commission, since the amendment of its regulations in 1987, no longer allows employees to file discrimination complaints over proposed disciplinary actions. See 29 CFR 1613.215(a)(2). This prohibition has been carried forward into Part 1614. See 29 CFR 1614.107(a)(5). The employee is not, however, prevented from raising the allegation that the disciplinary action is based on the discriminatory deprivation of rights in the reply process.

Depending upon the status of the employee, the action taken, whether that employee is a member of a collective bargaining unit, and whether any negotiated grievance procedure contains a provision for processing allegations of discrimination, the employee may have the option of pursuing his or her case in the negotiated grievance procedure or before the Merit Systems Protection Board. These options are discussed more fully in Chapter 20 under the subheadings "Negotiated Grievance Procedure" and "Mixed Cases Before the Merit Systems Protection Board."

Almost invariably, cases involving disciplinary personnel actions invoke the disparate treatment theory of discrimination. As such, they generally are analyzed under McDonnell Douglas v. Green, 411 U.S. 792, 93 S. Ct. 1817, 36 L. Ed.2d 668 (1973). That is, first the employee must demonstrate a prima facie case of discrimination. Then the agency is required to articulate a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for taking the disciplinary action. Finally, the employee is given an opportunity to demonstrate that the agency's proffered reason is a pretext to mask prohibited discrimination. However, it is possible for a complainant to allege that discipline is the result of a failure to make reasonable accommodation or is part of a hostile environment harassment claim.

Because Title VII, the Rehabilitation Act, and the ADEA cover all personnel actions, there is seldom a question of whether a personnel action taken by an agency falls within the jurisdiction of the Commission. However, because the antidiscrimination statutes also cover the terms, conditions, and privileges of employment, substantial questions can arise about whether a particular agency action invokes a term, condition, or privilege of employment. When such questions do arise, the reader should also consult the section on "Standing: Failure to State a Claim" in Chapter 3 in addition to this chapter.

In pursuing allegations of discrimination in disciplinary cases, it is a mistake to focus on the validity of the charges without regard for the issue of discrimination. Even if the complainant proved that female coworkers fabricated charges of sexual harassment, without demonstrating that the officials imposing the discipline had knowledge of the fabrication, this would not prove the discipline was retaliatory. See Reeves v. Postmaster General, 01933042 (1994). Likewise, the question in a discrimination case involving discipline for misconduct is not whether the misconduct occurred, but whether the agency's asserted belief that the misconduct occurred is a pretext for discrimination. See Bilby v. Secretary of Defense, AAFES, 01934204 (1994) (the administrative judge was correct in defining the issue as whether the agency's belief that the threat was made was a pretext for discrimination and not whether threat was actually made). In determining whether discipline is discriminatory, the focus is on whether the agency "subjectively believed" that the complainant engaged in the alleged misconduct. See Canady v. Secretary of Transportation, 01940947 (1994) (the relevant inquiry was not whether the complainant engaged in sexual harassment, but whether the agency "subjectively believed" he had). Of course, evidence that the employee engaged in misconduct is relevant. The fact that the agency based the action on unreliable evidence is relevant to the issue of pretext, particularly when combined with evidence establishing a prima facie case.

Because the jurisdiction of the EEOC is not confined to specific personnel actions, like the jurisdiction of the MSPB, there is a greater variety of ways in which a disciplinary action can be challenged. For example, in Cottrell v. Postmaster General, 05950917 (1996), the complainant did not challenge the actual disciplinary actions taken against her. Rather, she complained that the disciplinary actions were taken in a shorter time frame than for comparison employees. After initially dismissing the complaint, on reconsideration the Commission in Cottrell found:

The previous decision found that "although appellant attempted to frame the complaint as concerning the time span in which the disciplinary actions...were issued...[,] appellant's complaint concerned whether each disciplinary action was discriminatory." We disagree. A review of the record showed that appellant consistently has asserted that she was treated disparately with respect to the manner, i.e., the short time period, in which the agency issued the 3 disciplinary actions. Further, it appears that appellant timely contacted an EEO counselor in this regard. Consequently, on remand, the agency must investigate whether appellant was treated disparately with respect to the manner in which she was disciplined, not whether each disciplinary action was discriminatory. That is, the agency must investigate whether employees outside of appellant's protected class, who also failed to be regular in attendance, were similarly disciplined within the same time period, i.e., 3 months.
