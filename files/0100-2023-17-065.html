3. Failure to Produce or Destruction of Supervisory Notes

One area that has created confusion in the federal sector litigation of EEO complaints is the role that supervisory notes play in such cases and the agency's obligation to produce those notes during the investigation or in discovery. A brief summary is provided here. A more detailed discussion is included in Chapter 6 in the section on "Destruction of Evidence." Although OFO deals with sanctions on appeal, the issued of failure to produce or destruction of supervisory notes most often arises in the first instance before the administrative judge.

Part of the problem has been that supervisors often consider the notes to be "personal" and not agency records. As a result, they do not get produced in response to discovery requests but then appear at a later point in time as evidence in the case. Another related, though distinct, problem is the destruction of such notes.

Many are unaware that the Commission actually has a regulation on retention of documents at 29 CFR 1602.14, which provides:

Any personnel or employment record made or kept by an employer (including but not necessarily limited to requests for reasonable accommodation, application forms submitted by applicants and other records having to do with hiring, promotion, demotion, transfer, lay-off or termination, rates of pay or other terms of compensation, and selection for training or apprenticeship) shall be preserved by the employer for a period of one year from the date of the making of the record or the personnel action involved, whichever occurs later. In the case of involuntary termination of an employee, the personnel records of the individual terminated shall be kept for a period of one year from the date of termination. Where a charge of discrimination has been filed, or an action brought by the Commission or the Attorney General, against an employer under title VII, the ADA, or GINA, the respondent employer shall preserve all personnel records relevant to the charge or action until final disposition of the charge or the action. The term "personnel records relevant to the charge," for example, would include personnel or employment records relating to the aggrieved person and to all other employees holding positions similar to that held or sought by the aggrieved person and application forms or test papers completed by an unsuccessful applicant and by all other candidates for the same position as that for which the aggrieved person applied and was rejected. The date of final disposition of the charge or the action means the date of expiration of the statutory period within which the aggrieved person may bring an action in a U.S. District Court or, where an action is brought against an employer either by the aggrieved person, the Commission, or by the Attorney General, the date on which such litigation is terminated.

Although the provision is not part of the Commission's federal sector regulations at 29 CFR Part 1614, it does apply to the federal sector.

Initially, the Commission held that since an agency is not required to routinely retain the personal notes of selection panel members, it is improper for the administrative judge to draw an adverse inference because the notes have been destroyed. See Doyle v. Postmaster General, 01950090 (1996). There was no evidence in Doyle that the notes were destroyed after the complaint was filed, or that the agency engaged in bad faith in the destruction of the notes. Moreover, the Commission noted this was not a case where the selecting official was offering an explanation that lacked corroboration. All three members of the selection panel testified about the complainant's lackluster interview performance. See also Rivas v. Secretary of Air Force, 01970402 (1999) (adverse inference was improper where no regulation required panel members to retain notes and no evidence suggested that notes were destroyed after filing the complaint).

However, the Commission has now moved away from that view. For example, in Clayton v. Postmaster General, 0720070042 (2007), the Commission upheld the imposition of the sanction of an adverse inference against the agency where the selecting official destroyed his interview notes prior to learning of the EEO complaint. The Commission noted that its regulations do not require a showing of bad faith for the imposition of sanctions and the administrative judge acted within his discretion in imposing an inference that the notes would have reflected unfavorably on the agency. Such a sanction does not, in and of itself, guarantee that the complainant will prevail. The complainant in Clayton did not prevail because the agency was able to present legitimate reasons for its nonselection of the complainant. See also Jones v. Social Security Administration, 0120060831 (2007).

The Commission has held that an administrative judge errs in drawing an adverse inference when private notes are produced for the first time at the hearing where the notes were not previously requested. See Mason v. Postmaster General, 01950416 (1996). In that case, the administrative judge drew an adverse inference against the agency where the selecting official brought his private notes to the hearing because the agency withheld "vital evidence" from the complainant. However, the Commission stressed that neither the complainant nor the agency sought the production of the notes prior to the hearing and there was no evidence the notes had been fabricated after the selections.

But see Kerrie F. v. Social Security Administration, 0720140026 (2015) (upholding adverse inference where notes were destroyed).
