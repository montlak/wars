IV. FILING OF CIVIL ACTION

Under the Commission's former regulations at Part 1613, filing a complaint in U.S. district court did not terminate the administrative complaint with the agency. The agency was required to continue processing the complaint unless it was withdrawn. See 29 CFR 1613.283; Evono v. Department of Justice, 01811820 (1981). However, under the 1987 regulations of the Commission, the filing of a civil action did terminate the administrative processing of a complaint. See 29 CFR 1613.283. Part 1614 contains a similar provision. See 29 CFR 1614.107(a)(3). The proper course for the agency when a civil action is filed is to dismiss the complaint by issuing a final agency decision.

To determine if a complainant filed a civil action that involves the same claims as the administrative complaint, the Commission will look to the underlying factual allegations in the civil action and not the legal conclusions placed on those allegations. The fact that the civil action does not directly raise a claim of discrimination is not dispositive. In Leistiko v. Secretary of Army, 01922631 (1992), the Commission explained:

Appellant's attorney contends that the pending civil action is not identical to appellant's EEO complaint because the civil action contains three counts unrelated to unlawful employment discrimination: (1) a constitutional claim, (2) wrongful discharge, and (3) breach of contract. Appellant's attorney further contends that appellant's civil complaint expressly excludes the allegations contained in his EEO complaint.

The Commission is unpersuaded by this argument. The proper inquiry to determine whether rejection or cancellation is warranted is whether the issues in the EEO complaint and the civil action are the same, that is, whether the acts of alleged discrimination are identical. Curtis v. USPS, EEOC Request No. 05910400 (May 9, 1991). The factual allegation, and not the bases or the precise relief requested, should be the crux of the legal analysis here. Stromgren v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 05891079 (May 7, 1990). In this case, the Commission finds that the evidence of record establishes that the EEO complaint and the civil complaint both relate to appellant's termination from his position as a Supervisory Aircraft Pilot because the agency determined that he no longer met the requirements of his position. The Commission therefore determines that the agency properly canceled appellant's EEO complaint on the grounds that it is the basis of a pending civil action in a United States District Court of which appellant is a party. While it may be unfortunate, the mandatory termination of jurisdiction is designed to avoid a duplication of resources, judicial and administrative, which is unwarranted and unjustified, as well as giving rise to the possibility of inconsistent and conflicting decisions.

There is no requirement that the complainant be the party to initiate the civil action. Smith v. Postmaster General, 01893186 (1989). In Smith, the complainant filed an EEO complaint over his removal. He also filed a grievance. The EEO complaint resulted in the finding of no discrimination, but an arbitrator reversed the removal and ordered the agency to reinstate the complainant. The agency failed to comply with the arbitrator's decision and filed a civil action in U.S. district court seeking to overturn the arbitrator's order. In response, the complainant initiated a new EEO complaint alleging that the agency's failure to comply with the arbitration order was based on discrimination.

The Commission rejected the complaint finding "the subject of appellant's complaint is the agency's disagreement with the arbitrator's order and its resulting order to comply. That issue is also the subject of the civil action filed by the agency--the agency initiated that litigation to overturn the arbitrator's order." Id.

Nor is the determining factor necessarily in which court the complainant files a civil action. In Calixteiro v. Secretary of Navy, 01913687 (1991), the complainant filed a civil action over her termination in the Superior Court of Guam. The government filed a petition to remove the case to U.S. District Court of Guam. Although the available record does not indicate whether the transfer was granted, the Commission noted that upon such transfer it would be appropriate to dismiss the complaint.

When a complainant's civil action is dismissed on the basis of failing to fulfill jurisdictional prerequisites, he has no right to a resumption of the administrative processing of his complaint. Drake v. Secretary of Defense, 05900284 (1990). The district court properly advised the complainant of how to cure the jurisdictional defects and gave the complainant an opportunity to refile his complaint. The district court did not direct the agency to resume processing of the case and, thus, it was entitled to terminate the processing upon the filing of the complaint in district court.

When a civil action is dismissed without prejudice, the complainant is entitled to reinstatement of the administrative processing of her complaint. As the Commission explained in Jones v. Attorney General, 05910970 (1992):

EEOC Regulation 29 CFR 1613.215(a)(3) provides that the agency shall reject or cancel an EEO complaint that is the basis of a civil action filed in United States District Court in which the EEO complainant is a party. See also 29 CFR 1613.283 (filing of a civil action by employee involving complaint filed under Title VII subpart terminates processing of EEO complaint) and 29 CFR 1613.708 (complaints involving handicap discrimination are processed as under Title VII procedures, including 1613.283). The purpose of our regulation requiring rejection of an EEO complaint upon the filing of a civil action is to prevent a complainant from simultaneously pursuing both administrative and judicial remedies on the same matters, wasting resources and creating the potential for inconsistent or conflicting decisions. Gerald A. Curtis v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05910400 (May 9, 1991); Sylvia Stromgren v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 05891079 (May 7, 1990).

In the present case there is evidence that appellant's civil action was dismissed without prejudice prior to any determination on the merits. The Commission has held in the past that an agency's rejection of an EEO complaint pursuant to 29 CFR 1613.215(a)(3) will not stand when the civil action is subsequently dismissed without prejudice and there has been no determination on the merits of the EEO complaint. Laurence C. Jones. Jr. v. Department of Health and Human Services, EEOC Request No. 05900469 (April 19, 1990). We determine, therefore, that our previous decision upholding the agency's rejection of appellant's complaint pursuant to 29 CFR 1613.215(a)(3) was in error. With regard to appellant's three complaints filed prior to his discharge from employment, we find that the administrative forum should be reopened to him. Accordingly, the complaints filed on March 25, 1987, June 15, 1987, and December 31, 1987, are remanded to the agency for further processing in accordance with the Order below.

The civil action must be one that raises the same allegations of discrimination as the administrative complaint in order for the agency to dismiss the administrative complaint. The fact that an employee seeks and obtains a temporary restraining order in U.S. district court on due process grounds unrelated to his EEO complaint does not provide a basis for dismissing the administrative complaint. Malik v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 05910481 (1991).

A civil action in a case under Title VII or the Rehabilitation Act also may be filed within 90 days of a final agency decision. 42 USC 2000e-16(c). The complainant is entitled to a trial de novo in U.S. district court. It is worth noting that, because an ADEA complainant is not required to exhaust administrative remedies, the same 90-day rule may not apply. The statute of limitations under the ADEA in federal sector complaints raises complicated questions and the answers may vary from circuit to circuit. The reader is referred to Chapter 16 for a full discussion of the "Statute of Limitations" under the ADEA.

Not all courts have accepted the proposition that the 1991 Civil Rights Act extended the civil action filing period from 30 to 90 days. A few courts have held that the 30-day period still applies. The Commission has taken to issuing the following notice of rights, as evidenced in Hicks v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01955710 (1996):

It is the position of the Commission that you have the right to file a civil action in an appropriate United States District Court WITHIN NINETY (90) CALENDAR DAYS from the date that you receive this decision. You should be aware, however, that courts in some jurisdictions have interpreted the Civil Rights Act of 1991 in a manner suggesting that a civil action must be filed WITHIN THIRTY (30) CALENDAR DAYS from the date that you receive this decision. To ensure that your civil action is considered timely, you are advised to file it WITHIN THIRTY (30) CALENDAR DAYS from the date that you receive this decision or to consult an attorney concerning the applicable time period in the jurisdiction in which your action would be filed. If you file a civil action, YOU MUST NAME AS THE DEFENDANT IN THE COMPLAINT THE PERSON WHO IS THE OFFICIAL AGENCY HEAD OR DEPARTMENT HEAD, IDENTIFYING THAT PERSON BY HIS OR HER FULL NAME AND OFFICIAL TITLE. Failure to do so may result in the dismissal of your case in court." Agency" or "department" means the national organization, and not the local office, facility or department in which you work. If you file a request to reconsider and also file a civil action, filing a civil action will terminate the administrative processing of your complaint.

A petition to enforce a Commission decision, discussed below, will be dismissed if the complainant files a civil action in U.S. district court seeking a compliance order. Stith v. Secretary of Navy, 04960019 (1996); see also Chapter 3.
