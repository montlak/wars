24. Future Actions

The complainant must allege a specific harm that has already occurred. See Quinones v. Secretary of Defense, 05920051 (1992) (a confidential referral to an employee assistance counselor, alleged to have future impact on performance appraisals and promotion opportunities, presents no cognizable claim); cf. Hansen v. Postmaster General, 05980707 (1999) (an allegation that an employee was ordered to the employee assistance program, which was 20 miles away, states a claim).

Expressing a general concern about the prospective failure of an agency to abide by EEO practices is not a sufficient personal injury on which to base a complaint. The employee must "suffer direct and personal deprivation at the hands of the employer." Prater v. USPS, 01832284 (1984). An employee's request for prospective relief in the form of a light duty assignment if she failed her eye examination alleged no personal injury. Even though younger employees previously had been granted such assignments, the appellant's request was based on a "totally speculative injury." Id. The determination that a person is aggrieved must make reference to "the standing concepts articulated by the Supreme Court as injury in fact and an interest within the zone of those regulated by the statute in question." Keval v. Commodity Futures Trading Commission, 01832127 (1984). In Patel v. Postmaster General, 01963070 (1997), the Commission held that an allegation that the appellant was discriminatorily assigned duties because of her race, color, national origin, and/or disability failed to state a claim where there was no evidence that she was ever made to perform those duties. The complainant was within the zone of interests protected by Title VII and the Rehabilitation Act, but she had not alleged an injury in fact because she had never been required to perform the disputed duties.

When a complainant files a complaint based on speculation of future injury and the future event occurs, the agency should consider the speculative complaint as "merging" with the later complaint and accept it for processing. In Nichols v. Secretary of Transportation, 01940064 (1994), the complainant alleged that she anticipated that a previously existing hostile environment would continue to exist after her return from maternity leave. The agency dismissed the complaint on the basis that the allegation involved speculative actions and not an actual incident of discrimination. The Commission found that while the injury alleged may have been speculative at the time of the complaint, the employee had since returned from leave. The Commission reasoned that the alleged injury was no longer speculative. Similarly, when a complainant raises a personnel action with the counselor that has not taken place, but it subsequently does take place, the Commission employs the concept of merger to the complaint. As the Commission noted in Arnold C. v. Postmaster General, 0120093856 (2015), a case in which the complainant raised his alleged involuntary retirement with the counselor but had not yet retired, "where a complainant received EEO counseling on a future action which ultimately occurs, the otherwise premature allegation merges with the effectuated action. See Lacy v. General Serv. Admin., EEOC Appeal No. 01A01743 (June 1, 2000); Rose v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 05950240 (July 27, 1995)."
