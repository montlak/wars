IV. HEARING PROCEDURES

A number of good treatises have been written on hearing and trial practice. This section is not intended to be a complete primer on hearing presentations. Some advice and commentary is provided. To representatives with significant hearing experience, the advice may seem trivial. To those with little or no hearing experience, it may not seem to go far enough. Such was the continual dilemma of the author in putting together this section. The purpose of this section is to acquaint the reader with EEO hearing practices. For those who require further instruction, see Ernest C. Hadley and Deryn A. Sumner, Representing Agencies and Complainants Before the EEOC (Dewey Publications, Inc. 2013).

An additional preliminary word about hearing procedures is necessary. The hearing is not an entirely de novo proceeding. The complete agency complaint file is before the administrative judge. The parties should not waste time proving those facts that have been addressed adequately by the investigation. The purpose of the hearing is to supplement the investigation, not rehash it. If there is information in the file that should be challenged or clarified, by all means, do so. But given the fact that the administrative judges carry very heavy caseloads, they are not likely to be impressed or amused by a complainant or agency representative who uses the hearing solely to review the complaint file. To the extent possible, the hearing should be confined to placing evidence into the record that is not already there.

Certainly, the parties will want to bring information in the record which they believe is favorable to them or unfavorable to the opposing party to the attention of the administrative judge. Administrative judges permit both opening and closing statements. This is the time to highlight evidence for the judge and make arguments as to how it should be used.

EEOC hearings are not that different from most administrative hearings. Formal rules of evidence are not applied strictly. The Federal Rules of Evidence can be used by the administrative judge insofar as the underlying policy of the cited rule is applicable to the proceeding. The tendency here is to admit evidence that may be relevant and to deal with the weight to be accorded the evidence in the decision. There is nothing wrong with this policy. It should be noted, though, that many of the policies of the Federal Rules that require the exclusion of evidence are equally applicable to the weight that should be accorded the evidence. Parties should not hesitate to make these arguments to the judge on the assumption that the evidence will be admitted. It is entirely possible that the judge will admit the evidence but, for the reasons pointed out by an able representative, accord little or no weight to the evidence.

EEOC administrative judges have broad discretion over the manner in which a hearing is conducted. This discretion extends to the approval and disapproval of witnesses, including rebuttal witnesses. See, e.g., Giannelli v. Attorney General, 01A02647 (2002).
