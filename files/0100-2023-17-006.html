C. MSPB AND NEGOTIATED GRIEVANCE DECISIONS

The Commission may review a decision by the Merit Systems Protection Board (MSPB) or by an arbitrator to the extent that the decision addresses unlawful employment discrimination under the statutes enforced by the Commission.

With respect to decisions rendered pursuant to a negotiated grievance process, the Commission's regulations, at 29 CFR 1614.301(a), provide:

When a person is employed by an agency subject to 5 USC 7121(d) and is covered by a collective bargaining agreement that permits allegations of discrimination to be raised in a negotiated grievance procedure, a person wishing to file a complaint or a grievance on a matter of alleged employment discrimination must elect to raise the matter under either part 1614 or the negotiated grievance procedure, but not both. An election to proceed under this part is indicated only by the filing of a written complaint; use of the pre-complaint process as described in § 1614.105 does not constitute an election for purposes of this section. An aggrieved employee who files a complaint under this part may not thereafter file a grievance on the same matter. An election to proceed under a negotiated grievance procedure is indicated by the filing of a timely written grievance. An aggrieved employee who files a grievance with an agency whose negotiated agreement permits the acceptance of grievances which allege discrimination may not thereafter file a complaint on the same matter under this part 1614 irrespective of whether the agency has informed the individual of the need to elect or of whether the grievance has raised an issue of discrimination. Any such complaint filed after a grievance has been filed on the same matter shall be dismissed without prejudice to the complainant's right to proceed through the negotiated grievance procedure including the right to appeal to the Commission from a final decision as provided in subpart D of this part. The dismissal of such a complaint shall advise the complainant of the obligation to raise discrimination in the grievance process and of the right to appeal the final grievance decision to the Commission.

As with final agency decisions in the EEO process, the Commission has review authority over decisions on claims of discrimination in the negotiated grievance procedure. Title 29 CFR 1614.401(d) provides:

A grievant may appeal the final decision of the agency, the arbitrator or the Federal Labor Relations Authority (FLRA) on the grievance when an issue of employment discrimination was raised in a negotiated grievance procedure that permits such issues to be raised. A grievant may not appeal under this part, however, when the matter initially raised in the negotiated grievance procedure is still ongoing in that process, is in arbitration, is before the FLRA, is appealable to the MSPB or if 5 USC 7121(d) is inapplicable to the involved agency.

The Commission also may review decisions of the MSPB with regard to discrimination. An employee dissatisfied with the results obtained before the Board, whether in the first instance or subsequent to the agency processing of the case under EEO procedures, has the option of seeking review before the EEOC. The EEOC's jurisdiction is limited to conducting a review of the discrimination allegations only. The basic requirements for a petition for review are set out at 29 CFR 1614.303:

(a) Who may file. Individuals who have received a final decision from the MSPB on a mixed case appeal or on the appeal of a final decision on a mixed case complaint under 5 CFR part 1201, subpart E and 5 USC 7702 may petition EEOC to consider that decision. The EEOC will not accept appeals from MSPB dismissals without prejudice.

For a full discussion of decisions of the MSPB and decisions rendered pursuant to the negotiated grievance procedure, the reader is referred to Chapter 20 under the subheadings "Mixed Cases Before the Merit Systems Protection Board" and "Negotiated Grievance Procedures."

Note that the complainant is entitled only to appellate review of decisions of discrimination rendered in other forums. Where the complainant receives a full and fair hearing on the discrimination claims in another process, the agency is not required to afford an additional investigation and administrative hearing. Smith v. National Labor Relations Board, 05910640 (1991).
