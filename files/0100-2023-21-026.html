a. Gender-Based Comments

Certainly, gender-based comments can be a strong indication of discrimination. But under the general rubric of "boys will be boys," not all gender-based comments are discriminatory.

In Roja v. Secretary of Navy, 03910085 (1991), the male complainant alleged that he was harassed by male coworkers who called him "stupid," "useless," and, in one instance, a "pussy." The complainant brought this to the attention of his male supervisor who told him to stop crying "like a woman." With respect to the comments by coworkers, the Commission found there was no sexual harassment since the term "pussy" had been used interchangeably with such gender-neutral terms as "stupid" and "useless." The Commission further found the supervisor had not engaged in discrimination since his statement "without more, does not give rise to an inference that petitioner was treated differently because he is male. Instead, it suggests that the supervisor held a stereotypical view of women." Id. Another example is Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120142054 (2014), where the complainant alleged that she was subjected to discrimination on the bases of sex and religion by being required to deliver mail to a nudist colony, The Commission found that males and females were both subjected to the requirement and there was no evidence to show that the agency was aware of the complainant's religion.

The Roja case is still good law to the extent that it stands for the proposition that the complainant must demonstrate that the conduct at issue is gender-based and that one instance of use of a term that is gender-based may not be sufficient to establish that the use of other, neutral terms are also motivated by the complainant's gender. However, the reader should be aware, as discussed below in the section on Sex of Perpetrator and Victim, that there is growing trend at the Commission and in the federal courts to examine cases to determine if the use of terms that relate to an individual's sexual orientation are a form of gender stereotyping prohibited by Title VII.

Comments and actions that indicate hostility toward a person's gender can form the basis for a complaint of gender-based harassment. For example, the supervisor's statement in Studer v. Postmaster General, 01A02838 (2000), that "[a]ll men are pigs and I'm taking it out on you" was properly considered as part of the complainant's claim of harassment based on gender. See also Wild v. Secretary of Defense, 01984101 (2000) (comment by a supervisor, "Men have two faults--everything they say and everything they do," was properly considered as part of claim of gender-based harassment).

In Horkan v. Postmaster General, 01976837 (2000), the Commission found gender-based harassment where the "agency's own investigation revealed that the atmosphere at the facility was replete with remarks about wife bashing, negative generalizations applied to all women, and demeaning comments about female public figures...." The Commission found that the general atmosphere, coupled with comments specifically aimed at the complainant, created an abusive working environment.

Coworkers created a hostile environment based on gender in Complainant v. Secretary of Army, 0720130019 (2014), where many of the comments were directed at the complainant's childcare responsibilities:

Upon review, the Commission finds that the AJ's finding of a discriminatory hostile work environment is supported by substantial record evidence. The record revealed that Complainant's co-workers, including CW1 and CW2, were angry that Complainant was supervising them and engaged in harassing conduct. Specifically, they made comments such as it was a "slap in the face" that Complainant had a t-shirt with "Special Agent" printed on it and that she had better care what the other agents thought or "her life would be hell." The coworkers yelled at Complainant and were uncooperative in assisting her gain field experience. When Complainant raised her concerns with S1, he responded "Do you blame them?" Further, the co-workers teased Complainant after finding out that she was a single mother of several children. In addition, the two co-workers accused Complainant of being disruptive and unprofessional at the two-week training course which ultimately led to her being terminated. Finally, S1 continuously asked Complainant about her family obligations, whether her family status would affect her job performance, and informed her that he would be keeping a record of how her children interfered with her ability to perform her duties. Accordingly, the Commission affirms the AJ's findings as to the existence of a hostile work environment created by her co-workers and supervisors, and the liability of the Agency for the harm that resulted.

In an en banc decision issued on January 20, 2010, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the 11th Circuit held that even in a work environment permeated by general vulgarity, gender-specific comments can create a hostile work environment that violates Title VII. In addition, in Reeves v. C.H. Robinson Worldwide, Inc., 594 F.3d 798 (11th Cir. 2010), the court found that a hostile work environment could be created even where the gender-specific comments were not directed at the plaintiff.

Reeves alleged that she worked for several years in what was a predominantly male environment where "indiscriminate vulgarity" was common. Much of the vulgarity was gender-neutral and the court noted that "Reeves herself used generic swear words, such as 'shit' or 'damn' to express her frustration or anger." However, there was also a steady stream of gender-specific vulgarity. Referring to women as "bitches" was common, as was the use of words like "cunt" and "whore" to describe women. The men also routinely listened to a radio program that "regularly broadcasted shows on topics such as the size of women's breasts..." Despite complaints to both coworkers and a manager, the conduct continued.

In reversing a grant of summary judgment for the company, a unanimous court, in Reeves, wrote:

In particular, sexual language and discussions that truly are indiscriminate do not themselves establish sexual harassment under Title VII. The Supreme Court has "never held that workplace harassment, even harassment between men and women, is automatically discrimination because of sex merely because the words used have sexual content or connotations." Id. at 80. Title VII's test instead is whether "members of one sex are exposed to disadvantageous terms or conditions of employment to which members of the other sex are not exposed." Id.

Nevertheless, a member of a protected group cannot be forced to endure pervasive, derogatory conduct and references that are gender-specific in the workplace, just because the workplace may be otherwise rife with generally indiscriminate vulgar conduct. Title VII does not offer boorish employers a free pass to discriminate against their employees specifically on account of gender just because they have tolerated pervasive but indiscriminate profanity as well. See Williams v. Gen. Motors Corp., 187 F.3d 553, 564 (6th Cir. 1999) ("We do not believe that a woman who chooses to work in the male-dominated trades relinquishes her right to be free from sexual harassment.").

Equally important to our inquiry here is the common-sense rule that the context of offending words or conduct is essential to the Title VII analysis. Even gender-specific terms cannot give rise to a cognizable Title VII claim if used in a context that plainly has no reference to gender. Thus, for example, were a frustrated sales representative to shout "Son-of-a-bitch! They lost that truck," the term would bear no reference to gender. In contrast, however, when a co-worker calls a female employee a "bitch," the word is gender-derogatory. As we observed in Baldwin, the terms "bitch" and "slut" are "more degrading to women than to men." 480 F.3d at 1302. The original definition of the term "bitch" is "the female of the dog." Webster's Third New International Dictionary 222 (2002). The term's secondary meanings are likewise gender-specific: "a lewd or immoral woman" or "a malicious, spiteful, and domineering woman." Id. Calling a female colleague a "bitch" is firmly rooted in gender. It is humiliating and degrading based on sex. Cf. Harris, 510 U.S. at 23 (holding that, to prove a hostile work environment, courts may consider whether conduct is humiliating).

As the Supreme Court has observed, "[t]he real social impact of workplace behavior often depends on a constellation of surrounding circumstances, expectations, and relationships which are not fully captured by a simple recitation of the words used or the physical acts performed." Oncale, 523 U.S. at 81-82. See also Burlington N. & Santa Fe Ry. Co. v. White, 548 U.S. 53, 69 (2006) ("Context matters."); EEOC v. WC&M Enters., Inc., 496 F.3d 393, 398-99 (5th Cir. 2007) (emphasizing the importance of context for analyzing claims under Title VII). Thus, we proceed with "[c]ommon sense, and an appropriate sensitivity to social context," to distinguish between general office vulgarity and the "conduct which a reasonable person in the plaintiff's position would find severely hostile or abusive." Oncale, 523 U.S. at 82.

A final principle that guides us in this decision is that words and conduct that are sufficiently gender-specific and either severe or pervasive may state a claim of a hostile work environment, even if the words are not directed specifically at the plaintiff. Ocheltree v. Scollon Prods., Inc., 335 F.3d 325, 331-32 (4th Cir. 2003) (en banc) (concluding that Title VII may be violated even when the plaintiff is not individually targeted). See also Petrosino v. Bell Atlantic, 385 F.3d 210, 221 (2d Cir. 2004). It is enough to hear co-workers on a daily basis refer to female colleagues as "bitches," "whores" and "cunts," to understand that they view women negatively, and in a humiliating or degrading way. The harasser need not close the circle with reference to the plaintiff specifically: "and you are a 'bitch,' too." See Yuknis v. First Student, Inc., 481 F.3d 552, 553-54 (7th Cir. 2007) (observing that comments need not be directed specifically at a person to be discriminatory; comments addressed to the plaintiff's "target area"--that is, her protected group--may constitute actionable harassment). Similarly, words or conduct with sexual content that disparately expose members of one sex to disadvantageous terms or conditions of employment also may support a claim under Title VII. See Petrosino, 385 F.3d at 222 ("[T]he depiction of women in the offensive jokes and graphics was uniformly sexually demeaning and communicated the message that women as a group were available for sexual exploitation by men.").
