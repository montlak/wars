b. Obscene, Rude, Aggressive Conduct

Gender-based harassment, even without any sexual overtures, is actionable under Title VII. See, e.g., Kelly v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01951729 (1998). In that case, the harassment involved using obscene language, flying into rages against the complainant and threatening her life. In Jones v. Postmaster General, 01940362 (1994), the Commission applied the analysis for hostile environment harassment to a case involving sex-based harassment where the allegations were not sexual in nature:

In order to establish a claim of discriminatory harassment Commission and Court precedent hold that a complainant must show that she was "subjected to harassment...sufficiently pervasive so as to alter the conditions of employment and create an abusive working environment...." Sampson v. Dept. of the Air Force, EEOC Request No. 05891005 (March 16, 1990) (quoting from Henson v. City of Dundee, 682 F.2d 897, 901 (11th Cir. 1982). This showing must include discrete comments directed against appellant or disparate treatment of her which supports an inference of discriminatory harassment. See Hicks v. Gates Rubber Co., 833 F.2d 1406, 1412-13 (10th Cir. 1987) (a case involving sexual harassment). Whether the degree of harassment is sufficiently severe to trigger a violation of Title VII must be determined by looking at all the circumstances, including the frequency of the discriminatory conduct; its severity; whether it is physically threatening or humiliating or a mere offensive utterance; and whether it unreasonably interferes with an employee's work performance. Harris v. Forklift Systems, 114 S. Ct. 367 (1993). (Footnote omitted.) A few isolated incidents of harassment are usually insufficient to establish a violation of Title VII.

Instances of being rude and abrasive and other conduct of a nonsexual nature directed toward a female complainant can sustain a charge of gender-based hostile environment.

But rude and abrasive behavior, in and of itself, does not establish the behavior is based on gender. In rejecting a claim of gender-based harassment in Taran P. v. Postmaster General, 0120141570 (2015), the Commission observed:

The statements from these witnesses are more than sufficient to establish S1's harsh behavior toward his subordinates. However, Complainant appears to be relying upon this testimony to advance the proposition that S1's bad acts in and of themselves are sufficient to establish a discriminatory motive on his part. This is simply not true. Title VII is not a civility code. McFadden v. Department of the Army, EEOC Appeal No. 0120122405 (October 12, 2012). More is required to invoke the protection of Title VII than to merely opine that a supervisor discriminated against an employee just because he did some bad things to that employee. The statute cannot protect Complainant from S1's negative behavior toward her, unless that behavior arises from a motivation based upon Complainant's gender. And on this crucial issue, while Complainant has presented sworn statements from other witnesses that establish S1's dislike of her management style, her staff, her Post Office, and perhaps of her personally, those statements are not enough to challenge the veracity of S1's assertion that he did not act upon or harbor a discriminatory animus toward women. We therefore agree with the Agency that Complainant failed to establish the existence of an unlawful motive on the part of S1 with respect to any of the incidents comprising her claim of hostile work environment.

In Young v. Postmaster General, 07A60006 (2006), the Commission found that the complainant, a female, was subjected to gender--based harassment where a coworker intimidated her with his size and loud demeanor. The Commission noted that the coworker was over six feet tall and weighed more than 200 pounds. Also, there was no evidence that the coworker treated males in a similar manner.

A pattern of hostile behavior toward employees of one gender by a coworker can state a claim of gender-based harassment. In Cliatt v. Secretary of Transportation, 01A00950 (2000), the Commission found that the complainant stated a claim where she alleged that a male coworker informed her that he did not want to talk to her, and exhibited negative behavior toward women generally, including "refusal to speak to women in the office; episodes of him shouting at women and ignoring women; holding his hand up in the face of women when spoken to; refusing to make eye contact when spoken to; abruptly interrupting female coworkers during their conversations with male employees; and turning abruptly and facing the nearest wall whenever a woman passes near him in the offices."

However, the complainant must submit some proof that the comments, if not overtly gender-based, are in fact based on sex. For example, in Jenkins v. Postmaster General, 0120101628 (2010), the Commission found no evidence of gender-based harassment where the complainant alleged that a letter carrier told an EAP counselor that he was dreaming about killing the complainant. The Commission noted that the complainant, a station manager, had a history of not getting along well with letter carriers and there was insufficient evidence in the record to suggest that the comment was made because of the complainant's sex.

In Jackson v. Secretary of Air Force, 0720110036 (2012), the Commission upheld a finding of gender-based harassment where a supervisor treated female employees more harshly than males and also used derogatory language in addressing them:

Regarding element 1, the record is undisputed that Complainant, a female, is a member of a statutorily protected class. Regarding elements 2 and 3, the record supports the AJ's determination that Complainant was subjected to unwelcome verbal and non-verbal conduct based on her sex. We note that sex-based harassment--that is, harassment not involving sexual activity or language--may give rise to Title VII liability if it is "sufficiently patterned or pervasive" and directed at employees because of their sex. See EEOC Policy Guidance on Current Issues of Sexual Harassment, No. N-915-050, at Guidance, § C. (Mar. 19, 1990).

Complainant testified about S1's negative behavior towards her and the women in the office. For example, Complainant testified that S1's "tone of voice would change when he would talk to females...he was more aggressive, he was unprofessional...He would call them stupid, incompetent, question their abilities." In addition, Complainant testified that the "women were all scrutinized and acted like they didn't know their jobs and nothing that they did was right, and were talked to in a totally different tone of voice than [E3] was." Further, Complainant testified that, with E3, S1 was "[j]ovial, pleasant, he was professional, he was patient," but with the women "it was an ongoing onslaught of hostility and throwing up hands and acting disgusted." Moreover, Complainant testified that S1, "on a continuous basis," called her "stupid," belittled her in front of other people, and made curt and rude remarks to her. Finally, Complainant testified that she found S1's behavior physically intimidating because he would scream at her while standing in her office doorway, stand behind her and lean over her, slam doors, and slam his fists on desks.

Similarly, E1 and E2 testified about S1's hostility towards Complainant and women in general. For example, E1 testified that he called Complainant a "pig," called E2 a "slob," referred to his son's mother as a "bitch," and referred to his granddaughter's mother as a "slut." In addition, E1 testified that he called the women in the office names: "Well, we were [called] stupid. We were called idiots. We've been told we don't know anything. We--we've been called pigs, fat, a few other names." Further, E1 testified that S1 spoke to women in a curt manner and "he would be very, very sharp with you. And the tone of his voice, it was more the tone of his voice, the look on his face, his body language than anything else. He made it very well known that you were beneath him." Moreover, E1 testified that S1 would raise his voice towards women and was "overbearing, threatening, intimidating with females." Finally, E2 testified that S1 belittled Complainant "almost on a daily basis" and talked to Complainant, but not the men, in a very demeaning tone, "[m]ore like he was talking to a child."

Although the Agency argued that the AJ erred in crediting the testimony of Complainant and her witnesses over the Agency's witnesses, we decline to reweigh the parties' credibility on appeal. We note that the witnesses provided conflicting testimony about S1's behavior towards women. The Agency, however, has not pointed to any objective documentary evidence contradicting the testimony of Complainant and her witnesses, nor has it shown that their testimony so lacks in credibility that a reasonable fact finder would not credit it. See EEO MD-110, at Ch. 9, § VI.B.
