11. Poirrier Settlements

The new guidance on full relief is essentially a codification in MD-110 of what had become commonly known as a "Poirrier settlement." In Poirrier v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01933308 (1994), the Commission found at the point in time the case moves to the hearing stage, the agency no longer has jurisdiction over the case. Jurisdiction of the case has been transferred to the EEOC. As a result, the agency could not make an offer of full relief at the hearing stage and dismiss the case for a failure to accept that offer. However, under Poirrier, the agency could "unconditionally and unilaterally" agree to provide full relief as defined by the administrative judge. Upon the agency's written agreement, the case could then be remanded to the agency for dismissal if the complainant rejected the offer. With the elimination of recommended decisions, it is no longer necessary for the administrative judge to remand the case to the agency for a final decision dismissing the case. Instead, the judge can issue an order dismissing the case and the agency then has 40 days to determine if it will fully implement that decision. In other words, a decision on full relief is treated like a decision by an administrative judge on the merits of the case.

The Commission began to explain more fully the parameters of such a settlement in Gerber v. Chairman, Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 01940261 (1994). In Gerber, the Commission held:

EEOC Regulation 29 CFR § 1614.107(h) [1992] sets out the requirements which an agency must follow in order to tender an offer of full relief to a complainant. The offer must be in writing. Furthermore, it must be made before an agency issues the notice required by 29 CFR § 1614.108(f), which notifies a complainant that the investigation is completed and that he or she has the right to request a hearing. See William F. Lee v. U.S. Commission on Civil Rights, EEOC Appeal No. 01930705 (August 30, 1993).

Notwithstanding the above provisions, however, an AJ--prior to the hearing--may advise the parties as to the full and complete remedy to which a complainant would be entitled should there be a finding of discrimination. See 29 CFR § 1614.501. At that point, the agency may agree to give the complainant the full and complete remedy defined by the AJ without further processing of the case. Joseph Poirrier v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 01933308 (May 5, 1994). Poirrier requires that the agency unilaterally and unconditionally must promise, in writing, to provide the complainant with the full and complete remedy defined by the AJ. If the agency does so, the AJ then may remand the case to the agency to dismiss as moot. If the agency later fails to provide the complainant with the full and complete remedy as promised, the complainant may file an appeal with the Office of Federal Operations.

The Commission permits such an agency promise in recognition of the value an AJ can bring to the process, and in recognition as well of the AJ's responsibility to propitiate the resolution of a complaint. At the hearing stage, the AJ, who is impartial and who has expertise in antidiscrimination law, is qualified to determine whether an agency offer indeed constitutes full relief for a complainant. In addition, the AJ through prehearing conferences and first-hand discussions with the parties is in an excellent position to discuss the proper scope of full relief and to explain the consequences of refusal to a complainant.

In this case, the AJ advised the parties of her view of what would constitute a full and complete remedy prior to the hearing, and the agency made a remedy offer. The agency's offer was not written, however, but rather was made by an agency attorney over the telephone. Although the AJ submitted a written representation of what the offer entailed, an AJ's representation does not equate to a written promise, as we have no evidence of the exact terms. Consequently, the Commission finds that the offer was invalid.

In addition, the record does not adequately document the basis for the AJ's determination that the offer constituted full relief for complainant. Where an offer of full relief is in accord with the issues of a complaint, such documentation would not be necessary.

Here, however, the AJ made a determination of full relief on the basis of an issue that appears to differ from the issue of the complaint. In his complaint, appellant alleged that he was not hired because the agency looked at his age on his application, and decided not to interview him. In contrast, the AJ defined the issue to be whether the agency chose to interview persons from the GS-5 list, instead of the GS-7 list on which appellant's name appeared. This change may have eliminated from consideration the question of whether the agency intentionally chose the GS-5 list because of appellant's age. Although the AJ represented that appellant agreed to the issue as newly defined, we find that interpretation of appellant's understanding questionable, given his representations on appeal. As stated above, he contends on appeal that full relief would be his selection as an Investigator. Therefore, it is possible that the AJ's determination rested on an incorrect definition of the issues in the complaint. We recognize that AJs sometimes must clarify or better define the issues that are to be heard. When a hearing is conducted, there is a hearing transcript to show the appellant's understanding of such clarifications. Here, there is no hearing transcript. In such circumstances, the AJ should obtain the parties' written agreement with any clarification or re-definition of the issues in a complaint before proceeding to a full relief determination.

Since the agency's offer was not properly made, the appellant's failure to accept it cannot be used as the basis for dismissal of appellant's complaint. Therefore, the agency's FAD must be reversed. We note that as a result of the manner in which this offer was made, appellant did not obtain the hearing that he requested. On remand, the agency should again submit appellant's complaint to the appropriate EEOC District Office for a hearing on his complaint.
