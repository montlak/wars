42. Reliance Upon Other Statutes--Privacy Act and FOIA

One common scenario in EEO cases involves the complainant who alleges, in the EEO process, that the agency violated a statute that it is not within the EEOC's jurisdiction to enforce. Several cases before the Commission have involved allegations that the agency violated the Privacy Act, the Freedom of Information Act, or the Federal Employees Compensation Act. Many of those cases have involved the issue of whether the EEO complaint presents an issue that is identical to an action already filed in another forum. The reader is referred to the section below on "Identical Complaints" for further discussion of the interrelationship between those actions.

Assuming that no other actions are pending in another forum, the critical issue when a complainant alleges that an agency has violated a statute outside of the Commission's jurisdiction is whether the complainant further alleges the agency violation is based on discriminatory reasons. In Brown v. Secretary of Navy, 01941104 (1994), the Commission explained the distinction:

The agency dismissed allegation 3 for failing to state a claim. The agency found that appellant alleged in allegation 3 that she was discriminated against when the agency violated the Privacy Act. While appellant does claim that an agency official violated the Privacy Act in allegation 3, we find that appellant is also alleging that an agency official discriminated against her on the bases of race, color, sex, and reprisal, by releasing appellant's medical and EEO information to a Hotline complaint investigator. Therefore, we find that appellant stated a claim of discrimination under EEOC Regulations and that the agency improperly dismissed allegation 3 as outside the purview of EEOC Regulations.

The agency is correct in finding that an allegation of a violation of the Privacy Act, by itself, is not within the purview of EEOC Regulations. We find, however, that appellant's reference to the Privacy Act is merely background information to show why the release of information was discriminatory.

See also Osborne v. Postmaster General, 0120083279 (2008) (complainant stated claim for reprisal in alleging that agency disclosed information from settlement discussions and the disclosure violated the Privacy Act).

The focus in such a case should not be on whether the agency actually violated the Privacy Act, but whether it applies its interpretation of the Act to all employees in a nondiscriminatory fashion. Similarly, in George v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01A02910 (2000), the Commission held that the agency must investigate the claim that confidential information was released "not to determine if it was a violation of the Privacy Act itself, but to the extent that it was done in order to harass complainant."

The Commission, however, has been inconsistent in its handling of complaints that relate to FOIA and Privacy Act violations. For example, in Pletten v. Secretary of Army, 01940754 (1994), the Commission found:

The Commission has previously held that an individual's FOIA request is not within the purview of Title VII and the Commission's regulations. See Frank P. Bucci v. Department of Education, EEOC Appeal No. 01892837 (January 4, 1993). The Commission found that it was more appropriate for an individual to appeal the agency's denial of the requested information under the FOIA's own established procedures for violations. In the present case, the record indicates that the basis of allegation (1) is that appellant's FOIA request was denied. He is, therefore, attacking the agency's decision to deny his request. Therefore, the more appropriate forum for challenging the agency's denial of the request is through the FOIA's own established procedures for violations. The Commission will, therefore, AFFIRM the agency's dismissal of allegation (1).

The fact that the alleged adverse action involves a violation of the Privacy Act does not remove the claim from EEO purview. Where the complainant alleges the agency disclosed personal records--a violation of the Privacy Act--because of her EEO activity, a claim for relief is stated. See Jenkins v. Postmaster General, 01952972 (1996). But where the complainant merely alleges a violation of the Privacy Act it does not state a claim. See, e.g., Cruz v. Postmaster General, 01A11844 (2001).

Similarly, a claim that the agency has improperly obtained information about an individual may not invoke the Privacy Act exclusively when the claim relates to medical information. For example, in Pellegrin v. Postmaster General, 01A55805 (2006), the Commission found the complainant's claim that the agency violated his privacy when it obtained past medical information was improperly dismissed as asserting a violation of the Privacy Act. The Commission found that the complainant may also be claiming a violation of the Rehabilitation Act's prohibitions on the collection and dissemination of medical information.
