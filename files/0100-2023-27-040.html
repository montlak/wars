8. Selection Certificates

The agency articulates a legitimate reason for its nonselection of a candidate where it shows that regulations prohibit a selection from a certificate after the expiration of six months. Deed v. Department of Navy, 01831174 (1984). Nor is it discriminatory when a second selection panel ranks the same candidates from the previous certificate in a different order when the candidates are evaluated on the basis of newly submitted applications.

A complaint that an employee was not placed on a promotion certificate and one alleging that the employee was subsequently not selected for the position in question are not identical complaints. "The fact that not being placed on a register may be the reason for not being selected, does not mean that it is identical to the complaint of not being selected." Richtey v. USPS, 01831833 (1983).

The mere fact that the selectee appears on the highly qualified list does not preclude an appellant from demonstrating that the selectee is not highly qualified for the position. Nor does the fact that the selecting official relied on the selection certificate necessarily insulate the agency from a discrimination charge. Evans v. Department of Army, 01810785 (1983).

Whether or not a complainant is aggrieved by the agency's return of a promotional certificate and request for additional names is dependent upon the circumstances. To be aggrieved, return of the certificate must occur in circumstances that indicate a rejection of the complainant. Finding that the complainant was not aggrieved by return of a promotional certificate prior to her rejection for the position, the Commission, in Comer v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01912386 (1991), explained:

Relying on...the Commission's previous decisions in Dollie Williams v. Department of Treasury, EEOC Appeal No. 01893636 (December 14, 1989) and Rene R. Saunders v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 01870461 (December 14, 1987), the AJ found that the agency never articulated a legitimate nondiscriminatory reason for appellant's purported "rejection" on July 3, 1989....

The agency on the other hand insists that the SO's return of the Promotional Certificate on July 3, 1989, and her request for additional names did not constitute a rejection or nonselection of appellant for the Foreman position. Upon reviewing the record as a whole, the Commission finds that the AJ erred in characterizing the SO's return of the Promotional Certificate...as a nonselection of appellant for the Foreman position. It is for this reason as well as for the reasons discussed, infra, that the Commission disagrees with the AJ's finding that appellant was subjected to sex discrimination.

First, the Commission notes that neither the Williams nor the Saunders case are controlling in this instant case. The Williams case is distinguishable from the case at hand in that in Williams, the Selecting Official actually went through the process of interviewing candidates upon his receipt of the first Promotional Certificate. It was only after completing the interview process in Williams that the Selecting Official requested that the position be reannounced. By then he had determined that the candidates he interviewed were merely "minimally qualified" for the posted position. In the instant case, the SO never interviewed any of the two candidates on the Promotional Certificate. Her mere request for additional names to consider for the Foreman position cannot be viewed as a rejection or nonselection of appellant, as found by the AJ. As pointed out by the agency in its FAD, it is routine for an SO to request additional names when fewer than three qualified candidates appear on a Promotional Certificate.... It was well within the SO's managerial discretion to make such a request. The Courts have held that in the absence of any discriminatory animus, it will not interfere with the legitimate exercise of managerial discretion by an agency. Loeb v. Textron, Inc., 600 F.2d 1003 (1st Cir. 1979). Moreover, unlike the Williams case, there was no evidence of discriminatory animus on the part of the SO towards appellant. The AJ's conclusion that the SO nonselected appellant by returning the Promotional Certificate on July 3, 1989, is further undermined by the fact that appellant and the male [candidate] were interviewed at a later date by the SO for the Foreman position.

Demonstrating that there is an error on the selection certificate with respect to the ranking of candidates does not necessarily help the complainant's case. For example, in Foster v. Secretary of Commerce, 01A13756 (2002), the Commission found no significance in such errors where, even if the complainant had received the correct scores, he would not have made the best qualified list.
