N. OFFERS OF FULL RELIEF

Under the 1999 amendments to Part 1614, the Commission has abolished certified offers of full relief and agency's the corresponding right to dismiss a complaint for failure to accept full relief. In its place, the Commission has adopted a regulation providing for offers of resolution which are discussed fully in Chapter 5 under the heading "Offers of Resolution." The offer of full relief was an idea that looked good on paper, but worked abysmally in practice. Instead of resulting in the increased settlement of cases, the net effect of the regulation was to change the focus of litigation from whether or not the agency engaged in discrimination to whether or not the agency had properly made an offer of full relief and, if so, whether or not its offer actually constituted full relief. Under the Commission's 1999 regulation amendments, agencies may no longer dismiss cases for failure to accept an offer of full relief.

Although offers of full relief have been deleted from Part 1614, a version of offers of full relief still exists in MD-110. A certified offer of full relief may now be made only at the hearing stage. If the agency agrees in writing to provide full relief, as defined by the administrative judge, the judge may issue an order determining full relief. MD-110 at 7-16 provides:

Administrative Judges shall issue an order awarding full relief where the agency unilaterally and unconditionally promises in writing to provide the full and complete remedy as defined by the Administrative Judge. To permit him/her to determine the appropriate remedy for the complaint, the Administrative Judge may require the parties to submit statements of full relief, may receive evidence including testimony, and/or require oral argument. After issuing the order and a determination of the appropriate remedy, the Administrative Judge shall return the hearing file to the agency, which shall have forty (40) days to take final action. § 1614.110(a). Once the agency takes final action, the complainant will have thirty days within which to file an appeal. § 1614.402(a). If the agency fails to provide the full and complete remedy as promised, the complainant may seek compliance from the agency and, failing that, file an appeal with the Commission. See § 1614.504(a); see also Miller v. Department of the Treasury, EEOC Request No. 05980345 (July 20, 1998); Perlingiero v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 01941176 (Feb. 24, 1995); Poirrier v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 01933308 (May 5, 1994).

Prior to the 1999 amendments to Part 1614, the Commission's regulations provided that a complaint could be dismissed by the agency when it offered full relief in a settlement agreement which was rejected by the employee. 29 CFR 1614.107(a)(7) (1992). The employee had 30 calendar days to accept or reject an offer of full relief. Under the previous regulations, offers of full relief could be made during the investigative stage. Since the EEOC lacks jurisdiction during this phase of processing, administrative judges obviously could not determine full relief. Rather, the EEO director, chief legal officer, or a designee reporting directly to the EEO director or chief legal officer, was required to certify in writing that the settlement offer constituted full relief. If the case was at the hearing stage, administrative judges were permitted to determine full relief, referred to as Poirrier settlements, discussed below.

On its face, the old regulation regarding full relief made a great deal of sense. Certainly, there is little reason to continue litigating a complaint when the agency is willing to make the complainant whole. The problem was in the execution of the provision in cases where the precise scope of relief was less than clear. In those cases, if a complainant believed the agency offer did not in fact constitute full relief, he or she was required to reject the offer and litigate the issue of relief on appeal to the EEOC. The complainant who was wrong took the risk of losing both the right to proceed with a complaint and the benefits of the offered settlement.

The 1999 amendments to Part 1614 became effective on November 9, 1999 and apply to all cases in the administrative process at that time. As a result, an agency can no longer dismiss a complaint for failure to accept full relief. In Powers v. Postmaster General, 01994964 (1999), the Commission held:

On November 9, 1999, revised regulations governing the EEOC's federal sector complaint process went into effect. These regulations apply to all Federal sector EEO complaints pending at any stage in the administrative process. Under these revisions, the Commission deleted the dismissal basis of failure to accept a certified offer of full relief from the regulations. See 64 Federal Register 37,644, 37,645(1999). In other words, agencies may no longer dismiss administrative EEO complaints due to a complainant's failure to accept a certified offer of full relief. Any dismissal on the grounds of failure to accept a certified offer of full relief is now improper under the revised regulations. Accordingly, the agency's decision in the present case must be VACATED, and the complaint REMANDED for further processing in compliance with the revised EEOC Regulations.

See also Davis v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01986601 (1999).

In Moon v. Tennessee Valley Authority, 01991775 (1999), the Commission gave a good explanation of why offers of full relief should be preserved at the hearing stage centering on the neutrality and expertise of the administrative judge. Although the case was decided before the effective date of the 1999 amendments, Moon articulates the Commission's position on full relief at the hearing stage:

[A]n Administrative Judge, prior to the hearing, may advise the parties as to the full and complete remedy to which a complainant would be entitled should there be a finding of discrimination. See 29 CFR § 1614.501. At that point, the agency may agree to give the complainant the full and complete remedy defined by the Administrative Judge without further processing of the case. Poirrier v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 01933308 (May 5, 1994). Poirrier requires that the agency unilaterally and unconditionally must promise, in writing, to provide the complainant with the full and complete remedy defined by the Administrative Judge. If the agency does so, the Administrative Judge then may remand the case to the agency to dismiss as moot. If the agency later fails to provide the complainant with the full and complete remedy as promised, the complainant may file an appeal with the Commission.

The Commission permits such an action in recognition of the value an Administrative Judge can bring to the process, and in recognition as well of the Administrative Judge's responsibility to propitiate the resolution of complaint. At the hearing stage, the Administrative Judge, who is impartial and who has expertise in anti-discrimination law, is qualified to determine whether an agency offer indeed constitutes full relief for a complainant. In addition, the Administrative Judge, through the pre-hearing conferences and first-hand discussions with the parties, is in an excellent position to discuss the proper scope of full relief and to explain the consequences of refusal to a complainant.

Upon careful review of the record, it appears that the conditions of Poirrier have not been met. Contrary to the Poirrier requirements, the record indicates that the agency, rather than the Administrative Judge, first determined what constituted full relief and offered it to appellant. Then the Administrative Judge found that the agency's offer constituted full relief. This runs counter the conditions set forth in Poirrier. See Perlingiero v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 01941176 (February 24, 1995).

In Nicholson v. Postmaster General, 01A22195 (2002), the Commission gave a long and complicated explanation of the current status of an offer of full relief, finding that if the complainant disputes the relief the claims must be analyzed to determine if they are moot:

In the instant case, we find that the "Offer of Full Relief" brokered by the AJ constitutes a Poirrier Offer of Full Relief pursuant to Poirrier v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Appeal No. 01933308 (May 5, 1994). That is, we find that the record reflects that the July 3, 2001 Offer of Full Relief contained all of the relief specified by the AJ during the pre-hearing settlement conference, and that complainant, at that time, accepted it as such. We further note that Poirrier requires dismissal on the grounds of mootness.

Therefore, because the new regulations have, in effect, eliminated the "offer of full relief," as a means of resolving EEO complaints, the issue of compliance with the agency's Poirrier offer of full relief becomes an issue of whether claims 2 and 3 were properly dismissed on the grounds of mootness pursuant to 29 C.F.R. 1614.107(a)(5). See Miller v. Department of Treasury, EEOC Appeal No. 01993839 (February 14, 2002).

After careful review of the record, we find that claims 2 and 3 are not moot because of complainant's claim that he is not being afforded the correct salary due to the agency's failure to calculate his salary in his limited duty position under ELM 16.1, Regulation 546.143(e)(1). This regulation stipulates that: "Those individual who are re-employed into a position with the same grade or step as held at the time of injury or disability receive the current salary for that grade and the step that they would have acquired if there had been no injury or disability."

On appeal, complainant avers that the agency failed to provide him with the merit increases he should have received but for the disability related absences referenced in claims 2 and 3. Moreover, we also note complainant's argument on appeal that the agency's failure to properly restore his salary under the above regulations will result in a significant negative effect on his retirement benefits. Therefore, we find that claims 2 and 3 are not moot in light of this claim which demonstrates that the effects of the alleged discrimination have not been irrevocably eradicated by the agency. See County of Los Angeles v. Davis, 440 U.S. 625, 631 (1979); Kuo v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05970343 (July 10, 1998). Accordingly, we find that the agency improperly dismissed these claims, and that they must be reinstated for a hearing, as more fully set forth in the ORDER below.

Furthermore, with regard to the return of benefits received by complainant under the Offer of Full Relief, we find that complainant is under no obligation to return these benefits at this juncture. Complainant's discrimination claims in his complaint will be addressed at the hearing. It will then be determined whether complainant is entitled to any remedies, including the amount of relief and the form of relief. See Tripicchio v. Department of Justice, EEOC Request No. 05A20432 (May 17, 2002).

The discussion below deals with cases decided under the previous regulation on full relief. Many of the disputes in this area arise over what constitutes full relief. These disputes are not likely to go away simply because the administrative judge rather than the agency defines full relief. Particularly with regard to compensatory damages, full relief cannot be precisely determined, and therefore disputes over it will no doubt continue. Except for those changes related to the procedure for making an offer of full relief and determining full relief, there is no reason to believe the substantive law on offers of full relief will change under the current procedure.

Under the former provision on offers of full relief, an agency could not require that the complainant keep the terms and conditions of an offer confidential. In Murray v. Postmaster General, 05931058 (1994), the Commission held:

Next, on our own motion, we consider the provisions in the agency's offer that require appellant to agree to the confidentiality of its terms and that the agreement could not be cited. As stated above, under our regulations and applicable case law, full relief must include everything to which appellant would be entitled if a finding of discrimination had been made. When discrimination is found, an appellant is not required to keep the finding confidential. Thus, the provision in this regard inserted by the agency is extraneous to an offer of full relief.

If appellant had accepted the agency's offer, he would not have received full relief, since the agency's proffered terms prohibit publication or citation of the agreement. Since the agency's offer was not, in fact, full relief, the agency erroneously dismissed appellant's complaint for failure to accept the certified offer for this reason. Appellant's complaint is therefore remanded for processing in accordance with this decision.

An agency is not required to admit discrimination in making an offer of full relief. Douglas-Slade v. Secretary of Interior, 01956596 (1996). There is no reason to believe that the same would not apply to orders of full relief.
