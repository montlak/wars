5. Observances on Agency Premises

Title VII, on its face, neither prohibits nor permit observances of religious occasions on agency premises. The rule, though, is rather simple. An agency an ban all such observances. If it does not, the agency may be hard-pressed to establish a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for banning a particular observance or observances.

One example where an agency was able to justify placing some limitations on an observance is Kapic v. Postmaster General, 0120120880 (2013). In that case, the complainant requested permission to hold a potluck for a Muslim holiday. She was given permission subject to the observance not interfering with work performance. She filed a complaint of religious discrimination when the agency later determined the observance could be held in a basement break room but not on the workplace floor. The Commission, in upholding the restrictions in Kapic, wrote:

In the present case, the Agency has articulated legitimate, non-discriminatory reason for its actions. Person A, the Acting Postmaster at the time, stated that Complainant asked her to celebrate a Bosnian holiday with a potluck, and she approved the request as long as it did not disrupt work performance. Person A noted she also referred Complainant to a clerk who would help take care of setting a table and other preparations for the potluck. However, Person A stated that other employees verbally complained about Complainant's aggressive behavior and her insistence that they participate in the potluck. Person A averred that once other employees felt "bullied" to participate without regard to their interests, she suggested that Complainant hold the potluck in the break room instead of the work room floor in order to include those employees who wanted to participate. The record reveals the break room was located in the basement. Person A stated that other smaller celebrations had been held in the break room, such as birthday celebrations and potlucks where everyone did not want to participate.

In the present case, we find the Agency articulate legitimate, non-discriminatory reasons for its actions. Upon review, we find Complainant failed to show by a preponderance of evidence that the Agency's actions were based on discriminatory animus. Moreover, we note this is not a case where Complainant claimed failure to accommodate her religious practices or beliefs.
