38. Privileges of Employment

The Commission construes the actions that can aggrieve an employee as extending beyond personnel actions and the terms and conditions of employment to include the privileges of employment. An example is provided by Loats v. Secretary of Air Force, 0120130255 (2013). In Loats, the complainant, who was legally blind, claimed disability discrimination when he was required to provide certification for his service dog when checking into an agency-controlled lodge. The complainant alleged that the lodge had required that he provided certification that his service animal was not pet on more than one occasion and, as a result, delayed the check-in process. The agency dismissed the complaint for failure to state claim finding that the complainant had alleged no employment harm.

The Commission, in Loats, disagreed noting:

The regulation set forth at 29 C.F.R. § 1614.107(a)(1) provides, in relevant part, that an agency shall dismiss a complaint that fails to state a claim. An agency shall accept a complaint from any aggrieved employee or applicant for employment who believes that he or she has been discriminated against by that agency because of race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age or disabling condition. 29 C.F.R. §§ 1614.103, .106(a). The Commission's federal sector case precedent has long defined an "aggrieved employee" as one who suffers a present harm or loss with respect to a term, condition, or privilege of employment for which there is a remedy. Diaz v. Dep't of the Air Force, EEOC Request No. 05931049 (Apr. 21, 1994).

The Agency summarily stated that the events that occurred at the Turumi Lodge are not related to Complainant's employment. The Commission disagrees. The record shows that Complainant was on assignment in his position as the Inspector General when he was required to go to the Osan Air Force Base lodging facility, namely the Turumi Lodge. Further, the record indicated that this lodging facility is only available to employees, dependants of employees and veterans. As such, we find that access to the Turumi Lodge is considered a privilege of employment. Complainant has asserted that the Agency has created a barrier to this privilege of employment when he has been required to repeatedly provide certification for his service animal. See e.g. Ferebee v. Dep't of Homeland Security, EEOC Appeal No. 0720100039 (April 24, 2012) (requiring the Agency to engage in barrier analysis to ensure that a beneficial loan program provided by a private entity was available for all its employees under the Commission's Management Directive 715). Therefore, we find that the Agency's dismissal is not appropriate.
