3. Pro Se Complainants

It should not be surprising that pro se complainants are given wide latitude in responding to discovery requests. As a general rule, sanctions should not be imposed upon a complainant who has attempted to respond in good faith to an agency's discovery requests even if the responses are technically lacking. A good example of the Commission's approach here is Abulsaad v. Secretary of Navy, 0120102379 (2012), where the Commission reversed a order of the administrative judge sanctioning the complainant by canceling his hearing request and remanded the case to the agency for a final decision:

As an initial matter, we disagree with the AJ that Complainant did not fully respond to interrogatory 10. Consistent with his statement during the investigation, in his reply Complainant indicated he was not pursing sex discrimination as a basis, which was responsive to the interrogatory. No magic words or special language is required to drop a claim of discrimination. Next, the Agency did not file a motion to compel a response on interrogatory 18, and the AJ's order granting the agency's motion to compel discovery did not refer to this interrogatory. Accordingly, the AJ had no grounds to sanction Complainant for not responding to interrogatory 18.

Interrogatories 8, 9, and 11 broadly asked Complainant to specifically and in detail provide comprehensive information on every incident of alleged harassment. Given the broad nature of these interrogatories and the numerous alleged incidents of harassment, we find that Complainant's reference to the report of investigation, which contained extensive information and documentation on his claims, was sufficiently responsive to these interrogatories.

We disagree with the AJ's finding that Complainant's reference to his discovery request was not a complete response to interrogatory 12. In his interrogatory questions, Complainant set forth in detail the reasons he was questioning the credibility of witnesses. While in question format, the interrogatories stated Complainant's beliefs about credibility. Contrary to the finding of the AJ, we find Complainant fully responded to interrogatories 13 and 17. In his responses to these compensatory damages questions and his other discovery responses Complainant clarified that he was not seeking damages for any medical condition or emotional distress, mental anguish, or emotional pain. Rather, he was seeking damages for loss to his professional reputation, which resulted in a loss of income after he was separated from the Agency. In his discovery responses, Complainant indicated he tried to mitigate these damages by asking for his job back and continuing to practice. He wrote that no complete description could presently be provided on his loss. This is not surprising since Complainant responded that his loss in income was ongoing, and there is no indication he hired an expert financial witness to help him quantify the losses. We find that Complainant's response to these questions was sufficient.

We disagree with the AJ that Complainant did not fully respond to request for production of documents number 17. Complainant did so by clarifying he was not seeking non-pecuniary damages for emotional harm.

In response to request for production of documents numbers 6 and 8, Complainant referred to his "confidential" tax returns, a reference to his documentation on loss of income, but did not supply the tax returns. This reply was responsive insofar as it indicated Complainant was only seeking damages for a loss of income. Certainly, a complete response to request for production of documents numbers 6 and 8 would include a copy of the tax returns. However, we find that the AJ abused her discretion in dismissing Complainant's request for a hearing for this failing. Complainant did not ignore the Agency's discovery requests, and was responsive to them. He was not represented during the hearings process. He was never specifically ordered by the AJ to provide the tax returns, nor did the Agency ask for them in a follow-up discovery request. Even if Complainant supplied his tax return for 2008, which is what he appeared to have in mind, this alone would have been of little help since what would be probative in demonstrating a loss of income via tax returns would be years of tax returns before and after his separation to show income trends. Also, given the posture of the case at the time, the Agency was not prejudiced by Complainant's response.

For the reasons set forth above, we conclude that the AJ abused her discretion in imposing on Complainant the sanction of loss of his right to a hearing in this case. Accordingly, we VACATE the Agency's final decision and REMAND the complaint for further processing in accordance with the following Order.
