2. Education and Licenses

Advanced education may not provide adequate justification for a promotion selection when the vacancy announcement does not require such an education. See, e.g, Complainant v. Secretary of Navy, 0120131000 (2015) ("We note that even though a certain level of education may not be required for a position, an Agency is free to consider the education of the applicants in making selection decisions."). When the agency offers such a justification for its selection, other circumstances surrounding the promotion must be examined for pretext. See Miller v. Department of Army, 01830945 (1984) (appellant also adduced evidence showing that selecting official did not choose a black candidate in 15 prior selections and did not conduct interviews or review the official personnel file of the best qualified candidates). Conversely, the fact that an unsuccessful candidate possesses a college degree does not make that candidate better qualified than the selectee when the position does not require such a degree. See Wrenn v. Secretary of Navy, 01902959 (1990); Smith v. Department of Health and Human Services, 01830744 (1983). A college degree may add to a candidate's qualifications in much the same way as extensive work experience. See Currie v. Department of Navy, 01831303 (1987). See also Hardwick v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, 0120110520 (2013) (selecting 35 year-old candidate with law school degree was not evidence of agency discrimination where job did not require such a degree but ability to research and analyze legal issues were useful skills for an investigator).

When a candidate for a position does have the required education, he cannot complain of discrimination because his college failed to submit his transcript prior to the closing of the vacancy announcement. Such an allegation does not state a claim because there is no contention that he suffered harm as the result of any action taken by the agency. Dressler v. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, 01A13837 (2002).

Even though a particular license is not a requirement for a position does not mean it is not a relevant factor to consider for selection purposes. For example, in Crowder v. Secretary of Energy, 01QA53005 (2006), the selectee was a certified public accountant. Although a CPA was not required for the position, the Commission found that it was relevant to the position, which required an extensive knowledge of federal accounting principles.
