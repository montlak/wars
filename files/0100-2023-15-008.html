broida eeo Chapter 06: EEO Hearings Chapter 06: EEO Hearings, Subchapter 01: Prehearing Matters, Section B: Right to Hearing, Subsection 01: Request for Hearing, Part a: Timing
a. Timing

Requests for a hearing must be filed with the appropriate district office of the EEOC, in writing, within 30 calendar days of receipt of notice that the investigation is complete. See 29 CFR 1614.108(f) and (g). The complainant may also request a hearing at any time after the formal complaint has been pending for more than 180 days with the agency whether or not an investigation has been conducted and the notice of right to a hearing has issued. In cases where there has been an amendment to the complaint, the agency may have up to 360 days from the filing of the original complaint to complete an investigation. However, the complainant may request a hearing at any time after the original complaint has been pending for 180 days. The agency must inform the complainant of the proper EEOC office with which to file the hearing request. When the employee submits a late request for a hearing, the agency does not abuse its discretion by issuing a final decision on the complaint without a hearing. See McKenzie v. USPS, 01831158 (1983).

Although the Commission's regulation gives the complainant a right to request a hearing at any time after the formal complaint has been pending for 180 days regardless of whether a hearing notice has been issued, the same is not true of a counseling request. In Gill v. National Aeronautics and Space Administration, 01A54522 (2006), the Commission found that an administrative judge's dismissal of a complaint for untimely EEO counselor contact was premature where the complainant had never received a notice of final interview and, as a result, had never filed a formal complaint. The Commission remanded the case for counseling.

As with all other timeframes in the EEO process, when the complainant is represented by an attorney and the agency has been notified of that fact, all timelines run from receipt of documents by the attorney and not the complainant. A complainant has 30 days from receipt of the notice of hearing rights by her attorney to request a hearing. See, e.g., Crawford v. Postmaster General, 0120082178 (2010), citing 29 CFR 1614.605(d).

The employee has the full regulatory time period to elect a hearing. Even if the appellant initially indicates that he does not desire a hearing, he is entitled to one if he later informs the agency of his desire for a hearing in writing prior to the expiration of the time period. Frick v. USPS, 01852127 (1987). Similarly, in Conner v. Secretary of Defense, 01970665 (1999), the Commission rejected an agency argument that the complainant could have requested a hearing earlier in the 30-day period where he suffered a heart attack one week before expiration of the time limit. The Commission found that the agency should have granted the request of complainant's counsel for an extension of time to elect a hearing.

Just as with other EEO time limits, the time limit for requesting a hearing can be extended under limited circumstances. For example, in Mente v. Secretary of Army, 05A20062 (2002), the complainant timely requested a hearing but, because of his deployment to Bosnia, could not advise the administrative judge of when he would be available for a hearing. The judge remanded the case to the agency to hold the complainant in abeyance and gave the complainant the right to renew his request for a hearing, if exercised promptly, after his return from Bosnia. Although the complainant did not renew his request until five months after his return, the Commission reversed the agency's final decision on the record and remanded the case for a hearing. The Commission in Mente noted that, having served in Bosnia for over a year, the complainant had many personal things to attend to upon his return and that the agency was not prejudiced by the complainant's delay in renewing his request for a hearing since it did not issue it decision until after it was notified of the request.

In Stillinger v. Postmaster General, 01962749 (1998), the Commission tolled the time limit for requesting a hearing due to the severe illness of one of the complainant's two lawyers, who subsequently died, and the absence of the other lawyer, who was out of the country, when a copy of the investigative file was sent to the lawyers' firm.

In Burgess v. Postmaster General, 0120062895 (2007), the Commission rejected an argument that the complainant's untimely request for a hearing should be excused on the basis that the agency often transmitted complaint files to EEOC district offices in an untimely fashion. Without specifically addressing the argument, the Commission found that the complainant had not justified her untimely request.

In Woods v. Postmaster General, 01A61047 (2006), the Commission applied the principle of equitable estoppel to find that the complainant's hearing request was timely. In that case, the complainant received a copy of the right to hearing notice, but her representative did not. She contacted an EEO service analyst with whom she had previously worked and requested a 30--day extension to request a hearing. The agency, despite a hearing request submitted within the 30--day extension period, issued a final agency decision. On appeal, the agency argued that the service analyst was without authority to grant the extension. The Commission concluded that the hearing request was timely under the principle of equitable estoppel.

Under the previous regulations, when an agency claimed that it never received a request for a hearing, it was not sufficient for the complainant merely to produce a copy of the request. The complainant was also required to produce some evidence of mailing or receipt by the agency, such as a certified receipt or some other acknowledgment of receipt. Reilly v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, SSA, 01950622 (1996). There is no reason to believe the Commission would not apply a similar rule in evaluating the assertion of one of its district offices that it never received the complainant's request for a hearing.

In Currie v. Postmaster General, 01954361 (1997), decided prior to the 1999 amendments to Part 1614, the Commission held that the appellant was not entitled to a hearing because she mailed her request to the EEOC District Office on the last day in the time period, rather than directing it to the agency as required under the previous regulations:

Appellant's mailing to the EEOC District Office was received on the last day of the relevant time limitation period. Given this, even if the EEOC District Office acted expeditiously, the agency would not have timely received the renewal hearing request. Reeves v. Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05950035 (April 20, 1995) (A complainant incorrectly mailed an appeal from a final agency decision to the agency rather than the Commission. The agency received the appeal on a Thursday, two days before the expiration of the time limit to file the appeal. With a letter copied to the complainant and dated the following Monday, the agency forwarded the appeal to the Commission, and it was received eight days later. In finding the appeal untimely, the Commission ruled that the agency acted promptly when it forwarded the appeal, with notice to appellant, within two working days). Thus we find that appellant is not entitled to a hearing.

Of course, under the current regulations, the EEOC District Office is the proper place to mail the request. 29 CFR 1614.108(g). It is unclear if the converse would now hold true, and filing with the agency at or near the end of the time limit would render a request untimely if it was not received by the EEOC within the 30-day period.

But, in Reynolds v. Postmaster General, 01990618 (2000), the Commission found that a mis-faxed request for a hearing was timely. The Commission noted that the complainant's attorney used a facsimile number that appeared at the bottom of a letter from the agency's Vice President of Human Resources. The Commission remanded the complaint to the appropriate EEOC district office "[s]ince the request was sent timely and with the intent of requesting a hearing." Id.

An employee who suffers from severe medical and psychological conditions may be excused for failing to timely request a hearing. See Spiteri v. Department of Navy, 01870799 (1987). In that case, the agency was aware of the complainant's conditions and agreed to stay further proceedings until the complainant recovered. Having affirmatively agreed to a stay of the proceedings, the agency then proceeded to issue a proposed disposition of the complaint. The Commission did not make clear whether it would waive the time limitation for employees in the absence of affirmative agency action to mislead the complainant. The better view is that a waiver should be granted if the conditions legitimately interfere with the employee's ability to timely request a hearing. See also Conner v. Secretary of Defense, 01970665 (1999).
