PERFORMANCE IMPROVEMENT PLANS

Over the years, agencies have developed different mechanisms for providing employees with a formal opportunity to improve unacceptable performance. Most agencies are familiar with, and have used, the term "performance improvement period," often referred to as a "PIP." However, a number of agencies have leaned away from the term "PIP" in favor of "opportunity period," "demonstration opportunity period," "notices of opportunity to improve performance," or "demonstration period."

Regardless of the name applied, these formal opportunities are designed to give the employee a chance to bring their performance up to an acceptable level. It is also the manager's opportunity to clearly express their expectations and the consequences of not meeting those expectations. Since they all involve the same principles, we will use PIP as a generic reference to a formal period for improving unacceptable performance.

Initial placement on PIP. Employees should be placed on a PIP only if their performance is unacceptable. A PIP cannot be used to build a case to remove an employee for reasons that are discriminatory, if the employee has been performing acceptably. If an employee appeals a removal that is based on a PIP to the MSPB, the agency must justify its decision to place the employee on the PIP in the first place. The agency will need to prove that the employee's performance was unacceptable before the PIP, and that it remained unacceptable during the PIP. The employee also gets an opportunity to try to show that the motive for imposing the PIP (and ultimately the removal) was discriminatory.

Don't delay. Some managers hesitate to take action against an employee who is a poor performer because the process is time-consuming and complicated. Putting an employee on a performance improvement plan may seem like a lot of work. However, your agency expects you to act quickly when you have a poor performer. If you are pursuing a performance-based adverse action under Chapter 43 of Title 5, you are legally required to provide that employee with an opportunity to improve before taking the action.

The good news is that your HR office is ready to help. You should always consult HR for technical and strategic guidance when dealing with poor performers.

A performance improvement plan, or PIP, is a written memorandum that:

  • • Communicates the standards governing the employee's performance.
  • • Indicates how the employee's performance is unacceptable in one or more critical elements.
  • • Describes the level of performance the employee must achieve to be rated at the "successful" level.

It provides a deadline for performance improvement and specifies in writing that an action for unacceptable performance -- reassignment, reduction in grade, or removal -- will be taken if improvement does not take place by that time. It also provides the employee with a required "opportunity to improve."

When to use a PIP

A PIP is called for when:

  • • An employee's performance is unacceptable in one or more critical elements of their performance plan; and
  • • Prior efforts at corrective action, such as counseling and letters of warning, have not worked; and
  • • You have thoroughly documented examples of the employee's poor performance.

Let's say you have an employee who forgets a laptop with top secret information in it and leaves it in a taxi. Is that employee a candidate for a PIP? No. That's a conduct issue and not a performance issue.

You should also not use a PIP if an employee's poor performance is endangering the health and safety of the employee, other employees, or the public; compromising security; or costing serious money. You should move straight to a disciplinary action under Chapter 75 on the basis of misconduct.

Always check with your HR office to determine if an employee's work deficiencies should be addressed as a performance- or misconduct-related matter.

Do PIPs work? In other words, will the employee's performance improve by the end of the PIP period to a satisfactory level? Not always. The purpose of a PIP is to provide the employee with a chance to improve their performance. Your goal as manager is for the employee to improve and sustain that level of improvement.

However, not every employee will make the necessary progress. If the employee cannot or will not improve sufficiently, you should be prepared to move ahead with a reassignment, demotion or removal action at the end of the PIP period.

Preparing a PIP

Let's say you have an employee who is a prime candidate for a PIP. You've tried counseling and written warnings. None of them have worked. You spoke to HR and together you decided to go forward with the PIP. How do you put one together?

A PIP generally follows a format determined by each agency. However, they are all broadly similar. The PIP is a very detailed and lengthy memorandum, with all the elements of a counseling memorandum, but expanded and made very specific.

It provides a deadline for performance improvement and specifies in writing that a performance-based action -- reassignment, reduction in grade, or removal -- will be taken if improvement does not take place by that time, or if unacceptable performance returns within a year from the date of the PIP.

The PIP should be written in conjunction with a specialist in HR, who knows your agency's specific requirements. The PIP should include at least the following sections:

  • • Introduction. This explains the purpose of the PIP.
  • • Elements and standards. This lays out the precise critical performance elements and standards the employee has failed to meet.
  • • Deficiencies. This section describes specific examples of the deficiencies under each applicable standard.
  • • Improvements required. After each performance deficiency, list the precise improvements in performance that are required.
  • • Time period. State the time the PIP is to run, in accordance with agency regulations and the collective bargaining agreement, if there is one. Typically, PIPs run between 30 and 120 calendar days.

Be prepared to monitor and document the employee's progress once the PIP is issued.

It's also critical that you carry out any tasks that you agreed to perform in the PIP. If the PIP provides for meetings, make sure they are held as planned. If the PIP states that you will provide feedback to the employee, be sure to provide it and document it in writing.

Monitoring and follow-through

Preparing a PIP can be time-consuming and challenging. Once you've drawn up the document and issued it to your employee, your job is still far from over. The purpose of a PIP will be fully served with your continued involvement. While the burden of improvement ultimately rests with your employee, you must do all you can to enable and guide him through it. This approach will prove valuable for one of two reasons:

  1. 1. To facilitate the improvement of your employee's performance; or
  2. 2. To demonstrate that you made every effort to improve their performance if they fail.

Stay involved. Commit your involvement to writing by including the terms of your assistance to your employee in the PIP document. Assistance may come in many forms and may even involve other employees. Be sure you spell it out clearly and you actually perform all the tasks you have agreed to in the PIP. Your assistance may consist of regular meetings with your employee to examine progress, providing timely feedback, or arranging training or shadowing of another employee.

Warning: You are generally not required to provide expensive formal training at this point. Be realistic and never offer anything you can't actually deliver. Check with HR about your agency's specific practices on training during a PIP.

Remember to document any assistance you give, and whenever possible, provide your employee written feedback. Feedback in writing during a PIP:

  • • Creates a reference document for your employee.
  • • Reinforces to your employee the seriousness of your concern and involvement.
  • • Reduces employee claims of ignorance, miscommunication, or misunderstanding.
  • • Documents the guidance you provide.

Track and record progress. Your employee's response to the PIP will determine if it's successful or not. That's why your careful monitoring and documentation during the PIP period is critical. Tracking their progress will dictate your later actions, but nothing can happen without your detailed records. If the PIP works and your employee improves, it's a win-win for everybody. However, keeping a complete record of unacceptable performance and your efforts to help the employee improve will strengthen your case if personnel action is taken.

Specify improvement requirements. As your employee progresses through the PIP period, they may fail to reach a performance requirement along the way. Don't wait until the PIP expires to deal with the issue. As they occur, point out each performance deficiency and provide your employee the specific improvements required. Give precise instructions without contradicting prior directives. By doing so, you will document and establish the basis for later actions if needed.

You may encounter an employee who kicks their performance level into high gear during the PIP, but soon after the PIP period expires, they revert back to unacceptable performance levels. You don't have to tolerate this "selective performance roller coaster." The best way to account for this is to write a one-year clause into the PIP which states that action may be taken for up to one year following the start of the PIP period should unacceptable performance surface again.

PIP extensions

Depending on agency policy, extending the PIP period may be permissible. But think carefully before doing it. If you are considering an extension, it usually means that your employee has failed in some way to satisfy the requirements of the PIP within the allotted time frame. The performance areas that you list in the PIP are critical elements and standards of the job. If your employee is unable to raise their performance level to adequately address those important aspects of their job, then granting more time may be delaying the inevitable adverse action.

Every situation is unique so it's best to check your agency procedures manual and consult with HR to find out if you can or should extend the PIP period.

Take action when the PIP is unsuccessful

The obvious goal of a PIP is to improve employee performance. If this occurs and the improved performance sustains, then no further action need be taken aside from documenting that the employee satisfied the PIP requirements. However, you need to be prepared to act if the PIP does not succeed.

If your employee fails to improve, you must initiate an action for reassignment, reduction in grade, or removal. Keep in mind that reassignment is not usually your best option. It merely moves the problem to another supervisor.

If you are able to demonstrate that you met your responsibilities and gave your employee a legitimate chance and enough time to improve, then you don't have to wait until the end of the PIP period to take an action. Regardless of when you initiate the personnel action, a thoroughly prepared and properly executed PIP should be able to withstand an appeal to the MSPB or an arbitrator.

Stay in close contact with HR and your legal counsel during all phases of the PIP process. They will be able to offer you the best guidance.

Special considerations

While the opportunity period or PIP will usually go as planned, you may encounter some issues along the way that were unforeseen. These will require your attention and careful handling so always check with your servicing HR office before acting. Here are some of those issues.

Requests for accommodations. When counseling or taking performance-based actions, you may learn that a performance problem is caused by a mental or physical condition. As a result, the employee may request some type of accommodation. You are required to accommodate the employee if they have a disability and are a qualified individual with a disability, provided that the accommodation request does not cause your agency an undue hardship. In this situation, you'll need to involve HR sooner rather than later.

The purpose of an accommodation is to address an employee's physical or mental limitations so that the employee has as much of a chance to achieve acceptable performance as a person without a disability. Each situation is unique, but keep in mind that your responsibility is to resolve the performance problem, not the mental or physical disability. A request for accommodation does not preclude you from proceeding with a performance-based action. In many situations, accommodations can be put be in place at the same time an opportunity period is started.

Requests for leave. Another situation that you may encounter is what to do if an employee requests leave during the opportunity period. Consider each request for leave based on the specific circumstances in the request. Understand and follow the rules of your agency for leave approval and get advice from your HR office before you deny any leave during this time. Keep the following in mind:

  • • An employee on approved leave (annual, sick, or leave without pay) cannot be penalized for work that is not completed while on approved leave.
  • • Be sure the employee is aware of agency (or office) procedures for requesting leave and for providing medical documentation (especially for accommodation requests). The employee should also be aware of what action the agency may take if these procedures are not followed.
  • • Be sure you understand the various family-friendly leave entitlements available to employees, such as the Family and Medical Leave Act.
  • • If an employee is on approved leave for a significant period of time during the opportunity period, you may want to consider extending the period to allow the employee a "reasonable" time on the job to improve.

Ratings and within-grade increases. If an unacceptable rating is not given to the employee prior to the PIP, many managers question how this affects a within-grade increase. The general concern is that if the last rating of record is acceptable, then how can a manager justify denying a WIGI that comes due during an opportunity period?

First and foremost, the prospect of issuing a PIP is not a justifiable reason to change an acceptable rating to unacceptable. So, for purposes of issuing a PIP, the last rating of record is irrelevant. Just issue the PIP if it's time to do so and don't wait for the annual rating cycle to provide an unacceptable rating prior to initiating the PIP.

Now, if a within-grade increase comes due during an employee's PIP period, OPM regulations say that you need to review the employee's most recent rating and determine if a new rating is needed to support the within-grade decision. The regulations provide that a supervisor, in making an "acceptable level of competence" determination, may issue a new rating if the most recent rating does not reflect the employee's current performance. So, if the last rating of record does not support a within-grade denial, a new rating of record must be given for that purpose, and this is permissible.

If your agency uses the last rating of record which was at the Fully Successful level, and the WIGI is granted during the PIP, your agency is certifying that the employee is performing at that level. This would make it difficult to sustain any action against the employee on appeal. The whole point is to make sure that no employee who is performing unacceptably receives a within-grade increase. It's forbidden by law.

Also, you should not delay a within-grade determination while an employee completes a PIP. Being on a PIP is not a justifiable reason to delay a within-grade determination. Unfortunately, you can't try to time a PIP so that the rating and within-grade cycles come up after the PIP period.

Once a WIGI has been denied, you have the flexibility to approve a within-grade increase at any time thereafter once the employee is determined to be performing at an acceptable level of competence.

Before you issue a PIP, it's a good idea to check with HR to determine the employee's WIGI cycle so that everyone is on the same page with respect to the PIP period and the WIGI determination, if it comes due. Don't sit on a performance problem until you think the time is right. As indicated here, there are processes in place to ensure that ratings don't conflict with WIGI justifications.

Mistakes to avoid

Placing an employee on a performance improvement plan may seem like a daunting task. But it's really not that difficult in most cases -- it just takes time. When done correctly, it can prove to be an efficient way of dealing with poor performers -- they either improve or face adverse action up to and including removal. When done incorrectly, it can create more problems.

Do not:

  • • Fail to provide necessary instruction or assistance. If the performance standards for the position are not completely clear, it is up to you to supplement them. Similarly, if the employee could improve if you provided a reasonable accommodation of a disability, you must do so.
  • • Assume an employee's performance will improve when placed on a PIP. If you've placed an employee on a PIP, there is a problem with that employee's performance. You've tried counseling and warnings. They didn't work. Don't assume the PIP will automatically work. You must be prepared to monitor the employee's progress on the PIP and document problems.
  • • Change an employee's duties or duty station during a performance improvement period. This is almost always a bad idea. On appeal, the Merit Systems Protection Board or arbitrator will look at the employee's performance of their normally assigned duties as described in the position description during the PIP. This is no time to change the duties or where they are performed.
  • • Fail to take action when appropriate. There's no doubt that taking on performance problems requires time and effort. But not taking them on will undermine your agency's mission and brand you as an ineffective manager.

Ask FMG

Q. How can I encourage better performance?

A. First of all, model good performance yourself. Next, monitor your employees' work closely. Make sure your employees know that you notice and appreciate good work. Offer suggestions and assistance where needed, praise and reward good work, and deal promptly with unacceptable performance with counseling and formal action, if necessary.

Q. How long should a performance improvement period last?

A. The length of a PIP depends on the situation, your agency's policies, and the collective bargaining agreement, if there is one. PIPs have traditionally lasted for a period ranging from 30 to 120 days. After the PIP period concludes, a determination is made. If the employee has demonstrated acceptable performance at this point, the PIP continues in effect for one year from the date it was issued. Check with your HR office for help with a specific situation.

Q. Do I have to wait until a PIP is expired before I can take action?

A. No, you can take action at any time during the PIP -- so long as you have provided the employee a fair chance to demonstrate acceptable performance.

Q. Should I provide remedial training while the employee is on a PIP?

A. Not necessarily. You should provide an employee with assistance during the opportunity to improve. This should be in the form of feedback and guidance. If remedial training is likely to improve the employee's performance, it probably should be offered before the PIP stage. However, agencies differ in their approach to training during a PIP. Contact your HR office to see how your agency handles these situations.

Q. Do I have to issue another PIP if an employee improves to an acceptable level of performance, then sinks back to unacceptable a few months later?

A. Not if the relapse into poor performance happens within 12 months of the first PIP and is limited to those same critical element(s). You can propose an adverse action immediately in this case. Ask your HR expert for advice and assistance.

Q. Do I have to use a PIP to terminate an unacceptable performer who is on probation?

A. No. The point of probation is to make it fast and easy to remove employees who don't meet performance or conduct requirements. You should not issue a PIP to a probationary employee.

Q. Do I have to wait some minimum amount of time into the rating period before I can place an employee on a PIP?

A. No, you should not wait to act once the employee's performance falls below the acceptable standard of a critical element and counseling and warnings have not helped. The only exception is when you give an employee a new critical element during a performance rating. Then you must wait a reasonable amount of time -- say 30 to 60 days -- before placing the employee on a PIP for failing to meet the new element.
