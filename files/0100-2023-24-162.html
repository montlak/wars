1. Parking Spaces

While the ADA adopted the current "disability" terminology and abandoned the use of the term "handicapped," there remain some vestiges of its use most notable in terms like "handicapped parking space" and "handicapped ramp." Parking spaces near to facilities is both a commonly requested accommodation and a common accommodation. For employees with physical disabilities that make movement difficult, an accessible parking space is "'job related' because it concerns Complainant's ability to access the job site." Kim S. v. Postmaster General, 2020000819 (2021). Problems sometimes arise when facilities are controlled not by the employing agency, but by the General Services Administration. Agencies do, however, have an obligation to seek the cooperation of GSA in securing such an accommodation if it is otherwise appropriate.

In Gregory F. v. Postmaster General, 2021004708 (2021), the Commission found that failure to provide an employee a "disabled employee parking spot" stated a viable discrimination claim. The complainant further alleged that the two parking spots provided were not wheel chair accessible. The agency dismissed the complaint reasoning that it failed to "show he was harmed, subjected to an adverse action, or denied an entitlement related to a term, condition or privilege of his employment." The Commission reversed:

As noted above, the Agency asserted: "There is no indication that you suffered any harm or loss with respect to a term, condition, or privilege of employment for which there is a remedy[,]" and that the "complaint does not identify with any specificity what actions were taken against you and/or what specific harm you suffered as a result and is, therefore, a generalized grievance which fails to state an actionable claim." See Administrative File at 24. The Commission, however, disagrees.

Complainant asserted that he was not provided adequate, accessible, parking at the Facility. Further, he contended that, at least for a time, the Agency did not provide any disability accessible parking for employees. When the Agency did designate two spots, Complainant argued they were insufficient in both quantity and design to afford accessibility to disabled employees, including himself. Terms, conditions, or privileges of employment include, inter alia, promotions, demotions, discipline, reasonable accommodations, appraisals, awards, training, benefits, assignments, overtime, leave, tours of duty, etc. Cobb v. Dep't of the Treasury, EEOC Request No. 05970077 (March 13, 1997); Becerra v. Dep't of the Navy, EEOC Appeal No. 0120083490 (Jan. 1, 2012) (emphasis omitted); see also, Henery v. Dep't of the Navy, EEOC Appeal Nos. 01A22792 and 01A22794 (April 2, 2003) (Agency dismissal of claim that facility did not meet access and parking requirements for disabled employees, including Complainant, was reversed).

In light of the above, the Commission finds that Complainant's formal complaint presents a justiciable claim. The Agency's decision to dismiss the complaint was improper.

In Heard v. Secretary of Treasury, 0120110751 (2013), the Commission rejected an agency argument based in part on the premise that it would have been an undue hardship for the agency to attempt to purchase a space for the complainant from GSA. The Commission also found that the agency erred in taking away a temporary accommodation of a space and requesting updated medical information where the complainant had MS and the condition was permanent. The Commission in Heard found:

Upon review, we find that the Agency failed to provide Complainant with an effective reasonable accommodation. We note that Complainant and her doctor requested that she be provided with a permanent handicapped parking spot because of her MS. We note that the Agency only provided Complainant with a temporary handicapped spot, which it continuously threatened to take it away. The Agency does not dispute that it revoked Complainant's handicapped spot on March 19, 2010, and threatened to discipline her if she failed to return her parking card key. The record reflects that both Complainant's doctor and the Agency's EEO Manager communicated to management that Complainant's condition was permanent. As such, management was clearly provided with medical documentation that indicated that Complainant has MS, a permanent disability. The Agency's EEO Manager asked that the parking permit be renewed because Complainant's medical documentation indicated a need for a permanent handicapped parking space. Despite Complainant's medical documentation and the EEO Manager's request, management asked Complainant to return her handicapped parking key card, and informed her that failure to return the card would result in disciplinary action. Instead of simply providing Complainant with a permanent spot, management redundantly and needlessly asked Complaint for more information from her doctor. The temporary handicapped parking provided to Complainant from June 1, 2009, to March 19, 2010, was not an effective accommodation for a permanent condition. See U.S. Airways v. Barnett, 535 U.S. 391, 400 (2002) (a reasonable accommodation must be effective).

The burden now shifts to the employer to provide case-specific evidence proving that providing reasonable accommodation would cause an undue hardship in the particular circumstances. See Reasonable Accommodation Guidance. The Agency noted that it refused Complainant's request for a permanent handicapped parking spot because it only had one temporary space left, which it needed to make available to other employees. A determination of undue hardship should be based on several factors, including: (1) the nature and cost of the accommodation needed; (2) the overall financial resources of the facility making the reasonable accommodation; the number of persons employed at this facility; the effect on expenses and resources of the facility; (3) the overall financial resources, size, number of employees, and type and location of facilities of the employer; (4) the type of operation of the employer, including the structure and functions of the workforce, the geographic separateness, and the administrative or fiscal relationship of the facility involved in making the accommodation to the employer; and (5) the impact of the accommodation on the operation of the facility. See Preston v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 0120054230 (Aug. 9, 2007); Reasonable Accommodation Guidance.

We find that the Agency has failed to meet its burden of establishing that Complainant could not be accommodated without an undue hardship to the Agency. The record reflects that the AO notified management via e-mail that they should contact the GSA building manager to purchase a spot for Complainant in the Howard Street Lot. There is no evidence in the record that management contacted the GSA as instructed. Further, the investigation revealed that the Agency was responsible for contributing funds for parking spots for employees. Given the size and annual budget of the Agency, we find that any claim by the Agency that providing a permanent handicapped parking spot for Complainant in circumstances such as this would result in an undue hardship to be without merit. See Degnan v. U.S. Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01A53689 (Mar. 23, 2006); Reasonable Accommodation Guidance. Accordingly, we find that the Agency subjected Complainant to discrimination based on disability when it failed to accommodate Complainant by providing her with a permanent handicapped parking spot.
