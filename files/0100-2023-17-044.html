2. Witness Credibility

Witness credibility is covered extensively in Chapter 6 on Hearings under the subheading "Credibility Determinations" because it is the administrative judge, in the first instance, who makes findings of credibility. For a detailed discussion of credibility, see Chapter 6.

Rarely is credibility an issue of first impression at the appellate level. One of the few exceptions arises where a party attempts to make a post hoc attack on witness credibility. The Commission is not very receptive to post hoc attacks on witness credibility that are premised on subsequently obtained evidence that is not in the record. In Volpe v. Postmaster General, 01940140 (1994), the Commission rejected the complainant's credibility attack on the selecting official where the complainant, belatedly, attempted to impeach his testimony. The selecting official testified that he would not discriminate against persons of Italian origin because his father-in-law was from Italy. The complainant, on appeal, submitted a copy of a death certificate showing the selecting official's father-in-law had been born in the United States. The Commission rejected the attack, noting it "does not find it appropriate to invoke a measure as draconian as total exclusion of the SO's testimony because the SO apparently misidentified the birthplace of his late father-in-law." Id.

Likewise, noting that the complainant passed up the opportunity for a hearing, the Commission in Tompkins v. Secretary of Interior, 01955262 (1997), found that her allegations that the investigative record made her look untruthful were irrelevant:

On appeal, appellant contends that the FAD makes her look "as though [she is], in essence, not telling the truth." She submits a long factual narrative in support of her contention that she did not make too many errors, as maintained by the agency. We reject the appellant's contention. The investigative record includes detailed hearing-style testimony of the appellant and other witnesses regarding the performance issues, and we find that it supports the agency's findings. Having voluntarily foregone the opportunity for a hearing before an EEOC AJ where her credibility concerns would have been properly dealt with, we find it too late for the appellant to now argue that the investigative record makes her look as though she is not being truthful.

As fully discussed in Chapter 6 under the subheading "Credibility Determinations," the Commission has been granting increased deference to the credibility findings of administrative judges. For example, in Shamika P. v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120142594 (2016), the Commission noted, "It is not our function on appeal to assess the credibility of witnesses. Under the Commission's regulations, in cases where hearings are held, that responsibility is reserved to administrative judges." Commission deference may also include findings based on the internal consistency of testimony.

A good example of how the Commission treats credibility determinations on appeal both when there has been a hearing and when the agency issues a final agency decision is provided by Complainant v. Pension Benefit Guaranty Corp., 0120122605 (2014), a case involving two separate promotions. In the first complaint, the matter went to hearing and the second complaint was subject to a final agency decision:

The AJ noted that during the deposition SO1 stated that the appearance of Complainant's age triggered her to ask Complainant about his future employment plans. However, the AJ found SO 1 's testimony at the hearing credible when she denied considering Complainant's age and stated that she found the deposition question very confusing. We find that there is not sufficient evidence for us to disturb the AJ's credibility determinations, who had the chance to study the body language, tone of voice, eye contact and demeanor of the witnesses. A preponderance of the evidence supports the AJ's finding that Complainant was not subjected to age discrimination when he was asked this question during the interview.

. . .

During the second interview, SO2 articulated that the Selectee performed better than Complainant. For example, SO2 asked Complainant what he did differently to improve his performance rating between 2009 and 2010. Complainant allegedly responded that he "learned better how to play the game" and elaborated that he learned how to manipulate the system by producing a lot of work in the last few weeks of the rating period because his managers would only think about the end of the rating period when it came time to prepare his appraisal. SO2 stated that he was appalled by Complainant's response and attitude. Additionally, SO2 asked Complainant if he would be willing to take the GS-13 position, and Complainant stated that he was not. SO2 stated that Complainant demonstrated more of an interest in a promotion than in working in the Appeals Division. In contrast, SO2 stated that the Selectee interviewed with confidence, was more candid than Complainant, and demonstrated more of a desire to work for the Appeals Division regardless of the grade, which SO2 considered "very important."

. . .

We note that Complainant questions the credibility of SO2 and stated that he did not make the comments SO2 claims he made during the interview. While only Complainant and SO2 were present during the interview, two witnesses provided statements that SO2 told them after the interview that Complainant "shot himself in the foot" in response to a question about a performance appraisal rating, and that the Selectee's interview was much better. Unfortunately, Complainant withdrew his request for a hearing before an EEOC Administrative Judge, and as a result we do not have the benefit of an Administrative Judge's credibility determinations of the witnesses in this case. Complainant bears the burden to prove, by a preponderance of the evidence, that the alleged discriminatory acts occurred. When the evidence is at best equipoise, Complainant fails to meet that burden. See Lore v. Dep't of Homeland Security, EEOC Appeal No. 0120113283 (Sept. 13, 2013) (Complainant failed to establish that witnesses made false statements where he withdrew his request for a hearing and credibility determinations were unable to be made).

In Complainant v. Attorney General, 0720140029 (2015), the Commission upheld the administrative judge's determination that the supervisor was not a credible witness, particularly where that finding was supported by other evidence:

The AJ based her reprisal finding entirely on her determination that S3 was not a credible witness. An AJ's credibility determination based on the demeanor of a witness or on the tone of voice of a witness will be accepted unless documents or other objective evidence so contradicts the testimony or the testimony so lacks in credibility that a reasonable fact finder would not credit it. See EEOC Management Directive 110, Chapter 9, at § VLB. (November 9, 1999).

With regard to the first incident, S2 denied that he had recommended to S3 that the Houston office be credited for the seizure. He testified that he had explicitly told S3 that Complainant's group had done "99 percent of the work" the led to the seizure. HT 549-50. Concerning the second incident, Complainant testified that the arrest in question involved a non-cooperative witness and was therefore not important enough to justify a significant activity report, that his first-line supervisor (S1) and S2 agreed with him, and that several days after the fact, he received emails from S3 demanding to know why he did not file a significant activity report. HT 60-64. S1 testified he thought that S3 had overreacted, that S3 scrutinized Complainant more closely and chastised him more harshly than other group supervisors, and that he had warned Complainant that S3 would "take a shot at him" for any mistakes he might make. HT 397, 454-55, 458-60. Faced with these plainly observable contradictions in the evidentiary record, the Agency did not present any testimony from other witness or documents that undermine the credibility of S1 and S2 or which bolsters the credibility of S3. We therefore find no reason to disturb the AJ's credibility findings with respect to S3's hearing testimony, and find, as did the AJ, that S3 had retaliated against Complainant for naming him in two previously filed EEO complaints.

Similarly, in Complainant v. Attorney General, 0120123467 (2015), the Commission upheld the credibility findings of the administrative judge on the complainant's testimony, where he found part credible and part not credible, where the basis for those findings were explained in the decision:

With regard to her contention that the AO referred to her as a "bitch," the AJ found Complainant's hearing testimony was credible in both substance and demeanor on the actual "bitch" reference and her explanation for not mentioning such reference in her detailed accounts in her investigative record submissions. In contrast, the AJ found the AO's testimony denying the "bitch" reference not credible. The AJ noted that the AO denied making the epithet while he shifted, turned, and excessively blinked for one of the few times during his entire testimony. However, the AJ found the remainder of Complainant's testimony about the AO repeatedly and silently mouthing "bitch" to her when they made eye contact was not credible and had the ring of uncertainty or embellishment because this line of testimony was the only time that Complainant testified after a major pause and having looked at her lawyer for what appeared to be a cue.

But, the Commission will not grant deference to an administrative judge who makes evidentiary findings based upon a witness's testimony, but fails to find that the witness was credible. In Bailey v. Secretary of Navy, 01990980 (2000), the Commission noted:

The AJ found, without explanation, that the agency had presented clear and convincing evidence that it would have promoted C2 over complainant, absent discrimination. We disagree. While the AJ did not articulate the basis for this finding, we must conclude that she relied upon the testimony of SO, since his testimony is the only source of evidence distinguishing the top three candidates. While a particular witness clearly may be deemed credible on some points and not others, the AJ made no such distinction in her findings. More importantly, the AJ found the SO more than not credible, she suspected mendacity. Accordingly, a finding that the agency met its burden in showing that it would not have selected complainant, even absent discrimination, is not supported by substantial evidence. See Mahran v. USDA, EEOC Request No. 05940973 (June 20, 1996).
