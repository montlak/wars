2. Remedial Authority

There is a substantial question about what kind of relief the Commission can in fact order. Presumably, if it cannot review the merits of clearance decisions, then it cannot order the granting or reinstatement of a clearance as relief for the discriminatory application of clearance standards. It may, however, be able to order that an agency reconsider its clearance decision absent the discriminatory influence.

The Commission addressed the problem in Schroeder v. Secretary of Defense, DMA, 05930248 (1994):

The previous decision correctly noted that the Commission has no authority to review a security clearance determination or the validity of an employer's requirement of a security clearance. Thierjung v. Department of Defense (Defense Mapping Agency), EEOC Request No. 05880664 (November 2, 1989); see also "Policy Guidance on the Use of the National Security Exception contained in 703(g) of Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, as amended." EEOC Notice No. N-915-041 (May 1, 1989). The previous decision also correctly noted that the Commission is not precluded from determining whether the grant, denial, or revocation of a security clearance was conducted in a nondiscriminatory manner. Lyons v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05890839 (March 22, 1990).

In vacating, in part, the agency's decision canceling appellant's complaint, the previous decision emphasized that appellant had alleged that his supervisors and coworkers had provided discriminatory statements to officials conducting the security clearance investigation. The previous decision concluded that the inclusion of the allegedly discriminatory statements in appellant's official security file rendered him aggrieved and, therefore, he stated a claim within the purview of EEOC Regulations. See 29 CFR § 1613.212. Accordingly, the previous decision ordered the agency to process the portion of appellant's complaint related to his contention that statements that discriminated against him based on his epilepsy were entered into his official security file.

To the extent that it concluded that appellant stated a claim when he filed a complaint challenging allegedly discriminatory utterances by his supervisors and coworkers, the Commission agrees with and hereby denies reconsideration of the previous decision. (footnote omitted). Therefore, with respect to the allegedly discriminatory statements alone, the agency's request for reconsideration is denied.

However, we also hold that, once manifested in the security file, the statements were squarely within the rubric of a security clearance determination and, accordingly, beyond the Commission's jurisdiction. Therefore, the portion of the previous decision that vacated the agency's rejection of appellant's allegation addressing the inclusion, in his security file, of allegedly discriminatory assessments of his medical condition is hereby reversed. On this matter, the agency's request is granted.

As to the remaining arguments, we are not persuaded by the agency's suggestion that investigating appellant's claim of discriminatory utterances will have a chilling effect on the security clearance process. The agency's contention that appellant failed to produce evidence of disparate treatment and its representation that appellant was investigated in the same manner as everyone else go to the merits of his allegation. Since the issue before the Commission is limited to whether appellant stated a claim, such arguments are premature.

The Commission's treatment of security clearance cases presents an enigma. Apparently, a complainant can pursue a complaint of discrimination that relates to a security clearance under the limitations set forth above, but no relief with respect to the denial of the clearance can be granted.

However, the Commission in Fonda-Wall v. Attorney General, 0720060035 (2009), affirmed an administrative judge's order for the agency to reactivate an abandoned investigation into the suspension of complainant's security clearance. The administrative judge in Fonda-Wall found that the agency's initiation of the security clearance suspension was in retaliation for protected EEO activity and therefore the agency's subsequent reliance on the suspension of the security clearance to suspend the complainant and reassign her to another state was also tainted with discriminatory animus. As a result, the administrative judge "ordered the agency to either reactivate the complainant's clearance or reconsider its decision absent the retaliatory influences, to reinstate the complainant to her former position and revoke the suspension."

Upon review, we find that this case presents a situation within the Commission's authority. Here, it is undisputed that the motivation to investigate and suspend complainant's security clearance was based upon the discriminatory actions of S1. We note that we are not questioning the agency's judgment as to whether complainant should have access to sensitive information; the agency never made a determination on the merits of whether complainant's security clearance should be revoked for revealing sensitive information because the investigation was abandoned by the agency after complainant was relocated. While we find that the AJ was correct in finding that S1's motivation for initiating the suspension of complainant's security clearance was retaliatory, we agree with the agency that the Commission lacks jurisdiction to order an agency to reactivate an individual's security clearance. However, we find that the Commission does have jurisdiction to order the agency to reactivate the abandoned security clearance investigation. In its final agency decision, the agency acknowledged that it should reconsider complainant's suspension of her security clearance in light of S1's retaliation. Therefore, we affirm the AJ's finding that the investigation and security clearance suspension were based on retaliation, and affirm that the agency must reactivate the investigation into complainant's security clearance suspension.

The Commission affirmed its remedial authority to order the agency to reassess its investigation into a complainant's security clearance in Maxine C. v. Attorney General, 2019003421 (2020). The AJ in that case found direct evidence of reprisal as it related to the agency's decision to review and suspend the complainant's security clearance:

[T]he AJ concluded that the undisputed facts showed that the Agency "investigated and terminated Complainant based solely on the suspicion that Complainant lied or 'lacked candor' in the filing and investigation of her prior EEO complaint." The AJ noted that the misconduct referral and the termination letter both "specifically cite Complainant's allegations of retaliation in her prior EEO complaint." The AJ further found that, although the Commission does not have jurisdiction over an Agency's decision whether to grant or revoke security clearances, the Commission does have jurisdiction to analyze whether the decision to suspend and review a security clearance is discriminatory, and that the Agency's decision to review and suspend Complainant's security clearance was prompted by the Agency's retaliatory actions.

To remedy the reprisal, the AJ ordered the Agency to: (1) reactivate its investigation into Complainant's security clearance, "taking into account that it is unlawful for the Agency to deny Complainant a security clearance based on her participation in protected EEO activity..."; (2) fully reinstate Complainant to her prior position with the Agency, "provided she is able to reobtain her security clearance"; (3) "[c]alculate within 15 days the appropriate back pay amount for the period from the date of Complainant's effective termination...until the date of her full reinstatement to her prior position with the Agency"; (4) restore all leave used between May 4, 2015 and November 6, 2015, and credit Complainant with all leave she would have accumulated; (5) calculate and deposit into Complainant's retirement accounts all funds that would have been deposited; (6) pay attorney's fees and costs; and (7) to post a notice.

The AJ then invited Complainant to submit a petition for attorney's fees, which Complainant did on September 21, 2018. After the Agency opposed Complainant's petition, the AJ issued a decision on attorney's fees on December 10, 2018. The AJ agreed with the Agency that Complainant's request for $115,476.50 in attorney's fees was excessive. The AJ rejected Complainant's argument that her attorney was entitled to $500 per hour until February 2015, and then $625 per hour afterwards. The AJ noted that Complainant's attorney has been consistently awarded $400 per hour over the last several years, and "[i]n this case, where the attorney failed to argue the meritorious basis for the complaint, there is no basis to justify this increase."

The AJ explained that the decision in Complainant's favor resulted from the AJ, sua sponte, issuing summary judgment; Complainant chose only to defend against the Agency's motion for summary judgment, not to argue that judgment should be granted in her favor.

The AJ then turned to the time Complainant's attorney expended on the case. After reviewing Complainant's fee petition, the AJ concluded that the "time entries are not represented as attachments of weekly or monthly billing statements, nor as time-keeping statements from an electronic system." The AJ also noted that Complainant's attorney included time spent negotiating his retainer with Complainant or contacting members of Congress, and that "many entries are documented during periods of inactivity in this case." The AJ determined that the petition was excessive. After outlining the errors and deficiencies in Complainant's fee petition, the AJ opted to apply an across-the-board reduction of 50 percent to Complainant's request for attorney's fees awarding Complainant $37,450.00. The AJ did, however, grant Complainant the entirety of her request for costs equaling $5,698.55.

The Agency subsequently issued a final order implementing the AJ's decision in its entirety, including the AJ's decision on relief and attorney's fees.

The complainant filed an appeal with the Commission arguing that the AJ's order incorrectly concluded that her entitlement to reinstatement, back pay, and retirement benefits was contingent on the result of the security clearance investigation. The Commission in Maxine C. disagreed:

We first address Complainant's argument that the AJ incorrectly required Complainant to obtain her security clearance before the Agency should calculate any backpay benefits due to Complainant. We disagree with Complainant's argument. The AJ correctly found that the Commission does not have jurisdiction to determine whether a security clearance investigation was properly conducted, only to determine whether it was properly initiated. As the AJ determined that the record contained direct evidence that unlawful retaliation played a role in the Agency's decision to initiate the investigation, given the fact that the Commission cannot order reinstatement of a security clearance, the proper remedy is to order the Agency to reassess that decision without the influence of discriminatory animus. If that decision then sustains the decision to revoke Complainant's security clearance, then Complainant indeed will not be entitled to back pay benefits. Such an approach is consistent with Commission precedent. See Beaton v. Dep't of Justice, EEOC Appeal No. 01996580 (Sept. 10, 2002) (ordering that, if complainant successfully obtains the necessary security clearance, then complainant is entitled to appropriate back pay). Likewise, the Commission finds that the AJ properly ordered the Agency to calculate and deposit funds into Complainant's retirement account from when the Agency ceased making such payments until Complainant's reinstatement taking into account the considerations discussed above.

In James v. Secretary of Air Force, 07A50103 (2006), the Commission found that an investigation into the complainant's security clearance had been initiated in retaliation for protected activity. Relying on the Egan case, the Commission reversed an order of the administrative judge to reinstate the complainant's security clearance. The Commission, in James, held:

We agree that the Commission is precluded from reviewing the substance of security clearance decisions. See Chatlin v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05900188 (June 1, 1990). Accordingly, the order to reinstate complainant's security clearance, if in fact it has been revoked, exceeds the Commission's authority. However, as the agency concedes, the Commission may review the agency's initiation of the security clearance review and award damages related to the misuse of the security clearance process. Id. Accordingly, the AJ's award of compensatory damages associated with the retaliatory initiation of the security clearance file is legally and factually supported by substantial evidence in the record.

The Commission upheld an award of $80,000 in nonpecuniary damages and almost $20,000 in pecuniary damages. It also ordered training for several agency officials involved in the retaliation, as well as directing the agency to consider discipline of those officials.
