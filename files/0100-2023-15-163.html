4. Demeanor

As noted above, the greatest degree of deference is given to credibility determinations based on the demeanor of the witness. The administrative judge's demeanor observations must be consistent with his credibility determinations. The deference granted to an administrative judge's credibility determination based on witness demeanor is not automatic, but will not be overturned unless the challenging party presents "sufficient evidence which suggests that the credibility determination is erroneous." Smith v. Postmaster General, 01975505 (1999).

Stated another way, the Commission in Smith v. Secretary of Energy, 01A20227 (2002), said:

A credibility determination of an Administrative Judge based on the demeanor or tone of voice of a witness will be accepted unless documents or other objective evidence so contradicts the testimony of the witness or the testimony of the witness otherwise so lacks in credibility that a reasonable fact finder would not credit it.

See also Gibbs v. Postmaster General, 0120122208 (2012).

The Commission will overturn the credibility determinations of the administrative judge when those determinations are inconsistent with the judge's own observations about the demeanor of the witness. In Gibson v. USPS, 01862761 (1987), the judge found the RMO was a hostile witness and loud and boisterous during his testimony. The administrative judge then concluded, without explanation, that the hostility of the witness was directed at the appellant's representative instead of the appellant. Noting that the judge discounted the testimony of one of appellant's witness because it was "self-serving," the Commission found there was no basis for crediting the testimony of the RMO over the appellant's witness.

There is no requirement that the EEOC overrule the findings of an administrative judge with respect to demeanor on the issue of witness credibility in order to reach a contrary conclusion about a finding of pretext. The Commission may accept the credibility findings of the administrative judge but still find the other evidence of record is sufficient to rebut a finding of pretext. As the Commission noted in New Moon v. Postmaster General, 01933532 (1994):

We recognize that our finding of no pretext is contrary to the determinations made in the RD wherein the AJ, based in part on observing the Station Manager's demeanor, concluded that a pattern of adverse actions against women existed. The AJ relied on her perception that the Station Manager's responses regarding actions taken against female employees were abrupt and conclusory, and that he "showed little concern for the events that took place." While we accept these demeanor-based assessments as relevant factors in this case, there is sufficient counter evidence presented by the agency to dispel the inference of discrimination. See Rash v. Dept. of Navy, EEOC Request No. 05920717 (Mar. 11, 1993); and Gathers v. USPS, EEOC Request No. 05890894 (Nov. 9, 1989).

An administrative judge properly supports a finding that a witness is not credible when the judge notes "the [witness] indicated that it would be easier for her to testify 'if no one looked at her.' The Administrative Judge further indicated that the [witness] did not make eye contact and appeared nervous and unsure of her responses. Moreover, the Administrative Judge determined, and we agree, that the [witness] clearly harbored animus towards appellant based on the derogatory terms she used to describe appellant's appearance." Safford v. Postmaster General, 01940630 (1995). Likewise, it is proper for an administrative judge to consider the irritability and anger of a witness in determining credibility. In Evans v. Secretary of Army, 01964717 (1997), the Commission rejected an agency argument that the judge erred in finding discrimination based on the irritability and anger of the selecting official during his testimony. The Commission noted that the administrative judge did not reach her conclusion of discrimination based on those factors. Rather, she considered those factors as reflecting on the credibility of the selecting official and, after finding his testimony was not credible, concluded the reasons for the selection were a pretext for discrimination. Similarly, it is proper for an administrative judge to consider the demeanor of a witness, including evasiveness in answering questions, in making a credibility determination. Ansari v. Secretary of Treasury, 0720070054 (2007).

In Kitchen v. National Labor Relations Board, 0720110017 (2013), the Commission upheld the credibility findings of the administrative judge noting:

Testimony provided by management officials was inconsistent, contradictory, and purposefully vague. Officials gave statements in their depositions and in their answers to interrogatories but during the course of the hearing distanced themselves from their previous statements, contradicted them, feigned lack of understanding, or tried to explain their inconsistencies away. One management official, the Regional Attorney, admitted during his testimony that while he was serving as an acting Regional Director, he advised an attorney in the office to destroy a document which the attorney had surreptitiously taken from opposing counsel during a hearing.

In a footnote, the Commission noted that the regional attorney was suspended for his actions but the New York State bar did not find any disciplinary violation.

By contrast, in Long v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01950169 (1997), the Commission found that the reliance by the administrative judge on the complainant's "straightforward" testimony, without reference to any specific evidence, was an insufficient basis upon which to render a finding of discrimination:

In his decision, the AJ concluded, with respect to issues d and k, that based on appellant's "straight-forward" testimony at the hearing, appellant had established that the agency's reasons were a pretext for discrimination. However, the AJ did not identify what aspect of appellant's testimony demonstrated that the agency's reasons were a pretext for discrimination, and we find nothing in the record to support such a finding. Moreover, the AJ did not point to any other evidence, testimonial or otherwise, to indicate that the agency's actions were motivated by discrimination. While appellant may have believed that each of the actions at issue were based on her race, she failed to proffer any persuasive argument or evidence which would demonstrate that the agency's articulated reasons for its actions were motivated by a discriminatory animus based on her race. Appellant's assertions, without more, are an insufficient foundation on which to base a finding of discrimination, and the AJ's finding in this regard was in error.

The difference is that in Long the administrative judge used his observations about the demeanor of the witness to conclude that discrimination had occurred, whereas in Evans the administrative judge used her observations to draw conclusions about the credibility of the testimony and then used the credibility determination to support a finding of discrimination. The difference is subtle, but important--the first approach is reversible error.

In Humphries v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A14545 (2003), the Commission brushed aside arguments by the complainant that the length of her cross-examination and the side effects of medication impacted on the credibility determination of the administrative judge. In Humphries, the Commission observed:

Complainant also argues that she may have appeared less than credible due to the fact that the agency was permitted to cross examine her for a period of days which was exhausting. In addition, complainant argues that she was taking prescribed medication (anti-anxiety medications and beta blockers) during her testimony that may have affected her testimony. We do not find this argument persuasive or credible since complainant's testimony on direct examination did not appear evasive or vague. The record also shows that the AJ gave the parties many opportunities for breaks during the testimony. In addition, complainant gave six days of testimony which occurred over two non-consecutive weeks. Accordingly, she had many opportunities to stop taking the medication and/or advise the AJ of their potential side affects on her testimony. Moreover, complainant is a physician who was represented by counsel during the hearing. Neither complainant nor her counsel advised the AJ of her use of such medication even after numerous warnings from the AJ that her testimony appeared conflicting and evasive. Lastly, complainant provides no corroborating documentation that she was mentally incapacitated or even affected by the medications that she alleged to have taken. Accordingly, we do not find complainant's argument sufficient to show that the AJ's credibility determinations were erroneous.

Although often demeanor evidence is thought of in terms of whether a witness is testifying truthfully, it can also go to whether a witness simply has an accurate recollection of events. For example, in Nayeem v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 0120065158 (2008), the Commission upheld the administrative judge's credibility findings that were based on the manner in which the complainant responded to questions and directions. The Commission, in Nayeem, explained:

Here, the AJ found that while complainant is obviously very intelligent, his testimony was often confusing, vague, and not focused. Complainant did not listen to questions, did not completely answer questions, and misunderstood or disregarded directions given to him, The AJ found that complainant spoke in a free-flowing stream of consciousness style and-included extraneous details and lacked sequence and focus. The AJ further made a credibility determination and found that complainant misunderstood the supervisor's alleged discriminatory statements, disregarded the supervisor's directions, and lacked focus to follow through with instructions. The AJ found that the supervisor's recollection of the alleged discriminatory comments was more credible than complainant's recollection. There is nothing in the record to contradict the AJ's credibility determination.

The Commission's rulings on witness credibility, especially demeanor, must be viewed in the context of the entire body of law surrounding the issue of pretext, including the decision of the Supreme Court in St. Mary's Honor Center v. Hicks, 113 S. Ct. 2742 (1993), discussed fully in Chapter 10 under the heading "Hicks and the Burden of Persuasion."
