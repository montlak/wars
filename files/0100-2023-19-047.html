13. Training

The agency can rebut a prima facie showing of discrimination by demonstrating the complainant lacked the required training for the position at issue. The agency establishes a legitimate reason when it demonstrates that the complainant was terminated after failing a training course required for certification for her position. Frazier v. Department of Navy, 01830812 (1983).

Conversely, an agency does not meet its burden of producing a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its action when it gives no explanation of why it sent an unqualified employee for training, but did not send the complainant who was in fact qualified. In Colbert v. Postmaster General, 07A40107 (2005), the Commission found:

In this matter, the agency did not produce a witness at the hearing to explain why complainant was denied the training and the explanation set forth in the ES' affidavit testimony contained fails to address the issue. Specifically, the ES stated complainant was denied the training because the training was for Building Equipment Mechanics and Area Maintenance Technicians. However, the Painter was neither a Building Equipment Mechanic nor an Area Maintenance Technician, and the agency failed to produce evidence of circumstances or qualifications which made the Painter eligible for the training. To the extent that the training is a series of classes that teaches mechanics how to repair heating systems, the record does not establish that the painter worked on the heating system or in the building maintenance section. However, the record does show that other MPE mechanics have received this training in the past. Accordingly, we conclude that the agency failed to produce an explanation of sufficient clarity so that complainant had a full and fair opportunity to demonstrate pretext.

A complainant who was terminated from a risk management leadership program for being absent from required training failed to show that she was similarly situated to a male who was not terminated from the program for not attending the training. In Complainant v. Secretary of Agriculture, 0120114198 (2013), the Commission explained why the two were not similarly situated:

The record shows that Complainant applied for and was accepted into the Agency's Leadership Development Program (LDP) to be held in 2010-2011. The record also shows that the LDP had changed from prior years with respect to absences from scheduled training. Specifically, the newly accepted employees were required to acknowledge and agree that "[a]bsences from scheduled training will result in automatic removal from the program unless approved in advance by the Associate Deputy Administrator." The record also shows that Complainant was aware of the mandatory training dates at the time she applied for the LDP in the summer of 2009. In addition, the record shows that despite her knowledge of the LDP training dates, Complainant scheduled a family vacation for dates that conflicted with LDP training in August 2010. Complainant sought to be excused from attendance at the LDP training due to her vacation but her request was not granted and, upon her failure to attend the mandatory training dates in August 2010, the Agency removed her from the program.

Complainant argues that the Agency's reason for her removal is a pretext as demonstrated by the Agency's decision to allow a younger, male colleague to participate in the LDP training via video-conference for one session. Complainant asserts that the difference in treatment between two similarly-situated employees demonstrates discrimination. The record shows that the Agency allowed Complainant's colleague (C1) to participate via video-conference because of the late stage of his wife's pregnancy and the impending birth of their child. The record shows that the medical and unplanned nature of the request compelled the Agency to allow Cl to participate in training via video-conferencing. Moreover, Complainant's colleague actually participated in the training in contrast to Complainant who entirely missed the mandatory training dates. The LPD required removal only for "absences," (i.e., failures to participate). Complainant was absent for an entire week of the LDP whereas her colleague participated by video-conference. Accordingly, we agree with the Agency in concluding that Cl is not similarly-situated to her and the differences in their treatment do not demonstrate pretext.
