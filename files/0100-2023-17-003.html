1. Failure to Issue Final Agency Action

Following receipt of the decision of the administrative judge and the complaint file, the agency has 40 days to issue a final action either agreeing to implement the judge's decision or not implement the decision. If the agency does not agree to fully implement the decision of the administrative judge, it must notify the complainant of that fact and simultaneously file an appeal with OFO. 29 CFR 1614.110(a); 1614.402(a).

If the agency does not issue a final action within the 40 days, the decision of the administrative judge becomes binding on the agency. 29 CFR 1614.109(i) ("If the agency does not issue a final order within 40 days of receipt of the administrative judge's decision in accordance with 1614.110, then the decision of the administrative judge shall become the final action of the agency.")

In the past, the Commission treated the failure of an agency to issue a decision within 40 days as ripening the complainant's right of appeal, thus triggering the complainant's 30-day deadline to appeal. For example, in Stacie D. v. Secretary of Agriculture, 0120133016 (2015), the Commission determined that the complainant's appeal was untimely where she did not file it until some three months after the expiration of the 40-day period:

As an initial matter, we shall address the Agency's contention that the instant appeal should be dismissed on two grounds. First, the Agency argues that the appeal is untimely. Because the Agency did not issue a decision following the AJ's decision, the AJ's decision became the final order on May 8, 2013. Complainant did not file an appeal until August 23, 2013, well beyond the thirty-day time limitation. To the extent that Complainant is challenging the AJ's decision, we agree. Therefore, we shall not consider the portion of Complainant's appeal regarding the remedies awarded or denied.

See also Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120133296 (2015).

However, in the Commission in Stella K. v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 2019005673 (2020), took the opposite position, holding that "there is no regulatory provision that sets a specific time limitation for filing an appeal in circumstances. . .where an AJ's decision becomes final by operation of 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109(i)":

During the relevant period, Complainant worked as a Program Manager, GS-15, at the Agency's Resilient Systems Division (RSD), Science and Technology Directorate, in Washington, District of Columbia.

On April 23, 2014, Complainant filed an EEO complaint alleging that the Agency subjected her to harassment and discrimination on the bases of national origin (Hispanic), age, and in reprisal for prior protected EEO activity.

After an investigation of the formal complaint by the Agency, Complainant requested a hearing before an EEOC Administrative Judge (AJ). On January 30, 2017, the AJ assigned to the case granted the Agency's motion for summary judgement and concluded Complainant failed to prove she had been subjected to discrimination or reprisal as alleged. There is no evidence that the Agency issued a final order on the AJ's decision.

On May 17, 2018, Complainant filed an appeal, which was docketed as EEOC Appeal No. 0120181886. In the decision, Complainant's appeal of the AJ's January 30, 2017 decision was dismissed as untimely filed. In so doing, the decision noted that, pursuant to EEOC Regulation 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109(i), if an agency does not issue a final order within 40 days of receipt of the AJ's decision, the decision of the AJ becomes the final action of the agency. Therefore, EEOC Appeal No. 0120181886 found that the 40-day period began on January 30, 2017, and the Agency was required to issue a decision by March 13, 2017. The decision went on to conclude that Complainant was required to appeal within 30 days of March 13, 2017, which was April 12, 2017, and, because Complainant's appeal was postmarked on May 17, 2018, it was untimely.

In requesting reconsideration, Complainant's attorney argues that the appeal should be deemed timely. Following receipt of the AJ's decision, Complainant's attorney represents that she was in frequent contact with the Agency regarding its issuance of a final decision. Complainant's attorney asserts that it was not until May 4, 2018, that the Agency finally communicated that it had no intention of issuing a final decision. Complainant then promptly filed her appeal on May 17, 2018.

Upon further consideration of this issue, we now conclude that there is no regulatory provision that sets a specific time limitation for filing an appeal in circumstances such as this where an AJ's decision becomes final by operation of 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109(i). Rather, the time for Complainant to file an appeal in this instance is governed by the doctrine of laches--"an equitable remedy under which an individual's failure to diligently pursue their actions can bar their claims." O'Dell v. Dep't of Health and Human Services, EEOC Request No. 05901130 (Dec. 27, 1990). Here, we conclude that laches does not apply. The Commission has previously held that where the record shows there has been confusion as to whether the Agency would issue a final order, the time limit for filing an appeal to the Commission may be tolled. See e.g., Complainant v. Dep't of the Air Force, EEOC Appeal No. 0120120419 (April 16, 2014). Under the circumstances presented in this case, we are convinced by Complainant's argument that she was justified in believing the Agency planned to issue a final decision until May 2018, when it clarified it would not be issuing a final decision and she filed her appeal. Therefore, we exercise our discretion to extend Complainant's time to appeal. See 29 C.F.R. § 1614.604(c) (time limitations are subject to waiver, estoppel, or equitable tolling).

As such, we vacate our prior decision dismissing Complainant's appeal as untimely filed and will now address, for the first time, the merits of her allegations of discrimination and unlawful retaliation.

The complainant alleged that neither he nor his attorney received the agency's final order adopting the administrative judge's decision and, therefore, his deadline to appeal was never triggered. The decision in EEOC Appeal No. 0120180734 recognized that the Agency failed to meet its burden of proving Complainant's receipt of its final order. Nevertheless, the prior decision determined that even if Complainant did not receive the Agency's final order, the AJ's decision would have become the Agency's final decision 40 days after it was issued. EEOC Appeal No. 0120180734 concluded that Complainant should have filed his appeal within 30 days of the date the AJ's decision became the Agency's final action. Based on this rationale, EEOC Appeal No. 0120180734 determined that Complainant's December 22, 2017 filing was untimely and dismissed the appeal.

Upon further consideration, we now conclude that there is no regulatory provision that sets a specific time limitation for filing an appeal in circumstances such as this where an AJ's decision becomes final by operation of 29 C.F.R. § 1614.109(i). Rather, the time for Complainant to file an appeal in this instance is governed by the doctrine of laches--"an equitable remedy under which an individual's failure to diligently pursue their actions can bar their claims." O'Dell v. Dep't of Health and Human Services, EEOC Request No. 05901130 (Dec. 27, 1990). Here, we now conclude that the relatively brief period of time between the AJ's decision and Complainant's appeal (four months) did not justify the application of laches to dismiss the appeal as untimely.

As such, we vacate our prior decision dismissing Complainant's appeal as untimely filed and will now address, for the first time, the merits of his allegations that he was subjected to discrimination based on his disability and/or in reprisal for his EEO activity.
