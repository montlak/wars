D. CHOICE OF COUNSELOR

Where an agency facility has more than one EEO counselor, a potential complainant is free to choose among the available counselors. But if only one counselor is available for that particular facility, the potential complainants must use that counselor. Goza v. USPS, 01852047 (1988).

An employee is not entitled to be assigned the counselor of his or her choice. The failure of the agency to accede to the complainant's choice does not give rise to another complaint. Winston v. Secretary of Defense, 01976655 (1998).

Choice can be a two-way street. This is particularly true if the counselor has previous experience with the complainant. In Clark v. Secretary of Army, 01923680 (1993), the Commission cautioned the agency about the refusal of a counselor to provide counseling to a particular complainant, who the counselor maintained was unwilling "to listen to reason or to accept any type of settlement offered, not to mention overbearing and unappreciative" of the counselor's efforts. Id. The counselor threatened to resign if not allowed to select his "clients." In rejecting the agency's arguments in Clark, the Commission noted:

Moreover, the Commission rejects arguments raised by the agency on appeal. Specifically, the Commission determines that, by referring to appellant as a "client" of the agency EEO Counselor, the agency has misconstrued the relationship between an agency employee and an agency EEO Counselor. EEOC Regulation 29 CFR 1613.213, the regulation in effect at the time appellant sought EEO counseling, provided that the agency shall have required that an aggrieved person who believed that she was discriminated against because of race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age or disability was required to consult with an EEO Counselor to try to resolve the matter. The agency shall have required the EEO Counselor to make whatever inquiry he or she believed necessary, to seek an informal solution, to counsel the aggrieved person, and to keep a record of counseling activities.

It is essential to the mission of the EEO office that it retain the confidence of both the agency and potentially aggrieved employees. Clearly, the mission is not met if the employee is perceived as merely a potential client of an EEO Counselor, available for counseling at the Counselor's discretion. If, as in this case, a particular EEO Counselor is incapable or unwilling to counsel a potentially aggrieved complainant, the agency is to provide another EEO Counselor on the complainant's behalf.

The reverse is also true; an EEO counselor should not regard the agency as his or her "client."

It is not error for the agency to reassign a case to a different counselor where the first counselor has filed an EEO complaint against the management official who is alleged to have engaged in discrimination. Mariner v. Secretary of Army, 01962274 (1999). Assignment of the first counselor to the case, in those circumstances, would undermine the counselor's neutral role.

In order to challenge the assignment of a counselor, the complainant must show that the assignment in some way has a material effect on the complaint. In Chostner v. Secretary of Navy, 0120071440 (2007), the complainant failed to show any material effect where she alleged that all of the EEO counselors had previously worked for her and were not neutral. The Commission found that the complainant did not identify any effect with sufficient specificity to show it had any impact on her complaint.
