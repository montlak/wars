1. Definition of Claim

With the 1999 amendments to Part 1614 and the revision of MD-110 that followed, the Commission adopted the use of the term "claim" in favor of "allegation," "issue" or other terms to describe the content of a discrimination complaint. This distinction has been carried forward in the 2015 revisions.

In MD-110 at 5-6, the Commission states: "A claim refers to an assertion of an unlawful employment practice or policy for which, if proven, there is a remedy under the federal equal employment statutes." More precisely, a claim is an allegation that the complainant has suffered some harm because of an employment action on the part of the agency that is prohibited by Title VII, the Rehabilitation Act, the ADEA or the EPA. The EEOC breaks a claim down into two components: (1) A factual statement of the employment practice or policy being challenged, and (2) a legal statement of the basis or bases of prohibited discrimination.

The EEOC further explains what it means by the term "claim" in MD-110 at 5-8:

When defining a claim, two components must be identified. First, the claim must contain a factual statement of the employment practice or policy being challenged. As already discussed, it is critical that EEO Counselors, investigators, and other EEO staff members ensure that they understand the exact nature of the complainant's concerns so that the employment practice is defined broadly enough to reflect any allegation of a pattern of ongoing discrimination. Particular attention should be given to claims involving terms and conditions of employment...The second component of a legal claim is the identification of the basis (because of race, color, national origin, sex, religion, reprisal, age or disability) for a violation of an equal employment statute.

The discussion of the proper and precise definition of the term "claim" is more than mere semantics. It goes to the heart of a problem that has plagued the EEO process for years--seemingly endless litigation over the legitimate scope of complaints due to the failure to properly distinguish between the claims presented by a complaint, the issues involved in resolving those claims and the evidence offered in support of the factual allegations in the complaint. Because of the failure to properly distinguish among claims, issues, and evidence, agencies have erroneously dismissed complaints. Complainants must then appeal that dismissal to the Commission. The Commission then reverses the dismissal, sending the claim back to the agency for processing and investigation. For example, in Judie D. v. Social Security Administration, 0120172781 (2017), the complainant alleged discrimination when the agency denied her request for reassignment/permanent transfer in order to care for her husband who was undergoing chemotherapy. It was the counselor's job to help the complainant properly frame how the facts of her claim potentially translated into a viable EEO claim. The counselor in Judie D., however, framed the claim as discrimination based on "marital status," which is not a recognized basis of discrimination, and the agency dismissed the complaint for failure to state a claim. On appeal, the Commission noted that the counselor failed to advise the complainant as to her associational rights under the Rehabilitation Act. The counselor in Judie D. should have defined the claim as a violation of the Rehabilitation Act, rather than marital status. Incidentally, the agency representative who dismissed the claim, also could have righted that wrong and properly defined the claim rather than dismissing it. The Commission reversed the agency's dismissal, remanding the claim back to the agency for an investigation and allowing the agency another 150 days (in addition to the amount of time it took the complainant to pursue the at issue appeal) to complete the investigation.

Even more often, portions of complaints are dismissed because a failure to properly define a claim(s). Complainants have been forced to appeal those dismissals to the EEOC Office of Federal Operations, while the accepted portions of the complaint proceeded through the investigation and, many times, hearing and final agency decision stages of the process. The net result when the EEOC reversed an agency's decision to dismiss portions of a complaint was piecemeal litigation, as those dismissed portions of the complaint had to proceed through the investigation, hearing, and final agency decision stages of the EEO process. The EEOC has labeled the above problem "fragmentation of claims."
