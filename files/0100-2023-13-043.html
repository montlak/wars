B. FAILURE TO INTERVIEW WITNESSES

The agency has an obligation to interview witnesses with information relevant to the complaint, including witnesses who may be able to provide information favorable to the complainant. See, e.g., Hensley v. Postmaster General, 01A14162 (2002). Nevertheless, the EEOC will not remand a case for further investigation on the bare assertion that the agency failed to interview favorable witnesses when the complainant does not identify the witnesses or what information would have been obtained through their interviews. See Drisdom v. Department of Air Force, 01840052 (1985).

When the agency interviews a witness but then excludes that statement from the final investigative report, this is error only if the complainant can make a proffer of the testimony that would have been provided to show discrimination. See Jessie v. Postmaster General, 01882449 (1988). This is particularly true where the witness subsequently is approved to testify at the hearing but does not appear.

When an agency improperly fails to interview a witness during the investigative stage and the Commission remands the complaint to obtain an affidavit from that witness, the agency may be subject to a subsequent adverse inference if an affidavit is not obtained. See Bacon v. Secretary of Commerce, 05910907 (1992). This is true even if the witness is no longer employed by the federal government and not subject to the agency's control. Because the agency should have contacted the witness earlier, in Bacon an adverse inference was appropriate when the agency did not secure the relevant information from the witness or some other source.

If there is a material dispute of fact, a thorough investigation should entail more than simply interviewing the complainant and the RMO. In Kellogg-Smith v. Postmaster General, 01A00732 (2000), the investigator only obtained two affidavits: one from the complainant and one from the supervisor against whom the complaint was filed. Not surprisingly, their respective versions about what took place differed. The Commission faulted the investigator for failing to interview corroborating witnesses whom the complainant listed. The Commission remanded the case for a supplemental investigation to include "affidavits from all persons who might have reasonably witnessed the incidents at issue." Id.

Likewise, in Garnica v. Postmaster General, 01974929 (2000), the investigator obtained affidavits only from the complainant and the supervisor who allegedly discriminated against her. The complaint involved a reprimand on the workroom floor. The Commission found the investigation inadequate because the investigator did not obtain affidavits from any of the complainant's coworkers who may have overheard what transpired on the workroom floor, despite the complainant's identification of a witness.

The Kellogg-Smith and Garnica cases do not mean there are never any circumstances where an adequate investigation would include no more than an affidavit from the complainant and the RMO. In each of those cases, the error was not solely in the fact that the investigator interviewed only the complainant and the RMO. The error was that those interviews left facts critical to the resolution of the complaint in dispute where there were additional witnesses who had relevant testimony on the factual dispute. In a similar vein, the Commission found the investigative record inadequate in a nonselection case where the investigator refused to interview witnesses that were not involved in the selection process. Laurence L. v. Secretary of Defense, 0120140944 (2016). In Laurence L., the complainant provided unsworn statements of three witnesses and a witness affidavit to support his contention that the selectee's resume contained false information, i.e., that he had supervisory experience, and that the panelists who recommended his selection knew it. The Commission in Laurence L. noted that witnesses do not have to be directly involved in the action alleged to be discriminatory, but may also be called, for example, to establish that an agency's articulated reason was actually pretext for discrimination.

An agency investigation is incomplete when the investigator fails to interview and obtain affidavits from the official who screened applicants for qualifications and the official who made the ultimate selection. Jefferson v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, SSA, 01944529 (1996).

Even if an investigator makes an attempt to interview witnesses identified by the complainant and they do not respond, the investigator still must do more than simply interview the complainant and the RMO. See Powell v. Postmaster General, 01A00659 (2000). In Powell, the investigative record stated, without explanation, that two of the complainant's coworkers received requests for affidavits but failed to respond. The Commission in Powell found the investigator's attempts inadequate, noting:

The Commission finds that in the instant matter the agency failed to develop an impartial and appropriate factual record. Apart from the affidavit requests of Coworkers B and D, there is no indication that the investigator attempted to gather affidavits from the corroborating witnesses listed by complainant in his affidavit. Statements from the witnesses listed by complainant are relevant in the instant matter because the issue in this case involves a matter of credibility. Supervisor A and complainant have provided different versions of what comments were made and actions were taken by Supervisor A and the context in which those comments were made and actions were taken. The names of witnesses who would corroborate complainant's version of events were provided by complainant to the investigator. Even if such names were not provided by complainant, a proper investigation should have included affidavits from persons in complainant's work area who might have witnessed some of the incidents at issue.

The Commission remanded the case for a supplemental investigation to include "affidavits from all persons who might have reasonably witnessed the alleged harassment." Id.

When a complainant identifies similarly situated employees who were treated differently than him, the investigator should interview the comparatives. Failure to do so will result in remand by the Commission. See, e.g., Smith v. Postmaster General, 01A02620 (2000); McCollins v. Postmaster General, 01974491 (2000).
