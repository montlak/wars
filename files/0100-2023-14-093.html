broida eeo Chapter 05: Settlement of Complaints Chapter 05: Settlement of Complaints, Subchapter 06: Voiding Settlement Agreements, Section B: Bad Faith

B. BAD FAITH

When it comes to bad faith, there are two distinctly different scenarios: (1) bad faith in the negotiation of the agreement, and (2) bad faith in the execution of the agreement. Bad faith in executing the terms of the agreement is an issue involving breach of the agreement. As such, it does not provide a legal basis for rendering a settlement agreement void. Of course, the Commission, through its regulations, allows complainants to void the settlement agreement by electing reinstatement of a complaint as a remedy for breach.

Agencies must exercise good faith in administering the terms of a settlement agreement. Otherwise, the agreements can be set aside, or the Commission can order specific enforcement of the terms of the agreement. In Batton v. Secretary of Army, 01903212 (1990), the Commission determined the agency acted in bad faith by trying to construe its actions to conform to the terms of the agreement, rather than attempting to construe the agreement in a way that would determine the agency's actions. In that case, the agency agreed that the complainant would be given priority consideration for positions in which he expressed an interest. The agency then sought to fulfill this obligation by giving the complainant priority consideration for a position in which he had not expressed an interest, and by inquiring into his union affiliation in considering him for a position in which he had expressed an interest. Based on the agency's actions, the Commission found it acted in bad faith and that the complainant was entitled to specific enforcement of the agreement.

When an agency manifests no intention of performing under a settlement agreement, the agreement is voidable and the underlying complaint should be reinstated. Caldwell v. Postmaster General, 01952358 (1996). In that case, the agency agreed to consider the complainant for a position at a specific post office knowing that she had previously been terminated for cause. The Commission found the only way the complainant could have been given meaningful consideration was "to deliberately negate appellant's prior termination." Id.

An agency can ratify an otherwise invalid agreement through its subsequent actions. Most often this occurs when the agency representative enters into an agreement that exceeds his or her authority. The Commission has rebuffed a number of attempts by agencies to void such agreements, in part relying on agency actions that implement some of the terms of the agreement. Even if the representative lacked authority, such actions amount to a subsequent ratification of the agreement by the agency.

Just as an agency can ratify an otherwise invalid settlement agreement through subsequent actions, it also can invalidate an otherwise valid agreement. In Battle v. Secretary of Transportation, 01953323 (1996), the complainant alleged that the agency breached a previous settlement agreement. The agency then agreed to reinstitute the original complaint and processed the case through the hearing stage. However, in its final decision the agency tried to hold the complainant to the previous agreement. In overruling the agency in Battle, the Commission noted:

Regarding Complaint No. 0318, the agency stated in its final decision that a September 1992 settlement agreement which had resolved this complaint had not been breached by the agency, and it had never been the agency's intent to reopen this complaint subsequent to appellant's allegation of settlement breach. In our decision dated January 18, 1994 we expressly noted that subsequent to appellant's allegation of settlement breach dated December 11, 1992, the agency's internal processing of appellant's complaint became confused. We specifically noted in our decision of January 18, 1994, that a letter dated April 26, 1993, from the agency's Director of Civil Rights, informed appellant that his complaint would be reopened from the point where processing ceased. Moreover, we directed the agency to issue appellant a determination as to whether or not Complaint No. 0318 had in fact been reinstated. Events subsequent to the issuance of our decision on January 18, 1994, reflect, unfortunately, that the confusion surrounding the processing of Complaint No. 0318 has continued.

The Commission notes that the following events occurred subsequent to our decision of January 18, 1994: by letter to appellant dated February 15, 1994, agency Departmental Office of Civil Rights officials informed appellant that agency officials were directed to process Complaint No. 0318; that by letter dated May 2, 1994, an agency Administrator informed appellant of the precise issue that had been accepted for investigation in Complaint No. 0318; and that by letter dated August 5, 1994, the agency informed appellant that the investigation of Complaint No. 0318 had been completed. We note further that after a hearing was scheduled before an Administrative Judge, the agency engaged in extensive discovery proceedings. Despite all indications that it was the agency's intent to continue the processing of Complaint No. 0318 as manifested by the actions discussed above, the agency stated in its final decision, without elaboration, that it had not been its intent to reopen Complaint No. 0318 and that the complaint had been instead resolved by a settlement agreement that had been entered into approximately two and one half years prior to the date of the agency's final decision in March 1995. We find that appellant had been placed in a position where equity dictates the determination that the settlement agreement for Complaint No. 0138 is no longer in effect, and consequently, a determination of whether there has been a breach is no longer necessary. Accordingly, the agency's decision regarding Complaint No. 0138 was improper and is REVERSED.

When a complainant alleges bad faith in the negotiation of an agreement, the Commission will look to what the agency knew at the time it negotiated the agreement. For example, in Baynes v. Secretary of Navy, 0120082032 (2008), the Commission found that the agency engaged in bad faith negotiations where on November 15th it entered an agreement providing that the complainant's temporary reassignment would be made permanent retroactive for several months. On November 20th, the agency proposed the complainant's removal based on a revocation of his security clearance. The Commission found that under the circumstances, the agency likely knew of the complainant's proposed removal at the time it negotiated the agreement and that the removal would invalid any benefit the complainant received under the agreement. As a result, the Commission voided the settlement agreement.

When the Commission does find bad faith on the part of an agency in negotiating an agreement, it will not necessarily order a return to the status quo if the complainant elects to rescind the agreement and pursue the EEO complaint. For example, in Suter v. Postmaster General, 0120093523 (2010), the complainant and the agency entered into an agreement where the complainant was given a specific maintenance custodian position in a specific building. At the time of the agreement, the agency knew that building would be closing within a few months. The agency produced no evidence that informed the complainant or her representative of that fact during negotiations. As a result, the Commission found that the agreement had been negotiated in bad faith. The Commission, in Suter, found:

Complainant stated in her notice of breach that she wished to have her complaint reinstated. In most cases where a complainant received some of the benefits promised in the agreement, those benefits would have to be returned if the complainant wishes to reinstate the complaint for further processing. In the instant case, however, the only benefit received by Complainant was being placed in the Building Maintenance Custodian 5 position #95020103. Given that we find that the Agency acted in bad faith, and given the injustice in requiring Complainant to repay salary earned over a number of months for work she performed in the position, we find that Complainant does not have to return any benefits she received under the agreement.
