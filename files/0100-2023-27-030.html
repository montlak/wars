3. Failure to Apply for Position

As a general matter, a complainant must establish that he or she applied for a position as an element of a prima facie case of nonselection. However, the essence of the prima facie case is to allege any set of facts that would permit an inference of discrimination, McDonnell Douglas v. Green, supra, and establishing that he applied for a position is not always an element of the prima facie case if other facts would permit an inference of discriminatory nonselection. If the complainant were to allege, for instance, that the agency discouraged the complainant from applying for the position or otherwise made it so that the complainant did not know about the open position, such allegations may state a claim. See Ozinga v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 05910416 (1991). As an example, the Commission in Celine B. v. Smithsonian Institution, 2020003113 (2020), reversed the agency's dismissal for failure to state a claim where:

Complainant stated that management did not announce the position at monthly OCPPM staff meetings as is usually the practice. Complainant alleged that management acted improperly to exclude her and several other African-American colleagues from applying for the vacancy. Complainant also stated, in 2017, the Agency failed to give her the opportunity to apply for an Associate Director vacancy.

The Commission in Celine B. noted and held:

[W]e find that the Agency mischaracterized the information Complainant provided as background for her September non-selection as separate claims. The claim before us is Complainant's non-selection for the OCPPM Deputy Director vacancy. Complainant is alleging that the Agency manipulated the classification and qualification criteria for the Deputy Director vacancy to exclude Complainant from applying, and to facilitate choosing the selected candidate, who is outside of her protected class. Complainant also alleged that the Agency failed to convey information about the Deputy Director vacancy as it usually does at staff meetings to prevent knowledge of the vacant position.

Generally, a complainant who claims discriminatory non-selection is not sufficiently aggrieved to state a valid claim when she has not applied for the position in question. See Owen v. Social Security Administration, EEOC Request No. 05950865 (December 11, 1997). However, the Commission has found that, under certain circumstances, a complainant might be aggrieved by non-selection for a position for which she did not apply, where she alleges that the Agency discouraged her from applying or the application process was secretive. See Ozinga v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 05910416 (May 13, 1991). We find this to be the case here. Complainant's allegations that the Agency affirmatively acted discriminated against her by discouraging her from applying and/or having a secretive selection process are allegations of an injury or harm to a term, condition, or privilege of employment for which there is a remedy. See Diaz, supra. Therefore, we find that the Agency's dismissal of the complaint pursuant to 29 C.F.R. § 1614.107(a)(1), for failure to state a claim, was not appropriate.

In Pike v. Attorney General, 0120110159 (2011), the complainant had alleged that he was promised reassignment to a chief information officer position on several occasions and told he did not need to apply when the position was posted because he would be reassigned into the position noncompetitively. The Commission, in Pike reversed summary judgment for the agency explaining:

In finding no discrimination, the AJ relied on Complainant's failure to apply for the CIO position. After a careful review of the record, the Commission finds that the AJ erred when he concluded that there were no genuine issues of material fact in this case. The record contains conflicting testimony regarding whether Complainant was offered a reassignment to the CIO position and whether he was discouraged from applying when a vacancy announcement for the position was issued. The Commission has found that, under certain circumstances, a complainant might be aggrieved in a non-selection for a position for which he did not apply where the complainant alleges that the Agency discouraged him from applying or the application process was secretive. See Ozinga v. Dep't of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 05910416 (May 13, 1991). As a result, the Commission finds that issuance of a decision without a hearing was improper as genuine issues of material fact remain. Therefore, judgment as a matter of law for the Agency should not have been granted.

Similarly, in Karren N. v. Attorney General, 0120160848 (2016), the complainant maintained that her failure to apply for the at-issue position was due to repeated statements by the Special Agent in Charge that he "'would not allow' Complainant to transfer, and that she would need 'to do her time.'" The agency dismissed the complaint for failure to state a claim, because the complainant did not apply for the position. The Commission, in Karren N., reversed, noting that the agency missed the point:

Here, the Agency argued in its final decision that Complainant was not aggrieved as she had not applied for the position that E1 applied, and was accepted for. On appeal, the Agency further argued that the Agency had specific policies set in place for transfers, that the record did not demonstrate that the SAC had prevented any of Complainant's transfer requests, and that E1 and Complainant served in different positions at the Agency. We find that the Agency has articulated reasons that go to the merits of Complainant's complaint, and as such, is irrelevant to the procedural issue of whether she has stated a justiciable claim. See Ferrazzoli v. USPS, EEOC Request No. 05910642 (August 15, 1991). The essence of the subject claim is that an employee not in Complainant's protected classes was given more favored treatment in lateral transfer requests, and that Complainant was unsuccessful in her pursuits of lateral transfers. Complainant has addressed a personal loss or harm to a term, condition or privilege of employment for which there is a remedy.

A complainant states a viable claim of discrimination where she alleges that agency management actively discouraged her from applying for a job despite the complainant having failed to apply for the position. See Jill M. v. Secretary of Interior, 0120171607 (2017).

In Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120143106 (2015), the Commission found the complainant stated a claim for relief where she alleged she did not apply because the qualifications were designed to disqualify her and all other black females in the office for applying for the job and that she was actively discouraged from pursuing the job. See also Complainant v. Secretary of Air Force, 0120150569 (2016) (allegation that complainant was discouraged from applying stated a claim).
