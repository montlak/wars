A. PLAIN ENGLISH RULE

As noted above in the section on "Intent of the Parties," the Commission interprets settlement agreements by attempting to discern the true intent of the parties. Generally speaking, the Commission follows traditional contract law. First, the Commission will look to the language of the agreement itself, giving the words and phrases their plain English meaning. If the language of the agreement is clear and unambiguous, the Commission will not look outside the four corners of the agreement to determine the intent of the parties. However, the Commission does not adhere strictly to the parol evidence rule. If the language of the agreement is not clear, the Commission will review factors outside the four corners of the agreement that bear on the intent of the parties at the time the settlement agreement was entered. Ultimately, as the Commission explained in Martin v. Secretary of Defense, 01934244 (1994), if by review of the language and outside factors the Commission cannot determine the intent of the parties, it will set aside the agreement on the basis that there was no meeting of the minds:

In ascertaining the intent of the parties with regard to the terms of a settlement agreement, the Commission has generally relied on the plain meaning rule. See Hyon O v. U.S. Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05910787 (December 2, 1991). This rule states that if the writing appears to be plain and unambiguous on its face, its meaning must be determined from the four corners of the instrument without resort to extrinsic evidence of any nature. See Montgomery Elevator v. Building Eng'g Servs., 730 F.2d 377 (5th Cir. 1984).

Interpreting an agreement from the four corners of the instrument and giving the terms their plain English meaning does not require that common sense be abandoned. Actions that one would reasonably expect to flow from the language of the agreement are included within the general rule for interpreting agreements. For example, in Johnson v. Secretary of Defense, 01996168 (2001), the Commission rejected an agency contention that it was in compliance with the settlement agreement because it had in fact changed the complainant's position description and the plain language of the agreement did not require the agency to actually change the complainant's duties. "However, we determine that one would reasonably expect that the change of a 'major duty' in a position description would result in a change in the actual duties the incumbent was tasked to perform." Id.

When the language of the agreement is in plain English that is clear and specific, the parties will be bound by that language. For example, in Cain v. Secretary of Army, 0120070820 (2007), the agency agreed to send the complainant to a specific management course. It then denied his request for training on the basis of cost because it was not offered locally. The agency offered to send the complainant to what it viewed as comparable courses at a local community college. The Commission determined that the agency was in breach of the agreement in failing to comply with its specific terms.

Interpreting an agreement based on the four corners of the agreement and giving terms their plain English meaning does not always work to the advantage of the complainant. For example, in Mason v. Secretary of Transportation, 01A61344 (2006), the complainant alleged that the agency breached a settlement agreement giving him a transfer to a "comparable position" at another facility because the new position was a reduction in salary. The Commission determined that the agreement did not require that the "comparable position" be without loss of salary and found that the agency had not breached the agreement.
