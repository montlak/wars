E. NATIONAL ORIGIN-BASED HARASSMENT

National origin discrimination when "an individual (or their ancestors) is or is perceived to be from a certain place or has (or is perceived to have) the physical, cultural, or linguistic characteristics of a particular national origin group." Norbert K. v. Secretary of State, 2021001898 (2021). Harassment based on national origin or ethnic background can include "ethnic slurs, ridicule, intimidation, workplace graffiti, 'jokes,' physical violence, or other offensive conduct directed toward an individual because of his birthplace, ethnicity, culture, language, appearance, dress or foreign accent." Id.

Allegations of a hostile environment based on ethnicity or national origin will be evaluated from the perspective of a reasonable person in the complainant's circumstances. Hung v. Postmaster General, 01972003 (1997) (acts complained of were "severe enough for a reasonable person in the complainant's circumstances to have found the alleged behavior to be hostile").

However, the complainant, as in sexual harassment cases, must also satisfy the subjective element of the test for hostile environment harassment. For example, in Ltief v. Postmaster General 01A10245 (2002), the Commission found that the complainant failed to establish a claim of hostile environment harassment where he was called a "camel jockey," "camel driver," "tent maker" and "Ali Baba" because the complainant testified he never perceived the comments in a "hateful way" and did not complain of a hostile environment until after his removal. See also Smith v. Secretary of Interior, 01A11272 (2002) (complainant consented to being called "swamp rat" and "raging Cajun" by laughing at the expressions and engaging in similar conduct toward others).

As with harassment on other discriminatory bases, it is possible, though not common, for a single incident to rise to the level of creating a hostile environment. For example, in Ammon v. Postmaster General, 01A51264 (2006), the Commission found that a letter written by the complainant's supervisor on a shared hard drive stating that the complainant was "snubbing her nose at America," should "go back to Germany," and that Germany had "more liberal laws...and maybe Germany will allow [her] a fat disability check," created a hostile environment. See also Arellanes v. Secretary of Defense, 01A63349 (2006) (single incident with comments that agency scientist would not mind sending "one [or] two Mexican illegals" into a bomb test area). The Commission reached a similar result in Teterkin v. Postmaster General, 0120064909 (2007), where the complainant's supervisor, after being advised the complainant could not read English well enough to read and sign a safety talk, said, "If you can't read English get the fuck out. This is my country." The Commission found that the single incident stated a claim for harassment based on national origin.

In order to demonstrate that a hostile environment is based on the complainant's national origin, it is not necessary to demonstrate that the actual incidents of harassment make reference to the complainant's national origin. In Turner v. Secretary of Interior, 05940844 (1996), the Commission reversed its own earlier decision in which found that the complainant's harsh treatment was not because of her national origin. The harassment itself consisted of constant, harsh criticism from her supervisor, including throwing things and making faces at her. He also referenced her performance problems by indicating that she could be fired and her children would be without a home. In reversing its earlier decision, the Commission, in Turner, found:

While the previous decision determined that appellant was not subjected to harassment because there was no evidence of behavior based on national origin aimed at appellant, there is sufficient evidence that S-1 had hostility toward members of appellant's protected national origin group. The testimony of Employee A indicated that in the past, S-1 made slurs such as "dumb Indian" against him, appellant, and a former employee. Furthermore, S-1 made critical remarks about the Indian preference system and the Social Services staff which reflected her frustration about problems with American Indian employees that she thought could not be corrected. The Commission also notes that S-1 made critical remarks about pregnant American Indian women in general, which reflected a hostility in both her remarks to appellant and to appellant's co-workers about appellant's pregnancy.

Thus we find that the agency subjected appellant to a workplace that permeated with ridicule and insult based on national origin which was sufficiently severe and pervasive to create an objectively hostile work environment. Although no comments concerning American Indians were made directly to appellant, such remarks to other employees establish that the abusive environment in which appellant worked was the direct result of S-1's hostility toward American Indians. The Commission finds that the AJ erred in determining that appellant had to prove that for harassment to be considered conduct violative of Title VII, it must be pervasive or severe enough to significantly and adversely: affect the employees's emotional or psychological well-being. Instead, the AJ should have examined whether a reasonable person would find the particular work environment hostile or abusive. Although both the AJ and the previous decision determined that appellant had not been subjected to a discriminatory atmosphere "sufficiently pervasive so as to alter the conditions of employment and create an abusive working environment," the Commission finds that in fact appellant's working conditions were the result of hostile attitudes by S-1 based on national origin.
