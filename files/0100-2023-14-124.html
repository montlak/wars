broida eeo Chapter 05: Settlement of Complaints Chapter 05: Settlement of Complaints, Subchapter 09: Attorney Fee Issues, Section B: Severance of Fee Issues

B. SEVERANCE OF FEE ISSUES

The former EEO regulations, at 29 CFR 1613.217, provided that when the parties agreed on all other aspects of settlement but could not agree on the issue of attorney fees, the fee issue could be severed and subject to a final agency decision under 29 CFR 1613.221(d). The issue could involve whether fees should have been awarded, the amount of fees, or both matters.

Under Part 1614, the Commission has eliminated the provision permitting severance of fee issues. There is, however, no prohibition against severance of fees in Part 1614. Without specifically commenting on the issue of severance of fees, in Park v. Secretary of Treasury, 01933482 (1993), the Commission reviewed a breach allegation of a settlement agreement that provided the complainant's counsel would receive $6,000 in fees within 30 days of the agreement and would submit a detailed fee statement for a final decision on the total amount of fees to be awarded. The Commission did not agree with the complainant's counsel that the agreement guaranteed at least $11,000 in fees. However, it also did not indicate that it had any problem with the basic nature of the agreement insofar as it severed the issue of the exact fee award from the rest of the settlement agreement.

In Campbell v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01970890 (1998), the Commission specifically noted that Part 1614 does not provide for the severance of fee issues. Although the Campbell case did not involve settlement but involved the issue of when an agency decision becomes final, the Commission indicated that the parties could enter a specific agreement to sever the fee issue. Based on Campbell, it appears the Commission effectively preserved the option of severing the fee issue in settlement of an EEO complaint, provided the parties mutually consent to severance.

In Selmon v. Postmaster General, 01A53483 (2005), in reviewing a dispute over attorney fees that arose out of a settlement agreement, the Commission specifically held "where the parties agree on an adjustment of the complaint, but cannot agree on whether attorney's fees or costs should be awarded or on the amount of attorney's fees or costs, the issue of the award of the attorney's fees or costs or the amount which should be awarded may be severed and shall be the subject of an agency final decision."

The Commission explicitly found in Davis v. Secretary of Transportation, 05970101 (1999), that any waiver of fees in a settlement agreement under Part 1614 must be specifically set forth in the agreement. A waiver of costs and expenses was not an express waiver of fees. As the Commission explained in Davis:

In his July 20, 1995 letter to the agency, appellant advised that he no longer wished to pursue his remaining claims and sought only reimbursement for attorney's fees. Appellant attached to this letter an invoice for professional services from the attorney. The Commission finds that appellant's July 20, 1995 letter was a request for attorney's fees which properly was submitted to the agency.

Contrary to the agency's assertions, the Commission finds that the settlement agreement does not preclude appellant from requesting reimbursement for attorney's fees. In this regard, the settlement agreement states only that appellant agreed to withdraw "any action...and costs and expenses...." The agreement does not specify that appellant is waiving his right to request attorney's fees. See generally Parks v. Dept. of the Air Force, EEOC Request No. 05880609 (June 27, 1988) (waiver of attorney's fees must be express).

See also Shephard v. Secretary of Defense, 01972771 (1999) ("The agency's omission of any discussion related to attorney's fees in the Agreement whether intentional or inadvertent does not constitute an express waiver of appellant's request for attorney's fees.").

Under Part 1613, there was no requirement that the parties attempt to resolve the issue of fees as part of a settlement agreement, or that they formally agree to severance of the attorney fees issue. Parks v. Secretary of Air Force, 05880609 (1988). In Parks, the Commission further held that waiver of attorney fees in the settlement process must be express and not implicit. The agreement in Parks, without any specific mention of attorney fees, provided that:

This agreement constitutes the complete understanding between the complainant and the agency. No other promises or agreements will be binding unless signed by both parties.

The agency argued that the provision precluded the appellant from receiving fees. The Commission found that, in the absence of an express waiver, the appellant was entitled to pursue the issue of attorney fees through the severance procedures.

An agency could not agree to sever the issue of attorney fees while barring the complainant from seeking to collect those fees through the administrative process. In Cole v. Postmaster General, 05890779 (1989), the agency agreed to sever the fee issue and the settlement agreement further provided that if the parties could not settle on the amount of fees, the complainant would have the right to file a civil suit in federal district court for the fees. Perhaps predictably, the complainant and the agency were unable to agree on the amount of fees and the complainant filed a civil action. The case was dismissed on jurisdictional grounds. The agency argued the Commission did not have jurisdiction over the case because the appellant waived the administrative process and elected civil action in lieu of the administrative process. The Commission found the agency must issue a final decision on the fee issue and that decision would be appealable to the Commission if unsatisfactory to the complainant.

An agency cannot agree to pay attorney fees in a settlement agreement, unless it could legally pay fees if the complainant prevails. In Terracina v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01912460 (1991), the Commission held that once an agency agreed to pay reasonable attorney fees in a settlement agreement, it cannot sever the fee issue and deny fees on the basis that fees were not authorized under the Age Discrimination in Employment Act. Having agreed to pay attorney fees, "the agency cannot unilaterally abrogate its obligation by invoking extraneous provisions" to the settlement agreement. Id. The Commission found that the agency's acceptance of the provision was unconditional and to subsequently sever the fee issue was a breach of the agreement.

However, on reconsideration in Terracina v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 05910888 (1992), the Commission reversed its holding. The Commission found that an agency cannot, by settlement, obligate itself to relief that is not obtainable under statute.

Although the parties can agree to sever the fee issue, they apparently cannot agree to sever the fee issue and let OFO adjudicate the amount of the fee award. In Green v. Secretary of Agriculture, 01973651 (2000), the parties reached a settlement agreement during precomplaint ADR. The agency argued that it was barred from paying fees for precomplaint work beyond the couple of hours the attorney spent evaluating the case. The attorney, of course, sought fees for the 23 hours of time spent preparing for and participating in ADR. The settlement agreement in Green contained the following provision:

[T]he parties agree to waive the right to a USDA final decision on the amount of attorney['s] fees and cost[s] to be awarded and abide by a decision of the Office of Federal Operations (OFO), Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) on the entitlement to fees during the pre-complaint mediation component of the process.

The Commission decided to avoid the issue and sent it back to the agency in Green:

We note that the parties settled the case prior to the formal complaint being filed and that the agreement provided significant benefits to the complainants. Consistent with 29 CFR § 1614.501, the parties can agree that the agency will pay reasonable attorneys fees for precomplaint process representation. Accordingly, the matter is remanded to the agency. The parties are encouraged to reach an agreement regarding the payment of attorney's fees....
