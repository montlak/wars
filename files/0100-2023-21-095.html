o. Limiting Liability

Although an agency's response to sexual harassment allegations may initially be inadequate and, as a result, expose the agency to liability, the period of liability can be terminated by subsequent remedial action. As the Commission explained in Fuchs v. Postmaster General, 01993271 (2002):

Where the agency knows or has reason to know about the occurrence of acts of sexual harassment on the part of its employees but fails to take immediate and effective action reasonably calculated to end the harassment, including making the victim whole, it will be held liable for a violation of Title VII. Hayes v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01954703 (January 23, 1998), request for reconsideration denied, EEOC Request No. 05980372 (June 17, 1999); Policy Guidance on Current Issues of Sexual Harassment, EEOC Notice No. N-915-050 at 29-30 (March 19, 1990). We find that the record establishes that the complainant told the PM about CW's behavior on at least several occasions and that the PM failed to take any immediate or appropriate corrective action reasonably calculated to end the harassment, alleviate the hostile environment or make the complainant whole.

The record shows that the PM retired from the agency sometime in August 1996. While the record is unclear as to exactly when the PM became aware of CW's behavior and the complainant's resulting uneasiness, we find that the record shows that the agency is liable for a violation of Title VII, up until the time when the Acting Postmaster (APM) took over in September 1996. The record shows that when the APM took over in September 1996, she took immediate steps to prevent further harassment from occurring. The APM gave an EEO/Sexual Harassment talk and took CW aside, while she watched him read the agency's policy on harassment. She also averred that she posted an additional poster on the workroom floor so that it was obvious to everyone. Additionally, she avers that she spoke to the Postal Operations Manager (POM) regarding CW's behavior and she called in a Threat Assessment Team which mediated several meetings with the employees at the facility.

Complainants who establish that they were subjected to discriminatory harassment are entitled to make-whole relief specifically tailored to cure or correct such harassment and minimize the chance of its recurrence. Hayes, EEOC Appeal No. 01954703. At a minimum, the complainant is entitled to specific assurances that no further harassment will occur. Id. To the extent that separating the complainant from the offending co-worker is necessary to provide those assurances, such an action would be appropriate. Here, the record shows that the offending co-worker resigned from the agency's employ in August 1997 and that the PM retired in August of 1996. Therefore, the agency will not need to consider discipline against the individuals found responsible for the acts at issue in this complaint, nor provide training to the responsible manager. Id. The complainant is entitled to have restored any annual or sick leave taken in response to the harassment throughout the period for which the agency is liable. The record is unclear as to what periods of time the complainant was employed 1 between when CW's harassing behavior 2 began and September 1996 when the APM took over. On remand, the agency will determine the period(s) of liability and determine whether the complainant is entitled to the restoration of any leave.

However, in Davis v. Postmaster General, 01A01352 (2003), the Commission found that the agency was liable for sexual harassment where the offender was transferred shortly after the complainant notified the EEO manager, but the supervisor previously had knowledge of the harassment because he had witnessed it and told the perpetrator to stop. Because that action did not stop the harassment, the agency was liable for the resulting hostile environment.
