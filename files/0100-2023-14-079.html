broida eeo Chapter 05: Settlement of Complaints Chapter 05: Settlement of Complaints, Subchapter 05: Compliance With Settlement Agreements, Section B: Remedies for Failure of Agency to Comply

B. REMEDIES FOR FAILURE OF AGENCY TO COMPLY

When the Commission finds that an agency is in breach of a settlement agreement, it has the option of ordering reinstatement of the underlying complaint or compliance with the terms of the agreement. Reynolds v. Postmaster General, 05880955 (1989). In that case, the agency agreed to remove a letter of warning and all related documents from all agency records. Although the documents were removed from the complainant's official personnel file, the complainant's supervisor kept a copy of the documents and later tried to use them at an arbitration hearing to show a past history of problems with the employee. Under the circumstances of the case, the Commission ordered compliance with the agreement as the most appropriate remedy. The agency was advised to order the supervisor to destroy all documents in his possession, ensure that no other agency official had possession of the documents, and to counsel the supervisor on the binding nature of EEO settlement agreements and the seriousness of his conduct in breaching that agreement.

It was previously unclear, but in Pelle v. Postmaster General, 01921595 (1992), the Commission plainly held that it is the option of the complainant to select his remedy. He may request either specific enforcement or reinstatement of his complaint. If the complainant does not indicate his preference in his allegation of breach, it is incumbent upon the agency to determine which option the complainant desires. The agency may not choose which remedy it would prefer to impose. This choice is now codified in the Commission's regulations. See 29 CFR 1614.504(a); see also Klien v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01A04141 (2001); Batchelor v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01997215 (2000) (complainant was given the choice of a reinstatement of the complaint or a specific enforcement).

Even though the complainant can request either enforcement of the agreement or reinstatement of the complaint, enforcement of an agreement is not always possible. Sometimes, reinstatement of the complaint is the only available remedy. For example, in Fife v. Postmaster General, 01902702 (1990), the agency agreed to give the complainant two casual appointments by a certain date, subject to his meeting physical and suitability requirements. The complainant was later rejected on the basis of suitability, but the agency had not scheduled his physical examination until after the date it was required to appoint him to a position. The Commission found the agency was in breach of the clear and unambiguous terms of the agreement. It remanded the complaint for further processing as, obviously, no other remedy was appropriate. See also Wheaton v. Secretary of Agriculture, 0120093622 (2010) (Commission has discretion to order reinstatement of complaint even where complainant has requested specific enforcement); Brandon v. Postmaster General, 0120063291 (2006). (same).

The right to have the complaint reinstated due to breach flows from the Commission's regulations, and the complainant does not have to elect to have the complaint reinstated. In Fooks v. Secretary of Transportation, 01994748 (2001), the agency determined it breached the settlement agreement, but specific enforcement could not be obtained because the complainant no longer worked for the agency. The agency advised the complainant to contact the EEO office if she wished to have her complaint reinstated. The Commission in Fooks reversed the agency's dismissal of the breach allegation when the complainant failed to respond, holding "that the complainant was under no obligation to inform the agency of her desire to reinstate the settled matters. The agency should have automatically reinstated for processing such matters once it determined that the agreement was breached and enforcement of the terms was impossible." Id.

Reinstatement of the complaint is not a proper remedy where the agency complies with the agreement in response to the claim of breach. Drouillard v. Postmaster General, 05990346 (2001).

The election of remedies is given to the complainant by Commission regulations and the agency may not restrict that election in the terms of a specific agreement. In Hill v. Postmaster General, 01A60626 (2006), a provision was included in the settlement agreement to the effect that the parties agreed "that the Complainant may only seek to enforce the terms and conditions of this Agreement pursuant to those Equal Employment Opportunity Commission regulations that address the matter of enforcement." On appeal, the Commission in Hill found:

First, the Commission notes that the agency required complainant to waive her regulatory right to have her underlying complaint reinstated as a remedy in provision (2). However, EEOC Regulation 29 C.F.R. § 1614.405(c) provides that in the event of an agency's non-compliance with a settlement agreement, a complainant may, as a remedy, request either specific enforcement of the breached provision(s), or that the underlying complaint be reinstated for processing at the point at which processing ceased. An agency may not require a complainant to waive his/her rights under this regulation, and such a provision in a settlement agreement is invalid.

When an agency contends that it is in compliance with a settlement agreement, it must present proof of that compliance In Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120122041 (2014), the Commission found that the agency had not presented proof of compliance beyond its mere contention of compliance and ordered specific enforcement of the agreement:

The settlement agreement presently at issue provided that the Agency would process Complainant's request for reassignment upon proof of her having a valid, passing postal exam score. A fair reading of the record reveals that the parties clearly contemplated that Complainant would then be placed into an available career position. Neither party argued against that interpretation in the Commission's prior decision or in the instant case. Based on that interpretation, in the previous decision, the Commission ordered the Agency to produce evidence showing "whether there were any career position vacancies since December 13, 2007, for the purpose of Complainant's reinstatement under the September 13, 2007 settlement agreement." The Agency only produced evidence that it claimed showed that there were no new employees hired for mail handler or clerk positions since December 13, 2007, ignoring the Commission's order to show whether there were any career position vacancies. Complainant presented evidence showing that there were in fact numerous vacancies during that timeframe. Thus, the Commission finds that Complainant has presented sufficient evidence establishing that the Agency breached the settlement agreement. Further, the Commission is not persuaded by the Agency's argument that Complainant was not eligible for the identified positions given that the Agency failed to produce any evidence supporting that claim. Accordingly, the Commission finds that the Agency breached the settlement agreement.

Upon a finding that an agency has breached a settlement agreement, a complainant has the choice of specifically implementing its terms or reinstating the complaint from the point where processing ceased. 29 C.F.R. § 1614.504(a). Given the circumstances of this case, the Commission determines that specific performance is the appropriate remedy. In this case, the Commission finds that specific performance means that Complainant is entitled to be retroactively placed in one of the identified vacant positions along with back pay and all other benefits she would have earned.

If the remainder of the agreement has been executed and the term of the agreement has expired, the Commission will not set aside the agreement and reinstate the complaint even if the agency has breached a term of the agreement that is minor in significance. For example, in Johnson v. Postmaster General, 01A05353 (2002), the Commission found the agency had breached the settlement agreement by assigning a certain supervisor to the same facility as the complainant for a two-month period. However, the Commission also found that the complainant "already has received significant benefits under the agreement, i.e., the accreditation of 180 hours of sick and annual leave, as well as the implementation of provision 9 [relating to assignment of the supervisor] for 4 years and 10 months of its 5-year period, which has now expired. Consequently, although we find breach, we determine that there is no available remedy for this breach." Id.
