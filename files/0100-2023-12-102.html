broida eeo Chapter 03: Formal Complaint Process--Acceptance and Dismissal Chapter 03: Formal Complaint Process--Acceptance and Dismissal, Subchapter 03: Bases for Dismissal of Complaints, Section D: Standing: Failure to State a Claim--Agency Jurisdiction, Subsection 01: Advisory Councils

1. Advisory Councils

Whether an organization that advises the agency on issues related to minorities can subject the agency to a claim of discrimination depends upon the degree of agency involvement in the council. In Meise v. Social Security Administration, 01964905 (1997), the Commission found that failure to select a white participant in a mentoring program run by a Black Affairs Advisory Council (BAAC) stated a claim of discrimination. The Commission in Meise found the relationship between the agency and council contained sufficient indicia of agency involvement:

The record reflects that an employee organization at the agency, entitled the Black Affairs Advisory Council (BAAC) advises the agency's Commissioner on issues related to minorities, women, and the disabled.

On February 15, 1996, appellant filed a formal complaint, alleging that she was the victim of unlawful employment discrimination on the basis of race (white). Specifically, appellant alleged that she was not selected as a participant in a Mentoring Program conducted by the BAAC. On April 5, 1996, the agency issued a final decision, dismissing appellant's complaint for failure to state a claim. Specifically, the agency found that the members of the BAAC made the selection for mentors and participants to the Mentoring Program; that the Mentoring Program is a six-month pilot program administered by the BAAC; and that the final determination on matters relating to the Mentoring Program was made by BAAC members, and not by the agency. The agency concluded that the BAAC "is the responsible organization to whom the complaint should be addressed."

On appeal, appellant argues that the agency was more involved in the BAAC Mentoring Program than it had indicated in its final decision. Appellant submits on appeal various documents in support of this argument. These documents include a letter from the agency's Commissioner dated June 28, 1995, that announces the formation of the BAAC Mentoring Program; details regarding the implementation of this program; and information relating to the selection process for mentors. The Commissioner stated therein that the Mentoring Program is designed to enhance the career development of agency employees in grades GS-4 through 12 by offering a process between a mentor and participant to develop the participant's career potential. Appellant also submits a copy of several recruitment announcements for the Mentoring Program. One such announcement, prepared by the BAAC Chairperson, states that the BAAC and the agency "are embarking on a new partnership between employees." Another announcement states that the program will assist the agency in addressing the "strengths and weaknesses of the targeted population to enhance their job competitiveness." Moreover, appellant argues that the agency Commissioner provided government facilities for the Mentoring Program and designated agency employees to develop and execute the program on government time.

Appellant further argues that EEO regulations prohibit the agency's participation in the activities promoted by the BAAC. Moreover, appellant argues that her complaint was improperly dismissed based on evidence that was withheld from her during pre-complaint processing, and further argues that the agency purportedly conducted a "second inquiry" into her complaint. Finally, appellant requests that the Commission note that the agency official who signed the final agency decision is a participant in the Mentoring Program, and that his participation in the Mentoring Program caused him to issue a biased decision.

In response, the agency argues that appellant's complaint was properly dismissed for failure to state a claim. The agency notes that members of BAAC developed the Mentoring Program; developed the selection criteria; made the selections of both the mentors and the participants; and administered the pilot program. The agency argues that while it supports the program, it is an initiative of BAAC, and not an initiative of the agency. The agency also argues that the appellant's claim is insufficient to establish standing, because the allegation raised in appellant's complaint is a generalized grievance, shared substantially by a large class of individuals.

In its final decision, the agency stated that the BAAC made the selection of mentors and participants, and that the BAAC, not the agency, is the responsible organization to whom the complaint should be addressed.

The Commission in Meise found that the evidence contained sufficient indicia of agency involvement in the operations of the BAAC, "i.e., a letter from the agency Commissioner that provides a detailed discussion of the formation of the BAAC Mentoring Program, its implementation and its selection process; and a recruitment announcement prepared by a BAAC official categorizing the relationship between BAAC and the agency as a 'partnership.'" Id.

However, in Ramos v. Secretary of Treasury, 01A11919 (2001), the Commission found that the president of a chapter of HIRE, an independent organization formed to promote the recruitment, training, hiring and promotion of Hispanic IRS employees was not aggrieved for purposes of filing an EEO complaint when he was removed as head of the organization. The Commission found there was no allegation of agency involvement in the action. To the extent that the complainant alleged his removal as president of the chapter could affect his chances at future promotion with the agency, the Commission found that this was a speculative injury that could not form the basis of a present claim.
