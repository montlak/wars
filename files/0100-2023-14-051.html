b. Settlements Without Time Limitations

As a general matter, when an agency changes a work assignment that is the result of a settlement within one year of the agreement, it will need to justify that change. For example, in Hartman v. Postmaster General, 01A4590 (2004), the Commission found that the complainant's reassignment within one year of the agreement was a breach where the agency presented no evidence that the change was the result of "operational needs or agency directives that were unforeseen at the time the agreement was executed."

In Buchan v. Postmaster General, 01A31469 (2003), the Commission found that the agency breached the agreement where her supervisors ended a tour of duty within the complainant's medical restrictions only six months after the signing of the agreement. The Commission in Buchan rejected the agency's argument that the agreement was not breached because the supervisors did not know of the agreement, finding "that the individuals involved with executing the settlement provisions should have been informed about the specific terms." Id. Similarly, in Wakefield v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 0120071155 (2008), the Commission found that the agency was in breach of an agreement where 14 months after it agreed to take certain WS-14 duties away from the complainant, it assigned him other WS-14 duties. The Commission, in Wakefield, specifically rejected the agency's argument that the agreement only barred it from assigning WS-14 referenced in the agreement:

However, we find that the agency erred when it found that it complied with provision (4) of the settlement. In its December 2006 FAD, the agency claimed that provision (4) of the settlement agreement did not isolate complainant from subsequent events having an effect on his duties and responsibilities. On appeal, the agency places particular emphasis on the fact that provision (4) applies to "any and all identified WS-14 grade level responsibilities that the complainant may be currently performing" (emphasis added). The agency argues that it was only required to remove any WS-14 duties that complainant was performing at the time of the agreement, and that nothing in the settlement agreement precludes the agency from subsequently assigning complainant WS-14 duties. Thus the agency concludes that complainant's allegations raise new issues unrelated to the settlement agreement which must be raised in a separate EEO complaint.

The agency is correct in its assertion that settlement terms of an unspecified duration which entitle an employee to a certain position or to specific duties are not binding on the agency forever, particularly where the workplace has subsequently undergone reorganization. See, e.g., Holley v. Department of Veterans Affairs, EEOC Request No. 05950842 (November 13, 1997). However, we find the agency's interpretation of its duties under provision (4) to be in violation of the spirit, if not the letter, of the subject agreement as evidenced by the entirety of the agreement's terms. When provisions (2)-(4) of the agreement are read together, it is clear that the spirit of the agreement was to ensure that complainant be paid accurately for his assigned level of work. With this matter in mind, we reject the agency's argument that the agreement allowed it to assign complainant WS-14 duties a mere 14 months after the date of the settlement.

As to whether the agency actually assigned complainant WS-14 duties, complainant alleged that as a result of the 2002 audit called for in the settlement, the agency removed some supervisory responsibilities from the Westside facility position, downgraded the position from WS-14 to WS-13, and changed complainant's job title to WS-13 maintenance and operations general foreman for the Lakeside facility effective July 28, 2002. Complainant provided evidence that reflected this change in his job title. Complainant also claimed that in February 2003, he transferred to the Westside facility position after it became vacant. He provided evidence that from March 2003 to July 2005, the agency gave him one extra reporting duty and the responsibility to supervise anywhere from three to four additional employees. Complainant alleged that these were the same WS-14 duties that the agency removed from the Westside position in the 2002 audit in order to justify downgrading it from WS-14 to WS-13.

The agency has not provided any evidence or arguments to rebut complainant's detailed allegations. The agency's only response to complainant's allegations in this regard is their untenable argument that they may now assign complainant WS-14 duties as they please. Therefore, we find that the agency has failed to support the conclusions of its December 2006 FAD as it relates to provision (4) of the agreement. The Commission has held that the agency has the burden of providing evidence and/or proof to support its final decisions. Ericson v. Department of the Army, EEOC Request No. 05920623 (January 14, 1993); Marshall v. Department of the Navy, EEOC Request No. 05910685 (September 16, 1991). We have also previously found breach of a settlement agreement where the record does not support the agency's conclusion that they complied with the agreement's terms. Cardenas v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 0120064399 (February 4, 2008). In the instant case, we therefore find that the agency breached provision (4) of the agreement.

In Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120140306 (2014), the Commission found the reassignment of an employee to a different shift in a different facility three months after the agreement was signed constituted a breach. The agency and the complainant in that case agreed that he would be assigned to an early shift. However, he was subsequently accused of sexual harassment and the agency found it necessary to reassign him to a different facility during the investigation. In finding that the agency breached the agreement, the Commission explained:

In the instant case, it is undisputed that, starting just three months after the agreement was reached, Complainant was not permitted to work the earliest shift as agreed upon. The Agency argued Complainant was not promised the earlier shift "in perpetuity" and asserted the change was justified by the need to separate Complainant from another employee during the investigation of that employee's claim of sexual harassment.

We are, however, unpersuaded by the Agency's arguments. While we agree that the settlement agreement did not guarantee Complainant would remain on the early shift forever, it is clear that the parties anticipated that good faith compliance with the agreement would last for more than three months.

With regard to the sexual harassment investigation, the Agency may have been justified in moving Complainant to the Highland Annex during the course of that investigation. However, Complainant still should have been placed on the early shift at Highland. Instead, the Plant Manager at Highland was not even made aware of the agreement and its terms. There is no evidence that Complainant could not have been placed on the early tour at the Highland location had the Plant Manager been aware of the terms of settlement agreement. Moreover, the Agency has offered no evidence whatsoever that the investigation is still ongoing or that the sexual harassment charges against Complainant were found to be supported. In fact, according to Complainant, after the investigation ended, the Agency continued to deny Complainant the early shift or return her to her preferred tour at her regular duty station.
