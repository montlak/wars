2. Computation of Time

For the purposes of computing time limits, a document hand-delivered to the EEOC is deemed filed on delivery and a document filed by mail is deemed filed as of the date of postmark. If the postmark is illegible, the document will be considered as timely filed if received within five days of the expiration of the filing period. 29 CFR 1614.604.

There are two determinations to be made with respect to timeliness: when the complainant or the complainant's attorney received the notice of the agency's final action and when the appeal is filed. In its former regulations, in order to establish the appropriate time limits, the Commission required agencies to transmit its final decision to the employee via certified mail, return receipt requested. 29 CFR 1613.221(b)(1). Although there is no similar requirement in Part 1614, service by certified mail still remains the best practice.

Just as with the filing of formal complaints, the agency bears to burden of obtaining sufficient information to determine the timeliness of an appeal. For example, in Cintron v. Postmaster General, 0120111786 (2011), the Commission determined that the agency did not submit sufficient evidence to establish the appeal was not timely filed where it submitted a "Track and Confirm" notice that included only an address and zip code. The Commission found there was no evidence to establish who had actually received the agency's final decision.

In a case where the date of receipt cannot be determined from the agency file, the decision will be presumed to have been received five days from the date of decision. This presumption is rebuttable. If the fifth day is a holiday, receipt will be presumed as of the next working day. See Raab v. Department of Navy, 01832409 (1985). Likewise, when the last day of the filing period is a Saturday, Sunday or a holiday, the employee will be given until the next working day to file a timely appeal. See Christensen v. Secretary of Navy, 05991136 (2002); Delaney v. Veterans Administration, 01830864 (1985). See also 29 CFR 1614.604(d).

When an appeal bears both a postmark from a private postage meter and one from the U.S. Postal Service, the Commission will accept the latter. Benjamin v. Postmaster General, 05880324 (1988). In the unlikely circumstance that the appeal bears two Postal Service postmarks, the Commission will accept the earlier postmark where the complainant no longer is employed by the agency and, presumably, has no access to agency equipment. Id.

In addition, 29 CFR 1614.605(d), which applies to all official correspondence, provides in relevant part:

Unless the complainant states otherwise in writing, after the agency has received written notice of the name, address and telephone number of a representative for the complainant, all official correspondence shall be with the representative with copies to the complainant. When the complainant designates an attorney as a representative, service of all official correspondence shall be made on the attorney and the complainant, but time frames for receipt of materials shall be computed from the time of receipt by the attorney.

While the regulations clarify the notification issue when the employee is represented by an attorney, neither 1614.402(b) nor 1614.605(d) apply to layperson representatives. The Commission has, through its regulations, adopted a two-rule system and application of the rule will be dependent upon the status of the employee's representative. If the complainant's representative is an attorney, then the time limits will not run until the attorney receives the required notice. If the complainant is represented by a nonattorney, or is not represented, the time limits begin to run when the complainant receives the required notice.

In reviewing the timeliness of an appeal, the Commission will assess the situation in "the light most favorable to the appellant since a finding of untimeliness may terminate appellant's rights in the administrative process." Brown v. Postmaster General, 05890332 (1989). Conflicting evidence on postmarks should be construed in the appellant's favor.

An appeal will be accepted as timely where, through no fault of the employee, the postmark is illegible and the agency does not submit proof of the date of receipt. Potts v. USPS, 01832191 (1984).

As is apparent from the review of Commission decisions below, Part 1614 represents a vast improvement by defining, via regulation, when the appeal period begins to run for a complainant represented by an attorney. Under 29 CFR 1614.402(b):

If the complainant is represented by an attorney of record, then the 30-day time period. . .within which to appeal shall be calculated from the receipt of the required document by the attorney. In all other instances, the time within which to appeal shall be calculated from receipt of the required document by the complainant.

As noted above, 29 CFR 1614.605(d) provides that all official correspondence shall be served on the complainant's nonattorney representative with a copy to the complainant. This does not mean, however, that the complainant's receipt of a copy of the notice of final agency action is somehow not "official" enough to start the running of the appeal period. In fact, receipt by the complainant is the only thing that does trigger the time limit, where the complainant's representative is not an attorney. Williams v. Secretary of Veterans Affairs, 01956831 (1998). In Williams, the Commission rejected the agency's argument that the time limit began to run when the complainant's nonattorney representative received the final agency decision because of language in 29 CFR 1614.605(d) that characterizes correspondence to a complainant's lay representative as "official." The Commission, in Williams, noted that the more specific provision pertaining to appeals was 29 CFR 1614.402(b), and therefore that provision was controlling:

As a preliminary matter, the Commission notes the agency's contention on appeal that appellant's appeal is untimely. The agency notes that appellant filed her appeal 37 days after receipt of the FAD by her non-attorney representative, albeit within 30 days of the date on which appellant herself received a copy of the FAD. The agency bases its argument on 29 CFR § 1614.605(d), which characterizes correspondence sent to a non-attorney representative as "official," while copies of such correspondence sent to the complainant so represented are characterized as "unofficial." The agency further argues that to otherwise interpret the Commission's regulations "would render the term 'official correspondence' meaningless, and would have the potential for creating a multitude of problems."

The Commission's regulations contain a specific provision addressing the timeliness of appeals:

If the complainant is represented by an attorney of record, then the 30-day time period [provided in the preceding paragraph]. . .within which to appeal shall be calculated from the receipt of the required document by the attorney. In all other instances, the time within which to appeal shall be calculated from the receipt of the required document by the complainant.

29 CFR § 1614.402(b). Contrary to the agency's argument, application of the two provisions is in no way problematic. Section 1614.402 applies specifically to receipt of the document triggering the time for filing an appeal, while section 1614.605 applies to correspondence generally. It is well-settled that specific provisions govern general provisions which touch on the same matter. See, e.g., HCSC Laundry v. United States 450 U.S. 1, 6 (1981). Further, it is the agency's position which would render a provision of the Commission's regulations--section 1614.402(b)--meaningless in its entirety, since there would be no reason to distinguish between receipt of the triggering document by an attorney representative versus a non-attorney representative. Accordingly, the Commission finds the agency's argument to be wholly without merit.

Prior to the 1999 amendments to the regulation, the Commission had issued conflicting decisions regarding who must receive documentation to trigger time frames. Compare Brooking v. Department of Army, 01850212 (1985), and Bergesen v. Postmaster General, 05880790 (1988).

The new regulations are in accordance with Irwin v. Veterans Administration, 874 F.2d 1092 (5th Cir. 1989). Irwin was affirmed by the U.S. Supreme Court at 49 U.S. 89, 111 S. Ct. 453, 456 (1990). The Supreme Court in Irwin concluded:

[A]s the Court of Appeals observed, [42 USC] 2000e-16(c) requires only that the EEOC notification letter be "received"; it does not specify receipt by the claimant rather than by the claimant's representative. There is no question but that petitioner appeared by his attorney in the EEOC proceeding. Under our system of representative litigation, "each party is deemed bound by the acts of his lawyer-agent and is considered to have 'notice of all facts, notice of which can be charged upon the attorney.'" Link v. Wabash R. Co., 370 U.S. 626, 634, 82 S. Ct. 1386, 1390, 8 L. Ed.2d 734 (1962) (quoting Smith v. Ayer, 101 U.S. 320, 326, 25 L. Ed. 955 (1880)). Congress has endorsed this sensible practice in the analogous provisions of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, which provide that "[w]henever under these rules service is required or permitted to be made upon a party represented by an attorney the service shall be made upon the attorney unless service upon the party is ordered by the court." Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 5(b). To read the term "receipt" to mean only "actual receipt by the claimant" would render the practice of notification through counsel a meaningless exercise. If Congress intends to depart from the common and established practice of providing notification through counsel, it must do so expressly. . . .

The Court also found that, while Title VII claims are subject to equitable tolling, the statute of limitation would not be tolled for a "garden variety" claim of neglect by counsel in not promptly filing the complaint.
