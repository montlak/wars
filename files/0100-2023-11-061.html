4. Standards of Proof

A complainant is not required to use the "magic words" of continuing violation in his contact with an EEO counselor. It is sufficient for the factual basis of the complaint related to the counselor to reflect a continuing violation. Mathews v. Postmaster General, 01900982 (1990).

Although the burden is ultimately on the complainant to establish a continuing violation, the burden is on the agency to justify its dismissal of an allegation of a continuing violation. Such a dismissal may not be summary in nature. Lara v. Secretary of Army, 01940906 (1994). The agency must permit sufficient development of the record, from a factual perspective, in order to make a decision on whether to accept or dismiss a complaint of a continuing violation. In Lara, the Commission held:

It is well-settled that where an agency rejects allegations based on an issue of timeliness, "[a]n agency always bears the burden of obtaining sufficient information to support a reasoned determination as to timeliness." Williams v. Department of Defense, EEOC Request No. 05920506 (August 25, 1992). Moreover, where, as here, a complainant alleges a "trend and history" of discrimination, "an agency is obligated to initiate an inquiry into whether any allegations untimely raised fall within the ambit of the continuing violation theory." Guy v. Department of Energy, EEOC Request No. 05930703 (December 16, 1993) (citing Williams). As the Commission further held in Williams, where an agency's final decision failed to address the issue of continuing violation, the complaint "must be remanded for consideration of this question and issuance of a new final agency decision making a specific determination under the continuing violation theory."

Consistent with the Court's admonition in Morgan that equitable doctrines should be rarely applied, complainants may find the Commission's review of the reasons why discrimination was not suspected earlier will be more stringent.

Where an agency is ordered to conduct a supplemental investigation to determine if there is a basis for claiming a continuing violation, it may not dismiss the complaint for a second time based on the same record. Fite v. Postmaster General, 01964712 (1997). It must conduct a supplemental investigation and collect the evidence deemed relevant by the Commission.

However, it is not always necessary to remand a complaint to the agency for a decision on the continuing violation allegation if that allegation was not addressed by the agency in its final decision. When the agency addresses the continuing violation theory on appeal and the factual record is otherwise adequate, the Commission can proceed to issue a decision on the continuing violation claim. Rodriguez v. Postmaster General, 01975781 (1998).

Many complainants lost sight of the fact that the continuing violation theory deals only with the issue of timeliness. Once a complainant has established the requisites for a continuing violation claim involving several selections, each selection must be independently evaluated on the merits. As the Commission explained in Lapina v. Secretary of Navy, 01950656 (1996):

On appeal, appellant argues that her nonselection allegations should be treated as one allegation of a continual pattern of not selecting appellant. The Commission finds that the agency properly separated each nonselection into a separate allegation. Appellant's argument concerning her establishment of a continuing violation is only relevant for purposes of determining whether she timely contacted an EEO Counselor. Ultimately, if this complaint is to be decided on its merits, then a determination will have to be made as to whether each nonselection was discriminatory.
