CHAPTER 15

REASONABLE ACCOMMODATION AND GINA

I. OVERVIEW OF REASONABLE ACCOMMODATION AND UNDUE HARDSHIP

In general, an individual with a disability must inform his or her employer of the need for accommodation. See Enforcement Guidance: Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under the Americans with Disabilities Act, "Requesting Reasonable Accommodation," at Question 1. But, there are exceptions to that general rule. The employee is not required to use the phrase "reasonable accommodation" in making a request. A request for accommodation can be in plain English. Id. See also Chapter 14 under the headings "Constructive Knowledge" and "Requests for Accommodation."

The Commission set forth its position in its Enforcement Guidance: Reasonable Accommodation and Undue Hardship Under the Americans with Disabilities Act, "Requesting Reasonable Accommodation," noting that generally an employee is required to make a request for accommodation, but noting certain exceptions. There is no requirement that the employee use the phrase "reasonable accommodation" or any other particular language. "To request accommodation, an individual may use "plain English" and need not mention the ADA or use the phrase "reasonable accommodation." Id. at Question 1.

The Commission views the request for accommodation as "the first step in an informal, interactive process between the individual and the employer." Id. The request can be made by the individual, family member, friend, health care professional or representative. Id. at Question 2. There is no requirement that the request be in writing. Id. at Question 3. Although the employer may seek to have the employee make a request in writing or fill out a form, the employer cannot ignore the original request for accommodation. Id. In this informal process, the employer and the individual should:

clarify what the individual needs and identify the appropriate reasonable accommodation. The employer may ask the individual relevant questions that will enable it to make an informed decision about the request. This includes asking what type of reasonable accommodation is needed.

Id. at Question 5.

A request for reasonable accommodation is not a complaint under the Rehabilitation Act. It is, however, an essential part of the process of receiving consideration for an accommodation. It also is considered protected activity. Under Executive Order 13164, 65 Fed. Reg. 46,565 (2000), agencies were required to develop written procedures for the submission of reasonable accommodation requests. The individual agency should be consulted for procedures for submitting such a request. However, agencies should be aware that while they are required to have written procedures, an individual is not required to use the written procedure to trigger the reasonable accommodation process.

The concept of reasonable accommodation embodies both the process for consideration of reasonable accommodation and the end result of that process. The primary characteristic of the process for consideration of reasonable accommodation is that it must be "interactive," that is, it must be a fluid process that involves the complainant and the agency. There are four possible end results of that process: 1) a finding that the employee or applicant for employment is not a qualified individual with a disability entitled to reasonable accommodation; 2) a finding that no reasonable accommodation exists; 3) providing a specific reasonable accommodation or accommodations; or 4) a finding that any reasonable accommodation would impose an undue hardship on the agency. The ADAAA did not make any specific changes to the existing reasonable accommodation requirement under the ADA.

If the definition of a qualified individual with a disability involves a somewhat circular concept, reasonable accommodation and undue hardship would seem to be mutually exclusive notions. After all, in a practical sense, how could an accommodation simultaneously be reasonable and an undue hardship? Surely, if the accommodation imposes an undue hardship, it is not reasonable. Yet the order of the legal analysis allows such a result. The reason is that reasonable accommodation and undue hardship are two different perspectives on the same question: How far should an employer be required to go to make it possible for a disabled employee to work? Reasonable accommodation is evaluated from the employee's perspective; undue hardship is evaluated from the employer's perspective. In the Commission's legal analysis under the Rehabilitation Act, the effect of an accommodation on the employer is not taken into account in determining if it is reasonable. An accommodation is reasonable if there is at least a plausible reason to believe that implementing it would permit the individual to perform the essential functions of a position. Once an accommodation is deemed reasonable, the Commission's analysis takes the employer's perspective into account in the form of evaluating whether the accommodation would impose an undue hardship. There is nothing incorrect about the Commission's analysis of reasonable accommodation and undue hardship. In fact, that analysis closely tracks the structure of the ADA that has been incorporated into the Rehabilitation Act. It is simply a matter of recognizing what the Commission is doing in these cases in order to properly understand them.

The Commission requires agencies and employees to engage in the "interactive process" to identify possible accommodations. The interactive process is premised on the two assumptions: 1) the individual knows the most about his or her restrictions; and 2) the employer knows the most about what might be done in the workplace to accommodate those restrictions. However, the failure to engage in the interactive process, standing alone, is not a violation of the Rehabilitation Act. "[L]iability nevertheless depends on a finding that, had a good faith interactive process occurred, the parties could have found a reasonable accommodation that would enable the [individual with a disability] to perform the job's essential functions." Shelley v. Postmaster General, 0720070076 (2012), quoting Barnard v. Postmaster General, 07A10002 (2002) (citations omitted).

The basic requirements with regard to reasonable accommodation were summarized by the Commission in Brown v. Secretary of Interior, 03A00004 (2002):

Under the Commission's regulations, an agency is required to make reasonable accommodation to the known physical and mental limitations of an otherwise qualified individual with a disability unless the agency can show that accommodation would cause an undue hardship. 29 C.F.R. 1630.9. The Commission also notes that an employee must show a nexus between the disabling condition and the requested accommodation. See Wiggins v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01953715 (April 22, 1997).

When an employee demonstrates that he is a qualified individual with a disability, it is the agency's obligation to provide reasonable accommodation, unless the accommodation would create an undue burden on the agency. The agency's burden then is to demonstrate that it has in fact given consideration to accommodating the employee and, if it has rejected accommodation options, to demonstrate those options would create an undue hardship for the agency. See Hoang v. Postmaster General, 01923725 (1993) ("Once a prima facie case of...disability discrimination has been established, the burden of persuasion shifts to the employer to show that appellant's condition was such that either: (1) accommodation could not reasonably be made to enable appellant to perform the essential functions of the job adequately and safely; or (2) said accommodation would impose an undue hardship on the operation of the agency."; finding failure to accommodate where agency would not permit complainant to withdraw a letter of resignation submitted during a time that he was suffering from major depression). Unlike accommodation of religious beliefs, to make a showing of hardship, the agency must demonstrate that the accommodation would have more than a de minimis effect.

What constitutes reasonable accommodation for a given qualified employee with a disability in a particular position and the point at which such accommodation becomes an undue hardship on the agency is the focus of hundreds, if not thousands, of cases involving disability discrimination. In part, it is difficult to characterize the law with respect to reasonable accommodation because each case must be analyzed independently. Some generalizations are possible and, in fact, helpful in terms of understanding the law. In applying the following case law to any given situation, special attention should be given to the section on job analysis.
