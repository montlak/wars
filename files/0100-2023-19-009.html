1. Comparison Employees

The EEOC has not established any hard and fast rules for when an employee will be regarded as similarly situated. Although it is clear that an exact match is not required, the Commission's tendency has been to require a high degree of similarity; the employees must be nearly identical. Probably the best test is whether there is a legitimate reason for the difference in treatment between the appellant and the comparison employee. "In order to be considered similarly situated, the person with whom the complainant is comparing herself must be similar in substantially all aspects, so that it would be expected that they would be treated in the same manner." McNeil v. Secretary of Defense, 01980212 (2001) (complainant, who used profanity in front of the manager and the customer, was not similarly situated to coworkers who used profanity in front of each other); see also Colene M. v. Postmaster General, 2020000924 (2021) ("For purposes of establishing pretext, comparative evidence relating to other employees is considered relevant when they are similarly situated. See Anderson v. Dep't of Treasury, EEOC Appeal No. 01A22092 (Mar. 13, 2003). In other words, all relevant aspects of the employees' work situation are identical or nearly identical, i.e., the employees report to the same supervisor, perform the same job function, work during the same time periods, and, in instances where the Agency is responding to problem conduct (e.g. attendance deficiencies), engaged in the same conduct. See Stewart v. Dep't of Defense, EEOC Appeal No. 01A02890 (Jun. 27, 2001), Jones v. United States Postal Serv., EEOC Appeal No. 01983491 (Apr. 13, 2000).").

"Evidence of discriminatory intent generally relates to the intent (or absence of) of the supervisor(s) who recommended and/or took the complained of action." Wilson v. Postmaster General, 01985146 (2001) (no intent can be inferred where the comparison employee had a different supervisor). If there is a legitimate reason, the employees are probably not similarly situated. See Lopez v. Department of Health and Human Services, 01832694 (1985) (an employee seeking temporary promotion for duties performed out of grade was not similarly situated to employees who were assigned lateral details not involving temporary promotions). Likewise, a casual, temporary employee is not similarly situated to a career employee. See Brown v. USPS, 01852036 (1987). Management employees are not similarly situated to employees covered under the agency's collective bargaining procedure, see Nixon v. Postmaster General, 01920713 (1992), and generally are not similarly situated to nonmanagerial employees. See Reams v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120120433 (2013).

The absence of similarly situated comparative employees outside of the complainant's protected groups is not fatal to his prima facie case, because a prima facie case consists only of evidence from which an inference of discrimination can be drawn. On the other hand, the complete absence of any evidence to support an inference of unlawful discrimination is fatal to the complainant's ability to establish a prima facie case. Gray v. Secretary of Army, 01954069 (1996).

It is not sufficient for the complainant to establish that there are similarly situated employees. The complainant must also establish that she was treated differently than the similarly situated employees. See, e.g., Russell v. Postmaster General, 01996892 (2002) (finding that the complainant established that two coworkers who reported to the same supervisor and were on extended sick leave were similarly situated. However, the complainant failed to establish that she was treated differently than those employees because they submitted extensive medical documentation to justify their absences whereas the complainant had not). Where the complainant does establish that she is similarly situated to other employees, establishing disparate treatment is sufficient to sustain a finding of discrimination unless the agency can adequately explain the difference in treatment. For example, in Sibell v. Postmaster General, 0720080020 (2009), the agency terminated the complainant for allegedly arguing with a supervisor and touching him in a threatening manner. The complainant, a Korean female, demonstrated that two white males under the same supervisors were suspended for similar behavior. The Commission, in finding discrimination because the complainant was terminated instead of being subjected to progressive discipline, explained in Sibell:

The agency asserts that the individuals complainant identified are not similarly-situated to complainant because only CW4 and CW5 were supervised by S1 and S2, and their conduct did not deal with threatening physical contact. However, we find that complainant was similarly-situated to CW4. The agency articulated that CW4 did not engage in threatening and unwanted touching, yet the record reveals that CW4 was suspended for ten days for being intoxicated and touching the shoulder and back of a female co-worker. The touching was a central focus of the discipline, and the discipline was severe; therefore the touching was more likely than not unwelcome. Further, we also find that complainant was similarly situated to CW5. While CW5 tries to play down the incident by stating that he and S2 just "brushed shirts," perhaps in an attempt to avoid personal liability, we agree with the AJ's factual finding that CW5 and S2 came into physical contact approximately three times within a few months. Further, the physical contact was sufficient and intimidating enough to result in S2 threatening CW5 with termination if it happened again. Therefore, we find that complainant identified similarly-situated individuals not in complainant's protected classes who were treated more favorably than complainant. As a result, we agree with the AJ that complainant established that she was discriminated against based on her race and national origin when she was terminated instead of being subjected to progressive discipline.

It is error for the administrative judge not to consider evidence of disparate treatment where the complainant presents such evidence. For example, in Montiel v. Secretary of Homeland Security, 0120100100 (2010), the Commission remanded the complaint for further investigation where the judge did not analyze the case for disparate treatment evidence and the record was not fully developed on this issue. The Commission, in Montiel, explained that the judge had improperly focused on the evidence of misconduct and noted:

After a thorough review of the record, including the Report of Investigation and hearing transcripts, the Commission determines that the AJ erred by focusing solely on the June 20, 2008 incident. The record reflects that the AJ spend time determining whether or not the police had reason to come to the party location, whether or not complainant had been drinking, whether or not complainant was aware of the illegal status of two of the party-goers, whether there were underage drinkers in attendance, and whether or not complainant was aggressive and confrontational in his interactions with the police.

However, to properly address the complainant's claim of discrimination, the AJ's attention should have been more focused on the agency's response to the report it received of this incident, including whether or not complainant was treated differently than other similarly situated non-Hispanic agency employees who were reported to have engaged in "off-duty misconduct." We note that the investigation in this case revealed that of twenty-six probationary Border Patrol Agents were terminated within the same twelve-month period as complainant by the same management official, seventeen of those terminated were Hispanic. Only three employees, including complaint, were dismissed for "off-duty misconduct," and all three were Hispanic. In addition, two other Hispanic employees were terminated for a "DWI [driving while under the influence] arrest." Of the nine Caucasian employees terminated, all were terminated for failure to meet conditions of employment, or a failing examination score. Neither during the investigation nor at the hearing was information gathered about whether reports had been received concerning other non-Hispanic employees engaging in what could be construed as off-duty misconduct (i.e. DWI arrest, domestic violence situation, failure to pay taxes, etc.) who received a lesser penalty (or no penalty at all) than termination and why.

Moreover, complainant has also alleged that he was treated differently than other employees because of the agency's lack of independent investigation of the incident to determine whether complainant's actions were sufficient to support his termination. Further inquiry should have made into whether the agency typically relied upon a report from an outside agency (i.e. police report) without conducting its own investigation, especially in light of conflicting accounts. The AJ should have inquired into whether the agency's decision to rely simply on the disputed police account, rather than conduct its own investigation, was the result of discriminatory bias.

In summary, our review of the record reveals that there is insufficient evidence of record to properly adjudicate the issue of whether complainant's termination was discriminatory. Therefore, we will VACATE the agency's final order in this case and REMAND the matter to the agency for a supplemental investigation in accordance with the Order below.
