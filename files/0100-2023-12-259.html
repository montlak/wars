R. CONTRACTUAL OBLIGATIONS

The EEOC may consider contractual obligations in determining whether an agency engaged in discrimination. However, "[t]he Commission is not empowered to rule on purely contractual claims." Williams v. Postmaster General, 01974519 (1998) (Commission will interpret terms of collective bargaining agreement to determine if there has been a breach of a settlement agreement where settlement provides complainant will be given overtime in compliance with contract).; see also Gaudreau v. Veterans Administration, 01882427 (1988). Cf. Martely v. Postmaster General, 01A62430 (2006) (claim that agency violated the collective bargaining agreement is outside Commission's jurisdiction).

But, the fact that an allegation of discrimination implicates a provision of a collective bargaining agreement does not remove it from the EEO purview. If the employee alleges that the agency is implementing the provision on a discriminatory basis, he has stated a claim for relief. See Segarra v. Postmaster General, 01974725 (1998); Gonzalez v. Postmaster General, 01931506 (1993); Suarez v. Postmaster General, 05920492 (1992). See also Love-Boykin v. Postmaster General, 0120114263 (2012) (complainant's reference to collective bargaining agreement was to demonstrate agency departed from normal procedures as evidence of discrimination).

The Postal Service, in particular, has continually sought to convince the Commission that it should dismiss any complaint that relates to implementation of a collective bargaining agreement. The Commission has continually rejected such arguments and asserted its right to decide issues relating to implementation of labor contracts where the employee alleges such implementation is discriminatory. In Encomienda v. Postmaster General, 05920834 (1994), the Commission explained:

Further, the agency's interpretation of appellant's complaint as a challenge to its implementation of a collective bargaining agreement does not necessarily defeat her discrimination claim. The agency cautions that failure of the Commission to reverse our prior decision could result in thousands of EEO complaints filed through 1995, as the agency excesses and abolishes positions nationwide. In addition, the agency argues that such complaints would undoubtedly constitute an abuse of the EEO process because any complaint filed by an employee regarding the implementation of the negotiated collective bargaining agreement would fail to state a valid claim of discrimination.

We acknowledge the agency's concerns regarding the potential number of complaints filed by its employees as the agency progresses towards an automated system nationwide. However, the Commission has consistently held that, except for situations which clearly demonstrate that an appellant is abusing the EEO process, complaints alleging the discriminatory misapplication of a collective bargaining agreement are cognizable under Commission regulations. See Walter M. Houston v. United States Postal Service, EEOC Appeal No. 01913281 (September 10, 1991) (complaint alleging discriminatory application of grievance process is within purview of Title VII). Further, we note that the mere filing of a complaint by a United States Postal Service employee concerning matters also raised in a grievance does not constitute an abuse of the EEO process. In reaching this conclusion, we find that the grievance procedure utilized by appellant is not the type contemplated by § 1613.219, therefore appellant is not precluded from raising the same matters in both forums. Notwithstanding the agency's argument, an appellant's allegation that the agency has discriminatorily misapplied or implemented a term of employment covered by a collective bargaining agreement does not render the complaint beyond the scope of the EEOC Regulations or otherwise render it suitable for rejection.

Moreover, whether appellant can or will prevail on the merits of her discrimination claim is not relevant to the issue as to whether she has stated a claim under § 1613.212. Rather, the only relevant inquiry is whether appellant has alleged an injury arguably protected by EEO laws and regulations. We find that appellant's allegation that she was excused from her bid position on March 6, 1992 because of prohibited discriminatory reasons does state a claim under § 1613.212.

There are instances in which the Commission has upheld the dismissal of complaints relating to implementation of a collective bargaining provision where there is significant evidence that the EEO process is being manipulated for improper purposes. See section on "Abuse of Process," above.
