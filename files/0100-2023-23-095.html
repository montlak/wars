2. Burden Shifting

Once the appellant establishes a prima facie case, the burden shifts to the agency to establish a legitimate reason for its actions and the appellant has the opportunity to demonstrate those reasons are pretext. See McClinton v. Department of Army, 01839676 (1983) (agency rebuts a prima facie case of disability discrimination when it demonstrates that a rating panel, following a rating guide, ranked each candidate by the same criteria and the appellant failed to make the best qualified list). Finally, the burden shifts back to the appellant to come forward with evidence demonstrating that an accommodation can in fact be reasonably made. See Lincovich v. USPS, 01810610 (1982). If no accommodation is requested, but the case merely raises issues as to whether an employee with a disability has been treated differently from other employees, the burden is on the employee to demonstrate that the agency's proffered reasons are not the true reasons for its actions.

In examining the agency's proffered legitimate reason and the complainant's evidence of pretext, the Commission will give weight to statements that tend to indicate a bias against disabled individuals. For example, in Parnofiello v. Attorney General, 01966451 (1999), the Commission noted the selecting official's comments that the complainant's slurred speech made the selectee better able to communicate and "deal with lawyers better," and that the selectee appeared more physically fit as evidence of pretext. The Commission noted there was no criticism of the complainant's slurred speech when he performed the duties of the position on a detail and there were no physical requirements for the position in question.

Also in evaluating pretext, the Commission will examine the reasons given to the employee at the time the action is taken and any subsequent change in the explanation for the action. In Day v. Postmaster General, 01996097 (2000), the Commission found pretext, in part, based on the change in explanations for not hiring the complainant between the time of his rejection and the time of the hearing.

Persons with disabilities are not a homogenous group. It is not a legal barrier to establishing a prima facie case that the agency actually selected another person with a disability to fill a disputed position where the persons involved suffer from different disabling conditions. In Fields v. Secretary of Health and Human Services, 01901487 (1990), the Commission held:

[W]hile the agency argues on appeal that appellant has not presented a prima facie case because another handicapped employee was selected for the position, we do not find this logic persuasive. The Rehabilitation Act does not permit discrimination against one type of handicapped individual simply because another group does not suffer discrimination. Shirley v. Devine, 670 F.2d 1188, 1204 (D.C. Cir. 1982). We note as the D.C. Circuit did in Devine, that the "handicapped" are not a homogenous group and do not share the same traits. Thus, a failure to discriminate against an individual with a physical handicap such as that suffered by the selectee, who is an amputee, does not imply in any fashion that the [agency] did not illegally discriminate against appellant based on his wholly different mental impairment.

Like members of other classes, an employee with a disability must establish that he was qualified and applied for vacant positions. It was proper for the agency to dismiss a complaint of disability discrimination based on nonselection where the employee had excluded himself voluntarily from consideration for the positions in question because they lacked promotion potential and he believed his disability would hinder his performance. Gonzales v. Department of Air Force, 01822066 (1983). To bring the matter within the EEO purview, the employee must allege that he is aggrieved by an act or decision of the agency and not his own voluntary actions.
