10. Death or Illness in Family

In what appears to be a decision that is divorced from all reality, the Commission has held that a death in the family does not provide good cause for extending the 15-day time period for filing a formal complaint. In Williams v. Postmaster General, 01954300 (1996), the Commission held:

We find that appellant has not provided sufficient justification for extending the time limit for the filing of the formal complaint. In this case, the notice of right to file was received by appellant on January 26, 1995, specifically informing her that she had 15 days to file her formal complaint. Appellant did not file her complaint until February 27, 1995, 17 days after it was due. Although appellant indicated that her complaint was untimely because of the death of her son on February 10, 1995, nothing in the record supports a finding that appellant was unable to file her formal complaint in a timely manner, i.e., prior to February 10, 1995. Thus, appellant has provided no basis for waiving the time limit for the filing of her formal complaint, and the agency's dismissal of her complaint was proper....

The underlying complaint was that the agency had denied the complainant time to telephone her son, who was ill, to see how he was doing. Most reasonable people, a category that apparently does not include those who work at the Office of Federal Operations, would conclude that the illness and ultimate death of a son is good cause for missing a filing deadline.

In Benner v. Postmaster General, 01951850 (1996), the Commission took its callous disregard for the realities of life one step further in finding that the wife of a deceased complainant did not have good cause for the late filing of a complaint when the notice of final interview came just days after the complainant passed away. Explaining its rationale in Benner, the Commission wrote:

The record indicates that the EEO counselor issued the notice of appellant's right to file a written complaint on March 9, 1994, and mailed that notice to appellant's address by certified mail. Appellant passed away on February 28, 1994. That notice was received by appellant's wife on March 15, 1994.

The receipt of a document by a member of the complainant's family at the complainant's home address is effective receipt of that document by the complainant. Fisher v. USPS, EEOC Request No. 05900389 (May 3, 1990).

Appellant's wife filed the formal complaint on June 3, 1994. Therefore, the complaint was filed beyond the 15 day time period to file a complaint. The record indicates that appellant's wife was represented by an attorney when she filed the formal complaint on behalf of her late husband. Although appellant claims that she was not aware of the EEO process and the time limit to file a formal complaint, the notice of right to file a formal complaint which she received on March 9, 1994, clearly stated the time limits to file a complaint and if she had any doubts she could have contacted the EEO counselor for clarifications. This she did not do. Accordingly, the Commission finds that the complaint was filed untimely and the final agency decision dismissing the complaint is AFFIRMED.

Ultimately, the complainant in Benner did find a sympathetic ear. In Estate of Benner v. Postmaster General, 05960326 (1997), the Commission reversed its earlier decision, holding:

We find that appellant has submitted sufficient evidence to justify extending the 15 day filing period in this case. Her unrebutted testimony is that the agency did not provide her with a formal complaint when she received its March 9, 1994 letter. Appellant was not aware of the EEO process when she took over her husband's case on March 15, 1994. Upon contacting the agency's EEO office, she was only told to sign the notice as her husband's Personal Representative. Based on the facts before us, we find it reasonable that appellant, due to grief, ignorance of the EEO process, and the assumption that her husband had already submitted all other necessary documents, would not have inquired about the status of her case until May 1994. We further note appellant's contacts with the EEO office on May 20, 23 and 31, 1994, to seek assistance in completing the complaint form. Thus, we find that appellant, after she received the formal complaint form, acted in a diligent fashion in pursuing her complaint.

Finally, in Kobel v. Secretary of Treasury, 01A01996 (2000), the Commission found that the death of the complainant's mother during the 15-day filing period was good cause for extending the time limit. A similar result was reached in Complainant v. Postmaster General, 0120141584 (2014):

The record indicates that Complainant received the Notice on January 21, 2014, and the Agency received Complainant's formal complaint, postmarked February 6, 2014, one day after the expiration of the fifteen-day limitation period. However, Complainant explained her tardiness of one day, by detailing the difficulties she had experienced during the fifteen-day period, involving the deteriorating physical condition of her mother at the beginning of the fifteen-day period, the death of her mother on January 27, 2014, and the necessity to attend to the urgent personal and business affairs in the aftermath her mother's death. Given these circumstances, we exercise our discretion, pursuant to 29 C.F.R. 1614.604(c), in finding that equitable tolling is appropriate.

In Camie B. v. Secretary of Defense, 0120162689 (2016), the Commission excused a 15-day delay where the complainant's father passed away one day before the filing deadline. The Commission also noted the "relatively brief delay in filing her complaint" as additional justification for excusing her late filing. See also Kohlmeyer v. Air Force, 05960038 (1996) (appeal deemed timely where appellant alleged her father was gravely ill and she submitted a copy of the obituary).

In Thomas v. Postmaster General, 01960033 (1996), the complainant maintained that she had not timely filed her formal complaint because her fiance was shot seven times and she was caring for him during the relevant time period. She presented a doctor's note stating only that a named individual was seen in his office on certain dates. The Commission held it could not determine from the record if the person referred to in the note was appellant's fiance, under what circumstances that person entered the hospital, and whether and when appellant cared for her fiance when he was released from the hospital. The case was remanded for a supplemental investigation.
