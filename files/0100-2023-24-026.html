4. "Elevated Risk" Standard

In Schwein v. USPS, 10863035 (1988), the Commission explained the rationale for rejecting the so-called "elevated risk" standard. Again, the agency argued that there was an increased risk to the complainant in carrying out the essential functions of his position due to his disabling condition. The Commission, in Schwein, held:

The agency has argued that the appellant cannot be accommodated because the core duties of the positions in question would put the appellant "at risk." To support this argument, they presented no evidence of possible accommodations which still leave the appellant unable to do the job safely, but rather stand solely on the opinion of the agency Medical Officer that the appellant's [condition] places him "at risk" in the postal work environment. The Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals faced the same reasoning in Mantolete v. Bolger, 767 F.2d 1416 (9th Cir. 1985). The court first found that an "elevated risk" standard was not appropriate, since the remote possibility of some imagined future harm could be raised to block the employment of almost all handicapped persons. The court found that the question is whether in light of the work and medical histories of appellant, employment of that individual would pose a "reasonable probability of substantial harm."...The court in Mantolete went on to find that the Rehabilitation Act imposes an explicit requirement that accommodation of a handicap be considered in determining a handicapped person's qualifications for federal employment; "therefore, a good-faith or rational belief on behalf of an employer that no accommodation can be made to protect against a reasonable probability of substantial injury will not be sufficient defense to an act of discrimination...."

Similarly, in Shankar v. Secretary of Army, 01953386 (1998), the Commission found that an agency could not disqualify a pediatric physician from his duties on the basis of elevated risk merely based upon its perceived legal liability in the event of a medical misdiagnosis. The Commission, in Shankar, found the agency must evaluate its risk in light of whether the employee can be reasonably accommodated. See also Wesson v. Postmaster General, 01976166 (1999) ("[T]he Commission notes that the agency cannot exclude qualified individuals with disabilities from employment, based upon fear of a future risk of injury, without engaging in an individualized assessment which establishes that there is a 'significant risk of substantial harm' which cannot be eliminated or reduced with reasonable accommodation.").
