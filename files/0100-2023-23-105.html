9. Fear for Safety of Others

When an agency maintains that it has treated an employee with a mental disability different from other employees without disabilities due to a concern for the safety of others, the Commission will subject such a determination to strict scrutiny. In Emerson v. Secretary of Navy, 01873136 (1988), the Commission found disparate treatment when the supervisor insisted on counseling an employee with a mental disability in the presence of other employees, but conducted all other counseling on a one-to-one basis. The supervisor testified that the employee's doctor told her he might be "unpredictable."

The Commission, in addition to doubting the testimony of the supervisor, found that the statements of a general physician were entitled to less weight than the psychiatrist who had given no such caution; that what was likely unpredictable was the employee's emotional reaction to the counseling and not a reaction toward others; and that using coworkers instead of another supervisory employee was a questionable practice. "At best, by singling out the appellant for special treatment during counseling as she did, the [supervisor] engaged in the very kind of broad social generalization and simplistic stereotypes about persons with mental handicaps which the Rehabilitation Act was intended to eliminate." Id.

Likewise, in Kharoud v. Postmaster General, 01911941 (1991), the Commission found that a supervisor's fear for his own safety was based on his knowledge of the complainant's prior hospitalization and a possible diagnosis of paranoid schizophrenia. The Commission found the supervisor's version of events differed in some significant ways from the account of another eyewitness and it was likely the supervisor's perceptions were colored by his knowledge of the complainant's medical condition. Despite this finding, the Commission upheld the supervisor's action of placing the complainant in a nonduty, nonpay status. The Commission found that although the supervisor was partly motivated by his misconception of the complainant's condition, he also had legitimate reasons for his actions.

There are, however, some forms of conduct that are so egregious as to disqualify an employee from protection under the Rehabilitation Act. An agency is not required to run the risk of violent assault on supervisors and coworkers to accommodate an employee. For example, in McMillan v. Postmaster General, 03910114 (1991), the Commission found:

[E]ven assuming that the petitioner could demonstrate that his mental condition is a handicap for purposes of the Rehabilitation Act, he is clearly not a "qualified" handicapped person entitled to protection under the Act. Courts and the Commission have consistently held that certain types of misconduct committed by an employee will render him not qualified under the Act and the Commission's regulations. Specifically, a federal agency has no duty to accommodate an employee who engages in violent or threatening behavior which is a risk to the safety of others or to the mission of the agency. Adams v. Alderson, 723 F. Supp. 1531 (D.D.C. 1989); Vincent v. United Postal Service, EEOC Request No. 05900485 (June 14, 1990); Henkes v. General Services Administration, Petition No. 03840094 (July 5, 1988). As the court in Adams wrote:

One who is unable to refrain from doing physical violence to the person of a supervisor, no matter how unfair he believes the supervision to be or how provocative its manner, is simply not otherwise qualified for employment [and entitled to protection under the Rehabilitation Act].

723 F.2d at 1532.

An agency does not discriminate against a disabled employee whose condition is controlled through medication, when the employee engages in inappropriate behavior because of failure to take the medication. Balmes v. Postmaster General, 01922735 (1993). In that case, the complainant licked his palm and rubbed it down the face of another employee. The Commission found that the fact that the condition of manic depression was controlled through medication meant the employee was a qualified individual with a disability because, while on medication, he did not pose a risk to himself or others. However, his failure to take the medication did not excuse his conduct.

Some agencies, such as the U.S. Postal Service, have developed a "zero tolerance" policy for violence. In Sherman v. Runyon, 235 F.3d 406 (8th Cir 2000), the court found that such a policy did not violate the Rehabilitation Act where other, nondisabled employees, were treated similarly when the supervisor perceived a credible threat of violence.
