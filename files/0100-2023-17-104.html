C. COMPLIANCE

Repeated failures to comply with Commission orders or to timely submit reports or evidence of compliance may result in an audit of the agency's program. That was the case in Henry L. v. Secretary of Army, 2020002246 (2020), where the Commission found that the agency was 13 months late filing its ordered report of compliance. The Commission noted 20 other pending cases in which the agency failed to submit requested reports and/or evidence and ordered the agency to submit a written report detailing its compliance efforts, identifying problem areas in its program and an action plan to cure those deficiencies in Henry L.:

EEOC Regulations 29 C.F.R. § 1614.502(a) and § 1614.503 provide that relief ordered in a final2 EEOC decision is "mandatory and binding" on the agency. The regulations also provide that, on behalf of the Commission, its Office of Federal Operations (OFO) "shall take all necessary action to ascertain whether the agency is implementing the decision of the Commission." Finally, the regulations provide that failure to implement EEOC orders will subject the agency to a variety of enforcement actions, including the issuance of a notice to show cause to the head of the agency, a referral to the Office of Special Counsel, and/or judicial enforcement.

Here, on February 24, 2020, the Agency submitted a brief titled, "Agency Response to Petitioner's Request for Compensatory Damages," which addresses Petitioner's request for compensatory damages and leave restoration. The Agency also submitted a document indicating that the Agency requested restoration of Petitioner's leave. We find, however, that these remedial actions do not comply with the Commission's order in EEOC Appeal No. 0120172820. Regarding compensatory damages, the order expressly requires the Agency to issue a "final decision on the compensatory damages issue with appeal rights to this Commission." As for the order pertaining to leave restoration, the Agency provided no information how it calculated the amount of leave to restore nor any evidence that the leave was in fact restored. Furthermore, we find no evidence that the Agency complied any other orders.

Furthermore, for approximately 13 months, the Agency has failed to report on its completion of any of the Commission's orders in EEOC Appeal No. 0120172820. Moreover, the Agency has not submitted any statement of its position or explanation in response to notification of the docketing of the instant petition for enforcement.

Even more concerning, the Agency's refusal to report on its compliance with the Commission's order in this case appears to be part of a larger, ongoing pattern of similar failures. EEOC's OFO is currently faced with multiple cases involving the Agency where it is unable to effectively fulfill its regulatory responsibility to monitor compliance with its orders because the Agency has failed to provide the required reports and/or evidence of its compliance. See EEOC Petition Nos. 2019001866, 2019004318, 2019004319, 2019004320, 2019004321, 2019004322, 2019004323, 2019004324, 2019004325, 2019004327, 2019004328, 2019004329, 2019004330, 2019004334, 2019004335, 2019004336, 2019004337, 2019004338, 2019004339, 2019005948, 2020002241, 2020002242, and 2020002245.

Based on the Agency's failure to provide evidence of its compliance with EEOC's order in EEOC Appeal No. 0120172820, we cannot conclude that it is in compliance. The Commission directs the Agency to immediately take action consistent with the Order below.

Even more concerning, the Agency's failure to timely report on its compliance appears to be part of a larger, ongoing pattern of similar failures over the past several years. See Soo C. v. Dep't of State, EEOC Petition No. 2020003866 (Mar. 9, 2022) (although Agency substantially complied with EEOC's order, taking notice that compliance was not timely, that Agency did not timely submit compliance reports, and that some documentation submitted to demonstrate compliance was inadequate); Elise S. v. Dep't of State, EEOC Appeal Nos. 2020004019, 2020004020 (Nov. 22, 2021) (increasing compensatory damages award because Agency continued to delay in providing complainant with effective reasonable accommodation even after receiving EEOC's order); Leon B. v. Dep't of State, EEOC Petition No. 2020004262 (April 28, 2021) (declining to issue sanction for delayed compliance, but reminding Agency that relief ordered by Commission in final EEOC decision is "mandatory and binding" and ordering Agency to award petitioner attorney's fees incurred as a result of filing petition for enforcement); Harvey D. v. Dep't of State, EEOC Petition Nos. 2019005285, 2019005287 (Dec. 29, 2020) (finding Agency had not submitted any supporting documentation demonstrating compliance with EEOC orders concerning compensatory damages and attorney's fees); Madelaine G. v. Dep't of State, EEOC Petition No. 2019005970/EEOC Appeal No. 2020000241 (Sep. 2, 2020) (finding Agency failed to provide evidence of compliance with orders concerning back pay, reinstatement, and attorney's fees and ordering Agency to compute and award interest on back pay and attorney's fees in light of Agency's delay).

Accordingly, we also find it appropriate to order the Agency to analyze its performance in timely compliance, timely reporting of compliance, and providing adequate evidence of compliance with EEOC orders in accordance with the Commission's regulations, during this Fiscal Year and the previous two fiscal years, and to report its findings to the EEOC. In the event the Agency finds it is not in compliance with the Commission's regulations, the Agency is directed to propose to the EEOC a corrective plan of action. Our decision to sanction the Agency in this matter will effectively emphasize to the Agency the need to comply with Commission regulations and orders in a timely manner.
