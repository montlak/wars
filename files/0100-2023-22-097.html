6. Choice of Accommodation

Just as with accommodation of disabilities, the agency's obligation under Title VII is to provide reasonable accommodation and not the accommodation of the employee's choice. In Complainant v. Secretary of Interior, 0120113027 (2015), the complainant, a practicing Muslim, required a compressed work schedule to attend a mid-day prayer service on Fridays, followed by service referred to as "Juma." The agency accommodated the complainant for several years. After the birth of her child, she returned to work with a low leave balance and sought a maxi-flex schedule as an accommodation to better balance her professional and personal obligations. The Commission found that the agency had not failed to accommodate the complainant, explaining:

Based on a review of the record, we find that the AJ properly concluded that Complainant was not denied a religious accommodation. Complainant acknowledges that for years she was accommodated by working a Compressed Work Schedule, which enabled her to observe Juma. On appeal, Complainant contends that after the birth of her son, she returned from maternity leave with a low leave balance and the need to frequently take off additional time when her child was ill. Complainant acknowledges seeking the Maxi-flex Schedule to better balance her personal and professional responsibilities. Therefore, we agree with the AJ, that Complainant desire for the Maxi-flex Schedule was not a request for a religious accommodation. Instead, her religious practices were already being accommodated with the Compressed Work Schedule, and Complainant sought the change to better meet her personal responsibilities. An agency is not required to provide an employee's preferred accommodation if there is more than one effective alternative to choose from. Hyde v. Department of Homeland Security, EEOC Appeal No. 0720110003 n.1 (January 6, 2012). Alternatives for accommodating religious practices include flexible scheduling, which in this case the Agency has provided for years. 29 C.F.R. § 1605.2(d)(ii). Once the Agency reasonably accommodates Complainant's religious practice, the inquiry ends. Wilson v. U.S. West Communications, 58 F.3d 1337, 1342 (8th Cir. 1995) citing Ansonia Board of Education v. Philbrook, 479 U.S. 60, 68 (1986).
